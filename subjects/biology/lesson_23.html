Why Muscles Matter

Every movement you make — blinking, breathing, walking, your heart beating, food moving through your gut — is powered by muscle contraction. At its core, muscle contraction is a story of protein interactions: two filaments sliding past each other, driven by a molecular motor that converts the chemical energy of ATP into mechanical work.

Understanding muscle isn't just about anatomy. It's about seeing how the molecular world (protein conformations, ion gradients, ATP hydrolysis) translates into the macroscopic world (lifting a book, running a marathon, your heart pumping 70 times a minute for 80 years).

Part 1: The Three Types of Muscle Tissue

Skeletal Muscle

Appearance: Striated (striped pattern visible under the microscope) — alternating dark (A) and light (I) bands create the characteristic pattern.

Control: Voluntary — you consciously decide to contract your biceps. (Some skeletal muscles, like the diaphragm for breathing, can operate both voluntarily and involuntarily.)

Key features:

  • Multinucleated: Each muscle fiber (cell) contains many nuclei. This is because skeletal muscle fibers form during embryonic development by the fusion of many myoblasts (muscle precursor cells). Each myoblast contributes its nucleus, resulting in a syncytium — a single large cell with multiple nuclei.
  • Nuclei are located at the periphery (just under the cell membrane/sarcolemma), pushed aside by the densely packed myofibrils.
  • Long, cylindrical fibers — can be up to 30 cm long (e.g., sartorius muscle in the thigh)
  • Attached to bones by tendons — contraction pulls on bones, producing movement around joints
  • Rapid contraction, but fatigues relatively quickly (compared to cardiac)

Cardiac Muscle

Appearance: Striated (like skeletal), but with unique features — branched fibers and intercalated discs.

Control: Involuntary — you can't consciously change your heart rate (though you can influence it indirectly through breathing, exercise, emotions via the autonomic nervous system).

Key features:

  • Single nucleus per cell (occasionally two), centrally located
  • Branched fibers: Each cardiac muscle cell branches and connects to neighboring cells, forming a three-dimensional network
  • Intercalated discs: These are specialized junctions between adjacent cardiac cells, visible as dark lines under the microscope. They contain:
  • Desmosomes: Protein complexes that mechanically anchor cells together, preventing them from pulling apart during contraction
  • Gap junctions: Pores (connexon proteins) that allow ions and small molecules to pass directly from one cell to another. This electrical coupling means that when one cardiac cell depolarizes, the impulse spreads rapidly to all connected cells — the heart contracts as a functional syncytium (coordinated, unified contraction)
  • Autorhythmic: Cardiac muscle can contract without nervous stimulation (the SA node generates its own rhythm), though the rate is modulated by autonomic input
  • Does not fatigue — cardiac muscle has abundant mitochondria (~25-35% of cell volume, compared to ~2% in skeletal muscle) and relies almost exclusively on aerobic respiration

Smooth Muscle

Appearance: Non-striated (no visible banding pattern) — the contractile proteins are arranged in a less regular, more scattered pattern.

Control: Involuntary

Key features:

  • Spindle-shaped cells (tapered at both ends, wider in the middle)
  • Single, centrally located nucleus
  • No sarcomeres — actin and myosin filaments are arranged in a crisscross network anchored to dense bodies (equivalent to Z-lines) scattered throughout the cytoplasm and attached to the cell membrane
  • Slow, sustained contractions — smooth muscle can maintain contraction (tone) for long periods with very low ATP consumption (the "latch state")
  • Found in: Walls of blood vessels (regulating blood pressure and flow), walls of the digestive tract (peristalsis — the wave-like contractions that move food), walls of the bladder, uterus, respiratory airways, iris of the eye

Part 2: The Sarcomere — The Functional Unit of Contraction

Skeletal and cardiac muscle appear striated because their contractile proteins are organized into repeating units called sarcomeres. A sarcomere is the smallest functional unit of a muscle fiber — it's the fundamental unit of contraction. Understanding sarcomere structure is essential for understanding how muscles work.

The Hierarchy of Muscle Structure

Let's zoom in from the organ level to the molecular level:

Muscle (organ) → composed of fascicles (bundles of muscle fibers) → each fascicle contains many muscle fibers (muscle cells) → each muscle fiber contains hundreds to thousands of myofibrils → each myofibril is a chain of sarcomeres → each sarcomere contains myofilaments (actin and myosin).

Myofibrils are the contractile organelles that fill most of the muscle fiber's cytoplasm. They're about 1-2 μm in diameter and run the entire length of the fiber. The alignment of sarcomeres across parallel myofibrils is what creates the visible striation pattern.

Sarcomere Structure — The Banding Pattern

A sarcomere is the region between two adjacent Z-lines (also called Z-discs). In a relaxed muscle, a sarcomere is about 2.0-2.5 μm long. Let's identify each structure:

Z-line (Z-disc):

  • The boundary of the sarcomere — one sarcomere = Z-line to Z-line
  • A protein disc that anchors the thin (actin) filaments
  • Actin filaments extend from the Z-line toward the center of the sarcomere
  • During contraction, Z-lines move closer together (sarcomere shortens)

I-band (Isotropic band):

  • The light band — contains only thin (actin) filaments, no thick filament overlap
  • Appears light under the microscope because actin filaments are thinner and less dense
  • Named "I" for "isotropic" (doesn't polarize light differently in different directions)
  • Shortens during contraction (the actin filaments are pulled further into the A-band, reducing the region of actin-only)
  • Each I-band spans across two adjacent sarcomeres (Z-line runs through the middle)

A-band (Anisotropic band):

  • The dark band — contains the entire length of the thick (myosin) filaments
  • Appears dark because myosin filaments are thicker and denser, plus there's overlapping actin
  • Named "A" for "anisotropic" (polarizes light differently)
  • Does NOT change length during contraction — a crucial point! The A-band stays the same length because the myosin filaments themselves don't shorten; they just have more actin pulled into their zone.

H-zone (H-band):

  • A lighter region in the center of the A-band
  • Contains only myosin filaments — no actin overlap in the relaxed state
  • Named after Victor Hensen (Hensen's zone)
  • Narrows and may disappear during contraction as actin filaments are pulled inward, overlapping the myosin in this region

M-line:

  • A dark line in the exact center of the sarcomere (middle of the H-zone)
  • Contains proteins that hold adjacent myosin filaments together in register
  • Also contains creatine kinase, an enzyme that rapidly regenerates ATP from creatine phosphate

The Key Principle: What Changes During Contraction

During contraction:

  • Z-lines move closer together (sarcomere shortens)
  • I-band shortens (actin pulled toward center)
  • H-zone shortens or disappears (actin enters this zone)
  • A-band length remains constant (myosin filaments don't shorten)

This pattern is the visual evidence for the sliding filament theory — the filaments themselves don't change length; they slide past each other.

Part 3: The Molecular Players — Actin and Myosin

The Thin Filament — Actin

The thin filament is built around a core of F-actin (filamentous actin), which is a polymer of G-actin (globular actin) monomers. Imagine G-actin as individual beads, and F-actin as two strings of beads twisted together into a double helix — like a two-stranded pearl necklace.

Key components of the thin filament:

1. Actin: The actual protein that myosin binds to. Each G-actin monomer has a myosin-binding site — a specific location where the myosin head can attach. In the resting muscle, these sites are covered.

2. Tropomyosin: A long, thread-like protein that winds around the actin double helix, lying in the groove between the two strands. In the resting state, tropomyosin is positioned so that it physically blocks the myosin-binding sites on actin — like a strip of tape covering a row of buttons. When a muscle is relaxed, myosin cannot bind because tropomyosin is in the way.

3. Troponin: A complex of three proteins attached at regular intervals along the tropomyosin molecule:

  • Troponin C (TnC): Binds calcium ions (Ca²⁺). This is the calcium sensor.
  • Troponin T (TnT): Binds to tropomyosin, anchoring the troponin complex to the thin filament.
  • Troponin I (TnI): Inhibitory subunit — in the absence of Ca²⁺, it holds tropomyosin in the blocking position over the myosin-binding sites.

When Ca²⁺ binds to TnC: The entire troponin complex undergoes a conformational change. This pulls tropomyosin slightly deeper into the actin groove, exposing the myosin-binding sites. The "tape" has been removed from the "buttons."

The Thick Filament — Myosin

Myosin is a motor protein — it converts chemical energy (ATP) into mechanical force. Each myosin molecule is composed of:

The Tail:

  • A long, coiled-coil of two α-helices wound around each other (like two intertwined telephone cords)
  • The tails of many myosin molecules bundle together to form the backbone of the thick filament
  • The tails point toward the M-line (center) of the sarcomere

The Heads:

  • Two globular domains at the end of the tail, formed by the folding of the heavy chains plus associated light chains
  • Each head has two crucial sites:
  • Actin-binding site: Where the myosin head attaches to actin to form a cross-bridge
  • ATPase site: Where ATP binds and is hydrolyzed (ATP → ADP + Pi). This site is the "engine."
  • The heads project outward from the thick filament backbone at regular intervals, angled toward the Z-lines
  • The arrangement is such that heads on one half of the filament angle one way, and heads on the other half angle the opposite way — this bidirectional arrangement pulls actin from both sides toward the center, shortening the sarcomere

Important: The thick filament is bipolar — the M-line is the center, and myosin heads on either side pull in opposite directions (both toward the M-line).

Part 4: The Sliding Filament Theory — Step by Step

The sliding filament theory explains muscle contraction as the result of actin and myosin filaments sliding past each other, driven by repeated cycles of cross-bridge formation, power stroke, detachment, and re-cocking. Here's the complete sequence:

Step 0: The Resting State

In a relaxed muscle:

  • Ca²⁺ concentration in the sarcoplasm is very low (sequestered in the sarcoplasmic reticulum)
  • Troponin C has no Ca²⁺ bound
  • Tropomyosin is in the blocking position, covering myosin-binding sites on actin
  • Myosin heads are in the "cocked" position (energized — ADP + Pi bound, from previous ATP hydrolysis), ready to bind but unable to because the sites are blocked
  • ATP is available

Step 1: Excitation — The Signal to Contract

Action potential arrives at the neuromuscular junction:

  • A motor neuron releases acetylcholine (ACh) into the synaptic cleft
  • ACh binds to nicotinic ACh receptors on the muscle fiber membrane (sarcolemma)
  • These receptors are ligand-gated Na⁺ channels — they open, Na⁺ floods in → depolarization of the sarcolemma

Action potential propagates:

  • The depolarization spreads along the sarcolemma and into T-tubules (transverse tubules — deep invaginations of the sarcolemma that penetrate into the muscle fiber, ensuring the depolarization signal reaches deep into the cell)
  • T-tubules are closely associated with the sarcoplasmic reticulum (SR) — the specialized endoplasmic reticulum of muscle that stores Ca²⁺

Ca²⁺ release:

  • Depolarization of the T-tubules triggers voltage-sensitive proteins (dihydropyridine receptors) that are mechanically coupled to ryanodine receptors (Ca²⁺ release channels) on the SR
  • The ryanodine receptors open → Ca²⁺ floods out of the SR into the sarcoplasm (cytoplasm of the muscle fiber)
  • Sarcoplasmic Ca²⁺ concentration rises from ~0.1 μM (resting) to ~10 μM

Step 2: Ca²⁺ Binds to Troponin

  • Ca²⁺ binds to Troponin C (TnC)
  • This causes a conformational change in the troponin-tropomyosin complex
  • Tropomyosin is pulled away from the myosin-binding sites on actin → sites are exposed

Step 3: Cross-Bridge Formation

  • The energized myosin head (with ADP + Pi already bound — it was "cocked" from a previous cycle) binds to the exposed myosin-binding site on actin
  • This forms a cross-bridge — the physical link between thick and thin filaments

Step 4: The Power Stroke

  • The myosin head pivots (changes its angle from ~90° to ~45°), pulling the actin filament toward the M-line (center of sarcomere)
  • ADP and Pi (inorganic phosphate) are released during this pivot
  • The release of Pi is actually the trigger for the power stroke — Pi release causes the myosin head to snap into its low-energy conformation
  • This is the force-generating step — the chemical energy stored in the myosin head (from ATP hydrolysis) is converted to mechanical work

Step 5: Rigor — The End of the Power Stroke

  • After the power stroke, the myosin head remains tightly bound to actin in a rigor state (low-energy conformation)
  • The head will not release until a new ATP molecule binds

Step 6: ATP Binding and Detachment

  • A new ATP molecule binds to the myosin head
  • ATP binding causes a conformational change that releases the myosin head from actin (the cross-bridge breaks)
  • This is why rigor mortis occurs after death: with no ATP production, myosin heads remain locked onto actin, and muscles stiffen

Step 7: ATP Hydrolysis — Re-cocking the Head

  • The myosin head hydrolyzes ATP → ADP + Pi (both remain bound to the head)
  • The energy from hydrolysis is used to "re-cock" the myosin head — it returns to its high-energy, 90° position
  • The head is now ready to bind actin again and perform another power stroke

If Ca²⁺ is still present (tropomyosin still displaced), the cycle repeats → the filaments continue sliding → the sarcomere continues shortening.

If Ca²⁺ is removed (actively pumped back into the SR — see below), tropomyosin returns to the blocking position, and contraction stops.

Step 8: Relaxation

When the motor neuron stops firing:

  • Acetylcholinesterase in the synaptic cleft rapidly breaks down ACh → no more receptor activation
  • The sarcolemma repolarizes
  • Ca²⁺-ATPase pumps (SERCA) in the SR membrane actively pump Ca²⁺ back into the SR (using ATP)
  • Sarcoplasmic Ca²⁺ concentration drops
  • Ca²⁺ dissociates from troponin C
  • Tropomyosin returns to the blocking position
  • Myosin can no longer bind → contraction ceases
  • The muscle fiber relaxes

The Cross-Bridge Cycle — Summary Table:

Step What Happens ATP State Myosin-Actin State
1. Cross-bridge formation Energized myosin head binds actin ADP + Pi bound Attached
2. Power stroke Head pivots, pulls actin ADP and Pi released Attached (moving)
3. Rigor state Head tightly bound, cannot release No nucleotide bound Attached (rigor)
4. ATP binding ATP binds, head detaches ATP bound Detached
5. Re-cocking ATP hydrolyzed, head re-cocks ADP + Pi bound Detached (ready)

Key ATP roles in muscle contraction:

  1. ATP hydrolysis energizes the myosin head (re-cocking)
  2. ATP binding releases myosin from actin (detachment)
  3. ATP powers Ca²⁺ reuptake into the SR (SERCA pump)

Part 5: Sarcomere Changes During Contraction

Let's be absolutely clear about what changes and what doesn't during contraction:

I-band Wide Narrowed Actin pulled into A-band zone
A-band Same Same Myosin filaments don't change length
H-zone Visible Narrowed or gone Actin pulled into central region

The crucial concept: Contraction is not about filaments compressing like springs. It's about filaments sliding past each other like interlocking fingers of two hands being pulled closer together. This is why it's called the sliding filament theory and not the "shrinking filament theory."

Part 6: Motor Units

A single motor neuron doesn't innervate just one muscle fiber — it branches and innervates many. A motor unit is defined as:

> One motor neuron + all the muscle fibers it innervates

When that motor neuron fires, all fibers in its motor unit contract simultaneously. There's no partial activation — it's all-or-none for each motor unit.

Motor Unit Size and Control

Small motor units (fine control):

  • One motor neuron innervates just a few (5-20) muscle fibers
  • Example: Extraocular muscles (move the eyes) — motor units of ~10 fibers. This allows exquisitely precise control of eye position and movement.
  • Example: Muscles of the fingers for fine manipulation

Large motor units (gross force):

  • One motor neuron innervates hundreds to thousands of fibers
  • Example: Quadriceps (thigh) — motor units of ~1000+ fibers. You don't need fine control to extend your knee; you need force.
  • Example: Postural muscles of the back

Graded Force Production

If motor units are all-or-none, how do we produce finely graded force — lifting a feather vs. lifting a heavy box? Two mechanisms:

  1. Recruitment (spatial summation): For light tasks, only small motor units (with low activation thresholds) are recruited. As more force is needed, progressively larger motor units are activated. This is the size principle — small motor units first, large motor units last.
  1. Rate coding (temporal summation): Increasing the firing frequency of motor neurons. At low frequencies, individual twitches are visible. At higher frequencies, twitches summate (fuse) to produce sustained, smooth contraction (tetanus).

Part 7: The Neuromuscular Junction (NMJ)

The neuromuscular junction is a specialized synapse between a motor neuron and a muscle fiber. It's one of the largest and most studied synapses in biology.

Structure

Presynaptic terminal (motor neuron axon terminal):

  • Contains synaptic vesicles filled with the neurotransmitter acetylcholine (ACh)
  • Voltage-gated Ca²⁺ channels in the presynaptic membrane
  • Mitochondria to provide ATP for vesicle trafficking and ACh synthesis

Synaptic cleft:

  • The ~50 nm gap between neuron and muscle fiber
  • Contains basal lamina with acetylcholinesterase (AChE) — the enzyme that rapidly breaks down ACh after release

Postsynaptic membrane (motor end plate on the muscle fiber):

  • The sarcolemma is highly folded (junctional folds) to increase surface area
  • Densely packed with nicotinic acetylcholine receptors (ligand-gated Na⁺ channels)
  • The high receptor density ensures reliable transmission — every presynaptic action potential should trigger a muscle action potential

Signal Transmission at the NMJ

  1. Action potential arrives at the presynaptic terminal
  2. Voltage-gated Ca²⁺ channels open → Ca²⁺ enters the terminal
  3. Ca²⁺ triggers exocytosis of ACh-containing vesicles → ACh released into the synaptic cleft
  4. ACh diffuses across the cleft and binds to nicotinic receptors on the motor end plate
  5. Receptors open → Na⁺ enters the muscle fiber (and some K⁺ exits) → local depolarization called the end-plate potential (EPP)
  6. The EPP is always large enough to reach threshold (it's a very reliable synapse — unlike CNS synapses, the EPP is suprathreshold) → voltage-gated Na⁺ channels in the surrounding sarcolemma open → muscle action potential is generated
  7. The action potential propagates along the sarcolemma and into the T-tubules
  8. Acetylcholinesterase in the cleft rapidly hydrolyzes ACh → choline + acetate (choline is taken back up by the presynaptic terminal for recycling)
  9. The postsynaptic membrane repolarizes, ready for the next signal

Clinical Connections

  • Myasthenia gravis: Autoimmune disease where antibodies attack nicotinic ACh receptors → reduced receptor numbers → muscle weakness and fatigue. Treated with acetylcholinesterase inhibitors (e.g., pyridostigmine) to prolong ACh action.
  • Botulinum toxin (Botox): Blocks ACh release from presynaptic terminals → flaccid paralysis. Used medically for muscle spasms and cosmetically to reduce wrinkles.
  • Curare (and derivatives): Competitive antagonist at nicotinic ACh receptors — blocks ACh binding. Used as a muscle relaxant during surgery.
  • Nerve gases/insecticides (organophosphates): Irreversibly inhibit acetylcholinesterase → ACh accumulates in the cleft → continuous depolarization → spastic paralysis, death from respiratory failure.

Common Misconceptions

Myth #1: "The A-band shortens during contraction."

Reality: The A-band represents the length of the myosin filaments, which do NOT change length during contraction. The A-band stays the same. What changes is the amount of actin that overlaps with myosin — and thus the I-band and H-zone shorten.

Myth #2: "Muscles contract because the filaments themselves shorten."

Reality: Filaments slide past each other — they don't compress or fold. This is the entire point of the sliding filament theory.

Myth #3: "ATP is used for the power stroke."

Reality: ATP hydrolysis occurs before the power stroke — it's used to re-cock the myosin head into its high-energy position. The power stroke itself is driven by the release of stored energy (like releasing a stretched spring). ATP binding (not hydrolysis) then causes detachment.

Myth #4: "Ca²⁺ directly triggers the power stroke."

Reality: Ca²⁺ regulates the availability of binding sites by moving tropomyosin. Ca²⁺ doesn't directly interact with myosin at all. Without Ca²⁺, myosin can't bind, so no power stroke — but Ca²⁺ itself does not cause the power stroke.

Myth #5: "Each muscle fiber has its own nerve supply."

Reality: One motor neuron innervates multiple muscle fibers (a motor unit). Each muscle fiber receives input from only ONE motor neuron, but that neuron may serve many fibers.

Problem 1: Sarcomere Labeling

Draw and label a sarcomere, identifying Z-lines, I-band, A-band, H-zone, and M-line. Indicate which structures contain actin only, myosin only, or both. State what happens to each band/zone during contraction.

Click for Answer

Structures and composition:

  • Z-line: Boundary of sarcomere — anchors actin filaments
  • I-band: Actin only (light band). Shortens during contraction.
  • A-band: Entire length of myosin filaments — contains myosin + overlapping actin in the overlap zone; myosin only in H-zone. Does not change length.
  • H-zone: Myosin only (within A-band, center). Shortens/disappears during contraction.
  • M-line: Center of sarcomere — anchors myosin filaments.

During contraction:

  • Sarcomere shortens (Z-lines move closer)
  • I-band shortens (actin pulled toward center)
  • A-band unchanged (myosin filament length constant)
  • H-zone shortens/disappears (actin pulled into this region)
  • Overlap between actin and myosin increases

The key pattern: only regions containing actin (with or without myosin overlap) change length. The myosin-only A-band stays constant.

Problem 2: Cross-Bridge Cycle

Describe the role of ATP in muscle contraction, explaining its involvement at two distinct steps of the cross-bridge cycle and one additional role in muscle relaxation.

Click for Answer

ATP has three critical roles:

Role 1: Detachment (ATP binding)

After the power stroke, the myosin head is tightly bound to actin in the rigor state. ATP binding to the myosin head causes a conformational change that releases the head from actin. Without ATP, the head remains locked — this is rigor mortis.

Role 2: Re-cocking (ATP hydrolysis)

Once detached, the myosin head hydrolyzes ATP → ADP + Pi. The energy released drives the myosin head back to its high-energy "cocked" position (~90° angle). The ADP and Pi remain bound; the head is now energized and ready for the next cross-bridge cycle.

Role 3: Ca²⁺ reuptake during relaxation

For muscle relaxation, Ca²⁺ must be pumped back into the sarcoplasmic reticulum against its concentration gradient. This is performed by SERCA (Ca²⁺-ATPase) pumps, which hydrolyze ATP to actively transport Ca²⁺. Without this, Ca²⁺ would remain in the sarcoplasm, tropomyosin would stay displaced, and contraction would continue.

Thus, ATP is required at three distinct points: detachment of myosin, re-cocking of myosin, and Ca²⁺ reuptake for relaxation.

Problem 3: Troponin and Tropomyosin

Explain the roles of troponin and tropomyosin in regulating muscle contraction. What would happen if troponin C lost its ability to bind Ca²⁺?

Click for Answer

Tropomyosin: A long, fibrous protein that lies along the actin filament, physically covering the myosin-binding sites on actin. In a relaxed muscle, tropomyosin blocks these sites so myosin cannot bind.

Troponin: A complex of three proteins:

  • TnC: Binds Ca²⁺ — the calcium sensor
  • TnT: Binds tropomyosin — anchors the complex
  • TnI: Inhibitory — in the absence of Ca²⁺, holds tropomyosin in the blocking position

Mechanism: When Ca²⁺ is released from the SR and binds to TnC, the troponin complex changes shape. This conformational change pulls tropomyosin away from the myosin-binding sites on actin, exposing them and allowing cross-bridge formation.

If TnC couldn't bind Ca²⁺: Tropomyosin would remain permanently in the blocking position over the myosin-binding sites. Myosin heads, even when energized (cocked with ADP + Pi), could never bind to actin. No cross-bridges would form. The muscle would be permanently paralyzed (unable to contract), regardless of how much Ca²⁺ was released or how many action potentials arrived.

This hypothetical is similar to what happens in certain genetic mutations affecting troponin function, which can cause muscle weakness or cardiomyopathy.

Problem 4: Motor Units

Explain why the extraocular muscles (controlling eye movement) have motor units of ~10 fibers while the gastrocnemius (calf muscle) has motor units of ~1000 fibers.

Click for Answer

Motor unit size reflects the precision of control needed for a given muscle.

Extraocular muscles (small motor units, ~10 fibers):

  • These muscles control precise eye movements — tracking a moving object, reading, maintaining binocular vision. Tiny adjustments are needed.
  • With only ~10 fibers per motor unit, activating one motor unit produces a very small, finely graded increment of force. The brain can precisely control eye position by recruiting just a few motor units.
  • Many small motor units (more motor neurons relative to total fibers) = high resolution of control.

Gastrocnemius (large motor units, ~1000 fibers):

  • This muscle produces powerful plantar flexion (pointing toes, jumping, running). Fine, delicate adjustments are not needed — what matters is generating large forces.
  • With ~1000 fibers per motor unit, each motor unit contributes a substantial force increment. Fewer, larger steps between force levels are acceptable.
  • Large motor units are metabolically efficient — fewer motor neurons needed for a large muscle.

The size principle: The nervous system recruits small motor units first (for delicate tasks), then progressively larger motor units as more force is required. This gives smooth, graded force increase across the entire range — from threading a needle to lifting a heavy weight.

Problem 5: Neuromuscular Junction

Describe the sequence of events at the neuromuscular junction from the arrival of an action potential at the presynaptic terminal to the generation of a muscle action potential. Explain why acetylcholinesterase is essential.

Click for Answer

Sequence:

  1. Action potential arrives at the motor neuron axon terminal.
  2. Voltage-gated Ca²⁺ channels in the presynaptic membrane open → Ca²⁺ enters the terminal.
  3. Ca²⁺ triggers synaptic vesicles (containing ACh) to fuse with the presynaptic membrane → ACh is released into the synaptic cleft by exocytosis.
  4. ACh diffuses across the ~50 nm cleft and binds to nicotinic ACh receptors (ligand-gated Na⁺ channels) on the motor end plate of the muscle fiber.
  5. Receptor channels open → Na⁺ enters the muscle fiber (and some K⁺ exits) → the motor end plate depolarizes (this is the end-plate potential, EPP).
  6. The EPP is always suprathreshold — it triggers adjacent voltage-gated Na⁺ channels to open → a full muscle action potential is generated.
  7. The action potential propagates along the sarcolemma and into the T-tubules, ultimately triggering Ca²⁺ release from the SR.

Why acetylcholinesterase is essential:

Acetylcholinesterase (AChE), located in the basal lamina of the synaptic cleft, rapidly hydrolyzes ACh into choline and acetate. This terminates the signal by removing ACh from the receptors.

Without AChE:

  • ACh would remain in the cleft, continuously activating receptors
  • The motor end plate would remain depolarized
  • Continuous Na⁺ influx would keep voltage-gated Na⁺ channels inactivated → no new action potentials could be generated
  • The muscle would be unable to contract again after the initial sustained contraction (spastic paralysis, then failure)

This is precisely what organophosphate nerve gases do — they inhibit AChE, causing lethal respiratory paralysis.

Key Takeaways

  • Three muscle types: Skeletal (striated, voluntary, multinucleated), cardiac (striated, involuntary, branched with intercalated discs), smooth (non-striated, involuntary, spindle-shaped).
  • Sarcomere = Z-line to Z-line. I-band (actin only — shortens), A-band (myosin — stays same length), H-zone (myosin only — shortens), M-line (center).
  • Thin filament: F-actin (polymer of G-actin), tropomyosin (blocks binding sites), troponin (TnC binds Ca²⁺, TnT binds tropomyosin, TnI inhibitory).
  • Thick filament: Myosin with heads (ATPase + actin-binding sites) and coiled-coil tails.
  • Sliding filament cycle: (1) Ca²⁺ binds TnC → tropomyosin moves → sites exposed. (2) Energized myosin (ADP+Pi) binds actin. (3) Power stroke — head pivots, releases ADP+Pi. (4) ATP binds → myosin releases actin. (5) ATP hydrolyzed → head re-cocks.
  • Motor unit: One motor neuron + all fibers it innervates. Small = fine control; large = force.
  • NMJ: ACh released → binds nicotinic receptors → Na⁺ enters → EPP → muscle action potential → Ca²⁺ release. AChE breaks down ACh.
