5.1 Seeing Cells: Microscopy

Before we can understand cell structure, we must understand how we see cells. Cells are incredibly small — most are between 1 and 100 micrometers (µm) across. The unaided human eye can resolve objects down to about 0.1 mm (100 µm). To see cells and their organelles, we need microscopes.

The Two Key Measures: Magnification and Resolution

Magnification is how much larger an image appears compared to the real object. A 400× magnification means the image is 400 times larger than the actual specimen.

Resolution (or resolving power) is the minimum distance at which two points can be distinguished as separate. If two objects are closer together than the resolution limit, they blur into one. Resolution is the limiting factor — there's no point magnifying an image if you can't resolve the details.

Analogy: Think of a digital photo. Magnification is how far you zoom in. Resolution is how many megapixels the camera has. You can zoom in all you want, but if the resolution is low, the image becomes pixelated and blurry. Higher resolution = sharper, clearer images.

Light Microscopy

Light microscopes use visible light and glass lenses to magnify specimens.

Feature Value
Magnification Up to ~1,000×–1,500×
Resolution limit ~200 nm (0.2 µm)
What you can see Cells, nuclei, chloroplasts, mitochondria (as dots), bacteria
What you cannot see Ribosomes, membranes (as distinct structures), viruses, DNA molecules
Specimen Can be living or dead; can be stained with colored dyes

Limitation of light microscopy: The resolution is limited by the wavelength of visible light (~400–700 nm). Objects smaller than about 200 nm cannot be resolved, no matter how high the magnification. This is a fundamental physical limit.

Electron Microscopy

Electron microscopes use beams of electrons instead of light. Electrons have much shorter wavelengths than visible light, giving vastly better resolution.

Feature TEM (Transmission EM) SEM (Scanning EM)
How it works Electrons pass through an ultrathin specimen Electrons bounce off the surface of a specimen
Image shows Internal structures (cross-section) 3D surface topography
Resolution ~0.5 nm (can see individual large molecules) ~3–10 nm
Magnification Up to 500,000×+ Up to 100,000×
Specimen Dead, fixed, stained with heavy metals, in vacuum Dead, coated with gold/palladium, in vacuum
Image type 2D, black and white 3D appearance, black and white

Trade-off: Electron microscopy offers incredible resolution but requires dead specimens in a vacuum — no living cells can be observed. Light microscopy has lower resolution but can observe living cells in real time.

Resolution (Resolving Power): The smallest distance by which two points can be separated and still be distinguished as two separate points. Light microscopes: ~200 nm; TEM: ~0.5 nm.

Magnification: The ratio of the size of an image to the actual size of the object. Calculated as: Magnification = Image size ÷ Actual size.

5.2 Prokaryotic Cell Structure

Prokaryotes (Bacteria and Archaea) are the simplest and most ancient form of cellular life. Despite their simplicity, they are extraordinarily successful — they inhabit virtually every environment on Earth.

Key Characteristics of Prokaryotes

  • No membrane-bound nucleus (DNA is in a nucleoid region)
  • No membrane-bound organelles (no mitochondria, no ER, no Golgi)
  • Small: typically 0.5–5 µm in diameter
  • Single-celled
  • Reproduce by binary fission
  • Cell wall present (made of peptidoglycan in Bacteria)

Prokaryotic Cell Components — Structure and Function

Let's go through every part you need to know, from outside to inside:

#### 1. Capsule (or Slime Layer)

Structure: A thick, gelatinous layer of polysaccharides or polypeptides outside the cell wall. Not all bacteria have capsules.

Function:

  • Protection from desiccation (drying out) and from phagocytosis (being eaten by immune cells)
  • Attachment to surfaces (helps bacteria stick to teeth, medical implants, rocks in streams)
  • In pathogenic bacteria, the capsule is a major virulence factor — encapsulated bacteria cause more severe disease because they evade the immune system

#### 2. Cell Wall

Structure: A rigid layer outside the plasma membrane. In Bacteria (Domain Bacteria), the cell wall is made of peptidoglycan (also called murein) — a mesh-like polymer of sugars and amino acids.

Function:

  • Provides structural support and maintains cell shape
  • Prevents osmotic lysis — without a cell wall, water would rush into the cell (since the cytoplasm has a higher solute concentration than the environment) and the cell would burst
  • The cell wall is the target of many antibiotics (e.g., penicillin inhibits peptidoglycan synthesis)

Gram-positive vs. Gram-negative (for context, not strictly IB): Gram-positive bacteria have a thick peptidoglycan layer; Gram-negative bacteria have a thin peptidoglycan layer plus an outer membrane. This affects antibiotic susceptibility.

#### 3. Plasma Membrane

Structure: A phospholipid bilayer with embedded proteins — identical in basic structure to eukaryotic plasma membranes.

Function:

  • Selective permeability — controls what enters and exits the cell
  • Site of metabolic reactions — in prokaryotes, the plasma membrane houses the electron transport chain (since there are no mitochondria)

#### 4. Cytoplasm

Structure: The gel-like aqueous solution filling the cell. Contains water, dissolved ions, small organic molecules, proteins, and ribosomes.

Function: The site of metabolic reactions.

#### 5. Nucleoid Region

Structure: The region of the cytoplasm where the bacterial chromosome is located. The DNA is a single, circular, double-stranded molecule — not contained within a membrane.

Function: Contains the genetic information for the cell.

Key distinction: The nucleoid is not a nucleus — it has no nuclear envelope, no nucleolus, no histones (in Bacteria; Archaea have histone-like proteins).

#### 6. Plasmids

Structure: Small, circular, double-stranded DNA molecules separate from the main chromosome. A single bacterium can contain multiple plasmids, and plasmids can replicate independently.

Function:

  • Carry non-essential but advantageous genes — e.g., antibiotic resistance genes, genes for toxin production, genes for metabolic specializations
  • Can be transferred between bacteria (horizontal gene transfer) via conjugation — a major mechanism for the spread of antibiotic resistance
  • Used extensively in genetic engineering as vectors to introduce foreign genes into bacteria

#### 7. 70S Ribosomes

Structure: Ribosomes composed of a 50S large subunit and a 30S small subunit (S = Svedberg unit, a measure of sedimentation rate — NOT additive: 50S + 30S = 70S, not 80S). Made of rRNA and proteins.

Function: Site of protein synthesis (translation).

Comparison note: Eukaryotic cytoplasmic ribosomes are 80S (60S + 40S). This size difference is the basis for antibiotics (e.g., tetracycline, streptomycin) that selectively target bacterial 70S ribosomes without affecting human 80S ribosomes.

#### 8. Pili / Fimbriae

Structure: Hair-like protein filaments extending from the cell surface. Shorter and thinner than flagella.

Function:

  • Adhesion — help bacteria attach to surfaces, other bacteria, or host cells
  • Conjugation (sex pilus): A special, longer pilus that connects two bacteria during conjugation, allowing the transfer of plasmid DNA

#### 9. Flagellum (plural: Flagella)

Structure: A long, whip-like protein filament (made of flagellin) with a molecular motor embedded in the cell wall and membrane. The motor rotates, powered by a proton gradient (proton motive force).

Function: Motility — enables bacteria to swim toward favorable conditions (chemotaxis) or away from harmful ones.

Note: Bacterial flagella are fundamentally different from eukaryotic flagella — they rotate rather than bend, and are made of different proteins.

#### 10. Storage Granules / Inclusions

Structure: Aggregates of stored nutrients (glycogen, polyphosphate, lipids) in the cytoplasm.

Function: Nutrient reserves for times of scarcity.

Draw this — the prokaryotic cell diagram you need to be able to reproduce for IB:

Draw a rod-shaped (bacillus) cell. Include and label:

  • Outer layer: Capsule (dotted or fuzzy outer line)
  • Cell wall (thick solid line)
  • Plasma membrane (thin line just inside cell wall)
  • Cytoplasm (fill the interior)
  • Nucleoid region (tangled circle of DNA — draw it as a loosely coiled squiggle)
  • Plasmids (small circles separate from the main DNA)
  • 70S Ribosomes (small dots throughout cytoplasm)
  • Pili (short hairlines on surface)
  • Flagellum (one long wavy line extending from one end — note: can be at one end, both ends, or all around)
  • Storage granule (small filled circle)

5.3 Eukaryotic Cell Structure

Eukaryotic cells are larger and more complex than prokaryotes. The defining feature is compartmentalization — membrane-bound organelles that separate different biochemical functions.

The Animal Cell

Let's go through each organelle systematically:

#### Nucleus

Structure:

  • Nuclear envelope: A double membrane (two phospholipid bilayers) perforated by nuclear pores — protein-lined channels that allow selective transport of molecules (e.g., mRNA out, proteins in)
  • Nucleolus: A dense, non-membrane-bound region within the nucleus where ribosomal RNA (rRNA) is synthesized and ribosomal subunits are assembled
  • Chromatin: DNA complexed with histone proteins (see Lesson 3). In non-dividing cells, chromatin is dispersed; during division, it condenses into visible chromosomes

Function:

  • Stores genetic information (DNA)
  • Site of DNA replication and transcription (mRNA synthesis)
  • Nucleolus: rRNA synthesis and ribosome assembly

Draw this: Draw a large circle (nucleus) with a double line around it (nuclear envelope). Add small gaps in the double line labeled "Nuclear pore." Inside, draw a smaller dark circle labeled "Nucleolus." Fill the rest with squiggly lines labeled "Chromatin."

#### Rough Endoplasmic Reticulum (RER)

Structure: A network of flattened membrane sacs (cisternae) studded with 80S ribosomes on the cytoplasmic surface. Connected to the outer membrane of the nuclear envelope.

Function:

  • Protein synthesis for secretion or membrane insertion: Ribosomes on the RER synthesize proteins destined for secretion, the plasma membrane, or lysosomes
  • As the polypeptide is synthesized, it enters the RER lumen where it folds (aided by chaperone proteins) and may be glycosylated (sugar groups added)
  • Proteins are packaged into transport vesicles that bud off the RER and travel to the Golgi

Draw this: Draw a series of flattened, stacked, wavy lines (membranes). Draw black dots (ribosomes) along the outer surface. Label the internal space "lumen."

#### Smooth Endoplasmic Reticulum (SER)

Structure: A network of tubular membranes — continuous with the RER but lacking ribosomes.

Function:

  • Lipid synthesis — including phospholipids for membranes, steroid hormones
  • Detoxification — especially in liver cells; SER enzymes modify toxic compounds to make them more water-soluble for excretion
  • Calcium storage — in muscle cells, the SER (called sarcoplasmic reticulum) stores and releases Ca²⁺ for muscle contraction
  • Carbohydrate metabolism — involved in glycogen breakdown

#### Golgi Apparatus

Structure: A stack of flattened membrane sacs (cisternae) — not continuous, unlike the ER. Has a distinct polarity:

  • Cis face: The receiving side — faces the ER; transport vesicles from the ER fuse here
  • Trans face: The shipping side — faces the plasma membrane; vesicles bud off from here

Function:

  • Modification of proteins — glycosylation (adding/modifying sugar chains), phosphorylation (adding phosphate groups), sulfation
  • Sorting — proteins are tagged for their final destinations (lysosome, plasma membrane, secretion)
  • Packaging — proteins are packaged into vesicles for transport

Analogy: The Golgi is like a postal sorting office. Packages (proteins) arrive from the factory (RER) at the receiving dock (cis face). They are sorted, labeled, and repackaged, then sent out from the shipping dock (trans face) to their destinations.

Draw this: Draw a stack of 4-5 slightly curved, flattened sacs. Label the left (concave) side "Cis face (receiving) — vesicles arriving from RER." Label the right (convex) side "Trans face (shipping) — vesicles departing." Show small circles (vesicles) approaching the cis face and leaving the trans face.

#### Lysosomes

Structure: Membrane-bound spherical vesicles containing hydrolytic enzymes (enzymes that break down biological molecules — proteases, lipases, nucleases, glycosidases). The interior is maintained at an acidic pH (~5.0) for optimal enzyme activity. The lysosomal membrane protects the rest of the cell from these enzymes.

Function:

  • Intracellular digestion — breakdown of macromolecules (proteins, lipids, carbohydrates, nucleic acids)
  • Autophagy — digestion of worn-out organelles (the lysosome fuses with an autophagosome containing the damaged organelle)
  • Autolysis — programmed cell destruction (e.g., during metamorphosis, tadpole tail resorption)
  • Phagocytosis defense — in immune cells like macrophages, lysosomes fuse with phagosomes to digest engulfed bacteria

Draw this: Draw a circle with dots and shapes inside it (representing digestive enzymes). Label "Lysosome — contains hydrolytic enzymes (pH ~5)."

#### Mitochondrion (plural: Mitochondria)

Structure:

  • Outer membrane: Smooth, permeable to small molecules via porin proteins
  • Inner membrane: Highly folded into cristae — increases surface area for electron transport chain and ATP synthase
  • Intermembrane space: The space between the outer and inner membranes; H⁺ ions accumulate here during electron transport
  • Matrix: The innermost compartment, containing mitochondrial DNA, 70S ribosomes, and enzymes for the Krebs cycle and fatty acid oxidation

Function:

  • Aerobic respiration — Krebs cycle (matrix) + electron transport chain & oxidative phosphorylation (inner membrane)
  • Produces most of the cell's ATP (~36 ATP per glucose)

Draw this: Draw a bean-shaped or oval organelle. Outer line = outer membrane. Inner, highly folded line = inner membrane with cristae. Label: "Cristae (inner membrane folds)," "Matrix," "Intermembrane space."

#### Chloroplast (Plant Cells Only)

Structure:

  • Outer membrane and inner membrane (double membrane)
  • Thylakoids: Flattened, disc-shaped membrane sacs — the site of the light-dependent reactions of photosynthesis. Chlorophyll and other photosynthetic pigments are embedded in the thylakoid membrane.
  • Granum (plural: Grana): Stacks of thylakoids — like stacks of coins
  • Stroma: The fluid-filled space surrounding the thylakoids; contains chloroplast DNA, 70S ribosomes, and enzymes for the Calvin cycle (light-independent reactions)
  • Lamellae: Membrane connections between grana

Function: Photosynthesis — converting light energy into chemical energy (glucose). Light-dependent reactions occur in the thylakoid membrane; the Calvin cycle occurs in the stroma.

Draw this: Draw an elongated oval. Inside, draw 3-4 stacks of flattened discs (thylakoids in grana). Connect grana with thin lines (lamellae). Label: "Outer membrane," "Inner membrane," "Thylakoid," "Granum (stack of thylakoids)," "Stroma," "Lamella."

#### Vacuoles

Plant central vacuole:

  • A large, membrane-bound sac that occupies up to 90% of a mature plant cell's volume
  • Contains cell sap — water, ions, sugars, pigments, waste products
  • The membrane is called the tonoplast
  • Functions: Maintains turgor pressure (by drawing water in via osmosis, pushing the cell contents against the rigid cell wall — keeps plants upright), stores nutrients and waste products, contains pigments (e.g., anthocyanins in flower petals)

Contractile vacuole (in some protists, e.g., Paramecium):

  • Pumps excess water out of the cell in freshwater organisms (where water constantly enters by osmosis)

#### Cell Wall (Plant Cells)

Structure: A rigid layer outside the plasma membrane, composed primarily of cellulose (a polysaccharide of β-glucose). Has a fibrillar structure — cellulose microfibrils embedded in a matrix of other polysaccharides (hemicellulose, pectin).

Function:

  • Provides structural support and maintains cell shape
  • Prevents osmotic lysis (the cell wall resists the turgor pressure pushing outward)
  • Allows plants to grow tall without an internal skeleton

Fungal cell wall note: Made of chitin (a nitrogen-containing polysaccharide), not cellulose.

#### Free Ribosomes (80S)

Structure: 80S ribosomes (60S + 40S subunits) not attached to the ER. Found free in the cytoplasm, often in clusters called polyribosomes (polysomes).

Function: Synthesize proteins for use within the cytoplasm (not for secretion or membrane insertion). These proteins are released directly into the cytosol.

#### Cytoskeleton (Briefly — Full Details in Lesson 6)

The internal protein scaffolding of the cell. Three types of fibers:

  • Microtubules: Tubulin, 25 nm diameter
  • Microfilaments: Actin, 7 nm diameter
  • Intermediate filaments: Various proteins, 10 nm diameter

5.4 Animal Cell vs. Plant Cell — Side-by-Side Comparison

Feature Animal Cell Plant Cell
Cell wall Absent Present (cellulose)
Chloroplasts Absent Present (in photosynthetic cells)
Vacuole Small, temporary vacuoles Large, permanent central vacuole
Centrioles Present (involved in spindle formation) Absent in most (plants form spindles without centrioles)
Carbohydrate storage Glycogen Starch
Shape Irregular, variable Fixed, often rectangular (due to cell wall)
Plasmodesmata Absent Present (channels through cell walls connecting adjacent cells)

Shared features (both have): Nucleus, RER, SER, Golgi, mitochondria, 80S ribosomes, plasma membrane, cytoplasm, cytoskeleton.

5.5 Interpreting Electron Micrographs

IB exams often include electron micrographs (EM images) and ask you to identify organelles. Here's what to look for:

Organelle Appearance in TEM
Nucleus Large, roughly circular, double membrane with pores visible at high magnification. Dark-staining nucleolus inside.
RER Flattened membranes in parallel stacks, with dark dots (ribosomes) on one side. Often near the nucleus.
SER Tubular, interconnected membranes without ribosomes. Often more irregular than RER.
Golgi Stacked, curved, flattened sacs (like a stack of curved pancakes). Vesicles near the edges.
Mitochondrion Oval/bean-shaped, double membrane clearly visible, inner membrane folded into cristae (look like parallel lines).
Chloroplast Elongated, double membrane, internal stacks of thylakoids (grana) visible as dense layered discs. Often larger than mitochondria.
Lysosome Dark, roughly circular, uniformly electron-dense (dark staining).
Vacuole Large, clear/empty-looking area (plant cells — dominates the cell). Membrane (tonoplast) may be visible.
Cell wall (plant) Thick, rigid line outside the plasma membrane. May show layered structure.

Pro tip: If you see a structure with a double membrane AND internal folds (cristae), it's a mitochondrion. If it has a double membrane AND internal stacks of discs, it's a chloroplast. If it has a double membrane without internal folds AND pores are visible, it's the nucleus.

5.6 The Endomembrane System

The endomembrane system is the interconnected network of membrane-bound organelles involved in protein synthesis, modification, and transport. It includes:

  • Nuclear envelope (connected to RER)
  • Rough ER → protein synthesis
  • Smooth ER → lipid synthesis
  • Golgi apparatus → modification, sorting, packaging
  • Transport vesicles → shuttle between organelles
  • Lysosomes → digestion
  • Plasma membrane → final destination or secretion

The organelles are physically connected or communicate via vesicles. This system allows the cell to synthesize, process, and deliver proteins with remarkable precision.

Not part of the endomembrane system: Mitochondria, chloroplasts, peroxisomes. These are autonomous organelles.

5.7 Worked Examples

Question 1 (Magnification Calculation): An electron micrograph shows a mitochondrion. In the image, the mitochondrion measures 45 mm across. The actual diameter of the mitochondrion is 1.5 µm. Calculate the magnification of the image.

Solution:

Step 1: Convert to the same units.

Image size = 45 mm = 45,000 µm

Actual size = 1.5 µm

Step 2: Magnification = Image size ÷ Actual size

M = 45,000 ÷ 1.5 = 30,000

Answer: The magnification is 30,000× (or ×30,000).

Question 2 (Actual Size Calculation): In a TEM image at ×15,000 magnification, a nucleus measures 120 mm in diameter. Calculate the actual diameter of the nucleus in µm.

Solution:

Step 1: Rearrange M = I/A to A = I/M.

A = 120 mm ÷ 15,000 = 0.008 mm

Step 2: Convert to µm: 0.008 mm = 8 µm.

Answer: The actual diameter is 8 µm.

Question 3 (Scale Bar): An electron micrograph has a scale bar that measures 20 mm and is labeled "2 µm." What is the magnification?

Solution:

Step 1: Magnification = Image size of scale bar ÷ Actual size indicated

M = 20 mm ÷ 2 µm

Step 2: Convert: 20 mm = 20,000 µm

M = 20,000 ÷ 2 = 10,000

Answer: ×10,000.

5.8 Practice Problems

Problem 1 (Paper 1 — Multiple Choice)

Which of the following structures is found in prokaryotic cells?

A. Nuclear envelope

B. 80S ribosomes

C. Nucleoid region

D. Rough endoplasmic reticulum

Answer

C. Prokaryotes have a nucleoid region (DNA not enclosed by a membrane). They lack a nuclear envelope, have 70S ribosomes, and lack ER.

Problem 2 (Paper 1 — Multiple Choice)

Which organelle is responsible for the synthesis of proteins destined for secretion from the cell?

A. Free ribosomes

B. Rough endoplasmic reticulum

C. Smooth endoplasmic reticulum

D. Golgi apparatus

Answer

B. RER synthesizes proteins for secretion. Free ribosomes make proteins for cytoplasmic use. SER makes lipids. Golgi modifies and packages — it doesn't synthesize the proteins.

Problem 3 (Paper 2 — 4 marks)

Compare and contrast the structure of mitochondria and chloroplasts.

Model Answer

Similarities:

  • Both have double membranes (outer and inner). ✓
  • Both contain their own circular DNA and 70S ribosomes. ✓
  • Both are involved in energy metabolism (respiration and photosynthesis respectively). ✓

Differences:

  • Mitochondria have cristae (inner membrane folds); chloroplasts have thylakoids arranged in grana. ✓
  • Mitochondria are the site of aerobic respiration; chloroplasts are the site of photosynthesis. ✓
  • Chloroplasts contain chlorophyll and other photosynthetic pigments; mitochondria do not. ✓

Problem 4 (Paper 2 — 3 marks)

Outline the functions of the Golgi apparatus.

Model Answer

  1. Modifies proteins received from the RER (e.g., glycosylation — adding carbohydrate groups). ✓
  2. Sorts proteins according to their final destinations (lysosome, membrane, secretion). ✓
  3. Packages proteins into vesicles that bud off from the trans face for transport. ✓

5.9 Common Misconceptions

Misconception The Truth
"All bacteria have a capsule." Many do, but not all. Capsule is an additional layer outside the cell wall.
"Plasmids are essential for bacterial survival." Plasmids carry non-essential genes (though they may be advantageous, such as antibiotic resistance). The essential genes are on the main chromosome.
"The nucleoid is the same as a nucleus." The nucleoid has no membrane. It's just the region where DNA is located in prokaryotes.
"Ribosomes on RER are different from free ribosomes." They are structurally identical (both 80S). The difference is in where the synthesized protein goes.
"The Golgi produces proteins." The Golgi modifies and sorts proteins. Protein synthesis occurs on ribosomes (free or RER-bound).
"Plant cells don't have mitochondria — they have chloroplasts instead." Plant cells have BOTH mitochondria AND chloroplasts. They respire just like animal cells; they also photosynthesize.
