1. Why Classify? The Need for Order

Imagine walking into a library with millions of books but no organization system — no genres, no authors, no Dewey decimals. Finding a specific book would be hopeless. The same is true for the estimated 8.7 million eukaryotic species on Earth (and possibly trillions of bacterial species). Taxonomy — the science of classification — gives biologists a universal filing system.

Classification serves three purposes:

  1. Identification: Assign an unknown organism to a known group.
  2. Naming: Give every species a unique, universally recognized name (the binomial system we covered in Lesson 7).
  3. Prediction: If a species belongs to a group, it likely shares characteristics with other members. Finding a new plant in the nightshade family (Solanaceae) immediately warns you: it might be toxic.

2. The Hierarchical Classification System

2.1 The Eight Levels

Modern biological classification is a nested hierarchy — each level (called a taxon, plural taxa) is contained within a larger, more inclusive level. From broadest to most specific:

Rank Human Wolf Maize (Corn) Fruit Fly
Domain Eukarya Eukarya Eukarya Eukarya
Kingdom Animalia Animalia Plantae Animalia
Phylum Chordata Chordata Angiospermophyta Arthropoda
Class Mammalia Mammalia Monocotyledoneae Insecta
Order Primates Carnivora Poales Diptera
Family Hominidae Canidae Poaceae Drosophilidae
Genus Homo Canis Zea Drosophila
Species Homo sapiens Canis lupus Zea mays Drosophila melanogaster

2.2 The Classic Mnemonic

To remember the order: Domain, Kingdom, Phylum, Class, Order, Family, Genus, Species:

> Dear King Philip Came Over For Good Soup

Or, if you prefer:

> Dumb Kids Playing Catch On Freeways Get Squashed

Make up your own — the sillier, the more memorable it is.

Hierarchical classification: A system of grouping organisms into increasingly specific, nested categories (taxa). Each level is called a rank. The sequence is: Domain → Kingdom → Phylum → Class → Order → Family → Genus → Species. Organisms in the same lower-level taxon share more characteristics and a more recent common ancestor than organisms in the same higher-level taxon.

2.3 The Logic of the Hierarchy

Two organisms in the same genus are very closely related — they share a relatively recent common ancestor. Two organisms in the same class but different orders are more distantly related. The hierarchy reflects evolutionary history: it is (ideally) a summary of the tree of life.

For example, humans (Homo sapiens) and wolves (Canis lupus) are both:

  • Eukarya ✅
  • Animalia ✅
  • Chordata ✅
  • Mammalia ✅
  • But different orders: Primates vs. Carnivora

Humans and chimpanzees (Pan troglodytes) share everything up to family (Hominidae) and diverge at genus. The classification reflects that chimps are our closest living relatives.

3. The Three Domains: Life's Deepest Split

For centuries, biologists divided life into two groups: plants and animals. By the mid-20th century, a five-kingdom system (Monera, Protista, Fungi, Plantae, Animalia) was standard. Then, in 1977, Carl Woese and George Fox published a paper that fundamentally restructured biology.

3.1 The rRNA Revolution

Woese was studying the small subunit of ribosomal RNA (specifically 16S rRNA in prokaryotes and 18S rRNA in eukaryotes). Ribosomal RNA was a brilliant choice:

  • Universal: Every living organism has ribosomes, so rRNA exists in all forms of life.
  • Functionally constrained: rRNA plays a critical role in protein synthesis. Mutations that disrupt its function are lethal, so parts of its sequence change very slowly — like a molecular clock ticking over billions of years.
  • Slowly evolving vs. variable regions: Some regions are highly conserved (nearly identical across all life), allowing comparison across vast evolutionary distances. Other regions are more variable, allowing finer-scale comparison.

When Woese compared 16S rRNA sequences, he discovered that what everyone had been calling "bacteria" was actually two fundamentally different groups, as different from each other as both are from eukaryotes. He named these three groups domains — a rank above kingdom.

3.2 Archaea: The Extremophiles (But Not Only)

Archaea were initially found in extreme environments, leading to the misconception that they're all "extremophiles." In reality, archaea are found in every habitat on Earth, including the human gut and ocean surface waters.

Key characteristics of Archaea:

  1. Cell wall: Lacks peptidoglycan (the defining component of bacterial cell walls). Instead, archaeal cell walls are made of proteins, glycoproteins, or a pseudopeptidoglycan (pseudomurein).
  1. Membrane lipids: This is the most distinctive feature. Archaeal membrane phospholipids have:
  • Ether linkages (instead of ester linkages in Bacteria and Eukarya) — more chemically stable, which helps extremophiles survive boiling acid.
  • Branched isoprenoid chains (instead of straight fatty acid chains).
  • Some archaea have monolayer membranes (lipids joined end-to-end across the membrane) instead of the typical bilayer — this provides extreme thermal stability.

Draw this for comparison: Imagine a typical lipid as a head with two straight tails connected by ester bonds. An archaeal lipid has a head with branched tails connected by ether bonds. A monolayer has a single molecule spanning the entire membrane — two heads connected by a rigid chain.

  1. Genetic features:
  • Some archaeal genes contain introns (non-coding sequences within genes), which is unusual for prokaryotes and more typical of eukaryotes.
  • Their RNA polymerases and transcription machinery are more similar to eukaryotes than to bacteria.
  • Their DNA is associated with histone-like proteins — a feature shared with eukaryotes.
  1. Metabolism: Incredibly diverse — methanogens (produce methane), extreme halophiles (live in salt concentrations that would pickle other organisms), thermoacidophiles (thrive at >80°C and pH <2).

Examples: Methanobrevibacter smithii (common in the human gut), Sulfolobus acidocaldarius (hot springs, pH ~2, ~75°C), Halobacterium salinarum (salt lakes, produces a purple pigment called bacteriorhodopsin).

3.3 Bacteria: The Metabolic Masters

Bacteria are the most metabolically diverse domain. If an energy-yielding chemical reaction exists, some bacterium probably exploits it.

Key characteristics:

  1. Cell wall: Contains peptidoglycan (also called murein), a polymer of sugars (N-acetylglucosamine and N-acetylmuramic acid) cross-linked by short peptides. This is a defining feature — no other domain produces peptidoglycan. The Gram stain differentiates:
  • Gram-positive: Thick peptidoglycan layer, stains purple (e.g., Staphylococcus, Streptococcus, Bacillus).
  • Gram-negative: Thin peptidoglycan layer plus an outer lipopolysaccharide membrane, stains pink (e.g., Escherichia coli, Salmonella).
  1. Membrane lipids: Ester-linked straight fatty acids (the "standard" lipid structure). No isoprenoid chains.
  1. Genetic organization:
  • Circular DNA chromosome located in a nucleoid region (no membrane-bound nucleus).
  • Plasmids: Small, circular, independently replicating DNA molecules that carry non-essential but often useful genes (e.g., antibiotic resistance genes).
  • No introns in typical bacterial genes.
  1. Metabolic diversity:
  • Photoautotrophs: Use light energy + CO₂ (e.g., cyanobacteria — the organisms that oxygenated Earth's atmosphere ~2.4 billion years ago).
  • Chemoautotrophs: Use chemical energy + CO₂ (e.g., Nitrosomonas — oxidizes ammonia to nitrite).
  • Chemoheterotrophs: Obtain both energy and carbon from organic compounds (most bacteria, including all pathogens).
  1. Reproduction: Binary fission (asexual), but with extensive horizontal gene transfer: transformation (uptake of naked DNA), transduction (transfer via viruses/bacteriophages), and conjugation (direct transfer through a pilus).

3.4 Eukarya: The Compartmentalizers

Eukarya includes all organisms with membrane-bound organelles — a true nucleus, mitochondria (or chloroplasts in photosynthetic groups), endoplasmic reticulum, Golgi apparatus, etc.

Key characteristic: Compartmentalization. By isolating different biochemical processes in membrane-bound organelles, eukaryotes can run incompatible reactions simultaneously and regulate processes with greater precision.

The endosymbiotic origin: Mitochondria and chloroplasts were once free-living bacteria that were engulfed by an ancestral eukaryotic cell and formed a mutualistic relationship. Evidence:

  • Both have double membranes.
  • Both have their own circular DNA (like bacteria).
  • Both have ribosomes more similar to bacterial ribosomes (70S) than to eukaryotic cytoplasmic ribosomes (80S).
  • Both divide by binary fission.
  • Antibiotics that target bacterial ribosomes also affect mitochondrial/chloroplast ribosomes.

Domain: The highest (most inclusive) rank in biological classification. The three domains — Archaea, Bacteria, and Eukarya — represent the deepest division in the tree of life, identified through comparative analysis of ribosomal RNA sequences by Carl Woese in 1977.

3.5 Why the Three-Domain System Replaced the Five-Kingdom System

The old five-kingdom system (Whittaker, 1969) grouped all prokaryotes into Kingdom Monera. Woese showed that Monera was polyphyletic (not a natural evolutionary group) — it contained two lineages that are as genetically distinct as each is from Eukarya. The three-domain system reflects actual evolutionary history.

4. The Four Kingdoms of Eukarya

Within Domain Eukarya, traditional classification recognizes four kingdoms. But as you'll see, "traditional" classification is giving way to phylogenetics.

4.1 Plantae (Plants)

Defining characteristics:

  • Multicellular (with few exceptions like some green algae).
  • Autotrophic — produce their own food via photosynthesis using chlorophyll a and b in chloroplasts.
  • Cell walls made of cellulose.
  • Store carbohydrates as starch.
  • Most have alternation of generations (alternating haploid gametophyte and diploid sporophyte stages).
  • Examples: mosses, ferns, conifers, flowering plants.

4.2 Animalia (Animals)

Defining characteristics:

  • Multicellular.
  • Heterotrophic — consume other organisms for energy and carbon.
  • No cell walls (cells have only a plasma membrane, held together by extracellular matrix proteins like collagen).
  • Usually motile at some life stage.
  • Store carbohydrates as glycogen.
  • Most have nervous and muscle tissue (unique to animals).
  • Examples: sponges, jellyfish, insects, fish, birds, mammals.

4.3 Fungi

Defining characteristics:

  • Mostly multicellular (except yeasts, which are unicellular).
  • Heterotrophic by absorption — secrete digestive enzymes externally and absorb the digested nutrients (saprotrophic or parasitic nutrition).
  • Cell walls made of chitin (not cellulose — this is a key difference from plants).
  • Body composed of hyphae (thread-like filaments) forming a mycelium (network).
  • Store carbohydrates as glycogen (like animals!).
  • Reproduce by spores (sexually and asexually).
  • Examples: mushrooms, molds, yeasts, rusts, smuts.

Historical note — fungi used to be classified as plants: This will make you laugh: fungi were considered "non-photosynthetic plants" until the mid-20th century. Why? Because they grow in the ground and have cell walls. But chitin, glycogen storage, and heterotrophic nutrition by absorption place them closer to animals than to plants on the tree of life. More on this in the reclassification section.

4.4 Protista: The "Junk Drawer" Kingdom

Protista is not a natural evolutionary group — it is paraphyletic. This means it contains a common ancestor but not all of its descendants (because plants, animals, and fungi evolved from protist ancestors but are placed in separate kingdoms).

What protists are: All eukaryotes that are NOT plants, animals, or fungi. This creates an incredibly diverse grab-bag:

  • Protozoa (animal-like): Amoeba, Paramecium — unicellular heterotrophs, often motile.
  • Algae (plant-like): Chlamydomonas (unicellular green alga), kelp (multicellular brown alga, can grow 50+ meters!).
  • Slime molds (fungus-like): Cellular slime molds like Dictyostelium — fascinating social amoebae.

Why "paraphyletic" matters: Modern cladistics requires groups to be monophyletic — containing an ancestor and ALL its descendants. Protista fails this test. As molecular phylogenetics advances, Protista is being dismantled — its members are being redistributed into multiple new "supergroups" (Archaeplastida, SAR, Excavata, Amoebozoa, Opisthokonta). But for IB, Protista is still the textbook kingdom.

Paraphyletic group: A group that includes a common ancestor and some, but not all, of its descendants. Protista is paraphyletic because the protist ancestor also gave rise to plants, animals, and fungi, which are excluded from the group. Contrast with monophyletic (includes the ancestor and ALL descendants — a true evolutionary group, also called a clade).

IB Exam Tip: If asked "Why is Protista not a natural group?", answer: Protista is paraphyletic — it excludes the descendants (plants, animals, fungi) that evolved from protist ancestors. Natural taxonomic groups should be monophyletic (clades).

5. Cladistics: Classification by Common Ancestry

Cladistics is a method of classification based on evolutionary relationships. Instead of grouping organisms by overall similarity (which can be misleading), cladistics groups them by shared derived characteristics that indicate common ancestry.

5.1 Key Terminology

Term Definition
Clade A monophyletic group — an ancestor and ALL its descendants. A natural evolutionary unit.
Cladogram A branching diagram showing hypothesized evolutionary relationships based on shared characteristics.
Node A branching point on a cladogram, representing a common ancestor.
Root The base of the cladogram — the most ancient common ancestor of all organisms in the diagram.
Terminal taxon The organisms at the tips of the branches.
Sister groups Two taxa that share an immediate common ancestor not shared by any other group.
Outgroup A taxon known to have diverged before the rest of the group. Used to determine which characteristics are ancestral vs. derived.

5.2 Derived vs. Ancestral Characteristics

This distinction is the conceptual heart of cladistics:

  • Ancestral characteristic (plesiomorphy): A trait that was present in the common ancestor of the entire group being studied. Also called a primitive characteristic. For example, "having a backbone" is ancestral for mammals — the common ancestor of all vertebrates had a backbone, and mammals inherited it.
  • Derived characteristic (apomorphy): A trait that evolved in the lineage leading to a particular subgroup and was NOT present in the common ancestor of the larger group. For example, "having hair" is a derived characteristic of mammals — the vertebrate common ancestor did not have hair; it evolved later on the mammalian lineage.
  • Shared derived characteristic (synapomorphy): A derived characteristic shared by two or more taxa, indicating they inherited it from a recent common ancestor. Synapomorphies are the gold standard for defining clades. Mammals share hair, mammary glands, and three middle ear bones — these are mammalian synapomorphies.

Why the distinction matters: If you group organisms by ancestral characteristics, you'll create invalid groups. For instance, "things with backbones" includes fish, frogs, lizards, birds, and mammals — but this is not a useful group within vertebrates because the backbone is ancestral to all of them. It doesn't tell you which are more closely related to each other.

Derived characteristic (apomorphy): A novel trait that evolved in a lineage and was NOT present in the common ancestor of the broader group. Shared derived characteristics (synapomorphies) define monophyletic groups (clades). Example: Feathers are a derived characteristic of birds, not present in the common ancestor of all reptiles.

5.3 Reading a Cladogram

A cladogram shows hypothetical relationships. Let's walk through how to read one.

Imagine a cladogram with these taxa: Lancelet (outgroup), Tuna, Salamander, Lizard, Pigeon, Mouse, Chimp.

Branching pattern (left to right, root to tips):

```

Root ── Lancelet

└── Node A ── Tuna

└── Node B ── Salamander

└── Node C ── Lizard

└── Node D ── Pigeon

└── Node E ── Mouse

└── Node F ── Chimp

```

How to interpret this:

  1. Reading relationships: Tuna and Salamander share a more recent common ancestor (Node B) than Tuna does with Lancelet (root). Pigeon and Chimp share a more recent ancestor (Node F) than Pigeon does with Lizard (Node C).
  1. Sister groups: Mouse and Chimp are sister groups (they share Node F). Pigeon is the sister group to the clade containing Mouse + Chimp.
  1. Clades within clades: Everything from Node D onward (Pigeon, Mouse, Chimp) is a clade. Everything from Node C onward (Lizard, Pigeon, Mouse, Chimp) is also a clade. Clades are nested — this is the hallmark of evolution.
  1. The outgroup: Lancelet diverged before all the other taxa, so it helps "root" the tree and determine character polarity (which traits are ancestral).

Critical IB skill: Given a cladogram, be able to identify:

  • Which two taxa are most closely related? (Look for the pair sharing the most recent node.)
  • Which are most distantly related? (Look for taxa whose connecting path passes through the root.)
  • Is group X monophyletic, paraphyletic, or polyphyletic?

5.4 Constructing a Cladogram from Character Data

This is a core IB practical skill. The method:

Step 1: Choose your taxa and an outgroup.

Step 2: List observable characteristics (morphological or molecular).

Step 3: Score each taxon for each characteristic (present = 1, absent = 0).

Step 4: Identify shared derived characteristics (synapomorphies) by comparing to the outgroup.

Step 5: Group taxa that share derived characteristics.

Step 6: Build the cladogram, placing each branching event where a new derived characteristic appears.

Worked Example: Constructing a Simple Cladogram

Consider four organisms: Earthworm, Trout, Lizard, and Dog. The outgroup is a Sponge (no true tissues).

Character matrix:

Trait Sponge Earthworm Trout Lizard Dog
True tissues 0 1 1 1 1
Jaws 0 0 1 1 1
Lungs 0 0 0 1 1
Hair/Fur 0 0 0 0 1

Analysis:

  1. "True tissues" is present in all except Sponge → this is a derived trait for the clade Earthworm + Trout + Lizard + Dog.
  2. "Jaws" is present in Trout, Lizard, Dog → shared derived trait for this clade. Earthworm lacks it.
  3. "Lungs" is present in Lizard, Dog → shared derived trait.
  4. "Hair" is present only in Dog → derived trait unique to this lineage.

Cladogram structure:

```

Sponge (outgroup)

└── Earthworm (tissues)

└── Trout (jaws)

└── Lizard (lungs)

└── Dog (hair)

```

Each branch point represents the evolution of a new derived characteristic. The branching order reflects the sequence of evolutionary innovations.

6. Molecular Evidence in Classification

6.1 DNA Sequencing

Comparing DNA sequences has become the dominant method for determining evolutionary relationships. The logic: the more similar the DNA sequences between two organisms, the more recently they shared a common ancestor.

Types of DNA used:

  • Nuclear genes: Slowly evolving genes for deep relationships (e.g., rRNA genes), faster-evolving genes for recent divergences.
  • Mitochondrial DNA (mtDNA): Evolves faster than nuclear DNA, inherited only from the mother (in most species). Excellent for studying recent evolutionary events and population genetics. The cytochrome c oxidase and cytochrome b genes are commonly used.
  • Chloroplast DNA (cpDNA): Used for plant phylogenetics.

6.2 Amino Acid Sequences

Proteins are gene products. Comparing the amino acid sequences of the same protein across species reveals evolutionary relationships. Cytochrome c is the classic example — it's a highly conserved protein involved in the electron transport chain, found in virtually all organisms with mitochondria.

The number of amino acid differences in cytochrome c between species correlates with evolutionary divergence time:

Comparison Amino Acid Differences in Cytochrome c
Human vs. Chimpanzee 0 (identical!)
Human vs. Rhesus monkey 1
Human vs. Dog 11
Human vs. Tuna 21
Human vs. Baker's yeast 45

6.3 Molecular Clocks

The molecular clock hypothesis: mutations accumulate at a roughly constant rate over time for certain genes. If you know the mutation rate, you can use the number of differences between two sequences to estimate when they diverged.

  • Calibration: Must be calibrated using fossil evidence or known geological events (e.g., the formation of the Isthmus of Panama separating marine species).
  • Limitations: Different genes evolve at different rates; rates can vary between lineages; selection can accelerate or slow rates.
  • Utility: Despite limitations, molecular clocks provide testable hypotheses about divergence times, especially when fossil records are sparse.

6.4 How Molecules Reshaped the Tree

Molecular evidence has forced major reclassifications. Some dramatic examples:

  1. Fungi closer to animals than plants: rRNA and protein sequences consistently place fungi as the sister group to animals (together forming the Opisthokonta), not plants.
  1. Whales evolved from artiodactyls (even-toed ungulates): DNA evidence shows whales are nested within the artiodactyls, with hippopotamuses as their closest living relatives. The traditional order "Artiodactyla" (cows, pigs, deer) without whales was paraphyletic. Now they're grouped together as Cetartiodactyla.
  1. Birds are dinosaurs: Multiple molecular and fossil lines of evidence confirm birds are living theropod dinosaurs, most closely related to dromaeosaurids like Velociraptor. The traditional class "Reptilia" without birds is paraphyletic.

7. Famous Reclassifications

Science improves by revising its models. These reclassifications show how new evidence (especially molecular) reshapes taxonomy.

7.1 Fungi: From Plants to Their Own Kingdom

Old classification: Fungi were placed in Kingdom Plantae as "non-photosynthetic plants." They grew in soil, had cell walls, and didn't move — must be plants, right?

Why it was wrong:

  • Cell walls are chitin, not cellulose.
  • Energy storage is glycogen, not starch.
  • Nutrition is absorptive heterotrophy, not photosynthesis.
  • rRNA sequences place fungi closer to animals.

Reclassification: Fungi were moved to their own kingdom, and later phylogenetic analysis confirmed they are the sister group to animals.

7.2 Birds: Dinosaurs Still Walk (and Fly) Among Us

Old view: Birds and dinosaurs were separate groups. Birds were a distinct class (Aves) that appeared after dinosaurs went extinct.

Evidence for birds as dinosaurs:

  • Fossil record: Archaeopteryx (1861) had feathers and wings like a bird, but teeth, a bony tail, and claws like a dinosaur. More recent discoveries from China (e.g., Sinosauropteryx, Caudipteryx) revealed feathered non-avian dinosaurs — feathers evolved before flight.
  • Morphological synapomorphies: Birds share with theropod dinosaurs: hollow bones, three-fingered hands, a furcula (wishbone — fused clavicles), and a half-moon-shaped wrist bone (semilunate carpal).
  • Molecular evidence: Proteins extracted from Tyrannosaurus rex fossils (collagen) showed highest sequence similarity to birds.
  • Behavioral evidence: Some theropods slept with their heads tucked under their arms — just like modern birds.

Modern classification: Birds are placed within the clade Dinosauria (specifically Theropoda). There is no valid Linnaean rank "Class Aves" that excludes dinosaurs without being paraphyletic.

7.3 Giant Panda: Raccoon or Bear?

History: The giant panda (Ailuropoda melanoleuca) puzzled taxonomists for over a century. Its bamboo diet and modified wrist bone (acting as a "thumb") were so unusual that some placed it in its own family, some with raccoons, and some with bears.

Molecular resolution: In the 1980s, DNA hybridization and sequencing studies conclusively placed the giant panda within the bear family (Ursidae). Its closest living relative is the spectacled bear of South America. Case closed.

Lesser (red) panda: Interestingly, the red panda (Ailurus fulgens) — once considered the giant panda's relative — turned out to be more closely related to raccoons, skunks, and weasels (Musteloidea). The "panda thumb" (an enlarged radial sesamoid bone) evolved independently in both lineages — a beautiful example of convergent evolution.

8. Dichotomous Keys: Identification in the Field

A dichotomous key is a tool for identifying unknown organisms. It consists of a series of paired, mutually exclusive statements (couplets). At each step, you choose the statement that matches your organism, which leads you to the next couplet until you reach an identification.

8.1 Rules for Constructing a Dichotomous Key

  1. Couplets must be dichotomous — exactly two contrasting statements, with only one matching your specimen.
  2. Use observable, objective characteristics — not subjective ones like "pretty" or "big." Use measurable traits: presence/absence of structures, counts, shapes, colors.
  3. Start with the most general characteristics and move to more specific ones.
  4. Avoid overlapping characteristics — "leaves 5–10 cm long" and "leaves 8–15 cm long" overlap. Use "leaves less than 10 cm" vs. "leaves greater than 10 cm."
  5. Write in clear, comparative language — each couplet should present a clear choice: "Flowers red" vs. "Flowers not red" or "Petals fused at base" vs. "Petals free to base."
  6. Number each couplet and show where each choice leads.

8.2 Example: Simple Insect Key

1a. Has wings .............................................................. Go to 2

1b. No wings .............................................................. Go to 3

2a. One pair of wings (hindwings reduced to halteres) ............ Housefly (Musca domestica)

2b. Two pairs of wings .................................................. Go to 4

3a. Has three long tail filaments (cerci) ............................ Silverfish (Lepisma saccharina)

3b. Body covered in scales; proboscis coiled ....................... Butterfly (Order Lepidoptera — check, because most butterflies do have wings! This shows why keys must be carefully checked.)

4a. Forewings hardened into elytra (sheath-like covers) ............ Beetle (Order Coleoptera)

4b. All four wings membranous ........................................ Go to 5

5a. Wings held flat at rest; long, slender abdomen .................. Dragonfly (Order Odonata)

5b. Wings held roof-like over body at rest ............................ Lacewing (Order Neuroptera)

8.3 Using a Dichotomous Key: Systematic Approach

  1. Always start at couplet 1.
  2. Read BOTH statements before choosing.
  3. If unsure, try both paths — one will lead to a dead end.
  4. Verify your identification against a full description if available.
  5. Record the path (the sequence of numbers) for future reference.

IB Exam Tip: Dichotomous key questions appear in both Paper 1 (multiple choice: "At couplet 3, which path would you follow for organism X?") and Paper 2 (constructing a key from a set of organisms). When constructing a key, use visible external features, start broad, and ensure your couplets are truly dichotomous. Never use "flower color: red" vs. "flower color: blue" when there might be a white-flowered species not in your set — use "flower color: red" vs. "flower color: not red."

9. Worked Examples

Worked Example 1: Interpreting a Cladogram

Given this cladogram:

```

A B C D E

\ / / \ /

\ / / \ /

Node2 / Node4

\ / /

Node1 /

\ /

\ /

Root

```

Taxa: A = Shark, B = Trout, C = Frog, D = Crocodile, E = Pigeon

Questions:

a) Which pair are most closely related?

b) Is {B, C, D} a monophyletic group?

c) Where would you place "amniotic egg" as a derived characteristic?

Solutions:

a) D and E (Crocodile and Pigeon) are most closely related — they share Node4, which is the most recent common ancestor for any pair shown. Note: this reflects the fact that crocodilians are the closest living relatives of birds!

b) Look at the taxa {B, C, D}. They share Node1 as their common ancestor. But Node1 is also the ancestor of A and E (through Node2 and Node4). So Node1's descendants include A, B, C, D, AND E. The group {B, C, D} does NOT include all descendants of Node1 (it excludes A and E), therefore it is NOT monophyletic — it's paraphyletic.

c) The amniotic egg is shared by crocodilians (D) and birds (E) but not by fish (A, B) or amphibians (C). It should be placed on the branch between Node1 and Node4, or precisely at Node4, indicating it's a shared derived characteristic of the D+E clade.

Worked Example 2: Three-Domain vs. Five-Kingdom

Question: Explain why the three-domain system is considered a more accurate representation of evolutionary history than the five-kingdom system. (4 marks)

Model answer:

  1. The five-kingdom system grouped all prokaryotes into Kingdom Monera based on superficial similarity (lack of nucleus, small size) (1 mark).
  1. Molecular evidence (16S/18S rRNA sequencing by Carl Woese) revealed that prokaryotes comprise two fundamentally distinct lineages — Bacteria and Archaea — with Archaea being as genetically distinct from Bacteria as both are from Eukarya (1 mark).
  1. Archaea have unique characteristics: ether-linked membrane lipids, absence of peptidoglycan, and histone-associated DNA, distinguishing them from Bacteria (1 mark).
  1. The three-domain system (Archaea, Bacteria, Eukarya) reflects monophyletic groups based on common ancestry, while Kingdom Monera was polyphyletic/paraphyletic and thus an unnatural group in evolutionary terms (1 mark).

10. Common Misconceptions

Misconception Reality
"Cladograms and phylogenetic trees are the same thing" Cladograms only show branching order, not time or amount of change. Phylogenetic trees often include branch lengths proportional to time or genetic change.
"An organism at the end of a long branch is 'more evolved'" Every living organism is the product of the same amount of evolutionary time. Some have changed more, some less, but none is "more evolved."
"Humans evolved from chimpanzees" No — humans and chimpanzees share a recent common ancestor (about 6–8 million years ago). Both lineages have been evolving independently since then.
"'Primitive' organisms are simpler and worse-adapted" "Primitive" (ancestral) means the trait appeared earlier in evolutionary history. It doesn't mean inferior. Sharks have been around for 400+ million years — they're superbly adapted.
"Archaea are all extremophiles" While many archaea are extremophiles, they're also abundant in moderate environments including soils, oceans, and the human microbiome.
"The binomial name tells you the domain" Binomial names predate the domain system. Domains are a much higher rank and are not part of the binomial.

Problem 1 (Paper 1 style): Which of the following represents the correct hierarchical order, from most inclusive to least inclusive?

A. Kingdom → Phylum → Class → Order → Family → Genus → Species

B. Domain → Kingdom → Phylum → Class → Order → Family → Genus → Species

C. Domain → Phylum → Kingdom → Class → Order → Family → Genus → Species

D. Kingdom → Domain → Phylum → Order → Class → Family → Genus → Species

Problem 2 (Paper 2 style, 4 marks): Distinguish between ancestral and derived characteristics, and explain why classification should be based on derived characteristics.

Click for answer

Model answer:

  • Ancestral characteristic (plesiomorphy): A trait present in the common ancestor of a group, inherited by all members (1 mark). Example: backbone in vertebrates.
  • Derived characteristic (apomorphy): A trait that evolved within a lineage and was NOT present in the common ancestor (1 mark). Example: feathers in birds.
  • Classification should be based on shared derived characteristics (synapomorphies) because they indicate recent common ancestry and define monophyletic groups/clades (1 mark).
  • Using ancestral characteristics would group organisms by traits that evolved much earlier and would create unnatural (paraphyletic or polyphyletic) groups rather than true evolutionary units (1 mark).

Problem 3 (Paper 2 style, 5 marks): Describe the evidence used by Carl Woese to propose the three-domain system.

Click for answer

Model answer:

  • Woese compared the nucleotide sequences of 16S ribosomal RNA (in prokaryotes) and 18S rRNA (in eukaryotes) across diverse organisms (1 mark).
  • rRNA was chosen because it is universal (present in all organisms), functionally constrained (some regions evolve very slowly), and contains both conserved and variable regions (1 mark).
  • His analysis revealed that organisms traditionally classified as "bacteria" fell into two fundamentally distinct groups — Bacteria and Archaea (1 mark).
  • The genetic distance between these two groups was as great as the distance between each group and Eukarya, justifying three domains rather than two (1 mark).
  • Subsequent research confirmed that Archaea have unique molecular features: ether-linked membrane lipids, lack of peptidoglycan in cell walls, and histone-associated DNA more similar to eukaryotes than bacteria (1 mark).

Summary

  • Life is classified in eight hierarchical ranks: Domain → Kingdom → Phylum → Class → Order → Family → Genus → Species.
  • Three domains exist: Archaea (ether-linked lipids, no peptidoglycan), Bacteria (peptidoglycan, ester-linked lipids), and Eukarya (membrane-bound organelles). This was discovered by Carl Woese using rRNA sequencing.
  • Within Eukarya: Plantae (cellulose, autotrophs), Animalia (no cell walls, heterotrophs), Fungi (chitin, absorptive heterotrophs), Protista (paraphyletic grab-bag).
  • Cladistics classifies by common ancestry using shared derived characteristics (synapomorphies). A clade is a monophyletic group.
  • Molecular evidence (DNA, rRNA, amino acid sequences) provides powerful, quantitative data for phylogenetics and has driven major reclassifications.
  • Dichotomous keys use paired contrasting statements to identify organisms.
