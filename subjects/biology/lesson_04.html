4.1 The Big Question: How Did Life Begin?

This is one of the most profound questions in all of science. How did non-living matter — rocks, gases, water — give rise to the first living cells approximately 3.5–4 billion years ago?

The IB expects you to understand the scientific evidence for key steps in this process, not to memorize a single "correct" story (there isn't one yet). Think like a scientist: what conditions existed on early Earth? What chemical processes could have occurred under those conditions? What evidence supports each hypothesis?

4.2 Conditions of Early Earth (~4.5 Billion Years Ago)

A Very Different Planet

The Earth formed about 4.5 billion years ago (bya). For the first several hundred million years, it was a molten, inhospitable ball of rock, constantly bombarded by asteroids. As it cooled, a solid crust formed, and water vapor in the atmosphere condensed to form the first oceans around 4.0–4.2 bya.

The early Earth was nothing like the blue-green planet we see today:

The Reducing Atmosphere

The early atmosphere had no free oxygen (O₂). Free O₂ only appeared about 2.4 bya with the "Great Oxidation Event," caused by the evolution of oxygenic photosynthesis in cyanobacteria.

Instead, the atmosphere was reducing — meaning it contained gases that tend to donate electrons. The main gases were:

Gas Formula Source
Methane CH₄ Volcanic outgassing
Ammonia NH₃ Volcanic outgassing
Water vapor H₂O Evaporation from early oceans
Hydrogen H₂ Volcanic activity, reactions in atmosphere
Carbon dioxide CO₂ Volcanic outgassing (likely present, debated amount)

Crucially: No oxygen means no ozone (O₃) layer. Without ozone, the Earth's surface was exposed to intense ultraviolet (UV) radiation from the sun. This would be lethal to most modern organisms, but it may have actually helped drive the chemical reactions that produced the first organic molecules.

Other Conditions

  • High temperatures: The Earth was still cooling; surface temperatures were much higher than today. Deep-sea hydrothermal vents may have provided stable, warm environments.
  • Frequent lightning storms: Intense electrical activity in the atmosphere.
  • Volcanic activity: Extensive, releasing gases and minerals.
  • Meteorite bombardment: Delivering organic molecules and water from space (a source of prebiotic compounds).

4.3 The Miller-Urey Experiment (1953)

The Question

Could the organic molecules of life (amino acids, sugars, nucleotides) have arisen spontaneously from inorganic precursors on early Earth? This was the "prebiotic soup" hypothesis.

The Experiment

Stanley Miller (a graduate student) and Harold Urey (his supervisor) designed an apparatus to simulate early Earth conditions:

The Apparatus:

  1. A flask containing water — representing the early ocean. This was heated to produce water vapor.
  2. A second flask containing a mixture of gases: CH₄, NH₃, H₂, and H₂O (the "reducing atmosphere").
  3. Electrodes in the gas flask produced continuous electrical sparks — simulating lightning.
  4. A condenser cooled the gases, allowing liquid to drip back into the "ocean" flask, creating a continuous cycle.

Draw this: Draw three connected glass pieces:

  • Bottom left: a round flask with water, sitting on a flame. Label "Boiling water ('ocean')."
  • Above: a larger flask with two electrodes inside (like spark plug wires with a zigzag spark between them). Label "CH₄, NH₃, H₂, H₂O vapor" and "Electrodes (simulate lightning)."
  • Right side: a tube going downward with a cooling jacket. Label "Condenser."
  • The tube feeds back into the bottom flask. Label "Continuous circulation."

The Results

After running the apparatus for about a week, Miller analyzed the contents of the water flask. He found:

  • Amino acids — including glycine, alanine, aspartic acid, and others (over 20 amino acids in later, more refined runs).
  • Other organic compounds: simple sugars, lipids, and precursors to nucleotides.

The significance: This demonstrated that under conditions thought to exist on early Earth, inorganic molecules (CH₄, NH₃, H₂, H₂O) can react to form organic molecules — the building blocks of life. This is called abiotic synthesis (abiotic = without life).

Abiotic synthesis: The formation of organic molecules from inorganic precursors through non-biological chemical reactions. The Miller-Urey experiment provided the first experimental evidence that abiotic synthesis of amino acids is possible under early Earth conditions.

Limitations of the Miller-Urey Experiment

The IB expects you to critically evaluate this experiment. No single experiment perfectly captures early Earth:

  1. Atmospheric composition debated: The reducing atmosphere (rich in CH₄, NH₃, H₂) may not have been the actual composition. Some geochemists argue the early atmosphere was more neutral (CO₂, N₂, H₂O). Later experiments with neutral gas mixtures produce fewer amino acids, but still some.
  1. Continuous spark vs. real lightning: The experiment used continuous electrical discharge. Real lightning is intermittent and localized.
  1. No competing reactions: The closed apparatus didn't account for side reactions, degradation by UV, or dilution in vast oceans.
  1. Missing steps: Producing amino acids is not the same as producing life. Polymerization into proteins, encapsulation in membranes, and emergence of self-replication are additional steps not addressed.

Nonetheless: The Miller-Urey experiment was groundbreaking. It showed that under plausible early Earth conditions, the building blocks of life can form spontaneously. Follow-up experiments using different gas mixtures, UV radiation, or hydrothermal vent conditions have all produced organic molecules.

IB Exam Tip: Questions often ask: "Describe the Miller-Urey experiment and discuss its significance and limitations." Structure:

  • Apparatus description (2 marks)
  • Results (1 mark)
  • Significance — shows abiotic synthesis is possible (1 mark)
  • Limitations — atmosphere may have differed, only amino acids, not life itself (2 marks)

4.4 Self-Assembly: From Molecules to Protocells

Phospholipids and Spontaneous Bilayer Formation

One of the most elegant demonstrations in biology: if you put phospholipids (the molecules that make up cell membranes) into water, they spontaneously assemble into bilayers. No enzymes, no energy input, no genetic instructions needed. It's purely a physical-chemical process driven by the hydrophobic effect.

Why this happens: Phospholipids are amphipathic — they have a hydrophilic (water-loving) phosphate "head" and two hydrophobic (water-fearing) fatty acid "tails." When placed in water:

  • The hydrophobic tails are repelled by water and cluster together to avoid it.
  • The hydrophilic heads interact favorably with water.
  • The energetically most favorable arrangement is a bilayer: heads face outward (toward water on both sides), tails face inward (shielded from water).
  • This bilayer spontaneously closes into a sphere called a liposome to eliminate exposed hydrophobic edges.

Protocells and Microspheres

When phospholipids or other amphipathic molecules self-assemble into vesicles, they form hollow, spherical compartments filled with water. If organic molecules (amino acids, nucleotides) happen to be trapped inside during formation, the vesicle becomes a protocell — a primitive cell-like structure.

Key properties of protocells:

  • They form a boundary between inside and outside — a crucial step toward life.
  • They can grow by incorporating more phospholipids.
  • They can divide (pinch in two) when they reach a certain size.
  • They maintain an internal chemical environment different from the outside.

Sidney Fox's microspheres: In the 1960s, Sidney Fox showed that when mixtures of amino acids are heated, they polymerize into "proteinoids," which spontaneously form microscopic spheres (microspheres) in water. These microspheres can even exhibit some enzyme-like activity and show budding behavior resembling division. While they are not alive, they show that membrane-like compartments form easily under plausible prebiotic conditions.

Protocell: A self-organized, spherical, membrane-bound compartment containing organic molecules, capable of growth and division. Protocells are considered a key intermediate step between non-living chemistry and the first true cells.

4.5 The RNA World Hypothesis

The Chicken-and-Egg Problem

Life as we know it has a fundamental circular dependency:

  • DNA stores genetic information, but DNA needs proteins (enzymes) to replicate.
  • Proteins do the catalytic work (enzymes, structure), but proteins are made using instructions from DNA.

Which came first? This is a chicken-and-egg problem. If you need DNA to make proteins and proteins to replicate DNA, how did the system start?

RNA: The Dual-Function Molecule

The RNA World Hypothesis proposes that RNA was the first self-replicating genetic molecule — before DNA and before proteins. Why RNA?

  1. RNA can store genetic information — like DNA, RNA is a polynucleotide with a sequence of bases that can encode information. Some modern viruses (e.g., influenza, HIV, SARS-CoV-2) use RNA as their genome, proving RNA can function as genetic material.
  1. RNA can catalyze chemical reactions — certain RNA molecules, called ribozymes, have enzymatic activity. They can:
  • Cleave and ligate RNA strands (self-splicing introns)
  • Form peptide bonds (the peptidyl transferase activity of rRNA in the ribosome)
  • Polymerize RNA from nucleotide precursors (experimentally demonstrated ribozymes)

The key insight: RNA is the only known molecule that can do both jobs — information storage AND catalysis. It could have served as both "genome" and "enzyme" in primitive cells.

Evidence for the RNA World

  1. Ribozymes exist: Naturally occurring catalytic RNAs have been discovered, including self-splicing introns (discovered by Thomas Cech and Sidney Altman, Nobel Prize 1989) and RNase P.
  1. The ribosome is a ribozyme: The catalytic core of the ribosome — the peptidyl transferase center that forms peptide bonds — is composed entirely of rRNA. The ribosomal proteins provide structural support but are not catalytic. This suggests that the first "protein factory" was made of RNA.
  1. RNA monomers can form under prebiotic conditions: Ribonucleotides (the building blocks of RNA) can be synthesized from simpler precursors (cyanamide, glycolaldehyde, etc.) under plausible early Earth conditions, though this remains an active area of research.
  1. RNA can evolve: In laboratory experiments (in vitro evolution or SELEX), RNA molecules can be selected for specific catalytic functions, demonstrating that RNA can undergo Darwinian evolution.

Why DNA and Proteins Eventually Took Over

If RNA came first, why do modern cells use DNA for storage and proteins for catalysis?

  • DNA is more stable: The removal of the 2′ –OH (deoxyribose vs. ribose) makes DNA far more resistant to hydrolysis. This makes it a better long-term archive.
  • Proteins are more versatile catalysts: With 20 amino acids (vs. 4 RNA bases), proteins have far greater chemical diversity, enabling more efficient and specialized catalysis.
  • Division of labor: DNA specializes in information storage; proteins specialize in catalysis; RNA serves as the intermediary (mRNA) and retains some catalytic roles (rRNA, ribozymes).

RNA World Hypothesis: The hypothesis that RNA was the original genetic material, capable of both storing genetic information and catalyzing chemical reactions (as ribozymes), predating DNA and proteins. DNA later took over information storage, and proteins took over most catalytic functions.

Ribozyme: An RNA molecule with catalytic activity — an RNA enzyme.

4.6 LUCA: The Last Universal Common Ancestor

What Is LUCA?

All life on Earth — bacteria, archaea, plants, fungi, animals — shares fundamental biochemical features. The most parsimonious explanation is that all life descended from a single ancestral population. This population is called LUCA — the Last Universal Common Ancestor.

Important: LUCA was not necessarily the first life form. There may have been many earlier, simpler life forms. LUCA is simply the most recent organism that is ancestral to all living organisms — the root of the tree of life.

Evidence for LUCA

The evidence comes from the universal features shared by all domains of life:

  1. Universal genetic code: All organisms use the same triplet codons to specify the same amino acids (with very minor variations). The genetic code is nearly universal. For example, the codon AUG codes for methionine in bacteria, fungi, plants, and animals.
  1. DNA as genetic material: All cellular life uses DNA to store genetic information.
  1. Same molecular machinery for gene expression:
  • DNA → RNA via transcription (RNA polymerase)
  • RNA → protein via translation (ribosomes, tRNA, mRNA)
  • The ribosome itself — rRNA and ribosomal proteins — is highly conserved across all domains.
  1. ATP as energy currency: All cells use ATP as the primary energy carrier.
  1. Same set of 20 amino acids: All life uses the same 20 amino acids (with rare exceptions like selenocysteine).
  1. Conserved metabolic pathways: Glycolysis, the citric acid cycle, and other core metabolic pathways are fundamentally similar in all organisms, suggesting they evolved before the divergence of the three domains.
  1. Common chirality: All life uses L-amino acids and D-sugars exclusively — a purely arbitrary choice that is conserved across all organisms.

What Was LUCA Like?

Based on comparative genomics, LUCA was likely:

  • A single-celled organism
  • With a lipid membrane
  • Using DNA as genetic material
  • With ribosomes for protein synthesis
  • Capable of basic metabolism (likely anaerobic, as there was little free O₂)
  • Possibly a thermophile (heat-loving), given the position of thermophilic organisms near the root of phylogenetic trees

LUCA (Last Universal Common Ancestor): The most recent population of organisms from which all organisms now living on Earth descend. LUCA is inferred from the universal features of biochemistry shared across all domains of life (Bacteria, Archaea, Eukarya).

4.7 The Endosymbiotic Theory

The Puzzle of Eukaryotic Organelles

Eukaryotic cells have two organelles — mitochondria and chloroplasts — that stand out as unusual:

  • They have their own DNA — separate from the nuclear genome.
  • Their DNA is circular, like bacterial DNA, not linear like nuclear chromosomes.
  • They are surrounded by double membranes.
  • They have their own ribosomes, which are 70S (bacterial-type), not 80S (eukaryotic-type).
  • They divide by binary fission, like bacteria, not by mitosis.
  • Their inner membranes contain proteins and lipids that are more similar to bacterial membranes than eukaryotic ones.

All of these observations point to a startling conclusion: mitochondria and chloroplasts were once free-living bacteria.

Lynn Margulis and the Endosymbiotic Theory (1967)

Lynn Margulis proposed that eukaryotic cells originated through endosymbiosis — one organism living inside another. Specifically:

#### Origin of Mitochondria

  1. An ancestral anaerobic eukaryotic cell (or a proto-eukaryote — possibly an archaeon) engulfed an aerobic proteobacterium.
  2. Instead of digesting it, the host kept the bacterium as an endosymbiont.
  3. The host provided protection and nutrients; the bacterium provided efficient ATP production via aerobic respiration.
  4. Over evolutionary time, the endosymbiont lost genes it no longer needed (many transferred to the host nucleus) and became an organelle — the mitochondrion.

#### Origin of Chloroplasts

  1. A eukaryotic cell that already had mitochondria engulfed a photosynthetic cyanobacterium.
  2. The cyanobacterium became an endosymbiont, providing the host with photosynthetic capability (glucose from CO₂ and H₂O).
  3. Over time, the cyanobacterium became the chloroplast.

This is called serial endosymbiosis — mitochondria came first, then chloroplasts in the lineage leading to plants.

Draw this: Draw a sequence of three cells:

  1. Left: A large irregular cell labeled "Anaerobic ancestral eukaryote" with an arrow pointing toward a small rod-shaped bacterium labeled "Aerobic proteobacterium." Above the arrow: "Engulfment."
  1. Middle: The large cell now contains the bacterium inside a membrane vesicle. Label: "Endosymbiont inside host."
  1. Right: The large cell now has the endosymbiont fully integrated. Label: "Mitochondrion — integrated organelle with double membrane."

Below, show a fourth cell (with a mitochondrion) engulfing a cyanobacterium, leading to a cell with both mitochondria and chloroplasts.

Evidence for Endosymbiosis

The evidence is overwhelming and comes from multiple independent lines:

Evidence Mitochondria Chloroplasts
Double membrane Outer from host, inner from bacterial plasma membrane Outer from host, inner from cyanobacterial plasma membrane
Own DNA Circular, ~16,000 bp in humans Circular, similar to cyanobacterial DNA
Ribosomes 70S (bacterial-type) 70S (bacterial-type)
Antibiotic sensitivity Sensitive to chloramphenicol (inhibits bacterial ribosomes) Sensitive to chloramphenicol
Division Binary fission Binary fission
Genome similarity Mitochondrial genes most similar to α-proteobacteria Chloroplast genes most similar to cyanobacteria
Membrane lipids Inner membrane has bacterial-type lipids Inner membrane has bacterial-type lipids
Electron transport chain Similar to aerobic bacteria Similar to cyanobacteria

Gene Transfer from Organelle to Nucleus

Over evolutionary time, many genes from the endosymbiont's original genome were transferred to the host nucleus. This is why mitochondrial DNA today encodes only a small fraction of mitochondrial proteins (~13 in humans); the rest (~1,000+) are encoded by nuclear genes, translated in the cytoplasm, and imported into the mitochondrion.

This gene transfer provides additional evidence: many nuclear genes for mitochondrial function show clear bacterial ancestry when their sequences are analyzed.

Endosymbiotic Theory: The theory that mitochondria and chloroplasts originated as free-living bacteria (aerobic proteobacterium and cyanobacterium, respectively) that were engulfed by ancestral eukaryotic cells and evolved into permanent, integrated organelles.

4.9 Practice Problems

Problem 1 (Paper 1 — Multiple Choice)

Which gas was absent from the early Earth's atmosphere, making it a reducing atmosphere?

A. Methane (CH₄)

B. Ammonia (NH₃)

C. Oxygen (O₂)

D. Hydrogen (H₂)

Answer

C. The early atmosphere lacked free O₂, making it reducing. Oxygen accumulated only after the evolution of oxygenic photosynthesis.

Problem 2 (Paper 1 — Multiple Choice)

What property of RNA makes it central to the RNA World Hypothesis?

A. RNA is more stable than DNA.

B. RNA can both store genetic information and catalyze chemical reactions.

C. RNA is the only nucleic acid found in viruses.

D. RNA contains thymine instead of uracil.

Answer

B. RNA's dual function (information storage + catalysis as a ribozyme) makes it a plausible first genetic molecule. A is wrong (DNA is more stable), C is wrong (some viruses use DNA), D is wrong (RNA has uracil).

Problem 3 (Paper 2 — 4 marks)

Outline the evidence that all life on Earth shares a common ancestor (LUCA).

Model Answer

  1. All organisms use the same universal genetic code (same triplet codons specify the same amino acids). ✓
  2. All organisms use DNA as genetic material and the same molecular machinery (transcription, translation, ribosomes). ✓
  3. All organisms use ATP as the universal energy currency. ✓
  4. Core metabolic pathways (e.g., glycolysis) are conserved across all domains, suggesting they evolved before divergence from LUCA. ✓

Problem 4 (Paper 2 — 6 marks)

Discuss the evidence for the endosymbiotic origin of chloroplasts.

Model Answer

  1. Chloroplasts have a double membrane — the outer membrane is derived from the host's phagocytic vesicle, and the inner membrane is derived from the cyanobacterial plasma membrane. ✓
  2. Chloroplasts contain their own circular DNA, which is structurally similar to bacterial DNA and separate from nuclear DNA. ✓
  3. Chloroplasts possess 70S ribosomes, which are characteristic of bacteria (not the 80S ribosomes of eukaryotic cytoplasm). ✓
  4. Chloroplasts divide by binary fission, like bacteria. ✓
  5. DNA sequence analysis shows that chloroplast genes are most closely related to modern cyanobacteria. ✓
  6. Chloroplasts are sensitive to antibiotics (e.g., chloramphenicol) that target bacterial ribosomes, indicating their bacterial ancestry. ✓

4.10 Common Misconceptions

Misconception The Truth
"The Miller-Urey experiment created life in a test tube." It produced amino acids, not life. Amino acids are building blocks; assembling them into self-replicating systems is a separate and unsolved problem.
"LUCA was the first life form." LUCA was the most recent common ancestor of all living organisms. There were almost certainly earlier, simpler life forms that left no descendants.
"Endosymbiosis means one cell ate another." It was likely a failed predation or a parasitic/symbiotic relationship — the engulfed bacterium was not digested and instead became a mutualistic partner.
"Chloroplasts evolved before mitochondria." Mitochondria came first. All eukaryotes have (or had) mitochondria; only plants and algae have chloroplasts.
"RNA world means RNA was the first molecule of life." There may have been simpler, pre-RNA genetic systems (e.g., TNA, PNA). RNA world is the hypothesis that RNA was the first self-replicating genetic polymer.
