1. The Greenhouse Effect: Natural vs Enhanced

The Natural Greenhouse Effect

The greenhouse effect is the process by which certain gases in Earth's atmosphere trap heat, warming the planet. Without it, Earth's average surface temperature would be approximately −18°C instead of the current +15°C — a difference of ~33°C that makes life as we know it possible.

> Analogy: Think of the Earth as a car parked in the sun on a cold but sunny day. Sunlight (shortwave radiation) passes through the windows and is absorbed by the seats and dashboard, warming them. The warm interior radiates heat (longwave infrared), but the glass windows trap much of this heat inside — the car gets warmer than the outside air. Earth's greenhouse gases act like the car windows, trapping heat that would otherwise escape to space.

The Mechanism

> Draw and label: A diagram of Earth's energy budget:

> 1. Incoming solar (shortwave) radiation (~340 W/m² at top of atmosphere)

> 2. ~30% reflected by clouds, atmosphere, and surface (albedo)

> 3. ~70% absorbed by Earth's surface and atmosphere

> 4. Earth radiates longwave (infrared) radiation upward

> 5. Greenhouse gases (CO₂, H₂O, CH₄, N₂O, etc.) absorb some of this outgoing longwave radiation

> 6. GHGs re-radiate energy in all directions — some back toward Earth's surface

> 7. This "back-radiation" warms the surface beyond what direct solar heating alone would achieve

> 8. Label: "The natural greenhouse effect makes Earth habitable; the enhanced greenhouse effect causes global warming."

The Enhanced (Anthropogenic) Greenhouse Effect

Human activities have dramatically increased concentrations of greenhouse gases, especially since the Industrial Revolution (~1750). This enhances the natural greenhouse effect → more longwave radiation trapped → Earth warms.

Gas Pre-industrial Concentration Current Concentration (approx.) Increase
CO₂ ~280 ppm >420 ppm >50% increase
CH₄ ~700 ppb >1,900 ppb >170% increase
N₂O ~270 ppb >330 ppb >20% increase

2. The Major Greenhouse Gases

Carbon Dioxide (CO₂)

Property Detail
Main sources Fossil fuel combustion (coal, oil, natural gas — ~90%), deforestation and land-use change (~10%), cement production (calcination of limestone: CaCO₃ → CaO + CO₂)
Atmospheric lifetime Complex — not a single lifetime. ~50% removed within 30 years, ~30% remains for centuries, ~20% persists for thousands of years
Radiative forcing Largest contributor to anthropogenic warming (~66% of total)
Current emissions ~37 billion tonnes CO₂/year (2023) from fossil fuels + ~5-7 billion tonnes from deforestation

> Why CO₂ matters most: Despite being less potent molecule-for-molecule than some other GHGs, the sheer volume of CO₂ emissions and its long atmospheric lifetime make it the primary driver of climate change.

Methane (CH₄)

Property Detail
Main sources Agriculture (enteric fermentation in ruminants — cows, sheep; rice paddies; manure), fossil fuel extraction and distribution (natural gas leaks — "fugitive emissions"), landfills (anaerobic decomposition of organic waste), wetlands
Global warming potential (GWP) ~25–28× more potent than CO₂** per molecule over 100 years; ~84× over 20 years
Atmospheric lifetime ~12 years (much shorter than CO₂)
Oxidation pathway CH₄ + 2O₂ → CO₂ + 2H₂O (methane eventually oxidises to CO₂, which then persists)

> Key insight: Methane's short lifetime means that reducing methane emissions produces relatively rapid climate benefits. This makes methane reduction a powerful short-term lever for slowing warming.

Nitrous Oxide (N₂O)

Property Detail
Main sources Agricultural fertilisers (nitrification and denitrification of nitrogen fertilisers), industrial processes (nylon production, nitric acid), biomass burning, manure management
GWP ~265–298× more potent than CO₂** over 100 years
Atmospheric lifetime ~121 years
Additional effect Destroys stratospheric ozone (N₂O is now the largest ozone-depleting substance emitted by human activities)

Water Vapour (H₂O)

Property Detail
Role Most abundant greenhouse gas — responsible for ~50% of the natural greenhouse effect
Why it's not a driver Water vapour concentration is controlled by temperature (Clausius-Clapeyron relation — warmer air holds more water vapour). It acts as a feedback, not a forcing: warming → more water vapour → more greenhouse effect → more warming (positive feedback, roughly doubling the warming from CO₂ alone)
Atmospheric lifetime ~9 days (short — precipitates out as rain/snow)

CFCs (Chlorofluorocarbons)

Property Detail
Sources Refrigerants, aerosol propellants, foam-blowing agents (now largely phased out under the Montreal Protocol, 1987)
GWP Very high (CFC-12 = ~10,900× CO₂)
Atmospheric lifetime 50–100+ years
Additional effect Deplete stratospheric ozone (the "ozone hole")
Success story Montreal Protocol is widely regarded as the most successful international environmental agreement. CFC concentrations are declining, and the ozone layer is slowly recovering (expected to return to 1980 levels by ~2066 over Antarctica)

3. Evidence for Climate Change

1. Direct Temperature Records

  • Systematic instrumental temperature records began ~1850.
  • Global average temperature has risen by ~1.1°C since the pre-industrial period (1850–1900).
  • The rate of warming has accelerated: most warming has occurred since the 1970s.
  • The 10 warmest years on record have all occurred since 2010 (with 2023/2024 being the warmest).
  • Warming is not uniform: land warms faster than ocean; the Arctic warms ~3–4× faster than the global average (Arctic amplification).

2. Ice Core Data

Ice cores drilled from Antarctica (Vostok, EPICA Dome C) and Greenland provide a record of past atmospheric composition going back up to 800,000 years:

  • Snow accumulates in layers → compresses into ice → traps air bubbles (samples of ancient atmosphere)
  • Analysis of trapped air reveals past CO₂ and CH₄ concentrations
  • Analysis of oxygen isotopes (δ¹⁸O) in the ice itself reveals past temperatures

Key findings:

  • For 800,000 years, CO₂ oscillated between ~180 ppm (glacial periods) and ~280 ppm (interglacial periods).
  • CO₂ has never exceeded 300 ppm during this entire period — until the 20th century.
  • Current CO₂ (>420 ppm) is higher than at any point in at least 800,000 years — likely higher than any point in the last 3–5 million years.
  • The rate of CO₂ increase is unprecedented — ~100× faster than the rise at the end of the last ice age.

3. Sea Level Rise

Component Contribution (~1900–present)
Thermal expansion ~40% — water expands as it warms
Melting glaciers and ice caps ~35% — mountain glaciers worldwide retreating
Greenland ice sheet ~15% — accelerating mass loss
Antarctic ice sheet ~10% — net mass loss, especially West Antarctica

Total sea level rise since 1900: ~20 cm. Current rate: ~3.7 mm/year (and accelerating). Projections for 2100: 0.3–1.1 m (depending on emissions scenario and ice sheet dynamics).

4. Glacier Retreat

Glaciers worldwide are retreating at rates unprecedented in the historical record:

  • Alps: Lost ~50% of ice volume since 1850
  • Himalayas: Glaciers that feed the Ganges, Indus, and Brahmaputra rivers are retreating — threatening water supply for over 1 billion people
  • Greenland: Losing ~270 billion tonnes of ice per year
  • Antarctica: Losing ~150 billion tonnes per year (tripled since 2012)

5. Phenological Changes

Phenology = the timing of biological events. Climate change is causing:

Phenomenon Observation
Earlier spring Many plants are flowering 2–5 days earlier per decade
Earlier bird migration Migratory birds arriving earlier at breeding grounds
Earlier insect emergence Butterflies emerging earlier in spring
Mismatches Different species respond at different rates → ecological mismatches: e.g., birds arrive at breeding grounds but the caterpillar peak (food for chicks) has already passed

6. Species Range Shifts

Species are moving in response to climate change:

  • Poleward shifts: Average rate of ~17 km/decade toward the poles
  • Upward shifts: Average rate of ~11 m/decade in elevation
  • Marine shifts: Fish species shifting northward in the Northern Hemisphere; tropical species expanding ranges

Species that cannot shift (mountaintop species, island endemics, species with limited dispersal) face higher extinction risk.

7. The Keeling Curve

Charles David Keeling began continuous CO₂ measurements at the Mauna Loa Observatory (Hawaii) in 1958 — the longest continuous record of atmospheric CO₂.

Label: "Annual cycle: CO₂ falls during Northern Hemisphere summer (photosynthesis) and rises during winter (respiration)." "Overall trend: relentless increase due to fossil fuel combustion."

  • 1958: ~315 ppm
  • 2024: >425 ppm
  • Rate of increase: accelerating (from ~0.7 ppm/year in the 1960s to ~2.5 ppm/year in the 2020s)

4. Impacts on Biodiversity

Coral Bleaching

Corals have a mutualistic relationship with zooxanthellae (photosynthetic dinoflagellate algae living within coral tissues). Zooxanthellae provide >90% of the coral's energy through photosynthesis; the coral provides protection and access to light.

Bleaching mechanism:

  1. Water temperature exceeds tolerance threshold (typically >1°C above normal summer maximum for several weeks).
  2. Heat stress damages photosystem II in zooxanthellae → production of reactive oxygen species.
  3. Coral expels zooxanthellae as a stress response → coral tissue becomes transparent → white skeleton visible through tissue → "bleaching."
  4. Coral can recover if temperatures return to normal within weeks (zooxanthellae re-colonise).
  5. If elevated temperatures persist → coral starves → dies → reef structure erodes.

Mass bleaching events:

  • 1998: First global mass bleaching event (16% of world's coral reefs destroyed)
  • 2002, 2010, 2014–2017 (longest global event), 2020, 2023–2024
  • Great Barrier Reef has experienced 5 mass bleaching events since 2016

Consequences of reef loss:

  • Loss of habitat for ~25% of marine species
  • Loss of fisheries supporting hundreds of millions of people
  • Loss of coastal protection (reefs dissipate wave energy)
  • Loss of tourism revenue

Ocean Acidification

Ocean acidification is the ongoing decrease in ocean pH caused by the absorption of atmospheric CO₂. When CO₂ dissolves in seawater, it forms carbonic acid (H₂CO₃), which dissociates to release H⁺ ions → pH decreases.

Chemical reactions:

  • CO₂ + H₂O ⇌ H₂CO₃ (carbonic acid)
  • H₂CO₃ ⇌ H⁺ + HCO₃⁻ (bicarbonate)
  • HCO₃⁻ ⇌ H⁺ + CO₃²⁻ (carbonate)

Key facts:

  • The ocean has absorbed ~30% of anthropogenic CO₂ emissions
  • Ocean surface pH has dropped from ~8.2 (pre-industrial) to ~8.1 — a 30% increase in acidity (pH is logarithmic)
  • By 2100, pH may drop to ~7.8 under high-emission scenarios — a ~150% increase in acidity from pre-industrial levels

Biological impacts:

  • Reduced carbonate ion (CO₃²⁻) availability: Many marine organisms use carbonate to build calcium carbonate (CaCO₃) shells and skeletons.
  • Affected organisms: Corals (aragonite skeletons), mollusks (oysters, clams, mussels), pteropods (sea butterflies — base of the marine food web in polar regions), coccolithophores (phytoplankton with CaCO₃ plates), foraminifera.
  • Energetic cost: Even if organisms can still calcify, it requires more energy, leaving less for growth, reproduction, and immune function.
  • Shell dissolution: At sufficiently low pH, existing CaCO₃ structures begin to dissolve.

> Draw and label: A pteropod shell under normal pH vs after 45 days in acidified water (pH projected for 2100). Show the thin, pitted, partially dissolved shell. Caption: "Pteropods (sea butterflies) are a key link in marine food webs — their decline threatens fish, seabirds, and whales."

Permafrost Melting

Permafrost (permanently frozen ground) in the Arctic contains ~1,400–1,600 Gt of organic carbon — roughly double the carbon currently in the atmosphere. As the Arctic warms (~3-4× global average rate):

  1. Permafrost thaws → microbes decompose previously frozen organic matter.
  2. Decomposition releases CO₂ (aerobic conditions) or CH₄ (anaerobic conditions — in waterlogged soils, methane is produced).
  3. → Positive feedback on climate warming (see Lesson 53).

Impacts on Agriculture

Region Expected Impact
High latitudes Longer growing season → potential benefit for some crops (but limited by soil quality, pests expanding ranges)
Mid-latitudes Mixed — some regions may benefit from CO₂ fertilisation; others face drought, heat stress during critical growth periods
Tropics and subtropics Predominantly negative — crops already near thermal tolerance limits; increased drought; increased pest and disease pressure
Smallholder farmers Most vulnerable — least resources for adaptation

Specific concerns:

  • Wheat and maize yields declining in many regions
  • Rice yields threatened by heat stress during flowering
  • Coffee: Coffea arabica (Arabica) requires specific cool temperature range — suitable area may shrink by 50% by 2050
  • Fisheries: shifting fish stocks affect coastal communities; ocean acidification threatens shellfish aquaculture

6. Mitigation Strategies

Mitigation refers to actions that reduce greenhouse gas emissions or enhance carbon sinks to limit the magnitude of climate change. It addresses the cause.

Adaptation refers to actions that adjust to the impacts of climate change that are already occurring or are unavoidable. It addresses the consequences.

Renewable Energy

Technology How It Works Advantages Limitations
Solar photovoltaic (PV) Semiconductors convert sunlight directly to electricity Abundant, declining costs, scalable (rooftop to utility-scale) Intermittent (no sun at night); requires energy storage or grid integration; land use for large installations
Solar thermal (CSP) Mirrors concentrate sunlight to heat fluid → drives turbine Can include thermal storage (molten salt) → dispatchable power Requires direct sunlight (deserts); higher cost than PV
Wind (onshore & offshore) Wind turns turbine → generator produces electricity Low cost, mature technology, offshore = stronger, more consistent winds Intermittent; visual/noise impact; bird/bat mortality (though far less than buildings/cats)
Hydroelectric Flowing water turns turbine (dams or run-of-river) Reliable, dispatchable, low operating cost, long lifespan Dams flood ecosystems, displace communities, block fish migration, methane from submerged vegetation
Geothermal Heat from Earth's interior → steam → turbine Reliable (baseload), small land footprint Location-specific (tectonically active regions); high upfront cost; potential for induced seismicity
Tidal and wave Tidal flow or wave motion → turbine Predictable (unlike solar/wind) Early-stage technology; high cost; potential marine ecosystem impacts

Nuclear Energy

  • Low-carbon during operation (no direct CO₂ emissions)
  • High energy density, reliable baseload power
  • Challenges: Nuclear waste storage (long-lived radioactive waste), accident risk (Chernobyl, Fukushima), high upfront costs, public perception, nuclear proliferation concerns
  • Role in climate mitigation debated: some argue it's essential for decarbonising electricity; others argue renewables + storage can suffice

Carbon Capture and Storage (CCS)

  1. Capture: CO₂ separated from power plant or industrial emissions (post-combustion, pre-combustion, or oxyfuel combustion).
  2. Transport: CO₂ compressed and transported via pipeline.
  3. Storage: CO₂ injected deep underground into geological formations (depleted oil/gas reservoirs, deep saline aquifers, unmineable coal seams).

Challenges: Energy penalty (CCS uses 20–30% of the power plant's output), high cost, long-term storage security (leakage risk), limited deployment to date.

Direct air capture (DAC): Removes CO₂ directly from ambient air (not from a point source). Currently very expensive and energy-intensive, but may be necessary to achieve net-negative emissions.

Reforestation and Afforestation

  • Reforestation: Replanting trees where forests previously existed.
  • Afforestation: Planting trees where forests did not historically exist (caution: can harm native grassland biodiversity, reduce albedo in snowy regions → may have limited net climate benefit in high latitudes).
  • Potential: Global reforestation could sequester significant carbon (estimated 200+ GtC potential).
  • Co-benefits: Biodiversity habitat, watershed protection, soil stabilisation, livelihoods.

Reducing Meat Consumption

Livestock, especially cattle:

  • Produce methane (enteric fermentation)
  • Require extensive land (deforestation for pasture and feed crop production)
  • Have high water and feed footprints

Per gram of protein, beef produces ~50× more GHG emissions than legumes and ~10× more than poultry. Shifting diets toward plant-based proteins and reducing food waste are among the most impactful individual actions.

Energy Efficiency and Electrification

  • Building efficiency: Insulation, heat pumps (instead of gas heating), LED lighting, smart thermostats
  • Transportation: Electric vehicles (EVs), public transit, active transport (cycling, walking), high-speed rail (alternative to short-haul flights)
  • Industry: More efficient motors, waste heat recovery, process optimisation, electrification of industrial heat

7. Adaptation Strategies

Conservation Corridors

As species shift their ranges poleward and upward, habitat connectivity becomes critical. Conservation corridors (strips of natural habitat connecting protected areas) allow species to move through human-dominated landscapes to track suitable climate.

Seed Banks

The Svalbard Global Seed Vault (Norway) stores seeds of >1 million crop varieties from gene banks worldwide, buried in permafrost on a remote Arctic island. It serves as a backup against loss of crop diversity due to climate change, war, or natural disaster.

Assisted Migration

Assisted migration (assisted colonisation) is the deliberate movement of species to areas outside their historical range where climate is projected to become suitable, when natural dispersal is impossible (e.g., species trapped on mountaintops or islands, or blocked by human infrastructure).

Controversial because: Introduced species can become invasive, disrupt existing ecosystems, and introduce diseases. Risk-benefit analysis is essential and contested.

Flood Defences and Coastal Protection

  • Hard engineering: Sea walls, levees, storm surge barriers
  • Soft/nature-based solutions: Mangrove restoration, coral reef conservation (natural wave barriers), wetland restoration
  • Managed retreat: Relocating communities away from high-risk coastal areas (controversial, socially and economically difficult)

Drought-Resistant Crops

  • Traditional breeding and genetic modification for heat tolerance, drought tolerance, and salt tolerance
  • Changing crop varieties and planting dates
  • Improved irrigation efficiency (drip irrigation vs flood irrigation)

8. International Agreements

The IPCC

The Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) was established in 1988 by the UN Environment Programme (UNEP) and the World Meteorological Organization (WMO). It does not conduct its own research — instead, it synthesises and assesses the work of thousands of scientists worldwide.

Assessment Report Year Key Message
AR1 1990 First comprehensive assessment; led to UNFCCC
AR2 1995 "The balance of evidence suggests a discernible human influence on global climate" — first formal attribution statement
AR3 2001 Stronger attribution; "most of the observed warming...is due to the increase in greenhouse gas concentrations"
AR4 2007 "Warming of the climate system is unequivocal." IPCC awarded Nobel Peace Prize (with Al Gore)
AR5 2013–2014 "Human influence...is clear." Led to Paris Agreement. Introduced "carbon budget" concept
AR6 2021–2023 "Code red for humanity"** — "It is unequivocal that human influence has warmed the atmosphere, ocean and land"

Kyoto Protocol (1997)

Feature Detail
Goal Binding emission reduction targets for developed (Annex I) countries — average 5.2% below 1990 levels by 2008–2012
Coverage Only developed nations had targets (principle of "common but differentiated responsibilities")
Flexibility mechanisms Emissions trading, Clean Development Mechanism (CDM), Joint Implementation (JI)
Limitations US did not ratify (Senate voted 95-0 against); Canada withdrew (2011); developing nations including China and India had no binding targets; limited overall impact on global emissions

Paris Agreement (2015)

Feature Detail
Goal Limit global warming to well below 2°C, preferably 1.5°C, above pre-industrial levels
Approach Bottom-up: each country submits its own Nationally Determined Contributions (NDCs) — not imposed top-down
Coverage All** signatory nations (unlike Kyoto's Annex I/non-Annex I split) — universal participation
Ratchet mechanism Countries must submit updated NDCs every 5 years, each more ambitious than the last
Enforcement No binding enforcement mechanism — relies on transparency, peer pressure, and national commitment
Loss and damage Acknowledges need to address loss and damage from climate impacts in vulnerable countries
US withdrawal and re-entry Trump administration withdrew (2017, effective 2020); Biden administration re-joined (2021)

Challenges

Challenge Explanation
Economic interests in fossil fuels Fossil fuel industries employ millions; represent trillions in economic value; lobbying power against climate action
Right to develop Developing nations argue they should not be constrained by climate targets when their per-capita emissions remain far below developed nations and they need energy for development
Political will Short electoral cycles (~4–5 years) conflict with long-term climate planning (>decades); policy reversals with changes in government
Tragedy of the commons The atmosphere is a shared (common) resource — each nation benefits individually from fossil fuel use while the costs (climate change) are distributed globally → incentive to "free ride" on others' emission reductions
Equity Historical responsibility: developed nations are responsible for the majority of cumulative emissions since the Industrial Revolution. Yet developing nations face some of the worst impacts with fewer resources to adapt

9. Common Misconceptions

Misconception Reality
"The greenhouse effect is bad" The natural greenhouse effect is essential — without it, Earth would be frozen. The enhanced (anthropogenic) greenhouse effect is the problem
"There's no scientific consensus on climate change" >99% of peer-reviewed climate science papers agree that recent warming is caused by human activities. The consensus is as strong as for evolution or germ theory
"Climate has always changed — this is just natural variation" Past climate changes were driven by natural factors (orbital cycles, volcanic activity, solar output). The current warming cannot be explained by any natural factor — only by anthropogenic GHG emissions
"CO₂ is a pollutant" CO₂ is a natural and essential component of the carbon cycle. The problem is the rate and magnitude of increase
"If it's cold outside, global warming isn't real" Weather ≠ climate. Climate is the long-term average. A cold day or even a cold winter does not contradict a warming trend
"The ozone hole causes global warming" Ozone depletion and climate change are separate (but related) issues. The ozone hole is caused by CFCs; global warming is caused primarily by CO₂. CFCs are both ozone-depleters and GHGs, but the mechanisms are different
"Renewable energy can't replace fossil fuels" Multiple studies show that 100% renewable energy is technically feasible with existing technology, though it requires significant investment in grid infrastructure, energy storage, and demand management
"Planting trees will solve climate change" Reforestation is important but cannot offset continued fossil fuel emissions alone — there simply isn't enough land. Emissions reduction must be the primary strategy

Practice Problems

Problem 1 (Greenhouse Mechanism)

Explain, step by step, how the enhanced greenhouse effect leads to global warming. Include: shortwave radiation, absorption at Earth's surface, longwave radiation, greenhouse gas absorption, and re-radiation.

Answer

  1. Incoming solar radiation (shortwave/UV-visible): Sunlight passes through the atmosphere largely unimpeded.
  1. Absorption at surface: ~70% of incoming radiation is absorbed by Earth's surface (land and ocean), warming it.
  1. Re-radiation as longwave (infrared): The warm Earth surface radiates energy back upward as longwave infrared radiation.
  1. Greenhouse gas absorption: Greenhouse gases (CO₂, H₂O, CH₄, N₂O) in the atmosphere absorb some of this outgoing longwave radiation at specific wavelengths. Without GHGs, this energy would escape directly to space.
  1. Re-radiation: GHGs re-emit the absorbed energy in all directions — some upward (eventually escapes to space) and some downward back toward the surface.
  1. Surface warming: This "back-radiation" adds additional energy to Earth's surface beyond what direct solar heating provides → the surface warms until a new equilibrium is reached where outgoing radiation balances incoming.
  1. Enhanced effect: Increased GHG concentrations (from human activities) → more longwave radiation absorbed → more back-radiation → Earth warms further.

Problem 2 (Coral Bleaching)

Explain the process of coral bleaching, including the role of zooxanthellae, the trigger, the mechanism, and the consequences for the coral and the reef ecosystem.

Answer

The symbiosis: Corals host zooxanthellae (photosynthetic dinoflagellate algae) within their tissues. Zooxanthellae provide >90% of the coral's energy through photosynthesis; coral provides protection, access to light, and nitrogenous waste compounds.

Trigger: Sea water temperature exceeds the coral's thermal tolerance threshold (typically >1°C above the normal summer maximum for several weeks).

Mechanism: Heat stress damages photosystem II in zooxanthellae → production of reactive oxygen species (oxidative stress). The coral expels the zooxanthellae as a protective stress response.

Bleaching appearance: Without zooxanthellae, the coral tissue becomes transparent, revealing the white calcium carbonate skeleton beneath → "bleached" appearance.

Outcomes:

  • Short-term (<~8 weeks of elevated temperature): Coral can recover — zooxanthellae recolonise, symbiosis re-established.
  • Prolonged heat stress: Coral starves (loses its main energy source) → dies → skeleton erodes.

Ecosystem consequences: Dead coral → reef structure collapses → loss of habitat for ~25% of marine species → decline in fisheries → loss of coastal protection (reefs dissipate wave energy) → loss of tourism revenue.

Problem 3 (Ocean Acidification)

Write the chemical equations for ocean acidification. Explain why a decrease in ocean pH threatens organisms with calcium carbonate shells, even though ocean water remains alkaline (pH >7).

Answer

Chemical equations:

  1. CO₂ dissolves: CO₂ + H₂O ⇌ H₂CO₃ (carbonic acid)
  2. Carbonic acid dissociates: H₂CO₃ ⇌ H⁺ + HCO₃⁻ (bicarbonate)
  3. Bicarbonate further dissociates: HCO₃⁻ ⇌ H⁺ + CO₃²⁻ (carbonate)

Net effect: Adding CO₂ → ↑ H⁺ ions → ↓ pH (ocean acidification).

Why it threatens calcifying organisms despite pH >7:

The key issue is not the absolute pH but the availability of carbonate ions (CO₃²⁻):

  • Added H⁺ ions react with existing CO₃²⁻: H⁺ + CO₃²⁻ → HCO₃⁻
  • This reduces the concentration of free carbonate ions
  • Organisms that build CaCO₃ shells/skeletons need an adequate supply of CO₃²⁻: Ca²⁺ + CO₃²⁻ → CaCO₃
  • When CO₃²⁻ concentration falls below the saturation threshold, CaCO₃ becomes undersaturated → existing shells may begin to dissolve, and new shell formation becomes energetically more costly (or impossible)

The term "acidification" refers to the direction of pH change (decreasing), not crossing the pH 7 neutral line. A decrease from pH 8.2 to 8.1 represents a ~30% increase in hydrogen ion concentration — a significant chemical change even though the water is still slightly alkaline.

Problem 4 (Mitigation vs Adaptation)

Distinguish between climate change mitigation and adaptation. For each, provide two specific examples and evaluate their strengths and limitations.

Answer

Mitigation Adaptation
Definition Reducing GHG emissions or enhancing sinks to limit climate change magnitude Adjusting to the impacts of climate change that are occurring or unavoidable
Addresses The CAUSE The CONSEQUENCES

Mitigation Examples:

  1. Solar/wind energy deployment:
  • Strengths: Zero operational emissions, declining costs, job creation
  • Limitations: Intermittency (requires storage/grid upgrades), land use, material requirements
  1. Reforestation:
  • Strengths: Carbon sequestration, biodiversity co-benefits, watershed protection, low-tech
  • Limitations: Land competition with agriculture, slow carbon uptake (decades), vulnerability to fire/drought, cannot offset continued fossil fuel use alone

Adaptation Examples:

  1. Conservation corridors:
  • Strengths: Allows species to track suitable climate, maintains gene flow, relatively low-cost
  • Limitations: Requires land acquisition/protection across jurisdictions; not all species can move fast enough; doesn't help species with very specific habitat requirements
  1. Flood defences (sea walls):
  • Strengths: Protects coastal communities and infrastructure directly
  • Limitations: Expensive, can fail catastrophically if sea level exceeds design, may increase erosion in adjacent areas, doesn't address root cause, inequitable (wealthy areas can afford; poorer cannot)

Both are necessary: Even with aggressive mitigation, significant warming is already "locked in" from past emissions, making adaptation essential. Conversely, mitigation is essential to prevent climate change from exceeding our capacity to adapt.

Approach Top-down: binding targets imposed on developed nations Bottom-up: each nation determines its own NDC
Coverage Annex I (developed) countries only; developing nations (incl. China, India) had NO targets Universal — all signatory nations participate
Binding force Legally binding emission targets No legally binding emission targets — binding to submit and report on NDCs
Enforcement Compliance mechanism with potential penalties Transparency framework; no penalties — relies on peer pressure/naming-and-shaming
Goal 5.2% below 1990 levels (Annex I average) Well below 2°C, preferably 1.5°C
Ratchet Fixed commitment periods NDCs updated every 5 years with expectation of increased ambition

Why Paris was more politically viable:

Kyoto's binding targets were rejected by the US Senate (95-0 vote), and Canada later withdrew. The top-down approach was perceived as infringing on national sovereignty. By allowing each nation to determine its own contributions (NDCs), Paris respected national circumstances and political realities while still creating a framework for collective action.

Main weakness:

The absence of binding enforcement means success depends entirely on national political will. Current NDCs are insufficient to meet the 1.5°C or even 2°C goals. There is no penalty for failing to meet an NDC. This "pledge and review" system relies on international pressure and domestic political commitment — both of which can fluctuate with changes in government.

Summary

Topic Key Points
Greenhouse effect Natural = essential (Earth would be −18°C without it); Enhanced = anthropogenic GHG increase → warming
GHG mechanism Shortwave solar radiation passes through atmosphere → absorbed by Earth → re-radiated as longwave infrared → absorbed by GHGs → re-radiated in all directions → some returns to surface
Major GHGs CO₂ (fossil fuels, deforestation — main driver, long lifetime); CH₄ (agriculture, fossil fuel leaks — 25× CO₂, short lifetime); N₂O (fertilisers — 300× CO₂); H₂O (feedback, not driver)
Evidence Temperature records (+1.1°C), ice cores (CO₂ now >420 ppm, never >300 ppm in 800k years), sea level rise, glacier retreat, phenological shifts, species range shifts, Keeling curve
Impacts Coral bleaching (zooxanthellae expulsion), ocean acidification (CO₂ → carbonic acid → less CO₃²⁻), permafrost melt (CH₄/CO₂ positive feedback), agricultural stress
Mitigation Renewables, nuclear, CCS, reforestation, dietary change, energy efficiency, electrification
Adaptation Conservation corridors, seed banks, assisted migration, flood defences, drought-resistant crops
International IPCC (assesses science), Kyoto (1997 — binding, Annex I only, limited success), Paris (2015 — NDCs, universal, no binding enforcement)
