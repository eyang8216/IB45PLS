1. Quaternary Structure (4°): Subunit Assembly

1.1 Definition

Not all proteins stop at the tertiary level. Quaternary structure refers to the assembly of two or more polypeptide subunits (each with its own primary, secondary, and tertiary structure) into a single functional protein complex.

Quaternary structure (4°): The spatial arrangement of multiple folded polypeptide chains (subunits) into a functional protein complex. Subunits are held together by the same types of interactions that stabilize tertiary structure: hydrophobic interactions, H-bonds, ionic bonds, and sometimes disulfide bridges and covalent cross-links.

Key points:

  • Subunits may be identical (homomeric, e.g., homodimer, homotetramer) or different (heteromeric, e.g., hemoglobin's α₂β₂).
  • Quaternary structure adds a new dimension of regulation — cooperativity and allostery arise from subunit-subunit interactions.
  • The individual polypeptide chains are called protoners or subunits.

2. Hemoglobin: The Paradigm of Quaternary Structure

2.1 Structure

Hemoglobin (Hb) is an α₂β₂ tetramer — it contains four polypeptide subunits:

  • 2 α-globin subunits (141 amino acids each)
  • 2 β-globin subunits (146 amino acids each)
  • Each subunit contains one heme group — a porphyrin ring with an iron (Fe²⁺) atom at its center
  • Each heme group can bind one O₂ molecule
  • Therefore, one hemoglobin molecule can carry 4 O₂ molecules

Diagram to draw in your notes:

  1. Four subunits arranged in a roughly tetrahedral shape: two α (labeled) and two β (labeled).
  2. Each subunit has a heme group (draw as a flat rectangle with Fe in the center) in a hydrophobic pocket.
  3. Show O₂ bound to each heme Fe²⁺.
  4. Label: "α₂β₂ tetramer," "4 heme groups," "Binds 4 O₂."

2.2 Cooperative Binding and the Sigmoidal Curve

Hemoglobin exhibits cooperative binding of oxygen — the binding of O₂ to one subunit makes it easier for the next O₂ to bind. This is a property that arises from quaternary structure.

The mechanism (concerted model / MWC model):

  • Hemoglobin exists in two conformational states: T (tense, low affinity for O₂) and R (relaxed, high affinity for O₂).
  • Deoxyhemoglobin (no O₂ bound) is primarily in the T-state.
  • When the first O₂ binds, it triggers a conformational change in that subunit, which is transmitted to neighboring subunits, shifting them toward the R-state.
  • As more O₂ binds, the equilibrium shifts further toward the R-state, making subsequent O₂ binding progressively easier.
  • The fourth O₂ binds with approximately 300× higher affinity than the first.

The oxygen dissociation curve:

  • The curve of % saturation vs pO₂ (partial pressure of oxygen) is sigmoidal (S-shaped), NOT hyperbolic.
  • A hyperbolic curve (as for myoglobin) would indicate independent, non-cooperative binding.
  • The sigmoidal shape reflects cooperativity: shallow slope at low pO₂ (low initial affinity), steep slope at intermediate pO₂ (cooperativity kicks in), plateau at high pO₂ (saturation).

Diagram to draw: Plot % O₂ saturation (y-axis, 0-100%) vs pO₂ (x-axis, in kPa or mmHg). Draw:

  • A sigmoidal curve for hemoglobin — label "Cooperative binding (Hb)."
  • A hyperbolic curve for myoglobin — label "Non-cooperative binding (Mb)."
  • Mark: p50 (the pO₂ at 50% saturation) — ~26 mmHg for Hb, ~1 mmHg for Mb.

Physiological significance: The sigmoidal curve means hemoglobin is very sensitive to small changes in pO₂ in the physiological range:

  • In the lungs (pO₂ ~100 mmHg): Hb is ~98% saturated — it loads O₂ efficiently.
  • In resting tissues (pO₂ ~40 mmHg): Hb is ~75% saturated — it unloads ~23% of its O₂.
  • In exercising muscle (pO₂ ~15-20 mmHg): Hb is ~25% saturated — it unloads ~73% of its O₂.

This allows hemoglobin to deliver O₂ where it is needed most.

Cooperativity: A phenomenon in multi-subunit proteins where the binding of a ligand (e.g., O₂) to one subunit influences the binding affinity of the remaining subunits. Positive cooperativity (as in hemoglobin) means binding becomes progressively easier.

Sigmoidal curve: An S-shaped binding curve characteristic of cooperative systems. The slope changes with ligand concentration, reflecting the transition from low-affinity (T) to high-affinity (R) states.

3. Collagen: The Structural Triple Helix

Collagen is the most abundant protein in the human body (~25-35% of total body protein). It is the primary structural protein of connective tissues: tendons, ligaments, skin, bone, cartilage, and blood vessels.

3.1 Primary Structure: The Gly-X-Y Repeat

Collagen has a highly unusual amino acid composition:

  • Glycine (~33%): Every third amino acid is glycine.
  • Proline (~12%) and Hydroxyproline (~10%): Proline derivatives.
  • The repeating pattern is (Gly-X-Y)ₙ, where X is often proline and Y is often hydroxyproline.

3.2 Secondary and Tertiary Structure: The Triple Helix

Three collagen polypeptide chains (α-chains) wind around each other to form a triple helix — a right-handed supercoil.

Why every third residue must be glycine: The three chains pack tightly together with their backbones in the center. Glycine, with its tiny R-group (-H), is the only amino acid small enough to fit into the crowded interior of the triple helix. Any larger residue would create steric clashes and disrupt the structure.

Diagram to draw:

  1. Three parallel polypeptide chains shown as three intertwined ribbons (like a 3-stranded rope).
  2. Zoom in: show glycine residues (with just -H as R-group) pointing into the center.
  3. Show proline and hydroxyproline residues pointing outward.
  4. Label: "Gly-X-Y repeat," "Glycine every 3rd residue → fits in center," "Triple helix (tropocollagen)."

3.3 Post-Translational Modifications and Vitamin C

Collagen requires several post-translational modifications to be functional:

Proline hydroxylation → Hydroxyproline:

  • Enzyme: Prolyl hydroxylase
  • Cofactor required: Vitamin C (ascorbic acid)
  • Function: Hydroxyproline residues form additional H-bonds that stabilize the triple helix.

Lysine hydroxylation → Hydroxylysine:

  • Enzyme: Lysyl hydroxylase
  • Also requires Vitamin C.
  • Hydroxylysine residues serve as attachment sites for carbohydrate groups and later form covalent cross-links.

The Vitamin C connection — Scurvy:

If Vitamin C is deficient, prolyl hydroxylase cannot function. Collagen is synthesized but is under-hydroxylated and consequently unstable — the triple helix unravels at body temperature. The symptoms of scurvy (bleeding gums, fragile blood vessels, poor wound healing, bruising, tooth loss) are all consequences of defective collagen.

IB Exam Tip: The collagen-Vitamin C link is a classic IB question. Be able to explain: (1) Collagen has hydroxyproline formed by prolyl hydroxylase, (2) this enzyme requires Vitamin C as a cofactor, (3) without Vitamin C, collagen is unstable, (4) symptoms of scurvy are due to weakened connective tissue.

3.4 Covalent Cross-Links for Tensile Strength

After triple helices (tropocollagen molecules) are secreted from cells, they assemble into collagen fibrils. Lysyl oxidase (an extracellular enzyme) oxidizes lysine and hydroxylysine residues to aldehydes, which then form covalent cross-links between adjacent tropocollagen molecules.

These covalent cross-links give collagen fibrils their extraordinary tensile strength — they resist being pulled apart. This is why tendons (which connect muscle to bone) can withstand enormous forces without snapping.

5. Denaturation

5.1 What Is Denaturation?

Denaturation is the loss of a protein's native three-dimensional structure (secondary, tertiary, and quaternary), resulting in loss of biological function. The primary structure (peptide bonds) remains intact — only the non-covalent interactions and disulfide bonds are disrupted.

Denaturation: The process by which a protein loses its native conformation and biological activity due to disruption of non-covalent interactions (H-bonds, ionic bonds, hydrophobic interactions) and/or disulfide bridges. The primary structure (peptide bonds) is NOT broken. Denaturation is often irreversible.

Analogy: A protein is like a precisely folded origami crane. Denaturation is like unfolding it into a flat piece of paper. The paper (primary structure) is still intact, but the functional shape (tertiary structure) is lost. You might be able to refold it, but probably not into the exact same crane.

5.2 Denaturing Agents

#### 5.2.1 Heat

Heating increases the kinetic energy of the protein, causing atoms to vibrate more vigorously. This disrupts:

  • Hydrogen bonds (weak, overcome by thermal motion)
  • Hydrophobic interactions (disrupted as the protein unfolds and hydrophobic groups are exposed)
  • Ionic bonds (shielded by water as the protein opens up)

Most proteins denature at temperatures above 40-60°C. Some extremophile proteins (e.g., from thermophilic bacteria) are stable at >100°C due to additional ionic bonds and a more compact hydrophobic core.

Example: Cooking an egg. Egg white (albumin) is a clear liquid. Heating denatures albumin → it unfolds, exposes hydrophobic patches, and the unfolded proteins aggregate → white, opaque solid.

#### 5.2.2 pH Extremes

Changes in pH alter the ionization state of R-groups:

  • Acidic pH: Excess H⁺ protonates -COO⁻ groups (Glu, Asp lose negative charge) → disrupts salt bridges.
  • Basic pH: Excess OH⁻ deprotonates -NH₃⁺ groups (Lys, Arg lose positive charge) → disrupts salt bridges.

The loss of ionic bonds destabilizes tertiary structure, leading to unfolding.

#### 5.2.3 Heavy Metals

Heavy metal ions (e.g., Hg²⁺, Pb²⁺, Cd²⁺, Ag⁺) bind strongly to thiol (-SH) groups of cysteine residues:

  • They can break existing disulfide bonds.
  • They form metal-sulfur complexes that distort the protein structure.
  • This is why heavy metal poisoning is so toxic — it denatures enzymes and other essential proteins.

#### 5.2.4 Organic Solvents and Detergents

  • Organic solvents (ethanol, acetone) disrupt hydrophobic interactions by providing an alternative nonpolar environment.
  • Detergents (SDS — sodium dodecyl sulfate) surround hydrophobic regions with their own hydrophobic tails, causing proteins to unfold.
  • Urea and guanidinium chloride disrupt H-bonds and hydrophobic interactions.

5.3 Consequences of Denaturation

  • Loss of biological function: An enzyme loses its catalytic activity; a transport protein can no longer bind its ligand.
  • Decreased solubility: Exposed hydrophobic patches cause unfolded proteins to aggregate and precipitate.
  • Increased susceptibility to proteolysis: Unfolded proteins are more accessible to proteases (enzymes that degrade proteins).

6. Renaturation and the Anfinsen Experiment

Can a denatured protein refold? Sometimes yes — and this has profound implications for our understanding of protein folding.

6.1 Anfinsen's Ribonuclease Experiment (Nobel Prize, 1972)

Christian Anfinsen studied ribonuclease A (RNase A), a small enzyme with 124 amino acids and 4 disulfide bonds.

The experiment:

  1. RNase A was treated with urea (8 M — disrupts H-bonds and hydrophobic interactions) and β-mercaptoethanol (reduces disulfide bonds → breaks S-S to -SH) → the protein unfolded completely and lost all enzymatic activity.
  2. The urea and β-mercaptoethanol were slowly removed by dialysis.
  3. Result: The protein spontaneously refolded into its native conformation and regained full enzymatic activity.

What this proved: The primary amino acid sequence contains all the information necessary to specify the three-dimensional structure of the protein. The native conformation is the thermodynamically most stable state under physiological conditions.

Anfinsen's dogma (thermodynamic hypothesis): The three-dimensional structure of a protein is determined entirely by its amino acid sequence. The native conformation is the global minimum of the free energy landscape.

6.2 Limitations

Not all proteins can renature spontaneously:

  • Many proteins require chaperones for proper folding (see below).
  • Large, multi-domain proteins often aggregate irreversibly when denatured.
  • In the Anfinsen experiment, RNase A is relatively small (124 residues) and was denatured slowly and carefully. In a cell or a hard-boiled egg, denaturation often leads to irreversible aggregation.

8. Protein Structure Determination

How do scientists know the 3D structures of proteins?

8.1 X-Ray Crystallography

Principle: X-rays are directed at a protein crystal. The electrons in the crystal scatter the X-rays, producing a diffraction pattern (a pattern of spots). Using mathematical analysis (Fourier transforms), the diffraction pattern is converted into an electron density map, which reveals the positions of atoms.

Process:

  1. Purify the protein.
  2. Crystallize it (often the hardest step — requires finding exact conditions).
  3. Expose the crystal to X-rays (synchrotron radiation).
  4. Collect diffraction data.
  5. Calculate electron density map.
  6. Build an atomic model into the density.

Limitations:

  • The protein must crystallize (not all proteins do).
  • The crystal structure may not reflect the protein's conformation in solution.
  • Static "snapshot" — no dynamic information.

8.2 NMR (Nuclear Magnetic Resonance) Spectroscopy

Principle: Certain atomic nuclei (¹H, ¹³C, ¹⁵N) behave like tiny magnets in a strong magnetic field. Radio frequency pulses excite these nuclei, and the signals they emit depend on their chemical environment and their proximity to other nuclei. By measuring these signals, distances between atoms can be determined, and a 3D structure can be computed.

Advantages over X-ray crystallography:

  • Protein is in solution (closer to physiological conditions).
  • Can provide information about dynamics (flexibility, motion).
  • No crystallization needed.

Limitations:

  • Limited to relatively small proteins (< ~30-40 kDa typically).
  • Requires large amounts of purified, isotopically labeled protein.

IB Exam Tip: You should be able to name both methods and state one key advantage of each. For X-ray: very high resolution, works for large proteins. For NMR: protein in solution, no crystals needed, gives dynamic information.

9. Protein Folding in the Cell

9.1 The Folding Problem

In the test tube (Anfinsen experiment), a small protein can fold spontaneously. But in the crowded, concentrated environment of a cell (300-400 mg/mL protein), folding is more challenging:

  • Partially folded proteins expose hydrophobic patches → they tend to aggregate with other partially folded proteins.
  • Aggregation can lead to insoluble "inclusion bodies" and is often irreversible.
  • Aggregated proteins are non-functional and can be toxic to the cell.

9.2 Chaperone Proteins

Chaperones (also called heat shock proteins, Hsps) are proteins that assist the folding of other proteins. They do NOT specify the final folded structure — the amino acid sequence does that. Instead, chaperones prevent incorrect interactions (aggregation) and provide an isolated environment where folding can proceed correctly.

Chaperone protein: A protein that assists the non-covalent folding or unfolding of other proteins. Chaperones do not convey structural information; they prevent aggregation and provide an isolated folding environment. Many are "heat shock proteins" (Hsps) whose expression increases under stress conditions.

#### Hsp70 System

  • Binds to hydrophobic patches on nascent (newly synthesized) or partially folded polypeptides as they emerge from the ribosome.
  • Prevents aggregation by keeping the polypeptide in an extended, unfolded state until synthesis is complete.
  • ATP binding and hydrolysis drives cycles of binding and release.

#### Hsp60 (Chaperonin) System — GroEL/GroES in Bacteria

  • A large barrel-shaped complex (two stacked rings).
  • The unfolded protein enters the central cavity.
  • The chaperonin cap (GroES) closes the cavity.
  • Inside the isolated chamber, the protein can fold without interference from other proteins.
  • ATP hydrolysis drives the conformational changes.
  • After ~10-15 seconds, the cap opens, and the folded protein is released.

Diagram to draw in your notes:

  1. An unfolded polypeptide entering a large barrel-shaped Hsp60 complex.
  2. The cap (GroES) attaching → a sealed chamber.
  3. The polypeptide folding correctly inside the protected chamber.
  4. ATP → ADP + Pi → cap release → folded protein exits.

10. Protein Misfolding Diseases

When proteins misfold and aggregate, they can cause devastating diseases.

10.1 Prion Diseases

Prions (proteinaceous infectious particles) are misfolded proteins that can induce other proteins to misfold. They are infectious agents composed entirely of protein — no nucleic acid (DNA or RNA).

The prion protein (PrP):

  • PrP^C (cellular prion protein): Normal, α-helical form found on the surface of neurons. Function unclear (possibly copper transport or cell signaling).
  • PrP^Sc (scrapie prion protein): Misfolded form, rich in β-sheets. Insoluble, resistant to proteases, and aggregation-prone.

Mechanism of propagation:

  1. A PrP^Sc molecule encounters a PrP^C molecule.
  2. PrP^Sc acts as a template, inducing PrP^C to refold into the PrP^Sc conformation.
  3. The newly converted PrP^Sc can then convert more PrP^C molecules.
  4. This creates an exponential, chain-reaction amplification.

Prion diseases (Transmissible Spongiform Encephalopathies, TSEs):

Disease Host Notes
Creutzfeldt-Jakob disease (CJD) Humans Sporadic, inherited, or acquired (e.g., from contaminated surgical instruments, growth hormone)
Variant CJD (vCJD) Humans Acquired from eating beef contaminated with BSE
Bovine Spongiform Encephalopathy (BSE) Cattle "Mad cow disease" — spread by feeding cattle meat-and-bone meal from infected animals
Scrapie Sheep, goats The prototypical prion disease (known for >250 years)
Kuru Humans (Fore people, Papua New Guinea) Transmitted by ritual cannibalism

Pathology: Prion aggregates accumulate in the brain, causing neuronal death and giving the brain a characteristic sponge-like (spongiform) appearance — hence "spongiform encephalopathy."

Why prions are remarkable:

  • They are infectious proteins — the only known biological entity that propagates without nucleic acid.
  • They are resistant to sterilization methods that destroy nucleic acids (UV radiation, nucleases, standard autoclaving).
  • They challenge the central dogma of molecular biology (that information flows from nucleic acid → protein, not protein → protein).

10.2 Alzheimer's Disease

Alzheimer's disease is characterized by the accumulation of two types of protein aggregates in the brain:

  1. β-Amyloid plaques (extracellular):
  • The amyloid precursor protein (APP) is a normal transmembrane protein.
  • Abnormal proteolytic cleavage by β-secretase and γ-secretase produces Aβ peptides (especially Aβ₄₂).
  • Aβ peptides are prone to misfolding into β-sheet-rich aggregates.
  • These form amyloid plaques between neurons, which are toxic and trigger inflammation.
  1. Neurofibrillary tangles (intracellular):
  • The tau protein normally stabilizes microtubules (part of the cytoskeleton).
  • In Alzheimer's, tau becomes hyperphosphorylated.
  • Hyperphosphorylated tau dissociates from microtubules and aggregates into paired helical filaments → neurofibrillary tangles.
  • Loss of tau function leads to microtubule destabilization and impaired axonal transport.

10.3 Parkinson's Disease

Parkinson's disease involves the aggregation of α-synuclein, a protein normally involved in synaptic vesicle regulation.

  • Misfolded α-synuclein aggregates into Lewy bodies inside neurons.
  • Dopamine-producing neurons in the substantia nigra (a region of the midbrain) are particularly vulnerable.
  • Loss of these neurons leads to dopamine deficiency → the characteristic motor symptoms: tremor, rigidity, bradykinesia (slowness of movement), and postural instability.

11. Worked Examples

Worked Example 1: Cooperativity Calculation

At a pO₂ of 26 mmHg, hemoglobin is 50% saturated. At pO₂ of 100 mmHg (lung capillaries), hemoglobin is 98% saturated. At pO₂ of 40 mmHg (resting tissues), what percentage of oxygen is released, assuming hemoglobin is approximately 75% saturated at this pressure?

Solution:

  • O₂ loaded in lungs: 98% saturation → 0.98 × 4 = 3.92 O₂ molecules per Hb (out of 4 possible).
  • O₂ remaining in tissues: 75% saturation → 0.75 × 4 = 3.00 O₂ molecules per Hb.
  • O₂ released: 3.92 - 3.00 = 0.92 O₂ molecules per Hb.
  • Percentage of loaded O₂ released: (0.92 ÷ 3.92) × 100 = ~23.5%.

Compare with exercising muscle (pO₂ ~20 mmHg, saturation ~25%):

  • O₂ remaining: 0.25 × 4 = 1.00 O₂ per Hb.
  • O₂ released: 3.92 - 1.00 = 2.92 O₂ per Hb.
  • Percentage released: (2.92 ÷ 3.92) × 100 = ~74.5%.

This demonstrates how cooperativity allows hemoglobin to deliver much more O₂ to active tissues.

Worked Example 2: Anfinsen Experiment Interpretation

A researcher treats a small enzyme with 8 M urea and measures complete loss of activity. The urea is then removed by dialysis. In one trial, the enzyme recovers 100% activity. In a second trial (identical conditions plus a trace amount of a reducing agent that breaks disulfide bonds during denaturation), the enzyme recovers only 5% activity. Explain.

Solution:

In the first trial: Urea disrupts hydrogen bonds and hydrophobic interactions, unfolding the protein. But the disulfide bonds remain intact (urea does not break covalent bonds). The intact disulfide bonds constrain the unfolded protein, preserving some local structure. When urea is removed, the constraints guide correct refolding → 100% activity is recovered.

In the second trial: The reducing agent breaks the disulfide bonds during denaturation. Now the protein is completely unfolded with no constraints. When urea and reducing agent are removed, the 4 disulfide bonds can reform — but they may form incorrectly. With 8 cysteine residues, there are 105 possible ways to pair them into 4 disulfides (7 × 5 × 3 × 1 = 105), only one of which is native. Random reoxidation gives mostly incorrect disulfide pairing → the enzyme cannot achieve its native conformation → only ~5% activity (roughly 1/105 chance of getting all 4 correct ≈ 1%).

This is why the reducing agent dramatically reduces renaturation yield — it "scrambles" the disulfide bond pattern.

Worked Example 3: Collagen and Vitamin C

A sailor on a long 18th-century voyage develops bleeding gums, loose teeth, slow wound healing, and bruising. Explain the molecular basis of these symptoms.

Solution:

These are symptoms of scurvy, caused by Vitamin C (ascorbic acid) deficiency.

Molecular mechanism:

  1. Collagen is the primary structural protein of connective tissue, including gums, teeth sockets, blood vessel walls, and skin.
  2. Proper collagen synthesis requires prolyl hydroxylase and lysyl hydroxylase enzymes, which hydroxylate proline and lysine residues in the collagen polypeptide.
  3. Both enzymes require Vitamin C as an essential cofactor.
  4. Without Vitamin C, collagen is under-hydroxylated → the triple helix is unstable at body temperature → collagen molecules unravel.
  5. Defective collagen leads to weakened blood vessels (bruising, bleeding gums), poor wound healing (no stable collagen scaffold for new tissue), and loosening of teeth (degraded periodontal ligament).

Why fresh citrus fruit cures scurvy — it provides Vitamin C, restoring prolyl hydroxylase activity and allowing proper collagen synthesis.

Answer 1

  • Hemoglobin: α₂β₂ tetramer (2 α-globin + 2 β-globin subunits)
  • Collagen: Triple helix of 3 polypeptide chains (may be identical or different types)

Answer 3

Hemoglobin is a tetramer with cooperative oxygen binding. It exists in two conformations:

  • T (tense) state: Low affinity for O₂; stabilized by salt bridges between subunits.
  • R (relaxed) state: High affinity for O₂; salt bridges broken.

When no O₂ is bound, hemoglobin is mostly in the T-state. The first O₂ binds with low affinity (the curve starts shallow). However, O₂ binding triggers a conformational change (T → R transition) that is transmitted to neighboring subunits, making them more likely to be in the high-affinity R-state. As more O₂ binds, the equilibrium shifts further toward R, so each subsequent O₂ binds more easily.

This positive cooperativity produces a sigmoidal (S-shaped) curve: shallow at low pO₂ (hard to bind first O₂), steep at intermediate pO₂ (cooperativity makes binding easier), plateau at high pO₂ (saturation).

A hyperbolic curve (like myoglobin's) would indicate independent, non-cooperative binding where each O₂ binds with the same affinity regardless of how many are already bound.

Answer 4

At 80°C, the protein has undergone denaturation:

  1. The increased thermal energy overcomes the weak non-covalent interactions (hydrogen bonds, hydrophobic interactions, ionic bonds) that maintain the protein's tertiary structure.
  2. The protein unfolds, exposing hydrophobic patches that were previously buried in the core.
  3. These exposed hydrophobic regions interact with those of other denatured protein molecules, causing aggregation — many protein molecules stick together non-specifically.
  4. The aggregates are often very stable and the individual proteins become hopelessly entangled.

Upon cooling, the aggregated proteins cannot spontaneously refold because:

  • They are kinetically trapped in the aggregated state.
  • Refolding would require each molecule to disentangle from the aggregate (high activation energy barrier).
  • In the test tube, without chaperones, the correct folding pathway is lost.

This is why a boiled egg does not "un-boil" — the aggregated, denatured proteins cannot spontaneously return to their native, soluble state.

Answer 5

(a) Number of chains:

  • Collagen triple helix: 3 polypeptide chains (may be identical or different).
  • α-Helix: Single polypeptide chain — the helix is a secondary structure element within one chain.

(b) Role of glycine:

  • Collagen: Glycine is every 3rd residue (Gly-X-Y repeat). Its tiny R-group (-H) is the only one small enough to fit into the crowded center of the triple helix where the three chains meet. Any larger residue would cause steric clashes and disrupt the structure.
  • α-Helix: No special requirement for glycine. In fact, glycine is RARE in α-helices because its conformational flexibility is entropically costly to constrain into the helix.

(c) Post-translational modifications:

  • Collagen: Extensive modifications — proline hydroxylation (by prolyl hydroxylase → hydroxyproline, requires Vitamin C) and lysine hydroxylation (by lysyl hydroxylase → hydroxylysine, also requires Vitamin C). Hydroxyproline forms additional H-bonds that stabilize the triple helix. Hydroxylysine serves as attachment sites for carbohydrates and cross-links.
  • α-Helix: No specific post-translational modifications are required for helix formation (though the protein as a whole may have modifications elsewhere).

(d) Biological function:

  • Collagen triple helix: Structural — provides tensile strength in connective tissues (tendons, ligaments, skin, bone, blood vessels).
  • α-Helix: Versatile — found in both structural proteins (α-keratin in hair, where two α-helices form a coiled-coil) and globular proteins (hemoglobin, myoglobin, transcription factors where α-helices act as DNA-binding motifs).

Answer 6

The prion hypothesis proposes that the infectious agent in transmissible spongiform encephalopathies is a misfolded protein (PrP^Sc) that can transmit its abnormal conformation to normal cellular prion protein (PrP^C), converting it to the disease-associated form — all without any nucleic acid.

How a protein can be infectious without nucleic acid:

  1. PrP^Sc acts as a conformational template — when it contacts PrP^C, it induces PrP^C to refold from its normal α-helix-rich conformation into the β-sheet-rich PrP^Sc conformation.
  2. The newly converted PrP^Sc molecules can then template more conversions — a self-amplifying cascade.
  3. This is analogous to crystallization: once a seed crystal (PrP^Sc) is present, it can propagate its structure through the solution (PrP^C pool).
  4. No genetic material is needed because the "information" being transmitted is conformational, not genetic.

Why prion diseases are so difficult to treat:

  • PrP^Sc is extremely stable and resistant to proteases — it accumulates rather than being degraded.
  • The aggregates are insoluble and physically disrupt tissue.
  • Prions resist standard sterilization (UV, nucleases don't work because there is no nucleic acid).
  • The normal PrP^C is a host protein, so drugs targeting PrP^Sc may also affect PrP^C's normal (but poorly understood) function.
  • By the time symptoms appear, significant brain damage has already occurred.
  • No effective treatments currently exist to reverse prion aggregation.

14. Common Misconceptions

Misconception Reality
"Quaternary structure means 4 subunits" "Quaternary" refers to the level of structure, not the number. A dimer has quaternary structure. The word means "fourth" (fourth level, after primary, secondary, tertiary).
"Anfinsen proved all proteins can refold spontaneously" He showed it for one small protein (RNase A). Many proteins, especially large ones, require chaperones and do NOT refold after denaturation.
"Chaperones specify protein structure" Chaperones prevent aggregation and provide an environment for folding. The folding information is in the amino acid sequence. Chaperones are like a quiet room for studying — they don't tell you what to learn.
"Denaturation always breaks peptide bonds" Denaturation disrupts non-covalent interactions (2°, 3°, and 4° structure). Peptide bonds (1° structure) remain intact. Hydrolysis is required to break peptide bonds.
"All prion diseases are infectious" Some are sporadic (random misfolding), some are inherited (mutations in the PrP gene), and some are acquired (infectious transmission). Only the acquired forms are truly "contagious" in the traditional sense.
"Fibrous proteins have no tertiary structure" They do — the triple helix of collagen and coiled-coil of keratin are tertiary (and quaternary) structures. The difference is that fibrous proteins have a repeating, extended structure rather than a compact globular one.
