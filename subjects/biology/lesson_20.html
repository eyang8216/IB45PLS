Part 1: Gas Exchange in Plants

Plants face a unique challenge: they need CO₂ for photosynthesis (which produces O₂ as a waste product) but also need O₂ for respiration (which produces CO₂ as a waste product). During the day, photosynthesis typically outpaces respiration, so there's a net uptake of CO₂ and net release of O₂. At night, only respiration occurs — net O₂ uptake and net CO₂ release.

But unlike animals, plants have no specialized gas transport system (no blood, no heart). Instead, gas exchange happens directly at the cellular level through diffusion, facilitated by two key structures: stomata and the spongy mesophyll.

Stomata — The Controllable Pores

Stomata (singular: stoma) are microscopic pores found primarily on the underside (abaxial surface) of leaves. A typical leaf may have 100-300 stomata per mm² — on a large leaf, that's millions of pores.

Why on the underside? The lower leaf surface is shaded from direct sunlight, which reduces water loss by evaporation. It's also protected from dust and debris.

Each stoma is surrounded by two specialized cells called guard cells. Unlike the other epidermal cells of the leaf, guard cells contain chloroplasts, though they're not primarily photosynthetic — the chloroplasts are involved in energy production for ion pumping (and possibly blue-light sensing).

The structure:

  • Guard cells are kidney-shaped (in dicots) or dumbbell-shaped (in monocots like grasses)
  • Their cell walls are unevenly thickened: the inner wall (facing the pore) is thick and rigid; the outer wall is thin and flexible
  • When guard cells become turgid (swollen with water), they bow outward, and the pore opens
  • When guard cells lose turgor (become flaccid), they straighten, and the pore closes

Mechanism of Stomatal Opening and Closing

This is a beautiful example of how ion transport, osmosis, and cell mechanics work together:

Opening (triggered by light — specifically blue light):

  1. Blue light is detected by phototropin receptors in guard cell membranes.
  2. Proton pumps (H⁺-ATPases) are activated, pumping H⁺ ions out of the guard cells. This creates an electrochemical gradient.
  3. K⁺ channels open and K⁺ ions flow into the guard cells down the electrochemical gradient created by H⁺ pumping. (This is secondary active transport — the gradient for K⁺ entry was created by the active pumping of H⁺.)
  4. Chloride ions (Cl⁻) and malate²⁻ (produced from starch breakdown inside guard cells) also accumulate, balancing the positive charge of K⁺.
  5. The solute concentration inside guard cells increases dramatically, making the water potential very negative (hypertonic relative to surrounding epidermal cells).
  6. Water enters by osmosis from surrounding cells into the guard cells.
  7. Guard cells swell and become turgid. Because the inner walls are thick and the outer walls are thin, the cells curve outward, opening the stomatal pore.

Closing (triggered by darkness, water stress, high CO₂, or the hormone abscisic acid/ABA):

  1. K⁺ channels close; K⁺ may be pumped out.
  2. Solute concentration inside guard cells decreases.
  3. Water leaves by osmosis — guard cells lose turgor.
  4. Guard cells become flaccid and straighten, closing the pore.

Abscisic acid (ABA): During drought stress, roots produce ABA which is transported to leaves. ABA triggers Ca²⁺ influx into guard cells, which causes K⁺ efflux and stomatal closure — a critical water-conservation mechanism.

Why stomata close at night: At night, photosynthesis cannot occur (no light), so there's no need for CO₂ uptake. Keeping stomata closed minimizes unnecessary water loss through transpiration. The plant balances two competing needs: CO₂ acquisition (requires open stomata) vs. water conservation (requires closed stomata).

Spongy Mesophyll — The Gas Exchange Surface

Beneath the upper epidermis and palisade mesophyll (tightly packed columnar cells for light capture) lies the spongy mesophyll. This tissue is perfectly adapted for gas exchange:

  • Loosely packed cells with abundant air spaces between them — creating a large internal surface area for diffusion. This is the plant equivalent of the alveoli in your lungs.
  • The air spaces connect to each other and to the stomata, forming a continuous network through which gases can diffuse to every photosynthetic cell.
  • The cells of the spongy mesophyll are coated with a thin film of moisture, allowing gases to dissolve before entering cells (remember: all gas exchange surfaces must be moist).

Gas exchange pathway in a leaf:

  1. CO₂ diffuses through open stomata from the atmosphere into the leaf
  2. CO₂ diffuses through the intercellular air spaces of the spongy mesophyll
  3. CO₂ dissolves in the moist cell wall and diffuses into photosynthetic cells (palisade and spongy mesophyll)
  4. O₂ produced by photosynthesis follows the reverse path

Since the diffusion path in a leaf is very short (leaf thickness is typically 0.1–1 mm), simple diffusion is adequate — no ventilation mechanism is needed.

CAM Plants — A Clever Temporal Adaptation

Plants in arid environments (cacti, succulents, pineapple) face a dilemma: open stomata during the day for CO₂ and lose precious water, or close them and starve. Their solution: Crassulacean Acid Metabolism (CAM).

CAM plants open their stomata at night, take in CO₂, and fix it into malic acid (stored in vacuoles). During the day, they close their stomata and release the CO₂ from malic acid for use in the Calvin cycle. This temporal separation allows CO₂ uptake when evaporative demand is lowest (cool, humid night).

Part 2: Gas Exchange in Insects

Insects solved the gas exchange problem differently from vertebrates. Instead of a circulatory system that carries gases, they developed a tracheal system — a network of air-filled tubes that delivers oxygen directly to every cell in the body.

The Tracheal System

Spiracles: Openings along the sides of the insect's body (thorax and abdomen). Each spiracle is equipped with valves that can open and close — this is important because open spiracles lose water vapor. Insects can control spiracle opening to balance O₂ demand with water conservation.

Tracheae (singular: trachea): The spiracles open into larger tubes called tracheae. These are reinforced with chitin rings (spiral thickenings called taenidia) that prevent the tubes from collapsing under pressure — functionally analogous to the cartilage rings in your trachea. Chitin is the same tough polysaccharide that forms the insect's exoskeleton.

Tracheoles: The tracheae branch repeatedly into smaller and smaller tubes, ultimately forming tracheoles — microscopic tubes (diameter <1 μm) that penetrate between and even into individual cells. The tips of tracheoles are filled with fluid (not air), and this fluid is where gases dissolve before diffusing across the tracheole wall into cells.

How the Tracheal System Meets Fick's Law

  • Large surface area: The tracheoles form an incredibly extensive network, providing enormous total surface area for gas exchange — they contact virtually every cell.
  • Thin: Tracheole walls are a single layer of epithelial cells — extremely thin.
  • Moist: Fluid at the tracheole tips provides the moist medium for gas dissolution.
  • Short diffusion distance: Because tracheoles penetrate deep into tissues, no cell is more than a few micrometers from a tracheole. Oxygen doesn't need a blood transport system — it travels through air (where it diffuses ~10,000× faster than in water) almost all the way.
  • Steep concentration gradient: Respiring cells consume O₂ → low intracellular pO₂ → gradient maintained.

Ventilation in Insects

While diffusion alone suffices in small insects, larger and more active insects (like flying locusts or bees) use mechanical ventilation:

  1. Abdominal pumping: Rhythmic contraction and relaxation of abdominal muscles changes the volume of the body cavity, forcing air in and out of the tracheal system — analogous to your diaphragm.
  2. Air sacs: Some insects have expandable air sacs within the tracheal system that act as bellows, increasing the volume of air moved per pumping cycle.
  3. Thoracic pumping during flight: The flight muscles themselves, contracting at high frequency, compress and expand parts of the tracheal system.

During flight, insect metabolic rates can increase 50-100× above resting — ventilation is essential to meet this demand.

Limitation

The tracheal system imposes a size limit on insects. Beyond a certain body size, diffusion through tracheoles becomes too slow to reach central cells, even with ventilation. This is one reason why insects are relatively small (and why giant insects existed in the Carboniferous period when atmospheric O₂ was ~35% compared to today's 21% — higher pO₂ allowed larger body sizes).

Part 3: Gas Exchange in Fish — Gills and Countercurrent Exchange

Fish face the hardest gas exchange challenge of all: extracting O₂ from water. Water contains about 30× less oxygen than air (air is ~21% O₂; water typically holds 0.5-1% dissolved O₂), and O₂ diffuses 10,000× slower in water than in air. Fish need an exceptionally efficient system.

Gill Structure

Fish gills are located in the pharyngeal cavity, protected by a bony flap called the operculum (in bony fish; sharks have separate gill slits). The structure is hierarchical:

Gill Arch: The bony or cartilaginous support structure. There are typically 4 pairs of gill arches. The gill arch also contains the blood vessels supplying and draining the gills.

Gill Filaments (Primary Lamellae): Each gill arch bears two rows of gill filaments — long, thin structures that project into the water flow. There may be hundreds of filaments per arch. Each filament has its own blood supply (afferent and efferent filamental arteries).

Secondary Lamellae (Gill Plates): These are the functional gas exchange units. Each filament is lined on both sides with hundreds of tiny, flat, plate-like secondary lamellae — arranged perpendicular to the filament axis, like the teeth of a comb. The secondary lamellae are:

  • Extremely thin: The barrier between water and blood is just two cell layers (gill epithelium + capillary endothelium), about 1-5 μm thick
  • Richly perfused: Each lamella contains a dense capillary network
  • Massive total surface area: The combined SA of all secondary lamellae can be 10-60× the fish's body surface area

Ventilation of the Gills — The Buccal-Opercular Pump

Fish ventilate their gills by a two-phase pumping mechanism:

  1. Mouth opens, operculum closes, buccal floor lowers → volume of buccal (mouth) cavity increases → pressure drops → water is drawn in.
  2. Mouth closes, operculum opens, buccal floor raises → volume decreases → pressure increases → water is forced across the gills and out through the opercular opening.

This creates a one-way flow of water across the gills — unlike the tidal (in-and-out) ventilation of our lungs, which is much more efficient because fresh water constantly flows across the exchange surface.

Countercurrent Exchange — The Masterpiece of Gill Design

This is one of the most elegant physiological mechanisms in all of biology. To understand why it's so brilliant, let's first consider the alternative.

Concurrent flow: Imagine water and blood flowing in the same direction. At the start, water has high pO₂ and blood has low pO₂ — a large gradient, O₂ diffuses rapidly. But as they flow together, blood gains O₂ and water loses O₂. The gradient shrinks, and by the midpoint, pO₂ in both fluids is nearly equal. Equilibrium is reached at about 50% of water's original pO₂, and no further net diffusion occurs. The fish can only extract about 50% of the available O₂ from the water.

Countercurrent flow: Blood flows through the lamellae in the opposite direction to water flow. Here's the magic:

  • At the water exit end (where blood enters the gill), even though the incoming blood has the lowest pO₂, it encounters water that has already given up most of its O₂. But crucially, the water's pO₂ is still slightly higher than the incoming blood's pO₂ — so O₂ diffuses from water to blood.
  • At every point along the lamella, the water's pO₂ is slightly higher than the blood's pO₂ — the concentration gradient is maintained along the entire length of the exchange surface.
  • At the water entry end (where blood leaves the gill), blood with nearly full O₂ saturation encounters fresh, high-pO₂ water — the steepest gradient of all, driving the final bit of O₂ into the blood.

The result: Countercurrent exchange can extract up to 80-90% of the dissolved O₂ from water, compared to only about 50% with concurrent flow.

Why Countercurrent Is More Efficient — The Key Insight

With concurrent flow, the maximum O₂ the blood can achieve equals the O₂ concentration of the exiting water. Since they equilibrate at about 50%, that's the best you can do.

With countercurrent flow, blood leaving the gills equilibrates with incoming water (the freshest, highest-O₂ water) — it can achieve nearly the same pO₂ as inspired water. Meanwhile, the water that exits has equilibrated with the incoming blood (the lowest-O₂ blood) — meaning the fish has stripped almost all the oxygen from the water.

Part 4: Oxygen Dissociation Curves and Hemoglobin

The Problem: Oxygen Is Poorly Soluble in Water

At body temperature, 1 liter of blood plasma can dissolve only about 3 mL of O₂ (when equilibrated with air). But resting human O₂ consumption is about 250 mL/min — if we relied only on dissolved O₂, our hearts would need to pump over 80 liters of blood per minute. That's impossible.

The solution: hemoglobin (Hb) — the oxygen-carrying protein packed inside red blood cells (erythrocytes). With hemoglobin, 1 liter of blood carries about 200 mL of O₂ — over 60× more than plasma alone.

Hemoglobin Structure

Hemoglobin is a quaternary structure protein composed of:

  • 4 polypeptide subunits: 2 alpha (α) chains and 2 beta (β) chains (in adult hemoglobin, HbA)
  • 4 heme groups: One nestled in each subunit. A heme group consists of a porphyrin ring (a flat, cyclic organic structure) with an iron atom (Fe²⁺) at its center. The Fe²⁺ is what actually binds O₂ — one O₂ molecule per heme, so each hemoglobin molecule can carry up to 4 O₂ molecules.

The Fe²⁺ must remain in its reduced (ferrous) state to bind O₂. If it oxidizes to Fe³⁺ (ferric), it forms methemoglobin, which cannot bind O₂. Red blood cells contain methemoglobin reductase to prevent this.

Cooperative Binding — The Key to the Sigmoidal Curve

Hemoglobin does not bind O₂ independently at each heme site. Instead, it displays cooperative binding:

  • Deoxyhemoglobin (Hb with no O₂ bound) is in a "tense" (T) state — the four subunits are in a conformation that has relatively low affinity for O₂. The first O₂ molecule has difficulty binding.
  • Binding of the first O₂ triggers a conformational change — the hemoglobin molecule shifts toward the "relaxed" (R) state. This conformational change is transmitted to the other subunits, making it easier for the second and third O₂ molecules to bind.
  • The fourth O₂ binds most easily — affinity is highest when three O₂ are already bound.

This cooperative behavior produces the characteristic sigmoidal (S-shaped) oxygen dissociation curve:

  • At very low pO₂ (0-2 kPa): hemoglobin has low affinity, curve is shallow. This is the T state.
  • At moderate pO₂ (2-6 kPa): As the first O₂ binds and the switch to R state begins, affinity increases sharply — the steep part of the curve. This is where hemoglobin is most responsive — small changes in pO₂ cause large changes in saturation.
  • At high pO₂ (8-13 kPa): Hemoglobin approaches saturation, curve flattens. Most binding sites are occupied.

Why the sigmoidal shape matters physiologically:

  • Loading in the lungs: pO₂ in alveolar air is ~13 kPa. The curve is at its plateau here, meaning hemoglobin is ~97-98% saturated whether pO₂ is 13 or 11 kPa. This provides a safety margin — even at high altitude or with mild lung disease, Hb loads well in the lungs.
  • Unloading in the tissues: Active tissues have pO₂ around 3-5 kPa. This falls on the steep part of the curve. A small drop in pO₂ causes a large release of O₂ — exactly what respiring tissues need. Hemoglobin unloads more O₂ where it's needed most.

The Bohr Effect — pH and CO₂ Shift the Curve

Discovered by Christian Bohr (father of physicist Niels Bohr) in 1904, the Bohr effect describes how increased CO₂ and/or decreased pH reduce hemoglobin's affinity for O₂, shifting the curve to the right.

Mechanism:

  • CO₂ produced by respiring tissues reacts with water in red blood cells: CO₂ + H₂O → H₂CO₃ (carbonic acid) → H⁺ + HCO₃⁻ (bicarbonate). This reaction is catalyzed by carbonic anhydrase.
  • The increased H⁺ (lower pH) binds to specific amino acid residues on hemoglobin, stabilizing the T (low-affinity) state.
  • Additionally, CO₂ can directly bind to the N-terminal amino groups of hemoglobin to form carbaminohemoglobin, which also stabilizes the T state.

Result: In active tissues (high CO₂, low pH), hemoglobin releases O₂ more readily — exactly where O₂ is needed most. The curve shifts right, meaning for any given pO₂, hemoglobin saturation is lower (more O₂ is unloaded).

Other Factors Shifting the Curve

Increased Temperature → Right Shift:

Active muscles generate heat. Higher temperature reduces Hb-O₂ affinity (through similar stabilization of the T state), promoting O₂ unloading. This is complementary to the Bohr effect — both enhance O₂ delivery to working tissues.

2,3-Bisphosphoglycerate (2,3-BPG) → Right Shift:

2,3-BPG (formerly called 2,3-DPG) is a metabolite produced in red blood cells during glycolysis. It binds to deoxyhemoglobin (fitting into the central cavity between the four subunits) and stabilizes the T state, reducing O₂ affinity.

Clinical significance of 2,3-BPG:

  • High altitude adaptation: At altitude, 2,3-BPG levels increase, shifting the curve right and enhancing O₂ unloading in tissues (though this makes loading in the lungs slightly harder — it's a trade-off that favors delivery).
  • Stored blood: Blood stored for transfusion loses 2,3-BPG over time, causing a left-shifted curve that doesn't unload O₂ well. This can be problematic in massive transfusions.
  • Chronic hypoxia and anemia: 2,3-BPG levels increase, improving tissue oxygenation.

Part 5: Myoglobin — The Oxygen Store

Myoglobin is an O₂-binding protein found in muscle tissue. It's structurally and functionally distinct from hemoglobin:

Structure:

  • A single polypeptide chain with a single heme group
  • Can bind only one O₂ molecule (vs. 4 for hemoglobin)
  • Structurally similar to a single hemoglobin subunit

Oxygen Dissociation Curve:

  • Hyperbolic, not sigmoidal — since there's only one binding site, there's no cooperativity. The curve is rectangular hyperbola shape.
  • Myoglobin has much higher O₂ affinity than hemoglobin — it's half-saturated at a pO₂ of only about 0.3 kPa (vs. ~3.5 kPa for hemoglobin).

Function:

  • Myoglobin acts as an O₂ store in muscle, not a transporter. It binds O₂ when pO₂ is moderate (during rest) and releases it only when pO₂ drops very low (during intense exercise when muscle cells are rapidly consuming O₂).
  • It also facilitates intracellular O₂ diffusion by providing a "bucket brigade" of myoglobin molecules that pass O₂ from the cell membrane to the mitochondria.
  • Diving mammals (seals, whales) have exceptionally high myoglobin concentrations — this is what allows them to stay submerged for extended periods. Their muscles are dark red-black from the myoglobin.

Why myoglobin doesn't replace hemoglobin: If hemoglobin had myoglobin's affinity, it would load O₂ beautifully in the lungs but refuse to release it in the tissues — useless. Conversely, if myoglobin had hemoglobin's affinity, it wouldn't hold onto O₂ long enough to serve as a store — also useless. Each protein's affinity is precisely tuned to its role.

Part 6: Fetal Hemoglobin (HbF)

A developing fetus obtains all its O₂ from the mother's blood across the placenta. This presents a challenge: O₂ must transfer from maternal hemoglobin to fetal hemoglobin. If both had identical O₂ affinity, the gradient would be small and O₂ transfer inefficient.

Solution: Fetal hemoglobin (HbF) has a higher O₂ affinity than adult hemoglobin (HbA). Its dissociation curve is left-shifted relative to HbA.

Structural basis:

  • HbF consists of 2 alpha chains and 2 gamma chains (α₂γ₂), instead of the 2 alpha and 2 beta chains (α₂β₂) of adult HbA.
  • The gamma chains differ from beta chains in several amino acids. Critically, HbF binds 2,3-BPG less strongly than HbA. Since 2,3-BPG reduces O₂ affinity (stabilizes T state), its reduced binding to HbF means HbF is less inhibited — it stays in a higher-affinity state.

Physiological result:

At the placenta, maternal blood has a pO₂ of about 4-5 kPa (in the intervillous space). At this pO₂:

  • Adult Hb (mother) is about 50-60% saturated, so it readily unloads O₂
  • Fetal Hb is about 70-80% saturated at the same pO₂, so it readily loads O₂

The difference in affinity creates a concentration gradient that drives O₂ from maternal to fetal blood across the placenta. After birth, HbF is gradually replaced by HbA over the first few months of life (the gamma→beta globin switch).

IB Exam Tip: When comparing dissociation curves, always state: (1) which is left/right shifted, (2) what that means for O₂ affinity (left = higher affinity), and (3) the physiological significance (where O₂ is loaded vs. unloaded). Many students lose marks by just saying "the curve shifts."

Part 7: Carbon Dioxide Transport

Although the focus of this lesson is O₂, a brief note on CO₂ transport ties together the Bohr effect:

CO₂ is transported in blood in three forms:

  1. Dissolved in plasma: ~7-10%
  2. As bicarbonate ions (HCO₃⁻) in plasma: ~70% — CO₂ + H₂O → H₂CO₃ → H⁺ + HCO₃⁻ (in RBCs, catalyzed by carbonic anhydrase). HCO₃⁻ exits the RBC in exchange for Cl⁻ (the chloride shift).
  3. As carbaminohemoglobin: ~20-23% — CO₂ binds to amino groups of hemoglobin (different site from O₂ binding).

In the lungs, these reactions reverse: HCO₃⁻ re-enters RBCs, combines with H⁺ to re-form CO₂ and H₂O, and CO₂ diffuses out into alveolar air.

Problem 1: Countercurrent Exchange

Explain why countercurrent exchange in fish gills is more efficient than concurrent flow. Use approximate percentage values to illustrate your point.

Click for Answer

In concurrent flow, blood and water flow in the same direction. At the start, there is a large pO₂ gradient and O₂ diffuses rapidly. However, as they flow together, blood gains O₂ and water loses O₂. The gradient diminishes, and by about the midpoint, pO₂ is nearly equal in both fluids. Equilibrium is reached at about 50% of incoming water pO₂ — no further net diffusion occurs. The fish can extract only ~50% of available O₂.

In countercurrent flow, blood and water flow in opposite directions. Blood entering the gill (low pO₂) encounters water that is about to exit (which has already lost most of its O₂, but still has slightly higher pO₂ than the incoming blood). At every point along the lamella, water pO₂ > blood pO₂ — the gradient is maintained along the entire length of the exchange surface. Blood leaving the gill encounters fresh incoming water at its highest pO₂. As a result, 80-90% of dissolved O₂ can be extracted.

The key insight: with countercurrent flow, the blood leaving the gill equilibrates (nearly) with incoming water (high pO₂), and the water leaving the gill equilibrates with incoming blood (low pO₂). This maximizes extraction.

Problem 2: Stomatal Mechanism

Describe the process by which guard cells open stomata in response to light, explaining the role of K⁺ ions and osmosis.

Click for Answer

  1. Blue light is detected by phototropin receptors in guard cell plasma membranes.
  2. H⁺-ATPase pumps are activated, actively transporting H⁺ ions out of the guard cells. This creates an electrochemical gradient (inside of cell becomes more negative, and H⁺ concentration is lower inside).
  3. K⁺ channels open — K⁺ diffuses into guard cells down the electrochemical gradient (the negative interior attracts K⁺, and the outward H⁺ gradient energizes K⁺ influx via symport or channel-mediated facilitated diffusion).
  4. Cl⁻ and malate²⁻ also enter/accumulate to balance the positive charge. Malate²⁻ is produced from starch breakdown within guard cells.
  5. The solute concentration inside guard cells increases significantly → water potential becomes more negative (lower) than surrounding epidermal cells.
  6. Water enters by osmosis from surrounding cells into guard cells.
  7. Guard cells swell and become turgid. Due to uneven cell wall thickening (thick inner wall, thin outer wall), they curve outward, opening the stomatal pore.

Closing: K⁺ exits, water follows by osmosis, guard cells lose turgor and the pore closes. Abscisic acid (ABA), produced during water stress, triggers stomatal closure.

Problem 3: Hemoglobin Cooperativity

Explain why the oxygen dissociation curve of hemoglobin is sigmoidal while that of myoglobin is hyperbolic. Relate this to their respective functions.

Click for Answer

Hemoglobin:

  • Has 4 subunits, each with a heme group. Binding is cooperative: binding of the first O₂ induces a conformational change (T→R transition) that increases the affinity of remaining subunits. Successive O₂ molecules bind more easily.
  • This produces a sigmoidal (S-shaped) curve.
  • Functional significance: The steep portion (2-7 kPa) allows hemoglobin to be highly responsive to small pO₂ changes — it loads well in lungs (plateau region at high pO₂) and unloads well in tissues (steep region at moderate pO₂). The sigmoid shape maximizes the difference between % saturation at loading vs. unloading sites.

Myoglobin:

  • Has a single polypeptide with one heme group. With only one binding site, there can be no cooperativity.
  • Produces a hyperbolic curve — simple saturation kinetics, like a rectangular hyperbola.
  • Functional significance: Myoglobin has very high O₂ affinity (half-saturated at ~0.3 kPa), so it binds O₂ tightly at moderate pO₂ and releases it only when pO₂ drops very low (intense exercise). This makes it an excellent O₂ store in muscle, not a transporter. Its hyperbolic curve means it remains saturated over a wide pO₂ range and only unloads under extreme conditions.

Problem 4: Bohr Effect

During a 100-meter sprint, an athlete's leg muscles produce large amounts of CO₂ and lactic acid. Explain how these changes affect O₂ delivery to the muscles, referring to the oxygen dissociation curve of hemoglobin.

Click for Answer

The combination of ↑CO₂ and ↓pH (from lactic acid) triggers the Bohr effect:

  1. CO₂ produced by respiring muscles enters red blood cells. Carbonic anhydrase catalyzes: CO₂ + H₂O → H₂CO₃ → H⁺ + HCO₃⁻. This increases H⁺ concentration (lowers pH).
  2. Lactic acid from anaerobic respiration dissociates, further lowering pH.
  3. H⁺ binds to hemoglobin, stabilizing the T (tense, low-affinity) state. CO₂ also binds directly to hemoglobin as carbaminohemoglobin, further stabilizing the T state.
  4. The oxygen dissociation curve is shifted to the right — at any given pO₂, hemoglobin has lower O₂ affinity and is less saturated.

Result: In the exercising muscle (pO₂ ~3-4 kPa), hemoglobin releases more O₂ than it would at resting pH. This is a beautiful physiological adaptation — the very metabolites produced by active respiration (CO₂, H⁺) signal hemoglobin to deliver more O₂ precisely where it's needed.

Additional factor: Increased temperature in exercising muscle also shifts the curve right, contributing to enhanced O₂ unloading. Together, the Bohr effect plus temperature effect can increase O₂ delivery to working muscle by 2-3×.

Problem 5: Fetal Hemoglobin

Explain how fetal hemoglobin (HbF) facilitates oxygen transfer from mother to fetus across the placenta.

Click for Answer

Fetal hemoglobin (HbF, α₂γ₂) differs structurally from adult hemoglobin (HbA, α₂β₂) — it has gamma chains instead of beta chains. This structural difference has two key consequences:

  1. HbF binds 2,3-BPG less strongly than HbA. Since 2,3-BPG stabilizes the T (low-affinity) state, reduced 2,3-BPG binding means HbF remains in a higher-affinity state.
  2. HbF's oxygen dissociation curve is left-shifted relative to HbA.

Physiological effect at the placenta:

At the pO₂ in the placental intervillous space (~4-5 kPa):

  • Maternal HbA is about 50-60% saturated — it readily unloads O₂
  • Fetal HbF is about 70-80% saturated at the same pO₂ — it readily loads O₂

The difference in affinity creates a concentration gradient that favors O₂ transfer from maternal blood to fetal blood. Fetal Hb "pulls" O₂ off maternal Hb.

This is crucial because the pO₂ at the placenta is relatively low — if fetal and maternal Hb had identical affinities, insufficient O₂ would transfer to meet fetal demand.

After birth, HbF is gradually replaced by HbA (gamma→beta globin switch), which is complete by about 6 months of age.

Key Takeaways

  • Plants: Stomata (controlled by guard cell turgor via K⁺/osmosis) and spongy mesophyll provide large SA for gas exchange. CAM plants temporally separate CO₂ uptake and fixation.
  • Insects: Tracheal system — spiracles → tracheae (chitin rings) → tracheoles (fluid-filled tips, directly contact cells). Diffusion-based, aided by ventilation in larger insects.
  • Fish: Countercurrent exchange in gill lamellae maintains pO₂ gradient along entire exchange surface → 80-90% O₂ extraction efficiency (vs. ~50% for concurrent).
  • Hemoglobin: 4 subunits, cooperative binding → sigmoidal dissociation curve. Loads O₂ in lungs, unloads in tissues.
  • Bohr effect: ↑CO₂/↓pH/↑Temp/↑2,3-BPG → right shift → more O₂ released in active tissues.
  • Myoglobin: Single subunit, hyperbolic curve, high affinity — O₂ store in muscle.
  • Fetal Hb: Left-shifted curve (higher affinity than adult Hb) → transfers O₂ from mother to fetus across placenta.
