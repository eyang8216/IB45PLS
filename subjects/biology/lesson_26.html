1. Michaelis-Menten Kinetics

The Foundational Equation

In 1913, Leonor Michaelis and Maud Menten derived an equation describing how reaction rate depends on substrate concentration for a simple enzyme-catalyzed reaction. Their model assumes:

$$E + S \underset{k_{-1}}{\stackrel{k_1}{\rightleftharpoons}} ES \xrightarrow{k_{cat}} E + P$$

The key assumption: the ES complex reaches a steady state — its concentration remains constant because it forms as fast as it breaks down. This leads to:

$$v = \frac{V_{max}[S]}{K_m + [S]}$$

Where:

  • v = initial reaction rate (velocity) at given [S]
  • Vmax = maximum possible rate (all active sites saturated)
  • Km = Michaelis constant = [S] at which v = Vmax/2
  • [S] = substrate concentration

The Rate vs [S] Curve

Draw and label this graph:

```

Rate (v)

^

└──────────────────────────────────────────────→ [S]

Shape: RECTANGULAR HYPERBOLA

```

Three Kinetic Regimes

Regime [S] relative to Km Rate behavior Kinetic order
Low [S] [S] ≪ Km v ≈ (Vmax/Km)[S] — rate proportional to [S] First-order
[S] = Km [S] = Km v = Vmax/2 —
High [S] [S] ≫ Km v ≈ Vmax — rate independent of [S] Zero-order

When [S] ≪ Km: The denominator ≈ Km (since [S] is negligible). So v = Vmax[S]/Km, meaning rate increases linearly with [S]. Adding more substrate directly increases rate.

When [S] = Km: v = Vmax × Km / (Km + Km) = Vmax/2. This is the definition of Km.

When [S] ≫ Km: The denominator ≈ [S]. So v = Vmax[S]/[S] = Vmax. Rate plateaus.

Km (Michaelis constant): The substrate concentration at which the reaction rate is exactly half of Vmax. Km has units of concentration (typically μM or mM). Crucially, Km is an inverse measure of enzyme-substrate affinity — a low Km means the enzyme reaches half Vmax at low [S], indicating high affinity for the substrate. A high Km means weak binding; more substrate is needed.

What Km Actually Tells You

For many enzymes, Km approximates the dissociation constant of the ES complex (especially when kcat ≪ k₋₁):

$$K_m \approx \frac{k_{-1}}{k_1} = K_d$$

Analogy: Think of Km as the "stickiness" of an enzyme for its substrate:

  • Low Km (~μM or nM): The enzyme grabs substrate tightly, like a magnet. Even at low concentrations, most enzymes are busy. Example: hexokinase has Km ~0.1 mM for glucose — it works efficiently even when glucose is scarce.
  • High Km (~mM): The enzyme needs a lot of substrate to work efficiently, like a weak handshake. Example: glucokinase has Km ~10 mM for glucose — it only works when glucose is abundant (after a meal).

The Three Meanings of Vmax

Vmax is the theoretical maximum rate when every enzyme molecule is occupied by substrate. It depends on:

$$V_{max} = k_{cat}[E]_{total}$$

Where kcat (turnover number) is the number of substrate molecules converted to product per enzyme active site per second when saturated.

Enzyme kcat (s⁻¹) Meaning
Carbonic anhydrase 600,000 One of the fastest enzymes known; converts 600K CO₂ per second
Catalase 40,000,000 Turnover number so high it's nearly diffusion-limited
Lysozyme 0.5 Moderate; "slow" enzyme

IB Exam Tip (Paper 1 & 2): You don't need to memorize the Michaelis-Menten equation, but you MUST understand what Km and Vmax mean, how to read the hyperbolic curve, and the relationship: Km = [S] at half Vmax. This is a favorite HL question — especially interpreting what a low vs high Km means for enzyme affinity.

2. The Lineweaver-Burk Plot (Double Reciprocal)

Why Transform the Data?

The Michaelis-Menten curve is hyperbolic, making it difficult to accurately determine Vmax (it's an asymptote — you never quite reach it) and Km (must find exactly Vmax/2 on a curve).

Hans Lineweaver and Dean Burk devised a linear transformation by taking the reciprocals of both sides of the Michaelis-Menten equation:

$$\frac{1}{v} = \frac{K_m}{V_{max}} \cdot \frac{1}{[S]} + \frac{1}{V_{max}}$$

This has the form y = mx + c (a straight line!):

  • y = 1/v
  • x = 1/[S]
  • slope (m) = Km/Vmax
  • y-intercept (c) = 1/Vmax
  • x-intercept = −1/Km

Drawing the Lineweaver-Burk Plot

```
= −1/Km = 1/Vmax

```

Key features to label:

  1. y-intercept = 1/Vmax — the point where the line crosses the y-axis (1/[S] = 0, meaning infinite [S])
  2. x-intercept = −1/Km — where the line crosses the x-axis (1/v = 0, meaning infinite rate); this is NEGATIVE — extend the line leftwards
  3. Slope = Km/Vmax — steeper slope means higher Km/Vmax ratio

How to Construct a Lineweaver-Burk Plot

Worked Example: From Data to L-B Plot

A student measures initial rate at various substrate concentrations:

[S] (mM) 1/[S] (mM⁻¹) v (μmol/min) 1/v (min/μmol)
0.5 2.000 2.0 0.500
1.0 1.000 3.3 0.303
2.0 0.500 5.0 0.200
5.0 0.200 7.1 0.141
10.0 0.100 8.3 0.120

Plot 1/v (y-axis) vs 1/[S] (x-axis). The data should form a straight line.

From the graph:

  • y-intercept ≈ 0.100 → Vmax = 1/0.100 = 10.0 μmol/min
  • x-intercept ≈ −0.50 → Km = −1/(−0.50) = 2.0 mM

Or using slope: slope = (0.303 − 0.120)/(1.000 − 0.100) = 0.183/0.900 = 0.203

Km = slope × Vmax = 0.203 × 10.0 = 2.03 mM ✓

4. Designing Experiments to Determine Km and Vmax

Experimental Design

To determine Km and Vmax for an enzyme:

  1. Fix enzyme concentration — use the same amount of enzyme in every tube
  2. Vary substrate concentration — use at least 6-8 different [S] values, spanning approximately 0.2×Km to 5×Km (you may not know Km beforehand, so go from very low to very high)
  3. Control other variables — constant temperature (water bath), constant pH (buffer), constant ionic strength
  4. Measure initial rate (v₀) at each [S]:
  • Take measurements early (first 10% of reaction)
  • Measure product appearance or substrate disappearance
  • Plot [Product] vs Time; slope of initial linear portion = v₀
  1. Plot v vs [S] → estimate Vmax and Km from hyperbolic curve
  2. Plot 1/v vs 1/[S] → Lineweaver-Burk → more accurate determination

Practical Considerations

Issue Solution
Cannot reach saturation Substrate may have limited solubility; sometimes impossible to reach true Vmax. L-B extrapolation helps.
Substrate inhibition At very high [S], some enzymes are inhibited. Avoid these concentrations.
Product inhibition Only measure initial rate, before product accumulates.
Temperature fluctuations Use thermostatted water bath (±0.1°C)
pH drift Use buffer at optimal pH; verify buffer capacity is adequate

Statistical Note

Modern practice uses non-linear regression (fitting the Michaelis-Menten equation directly to v vs [S] data) rather than Lineweaver-Burk, because:

  • L-B plots distort error structure (small v values get disproportionately large 1/v errors)
  • Points at low [S] (high 1/[S]) have excessive leverage on the regression

However, L-B plots remain excellent for visualizing inhibitor effects, which is why IB emphasizes them.

5. Mechanism-Based (Suicide) Inhibitors

What Makes Them "Suicide"?

Mechanism-based inhibitors are a special class of irreversible inhibitors. They enter the active site like a normal substrate and the enzyme begins to catalyze a reaction — but instead of producing a normal product, the enzyme converts the inhibitor into a highly reactive intermediate that forms a covalent bond with the enzyme, permanently inactivating it.

Analogy: A spy (inhibitor) infiltrates an enemy base (active site) by pretending to be a friend (substrate). Once inside, the spy detonates a bomb (forms covalent bond), destroying both the base and himself. The enzyme is "tricked" into catalyzing its own destruction.

The General Mechanism

```

Step 1: E + I ⇌ E·I (inhibitor binds like substrate)

Step 2: E·I → E-I (covalent) (enzyme converts inhibitor to reactive species

that permanently attaches)

Step 3: Enzyme is dead (irreversibly inactivated)

```

Unlike competitive or non-competitive inhibitors, suicide inhibitors permanently inactivate the enzyme. Recovery requires synthesis of new enzyme molecules.

Penicillin: The Classic Example

Penicillin is arguably the most famous mechanism-based inhibitor in biochemistry and medicine.

The target enzyme: Transpeptidase (also called penicillin-binding protein, PBP) — a bacterial enzyme that cross-links peptidoglycan strands in the bacterial cell wall.

How it works:

  1. Structural mimicry: Penicillin contains a β-lactam ring that mimics the D-Ala-D-Ala dipeptide tail of peptidoglycan — the normal substrate of transpeptidase.
  1. Binding: Penicillin enters the active site of transpeptidase. The enzyme recognizes it as a substrate.
  1. Suicide: The enzyme's catalytic serine residue attacks the β-lactam ring, opening it — but instead of releasing a product, the ring opening creates a covalent penicilloyl-enzyme complex. The enzyme is permanently acylated and inactivated.
  1. Consequence: Without functional transpeptidase, bacteria cannot cross-link their peptidoglycan cell wall. As bacteria grow and divide, the cell wall weakens → osmotic lysis → bacterial death.

```

Normal reaction:

Transpeptidase + D-Ala-D-Ala → cross-linked peptidoglycan + enzyme (free)

With penicillin:

Transpeptidase + Penicillin → Penicilloyl-Enzyme (COVALENT, DEAD)

```

Why penicillin is selective: Human cells don't have peptidoglycan cell walls or transpeptidase. The drug targets a structure unique to bacteria → selective toxicity.

Mechanism-based (suicide) inhibitor: An unreactive molecule that binds to an enzyme's active site and is converted by the enzyme's normal catalytic mechanism into a reactive species that covalently and irreversibly inactivates the enzyme. The enzyme participates in its own destruction.

6. Allosteric Regulation

Beyond Simple Michaelis-Menten

Many enzymes do NOT follow Michaelis-Menten kinetics. Instead, they show sigmoidal (S-shaped) rate vs [S] curves, indicating cooperativity. These are allosteric enzymes.

Allosteric enzyme: An enzyme whose activity is regulated by the reversible binding of an effector molecule at a site different from the active site (an allosteric site). Allosteric enzymes typically have quaternary structure (multiple subunits) and display cooperativity — binding of substrate to one subunit influences substrate binding to other subunits.

Sigmoidal vs Hyperbolic Kinetics

Draw and label both curves:

```

Rate (v)

^

└──────────────────────────→ [S]

```

The Molecular Basis of Cooperativity

Allosteric enzymes exist in (at least) two conformational states:

  • T-state (Tense): Low affinity for substrate; the "off" or less active conformation. More stable in the absence of substrate.
  • R-state (Relaxed): High affinity for substrate; the "on" or more active conformation. Stabilized by substrate binding.

The MWC (Monod-Wyman-Changeux) Concerted Model:

```

T-state (low affinity) R-state (high affinity)

┌──┐ ┌──┐ ┌──┐ ┌──┐

│ │ │ │ + Substrate │██│ │██│

│ │ │ │ ─────────────→ │██│ │██│

└──┘ └──┘ └──┘ └──┘

All subunits switch together (concerted transition).

At low [S]: mostly T-state → low activity.

As [S] increases: more enzymes switch to R-state → sudden activity surge.

```

How sigmoidicity emerges:

  1. At very low [S], most enzymes are in T-state → rate is low (lag phase)
  2. As [S] increases, some subunits bind substrate → shift equilibrium toward R-state
  3. Once a few enzymes are in R-state, substrate binds more readily to remaining sites → cooperative surge
  4. At high [S], all enzymes are in R-state → saturation → Vmax

Analogy: An allosteric enzyme is like a reluctant dancer at a party. At first (low music/substrate), no one dances. Then a few brave souls start → suddenly the energy shifts → everyone rushes to the dance floor (cooperative transition). The response to the "music" (substrate) is not gradual but explosive.

7. ATCase: The Model Allosteric Enzyme

Aspartate Transcarbamoylase (ATCase)

ATCase catalyzes the first committed step in pyrimidine biosynthesis (CTP, UTP — needed for RNA and DNA):

$$\text{Carbamoyl phosphate} + \text{Aspartate} \xrightarrow{ATCase} \text{N-carbamoylaspartate}$$

ATCase is the textbook example of allosteric regulation, studied extensively by John Gerhart and Howard Schachman.

Structure

ATCase has a complex quaternary structure:

  • 2 catalytic trimers (c₃ × 2 = 6 catalytic subunits): Contain the active sites where aspartate binds
  • 3 regulatory dimers (r₂ × 3 = 6 regulatory subunits): Contain the allosteric sites

Total: 12 subunits. The catalytic and regulatory subunits can be separated experimentally.

Kinetics: Sigmoidal

The rate vs [aspartate] curve for ATCase is sigmoidal, not hyperbolic. This makes metabolic sense: pyrimidines are needed in large amounts during cell division, so the pathway should be sharply "on" or "off," not gradually responsive.

Allosteric Effectors

Effector Type Binding Site Effect on Kinetics
CTP** (end product) Inhibitor** (negative effector) Regulatory subunits Shifts curve right → more aspartate needed; favors T-state
ATP** (purine) Activator** (positive effector) Regulatory subunits Shifts curve left → less aspartate needed; favors R-state

Why ATP activates: Cells need balanced pools of purines (ATP, GTP) and pyrimidines (CTP, UTP). When ATP is abundant, the cell signals "we have purines, make pyrimidines to balance." ATP binding favors the R-state → ATCase more active → more pyrimidines made.

Why CTP inhibits: CTP is the end product. High CTP means "enough pyrimidines — stop making more." CTP binding stabilizes the T-state → ATCase less active.

The Physiological Logic

```

High ATP, low CTP → ATCase activated → pyrimidine synthesis ON

High CTP → ATCase inhibited → pyrimidine synthesis OFF

```

This dual regulation ensures balanced nucleotide pools — one of the most elegant examples of metabolic homeostasis.

8. Hemoglobin as an Allosteric Protein

Hemoglobin: Enzyme or Not?

Hemoglobin is not an enzyme — it binds O₂ reversibly without chemically transforming it. However, hemoglobin is the classic example of an allosteric protein displaying cooperativity.

Structure and Cooperativity

Hemoglobin is a tetramer: α₂β₂ (two α-globin and two β-globin subunits). Each subunit contains a heme group with an Fe²⁺ that binds one O₂ molecule.

Positive cooperativity: When one subunit binds O₂, the remaining subunits bind O₂ more easily. When one subunit releases O₂, others release more easily.

The Oxygen Dissociation Curve — Sigmoidal

Draw and label:

```

% O₂ Saturation

^

└────────────────────────────────→ PO₂ (partial pressure of O₂)

```

The curve is sigmoidal because of cooperativity. Compare with myoglobin (monomeric O₂-binding protein in muscle) — its curve is hyperbolic (no cooperativity).

The Bohr Effect: H⁺ and CO₂ are allosteric effectors of hemoglobin:

  • High H⁺ (low pH) and high CO₂ → decrease O₂ affinity → curve shifts right → more O₂ released in tissues (where CO₂ and H⁺ are high)
  • Low H⁺ (high pH) and low CO₂ → increase O₂ affinity → curve shifts left → more O₂ loaded in lungs

2,3-BPG (bisphosphoglycerate): Another allosteric effector — binds to deoxyhemoglobin, stabilizing the T-state → reduces O₂ affinity. Production increases at high altitude to enhance O₂ unloading.

T ↔ R Transition in Hemoglobin

  • T-state (deoxyhemoglobin): Low O₂ affinity; stabilized by salt bridges between subunits. The iron atom is slightly out of the heme plane.
  • R-state (oxyhemoglobin): High O₂ affinity; iron moves into heme plane upon O₂ binding → pulls proximal histidine → triggers conformational changes propagated to other subunits.

The transition is concerted (MWC model): all four subunits switch between T and R states together.

9. Worked Examples

Worked Example 1: L-B Plot Analysis

An enzyme is assayed with and without an inhibitor. Lineweaver-Burk plots give:

Condition y-intercept (min/nmol) x-intercept (mM⁻¹)
No inhibitor 0.050 −0.25
+Inhibitor 0.050 −0.10

a) Identify the type of inhibitor. [1]

b) Calculate Vmax for the uninhibited reaction. [1]

c) Calculate Km with and without inhibitor. [2]

d) Explain the molecular basis for your answer to (a). [2]

Answer:

a) Competitive inhibitor. The y-intercept is identical (0.050) for both conditions — this is the hallmark of competitive inhibition on L-B plots.

b) Vmax = 1/(y-intercept) = 1/0.050 = 20 nmol/min

c)

  • Without inhibitor: x-intercept = −1/Km → −0.25 = −1/Km → Km = 1/0.25 = 4.0 mM
  • With inhibitor: x-intercept = −1/Km,app → −0.10 = −1/Km,app → Km,app = 1/0.10 = 10.0 mM

The apparent Km increased from 4.0 to 10.0 mM with inhibitor.

d) The inhibitor resembles the substrate and competes for the same active site. It binds reversibly, preventing substrate binding. Increasing [S] can overcome inhibition (at infinite [S], same Vmax is reached → same y-intercept). However, more substrate is needed to reach half Vmax → Km appears larger (x-intercept shifts).

Worked Example 2: Sigmoidal Kinetics

Enzyme X shows a sigmoidal rate vs [S] curve. Enzyme Y shows hyperbolic kinetics. Both reach the same Vmax.

a) What structural feature likely accounts for the difference? [1]

b) How would the presence of a negative allosteric effector change Enzyme X's curve? [2]

c) Why would a metabolic enzyme involved in a committed regulatory step be more likely to resemble Enzyme X than Enzyme Y? [2]

Answer:

a) Enzyme X likely has quaternary structure (multiple subunits) and displays cooperativity, where substrate binding to one subunit increases the affinity of other subunits. Enzyme Y is likely monomeric or has independently acting subunits.

b) A negative allosteric effector would shift the sigmoidal curve to the right. The enzyme would require higher [S] to achieve the same rate because the effector stabilizes the T-state (low-affinity conformation). The curve would remain sigmoidal but with increased apparent Km.

c) Committed regulatory steps benefit from switch-like behavior. A hyperbolic enzyme responds gradually to [S] — rate changes smoothly. A sigmoidal enzyme shows a sharp transition from low to high activity over a narrow [S] range, making it function more like an "on/off switch." This allows tight control: below a threshold [S], little flux through the pathway; above threshold, pathway is fully active. This prevents wasteful intermediate accumulation when substrate is scarce while allowing rapid production when needed.

Practice Problems

Question 1 (Paper 1 Style — HL)

The Lineweaver-Burk plot for an enzyme in the presence of inhibitor X shows lines with the same x-intercept but different y-intercepts compared to the uninhibited enzyme. Inhibitor X is:

A. A competitive inhibitor

B. A non-competitive inhibitor

C. An uncompetitive inhibitor

D. An allosteric activator

Answer

B. Same x-intercept (−1/Km) = Km unchanged. Different y-intercept (1/Vmax) = Vmax changed (decreased). This pattern is diagnostic for non-competitive inhibition.

Question 2 (Paper 1 Style — HL)

Penicillin is an effective antibiotic because it:

A. Competitively inhibits transpeptidase by resembling D-Ala-D-Ala

B. Non-competitively inhibits bacterial ribosomes

C. Acts as a mechanism-based inhibitor that covalently inactivates transpeptidase

D. Denatures bacterial proteins at the active site

Answer

C. Penicillin is a suicide inhibitor. It enters the transpeptidase active site (structurally mimicking D-Ala-D-Ala), but the enzyme's catalytic mechanism converts penicillin into a reactive species that forms a covalent penicilloyl-enzyme complex, irreversibly inactivating the enzyme. A is tempting but incorrect — penicillin goes beyond competition; it causes permanent covalent modification.

Question 3 (Paper 2 Style — Data Analysis)

The following data were obtained for an enzyme with and without an inhibitor:

[S] (mM) v₀, no inhibitor (μmol/min) v₀, +inhibitor (μmol/min)
1.0 2.5 1.1
2.0 4.0 2.0
4.0 5.7 3.3
8.0 7.3 5.0
16.0 8.5 7.0

a) Explain how you would determine whether this is competitive or non-competitive inhibition using these data alone (without plotting). [2]

b) Estimate Vmax in the absence of inhibitor. [1]

c) Predict the effect of doubling the enzyme concentration on both Km and Vmax. [2]

Answer

a) Look at whether the rate with inhibitor approaches the rate without inhibitor at high [S]. At 16 mM [S], v₀ without inhibitor = 8.5, and with inhibitor = 7.0. These have not converged. As [S] increases from 1 to 16 mM, the gap narrows (2.5→1.1 is a 56% reduction; 8.5→7.0 is an 18% reduction) but hasn't closed. To distinguish: extrapolate. If Vmax values converge, it's competitive. If they remain different, it's non-competitive. Based on the trend of narrowing but not equalizing, this is most consistent with non-competitive inhibition (Vmax appears different).

Alternatively, construct L-B plot: same x-intercept → non-competitive; same y-intercept → competitive.

b) Vmax without inhibitor appears to be approaching approximately 10 μmol/min at saturation.

c) Doubling [E] doubles Vmax (Vmax = kcat[E]total). Km is unchanged — Km is an intrinsic property of the enzyme-substrate interaction, independent of enzyme concentration.

Question 4 (Paper 2 Style — Extended Response)

Explain the mechanism of allosteric regulation using ATCase as an example. Include reference to quaternary structure, cooperativity, and the physiological significance of regulation by ATP and CTP. [7]

Answer

Quaternary structure (1-2 marks): ATCase consists of 12 subunits — 6 catalytic subunits (organized as 2 trimers) containing the active sites for aspartate and carbamoyl phosphate, and 6 regulatory subunits (organized as 3 dimers) that bind allosteric effectors. This separation of catalytic and regulatory functions is characteristic of allosteric enzymes.

Cooperativity and kinetics (2-3 marks): ATCase displays sigmoidal (not hyperbolic) kinetics. The enzyme exists in equilibrium between a low-affinity T-state (tense) and high-affinity R-state (relaxed). Substrate binding to one catalytic subunit shifts the equilibrium toward the R-state, making it easier for remaining subunits to bind substrate — this is positive cooperativity. The sigmoidal curve provides switch-like behavior: below a threshold [aspartate], activity is very low; above it, activity rises sharply.

Allosteric effectors (2-3 marks):

  • CTP (the end product of the pyrimidine pathway) is a negative allosteric effector. It binds to regulatory subunits and stabilizes the T-state → shifts the sigmoidal curve right → more aspartate needed to achieve same rate → pathway slows when pyrimidines are abundant. This is classic end-product/feedback inhibition.
  • ATP (a purine) is a positive allosteric effector. It binds to regulatory subunits and stabilizes the R-state → shifts curve left → less aspartate needed → pathway accelerates. This balances purine and pyrimidine pools: when ATP is high, the cell signals "make more pyrimidines."

Physiological significance (1 mark): This dual regulation ensures balanced nucleotide pools for DNA and RNA synthesis, preventing wasteful overproduction while allowing rapid response to metabolic demands.

Question 5 (Paper 1 Style — HL)

An allosteric enzyme displays which of the following?

I. A hyperbolic rate vs [S] curve

II. Quaternary structure

III. Cooperativity between subunits

A. I only

B. I and II only

C. II and III only

D. I, II, and III

Answer

C. Allosteric enzymes display sigmoidal, not hyperbolic, kinetics (eliminating I). They require quaternary structure (multiple subunits) for cooperativity — a monomer cannot show cooperativity. Cooperativity is the defining feature: binding at one subunit affects binding at others.

Common Misconceptions

Misconception Reality
"Km is the substrate concentration at Vmax" Km is the [S] at half Vmax, not full Vmax.
"A low Km means weak substrate binding" A low Km means high affinity — less substrate needed to reach half Vmax.
"Lineweaver-Burk x-intercept is +1/Km" The x-intercept is at −1/Km. The line extends into negative x-values.
"All enzymes follow Michaelis-Menten kinetics" Allosteric enzymes show sigmoidal kinetics. Only simple (usually monomeric) enzymes are hyperbolic.
"Km changes when you add more enzyme" Km is independent of enzyme concentration. Vmax increases with [E].
"Suicide inhibitors are just competitive inhibitors that bind tightly" They are irreversibly covalently bound. The enzyme chemically transforms them into a reactive species — fundamentally different from reversible competitive inhibition.
"Hemoglobin is an enzyme" Hemoglobin is an allosteric transport protein. It binds O₂ reversibly without chemically modifying it.
"Cooperativity means all subunits bind simultaneously" Cooperativity means binding to one subunit affects the affinity of other subunits — binding is sequential or concerted but not simultaneous.
"Non-competitive inhibitors change Km" Pure non-competitive inhibitors change Vmax only. Km remains unchanged.
"The L-B plot is the most accurate way to determine Km" Non-linear regression of the hyperbolic curve is actually more accurate; L-B is useful for visualization.

$$\frac{1}{v} = \frac{K_m}{V_{max}} \cdot \frac{1}{[S]} + \frac{1}{V_{max}}$$END Lineweaver-Burk: linear transformation
$$V_{max} = k_{cat}[E]_{total}$$END Vmax depends on enzyme concentration and turnover number
$$K_m = [S] \text{ when } v = V_{max}/2$$END Operational definition of Km

Key Terms Summary

Term Definition
Km Substrate concentration at half Vmax; inverse measure of enzyme-substrate affinity
Vmax Maximum reaction rate when all enzyme active sites are saturated
kcat Turnover number — substrate molecules converted per active site per second at saturation
Lineweaver-Burk plot Double reciprocal plot (1/v vs 1/[S]); linearizes Michaelis-Menten data
Suicide inhibitor Substrate analog converted by enzyme into reactive species that covalently inactivates it
Allosteric enzyme Multi-subunit enzyme regulated by effector binding at sites distinct from active site
Cooperativity Binding at one subunit affects binding affinity at other subunits
T-state Tense, low-affinity conformation of allosteric enzyme
R-state Relaxed, high-affinity conformation of allosteric enzyme
Sigmoidal kinetics S-shaped rate vs [S] curve characteristic of cooperative enzymes
ATCase Aspartate transcarbamoylase; model allosteric enzyme for pyrimidine synthesis
CTP End-product inhibitor of ATCase
ATP Heterotropic activator of ATCase (balances purine/pyrimidine pools)
