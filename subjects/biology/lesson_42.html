1. What Is a Mutation?

Mutation: A permanent change in the nucleotide sequence of DNA. Mutations can occur in coding regions (affecting protein sequence), regulatory regions (affecting gene expression), or non-coding regions (may have no effect). Mutations are the ultimate source of all genetic variation and the raw material for evolution.

Analogy: DNA is like an instruction manual for building and operating a cell. A mutation is a typographical error in that manual. Some typos don't change the meaning (silent mutations), some change a word slightly (missense), some turn a sentence into nonsense (nonsense), and some scramble everything that follows (frameshift).

2. Types of Mutations

2.1 Substitution (Point Mutation)

A single nucleotide is replaced by a different nucleotide.

Type What happens Effect on protein Example
Silent New codon codes for the same amino acid (due to degeneracy of the genetic code) No change** to the amino acid sequence. Protein is identical. GAA → GAG: both code for Glutamate.
Missense New codon codes for a different amino acid Protein has one amino acid changed. Effect ranges from negligible to severe. GAG → GTG: Glutamate → Valine. This is the sickle cell mutation.
Nonsense New codon is a stop codon (UAA, UAG, UGA) Translation terminates prematurely → truncated protein. Usually non-functional. CAG (Gln) → UAG (STOP). Severe consequence.

2.2 Insertion and Deletion (Indels)

Insertion: One or more nucleotides are added to the DNA sequence.

Deletion: One or more nucleotides are removed.

If the number of inserted/deleted nucleotides is not a multiple of 3, the result is a frameshift mutation — the reading frame of the ribosome shifts, and every codon downstream is read incorrectly.

Analogy: Imagine the sentence "THE FAT CAT ATE THE RAT." A deletion of the first "E" shifts everything: "THF ATC ATA TET HER AT..." — every three-letter word after the deletion is nonsense. This is exactly what happens in a frameshift.

If the insertion or deletion is a multiple of 3, the reading frame is preserved downstream, but one or more amino acids are added/removed. This is usually less severe than a frameshift.

3. Causes of Mutations

3.1 Spontaneous Mutations

Even without external agents, mutations occur naturally:

  • DNA polymerase errors: Despite proofreading (3'→5' exonuclease), DNA Pol III makes about 1 error per 10⁹ bases replicated. The error rate after proofreading is ~10⁻⁹ to 10⁻¹⁰ per base pair per replication.
  • Tautomeric shifts: Bases can temporarily exist in rare tautomeric forms that pair incorrectly (e.g., a rare enol form of thymine pairs with guanine instead of adenine).
  • Depurination: The bond between a purine base (A or G) and the deoxyribose sugar can hydrolyse spontaneously, leaving an apurinic site.
  • Deamination: Cytosine can spontaneously deaminate to uracil. If unrepaired, this creates a C→U transition.

3.2 Induced Mutations (Mutagens)

A mutagen is any agent that increases the rate of mutation above the spontaneous background level.

#### Chemical Mutagens

Mutagen Mechanism Source

Benzopyrene Intercalates between bases; forms bulky DNA adducts that distort the helix Tobacco smoke, charred meat

#### Radiation

Type Mechanism Source
UV radiation Causes thymine dimers — covalent bonds between adjacent thymines on the same strand. This distorts the helix and blocks replication/transcription. Sunlight, tanning beds

Carcinogen: A mutagen that specifically leads to cancer. Not all mutagens are carcinogens (some cause mutations that don't lead to cancer), but many carcinogens are mutagens (they cause the mutations that initiate and drive tumour development).

Paper 1: "Distinguish between a mutagen and a carcinogen." → A mutagen is any agent that causes changes to the DNA sequence. A carcinogen is a mutagen that specifically leads to cancer. All carcinogens are mutagens (broadly speaking), but not all mutagens cause cancer.

4. Sickle Cell Anemia: A Case Study in Mutation

Sickle cell anemia is the classic IB example of a point mutation with profound consequences — and a fascinating evolutionary story.

4.1 The Mutation

  • Gene: β-globin gene (HBB) on chromosome 11.
  • Mutation: A single base substitution in the coding strand: GAG → GTG.
  • In mRNA: GAG → GUG.
  • Amino acid change: Glutamate (Glu) → Valine (Val) at position 6 of the β-globin chain.
  • Type: Missense mutation.

4.2 The Molecular and Cellular Effect

Normal hemoglobin (HbA) is a tetramer: 2 α-globin + 2 β-globin chains. The β-globin has a glutamate at position 6 — a hydrophilic (water-loving), negatively charged amino acid that sits on the surface of the protein.

In sickle cell hemoglobin (HbS), the glutamate is replaced by valine — a hydrophobic (water-hating) amino acid. This creates a sticky hydrophobic patch on the hemoglobin surface.

Consequence at low oxygen tension (e.g., high altitude, exercise, dehydration):

  1. Deoxygenated HbS molecules aggregate via the hydrophobic valine patches.
  2. They polymerise into long, rigid fibres.
  3. These fibres distort the red blood cell into a sickle (crescent) shape.
  4. Sickled RBCs are:
  • Fragile → haemolyse easily → anaemia.
  • Less flexible → block small capillaries → painful vaso-occlusive crises, organ damage (spleen, kidneys, brain, lungs).
  • Removed faster by the spleen → chronic anaemia.

Draw and label: A diagram comparing:

  • Normal RBC: biconcave disc, flexible, flowing smoothly through a capillary.
  • Sickled RBC: crescent-shaped, rigid, stuck in a capillary, blocking blood flow. Downstream tissue shown as oxygen-starved.

4.3 The Heterozygote Advantage

Genotypes:

  • HbA/HbA: Normal hemoglobin. Susceptible to malaria.
  • HbA/HbS: Heterozygous (sickle cell trait). Generally asymptomatic (some sickling under extreme conditions). Resistant to malaria.
  • HbS/HbS: Homozygous. Sickle cell disease. Severe anemia, pain crises, reduced life expectancy.

Why are heterozygotes resistant to malaria?

The malaria parasite (Plasmodium falciparum) spends part of its life cycle inside red blood cells. In HbA/HbS individuals:

  • Parasitised RBCs tend to sickle (the parasite lowers oxygen tension).
  • Sickled cells are removed by the spleen — eliminating the parasite along with the cell.
  • Potassium levels may be altered in HbS-containing cells, inhibiting parasite growth.
  • The immune system may clear infected sickled cells more efficiently.

Evolutionary consequence: In malaria-endemic regions (sub-Saharan Africa, parts of India, the Mediterranean), the HbS allele frequency can be as high as 10-20%. The heterozygote advantage (balancing selection) maintains the allele despite the lethality of the homozygous state. This is a classic example of balanced polymorphism.

Analogy: The sickle cell allele is like a genetic "seatbelt." In a world without car crashes (malaria), wearing a seatbelt might be slightly inconvenient (mild trait). In a world with frequent crashes (malaria), the seatbelt saves your life. But having two seatbelts (homozygous) somehow strangles you. The best situation is exactly one seatbelt.

5. Consequences of Mutations

Not all mutations are bad. Evolution depends on them.

Category Description Examples
Neutral No effect on fitness. Silent mutations, mutations in non-coding DNA, or missense mutations that don't alter protein function. Silent mutation in a codon; SNP in an intron that doesn't affect splicing
Beneficial Increases fitness. Rare but essential for adaptation and evolution. Antibiotic resistance in bacteria; lactose tolerance in humans (persistence of lactase into adulthood); sickle cell trait in malaria regions; CCR5-Δ32 mutation conferring HIV resistance
Harmful Decreases fitness. Causes genetic disease or increased susceptibility. Cystic fibrosis (CFTR ΔF508 deletion); Huntington's disease (CAG repeat expansion); sickle cell disease (homozygous); many cancers

Classifying Mutations

Classify each scenario and state whether the mutation is likely neutral, beneficial, or harmful:

(a) A substitution in the third base of a codon changes GAU to GAC. Both code for aspartate.

(b) A deletion of three nucleotides removes a single amino acid from a non-critical loop region of a protein.

(c) A substitution creates a premature stop codon in the first exon of the BRCA1 tumour suppressor gene.

(d) In a population of bacteria exposed to an antibiotic, a mutation in the ribosomal protein gene prevents antibiotic binding while still allowing ribosome function.

Answers:

(a) Silent mutation. The amino acid is unchanged → protein identical → neutral.

(b) In-frame deletion. One amino acid missing. If the loop region is non-critical for folding/function, likely neutral (or mildly deleterious).

(c) Nonsense mutation. Premature stop codon → truncated, non-functional BRCA1 protein. Loss of tumour suppressor function → increased cancer risk. Harmful.

(d) Missense mutation that confers antibiotic resistance. Allows survival and reproduction in antibiotic environment. Beneficial (for the bacteria — harmful for us!).

6. CRISPR-Cas9: Rewriting the Code of Life

CRISPR-Cas9 is the most transformative tool in molecular biology since PCR. It allows scientists to edit genes with unprecedented precision, efficiency, and low cost.

6.1 The Natural Origin: A Bacterial Immune System

CRISPR (Clustered Regularly Interspaced Short Palindromic Repeats) was discovered as part of the bacterial adaptive immune system against viruses (bacteriophages).

How it works naturally in bacteria:

  1. When a bacterium survives a viral infection, it incorporates a short fragment of the viral DNA into its own genome at the CRISPR locus (as a "spacer").
  2. The CRISPR locus is transcribed into a long RNA, which is processed into short crRNAs (CRISPR RNAs), each containing one spacer sequence.
  3. The crRNA complexes with Cas9 (CRISPR-associated protein 9), a nuclease.
  4. The crRNA guides Cas9 to complementary viral DNA. Cas9 cuts both strands of the invading DNA, destroying the virus.
  5. This is adaptive immunity — the bacterium "remembers" past infections and can rapidly destroy the same virus if it attacks again.

Analogy: The CRISPR locus is like a library of "mug shots" (viral DNA fragments). Cas9 is the police officer. The crRNA is the officer holding a mug shot, scanning crowds (incoming DNA) for a match. If found, Cas9 "arrests" (cuts) the suspect DNA.

6.2 CRISPR-Cas9 as a Gene-Editing Tool

Scientists adapted this system for gene editing by creating a simplified version:

Components:

  1. Cas9 nuclease: The "molecular scissors." Cuts both strands of DNA at a specific site.
  2. Guide RNA (gRNA): A synthetic RNA (~20 nucleotides) designed to be complementary to the target DNA sequence. The gRNA is a fusion of the crRNA and tracrRNA (trans-activating CRISPR RNA) into a single molecule.

Mechanism:

  1. Design: Scientists design a gRNA complementary to the specific gene/sequence they want to edit.
  2. Binding: The gRNA-Cas9 complex scans the genome. The gRNA base-pairs with complementary DNA. Cas9 requires a PAM sequence (Protospacer Adjacent Motif — typically NGG, where N is any base) adjacent to the target sequence. This ensures Cas9 only cuts where the gRNA binds AND the PAM is present.
  3. Cutting: Cas9 makes a double-strand break (DSB) precisely 3-4 nucleotides upstream of the PAM.
  4. Repair: The cell's DNA repair machinery fixes the break. Two pathways:

a) Non-Homologous End Joining (NHEJ):

  • The "quick and dirty" repair pathway.
  • The broken ends are directly ligated together without a template.
  • Error-prone: Often results in small insertions or deletions (indels) at the break site.
  • Indels in a coding sequence → frameshift → gene knockout (the gene is disrupted and non-functional).
  • Used when the goal is to inactivate a gene.

b) Homology-Directed Repair (HDR):

  • The "precise" repair pathway.
  • Requires a homologous template — a donor DNA molecule with sequences matching the regions flanking the break, but containing the desired edit in between.
  • The cell uses the donor template to repair the break accurately.
  • Used when the goal is to correct a mutation or insert a specific sequence.

Application How CRISPR is used Status

Cancer immunotherapy Edit T-cells to enhance tumour recognition (knock out PD-1, engineer CAR-T cells) Clinical trials ongoing

Disease models Create animal models (mice, zebrafish) with specific mutations to study disease mechanisms Widely used in research
Xenotransplantation Edit pig genomes to remove endogenous retroviruses and reduce immunogenicity for organ transplantation Early clinical development

6.4 Gene Drives

A gene drive is a genetic system that ensures a particular gene is inherited by more than the normal 50% of offspring — it "drives" itself through a population.

How CRISPR gene drives work:

  1. Insert the CRISPR-Cas9 system (both Cas9 gene + gRNA gene) into the target locus on one chromosome.
  2. When the organism reproduces, the CRISPR system on the modified chromosome cuts the unmodified (wild-type) chromosome at the target site.
  3. The cell repairs the break using the modified chromosome as a template (HDR).
  4. Result: the offspring is homozygous for the modification — both chromosomes carry the gene drive.
  5. Over generations, the gene drive spreads exponentially.

Application — malaria control:

  • Engineer mosquitoes with a gene drive that:
  • Makes them resistant to the Plasmodium parasite (so they can't transmit malaria), OR
  • Skews sex ratios toward males (reducing the population), OR
  • Causes female sterility.
  • Release into wild populations → the gene drive spreads → malaria transmission is reduced or eliminated.

Analogy: A gene drive is like a computer virus for genes. Once released into a population, it copies itself onto the matching chromosome in every generation, ensuring near-100% inheritance instead of the usual 50%. It spreads whether or not it benefits the organism.

7. Ethical Issues in Gene Editing

7.1 Somatic vs Germline Editing

Somatic Gene Therapy Germline Editing
Cells edited Body cells (e.g., blood stem cells, lung cells) Germ cells (sperm, eggs) or embryos
Heritable? No** — affects only the treated individual Yes** — passed to all future generations
Consent The patient can consent Future generations cannot consent
Regulatory status Generally accepted (with oversight) Heavily restricted or banned in most countries
Example CTX001 for sickle cell (editing patient's blood stem cells ex vivo, then reinfusing) The 2018 case of He Jiankui, who edited embryos resulting in the birth of twin girls (CCR5 gene for HIV resistance) — widely condemned

7.2 Key Ethical Concerns

1. Germline Editing and "Designer Babies"

  • Editing embryos creates heritable changes that will affect all future generations.
  • The line between "therapy" (correcting disease) and "enhancement" (selecting for intelligence, height, athletic ability, eye colour) is blurry.
  • Concerns about a new form of eugenics and increasing social inequality ("only the rich can afford gene-edited children").

2. Off-Target Effects

  • The gRNA may bind to sequences that are similar (but not identical) to the target, causing Cas9 to cut at unintended sites.
  • Off-target mutations could inactivate tumour suppressor genes or activate oncogenes → cancer risk.
  • Current research focuses on improving gRNA specificity and using "high-fidelity" Cas9 variants.

3. Equity of Access

  • CRISPR therapies (e.g., CTX001 for sickle cell) are extremely expensive (~$2 million+ per patient).
  • Will these treatments only be available to the wealthy in developed countries, while the majority of sickle cell patients live in sub-Saharan Africa and India?

4. Ecological Risks of Gene Drives

  • Once released, a gene drive could spread uncontrollably across national borders.
  • Unforeseen ecological consequences: what happens if we eliminate a mosquito species? Effects on food webs (fish, birds, bats that eat mosquitoes)?
  • Could the gene drive mutate and have unintended effects in the target species or jump to other species (horizontal gene transfer)?
  • Who decides whether to release a gene drive? Global governance is lacking.

5. Informed Consent

  • Germline editing affects future generations who cannot consent.
  • The 2018 He Jiankui case is a stark example of violating ethical norms — no proper regulatory approval, inadequate informed consent, uncertain medical necessity (CCR5-Δ32 provides HIV resistance, but the twins were not at high risk for HIV).

Common Misconceptions

Misconception Reality
"All mutations are harmful." Most mutations are neutral. Some are beneficial (and are the raw material for evolution). Only a fraction cause disease.
"Frameshift mutations only occur from insertions." Both insertions AND deletions cause frameshifts — if the number of bases added/removed is not a multiple of 3.
"Sickle cell heterozygotes are completely immune to malaria." They are resistant, not immune. They can still contract malaria, but they are much less likely to develop severe, life-threatening disease.
"CRISPR was invented by scientists." CRISPR is a natural bacterial immune system that scientists adapted for gene editing. Jennifer Doudna and Emmanuelle Charpentier (Nobel Prize 2020) showed how to reprogram it.
"CRISPR always edits the right gene." Off-target effects are a major concern. The gRNA can bind to similar sequences elsewhere in the genome.
"Somatic gene therapy changes are passed to children." Somatic therapy edits body cells only. These changes are not heritable. Only germline editing is heritable.
"Gene drives are already being used in the wild." Gene drives have been demonstrated in laboratory settings but have not yet been released into wild populations. Extensive safety testing and regulatory frameworks are still under development.

Answer 4

(a) A CRISPR gene drive cassette (containing Cas9 + gRNA genes) is inserted into one chromosome at the target locus. In a heterozygous organism (one modified, one wild-type chromosome), the Cas9 and gRNA are expressed. The gRNA-Cas9 complex cuts the wild-type chromosome at the target site. The cell repairs the break using the modified chromosome as an HDR template, copying the gene drive cassette onto the wild-type chromosome. Both chromosomes now carry the drive → the organism is homozygous. This happens in the germline, so all gametes carry the drive → all offspring inherit it → >99% inheritance instead of 50%.

(b) Benefit: Could drastically reduce or eliminate malaria transmission, saving hundreds of thousands of lives annually, particularly children under 5 in sub-Saharan Africa. This would be a more sustainable and equitable intervention than drugs, bed nets, or insecticide spraying, which require ongoing effort and resources.

Risk: Unintended ecological consequences. Mosquitoes are part of food webs — their elimination/reduction could affect species that depend on them (fish, birds, bats). Additionally, the gene drive could mutate or spread to non-target mosquito species. Once released, it may be impossible to recall.

(c) Gene drives are self-propagating — they spread through a population even if they reduce fitness. Unlike standard GMOs (which can be contained or will die out without selection), a gene drive is designed to spread to every individual in the population and across national borders. There is no effective "undo" button. Ecological impacts (trophic cascades, effects on pollination, competition with other species) are unpredictable and could be irreversible.

Answer 5

(a) Somatic gene therapy edits body cells (e.g., blood stem cells) of an existing individual with a disease. The changes affect only that individual and are not inherited. The patient (or guardians) can consent. Germline enhancement edits embryos to introduce traits into children who don't yet exist and have no medical need. Changes are heritable — affecting all future generations who cannot consent.

(b) Arguments in favour:

  • Parental autonomy: Parents already shape their children's traits through education, nutrition, and environment. Genetic enhancement is an extension of this — parents should have the right to give their children the best possible start.
  • Reducing suffering: If we can eliminate genetic predispositions to diseases (Alzheimer's, cancer) or confer beneficial traits (resistance to infectious diseases), we have a moral obligation to do so, just as we vaccinate children today.

(c) Arguments against:

  • Inequality and eugenics: Genetic enhancements would be expensive, accessible only to the wealthy. This would create a genetic "upper class," deepening social inequality and potentially leading to a new form of eugenics where certain genetic traits are valued over others.
  • Unpredictable consequences and loss of diversity: We don't fully understand the pleiotropic effects of genes — a gene variant that increases height might also increase cancer risk. Eliminating genetic variation (even for "negative" traits) reduces the gene pool's resilience. The CCR5-Δ32 mutation (HIV resistance) also increases susceptibility to West Nile virus — genes often have trade-offs.

(d) Germline editing makes changes that will be passed to all future descendants. These future generations have no say in decisions that will affect their genetic makeup, their health, and potentially their identity. This raises intergenerational justice concerns: do current generations have the right to make irreversible genetic decisions for all future humanity? Somatic therapy does not raise this issue because the changes die with the treated individual.

Key Takeaways

Concept Summary
Substitution Silent (same amino acid), missense (different amino acid), nonsense (premature stop).
Indels Insertions/deletions. Frameshift if not multiple of 3 → all downstream codons changed.
Mutagens Chemical (nitrosamines, benzopyrene), radiation (UV → thymine dimers; ionising → double-strand breaks).
Sickle cell GAG→GTG, Glu→Val at position 6 of β-globin. HbS polymerises at low O₂ → sickled RBCs. Heterozygote advantage vs malaria.
CRISPR-Cas9 gRNA guides Cas9 to target → double-strand break. NHEJ → indels/knockout. HDR (+ donor template) → precise edit.
Gene drive CRISPR cassette copies itself to wild-type chromosome via HDR → >99% inheritance → spreads through population.
Somatic vs germline Somatic: body cells only, not inherited. Germline: embryos/gametes, heritable, affects future generations.
Ethics Off-target effects, designer babies, equity, consent of future generations, ecological risks of gene drives.
