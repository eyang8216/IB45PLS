1. Speciation: The Origin of Species

Speciation is the evolutionary process by which one species splits into two (or more) distinct species. It is the engine of biodiversity — the reason there are ~8.7 million eukaryotic species rather than one.

The unifying principle: for speciation to occur, gene flow must be interrupted. Gene flow is the movement of alleles between populations (through migration and interbreeding). Gene flow homogenizes populations — it prevents divergence. When gene flow stops, populations can evolve independently and accumulate differences until they become separate species.

1.1 The General Model

All speciation follows this pattern:

  1. Isolation: Gene flow between populations is reduced or eliminated.
  2. Divergence: Populations experience different mutations, different selection pressures, and/or genetic drift.
  3. Reproductive isolation: Differences accumulate to the point where individuals from the two populations can no longer successfully interbreed.
  4. Two species: Even if the populations come back into contact, they remain distinct.

The two main modes of speciation differ in how isolation is achieved.

2. Allopatric Speciation: A Physical Barrier

Allopatric speciation (Greek: allos = other, patris = homeland) occurs when a population is divided by a geographic barrier — a physical feature that prevents gene flow.

2.1 The Process

  1. A population occupies a continuous geographic range.
  2. A geographic barrier arises, splitting the population into two (or more) isolated subpopulations.

Barriers can be:

  • Mountain ranges forming through tectonic uplift
  • Rivers changing course, creating impassable water barriers for terrestrial organisms
  • Oceans rising and flooding land bridges (e.g., the formation of islands)
  • Glaciers advancing during ice ages, fragmenting habitats
  • Deserts expanding
  • Land bridges forming (the Isthmus of Panama, ~3 million years ago — this is a barrier for marine organisms)
  • Human activity — roads, dams, deforestation fragmenting habitats
  1. Isolated populations experience:
  • Different selection pressures (different climates, food sources, predators, diseases on each side of the barrier).
  • Independent mutations (different random mutations arise in each population).
  • Genetic drift (random changes in allele frequencies, especially powerful in small populations).
  1. Over many generations, the populations diverge genetically, morphologically, and behaviorally.
  1. If the barrier is removed and the populations come back into contact, they can no longer interbreed successfully — they have become reproductively isolated. Allopatric speciation is complete.

2.2 Classic Examples

Darwin's Finches: A single ancestral finch species colonized the Galápagos Islands from mainland South America (about 2–3 million years ago). Different islands presented different food sources. Finches on each island adapted to their local food — seed-eaters developed thick, strong beaks; insect-eaters developed slender, probing beaks; a "woodpecker finch" uses cactus spines as tools to extract grubs. Occasional migration between islands was rare enough that populations on each island followed independent evolutionary trajectories. Today, there are ~14 recognized species across the archipelago.

Grand Canyon Squirrels: The Kaibab squirrel (Sciurus aberti kaibabensis) lives on the north rim of the Grand Canyon. The Abert's squirrel (Sciurus aberti aberti) lives on the south rim. They share a common ancestor whose range was split when the Colorado River carved the canyon ~6 million years ago. The Kaibab squirrel has a distinctive dark belly and white tail; the Abert's squirrel has a white belly and gray tail. They are currently classified as subspecies — but if the canyon keeps them isolated long enough, they may eventually become fully separate species.

Isthmus of Panama: About 3 million years ago, the formation of the Isthmus of Panama connected North and South America — but it also separated populations of marine organisms that previously existed in a continuous ocean. Snapping shrimp (Alpheus) populations on the Pacific and Caribbean sides are now reproductively isolated from each other. When placed together in the lab, they display aggressive behavior rather than mating — behavioral isolation has evolved during their physical separation.

Allopatric speciation: Speciation resulting from geographic isolation — a physical barrier divides a population, preventing gene flow. Separated populations diverge through natural selection, mutation, and genetic drift until they become reproductively isolated. Also called "geographic speciation."

3. Sympatric Speciation: No Geographic Barrier

Sympatric speciation (Greek: sym = together, patris = homeland) is the evolution of reproductive isolation within the same geographic area — without a physical barrier separating populations.

This is rarer and harder to achieve than allopatric speciation because gene flow typically prevents divergence when organisms live in the same place. Sympatric speciation requires a mechanism that creates reproductive isolation without geographic isolation.

3.1 Mechanisms of Sympatric Speciation

1. Polyploidy (especially in plants)

Polyploidy — having more than two complete chromosome sets — is the most common mechanism of sympatric speciation, especially in plants. It's essentially instant speciation because a polyploid individual is immediately reproductively isolated from its diploid parents.

Recall from Lesson 7:

  • Autopolyploidy: Chromosome doubling within a species. A diploid (2n) plant produces diploid gametes due to meiotic error. Self-fertilization or fusion with another 2n gamete → tetraploid (4n) offspring. The tetraploid can breed with other tetraploids but produces sterile triploid offspring with diploids → instant reproductive isolation.
  • Allopolyploidy: Hybridization between two species followed by chromosome doubling. The initial hybrid is sterile (chromosomes from different species can't pair in meiosis), but chromosome doubling restores fertility. The new allopolyploid is reproductively isolated from both parent species.

Many crop species originated this way: bread wheat (Triticum aestivum, hexaploid), tobacco (Nicotiana tabacum, tetraploid), cotton (Gossypium hirsutum, tetraploid).

2. Habitat Differentiation

Within the same geographic area, subpopulations may exploit different habitats or resources. If mating occurs preferentially within each habitat, gene flow is reduced.

Example — Apple maggot fly (Rhagoletis pomonella): This North American fly originally laid its eggs only on hawthorn fruits. When European settlers introduced apple trees ~200 years ago, some flies began using apples instead. Because flies mate on their host fruit, apple-preferring flies mate mainly with other apple-preferring flies, and hawthorn-preferring flies mate with hawthorn-preferring flies. Gene flow between the two groups is reduced. Additionally, apples fruit earlier than hawthorns, creating temporal isolation. This is incipient speciation happening right now.

3. Sexual Selection

If mate preferences diverge within a population — say, some females prefer red-colored males and others prefer blue-colored males — assortative mating (like-with-like) can reduce gene flow and eventually lead to speciation.

Example — Cichlid fish in Lake Victoria: In one of the most spectacular evolutionary radiations, ~500 species of cichlid fish evolved in Lake Victoria in just ~15,000 years. Many species differ primarily in male coloration, and females show strong mate preferences for particular color patterns. Sexual selection, combined with ecological specialization, drove this rapid speciation.

4. Temporal Isolation (within sympatry)

If subpopulations breed at different times (different seasons, different times of day), gene flow is reduced even if they occupy the same space. Over generations, this temporal separation can lead to full reproductive isolation.

3.2 Why Is Sympatric Speciation Controversial?

The challenge: when organisms live in the same place, gene flow should homogenize the gene pool. For sympatric speciation to work, disruptive selection (favoring extreme phenotypes over intermediate ones) must be strong enough to overcome gene flow, AND there must be some mechanism (like assortative mating) that keeps the diverging groups reproductively separated. The conditions are stringent, which is why allopatric speciation is considered far more common.

Sympatric speciation: Speciation occurring within the same geographic area, without a physical barrier. It requires a mechanism that creates reproductive isolation despite the potential for gene flow. The most common mechanism is polyploidy (especially in plants), but habitat differentiation, sexual selection, and temporal isolation can also drive it.

4. Reproductive Isolating Mechanisms

Once populations have begun to diverge, reproductive isolating mechanisms prevent them from merging back into a single species. These barriers are classified by when they act — before fertilization (prezygotic) or after (postzygotic).

4.1 Prezygotic Barriers: Preventing Mating or Fertilization

Prezygotic barriers prevent mating from occurring or prevent fertilization if mating does occur. They are generally more "efficient" from an evolutionary perspective because they prevent the waste of gametes and energy on unsuccessful reproduction.

Barrier Description Examples
Temporal isolation Different breeding seasons or times of day Western spotted skunk mates in late summer; eastern spotted skunk mates in late winter. Some corals release gametes on different nights.
Habitat isolation Populations occupy different habitats within the same area, so they rarely encounter each other Two species of garter snakes in the same region — one lives mainly in water, the other is terrestrial. They rarely meet.
Behavioral isolation Differences in courtship rituals, mating calls, or other signals prevent attraction Female fireflies respond only to the specific flash pattern of males of their own species. Bird songs differ between closely related species. Blue-footed boobies perform elaborate foot-display dances; if the dance is wrong, mating doesn't happen.
Mechanical isolation Structural differences in reproductive organs prevent successful mating Insect genitalia are often species-specific — like a lock-and-key mechanism. Flowers with different shapes/pollinator-specific structures.
Gametic isolation Gametes meet but cannot fuse In aquatic organisms that release gametes into water, sperm of one species may not have the correct proteins to bind to and penetrate eggs of another species. Sea urchin species that spawn simultaneously have species-specific proteins on sperm and egg surfaces. In plants, pollen may not germinate on the stigma of a different species.

4.2 Postzygotic Barriers: Preventing Viable, Fertile Offspring

Postzygotic barriers act after fertilization. Mating occurs, a zygote forms, but the hybrid offspring either doesn't develop, doesn't survive, or is sterile.

Barrier Description Examples
Hybrid inviability Hybrid zygote fails to develop or hybrid offspring dies before reaching reproductive age Hybrids between some frog species in the genus Rana develop abnormally and die as embryos. Sheep-goat hybrids usually die before birth.
Hybrid sterility Hybrid offspring is viable (survives to adulthood) but cannot produce functional gametes — it is sterile Mule (horse × donkey): viable, strong, but sterile — horses have 64 chromosomes, donkeys have 62, so mules have 63. The odd number prevents proper chromosome pairing in meiosis. Liger** (lion × tiger) males are sterile.
Hybrid breakdown First-generation (F1) hybrids are viable and fertile, but when they mate with each other or with parent species, the second-generation (F2) hybrids are inviable or sterile Different species of cotton (Gossypium) produce fertile F1 hybrids, but F2 offspring die as seeds or are weak and sterile. The mixing of incompatible gene combinations in recombination causes genetic chaos.

Reproductive isolating mechanisms: Biological features (behaviors, structures, physiological processes) that prevent gene flow between populations. Prezygotic barriers prevent mating or fertilization (temporal, habitat, behavioral, mechanical, gametic). Postzygotic barriers prevent hybrid offspring from surviving or reproducing (hybrid inviability, hybrid sterility, hybrid breakdown).

4.3 The Evolutionary Logic

Prezygotic barriers tend to evolve when diverging populations come back into contact (secondary contact). If hybrids have reduced fitness (they're sterile or inviable), any allele that promotes mating with one's own population type will be favored by natural selection. This process of reinforcement strengthens prezygotic barriers — natural selection itself completes the speciation process.

5. Adaptive Radiation: Rapid Diversification

Adaptive radiation is the rapid evolution of many species from a single common ancestor, each adapted to exploit a different ecological niche. It typically occurs when:

  1. Organisms colonize an environment with many unoccupied niches (e.g., an isolated island or lake with few competitors).
  2. A key innovation (an evolutionary novelty) opens up new ways of making a living.
  3. After a mass extinction clears out competitors.

5.1 Darwin's Finches: A Case Study in Adaptive Radiation

The ~14 species of Darwin's finches on the Galápagos Islands radiated from a single ancestral population of seed-eating finches that arrived 2–3 million years ago. The radiation was driven by two factors:

  1. Ecological opportunity: The remote islands had few competing bird species. Many niches were vacant.
  2. Divergent selection on beak morphology: Different food sources required different beak types.

The beak-food connection:

Beak Type Food Source Example Species
Large, deep, powerful beak Large, hard seeds (tough to crack) Large ground finch (Geospiza magnirostris)
Medium beak Medium seeds Medium ground finch (G. fortis)
Small beak Small, soft seeds Small ground finch (G. fuliginosa)
Sharp, pointed beak Insects Small tree finch (Camarhynchus parvulus)
Long, probing beak; uses cactus spine as tool Insect larvae in wood Woodpecker finch (Camarhynchus pallidus)
Parrot-like beak Fruit and buds Vegetarian finch (Platyspiza crassirostris)
Long, curved beak; drinks nectar Cactus flowers (nectar and pollen) Cactus finch (Geospiza scandens)
Sharp beak; pecks at skin Blood of seabirds (yes, really — the "vampire finch") Sharp-beaked ground finch (G. difficilis)

Key IB point: The variation in beak morphology is heritable and associated with different ecological niches. This reduces competition — each species specializes, so they don't all compete for the same food.

5.2 Other Examples

Hawaiian Honeycreepers: From a single ancestral finch ~5 million years ago, over 50 species evolved across the Hawaiian Islands. Beak morphologies range from thin, curved bills for nectar-feeding to thick, parrot-like bills for seed-cracking, to woodpecker-like bills for probing bark. Sadly, many are now extinct due to habitat loss, introduced predators, and avian malaria.

Cichlid Fishes of African Great Lakes:

  • Lake Victoria: ~500 species in ~15,000 years.
  • Lake Malawi: ~1,000 species in ~2 million years.
  • Lake Tanganyika: ~250 species, the oldest radiation.

Diversification was driven by jaw morphology adaptations (algae scraping, insect eating, fish eating, scale eating, egg eating — some cichlids specialize in stealing eggs from the mouths of mouth-brooding species!) and by sexual selection on male coloration.

Adaptive radiation: Rapid diversification of a single ancestral lineage into many species, each adapted to a distinct ecological niche. Characterized by: (1) common ancestry, (2) rapid speciation, (3) phenotype-environment correlation (morphology matches ecology), and (4) occupation of diverse niches.

6. The Tempo of Evolution: Gradualism vs. Punctuated Equilibrium

Does evolution proceed at a steady, slow pace, or in rapid bursts separated by long periods of stasis? This debate concerns the tempo of evolution, not its mechanism.

6.1 Gradualism (Darwin's View)

Phyletic gradualism holds that evolution proceeds through the slow, steady accumulation of small changes over vast periods. In Darwin's words (paraphrased), "Nature does not make leaps." New species arise by the gradual transformation of entire ancestral populations.

Prediction for the fossil record: We should find numerous intermediate forms connecting ancestor and descendant species in a smooth gradient of transitional fossils.

The problem: The fossil record often does NOT show this pattern. Instead, we typically see species appearing suddenly, persisting with little change for millions of years, and then disappearing — a pattern called stasis. Darwin recognized this and attributed it to the imperfection of the fossil record.

6.2 Punctuated Equilibrium (Eldredge & Gould, 1972)

Niles Eldredge and Stephen Jay Gould proposed that the pattern in the fossil record might be real, not an artifact. In punctuated equilibrium:

  • Species experience long periods of stasis (little or no morphological change), sometimes lasting millions of years.
  • These are punctuated by rapid bursts of change (geologically rapid — thousands to tens of thousands of years) during which speciation occurs.
  • Speciation happens in small, isolated populations at the edge of the species range (peripatric speciation — a variant of allopatric speciation).
  • Once a new species is established, it may replace the ancestral species or coexist with it.

Why stasis? Large, well-adapted populations have strong genetic inertia. Gene flow, stabilizing selection, and developmental constraints resist change. The species is in equilibrium with its environment.

Why rapid change? When a small population becomes isolated (e.g., on the periphery of the range), genetic drift and strong selection in a new environment can cause rapid divergence.

Prediction for the fossil record: New species should appear relatively suddenly (in geological terms) and then persist with little change. This matches the observed pattern better than gradualism — but not perfectly.

6.3 Synthesis: Both Patterns Occur

Most evolutionary biologists today recognize that both patterns occur in nature. Some lineages show gradual change; others show stasis punctuated by rapid speciation. The debate is about their relative frequency, not their existence.

Gradualism: The hypothesis that evolution proceeds by the slow, steady accumulation of small changes over long periods; speciation involves the gradual transformation of entire populations.

Punctuated equilibrium: The hypothesis that species experience long periods of morphological stasis interspersed with geologically rapid bursts of change during speciation, typically in small, peripheral populations.

7. Hardy-Weinberg Equilibrium: The Null Model

The Hardy-Weinberg principle is the mathematical foundation of population genetics. It describes conditions under which allele and genotype frequencies in a population remain constant across generations — i.e., the population does not evolve.

7.1 The Principle

In 1908, G.H. Hardy (an English mathematician) and Wilhelm Weinberg (a German physician) independently demonstrated that:

> In a large, randomly mating population with no mutation, no migration, and no natural selection, allele and genotype frequencies will remain constant from generation to generation.

This provides a null hypothesis for evolution studies. If a real population deviates from Hardy-Weinberg predictions, one of the assumptions is violated — and the population is evolving (or experiencing gene flow, genetic drift, or non-random mating).

7.2 The Mathematics

Consider a gene with two alleles: A (dominant) and a (recessive).

  • Let p = frequency of the dominant allele A
  • Let q = frequency of the recessive allele a
  • Since there are only two alleles: p + q = 1

Genotype frequencies under Hardy-Weinberg:

Genotype Frequency Derivation
AA (homozygous dominant) p² Probability of A from mother (p) × A from father (p)
Aa (heterozygous) 2pq Probability of A from mother (p) × a from father (q) + a from mother (q) × A from father (p) = pq + pq
aa (homozygous recessive) q² Probability of a from mother (q) × a from father (q)

The Hardy-Weinberg equation:

$$p^2 + 2pq + q^2 = 1$$

This equation allows us to:

  1. Calculate allele frequencies from genotype frequencies.
  2. Predict genotype frequencies if the population is in equilibrium.
  3. Test whether a population is evolving by comparing observed vs. expected genotype frequencies.

Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium: The state in which allele and genotype frequencies in a population remain constant across generations. The equation p² + 2pq + q² = 1 describes genotype frequencies at equilibrium. The conditions required are: (1) large population size (no genetic drift), (2) random mating, (3) no mutations, (4) no gene flow (migration), and (5) no natural selection.

7.3 The Five Conditions (and What Happens When They're Violated)

Condition Meaning What Happens If Violated
Large population No genetic drift — allele frequencies don't change by chance Small populations experience random fluctuations in allele frequencies (genetic drift), which can fix or eliminate alleles by chance
Random mating Every individual has an equal chance of mating with any other Non-random mating (e.g., inbreeding, assortative mating) changes genotype frequencies (but NOT allele frequencies)
No mutation No new alleles created Mutation introduces new alleles and alters frequencies (though usually slowly)
No gene flow No migration of individuals between populations Migration introduces or removes alleles, changing frequencies
No natural selection All genotypes have equal fitness Selection changes allele frequencies as certain genotypes contribute more offspring to the next generation

7.4 Worked Calculations

Worked Example 1: From Recessive Phenotype to Allele Frequencies

In a population of 1000 pea plants, 160 have white flowers (recessive trait, genotype aa). Calculate:

a) The frequency of the recessive allele (q)

b) The frequency of the dominant allele (p)

c) The expected number of heterozygous plants (Aa)

Step-by-step solution:

Step 1: Find q² (frequency of aa genotype).

  • Total plants = 1000
  • White-flowered plants (aa) = 160
  • q² = 160/1000 = 0.16

Step 2: Find q.

  • q = √(0.16) = 0.40

Step 3: Find p.

  • p + q = 1
  • p = 1 − 0.40 = 0.60

Step 4: Calculate expected genotype frequencies.

  • AA: p² = (0.60)² = 0.36
  • Aa: 2pq = 2(0.60)(0.40) = 0.48
  • aa: q² = 0.16

Step 5: Convert to expected numbers.

  • AA: 0.36 × 1000 = 360 plants
  • Aa: 0.48 × 1000 = 480 plants
  • aa: 0.16 × 1000 = 160 plants

Answer: q = 0.40, p = 0.60, expected heterozygotes = 480.

Worked Example 2: Testing for Evolution

Sickle cell disease is caused by a recessive allele. In a region of Nigeria, a sample of 10,000 individuals shows:

Genotype Number Phenotype
HbA/HbA 7,200 Normal
HbA/HbS 2,600 Carrier (malaria resistant)
HbS/HbS 200 Sickle cell disease

Question: Is this population in Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium? Show your calculations.

Step-by-step solution:

Step 1: Calculate actual allele frequencies from the observed data.

Total individuals = 10,000. Total alleles = 20,000.

Number of HbS alleles:

  • Each HbS/HbS individual has 2 HbS alleles: 200 × 2 = 400
  • Each HbA/HbS individual has 1 HbS allele: 2,600 × 1 = 2,600
  • Total HbS alleles = 3,000
  • q = 3,000/20,000 = 0.15

Number of HbA alleles:

  • p = 1 − 0.15 = 0.85

Step 2: Calculate EXPECTED genotype frequencies under Hardy-Weinberg.

  • Expected HbA/HbA: p² = (0.85)² = 0.7225
  • Expected HbA/HbS: 2pq = 2(0.85)(0.15) = 0.2550
  • Expected HbS/HbS: q² = (0.15)² = 0.0225

Step 3: Convert to expected numbers.

  • Expected HbA/HbA: 0.7225 × 10,000 = 7,225
  • Expected HbA/HbS: 0.2550 × 10,000 = 2,550
  • Expected HbS/HbS: 0.0225 × 10,000 = 225

Step 4: Compare observed vs. expected.

Genotype Observed Expected
HbA/HbA 7,200 7,225
HbA/HbS 2,600 2,550
HbS/HbS 200 225

Observed frequencies are close to expected but not identical. To determine if the difference is statistically significant, we would use a chi-squared test (which IB requires you to be able to perform and interpret).

Chi-squared calculation: χ² = Σ (O−E)²/E

  • HbA/HbA: (7200 − 7225)²/7225 = 625/7225 = 0.0865
  • HbA/HbS: (2600 − 2550)²/2550 = 2500/2550 = 0.9804
  • HbS/HbS: (200 − 225)²/225 = 625/225 = 2.7778

χ² = 0.0865 + 0.9804 + 2.7778 = 3.845 (approximately)

Degrees of freedom: For Hardy-Weinberg, df = number of genotypes − number of alleles = 3 − 2 = 1.

Critical value at p = 0.05 for df = 1: 3.841.

Our χ² (3.845) is slightly above 3.841, so the deviation is (barely) statistically significant at the 5% level.

Conclusion: The population may not be in Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium. The most likely reason: natural selection — the HbA/HbS heterozygote has a selective advantage in malarial regions (heterozygote advantage), which increases heterozygote frequency above Hardy-Weinberg expectations.

Worked Example 3: Dominant Phenotype — Can't Assume Homozygous

In a population, 64% of individuals show the dominant phenotype. The remaining 36% show the recessive phenotype.

Question: What percentage of the population is heterozygous?

Step-by-step solution:

Step 1: Identify what we know.

  • Recessive phenotype frequency = q² = 0.36
  • Therefore, q = √(0.36) = 0.60

Step 2: Find p.

  • p = 1 − q = 1 − 0.60 = 0.40

Step 3: Calculate heterozygous frequency.

  • 2pq = 2(0.40)(0.60) = 0.48 = 48%

Common mistake warning: Students often see "64% show dominant phenotype" and think p² = 0.64. Wrong! The dominant phenotype includes BOTH AA (p²) and Aa (2pq). You cannot directly calculate p from the dominant phenotype. You MUST use the recessive phenotype to find q², then q, then p.

IB Exam Tip: Hardy-Weinberg calculations are a guaranteed Paper 2 feature, often worth 3–5 marks. The most common mistakes: (1) trying to calculate p from the dominant phenotype directly, (2) forgetting to square root q² to get q, (3) forgetting that heterozygotes have frequency 2pq (not just pq), (4) forgetting to multiply by population size when asked for "number of individuals," not frequency. Always show your work step by step — the mark scheme awards partial marks for correct method even if the final number is wrong.

7.5 Using Hardy-Weinberg to Detect Evolution

The practical power of Hardy-Weinberg:

  1. Calculate alleles frequencies from observed genotypes.
  2. Calculate expected genotype frequencies under Hardy-Weinberg.
  3. Compare observed vs. expected (with chi-squared test if needed).
  4. If there's a significant difference → at least one Hardy-Weinberg condition is violated → the population is evolving or experiencing gene flow/drift/non-random mating.

Example interpretation: If heterozygote frequency is higher than expected (as in the sickle cell example), this suggests heterozygote advantage (natural selection). If all genotypes show reduced heterozygosity (more homozygotes than expected), this suggests inbreeding (non-random mating). If allele frequencies change rapidly over time, this suggests genetic drift (small population) or gene flow.

8. Common Misconceptions

Misconception Reality
"Allopatric and sympatric speciation are the only modes" There are also parapatric speciation (divergence across an environmental gradient with limited gene flow) and peripatric speciation (a special case of allopatric speciation involving small peripheral populations).
"Postzygotic barriers are evolutionarily 'better'" Prezygotic barriers are more efficient because they prevent wasted reproductive effort. Natural selection actually favors the evolution of prezygotic barriers when postzygotic barriers already exist (reinforcement).
"Punctuated equilibrium means evolution happens in sudden jumps in single generations" "Rapid" in geological terms means thousands to tens of thousands of years — many, many generations. It's rapid compared to millions of years, not rapid in a single lifetime.
"Hardy-Weinberg describes real populations" Almost no real population is in perfect Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium. The equation is a null model used to detect evolution, not a description of reality.
"p² + 2pq + q² = 1 calculates allele frequencies" The equation calculates genotype frequencies. p and q are allele frequencies. p², 2pq, and q² are genotype frequencies.
"If a population is in Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium, it means it's perfect and won't change" It means the measured allele and genotype frequencies would stay constant IF conditions remained unchanged. Real environments always change.

Problem 1 (Paper 1 style): Two species of frog live in the same pond but one breeds in early spring and the other in late summer. This is an example of:

A. Mechanical isolation

B. Temporal isolation

C. Habitat isolation

D. Gametic isolation

B. Temporal isolation — they breed at different times, so they never have the opportunity to mate. This is a prezygotic barrier.

Problem 2 (Paper 2 style, 4 marks): Distinguish between allopatric and sympatric speciation.

Click for answer

Model answer:

  • Allopatric speciation requires geographic isolation — a physical barrier (mountain, river, ocean) separates a population, preventing gene flow (1 mark). Sympatric speciation occurs within the same geographic area without a physical barrier (1 mark).
  • In allopatric speciation, populations diverge due to different selection pressures, mutation, and genetic drift in separate locations (1 mark). In sympatric speciation, reproductive isolation arises through mechanisms like polyploidy, habitat differentiation, or sexual selection while populations remain in contact (1 mark).
  • Allopatric speciation is considered more common and better-documented; sympatric speciation requires strong disruptive selection to overcome gene flow and is rarer (accepted as part of the answer but not required).

Problem 3 (Paper 2 style, 5 marks): In a population of 500 individuals, 20 individuals have a recessive genetic disease (genotype aa). Assuming Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium, calculate:

a) The frequency of the recessive allele

b) The number of heterozygous carriers

c) Explain why the frequency of the recessive allele is much higher than the disease frequency might suggest.

Click for answer

a) q² = 20/500 = 0.04. q = √(0.04) = 0.20. [1 mark]

b) p = 1 − 0.20 = 0.80. Heterozygous frequency = 2pq = 2(0.80)(0.20) = 0.32. Number of carriers = 0.32 × 500 = 160 individuals. [2 marks]

c) The recessive allele is mostly "hidden" in heterozygotes (carriers). Only homozygous recessive individuals (q² = 4%) show the disease. But the allele frequency q = 20% — five times higher — because most copies of the recessive allele exist in heterozygous carriers (160 individuals carrying one copy each = 160 alleles, plus 40 alleles in the 20 homozygous recessive individuals = 200 copies total out of 1000 alleles = 20%). This is why rare recessive diseases can persist in populations — selection cannot "see" the allele in heterozygotes. [2 marks]

Problem 4 (Paper 2 style, 6 marks): Explain how adaptive radiation occurred in Darwin's finches on the Galápagos Islands.

Click for answer

Model answer:

  • A single ancestral finch species colonized the Galápagos Islands from mainland South America ~2–3 million years ago (1 mark).
  • The remote islands had many unoccupied ecological niches with few competing bird species, providing ecological opportunity (1 mark).
  • Finch populations on different islands became geographically isolated, allowing allopatric speciation to begin (1 mark).
  • Different food sources (seeds of various sizes, insects, nectar, buds) exerted different selection pressures on beak morphology. Finches with beak shapes best suited to their local food source had higher survival and reproductive success (1 mark).
  • Over generations, beak morphology diverged — large crushing beaks for hard seeds, slender probing beaks for insects, curved beaks for nectar — each adaptation reducing competition through niche partitioning (1 mark).
  • As populations accumulated differences and developed reproductive isolation, ~14 distinct species evolved from the single ancestor, representing an adaptive radiation — rapid speciation driven by ecological opportunity and divergent natural selection (1 mark).

Problem 5: In a population, the frequency of the dominant allele A is 0.7. What is the expected frequency of heterozygous individuals under Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium?

Click for answer

p = 0.7, so q = 0.3. Heterozygous frequency = 2pq = 2(0.7)(0.3) = 0.42 = 42%.

Summary

  • Speciation requires interruption of gene flow. Allopatric speciation uses geographic barriers; sympatric speciation occurs without geographic isolation (polyploidy, habitat differentiation, sexual selection).
  • Reproductive isolating mechanisms are classified as prezygotic (temporal, habitat, behavioral, mechanical, gametic — prevent mating/fertilization) or postzygotic (hybrid inviability, sterility, breakdown — prevent viable/fertile offspring).
  • Adaptive radiation is rapid speciation from a single ancestor into many niches, driven by ecological opportunity and divergent selection (Darwin's finches, cichlids, Hawaiian honeycreepers).
  • Gradualism posits slow, steady change; punctuated equilibrium posits long stasis interrupted by rapid speciation. Both patterns occur in nature.
  • Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium (p² + 2pq + q² = 1) is the null model: if allele/genotype frequencies change across generations, one of the five conditions is violated, and the population is evolving.
