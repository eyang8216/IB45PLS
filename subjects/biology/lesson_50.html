1. Linked Genes: The Exception to Independent Assortment

The Problem with Mendel's Second Law

Recall the Law of Independent Assortment: alleles of different genes assort independently during meiosis. This holds true for genes on different chromosomes — but what about genes on the same chromosome?

Humans have ~20,000–25,000 genes but only 23 pairs of chromosomes. This means each chromosome carries hundreds to thousands of genes. Genes located on the same chromosome are said to be linked and tend to be inherited together as a unit.

> Analogy: Imagine each chromosome is a train carriage, and genes are passengers. Passengers in different carriages (different chromosomes) can independently choose when to get off at each station. But passengers in the same carriage tend to travel together — they are "linked." Only if they switch carriages at a stop (crossing over) do they separate.

How Linkage Alters Expected Ratios

Consider a dihybrid cross with two genes on the same chromosome. If the genes are completely linked (no crossing over between them), the F₂ ratio deviates from the expected 9:3:3:1.

Example: In Drosophila, body color (b⁺ = gray, dominant; b = black, recessive) and wing shape (vg⁺ = normal, dominant; vg = vestigial, recessive). These genes are on the same chromosome.

P generation: b⁺b⁺ vg⁺vg⁺ (gray, normal) × bb vgvg (black, vestigial)

F₁: All b⁺b vg⁺vg (gray, normal — heterozygous at both loci)

Test cross (F₁ × homozygous recessive): b⁺b vg⁺vg × bb vgvg

If unlinked (independent assortment): Expected 1:1:1:1 ratio (25% each of four phenotypes). If completely linked: Expect only parental types (gray-normal and black-vestigial) in ~50:50 ratio, with no recombinants.

What Morgan actually observed: Mostly parental types, but some recombinants (gray-vestigial and black-normal). This revealed that crossing over can separate linked genes.

2. Crossing Over and Recombination

The Mechanism

During prophase I of meiosis, homologous chromosomes pair up (synapsis) and form chiasmata (points of physical exchange). At each chiasma, non-sister chromatids break and rejoin, exchanging segments of DNA.

> Draw and label: Two homologous chromosomes (one maternal, one paternal) during prophase I. Show: (1) synaptonemal complex holding them together, (2) chiasma where crossing over occurs, (3) resulting recombinant chromatids with mixed maternal/paternal segments. Label: "Crossing over at chiasma creates recombinant chromatids."

Parental vs Recombinant Types

  • Parental types (non-recombinants): Offspring with the same combination of alleles as one of the parents (no crossing over between the genes in question).
  • Recombinant types: Offspring with new combinations of alleles, resulting from crossing over between the linked genes.

Crossing over is a random event. The probability of crossing over between two linked genes depends on the distance between them: the farther apart, the more likely a crossover will occur between them.

3. Recombination Frequency and Gene Mapping

Calculating Recombination Frequency

Recombination Frequency (RF) = (Number of recombinant offspring ÷ Total number of offspring) × 100%

RF is expressed as a percentage.

Example: A test cross of Drosophila heterozygous for linked genes A and B with a homozygous recessive (aabb) produced:

  • 420 offspring with parental phenotype AB
  • 380 offspring with parental phenotype ab
  • 90 offspring with recombinant phenotype Ab
  • 110 offspring with recombinant phenotype aB

Total offspring: 420 + 380 + 90 + 110 = 1000

Total recombinants: 90 + 110 = 200

RF = (200/1000) × 100% = 20%

This means genes A and B are 20 map units apart.

Map Units and CentiMorgans

  • 1% recombination frequency = 1 map unit = 1 centiMorgan (cM)
  • Map distance is an estimate of physical distance (not exact, but generally proportional).
  • Maximum RF = 50% — at this point, genes assort as if they were on different chromosomes (independent assortment). This occurs either because the genes truly are on different chromosomes OR because they are so far apart on the same chromosome that at least one crossover always occurs between them.

> Why 50% max? Each meiosis with a single crossover between two genes produces 50% recombinant and 50% parental chromatids. Even with multiple crossovers, the maximum observable RF is 50% because double crossovers can restore parental combinations.

Constructing Gene Maps

Given recombination frequencies between pairs of genes, we can determine their relative order and distances on a chromosome.

Example: Recombination frequencies between three genes on the same chromosome:

Gene pair RF
A — B 12%
A — C 5%
B — C 17%

Step 1: B and C are farthest apart (17%). They are the outer markers.

Step 2: A is 5 cM from C and 12 cM from B. Since 5 + 12 ≈ 17, A must be between C and B.

Gene order: C ——5 cM—— A ———12 cM——— B

(Total distance = 17 cM, which matches the calculated RF for C–B.)

> Note: If the sum of two distances is slightly less than the third, it may be due to double crossovers (two crossovers between the outer markers), which are not detected as recombinants in a two-point cross.

4. Morgan's Experiments with Drosophila melanogaster

Why Drosophila?

Thomas Hunt Morgan (1866–1945) at Columbia University chose the fruit fly (Drosophila melanogaster) for genetic research. Like Mendel's peas, the choice of organism was critical:

Advantage Explanation
Short generation time ~10 days from egg to adult at 25°C
Many offspring Females lay hundreds of eggs
Small and cheap Thousands of flies kept in milk bottles with banana-based medium
Only 4 chromosome pairs 3 pairs of autosomes + 1 pair of sex chromosomes (XX/XY) = simple karyotype
Polytene chromosomes Giant chromosomes in salivary glands with distinct banding patterns — mutations could be correlated with physical changes visible under microscope
Many visible mutations Eye color, wing shape, body color, bristle shape — easily scored under a dissecting microscope

Discovery of Sex Linkage (1910)

Morgan's breakthrough came when a white-eyed male appeared spontaneously in a culture of red-eyed flies:

  1. Cross 1: White-eyed male × red-eyed female → all F₁ red-eyed (showed white is recessive).
  2. Cross 2: F₁ × F₁ → F₂: all females red-eyed; males 50% red-eyed, 50% white-eyed.
  3. Test cross: White-eyed female × red-eyed male → all daughters red-eyed, all sons white-eyed (criss-cross inheritance).

Morgan correctly deduced that the white-eye gene is on the X chromosome, providing the first evidence that genes are located on chromosomes (the chromosome theory of inheritance).

Sturtevant and the First Genetic Map

Alfred Sturtevant, Morgan's undergraduate student, realised that recombination frequency could be used to map genes. In 1913, he constructed the first genetic map, placing six X-linked Drosophila genes in order based on pairwise recombination frequencies. This was revolutionary — genes could be mapped before anyone knew what DNA was!

> Draw and label: Sturtevant's first genetic map, showing: yellow body (0.0), white eye (1.5), vermilion eye (33.0), miniature wing (36.1), rudimentary wing (54.5). Label X-axis as "Map distance in centiMorgans (cM)."

5. Polygenic Inheritance

Beyond Mendel: Continuous Variation

Mendel studied discrete (discontinuous) traits — tall vs dwarf, round vs wrinkled. But most traits in nature show continuous variation: they don't fall into clear categories but form a smooth spectrum (e.g., human height ranges from ~140–210 cm).

Polygenic inheritance occurs when a trait is controlled by multiple genes (polygenes), each with a small, additive effect on the phenotype. The more genes involved, the more continuous the distribution appears.

> Analogy: Think of each polygene as dropping a small weight onto a scale. One gene alone produces three possible "weights" (AA, Aa, aa). Add a second gene → 5 possible categories. Add a third → 7 categories. With many genes, the distribution becomes a smooth, bell-shaped curve — the normal distribution.

Characteristics of Polygenic Traits

  • Continuous (quantitative) variation — no discrete categories
  • Normal (bell-shaped) distribution in the population
  • Strong environmental influence — environment can shift the phenotype
  • Each polygene has a small, additive effect
  • No single gene is "dominant" for the trait in a Mendelian sense

Examples

Trait Polygenes Environmental Factors
Human height Hundreds of genes (each tiny effect) Nutrition, health during childhood
Human skin color ~10+ genes (melanin production, distribution) Sun exposure (tanning)
Intelligence Many genes Education, nutrition, stimulation
Milk yield in cattle Multiple QTLs (quantitative trait loci) Diet, health, milking frequency
Grain yield in wheat Multiple genes Water, fertilizer, sunlight
Body mass Many genes (appetite regulation, metabolism) Diet, exercise

Polygenic vs Mendelian: A Comparison

Feature Mendelian (Discrete) Polygenic (Continuous)
Number of genes 1 (or few) Many
Phenotypic categories Clear, distinct Continuous spectrum
Distribution graph Bar chart (categories) Bell-shaped curve
Environmental effect Usually small Often large
Examples Blood type, pea shape, albinism Height, skin color, IQ
Punnett square Useful Impractical (too many combinations)

6. The Chi-Squared (χ²) Test in Genetics

Why Use Statistics?

When you perform a genetic cross, the observed offspring ratios rarely match the expected ratios exactly due to random chance. The χ² test tells us whether the deviation is small enough to be due to chance (accept null hypothesis) or large enough to suggest something else is going on (reject null hypothesis — maybe linkage, maybe the wrong model).

The χ² Formula

$$ \chi^2 = \sum \frac{(O - E)^2}{E} $$

Where:

  • O = observed frequency (what you counted)
  • E = expected frequency (what the Mendelian model predicts)
  • The sum (Σ) is taken over all phenotypic classes

Step-by-Step Protocol

Steps for χ² test in genetics:

  1. State the null hypothesis (H₀): "There is no significant difference between the observed and expected ratios. Any deviation is due to chance."
  2. State the alternative hypothesis (H₁): "There is a significant difference between observed and expected ratios. The deviation is NOT due to chance alone."
  3. Calculate expected numbers for each class based on the genetic model.
  4. Calculate χ² using the formula.
  5. Determine degrees of freedom (df): df = number of phenotypic classes − 1
  6. Compare χ² to the critical value at p = 0.05 (from a χ² table).
  7. Decision: If χ² < critical value → accept H₀ (data fit the model). If χ² ≥ critical value → reject H₀ (data do NOT fit the model).

Critical Values Table (for p = 0.05)

Degrees of Freedom (df) Critical χ² value (p = 0.05)
1 3.84
2 5.99
3 7.81
4 9.49
5 11.07

Example 1: Monohybrid Cross

A heterozygous tall pea plant (Tt) is crossed with a dwarf (tt). Expected ratio = 1 tall : 1 dwarf. Results: 62 tall, 44 dwarf. Do these data fit the 1:1 ratio?

H₀: Data fit a 1:1 ratio.

Total offspring = 106

Expected: Tall = 53, Dwarf = 53 (106 ÷ 2)

Phenotype O E O−E (O−E)² (O−E)²/E
Tall 62 53 +9 81 1.53
Dwarf 44 53 −9 81 1.53

χ² = 1.53 + 1.53 = 3.06

df = 2 − 1 = 1

Critical value at p=0.05, df=1 = 3.84

3.06 < 3.84 → Accept H₀. The deviation is not statistically significant; the data fit the expected 1:1 ratio.

Example 2: Dihybrid Cross

Two heterozygous pea plants (RrYy × RrYy) are crossed. Expected F₂ ratio = 9:3:3:1. Observed: 315 round-yellow, 108 round-green, 101 wrinkled-yellow, 32 wrinkled-green. Total = 556.

H₀: Data fit a 9:3:3:1 ratio.

Phenotype O Expected proportion E (prop. × 556) O−E (O−E)²/E
Round, Yellow 315 9/16 312.75 +2.25 0.016
Round, Green 108 3/16 104.25 +3.75 0.135
Wrinkled, Yellow 101 3/16 104.25 −3.25 0.101
Wrinkled, Green 32 1/16 34.75 −2.75 0.218

χ² = 0.016 + 0.135 + 0.101 + 0.218 = 0.470

df = 4 − 1 = 3

Critical value at p=0.05, df=3 = 7.81

0.470 < 7.81 → Accept H₀. The data fit the expected 9:3:3:1 ratio — genes are likely unlinked.

Example 3: Testing for Linkage

In a test cross of Drosophila, the following results were obtained:

Phenotype Observed
Gray body, normal wings 965
Black body, vestigial wings 944
Gray body, vestigial wings 206
Black body, normal wings 185
Total 2300

If the genes are unlinked, we expect a 1:1:1:1 ratio (575 of each).

H₀: Data fit a 1:1:1:1 ratio (genes unlinked).

Phenotype O E (O−E)²/E
Gray, Normal 965 575 (390)²/575 = 264.5
Black, Vestigial 944 575 (369)²/575 = 236.8
Gray, Vestigial 206 575 (−369)²/575 = 236.8
Black, Normal 185 575 (−390)²/575 = 264.5

χ² = 264.5 + 236.8 + 236.8 + 264.5 = 1002.6

df = 4 − 1 = 3

Critical value = 7.81

1002.6 > 7.81 → Reject H₀. The genes are linked.

Recombination frequency: (206 + 185)/2300 × 100% = 17% → genes are ~17 map units apart.

7. Common Misconceptions

Misconception Reality
"Linked genes can never be separated" Crossing over during meiosis CAN separate linked genes; the probability depends on their distance apart
"Recombination frequency can be >50%" Maximum observable RF is 50% — genes with 50% RF are indistinguishable from unlinked genes
"Map distance = exact physical distance" Map distance is proportional to but not identical to physical distance. Some regions have higher or lower recombination rates
"Polygenic traits are not genetic" They ARE genetic — just controlled by many genes, each with a small effect, plus environmental influences
"If χ² < critical value, the hypothesis is proven true" Statistics never "prove" a hypothesis. We can only say the data are consistent with (do not contradict) the hypothesis
"Degrees of freedom = sample size − 1" For χ² in genetics: df = number of phenotypic classes − 1
"R² values and χ² are the same thing" R² (coefficient of determination) is used in correlation/regression. χ² is for categorical data and goodness-of-fit

Practice Problems

Problem 1 (Recombination Frequency)

A test cross between a heterozygous individual (AaBb) and a homozygous recessive (aabb) produces:

  • AaBb: 450
  • aabb: 430
  • Aabb: 55
  • aaBb: 65

Calculate the recombination frequency. Are the genes linked?

Answer

Total offspring: 450 + 430 + 55 + 65 = 1000

Parental types: AaBb (450) + aabb (430) = 880

Recombinant types: Aabb (55) + aaBb (65) = 120

RF = (120/1000) × 100% = 12%

Yes, the genes are linked. With unlinked genes, we'd expect ~25% of each type (1:1:1:1). Here, recombinants are only 12%, showing linkage with crossing over between them occurring in 12% of meioses.

Problem 2 (Gene Mapping)

Recombination frequencies for four genes on the same chromosome:

Gene pair RF
P–Q 8%
P–R 3%
P–S 18%
Q–R 11%
Q–S 10%
R–S 21%

Determine the order and map distances.

Answer

Largest distance: R–S = 21% → these are outer markers.

Check: P–R (3) + P–S (18) = 21 ✓ and Q–R (11) + Q–S (10) = 21 ✓

Now position P: P–R = 3 and P–S = 18, so P is near R.

Position Q: Q–R = 11, so Q is 11 from R. Q–S = 10, so Q is 10 from S. 11 + 10 = 21 ✓.

Check P–Q: RF should be |11−3| = 8 ✓

Gene order and map:

```

R ——3 cM—— P ——5 cM—— Q ———10 cM——— S

<---------------21 cM---------------->

```

(Wait: R to P = 3, P to Q must be 5 to get R to Q = 8? But RF R–Q = 11. Let me recalculate.)

Re-examining: R–Q = 11, P–Q = 8, so P is between R and Q: R —3— P —8— Q.

Then Q–S = 10, R–S = 21 = 3 + 8 + 10 = 21 ✓.

Correct order: R — P — Q — S

Distances: R–P = 3 cM, P–Q = 8 cM, Q–S = 10 cM

Problem 3 (Chi-Squared)

A geneticist hypothesises that two genes assort independently (expected 9:3:3:1). Observed data: 290 round-yellow, 95 round-green, 105 wrinkled-yellow, 30 wrinkled-green. Total = 520. Use χ² to test the hypothesis at p = 0.05.

Answer

H₀: Data fit 9:3:3:1 ratio (independent assortment).

Phenotype O E (prop. × 520) (O−E)²/E
Round, Yellow 290 9/16 × 520 = 292.5 (290−292.5)²/292.5 = 0.0214
Round, Green 95 3/16 × 520 = 97.5 (95−97.5)²/97.5 = 0.0641
Wrinkled, Yellow 105 3/16 × 520 = 97.5 (105−97.5)²/97.5 = 0.5769
Wrinkled, Green 30 1/16 × 520 = 32.5 (30−32.5)²/32.5 = 0.1923

χ² = 0.0214 + 0.0641 + 0.5769 + 0.1923 = 0.8547

df = 4 − 1 = 3

Critical value (p = 0.05, df = 3) = 7.81

0.85 < 7.81 → Accept H₀. The data are consistent with independent assortment.

Problem 4 (Polygenic Inheritance)

Explain why human height shows a continuous, bell-shaped distribution in the population rather than discrete categories like Mendel's pea traits. Reference genetic and environmental factors.

Answer

Human height is a polygenic trait controlled by hundreds of genes, each with a small additive effect. With many genes contributing, the possible number of genotypic combinations is enormous, producing a continuous range rather than distinct categories.

Additionally, environmental factors (nutrition, health during childhood, disease, hormone levels) influence the final phenotype, smoothing the distribution further.

The combination of multiple additive genes + environmental variation produces the characteristic normal (bell-shaped) distribution seen in population data for height.

This contrasts with Mendelian traits (like pea plant height in Mendel's pure lines) where a single gene with two alleles produces three distinct genotypic classes (TT, Tt, tt) and two phenotypes under complete dominance.

Problem 5 (Linked Gene Cross)

In sweet peas, purple flower (P) is dominant to red (p) and long pollen (L) is dominant to round (l). A test cross of a PpLl plant yields: 242 purple-long, 248 red-round, 9 purple-round, 7 red-long. What is the recombination frequency, and what does it tell you?

Answer

Total: 242 + 248 + 9 + 7 = 506

Parental types: Purple-long (242) + Red-round (248) = 490

Recombinants: Purple-round (9) + Red-long (7) = 16

RF = (16/506) × 100% = 3.16%

This very low RF indicates that the genes for flower color and pollen shape are tightly linked — they are very close together on the same chromosome (~3.16 map units apart). Crossing over between them occurs in only ~3% of meioses.

The original heterozygous parent must have had the genotype PL/pl (dominant alleles on one homologue, recessive on the other), producing mostly PL and pl gametes.
