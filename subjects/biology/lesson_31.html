What You'll Learn

Imagine you're in a crowded stadium. You could shout to the person next to you, send a message to your section, announce something over the loudspeaker to everyone, or pass a note directly into someone's hand. Cells face the same communication challenges — and they've evolved remarkably similar strategies. In this lesson, you'll discover how cells talk to each other through chemical signals, from a single cell whispering to itself to hormones broadcasting across an entire organism. By the end, you'll understand:

  • The four types of chemical signaling: autocrine, paracrine, endocrine, and exocrine
  • How steroid hormones work slowly through gene expression, while peptide hormones act fast through second messengers
  • The critical role of the hypothalamus-pituitary axis as the body's master control system
  • How plant hormones regulate growth without a nervous system
  • Why understanding signaling mechanisms is essential for Paper 1 multiple-choice questions and Paper 2 data-analysis questions

1. The Four Types of Chemical Signaling

Cells don't just release chemicals randomly — they use distinct "delivery methods" depending on who the target is and how far away they are. Think of these as different postal services operating simultaneously in your body.

Chemical signaling — The process by which cells release signaling molecules (ligands) that bind to receptors on target cells, triggering a response. The distance between the signaling cell and the target cell defines the type.

1.1 Autocrine Signaling: "Talking to Yourself"

Autocrine (auto = self, crine = secrete) signaling occurs when a cell releases a signal that binds to receptors on its own surface. This is like leaving yourself a sticky note on your own desk.

  • The signaling cell and the target cell are the same cell
  • Common in the immune system — activated T cells release interleukin-2 (IL-2) that binds to their own IL-2 receptors, stimulating their own proliferation
  • Also seen in cancer cells, which may produce growth factors that stimulate their own uncontrolled division
  • Critical during development — cells in a developing embryo signal themselves to differentiate into specific cell types

Draw and label: A single cell with arrows showing signal molecules leaving the cell, then curving back to bind to receptors on that same cell's surface. Label: "Signaling cell = Target cell."

1.2 Paracrine Signaling: "Talking to Neighbors"

Paracrine (para = beside) signaling involves signals that travel short distances through the extracellular fluid to nearby cells. This is like whispering to the people sitting right next to you.

  • The signal diffuses locally — it does not enter the bloodstream
  • Typical range: a few cell diameters
  • Neurotransmitters are a specialized form of paracrine signaling — they cross a synapse (only ~20-40 nm)
  • Growth factors like platelet-derived growth factor (PDGF) stimulate nearby cells to divide during wound healing
  • Histamine released by mast cells acts on nearby blood vessel cells during inflammation

Draw and label: Two cells close together. Arrows show signal molecules released by the signaling cell diffusing across a short distance to receptors on the neighboring cell. Label: "Signaling cell" and "Nearby target cell." Note the short diffusion distance.

1.3 Endocrine Signaling: "Broadcasting to the Whole Body"

Endocrine (endo = within) signaling uses the bloodstream to carry hormones to distant target cells anywhere in the body. This is like using a public address system — the message reaches everyone, but only cells with the right "receiver" (receptor) respond.

  • Hormones are released by endocrine glands (pituitary, thyroid, adrenal, pancreas, ovaries/testes)
  • Travel through blood — can reach cells meters away from the source
  • Only cells expressing the specific receptor respond (e.g., thyroid-stimulating hormone only affects thyroid cells)
  • Effects are often slower but longer-lasting than neural signals

Key IB distinction: Endocrine glands are ductless — they secrete directly into blood. This distinguishes them from exocrine glands.

Draw and label: An endocrine gland (e.g., pituitary at base of brain) releasing hormone molecules into a blood vessel. Arrows show the hormone traveling through the blood to distant target cells with matching receptors. Cells without the receptor are unaffected despite being bathed in the hormone.

Feature Endocrine Paracrine Autocrine
Distance Long (via blood) Short (extracellular fluid) Zero (same cell)
Speed Slower (seconds to hours) Fast (milliseconds to seconds) Fast
Duration Prolonged Brief Brief
Example Insulin from pancreas Neurotransmitters at synapse IL-2 on T cells

1.4 Exocrine Signaling: "Releasing to the Outside"

Exocrine (exo = outside) signaling is fundamentally different — exocrine glands release substances through ducts to body surfaces or cavities, not into the blood. These are not strictly "signaling" in the same sense but are often tested alongside endocrine glands for comparison.

  • Exocrine glands have ducts — this is the defining IB feature
  • Secrete enzymes, sweat, saliva, mucus, milk, digestive juices
  • Examples: salivary glands (ducts to mouth), sweat glands (ducts to skin surface), pancreas (has BOTH: exocrine — digestive enzymes via pancreatic duct; endocrine — insulin/glucagon into blood from islets of Langerhans)

Paper 1 Tip: The IB loves to ask: "Which type of signaling involves hormones traveling through the bloodstream?" Answer: endocrine. They may also ask you to distinguish between endocrine and exocrine glands — the key word is duct. Endocrine = ductless, exocrine = has ducts.

Paper 2 Tip: If given a diagram of a gland with a duct leading to a surface, identify it as exocrine. If hormones are shown entering blood vessels directly, it's endocrine. The pancreas is a favorite because it's both — be precise about which part you're describing.

2. Hormone Classes: Steroid vs. Peptide/Protein

Hormones fall into two major classes based on their chemical nature. This distinction is absolutely critical because it determines everything about how they work — where their receptors are, how fast they act, and how long their effects last.

Hormone — A chemical messenger produced in one part of the body that travels via the bloodstream to target cells elsewhere, where it binds to specific receptors and triggers a response.

2.1 Steroid Hormones: Lipid-Soluble, Slow, and Powerful

Steroid hormones are derived from cholesterol — yes, the same molecule that's famously associated with heart disease is the precursor for testosterone, estrogen, cortisol, and aldosterone.

Key properties:

  • Lipid-soluble (hydrophobic): They can dissolve through the phospholipid bilayer of cell membranes
  • Derived from cholesterol: All share the characteristic four-ring steroid structure
  • Receptors are intracellular: Located in the cytoplasm or nucleus — because the hormone can simply diffuse across the membrane
  • Slow-acting but long-lasting: Effects take hours to days because they involve gene transcription and protein synthesis
  • Travel in blood bound to carrier proteins: Since they're hydrophobic, they need transport proteins (e.g., albumin, sex hormone-binding globulin)

Examples of steroid hormones:

Hormone Source Major Function
Cortisol Adrenal cortex Stress response, glucose metabolism, anti-inflammatory
Aldosterone Adrenal cortex Sodium/water retention, blood pressure regulation
Testosterone Testes (and adrenal cortex) Male sexual development, muscle growth
Estradiol (Estrogen) Ovaries Female sexual development, menstrual cycle
Progesterone Ovaries, placenta Uterine preparation, pregnancy maintenance

Steroid Mechanism of Action (step-by-step):

This is the classic "genomic" pathway. Imagine the hormone as a key that unlocks specific genes.

  1. Diffusion: The steroid hormone diffuses freely through the plasma membrane (it's hydrophobic, so no transporter needed)
  2. Receptor binding: Inside the cell, the hormone binds to its specific intracellular receptor protein (often in the cytoplasm). This causes the receptor to change shape (conformational change), releasing inhibitory chaperone proteins (like Hsp90)
  3. Translocation: The hormone-receptor complex moves into the nucleus
  4. DNA binding: The complex binds to specific DNA sequences called Hormone Response Elements (HREs) in the promoter regions of target genes. Think of HREs as "docking stations" on DNA
  5. Transcription: This binding activates (or represses) transcription of specific genes by RNA polymerase II
  6. mRNA production: mRNA transcripts are produced and processed
  7. Translation: mRNA exits the nucleus and is translated on ribosomes into new proteins
  8. Cellular response: The newly synthesized proteins mediate the hormone's effect (e.g., enzymes, structural proteins, transporters)

Draw and label: A diagram showing:

  • Top: A steroid hormone molecule (small, hydrophobic) approaching the cell membrane
  • Step 1: Hormone crosses the phospholipid bilayer (show it passing through — label "Simple diffusion")
  • Step 2: Inside cytoplasm, hormone binds to receptor (label "Hormone-receptor complex forms")
  • Step 3: Complex moves into nucleus (arrow through nuclear pore)
  • Step 4: Complex binds to DNA at HRE (label "HRE — Hormone Response Element")
  • Step 5: RNA polymerase transcribes gene → mRNA
  • Step 6: mRNA exits nucleus, ribosome translates → protein
  • Step 7: New proteins produce the cellular response

Worked Example: Cortisol Action

Scenario: During a stressful exam, your adrenal cortex releases cortisol. How does cortisol increase blood glucose?

Step-by-step mechanism:

  1. Cortisol (steroid hormone, derived from cholesterol) is released from the adrenal cortex into the blood
  2. It travels bound to corticosteroid-binding globulin (carrier protein)
  3. At liver cells (hepatocytes), cortisol dissociates from its carrier and diffuses across the plasma membrane
  4. Inside the cytoplasm, cortisol binds to the glucocorticoid receptor (GR), causing a conformational change that releases Hsp90 chaperone proteins
  5. The cortisol-GR complex translocates to the nucleus
  6. It binds to Glucocorticoid Response Elements (GREs) in the promoter regions of genes encoding gluconeogenic enzymes (e.g., glucose-6-phosphatase, phosphoenolpyruvate carboxykinase — PEPCK)
  7. Transcription of these genes increases → more mRNA → more enzyme proteins synthesized on ribosomes
  8. These enzymes increase gluconeogenesis (glucose synthesis from amino acids and other non-carbohydrate precursors) in the liver
  9. Result: Blood glucose rises — but this takes hours, not seconds

Why this matters: Cortisol cannot raise blood glucose instantly. This is why stress doesn't immediately spike your blood sugar within seconds — the genomic mechanism is inherently slow.

Paper 1 Tip: A classic question: "Steroid hormones act slowly because they..." — the answer always involves gene transcription and protein synthesis. Look for keywords like "transcription," "translation," or "gene expression."

Paper 2 Tip: When comparing steroid and peptide hormone action in a long-answer question, structure your answer around: (1) chemical nature → (2) receptor location → (3) mechanism → (4) speed → (5) duration. Use a table if appropriate.

2.2 Peptide/Protein Hormones: Water-Soluble, Fast, and Amplified

Peptide and protein hormones are chains of amino acids. They range from small peptides (like ADH — just 9 amino acids) to large proteins (like insulin — 51 amino acids in two chains).

Key properties:

  • Water-soluble (hydrophilic): Cannot cross the phospholipid bilayer
  • Receptors are on the cell surface: Transmembrane proteins with extracellular binding sites
  • Use second messenger systems: The hormone (first messenger) triggers production of an intracellular second messenger (e.g., cAMP, Ca²⁺, IP₃)
  • Fast-acting but shorter duration: Effects in seconds to minutes — because they modify existing proteins (via phosphorylation), not gene expression
  • Travel freely in blood: No carrier proteins needed (they're water-soluble)
  • Signal amplification: One hormone molecule can trigger production of many second messenger molecules, each activating multiple enzymes — a massive amplification cascade

Examples of peptide/protein hormones:

Hormone Type Source Function
Insulin Protein (51 aa) Pancreatic β-cells Lowers blood glucose
Glucagon Peptide (29 aa) Pancreatic α-cells Raises blood glucose
ADH Peptide (9 aa) Hypothalamus → posterior pituitary Water retention
Growth hormone Protein (191 aa) Anterior pituitary Growth, metabolism
FSH, LH Glycoproteins Anterior pituitary Reproduction

3. The GPCR → cAMP → PKA Pathway (Classic Peptide Hormone Mechanism)

This is arguably the most important signaling pathway in IB Biology HL. It illustrates second messenger action, signal amplification, and the general principle of how peptide hormones work.

3.1 Step-by-Step: Glucagon as the Example

Let's trace glucagon's action on a liver cell (hepatocyte) to raise blood glucose:

Step 1 — Hormone binding: Glucagon (first messenger) binds to its specific GPCR (G Protein-Coupled Receptor) on the hepatocyte surface. The receptor spans the membrane 7 times (7-transmembrane domain receptor).

Step 2 — G-protein activation: The occupied receptor activates a G-protein (a trimeric protein with α, β, and γ subunits) on the inner leaflet of the membrane. The G-protein exchanges GDP for GTP on its α-subunit and dissociates — the α-subunit is now active.

Step 3 — Adenylyl cyclase activation: The activated Gα-GTP moves along the membrane and activates the enzyme adenylyl cyclase (also spelled adenylyl cyclase or adenylate cyclase).

Step 4 — cAMP production: Adenylyl cyclase converts ATP → cAMP (cyclic adenosine monophosphate). This is the second messenger. Crucially, one activated adenylyl cyclase produces many cAMP molecules — this is the first stage of signal amplification.

Step 5 — PKA activation: cAMP binds to Protein Kinase A (PKA). PKA is a tetramer with two regulatory subunits and two catalytic subunits. cAMP binding to the regulatory subunits releases the catalytic subunits, which are now active kinases.

Step 6 — Phosphorylation cascade: Active PKA phosphorylates (adds phosphate groups to) multiple target enzymes. Each PKA molecule phosphorylates many targets — second stage of amplification.

Key targets in the glucagon response:

  • Phosphorylase kinase → activates glycogen phosphorylase → breaks down glycogen to glucose-1-phosphate (glycogenolysis)
  • Glycogen synthase (inhibited when phosphorylated) → stops glycogen synthesis
  • Fructose-2,6-bisphosphatase → reduces glycolysis, promotes gluconeogenesis
  • Transcription factors (CREB) → long-term changes in gene expression

Step 7 — Cellular response: Glucose is released from the hepatocyte into the blood. Blood glucose rises.

Draw and label: A detailed diagram of this pathway:

  • Cell membrane with GPCR (7 wavy transmembrane segments)
  • Outside: Glucagon (labeled "First messenger") binding to receptor
  • Inside membrane: G-protein (α, β, γ subunits) → activation → α-GTP moves to adenylyl cyclase
  • Adenylyl cyclase converting ATP → cAMP (labeled "Second messenger")
  • Multiple cAMP molecules (show at least 4-5) binding to PKA
  • PKA releasing catalytic subunits
  • Catalytic subunits phosphorylating target enzymes
  • Arrows showing amplification: "1 hormone → many cAMP → many PKA → many phosphorylated enzymes"
  • Final effect: Glycogen → Glucose-1-P → Glucose → Blood

Worked Example: Calculating Signal Amplification

Scenario: A single glucagon molecule binds to a liver cell. If each adenylyl cyclase produces 100 cAMP molecules, each cAMP activates 1 PKA (which can phosphorylate 10 target enzymes), and each target enzyme processes 1000 substrate molecules per second, how many glucose molecules can be produced per second from one glucagon binding event?

Calculation:

  1. 1 glucagon → 1 activated GPCR → 1 activated G-protein → 1 activated adenylyl cyclase
  2. 1 adenylyl cyclase → 100 cAMP molecules
  3. 100 cAMP → activate 100 PKA molecules
  4. 100 PKA × 10 phosphorylations each = 1,000 phosphorylated target enzymes
  5. 1,000 enzymes × 1,000 substrate/second = 1,000,000 glucose molecules/second

Total amplification: One glucagon molecule → 1,000,000 glucose molecules per second. This is a 10⁶-fold amplification!

IB Significance: This explains why very low hormone concentrations (picomolar to nanomolar) can produce robust physiological responses. The cascade multiplies the signal at each step.

3.2 The cAMP → PKA Pathway is a "Cascade"

The power of this system is signal amplification — think of it like a pyramid scheme where one person recruits ten, who each recruit ten more.

Step Molecules Fold Amplification
Hormone bound 1 —
cAMP produced ~100 ×100
PKA activated ~100 ×1
Enzymes phosphorylated ~1,000 ×10
Product molecules ~1,000,000 ×1,000
Total ~10⁶

Paper 1 Tip: Questions about signal amplification often ask: "What is the advantage of a second messenger system?" The correct answer involves amplification — one extracellular signal produces a large intracellular response. The key comparison: steroid hormones cannot amplify signals this way because they work 1:1 with DNA.

4. Insulin Signaling: The Tyrosine Kinase Receptor Pathway

Insulin doesn't use the GPCR-cAMP pathway. Instead, it uses a receptor tyrosine kinase (RTK) — a fundamentally different receptor type that is equally important for IB.

4.1 The Insulin Receptor

The insulin receptor is a tyrosine kinase receptor:

  • It has two α-subunits (extracellular, hormone-binding) and two β-subunits (transmembrane, with intrinsic kinase activity)
  • Insulin binding causes the receptor to autophosphorylate — the β-subunits phosphorylate tyrosine residues on each other
  • This activates the kinase domain, which then phosphorylates insulin receptor substrate (IRS) proteins
  • IRS activates a cascade involving PI3-kinase → Akt/PKB

4.2 GLUT4 Translocation — The Key Outcome

The most important downstream effect for IB is GLUT4 translocation:

  1. Insulin → receptor activation → signaling cascade
  2. In muscle and adipose (fat) cells, GLUT4 (glucose transporter type 4) is stored in intracellular vesicles
  3. Insulin signaling triggers these vesicles to fuse with the plasma membrane
  4. GLUT4 is inserted into the membrane, creating channels for glucose to enter the cell
  5. Blood glucose decreases as glucose is taken up by cells

Draw and label: A cell (muscle or fat cell) before and after insulin:

  • Before: GLUT4 shown inside the cell in vesicles (label: "GLUT4 in cytoplasmic vesicles"). Plasma membrane has no GLUT4. Label: "Low glucose uptake."
  • After insulin: Arrows showing vesicles fusing with membrane. GLUT4 now embedded in plasma membrane. Glucose molecules shown entering through GLUT4. Label: "High glucose uptake."

Paper 2 Tip: In Type 2 diabetes, insulin resistance means this GLUT4 translocation pathway is impaired — cells don't respond to insulin by moving GLUT4 to the surface. This is a common data-analysis question: graphs showing glucose uptake in normal vs. diabetic cells ± insulin.

5. The Hypothalamus-Pituitary Axis

The hypothalamus and pituitary gland form the master control system of the endocrine system. This is like the conductor of an orchestra — it doesn't play every instrument, but it coordinates them all.

5.1 Anatomy Overview

Draw and label: A sagittal (side) view of the brain showing:

  • Hypothalamus: Small region at base of brain, above pituitary. Label: "Hypothalamus — integrates neural and endocrine signals."
  • Infundibulum (pituitary stalk): Connects hypothalamus to pituitary. Contains blood vessels (hypophyseal portal system) and nerve fibers.
  • Anterior pituitary (adenohypophysis): Glandular tissue, front portion. Label: "Anterior pituitary — produces and releases tropic hormones."
  • Posterior pituitary (neurohypophysis): Neural tissue, back portion. Label: "Posterior pituitary — stores and releases ADH and oxytocin."
  • Hypophyseal portal system: A special capillary network that carries releasing/inhibiting hormones from hypothalamus directly to anterior pituitary without dilution in general circulation.

5.2 Anterior Pituitary: The Portal System

The anterior pituitary is controlled by releasing and inhibiting hormones from the hypothalamus, delivered via the hypophyseal portal system.

Mechanism:

  1. Hypothalamic neurons synthesize releasing/inhibiting hormones
  2. These are secreted into capillaries at the base of the hypothalamus
  3. The hypophyseal portal veins carry them directly to a second capillary bed in the anterior pituitary
  4. Anterior pituitary endocrine cells respond by releasing (or inhibiting release of) tropic hormones into the general circulation

Tropic hormone — A hormone whose target is another endocrine gland, stimulating it to release its own hormone. From the Greek "tropos" meaning "turning toward."

5.3 Posterior Pituitary: Direct Neural Connection

The posterior pituitary is fundamentally different — it's an extension of the brain, not a true gland.

  • Neurons with cell bodies in the hypothalamus (supraoptic and paraventricular nuclei) extend their axons down through the infundibulum to terminate in the posterior pituitary
  • These neurons synthesize ADH (antidiuretic hormone, also called vasopressin) and oxytocin
  • The hormones travel down the axons and are stored in axon terminals in the posterior pituitary
  • When the neurons fire action potentials, the hormones are released directly into the blood

Key point: The posterior pituitary does NOT synthesize hormones — it only stores and releases them. The synthesis occurs in the hypothalamus.

Connection to hypothalamus Hormonal (portal blood vessels) Neural (axons)
Hormones TSH, ACTH, FSH, LH, GH, Prolactin ADH, Oxytocin
Synthesis site Anterior pituitary itself Hypothalamic neurons
Control Releasing/inhibiting hormones Action potentials
Tissue type Glandular epithelium Neural tissue

Worked Example: Tracing the Stress Response

Scenario: You encounter a bear while hiking. Trace the hormonal pathway from perception to physiological response.

Step-by-step:

  1. Sensory input: Visual and auditory cortex detect the bear → signals sent to amygdala and hypothalamus
  1. Immediate response — Sympathetic nervous system (neural): Hypothalamus activates sympathetic neurons → adrenal medulla releases epinephrine (adrenaline) → fight-or-flight response in seconds (increased heart rate, dilated pupils, etc.)
  1. Sustained response — HPA Axis (hormonal):
  • Hypothalamus releases CRH into the hypophyseal portal system
  • CRH reaches anterior pituitary → releases ACTH into general circulation
  • ACTH travels to adrenal cortex → stimulates cortisol release
  • Cortisol (steroid hormone) enters target cells → binds intracellular receptor → alters gene expression
  • Effects: increased blood glucose (gluconeogenesis), suppressed immune function, enhanced alertness
  • This takes minutes to hours and sustains the stress response
  1. Negative feedback: Rising cortisol inhibits CRH and ACTH release → system returns to baseline when stressor is removed

Exam relevance: This illustrates the difference between fast neural responses (seconds, via epinephrine) and slower hormonal responses (hours, via cortisol — a steroid working genomically).

6. Plant Hormones (Phytohormones)

Plants don't have a nervous system, but they're far from passive. They use hormones to coordinate growth, development, and responses to the environment. Three plant hormones are essential for IB HL.

6.1 Auxin (IAA) and Phototropism

Auxin (specifically IAA — indole-3-acetic acid) is the master growth regulator in plants. Its most famous role is in phototropism — growth toward light.

The Cholodny-Went Hypothesis:

This is one of the classic experiments in biology and is frequently tested.

  1. When light shines on one side of a shoot tip (coleoptile), auxin is redistributed to the shaded side
  2. Higher auxin concentration on the shaded side stimulates cell elongation on that side
  3. Unequal elongation causes the shoot to bend toward the light (the shaded side grows faster, pushing the tip toward the light)

Acid Growth Hypothesis (mechanism of auxin action):

  1. Auxin binds to its receptor (TIR1/AFB) → stimulates proton pumps (H⁺-ATPases) in the plasma membrane
  2. H⁺ ions are pumped into the cell wall (apoplast), lowering the pH (acidifying the cell wall)
  3. Low pH activates expansin proteins that break hydrogen bonds between cellulose microfibrils and hemicellulose
  4. The cell wall loosens → turgor pressure from the vacuole expands the cell → cell elongation

Draw and label: A shoot tip with unilateral light:

  • Left side: Light. Label: "Light — auxin moves to shaded side."
  • Right side: Shaded. Higher auxin concentration shown by more dots.
  • Below the tip: Cells on the shaded (right) side are longer than cells on the illuminated (left) side.
  • The shoot bends toward the light. Label the bending region: "Cells on shaded side elongate more → bending toward light."

Paper 1 Tip: Phototropism is positive phototropism in shoots (grow toward light). Roots show negative phototropism (grow away from light). In roots, high auxin concentration inhibits cell elongation — the opposite of shoots. This is a common trick question.

6.2 Gibberellin and Seed Germination

Gibberellins are a family of plant hormones that promote stem elongation, fruit development, and — most importantly for IB — seed germination.

Mechanism of gibberellin in seed germination:

Think of a seed as a packed lunch box waiting for the right signal to open.

  1. Water uptake by the dry seed (imbibition) triggers the embryo to produce gibberellin
  2. Gibberellin diffuses to the aleurone layer (a protein-rich layer surrounding the endosperm in cereal grains like barley)
  3. In aleurone cells, gibberellin triggers gene expression → synthesis of α-amylase and other hydrolytic enzymes
  4. The aleurone cells secrete α-amylase into the endosperm (the starchy food reserve)
  5. α-Amylase hydrolyzes starch → maltose → glucose
  6. Glucose is transported to the growing embryo, providing energy and carbon for growth until the seedling can photosynthesize

Draw and label: A barley grain cross-section:

  • Outer layer: seed coat
  • Aleurone layer (label: "Aleurone layer — receives gibberellin signal, produces α-amylase")
  • Endosperm (label: "Endosperm — starch stores. α-amylase breaks down starch → sugar")
  • Embryo (label: "Embryo — produces gibberellin, receives sugars for growth")

Arrows: Embryo → Gibberellin → Aleurone → α-Amylase → Endosperm (starch → sugar) → Embryo (growth)

6.3 Ethylene and Fruit Ripening

Ethylene (C₂H₄) is unique — it's a gas at room temperature, making it the only gaseous hormone. It's central to fruit ripening and senescence (aging).

Ethylene and fruit ripening — a positive feedback loop:

  1. A ripening fruit produces a small amount of ethylene
  2. Ethylene gas diffuses to neighboring cells and fruits
  3. It triggers more ethylene production in those cells (positive feedback)
  4. Ethylene activates genes for enzymes that:
  • Break down chlorophyll (loss of green color)
  • Synthesize carotenoids and anthocyanins (color change)
  • Convert starch to sugar (sweetening)
  • Break down pectin in cell walls (softening)
  • Produce volatile aromatic compounds (the "ripe smell")

"One bad apple spoils the bunch" — This saying has a basis in ethylene biology. A single ripe fruit producing ethylene accelerates ripening in surrounding fruits.

Worked Example: Commercial Fruit Ripening

Scenario: Bananas are harvested green in Central America and shipped to Europe. How is ethylene used commercially, and why is this economically important?

Analysis:

  1. Harvest: Bananas are picked unripe (green, hard) — they survive shipping better and are less susceptible to bruising
  2. Shipping: Transported in controlled-atmosphere containers at ~13°C with low oxygen to minimize ethylene production and ripening
  3. At destination: Bananas are placed in ripening rooms and exposed to ethylene gas (typically 100-150 ppm)
  4. Ripening: Ethylene triggers the ripening cascade — chlorophyll breakdown, starch-to-sugar conversion, pectin degradation, color change to yellow
  5. Economic significance: This allows supply to match demand. Ripening can be timed so bananas are perfectly yellow when they reach supermarket shelves. Without ethylene control, bananas would either arrive spoiled or remain unripe.

IB connection: This illustrates the practical application of understanding plant hormone biology. It also demonstrates how ethylene's gaseous nature makes it uniquely suited for treating large batches of fruit in sealed rooms.

8. Common Misconceptions

Misconception Reality
"Steroid hormones bind to receptors on the cell surface." No.** Steroids are lipid-soluble and cross the membrane to bind intracellular receptors. Only peptide hormones use cell-surface receptors.
"Peptide hormones enter the cell to work." No.** Peptide hormones cannot cross the membrane. They bind to surface receptors and generate second messengers inside.
"The posterior pituitary synthesizes ADH and oxytocin." No.** These are synthesized in hypothalamic neurons and only stored/released by the posterior pituitary.
"All hormones travel via the bloodstream." Most do, but paracrine signals act locally and autocrine signals act on the same cell. Exocrine secretions go through ducts.
"Signal amplification means the hormone concentration increases." No.** It means one hormone-receptor binding event triggers production of many intracellular second messengers, amplifying the signal strength.
"Phototropism occurs because light destroys auxin on the illuminated side." No. The Cholodny-Went hypothesis states auxin is redistributed** to the shaded side, not destroyed.
"Insulin works through the cAMP second messenger system." No.** Insulin uses a receptor tyrosine kinase, not GPCR-cAMP. Glucagon uses the cAMP pathway.
"All exocrine glands are part of the endocrine system." No.** Exocrine glands have ducts and release to surfaces/cavities. Only endocrine glands (ductless, release into blood) are part of the endocrine system.

9. Practice Problems

Problem 1 (Paper 1 Style — Multiple Choice)

Which of the following correctly describes the mechanism of action of a peptide hormone?

A. It diffuses through the plasma membrane and binds to an intracellular receptor, which then activates gene transcription.

B. It binds to a cell surface receptor, which activates a G-protein that stimulates adenylyl cyclase to produce cAMP.

C. It enters the nucleus directly and binds to a hormone response element on DNA.

D. It dissolves in the phospholipid bilayer and activates membrane-bound enzymes.

Answer: B. Peptide hormones cannot cross the membrane (ruling out A, C, and D). They bind to cell surface receptors, often GPCRs, which activate G-proteins and generate second messengers like cAMP.

(a) Compare the time course of the two hormones. [2 marks]

(b) Explain the difference in timing based on the mechanisms of action of steroid and peptide hormones. [4 marks]

(c) Predict what would happen to the peptide hormone response if the cells were pretreated with a drug that inhibits adenylyl cyclase. Justify your answer. [2 marks]

Answers:

(a) The peptide hormone acts rapidly — the response is detectable within 1 minute and reaches near-maximum by 5-30 minutes, then declines. The steroid hormone acts slowly — no detectable response at 1 minute, increasing gradually to maximum only after 12 hours. [2 marks]

(b) The peptide hormone binds to a cell surface receptor and activates a second messenger cascade (e.g., GPCR → cAMP → PKA). This modifies pre-existing proteins by phosphorylation — a rapid process (seconds to minutes). However, the effect is transient as second messengers are degraded and phosphatases reverse phosphorylation.

The steroid hormone must diffuse into the cell, bind an intracellular receptor, translocate to the nucleus, bind to HREs, and activate transcription → mRNA production → translation → protein synthesis. This requires hours. However, the newly synthesized proteins persist longer, giving a more sustained effect. [4 marks — 2 for each mechanism with timing explanation]

(c) The peptide hormone response would be greatly reduced or abolished. If the peptide hormone signals through the GPCR → adenylyl cyclase → cAMP pathway, inhibiting adenylyl cyclase would prevent cAMP production. Without the second messenger, PKA would not be activated and downstream targets would not be phosphorylated, so the cellular response would not occur. [2 marks]

Problem 3 (Paper 2 Style — Long Answer)

Explain the role of the hypothalamus and pituitary gland in coordinating hormone secretion, using a named example. [7 marks]

Model Answer:

The hypothalamus is the integrating center that links the nervous system to the endocrine system. It controls the pituitary gland, which in turn regulates many other endocrine glands.

Anterior pituitary (hormonal connection):

  • Hypothalamic neurons secrete releasing hormones (e.g., TRH — thyrotropin-releasing hormone) into capillaries at the base of the hypothalamus
  • These hormones travel via the hypophyseal portal system (a specialized blood vessel network) directly to the anterior pituitary
  • TRH stimulates specific cells in the anterior pituitary to release TSH (thyroid-stimulating hormone, a tropic hormone)
  • TSH enters the general circulation and stimulates the thyroid gland to produce and release thyroxine (T₄ and T₃)
  • Thyroxine increases metabolic rate in target tissues throughout the body
  • Negative feedback: When thyroxine levels rise, they inhibit TRH release from the hypothalamus and TSH release from the anterior pituitary, maintaining homeostasis

Posterior pituitary (neural connection):

  • Neurons in the hypothalamus (supraoptic and paraventricular nuclei) synthesize ADH
  • ADH is transported down axons through the infundibulum to the posterior pituitary, where it is stored in axon terminals
  • When osmoreceptors in the hypothalamus detect increased blood osmolarity (dehydration), these neurons fire action potentials
  • This triggers exocytosis of ADH from the posterior pituitary into the blood
  • ADH acts on the collecting ducts of the kidney to increase water reabsorption, lowering blood osmolarity

The key distinction is that the anterior pituitary is controlled hormonally (releasing factors via portal blood) and synthesizes its own hormones, while the posterior pituitary is controlled neurally and only stores/releases hormones made in the hypothalamus. [7 marks]

Problem 4 (Paper 1 Style — Multiple Choice)

In the Cholodny-Went hypothesis of phototropism, what causes the shoot to bend toward light?

A. Light destroys auxin on the illuminated side of the shoot.

B. Auxin is redistributed to the shaded side, where it stimulates cell elongation.

C. Light stimulates auxin production on the illuminated side.

D. Auxin inhibits cell elongation on the illuminated side of the shoot.

Answer: B. The Cholodny-Went hypothesis states that auxin moves to the shaded side, where higher concentrations stimulate greater cell elongation, causing the shoot to bend toward the light.

Problem 5 (Paper 2 Style — Application)

In barley seed germination, gibberellin produced by the embryo triggers α-amylase production in the aleurone layer. A student investigates this by treating de-embryonated barley seeds (embryo removed) with either water or gibberellin solution and measuring α-amylase activity after 24 hours.

Treatment α-Amylase activity (arbitrary units)
Intact seeds (control) 85
De-embryonated + water 5
De-embryonated + gibberellin 78

(a) Explain why de-embryonated seeds with water show almost no α-amylase activity. [2 marks]

(b) Explain why adding gibberellin restores α-amylase activity. [2 marks]

(c) Suggest why the gibberellin-treated seeds still show slightly less activity than intact seeds. [1 mark]

Answers:

(a) The embryo is the natural source of gibberellin in germinating seeds. Without the embryo, no gibberellin is produced, so the aleurone layer does not receive the signal to synthesize α-amylase. Water alone cannot substitute for the hormonal signal. [2 marks]

(b) Gibberellin is the signal that normally comes from the embryo. Adding exogenous gibberellin replaces the missing embryo-derived signal. The gibberellin diffuses to the aleurone layer, activates gene expression for α-amylase synthesis, and the enzyme is produced and secreted. [2 marks]

(c) The intact embryo may produce gibberellin continuously with optimal timing and concentration, or the embryo produces other factors that synergize with gibberellin. Also, de-embryonation may damage the seed. [1 mark for any reasonable suggestion]

Term Definition

This lesson covers the core C2.1 HL content on chemical signaling. The key to mastering this material is understanding why different hormone classes use different mechanisms — it all follows logically from their chemical properties. If a hormone can cross the membrane (steroid), it uses intracellular receptors and changes gene expression. If it can't (peptide), it must signal through surface receptors and second messenger cascades. Keep returning to this fundamental logic in your revision.
