1. What Are Enzymes?

The Catalyst Concept

Imagine you're at a party in a huge room. Two people who would love to meet are standing at opposite ends. They could eventually wander into each other, but it would take forever. Now imagine a mutual friend who grabs each by the hand and pulls them together — suddenly the introduction happens in seconds.

Enzymes are the mutual friends of biochemistry. They bring reactants together in exactly the right orientation, dramatically speeding up reactions without being consumed themselves.

Enzyme: A biological catalyst — almost always a globular protein — that speeds up a specific chemical reaction by lowering its activation energy. Enzymes are not consumed or permanently changed by the reaction; each enzyme molecule can catalyze thousands of reactions per second.

Why Globular Proteins?

Globular proteins have a roughly spherical shape with hydrophilic amino acids on the outside (soluble in aqueous environments like cytoplasm) and a carefully folded interior that creates a precise three-dimensional pocket — the active site. This folding is maintained by hydrogen bonds, ionic bonds, hydrophobic interactions, and disulfide bridges. Without this precise folding, the enzyme cannot function.

Key enzyme properties:

Property Explanation
Specificity Each enzyme catalyzes only one reaction (or one type of reaction)
Reusability Not consumed; emerges unchanged after the reaction
Efficiency Increases reaction rates by factors of 10⁶ to 10¹²
Sensitivity Activity depends on temperature, pH, and other conditions
Regulation Activity can be turned on/off by inhibitors and activators

2. Activation Energy: The Energy Hill

Why Don't Reactions Happen Spontaneously?

Even energetically favorable reactions (negative ΔG) don't happen instantly at room temperature. Consider glucose and oxygen: they're thermodynamically eager to react (that's why respiration works), but you can leave a sugar cube on the counter for years without it bursting into flames.

Why? Because molecules must first reach a high-energy, unstable transition state before they can convert to products. The energy required to reach this state is the activation energy (Eₐ).

Activation Energy (Eₐ): The minimum energy that colliding reactant molecules must possess for a reaction to occur. It's the height of the energy barrier separating reactants from products.

Enzymes Lower the Activation Energy

Enzymes provide an alternative reaction pathway with a lower Eₐ. They do NOT change the overall ΔG of the reaction — the starting and ending energy levels are the same. They simply lower the "hill" that reactants must climb.

Draw and label this energy profile diagram:

```

Energy

^

└──────────────────────────────→ Reaction Progress

Catalyzed pathway (dashed line): lower "hill" in between

```

Key points for your diagram:

  1. Draw the uncatalyzed curve as a high curve — large Eₐ
  2. Draw the catalyzed curve with a much smaller hump
  3. Label: "Eₐ (uncatalyzed)" and "Eₐ (catalyzed)" — show the catalyzed one is much lower
  4. Label the transition state at the peak
  5. Label ΔG — note it is identical for both pathways
  6. The starting energy of reactants and ending energy of products are the same

How Enzymes Actually Lower Eₐ

Enzymes lower activation energy through several mechanisms:

  1. Proximity and orientation: Binding substrates brings them close together in the correct orientation
  2. Strain/distortion: The active site may slightly distort substrate bonds, making them easier to break
  3. Microenvironment: The active site may have a different pH or polarity than the surrounding solution
  4. Direct participation: Amino acid side chains may temporarily form bonds with the substrate
  5. Desolvation: Removing water molecules from the active site creates a more reactive environment

3. Active Site and Substrate Specificity

The Active Site

The active site is a three-dimensional cleft or pocket on the enzyme surface, formed by only a few amino acids (typically 3-12 residues). These amino acids may be far apart in the primary sequence but are brought together by protein folding.

4. Lock-and-Key vs Induced-Fit Models

The Lock-and-Key Model (Historical, Now Superseded)

Emil Fischer proposed this model in 1894. It imagines the active site as a rigid, pre-shaped "lock" into which only a perfectly complementary substrate "key" fits.

Analogy: A key that precisely matches a lock's shape. Only one key works.

Problems with lock-and-key:

  • It cannot explain why enzymes can stabilize the transition state (which has a different shape from the substrate)
  • It predicts perfect initial complementarity, but X-ray crystallography shows many active sites change shape upon substrate binding
  • It doesn't explain how some enzymes can act on multiple related substrates

The Induced-Fit Model (Correct Model)

Daniel Koshland proposed this model in 1958. The active site is not perfectly complementary to the substrate initially. Instead, when the substrate binds, it induces a conformational change in the enzyme that molds the active site around the substrate.

Analogy: A handshake. Your hand (active site) adjusts its grip to match the other person's hand (substrate). The grip isn't pre-formed; it changes as you make contact.

Enzyme-Substrate Complex (ES Complex)

The transient structure formed when substrate binds to the enzyme's active site.

$$E + S \rightleftharpoons ES \rightarrow E + P$$

The ES complex is held together by multiple weak, non-covalent interactions. This is crucial — strong covalent bonds would make product release too slow.

5. Factors Affecting Enzyme Activity

Temperature

Graph to draw: Rate vs Temperature — a bell-shaped curve that rises to a peak (optimum) then drops sharply.

```

Rate

^

└──────────────────────→ Temperature (°C)

↑

Optimum

(usually ~37-40°C

for human enzymes)

```

The rising phase (low → optimum temperature):

  • Higher temperature = more kinetic energy = molecules move faster
  • More frequent collisions between enzyme and substrate
  • More collisions with energy ≥ Eₐ
  • Q₁₀ value: The factor by which rate increases for a 10°C temperature rise. For enzyme reactions, Q₁₀ ≈ 2 (rate doubles for every 10°C rise, until denaturation)

The falling phase (beyond optimum):

  • Thermal energy overwhelms the weak bonds maintaining tertiary structure
  • Enzyme loses its specific three-dimensional shape
  • Active site is distorted → substrate can no longer bind
  • This is denaturation

Denaturation: The permanent loss of a protein's three-dimensional structure (secondary, tertiary, and quaternary) due to disruption of hydrogen bonds, ionic bonds, hydrophobic interactions, and disulfide bridges. The primary structure (amino acid sequence) remains intact. Denatured enzymes lose catalytic activity permanently.

Denaturation at the Molecular Level

When an enzyme denatures:

  1. Hydrogen bonds break — between backbone C=O and N—H groups, and between side chains
  2. Ionic bonds break — between charged side chains (e.g., lysine⁺ and aspartate⁻)
  3. Hydrophobic interactions collapse — hydrophobic core is exposed to water
  4. Disulfide bridges may break — at very high temperatures, covalent S—S bonds can be cleaved

The result: the precisely folded globular protein unravels into a random coil. The active site disintegrates.

IB Exam Tip (Paper 1 & 2): When explaining the effect of temperature on enzyme activity, always describe BOTH phases: (1) increasing rate due to more kinetic energy/collisions, and (2) sharp decline due to denaturation. Use the word "denaturation" explicitly. Do NOT say the enzyme "dies" — enzymes are not alive. Say "the enzyme is denatured" or "loses its tertiary structure."

pH

Every enzyme has an optimal pH at which its activity is maximal. Deviation from this pH reduces activity, and extreme pH causes denaturation.

Why pH matters:

  • pH changes the ionization state of amino acid side chains at the active site
  • Charged residues (e.g., His, Asp, Glu, Lys, Arg) are critical for substrate binding and catalysis
  • pH also affects the ionization of the substrate itself
  • Extreme pH disrupts ionic bonds maintaining tertiary structure → denaturation

Substrate Concentration

Graph to draw: Rate vs [Substrate] — a rectangular hyperbola that rises and plateaus.

```

Rate

^

└────────────────────────────────→ [Substrate]

```

Three phases:

  1. Low [S]: Rate is limited by substrate availability. Not all active sites are occupied. Rate increases almost linearly with [S]. The reaction is first-order with respect to substrate.
  1. Intermediate [S]: Rate still increases but curve begins to flatten. Active sites are becoming occupied. Transition between first and zero-order.
  1. High [S] (saturation): All enzyme active sites are occupied at any given moment. Adding more substrate has no effect — the enzyme is working at its maximum possible rate. This maximum is called Vmax.

Vmax: The maximum rate of an enzyme-catalyzed reaction, achieved when all enzyme active sites are saturated with substrate. It is a measure of how fast the enzyme can convert substrate to product when operating at full capacity.

At Vmax, the rate depends only on:

  • How fast the enzyme can convert ES → E + P (the turnover number, kcat)
  • The total enzyme concentration

Adding more enzyme would increase Vmax (more active sites available).

6. Enzyme Inhibition

Inhibitors are molecules that reduce enzyme activity. They are classified by how they bind and their effects on kinetic parameters.

Competitive Inhibition

How it works: The inhibitor molecule resembles the substrate in shape and competes for the same active site. It binds reversibly to the active site, physically blocking the real substrate.

```

Normal: Competitive inhibition:

Enzyme Substrate Enzyme Inhibitor (looks like substrate)

┌─────┐ ┌──┐ ┌─────┐ ┌──┐

│ │ │ │ │ ██ │ │ │ ← Can't bind!

│ ╲__│ └──┘ │ ╲__│ └──┘

└─────┘ └─────┘

↓ ↓

ES complex forms Inhibitor occupies active site

```

Key characteristics:

  • Inhibitor competes with substrate for the active site
  • Inhibition can be overcome by increasing substrate concentration (add enough substrate to outcompete inhibitor)
  • Vmax is unchanged — if you add enough substrate, you can still reach the same maximum rate
  • Km (apparent) increases — more substrate is needed to reach half Vmax because inhibitor is competing
  • Does NOT bind covalently (reversible)

Example: Statin drugs (cholesterol-lowering) are competitive inhibitors of HMG-CoA reductase. Methotrexate competes with dihydrofolate for dihydrofolate reductase.

Km (Michaelis constant): The substrate concentration at which the reaction rate is half of Vmax. It is an inverse measure of enzyme-substrate affinity — a lower Km means higher affinity (less substrate needed to reach half Vmax).

Non-Competitive Inhibition

How it works: The inhibitor binds at an allosteric site (a site different from the active site). This binding changes the shape of the enzyme, distorting the active site so the substrate can no longer bind effectively (or at all). The inhibitor does NOT resemble the substrate.

```

Normal: Non-competitive inhibition:

Enzyme Substrate Enzyme Substrate Inhibitor

┌─────┐ ┌──┐ ┌─────┐ ┌──┐

│ │ │ │ │ │ │??│ ██

│ ╲__│ └──┘ │ ╲__│ ? └──┘ ↓

└─────┘ └─────┘ Allosteric

↓ ↓ site

ES complex forms Active site distorted

Substrate can't bind

```

Key characteristics:

  • Inhibitor binds at a site different from the active site (allosteric site)
  • Inhibition cannot be overcome by increasing substrate concentration (substrate and inhibitor don't compete)
  • Vmax decreases — fewer functional enzyme molecules → lower maximum rate
  • Km is unchanged — the enzymes that remain unbound by inhibitor have normal affinity
  • Can be reversible or irreversible (some bind covalently)

Example: Cyanide (CN⁻) is a non-competitive inhibitor of cytochrome c oxidase (Complex IV of ETC). Heavy metal ions (Pb²⁺, Hg²⁺) often act as non-competitive inhibitors by binding to —SH groups on cysteine residues.

7. End-Product (Feedback) Inhibition

The Problem: Metabolic Overproduction

Metabolic pathways involve multiple sequential enzyme-catalyzed reactions:

$$A \xrightarrow{E_1} B \xrightarrow{E_2} C \xrightarrow{E_3} D \xrightarrow{E_4} E \text{ (end product)}$$

If the cell keeps producing E when it already has enough, it wastes energy and resources. The solution: end-product inhibition.

How It Works

The final product (E) binds to an allosteric site on the first enzyme (E₁) in the pathway, inhibiting it. This is a form of non-competitive inhibition.

```

Pathway: E₁ E₂ E₃ E₄

A ────→ B ────→ C ────→ D ────→ E

↑ │

│ │

└────────── INHIBITS ─────────────┘

(allosteric site on E₁)

```

This is brilliant because:

  1. It shuts down the entire pathway at the very first committed step
  2. It prevents wasteful accumulation of all intermediates
  3. It's self-regulating: as E is used up, inhibition decreases, and production resumes

The Isoleucine Pathway (Classic IB Example)

In bacteria, the amino acid isoleucine is synthesized from threonine through a five-step pathway. The first enzyme, threonine deaminase, is allosterically inhibited by isoleucine (the end product).

```

Threonine ──→ α-ketobutyrate ──→ ... ──→ Isoleucine

↑ │

│ │

└─────────── INHIBITS ─────────────────────┘

(threonine deaminase)

```

When isoleucine accumulates, it binds to threonine deaminase, changing its shape and preventing threonine binding. This is a classic example of allosteric regulation and feedback inhibition.

IB Exam Tip (Paper 2): If asked to explain feedback inhibition, use the isoleucine/threonine deaminase example. Draw the pathway, show the end product inhibiting the first enzyme, and explain WHY this is efficient (saves energy, prevents toxic intermediate buildup, self-regulating).

8. Immobilized Enzymes

What Are Immobilized Enzymes?

Free enzymes dissolved in solution are hard to recover and reuse. Immobilization fixes enzymes to or within a solid support, allowing:

  • Reuse — enzymes can be recovered and used repeatedly
  • Product purity — enzyme doesn't contaminate the product
  • Stability — immobilized enzymes are often more resistant to denaturation
  • Continuous processing — substrate flows over immobilized enzyme column

Methods of Immobilization

Method How It Works Advantages Disadvantages
Entrapment Enzyme trapped within gel matrix (e.g., alginate beads) Simple, gentle Substrate must diffuse in; diffusion limitation
Adsorption Enzyme bound to surface by weak forces Simple, cheap Enzyme may leak off
Covalent binding Enzyme covalently attached to support Strong binding, no leakage May damage active site
Membrane confinement Enzyme behind semipermeable membrane Product separation easy Diffusion limitation

Lactase in Alginate Beads: The Classic IB Example

Lactose-free milk production is the go-to IB example of immobilized enzymes.

The problem: Many people are lactose intolerant — they lack the enzyme lactase (β-galactosidase) and cannot digest lactose (milk sugar). Undigested lactose causes bloating, diarrhea, and discomfort.

The solution: Treat milk with immobilized lactase to break down lactose into its absorbable monosaccharides: glucose and galactose.

$$\text{Lactose} + \text{H}_2\text{O} \xrightarrow{\text{lactase}} \text{Glucose} + \text{Galactose}$$

Industrial process:

  1. Lactase enzyme is entrapped in calcium alginate beads (sodium alginate solution dripped into CaCl₂ solution)
  2. Beads are packed into a column
  3. Milk flows through the column
  4. Lactose is hydrolyzed as milk passes over beads
  5. Lactose-free milk exits; lactase beads remain for reuse

Advantages of this method:

  • No enzyme in the final product (cleaner label)
  • Beads can be reused many times (cost-effective)
  • Continuous process (efficient for large-scale production)
  • Enzyme is protected from degradation within the alginate matrix

9. Measuring Enzyme Activity

The Initial Rate Method

When measuring enzyme activity, you must measure the initial rate — the rate at the very beginning of the reaction, before any significant change in substrate concentration or product accumulation.

Why initial rate?

```

Product

^

└────────────────→ Time

```

  • At the start, [S] is known precisely (what you added)
  • The reaction is fastest (maximum [S])
  • Product hasn't accumulated yet (no product inhibition)
  • Rate is approximately linear

How to measure:

  1. Set up reaction with known [E] and [S]
  2. Monitor product formation (or substrate disappearance) over time
  3. Plot [Product] vs Time
  4. Draw tangent to curve at t = 0
  5. Slope of tangent = initial rate (units: concentration/time, e.g., μM/min)

Practical methods for monitoring:

  • Colorimetric: If product/substrate absorbs light at a specific wavelength (e.g., ONPG → yellow product with lactase)
  • pH changes: Monitor H⁺ production/consumption with a pH meter
  • O₂ production/consumption: Use an oxygen electrode (e.g., catalase: 2H₂O₂ → 2H₂O + O₂)
  • NADH absorbance at 340 nm: Many dehydrogenase reactions

10. Worked Examples

Worked Example 1: Interpreting Temperature Data

An experiment measured catalase activity at different temperatures (rate in arbitrary units):

Temperature (°C) Rate (AU)
10 3
20 6
30 11
40 18
50 8
60 1

Explain the rate at 50°C and 60°C.

Answer:

At 50°C, the rate has dropped to 8 AU (from 18 at 40°C). This is because the enzyme has begun to denature — the thermal energy is disrupting hydrogen bonds and ionic bonds that maintain the tertiary structure. Some active sites are distorted and can no longer bind substrate.

At 60°C, the rate is only 1 AU. The enzyme is almost completely denatured. The precise three-dimensional shape of the active site has been lost. The few remaining active enzyme molecules can still catalyze, but most are permanently inactivated.

Why Q₁₀ doesn't apply here: Between 30°C and 50°C, rate doesn't double — it drops. Q₁₀ only applies below the denaturation temperature.

Worked Example 2: Competitive vs Non-Competitive

A student adds a mysterious inhibitor to an enzyme and obtains the following:

[Substrate] (mM) Rate without inhibitor Rate with inhibitor
1 10 4
2 17 8
5 29 17
10 38 27
20 44 44

What type of inhibitor is this? Explain.

Answer: This is a competitive inhibitor.

Evidence: At very high substrate concentration (20 mM), the rate with inhibitor (44) equals the rate without inhibitor (44). This means Vmax is unchanged — the enzyme can still reach the same maximum rate if enough substrate is provided. This is the hallmark of competitive inhibition.

At low [S], the inhibitor significantly reduces rate (10→4 at 1 mM) because the inhibitor and substrate compete for the active site. As [S] increases, substrate outcompetes inhibitor.

The apparent Km has increased — at 2.5 mM without inhibitor (roughly half of 44), vs approximately 5 mM with inhibitor.

Practice Problems

Question 1 (Paper 1 Style)

Which statement best describes the induced-fit model of enzyme action?

A. The active site is a rigid shape that exactly matches the substrate

B. The substrate changes shape to fit the active site

C. Binding of the substrate causes a conformational change in the enzyme that molds the active site around the substrate

D. The enzyme permanently changes shape after catalyzing one reaction

Answer

C. The induced-fit model (Koshland, 1958) proposes that the active site is not perfectly complementary initially; substrate binding induces a conformational change that molds the enzyme around the substrate, properly orienting catalytic residues. A describes the lock-and-key model. B is incorrect — the enzyme changes, not primarily the substrate. D is wrong because enzymes return to original shape after catalysis.

Question 2 (Paper 2 Style — Data Analysis)

Liver catalase was tested at various pH values:

pH Rate of O₂ production (cm³/min)
3 0.1
5 0.8
7 2.4
9 2.1
11 0.3

a) Determine the optimum pH for catalase. [1]

b) Explain why the rate decreases at pH 3 and pH 11. [3]

c) Predict whether pepsin or trypsin would have a similar pH profile to catalase. Justify. [2]

Answer

a) The optimum pH is approximately pH 7 (rate = 2.4 cm³/min).

b) At pH 3: The high H⁺ concentration alters the ionization state of amino acid side chains at the active site. This disrupts ionic bonds and hydrogen bonds that maintain tertiary structure. The enzyme's three-dimensional shape is altered, causing the active site to lose its specific conformation → substrate cannot bind → activity decreases. At extreme pH (3), denaturation occurs.

At pH 11: Similarly, high OH⁻ concentration disrupts ionic bonds and hydrogen bonds. The enzyme denatures, losing tertiary structure and active site integrity.

c) Neither pepsin (optimum pH ~2) nor trypsin (optimum pH ~8) would have the same profile as catalase (optimum ~7). Catalase is an intracellular enzyme adapted to the neutral pH of cytoplasm (~7.4), so its profile is closer to trypsin's alkaline optimum than pepsin's highly acidic optimum. Each enzyme's optimum reflects its physiological environment.

Question 3 (Paper 2 Style)

Outline how end-product inhibition regulates the isoleucine synthesis pathway in bacteria. Include the key enzyme involved. [4]

Answer

  1. Isoleucine is synthesized from threonine through a multi-step pathway
  2. The first committed step is catalyzed by threonine deaminase, which converts threonine to α-ketobutyrate
  3. When isoleucine accumulates to sufficient levels, it binds to an allosteric site (not the active site) on threonine deaminase
  4. This binding induces a conformational change that inhibits the enzyme's activity
  5. The entire pathway is shut down, preventing wasteful overproduction of isoleucine and intermediate compounds
  6. When isoleucine levels drop (used in protein synthesis), it dissociates from the enzyme, relieving inhibition, and the pathway resumes

This is a classic example of non-competitive, allosteric inhibition and feedback regulation.

Question 4 (Paper 1 Style)

A non-competitive inhibitor is added to an enzyme-catalyzed reaction. What is the effect on Vmax and Km?

A. Vmax unchanged, Km increased

B. Vmax decreased, Km unchanged

C. Vmax decreased, Km increased

D. Vmax unchanged, Km unchanged

Answer

B. Non-competitive inhibitors bind to an allosteric site, reducing the number of functional enzyme molecules. This decreases Vmax. However, the remaining uninhibited enzyme molecules have normal affinity for substrate, so Km is unchanged.

Question 5 (Paper 2 Style — Extended Response)

Explain the advantages of using immobilized lactase in alginate beads for the production of lactose-free milk. [5]

Answer

  1. Enzyme reuse: Lactase trapped in alginate beads can be recovered and reused many times, reducing enzyme costs significantly
  2. Product purity: The enzyme remains in the beads and does not contaminate the milk — no need to remove enzyme from the final product; this is important for food labeling
  3. Continuous processing: Milk can flow continuously through a column packed with lactase beads, enabling efficient large-scale industrial production rather than batch processing
  4. Enhanced stability: Immobilization within the alginate matrix protects lactase from denaturation, extending its functional lifetime
  5. Easy separation: Simple filtration separates the milk product from the enzyme beads
  6. Lactose intolerance addressed: The process produces milk where lactose is hydrolyzed to glucose and galactose — both absorbable monosaccharides — making milk digestible for lactose-intolerant individuals

Common Misconceptions

Misconception Reality
"Enzymes provide energy for reactions" Enzymes lower activation energy but do NOT provide energy. They do not change ΔG.
"Enzymes are used up in reactions" Enzymes are catalysts — they emerge unchanged and can be reused thousands of times.
"Denaturation breaks peptide bonds" Denaturation disrupts non-covalent bonds (H-bonds, ionic, hydrophobic) and sometimes disulfide bridges. The primary structure (peptide bonds) remains intact.
"Higher temperature always increases enzyme activity" Only up to the optimum. Beyond that, denaturation causes a sharp decline. At very low temperatures, enzymes are inactive but NOT denatured — activity resumes upon warming.
"All enzymes have a pH optimum of 7" Different enzymes have different pH optima adapted to their environment (pepsin: pH 2, trypsin: pH 8).
"The induced-fit model says the substrate changes shape" The enzyme primarily changes shape. The substrate may be slightly distorted, but the major conformational change is in the enzyme.
"Competitive inhibitors permanently destroy enzyme activity" Competitive inhibition is usually reversible. Increasing substrate concentration can outcompete the inhibitor.
"Vmax is the substrate concentration at half-maximum rate" That's Km. Vmax is the maximum rate when all active sites are saturated.
"Immobilized enzymes are less stable than free enzymes" Immobilized enzymes are often MORE stable due to protection within the matrix.
"End-product inhibition happens at the last enzyme in the pathway" It specifically targets the FIRST enzyme (committed step) to shut down the entire pathway and prevent intermediate accumulation.
