What You'll Learn

If mitosis is a photocopier making identical copies, meiosis is a master shuffler creating unique, one-of-a-kind genetic combinations. This lesson explores how diploid cells produce haploid gametes through two sequential divisions, and how this process generates the genetic variation essential for evolution and adaptation.

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to:

  • Compare mitosis and meiosis in a detailed table
  • Describe each stage of meiosis I and meiosis II, identifying key differences from mitosis
  • Explain crossing over and independent assortment as sources of genetic variation
  • Define non-disjunction and describe its consequences (including Down syndrome)
  • Interpret karyograms to identify chromosomal abnormalities
  • Evaluate prenatal testing methods (amniocentesis, chorionic villus sampling)

1. The Purpose of Meiosis: From Diploid to Haploid

1.1 The Fundamental Problem

Sexual reproduction poses a problem: if two gametes (sperm and egg) each had a full diploid set of chromosomes, the zygote would have 4n (four sets). If that zygote then produced 4n gametes, the next generation would be 8n, and so on — chromosome numbers would explode exponentially with each generation.

Meiosis solves this: It halves the chromosome number from diploid (2n) to haploid (n), so that when two gametes fuse at fertilization, the diploid number is restored:

$$n + n = 2n$$

In humans: 23 (sperm) + 23 (egg) = 46 (zygote)

Meiosis: A type of cell division that reduces the chromosome number by half (2n → n). It consists of two sequential divisions — meiosis I (reductional division) and meiosis II (equational division) — producing four genetically unique haploid daughter cells.

1.2 Overview: Two Divisions, Four Products

Meiosis I Meiosis II
What separates Homologous chromosomes Sister chromatids
Centromeres split? No Yes
Result 2 haploid cells (chromosomes still as sister chromatids) 4 haploid cells (single chromatids)

Analogy: Think of meiosis I as splitting a deck of cards into two half-decks (each still with double cards stuck together), and meiosis II as separating each stuck pair into individual cards.

2. Meiosis I: The Reductional Division

Meiosis I is where the chromosome number is halved and where most genetic variation is generated.

2.1 Prophase I: "The Longest Phase, With the Most Action"

Prophase I is the most complex and prolonged phase of meiosis. In human females, primary oocytes arrest in prophase I from before birth until ovulation (potentially decades!). Prophase I is subdivided into five stages:

#### Leptotene: "Chromosomes Become Visible"

  • Chromatin begins to condense into visible chromosomes.
  • Each chromosome already consists of two sister chromatids (DNA replicated in preceding S phase).
  • Telomeres attach to the nuclear envelope.

#### Zygotene: "Pairing Up — Synapsis"

  • Homologous chromosomes find each other and pair up precisely, gene for gene.
  • This process is called synapsis.
  • The paired structure is a bivalent (two homologous chromosomes) or tetrad (four chromatids, because 2 chromosomes × 2 chromatids each = 4 chromatids).
  • A protein structure called the synaptonemal complex holds the homologues together.

Bivalent/Tetrad: A pair of homologous chromosomes (each with two sister chromatids) held together by the synaptonemal complex during prophase I of meiosis. "Bivalent" emphasizes the two chromosomes; "tetrad" emphasizes the four chromatids.

Draw and label: Draw two homologous chromosomes side by side. One from the maternal set (label "M"), one from paternal (label "P"). Each should be drawn as two parallel lines (sister chromatids) joined at a centromere. Label the entire structure: "Bivalent/Tetrad — 2 chromosomes, 4 chromatids."

#### Pachytene: "Crossing Over"

  • The synaptonemal complex is fully formed.
  • At specific points, non-sister chromatids (one maternal, one paternal) break and exchange segments.
  • This is crossing over — a precise, enzymatic process of breaking and rejoining DNA.
  • The visible points of crossing over are called chiasmata (singular: chiasma).

#### Diplotene: "Chiasmata Visible"

  • The synaptonemal complex disassembles.
  • Homologous chromosomes begin to separate but remain attached at chiasmata.
  • Chiasmata are the physical manifestation of crossing over — they hold bivalents together.

#### Diakinesis: "Final Condensation"

  • Chromosomes condense further.
  • Nuclear membrane begins to break down.
  • Spindle fibers begin to form.
  • Chiasmata move toward the ends of chromosomes (terminalization).

Draw and label the key event — Crossing Over:

  • Draw two homologous chromosomes, one shaded maternal, one shaded paternal.
  • Show non-sister chromatids crossing at a chiasma.
  • Below, show the result: the two chromatids now have exchanged segments.
  • Label: "Non-sister chromatids," "Chiasma," "Recombinant chromatids."

IB Exam Tip (Paper 2): When explaining how crossing over generates genetic variation, always emphasize: (1) it occurs between non-sister chromatids of homologous chromosomes, (2) it creates new combinations of alleles on the same chromosome — alleles that were previously on different parental chromosomes are now together, and (3) this happens during prophase I of meiosis.

2.2 Metaphase I: "Independent Assortment"

In metaphase I:

  • Bivalents align at the metaphase plate (equator).
  • Spindle fibers from each pole attach to the kinetochore of one homologue. The sister chromatids' kinetochores act as one functional unit.
  • Crucially: The orientation of each bivalent is random. The maternal chromosome can face either pole; the paternal chromosome faces the opposite pole. This bivalent's orientation is independent of every other bivalent's orientation.

This randomness is independent assortment.

Draw and label: Draw the cell at metaphase I. Show two bivalents aligned at the equator. For one bivalent, label "Maternal → left pole, Paternal → right pole." For the other, "Paternal → left pole, Maternal → right pole." Show spindle fibers attaching to one kinetochore per homologue. Label: "Random orientation = independent assortment."

Independent assortment: The random orientation of bivalents at metaphase I means that maternal and paternal chromosomes are distributed independently into gametes. For n chromosome pairs, this creates 2ⁿ possible combinations of chromosomes (2²³ ≈ 8.4 million in humans).

2.3 Anaphase I: "Homologues Separate"

In anaphase I:

  • Homologous chromosomes separate and move to opposite poles.
  • Centromeres do NOT split — sister chromatids remain attached.
  • This is the key difference from anaphase of mitosis (where sister chromatids separate).
  • Chiasmata are resolved as homologues are pulled apart.
  • The shortening of kinetochore microtubules pulls chromosomes poleward.

Result: Each pole receives a haploid set of chromosomes, but each chromosome still consists of TWO sister chromatids.

Draw and label: Show chromosomes being pulled to poles. Each chromosome at each pole should still look like an "X" (two chromatids joined). Label: "Homologous chromosomes separating," "Centromeres intact — sister chromatids stay together."

2.4 Telophase I and Cytokinesis

  • Chromosomes may partially decondense (varies by species).
  • Nuclear membranes may or may not reform.
  • Cytokinesis divides the cell into two haploid daughter cells.

Key: Each daughter cell now has n chromosomes, but each chromosome has 2 chromatids — the cell still has 2C of DNA (where C = haploid DNA content). The chromosome number is halved, but DNA content is NOT yet halved relative to a gamete.

3. Meiosis II: The Equational Division

Meiosis II is essentially mitosis of a haploid cell. It separates the sister chromatids.

Important: There is NO DNA replication between meiosis I and meiosis II. The interphase-like period (interkinesis) is brief or absent.

3.1 Prophase II

  • Chromosomes condense (if they decondensed in telophase I).
  • Nuclear membrane breaks down (if it reformed).
  • New spindle fibers form at right angles to the meiosis I spindle.

3.2 Metaphase II

  • Chromosomes align at the metaphase plate.
  • Spindle fibers from opposite poles attach to kinetochores of each sister chromatid.
  • Each kinetochore acts independently now (unlike in metaphase I).

3.3 Anaphase II: "Sister Chromatids Finally Separate"

  • Centromeres split — this is the moment they've been waiting for since S phase.
  • Sister chromatids (now individual chromosomes) are pulled to opposite poles.

3.4 Telophase II and Cytokinesis

  • Nuclear membranes reform around each set of chromosomes.
  • Chromosomes decondense to chromatin.
  • Cytokinesis occurs — in animals by cleavage furrow, in plants by cell plate.
  • Result: 4 genetically unique haploid daughter cells, each with n chromosomes, each chromosome with 1 chromatid (1C DNA).

Draw and label the overall outcome: Start with one diploid cell (2n=4). Show: Meiosis I → two haploid cells (n=2, each chromosome has 2 chromatids). Meiosis II → four haploid cells (n=2, each chromosome with 1 chromatid). Use different shading for maternal and paternal chromosomes to show independent assortment.

4. Sources of Genetic Variation

Three mechanisms in meiosis generate genetic variation:

4.1 Crossing Over (Prophase I)

  • Exchange of DNA segments between non-sister chromatids of homologous chromosomes.
  • Creates recombinant chromosomes — chromosomes with DNA from BOTH parents.
  • This mixes alleles that were previously on different homologous chromosomes.
  • Multiple crossovers per chromosome pair are common.

Result: Alleles on the same chromosome that would normally be inherited together can now be reshuffled. This is why siblings look different even though they get chromosomes from the same two parents.

4.2 Independent Assortment (Metaphase I)

  • Random orientation of each bivalent at the metaphase plate.
  • For 23 chromosome pairs: $$2^{23} \approx 8.4$$ million possible chromosome combinations per gamete.
  • This is independent of crossing over — the two mechanisms multiply.
  • Total possible gametes: $$2^{23} \times$$ (additional variation from crossing over) — an astronomically large number.

4.3 Random Fertilization

  • Which sperm fertilizes which egg is random.
  • $$8.4 \text{ million} \times 8.4 \text{ million} \approx 70$$ trillion possible zygotes from chromosome assortment alone (ignoring crossing over).
  • Adding crossing over increases this to an effectively infinite number — explaining why each individual (except identical twins) is genetically unique.

Worked Example: Calculating combinations

A diploid organism has n = 2 chromosomes (two pairs).

Question: How many different gametes are possible from independent assortment alone?

Answer: For each bivalent (chromosome pair) at metaphase I, there are 2 orientations possible. With 2 pairs: $$2^2 = 4$$ gamete types.

Draw all 4 possible gametes, using M₁, M₂ for maternal chromosomes and P₁, P₂ for paternal chromosomes:

  • Gamete 1: M₁, M₂
  • Gamete 2: M₁, P₂
  • Gamete 3: P₁, M₂
  • Gamete 4: P₁, P₂

For humans (n = 23): $$2^{23} = 8,388,608$$ combinations per gamete.

5. Non-Disjunction: When Meiosis Goes Wrong

5.1 What is Non-Disjunction?

Non-disjunction: The failure of chromosomes to separate properly during meiosis.

This can occur at:

  • Meiosis I: Homologous chromosomes fail to separate → two gametes with n+1, two with n−1.
  • Meiosis II: Sister chromatids fail to separate → one gamete with n+1, one with n−1, two normal.

Draw and label: For meiosis I non-disjunction: show a bivalent both going to the same pole. Result: two cells with n+1 and n−1 chromosomes. For meiosis II: show sister chromatids both going to one pole. Result: one cell with n+1, one with n−1, two normal.

5.2 Aneuploidy

Aneuploidy: An abnormal number of chromosomes (not an exact multiple of n). If a gamete with an abnormal chromosome number is fertilized, the zygote will have an abnormal number of chromosomes.

#### Trisomy: 2n + 1

  • Three copies of one chromosome instead of two.
  • Usually lethal in early development (most trisomies result in miscarriage).
  • Some trisomies are compatible with survival:
Condition Chromosome Affected Key Features
Down syndrome Trisomy 21 Intellectual disability, characteristic facial features, increased risk of heart defects and leukemia
Edwards syndrome Trisomy 18 Severe developmental abnormalities; most die within first year
Patau syndrome Trisomy 13 Severe abnormalities; most die within first year

#### Monosomy: 2n − 1

  • Only one copy of a chromosome.
  • Almost always lethal in humans (except Turner syndrome).
Condition Chromosome Affected Key Features
Turner syndrome Monosomy X (XO) Female phenotype, short stature, webbed neck, infertility (non-functional ovaries), normal intelligence

#### Sex Chromosome Aneuploidies

Condition Karyotype Key Features
Klinefelter syndrome XXY Male phenotype, tall stature, reduced fertility, may have learning difficulties
Triple X syndrome XXX Female phenotype, usually mild or no effects
Jacob's syndrome XYY Male phenotype, tall stature, usually mild or no effects

5.3 Down Syndrome and Maternal Age

Down syndrome (trisomy 21) is the most common autosomal trisomy in live births (about 1 in 700). Its incidence increases dramatically with maternal age:

Maternal Age Risk of Down Syndrome
20 ~1 in 1,500
35 ~1 in 350
40 ~1 in 100
45 ~1 in 30

Why? Females are born with all their primary oocytes arrested in prophase I. As a woman ages, the meiotic spindle degrades, and cohesion between sister chromatids deteriorates, increasing the risk of non-disjunction when meiosis resumes decades later.

IB Exam Tip (Paper 1): Expect questions linking maternal age to Down syndrome incidence. The key explanation: oocytes are "older" in older women (arrested in prophase I since fetal development), and the meiotic apparatus deteriorates with age.

6. Karyotypes and Prenatal Testing

6.1 Karyotyping

A karyotype (or karyogram) is a photograph of an individual's chromosomes, arranged by size, centromere position, and banding pattern.

Process:

  1. Cells are cultured and arrested in metaphase (when chromosomes are maximally condensed).
  2. Chromosomes are stained (Giemsa banding = G-banding) to reveal characteristic banding patterns.
  3. A photograph is taken, and chromosomes are cut out and arranged in homologous pairs.
  4. The organized image is a karyogram.

Uses:

  • Determine sex (XX vs XY)
  • Detect aneuploidies (trisomies, monosomies)
  • Detect large chromosomal rearrangements (translocations, deletions)

Draw and label a simplified karyogram: Draw 23 pairs of chromosomes arranged by decreasing size, with the sex chromosomes as the 23rd pair. Label: "Homologous pairs arranged by size," "Banding pattern," "Sex chromosomes."

6.2 Prenatal Testing

Two main invasive procedures are used to obtain fetal cells for karyotyping:

Feature Amniocentesis Chorionic Villus Sampling (CVS)
When? ~16 weeks gestation ~11–13 weeks gestation
Sample Amniotic fluid (contains fetal cells shed from skin, urinary tract) Chorionic villi (fetal part of placenta)
Procedure Needle through abdominal wall into amniotic cavity, ultrasound-guided Catheter through cervix or needle through abdomen to placenta, ultrasound-guided
Risk ~1% risk of miscarriage ~1–2% risk of miscarriage
Advantage Can also test for neural tube defects via AFP levels Results available earlier in pregnancy
Disadvantage Results available later (~18–20 weeks) Slightly higher miscarriage risk; may cause limb defects if done before 10 weeks

Ethical considerations:

  • Risk of miscarriage from the procedure
  • What to do with the information if an abnormality is detected (termination vs. preparation)
  • Access and cost of testing
  • Potential for discrimination based on genetic conditions
  • Misuse for sex selection in some cultures

7. Mitosis vs. Meiosis: The Definitive Comparison

Feature Mitosis Meiosis
Purpose Growth, repair, asexual reproduction Production of gametes for sexual reproduction
Number of divisions 1 2 (meiosis I and meiosis II)
Number of daughter cells 2 4
Ploidy of daughter cells Diploid (2n) — same as parent Haploid (n) — half the parent
Genetic variation Daughter cells are genetically identical to parent cell Daughter cells are genetically unique — different from parent and from each other
Homologous pairing No — homologous chromosomes act independently Yes — synapsis in prophase I (bivalents/tetrads)
Crossing over No Yes — prophase I (chiasmata between non-sister chromatids)
Independent assortment No Yes — random orientation of bivalents at metaphase I
Centromere behavior Centromeres split in anaphase Centromeres do NOT split in anaphase I; they split in anaphase II
DNA replication Once per division Once before meiosis I; none before meiosis II
Where in body All somatic cells Germline cells (gonads: testes and ovaries)

Draw and label a side-by-side comparison diagram: On the left, show the stages of mitosis for a 2n=4 cell. On the right, show meiosis for a 2n=4 cell. Use shading to distinguish maternal and paternal chromosomes. The meiosis side should clearly show synapsis in prophase I, independent assortment at metaphase I, homologous separation in anaphase I, and sister chromatid separation in anaphase II.

IB Exam Tip (Paper 1 & 2): Comparison questions are very common. You must be able to produce a table like the one above. Focus especially on: number of daughter cells, ploidy, genetic variation, and what separates in each anaphase. The most frequently tested distinction: "In anaphase of mitosis, sister chromatids separate. In anaphase I of meiosis, homologous chromosomes separate (sister chromatids stay together)."

8. Common Misconceptions

Misconception Reality
"Meiosis II is just a second DNA replication" There is NO DNA replication between meiosis I and II. Meiosis II separates sister chromatids that were already present.
"Crossing over happens between sister chromatids" Crossing over occurs between non-sister chromatids of homologous chromosomes. Sister chromatids are genetically identical, so exchange between them would produce no variation.
"After meiosis I, cells are haploid in DNA content" After meiosis I, cells have n chromosomes but each chromosome has 2 chromatids (still 2C DNA). Only after meiosis II is DNA content 1C.
"Independent assortment happens in metaphase II" Independent assortment occurs at metaphase I, when bivalents align randomly. Metaphase II is like mitotic metaphase — individual chromosomes align.
"Down syndrome is inherited from parents" Down syndrome is almost always caused by non-disjunction during gamete formation in one parent, not inherited. (Rare cases of translocation Down syndrome can be inherited.)
"All non-disjunction results in visible syndromes" Most aneuploidies are lethal in early development and result in miscarriage. Only a few are compatible with live birth.
"Karyotyping can detect single-gene mutations" Karyotyping detects chromosomal abnormalities (number, large rearrangements). It cannot detect point mutations or small deletions — those require DNA sequencing.

10. Answers

Answer 1

(a) Metaphase II [1]. Reasoning: Individual chromosomes (not bivalents) are aligned at the equator. Each chromosome has two chromatids with spindle fibers attached to both sides of the centromere. This is characteristic of metaphase II (sister chromatids about to separate) rather than metaphase I (where bivalents align). [1]

(b) 3 bivalents. Since 2n = 6, there are n = 3 homologous pairs, which form 3 bivalents at metaphase I. [1]

(c) 3 chromosomes per gamete (n = 3). [1]

Answer 2

(a) Crossing over occurs during prophase I when non-sister chromatids of homologous chromosomes break and exchange DNA segments at chiasmata [1]. This creates recombinant chromosomes containing alleles from both parents on the same chromosome — new combinations of alleles that did not exist in either parent. This increases the genetic diversity of gametes. [1]

(b) Independent assortment is the random orientation of bivalents at the metaphase I plate [1]. For n chromosome pairs, there are $$2^n$$ possible combinations of maternal and paternal chromosomes in gametes. In humans, $$2^{23} \approx 8.4$$ million possible combinations. [1]

(c) The two mechanisms affect different aspects of genetic transmission: independent assortment shuffles whole chromosomes, while crossing over shuffles alleles within chromosomes. The number of possible gamete combinations is the product of both, making it effectively unlimited. [1]

Answer 3

(a) In meiosis I non-disjunction, homologous chromosomes fail to separate during anaphase I, so all four resulting gametes are abnormal (two with n+1 and two with n−1) [1]. In meiosis II non-disjunction, sister chromatids fail to separate during anaphase II, resulting in two normal gametes and two abnormal gametes (one n+1, one n−1) [1].

(b) Females are born with all primary oocytes arrested in prophase I [1]. As a woman ages (decades later at ovulation), the meiotic spindle and chromosome cohesion proteins deteriorate, increasing the probability of non-disjunction when meiosis I finally completes. This is why the risk of trisomy 21 rises sharply with maternal age. [1]

(c) Monosomy of autosomes means the loss of a chromosome with many essential genes — the gene dosage imbalance is too severe for survival [1]. The X chromosome has a special compensation mechanism (X-inactivation in females), and the Y chromosome has few essential genes, so monosomy X is partially compensated for, allowing survival (though with developmental consequences). [1]

Answer 4

(a) Down syndrome (trisomy 21). [1]

(b) Non-disjunction during meiosis in one parent: either homologous chromosomes 21 fail to separate in meiosis I, or sister chromatids of chromosome 21 fail to separate in meiosis II [1]. This produces a gamete with two copies of chromosome 21. Fertilization by a normal gamete (one copy of 21) results in a zygote with three copies of chromosome 21. [1]

(c) Amniocentesis: performed at ~16 weeks; samples amniotic fluid containing fetal cells; ~1% miscarriage risk; results available ~2–4 weeks later [1]. CVS: performed earlier at ~11–13 weeks; samples chorionic villi (placental tissue); ~1–2% miscarriage risk; results available earlier [1]. CVS gives earlier results (important for decision-making), but carries a slightly higher miscarriage risk; amniocentesis can also detect neural tube defects. [1]
