Why Do We Need a Circulatory System?

In Lesson 19, we saw how O₂ enters the blood at the lungs and CO₂ leaves. In Lesson 20, we saw how hemoglobin carries that O₂. But none of that matters if the O₂-carrying blood doesn't actually reach the cells in your big toe, your brain, and your liver.

Simple diffusion works beautifully over distances of about 1 mm or less. But your body is ~1.7 meters tall. A molecule of O₂ diffusing from your lungs to your foot would take days or weeks. You'd be dead long before it arrived.

The solution: a mass transport system — a network of tubes (blood vessels) filled with a carrier fluid (blood) driven by a pump (the heart). This is the circulatory system, and it solves the distance problem by using bulk flow (convection) rather than diffusion for long-distance transport. Diffusion only handles the final, short-distance exchange at the capillaries.

Part 1: Open vs. Closed Circulatory Systems

Not all circulatory systems use sealed vessels. The animal kingdom uses two fundamentally different designs:

Open Circulatory System

In an open system, the circulating fluid (called hemolymph, not blood) is pumped from the heart into large vessels that open directly into the body cavity (the hemocoel). The hemolymph bathes the organs and tissues directly, then drains back to the heart through openings called ostia.

Key features:

  • Hemolymph — combines functions of blood and interstitial fluid (the fluid that normally surrounds cells). It's not separated from the body cavity.
  • Low pressure — because there are no sealed vessels maintaining pressure, flow is slow and sluggish.
  • Direct bathing — organs sit in pools of hemolymph, and exchange occurs directly across their surfaces.
  • No distinction between blood and interstitial fluid.

Examples:

  • Insects: A tubular heart runs along the dorsal side of the abdomen. It pumps hemolymph forward; the hemolymph flows through the hemocoel, bathing organs, and re-enters the heart through ostia (one-way valves).
  • Most mollusks (snails, clams, but NOT cephalopods — squid and octopus have closed systems!): The heart pumps hemolymph into sinuses; it percolates through tissues and collects in larger sinuses before returning.

Advantages:

  • Low energy cost (low pressure)
  • Suitable for small organisms with low metabolic rates
  • In insects, the tracheal system handles O₂ — the open circulation mainly transports nutrients, not gases

Disadvantages:

  • Slow circulation limits metabolic rate
  • Cannot selectively direct flow to specific organs
  • Not suitable for large, active organisms

Closed Circulatory System

In a closed system, blood is always contained within vessels. A separate interstitial fluid bathes the cells; exchange between blood and interstitial fluid occurs across capillary walls.

Key features:

  • Blood confined to vessels — arteries → arterioles → capillaries → venules → veins
  • High pressure — the sealed system allows the heart to generate significant pressure, driving rapid flow
  • Fast, directed flow — blood can be selectively directed to different organs by constricting/dilating specific vessels
  • Separation of blood and interstitial fluid — allows different compositions in each compartment

Examples:

  • Vertebrates (fish, amphibians, reptiles, birds, mammals)
  • Annelids (earthworms — have multiple "hearts" or aortic arches)
  • Cephalopods (squid, octopus — remarkably, these mollusks independently evolved closed circulation to support their high metabolic rates and active predatory lifestyle)

Advantages:

  • Higher pressure and faster flow → supports higher metabolic rates
  • Selective distribution — can shunt blood to active organs (e.g., muscles during exercise)
  • Allows larger body sizes

Interstitial Fluid Same as hemolymph Separate from blood

Part 2: The Human Heart — A Double Pump

The human heart is a muscular organ about the size of your fist, located slightly left of center in the chest, behind the sternum. It's not one pump but two pumps arranged in series, sharing a common wall (the septum):

  • Right side: Pumps blood to the lungs (pulmonary circulation) — a low-pressure, short-distance circuit
  • Left side: Pumps blood to the rest of the body (systemic circulation) — a high-pressure, long-distance circuit

The Four Chambers

Imagine a house with two floors and a dividing wall down the middle. Each side has an upstairs room (atrium) and a downstairs room (ventricle):

Right Atrium:

  • Receives deoxygenated blood from the body via two large veins:
  • Superior vena cava: Blood from the head, neck, upper limbs
  • Inferior vena cava: Blood from the lower body
  • Thin-walled — it's a receiving chamber, not a pumping chamber
  • Pushes blood through the tricuspid valve into the right ventricle

Right Ventricle:

  • Receives blood from the right atrium
  • Pumps deoxygenated blood through the pulmonary semilunar valve into the pulmonary artery → lungs
  • Wall is thicker than the atria, but thinner than the left ventricle — it only needs to pump blood a short distance to the nearby lungs (pulmonary circulation is low-pressure, low-resistance)

Left Atrium:

  • Receives oxygenated blood from the lungs via four pulmonary veins (two from each lung)
  • Thin-walled, like the right atrium
  • Pushes blood through the bicuspid (mitral) valve into the left ventricle

Left Ventricle:

  • Receives blood from the left atrium
  • Pumps oxygenated blood through the aortic semilunar valve into the aorta → entire body
  • Thickest wall of any chamber — it must generate enough pressure to push blood through the entire systemic circuit (all the way to the toes and back). The wall is 2-3× thicker than the right ventricle.

The Valves — One-Way Doors

Heart valves ensure unidirectional blood flow. They open and close passively in response to pressure differences — they have no muscles. There are two types:

Atrioventricular (AV) Valves:

  • Tricuspid valve (right side): Three flaps (cusps)
  • Bicuspid/Mitral valve (left side): Two flaps
  • Located between atria and ventricles
  • Open when atrial pressure > ventricular pressure (during heart relaxation/filling)
  • Close when ventricular pressure > atrial pressure (during ventricular contraction) — prevents backflow into atria
  • Chordae tendineae: Thin, tough connective tissue cords ("heart strings") that attach the valve flaps to papillary muscles in the ventricle wall. These prevent the valves from inverting (flipping inside out into the atria) during the high pressure of ventricular contraction. Think of them as the ropes holding a parachute — they don't open the parachute, they prevent it from turning inside out.

Semilunar Valves:

  • Pulmonary semilunar valve (between right ventricle and pulmonary artery)
  • Aortic semilunar valve (between left ventricle and aorta)
  • Each has three half-moon (semilunar) shaped cusps
  • Open when ventricular pressure > pressure in the artery (during ventricular contraction)
  • Close when arterial pressure > ventricular pressure (during relaxation) — prevents backflow from arteries into ventricles
  • These valves have no chordae tendineae — their shape and the pressure dynamics are sufficient to prevent inversion

Part 3: The Cardiac Cycle

The cardiac cycle is the sequence of events in one complete heartbeat. If your heart rate is 72 beats per minute, one cardiac cycle lasts about 0.83 seconds. The cycle has three main phases:

Phase 1: Atrial Systole (Atrial Contraction)

Duration: ~0.1 seconds

What happens:

  • The sinoatrial (SA) node fires (the heart's natural pacemaker — more on this in Lesson 22)
  • Both atria contract simultaneously
  • Pressure in the atria rises above pressure in the ventricles
  • AV valves (tricuspid and mitral) are open (they were already open during the passive filling that preceded atrial systole)
  • Semilunar valves are closed (pressure in arteries exceeds ventricular pressure)
  • Blood is pushed from atria into ventricles — this "tops off" the ventricles, contributing about 20-30% of ventricular filling (the other 70-80% occurs passively during the previous diastole)

Heart sound: Quiet. Atrial contraction itself doesn't produce a major sound.

Phase 2: Ventricular Systole (Ventricular Contraction)

Duration: ~0.3 seconds

What happens:

  • Ventricular muscles contract forcefully
  • Ventricular pressure rises sharply
  • When ventricular pressure > atrial pressure: AV valves slam shut → "LUB" (first heart sound, S1)
  • For a brief moment, all four valves are closed (isovolumetric contraction — volume stays the same while pressure rises)
  • When ventricular pressure > pressure in aorta/pulmonary artery: semilunar valves open
  • Blood is ejected: Right ventricle → pulmonary artery → lungs. Left ventricle → aorta → body
  • At the peak of ejection, ventricular pressure in the left ventricle reaches about 120 mmHg (systolic pressure)
  • Ventricles empty about 60-70% of their volume (ejection fraction; the rest is the end-systolic volume)

During this phase, the atria are in diastole (relaxed) and filling with blood from the veins.

Phase 3: Diastole (Relaxation)

Duration: ~0.4 seconds

What happens:

  • Ventricular muscles relax
  • Ventricular pressure drops rapidly
  • When ventricular pressure < pressure in aorta/pulmonary artery: semilunar valves snap shut → "DUB" (second heart sound, S2)
  • For a brief moment, all four valves are closed again (isovolumetric relaxation)
  • When ventricular pressure drops below atrial pressure: AV valves open
  • Blood that has been accumulating in the atria (which have been filling throughout ventricular systole) rushes passively into the ventricles — this is the rapid filling phase
  • The cycle is ready to begin again

Summary Table — Cardiac Cycle Phases:

Phase Duration Atria Ventricles AV Valves Semilunar Valves Heart Sound
Atrial Systole ~0.1 s Contract Relaxed Open Closed (quiet)
Ventricular Systole ~0.3 s Relaxed Contract Closed Open "LUB" (S1) at start
Diastole ~0.4 s Relaxed Relaxed Open (later) Closed "DUB" (S2) at start

S1 ("lub"): AV valves closing — lower pitch, longer duration

S2 ("dub"): Semilunar valves closing — higher pitch, shorter, sharper

Part 4: Pressure Changes During the Cardiac Cycle

One of the most important IB skills is interpreting the pressure-volume graph of the cardiac cycle. Let's build it step by step.

Imagine three lines on the same graph (x-axis = time, y-axis = pressure in mmHg):

Aortic Pressure

  • Starts high (~80 mmHg — diastolic pressure) because the elastic aorta is stretched and exerting pressure on the blood within
  • When the left ventricle ejects, aortic pressure rises sharply to a peak (~120 mmHg — systolic pressure)
  • When the semilunar valve closes, there's a small but characteristic dicrotic notch (incisura) — a brief dip caused by the backflow of blood against the closing valve, followed by a small rebound rise
  • Then pressure declines gradually as blood flows out of the arteries into the capillaries (elastic recoil of arterial walls maintains flow during diastole)

Ventricular Pressure

  • Starts low (~0-5 mmHg) during diastole
  • Rises slightly during atrial systole
  • Rises dramatically during ventricular systole — peaks at ~120 mmHg (left ventricle)
  • Drops rapidly at the start of diastole, falling below atrial pressure

Atrial Pressure

  • Shows small, gentle waves
  • Rises slightly during atrial systole (the a wave)
  • Rises slightly when AV valves bulge back into atria during ventricular contraction (the c wave)
  • Gradually rises as blood returns from veins while AV valves are closed (the v wave)
  • Drops sharply when AV valves open and blood rushes into ventricles

Key Events to Mark on the Graph

  1. AV valves close: The point where ventricular pressure crosses above atrial pressure
  2. Semilunar valves open: The point where ventricular pressure crosses above aortic/pulmonary artery pressure
  3. Semilunar valves close: The point where ventricular pressure falls below aortic pressure (and the dicrotic notch appears on the aortic trace)
  4. AV valves open: The point where ventricular pressure falls below atrial pressure

Diagram to Draw — The Wiggers Diagram:

Draw axes: x-axis = time (label one cardiac cycle, ~0.8 s), y-axis = pressure (0-120 mmHg).

Plot three lines:

  • Aortic pressure (highest, red): starts ~80, rises to ~120, dicrotic notch, gradually declines
  • Left ventricular pressure (middle, orange): starts ~5, huge spike to ~120, drops rapidly
  • Left atrial pressure (lowest, blue): gentle waves, a-wave, c-wave, v-wave

Mark the four valve events with vertical dashed lines.

Below, add a row for heart sounds ("LUB" and "DUB" marked).

Below that, add an ECG trace (P, QRS, T — preview of Lesson 22).

This is called a Wiggers diagram and is the ultimate IB cardiac cycle summary.

IB Exam Tip: When analyzing cardiac cycle graphs, always look at pressure crossings. When ventricular pressure exceeds atrial pressure → AV valves close. When it exceeds aortic pressure → semilunar open. When it falls below aortic → semilunar close. When it falls below atrial → AV valves open. Practice tracing these crossing points — they're the most commonly tested feature.

Part 5: Blood Vessels — The Plumbing

Blood vessels form a closed loop: heart → arteries → arterioles → capillaries → venules → veins → heart. Each vessel type is structurally adapted to its function.

Arteries — The Pressure Vessels

Arteries carry blood away from the heart (with one exception — the pulmonary artery carries deoxygenated blood to the lungs). They must withstand and smooth out the pulsatile, high-pressure flow from ventricular ejection.

Structure (from outside in):

  1. Tunica externa (adventitia): Outer layer of connective tissue (collagen fibers). Provides structural support and anchors the artery to surrounding tissues.
  2. Tunica media: Thickest layer — composed of smooth muscle and elastin fibers. This is the defining feature of arteries.
  • The elastic fibers allow arteries to stretch during systole (when pressure is high) and recoil during diastole. This is the Windkessel effect — the elastic recoil maintains blood pressure and flow during diastole when the heart is not pumping. Without it, blood flow would be pulsatile and stop between heartbeats.
  • The smooth muscle allows constriction/dilation for blood pressure regulation.
  1. Tunica intima: Innermost layer — a single layer of endothelial cells (squamous epithelium) providing a smooth, low-friction surface. This is the only layer that directly contacts blood.

Key features:

  • Thick, muscular, elastic wall
  • Narrow lumen (internal space) relative to wall thickness
  • No valves (pressure is high enough to prevent backflow)
  • Blood flows in pulses, at high pressure and high velocity
  • Deeply located (protection)

Arterioles: Smaller branches of arteries with proportionally more smooth muscle and less elastin. They are the resistance vessels — by constricting or dilating, they control blood flow into capillary beds and are the main determinants of total peripheral resistance (and thus blood pressure).

Veins — The Capacitance Vessels

Veins carry blood toward the heart (exception: pulmonary veins carry oxygenated blood from lungs to heart). They operate at much lower pressure than arteries.

Structure:

  1. Tunica externa: Thickest layer in veins — provides structural support.
  2. Tunica media: Much thinner than in arteries — less smooth muscle, less elastin. Veins don't need to withstand high pressure or provide elastic recoil.
  3. Tunica intima: Endothelial layer, similar to arteries. In many veins, this layer is folded into valves — flap-like structures that prevent backflow (see below).

Key features:

  • Thin wall relative to diameter
  • Wide lumen — veins hold about 60-70% of the total blood volume at any given time (they are the "blood reservoirs")
  • Valves in medium-sized veins (especially in limbs) — these are folds of the tunica intima that act as one-way doors, allowing blood to flow toward the heart but snapping shut if blood tries to flow backward. If you've ever seen bulging varicose veins, those are veins where the valves have failed and blood pools.
  • Low pressure, low velocity
  • Often superficial (visible through skin)

How does blood return to the heart against gravity?

Since venous pressure is very low (often only 5-15 mmHg), gravity would tend to pool blood in your feet when you stand. Several mechanisms assist venous return:

  1. Skeletal muscle pump: When you walk or contract leg muscles, they compress veins, squeezing blood upward. The valves ensure it flows only toward the heart. This is why soldiers standing at attention for long periods can faint — no muscle pump, blood pools in legs, reduced venous return, reduced cardiac output, reduced cerebral blood flow.
  1. Respiratory pump: During inhalation, thoracic pressure decreases and abdominal pressure increases. This pressure gradient helps draw blood from abdominal veins into thoracic veins.
  1. Smooth muscle tone: Venous smooth muscle maintains some constriction, slightly increasing pressure.
  1. Cardiac suction: The relaxing ventricles create a slight negative pressure that draws blood in.

Capillaries — The Exchange Vessels

Capillaries are the microscopic vessels where the actual exchange of O₂, CO₂, nutrients, and waste occurs between blood and tissues. The entire circulatory system exists to deliver blood to the capillaries.

Structure:

  • Wall: A single layer of endothelial cells — one cell thick. This is the absolute minimum diffusion barrier possible.
  • Basement membrane: A thin, acellular layer supporting the endothelium.
  • Diameter: ~7-10 μm — just wide enough for a red blood cell (erythrocyte, ~7-8 μm) to squeeze through single file. This maximizes contact between erythrocytes and the capillary wall, minimizing diffusion distance for O₂.
  • No smooth muscle, no elastin, no connective tissue — stripped down to the essential minimum.

Types of Capillaries:

  • Continuous capillaries: Endothelial cells are tightly joined (tight junctions). Found in muscle, skin, nervous tissue. Limited permeability — most exchange is transcellular (through the cells, mainly for lipid-soluble substances) or via small intercellular clefts (for water and small solutes).
  • Fenestrated capillaries: Endothelial cells have pores (fenestrations) covered by thin diaphragms. Found in kidneys (glomeruli), endocrine glands, small intestine. Permit rapid exchange of water, small solutes, and even some proteins.
  • Sinusoidal (discontinuous) capillaries: Large gaps between and within endothelial cells; incomplete basement membrane. Found in liver, spleen, bone marrow. Allow passage of large molecules and even whole cells (e.g., newly formed blood cells entering circulation in bone marrow).

Why capillary flow is slow:

Although each capillary is tiny, the total cross-sectional area of all capillaries combined is enormous — much larger than the aorta. By the principle of continuity (flow rate = velocity × cross-sectional area), the huge total area means velocity drops dramatically in capillaries. This is critical: slow flow allows time for exchange (diffusion, filtration, reabsorption).

Part 6: Vasodilation and Vasoconstriction

Arterioles (the small arteries leading into capillary beds) contain rings of smooth muscle in their walls. These muscles can contract or relax to change the vessel diameter:

Vasoconstriction

  • Smooth muscle contracts → vessel diameter decreases
  • Reduces blood flow to the downstream capillary bed
  • Increases total peripheral resistance → increases blood pressure
  • Controlled by: sympathetic nervous system (noradrenaline acting on α-adrenergic receptors), angiotensin II, vasopressin, cold

Vasodilation

  • Smooth muscle relaxes → vessel diameter increases
  • Increases blood flow to the downstream capillary bed
  • Decreases total peripheral resistance → decreases blood pressure
  • Controlled by: local metabolites (↑CO₂, ↓O₂, ↑H⁺ — active tissues signal their own increased blood flow, called active hyperemia), nitric oxide (NO, a potent vasodilator released by endothelial cells), histamine (in inflammation), heat

Thermoregulation:

Vasoconstriction/dilation in skin arterioles is a key thermoregulatory mechanism:

  • Cold: Vasoconstriction in skin → reduces blood flow to surface → conserves heat
  • Hot: Vasodilation in skin → increases blood flow to surface → promotes heat loss (skin looks flushed)

Common Misconceptions

Myth #1: "Arteries always carry oxygenated blood and veins always carry deoxygenated blood."

Reality: Arteries are defined by direction (away from heart), not oxygen content. The pulmonary artery carries deoxygenated blood from the right ventricle to the lungs. The pulmonary veins carry oxygenated blood from the lungs to the left atrium. In the systemic circuit, arteries do carry oxygenated blood and veins carry deoxygenated — but the definitions are directional.

Myth #2: "The heart fills by sucking blood in."

Reality: The heart fills passively when ventricular pressure drops below atrial pressure. There's some "suction" effect from ventricular recoil, but most filling is driven by the pressure in the veins and atria — blood is pushed in, not sucked.

Myth #3: "Chordae tendineae actively open the AV valves."

Reality: Chordae tendineae prevent the AV valves from inverting (prolapsing) into the atria. Valves open and close passively based on pressure differences. The chordae tendineae are like doorstops, not door handles.

Myth #4: "Blood pressure is highest in capillaries because they're narrowest."

Reality: Pressure drops progressively from arteries to veins. Capillaries are narrow individually, but their huge combined cross-sectional area means resistance is moderate and pressure has already dropped significantly (to ~35 mmHg at the arteriole end, declining to ~15 mmHg at the venous end). The biggest pressure drop actually occurs across the arterioles (the resistance vessels).

Practice Problems

Problem 1: Heart Structure

Describe the pathway of a red blood cell from the right atrium to the aorta, naming all chambers, valves, and blood vessels in order. Explain why the left ventricle has a thicker wall than the right.

Click for Answer

Pathway:

Right atrium → tricuspid (AV) valve → right ventricle → pulmonary semilunar valve → pulmonary artery → lungs (pulmonary capillaries — gas exchange, blood becomes oxygenated) → pulmonary veins → left atrium → bicuspid/mitral (AV) valve → left ventricle → aortic semilunar valve → aorta

Why left ventricle wall is thicker:

The left ventricle pumps blood through the systemic circulation — to the entire body, a circuit with high resistance and long distance. It must generate high pressure (~120 mmHg systolic) to overcome this resistance. The thicker muscular wall provides the force needed.

The right ventricle only pumps blood through the pulmonary circulation — to the nearby lungs, a low-resistance, low-pressure circuit (~25 mmHg systolic). Less force needed = thinner wall.

This difference reflects the different afterloads (the pressure the ventricle must overcome to eject blood).

Problem 2: Cardiac Cycle Timing

During one cardiac cycle lasting 0.8 seconds, calculate:

(a) Approximately how long the ventricles are relaxing (diastole)

(b) If heart rate increases to 120 bpm, which phase shortens the most?

Click for Answer

(a) At 72 bpm, one cycle = 0.83 s.

  • Ventricular systole = ~0.3 s
  • Therefore ventricular diastole = 0.83 - 0.3 = ~0.5 s

The ventricles spend more time relaxed than contracted.

(b) At 120 bpm, cycle duration = 60/120 = 0.5 s.

  • Ventricular systole shortens only slightly (maybe to ~0.25 s)
  • Diastole shortens dramatically (from ~0.5 s to ~0.25 s)

This is significant because coronary arteries fill during diastole (when the heart muscle is relaxed and not compressing its own blood vessels). Very high heart rates reduce diastolic filling time, which can reduce coronary perfusion — this is why sustained tachycardia can cause chest pain (angina) even in people with normal coronary arteries.

  • Narrow lumen → characteristic of arteries (thick wall, narrow lumen)

Problem 4: Pressure Graph Analysis

On a graph of left ventricular pressure during one cardiac cycle, the line rises from near 0 to 120 mmHg and then falls.

(a) What event causes the pressure to rise?

(b) At what two pressure values do the AV and semilunar valves close? Explain your reasoning.

(c) Why is there a slight pressure rise before the main ventricular contraction?

Click for Answer

(a) Ventricular systole — the contraction of ventricular myocardium increases pressure in the closed chamber (isovolumetric contraction until the semilunar valve opens, then ejection).

(b) AV valves close when ventricular pressure rises above atrial pressure (~5-10 mmHg, near the start of the pressure rise).

Semilunar valves close when ventricular pressure falls below aortic pressure (~100-120 mmHg near peak, but actually at about 80-100 mmHg on the way down — specifically when the ventricular pressure trace crosses below the aortic pressure trace).

(c) This slight rise is caused by atrial systole — the atria contract, pushing the last ~20-30% of blood into the ventricles, which slightly increases ventricular pressure and volume just before the main ventricular contraction. This is sometimes called the "atrial kick."

Problem 5: Capillary Adaptations

Explain how the structure of capillaries is adapted for their function of exchange with tissues. Refer to at least three structural features.

Click for Answer

  1. Wall is one cell thick (single layer of endothelial cells): This minimizes the diffusion distance for O₂, CO₂, nutrients, and waste — directly related to Fick's Law (rate ∝ 1/d). At just ~0.5-1 μm thick, diffusion is extremely rapid.
  1. Narrow diameter (~7-10 μm): Erythrocytes (7-8 μm) must pass through single file. This maximizes contact between each red blood cell and the capillary wall, reducing the diffusion distance for O₂ from hemoglobin to the tissue. It also slows flow slightly, allowing more time for exchange.
  1. Extensive branching and vast total cross-sectional area: The huge combined cross-sectional area dramatically reduces blood velocity in capillaries (continuity principle), providing adequate time for diffusion and filtration/reabsorption to occur.
  1. Fenestrations (in some types): Pores in endothelial cells allow rapid exchange of water and small solutes, especially important in kidneys (filtration) and endocrine glands (hormone release).
  1. Thin basement membrane: Provides structural support without significantly increasing diffusion distance.

All of these features maximize the efficiency of exchange — the primary function of capillaries — by minimizing diffusion distance, maximizing contact area, and ensuring adequate residence time.

Key Takeaways

  • Open systems (hemolymph in hemocoel) are low-pressure, found in insects and most mollusks. Closed systems (blood in vessels) are high-pressure, found in vertebrates and cephalopods.
  • The human heart has four chambers — right atrium/ventricle (pulmonary) and left atrium/ventricle (systemic). The left ventricle wall is thickest.
  • AV valves (tricuspid, bicuspid/mitral) prevent backflow into atria; semilunar valves (pulmonary, aortic) prevent backflow from arteries. Chordae tendineae prevent AV valve inversion.
  • Cardiac cycle: atrial systole → ventricular systole ("lub") → diastole ("dub"). AV valves close when ventricular pressure > atrial. Semilunar valves open when ventricular pressure > arterial.
  • Arteries: thick, elastic, narrow lumen, high pressure. Capillaries: one-cell-thick, site of exchange. Veins: thin, wide lumen, valves, low pressure.
  • Vasoconstriction (↓ diameter) and vasodilation (↑ diameter) in arterioles regulate blood flow distribution and pressure.
