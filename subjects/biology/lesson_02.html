2.1 What Is DNA? The Big Picture First

Before we zoom into atoms and bonds, let's get the big picture:

DNA (deoxyribonucleic acid) is the molecule that stores genetic information in all living organisms. Think of it as a library of blueprints — every instruction for building and running an organism is encoded in its DNA.

DNA belongs to a larger family called nucleic acids. There are two types:

  • DNA: Deoxyribonucleic acid — the permanent genetic archive
  • RNA: Ribonucleic acid — a temporary working copy of genetic information (we'll cover RNA in Lesson 3)

DNA is a polymer — a long chain molecule made of repeating subunits. The subunits are called nucleotides. To understand DNA, we must first understand what a nucleotide is.

2.2 The Nucleotide: DNA's Building Block

Starting From the Very Beginning

A nucleotide is a relatively small organic molecule. Every nucleotide — whether in DNA or RNA — has three parts:

  1. A phosphate group — containing phosphorus (P) bonded to oxygen (O)
  2. A pentose sugar — a 5-carbon sugar (pent- = five, -ose = sugar)
  3. A nitrogenous base — a ring structure containing nitrogen (N) atoms

Draw this: Draw three connected boxes in a row. Label them (from left to right): "Phosphate" → "Sugar" → "Base". Below that, write: "These three components = ONE nucleotide." This is the universal structure.

Component 1: The Phosphate Group

A phosphate group consists of a phosphorus atom surrounded by four oxygen atoms. In biological conditions (pH ~7.4), the phosphate group carries a negative charge. This negative charge is important — it makes DNA acidic (hence the name nucleic acid) and contributes to DNA's solubility in water.

The phosphate group serves as the "linker" — it connects the sugar of one nucleotide to the sugar of the next nucleotide in the chain.

Memory aid: Phosphate = the "backbone connector."

Component 2: The Pentose Sugar

A pentose is any sugar with 5 carbon atoms. In DNA, the sugar is deoxyribose (de-oxy = "lacking oxygen"). In RNA, the sugar is ribose.

The difference? Deoxyribose has an –H at the 2′ carbon position. Ribose has an –OH at the 2′ carbon position. That single oxygen atom is the only chemical difference — but it makes DNA much more chemically stable than RNA, which is why DNA is the long-term storage molecule and RNA is the short-term working copy.

The carbon atoms in the sugar are numbered 1′ to 5′ (pronounced "one prime" to "five prime"). The prime (′) distinguishes sugar carbons from base ring carbons. This numbering is crucial for understanding directionality.

Draw this: Draw a pentagon (5-sided ring) with an oxygen atom at one vertex. Number the vertices 1′ through 4′ going around the ring, and put the 5′ carbon as a "branch" off the ring at the 4′ position (this is the CH₂OH group). This is the ribose/deoxyribose ring.

Component 3: The Nitrogenous Base

A nitrogenous base is a ring-shaped molecule containing nitrogen. There are five nitrogenous bases in nucleic acids, but DNA uses only four:

Purines (larger — two fused rings):

Base Symbol Pairs with
Adenine A Thymine (T)
Guanine G Cytosine (C)

Pyrimidines (smaller — one ring):

Base Symbol Pairs with
Cytosine C Guanine (G)
Thymine T Adenine (A)

Memory aid: "Pure As Gold" — Purines are Adenine and Guanine. The pyrimidines are C, T, and U (uracil — in RNA only).

Draw these:

  • For a purine: Draw a hexagon fused to a pentagon (like a wide keyhole). Label it "Purine (A or G)."
  • For a pyrimidine: Draw a single hexagon. Label it "Pyrimidine (C, T, or U)."

The base is attached to the 1′ carbon of the sugar.

Putting a Nucleotide Together

Here is the assembly order:

  1. The base attaches to the 1′ carbon of the sugar via a glycosidic bond.
  2. The phosphate attaches to the 5′ carbon of the sugar via an ester bond.
  3. The 3′ carbon of the sugar has a free –OH group (this will be important for polymerization).

The whole unit is called a nucleotide. Without the phosphate, it's called a nucleoside (base + sugar only).

Nucleotide: The monomer (building block) of nucleic acids, consisting of a phosphate group, a pentose sugar (deoxyribose in DNA, ribose in RNA), and a nitrogenous base. Nucleotides are joined by phosphodiester bonds to form polynucleotides.

Nucleoside: A nitrogenous base bonded to a pentose sugar, without the phosphate group.

2.3 Building a DNA Strand: The Sugar-Phosphate Backbone

The Phosphodiester Bond

Nucleotides are linked together into a chain. The bond that connects them is called a phosphodiester bond (phospho- = phosphate, di- = two, ester = type of covalent bond). Specifically:

  • The phosphate group of one nucleotide forms a covalent bond with the 3′ –OH group of the sugar of the next nucleotide.
  • This creates a repeating pattern: ...sugar–phosphate–sugar–phosphate–sugar–phosphate...

This repeating sugar-phosphate chain is called the sugar-phosphate backbone. The bases project outward from this backbone like flags on a rope.

Draw this: Draw three boxes in a vertical line. Each box represents one nucleotide. Draw horizontal lines connecting them — label these "phosphodiester bonds." From each box, draw a horizontal line pointing right, ending in a shape (the base). Label "Sugar-phosphate backbone" along the vertical chain and "Bases projecting outward" for the horizontal extensions.

The 5′→3′ Directionality

This is one of the most important concepts in molecular biology, and IB exams love it.

Because of how the phosphodiester bonds form, every polynucleotide strand has a direction:

  • The 5′ end of the strand has a free phosphate group attached to the 5′ carbon of the terminal sugar (nothing is attached to it on that side).
  • The 3′ end of the strand has a free –OH group on the 3′ carbon of the terminal sugar.

Biochemists always write nucleotide sequences from 5′ to 3′: e.g., 5′-ATGCCGTA-3′.

Why does this matter? DNA replication, transcription, and translation all proceed in the 5′→3′ direction. Enzymes that build new DNA or RNA strands (DNA polymerase, RNA polymerase) can only add new nucleotides to the 3′ end. This directionality governs everything about how genetic information is copied and read.

Draw this: Draw a simple strand as a line. At the top left, write "5′ end" and draw a circle labeled "P" (phosphate). At the bottom right, write "3′ end" and draw "–OH." Draw an arrow from top to bottom labeled "Direction: 5′ → 3′."

5′ end: The end of a polynucleotide strand where the 5′ carbon of the terminal sugar has a free phosphate group (not linked to another nucleotide).

3′ end: The end of a polynucleotide strand where the 3′ carbon of the terminal sugar has a free hydroxyl (–OH) group.

2.4 The Double Helix: Two Strands Intertwined

Antiparallel Strands

DNA is double-stranded. Two polynucleotide strands wind around each other to form a double helix. The two strands run in opposite directions — they are antiparallel:

  • One strand runs 5′ → 3′ (let's call this the "forward" strand)
  • The other strand runs 3′ → 5′ (the "reverse" strand)

If you walk along the helix, the two strands point in opposite directions, like two escalators going opposite ways.

The Backbones on the Outside, Bases on the Inside

The sugar-phosphate backbones form the "rails" on the outside of the double helix. The nitrogenous bases point inward, toward the center, where they pair with bases from the opposite strand.

This arrangement protects the genetic information — the reactive bases are shielded inside the helix, while the chemically robust sugar-phosphate backbone faces the aqueous environment of the cell.

Complementary Base Pairing

The bases from the two strands pair up in the center via hydrogen bonds. The pairing is exclusive and specific:

  • Adenine (A) always pairs with Thymine (T) — via 2 hydrogen bonds
  • Cytosine (C) always pairs with Guanine (G) — via 3 hydrogen bonds

This is called complementary base pairing. If you know the sequence of one strand, you can deduce the sequence of the other. For example:

```

5′-A T G C C G T A-3′ (coding strand)

3′-T A C G G C A T-5′ (complementary strand)

```

Why Purines Pair with Pyrimidines

Adenine and guanine are purines (two fused rings — larger). Thymine and cytosine are pyrimidines (one ring — smaller).

A purine always pairs with a pyrimidine: A–T and G–C. If two purines paired (A–G), the combined width would be too large. If two pyrimidines paired (C–T), the combined width would be too small. A purine–pyrimidine pair has a consistent width.

This ensures that the DNA double helix has a uniform diameter of 2 nm (nanometers) along its entire length — like a perfectly uniform spiral staircase. This uniformity was a crucial clue that helped Watson and Crick deduce the structure.

Draw this: Draw two parallel vertical lines (2 nm apart, label the distance). Between them, draw base pairs: A=T (2 H-bonds as dotted lines) and C≡G (3 H-bonds as dotted lines). Show that the A–T pair and the C–G pair both span exactly the same distance between the backbones.

Major and Minor Grooves

As the two strands wind around each other, the helical twist creates uneven spacing. There are two types of grooves:

  • Major groove — wider, allows proteins (transcription factors) to "read" the base sequence without unwinding the DNA
  • Minor groove — narrower

These grooves are essential for gene regulation. Proteins that turn genes on and off bind to specific base sequences exposed in the major groove.

2.5 The Chemical Bonds in DNA: A Summary

Bond Type Location Strength Function
Covalent (phosphodiester) Between sugar and phosphate — forms the backbone of each strand Strong Provides structural integrity to the strand
Hydrogen Between complementary bases (A–T: 2 H-bonds; C–G: 3 H-bonds) Weak (individually) Holds the two strands together; allows them to separate for replication and transcription
Glycosidic Between base and sugar (1′ carbon) Covalent Attaches base to the sugar

Crucial IB point: The two strands are held together by hydrogen bonds between bases — NOT by covalent bonds. This means the strands can be separated (denatured by heat or enzymes) without breaking the covalent backbone of each strand. This is essential for DNA replication and transcription.

2.6 The Historical Evidence for DNA Structure

The IB syllabus requires you to know the evidence that led to our understanding of DNA. This is a classic Nature of Science (NOS) topic — how scientists build models from multiple lines of evidence.

Chargaff's Rules (1950)

Erwin Chargaff analyzed the base composition of DNA from many different organisms. He discovered two key patterns:

  1. In any DNA sample, the amount of adenine (A) equals the amount of thymine (T). That is, A = T.
  2. The amount of cytosine (C) equals the amount of guanine (G). That is, C = G.

This is Chargaff's rule. However, the ratio (A+T)/(G+C) varies between species — human DNA has a different A+T content than bacterial DNA.

What this implied: A pairs with T, and C pairs with G. Chargaff didn't know this yet, but his data was a key constraint for model building.

Franklin's X-ray Crystallography (1952)

Rosalind Franklin (working with Maurice Wilkins at King's College London) used X-ray crystallography to study DNA fibers. Here's how this technique works:

  1. A purified DNA sample is crystallized (molecules arranged in a regular pattern).
  2. X-rays are fired at the crystal.
  3. The X-rays diffract (bend) as they pass through the crystal, producing a pattern of spots on photographic film.
  4. The pattern of spots reveals the three-dimensional arrangement of atoms.

Franklin's famous "Photo 51" showed a distinctive X-shaped pattern. From this, she deduced:

  • DNA has a helical structure (the X shape is characteristic of a helix).
  • The helix has a regular, repeating structure with a periodicity of 3.4 nm per full turn.
  • The phosphate groups are on the outside of the molecule.
  • The molecule has a uniform diameter of 2 nm.

Franklin's data were shown to Watson and Crick without her knowledge, and they were the crucial missing pieces that allowed them to build their correct model.

IB Tip: The exam expects you to credit Franklin specifically for the helical shape, the dimensions (2 nm width, 3.4 nm per turn), and the phosphate backbone being on the outside.

Watson and Crick's Model Building (1953)

James Watson and Francis Crick at Cambridge University took a "model-building" approach. They constructed physical models of DNA using wire and metal plates, trying to fit all known chemical constraints:

  1. Chargaff's rules — A=T and C=G meant bases must pair specifically.
  2. Franklin's X-ray data — helix with 2 nm diameter, 3.4 nm per turn, phosphates outside.
  3. Chemical knowledge — the tautomeric forms of the bases (the forms that exist at cellular pH) dictated the specific hydrogen-bonding patterns.

They discovered that when A pairs with T (via 2 H-bonds) and C pairs with G (via 3 H-bonds), the pairs have identical shapes, fitting perfectly within the 2 nm cylinder. They also realized the strands must be antiparallel for the base pairs to align properly.

Their key insight: Complementary base pairing explains both Chargaff's ratios and the uniform diameter. It also immediately suggested a mechanism for replication: if you separate the strands, each can serve as a template to build a new complementary strand.

Watson, Crick, and Wilkins received the 1962 Nobel Prize. Franklin had died in 1958 and Nobels are not awarded posthumously, but her contribution is now widely recognized.

IB Paper 2 — NOS question style: "Describe how multiple lines of evidence contributed to the determination of DNA structure." Your answer should mention all three: Chargaff (base ratios), Franklin (X-ray: helix, 2 nm, phosphates outside), Watson & Crick (model building, base pairing). Explain what each contributed.

2.7 The Hershey-Chase Experiment: DNA Is the Genetic Material

Before 1952, many scientists thought proteins — not DNA — were the genetic material. Proteins are more chemically diverse (20 amino acids vs. 4 bases), so they seemed like better candidates for encoding complex information.

Alfred Hershey and Martha Chase designed an elegant experiment using bacteriophages (viruses that infect bacteria) to determine whether DNA or protein carries genetic information.

The Experimental Design

Background: A T2 bacteriophage consists of a protein coat (capsid) surrounding a DNA core. When it infects a bacterium (E. coli), it attaches to the surface and injects something into the cell. That "something" must be the genetic material because it redirects the bacterium to produce new phages.

The question: Is the injected material DNA or protein?

The trick: DNA contains phosphorus (in its phosphate backbone) but not sulfur. Proteins contain sulfur (in the amino acids cysteine and methionine) but not phosphorus.

Hershey and Chase grew two batches of phages:

  1. Batch 1: Phages grown in a medium containing radioactive phosphorus-32 (³²P) → The phage DNA became radioactive (labeled).
  2. Batch 2: Phages grown in a medium containing radioactive sulfur-35 (³⁵S) → The phage protein coats became radioactive.

They then used each batch to infect separate cultures of E. coli. After allowing time for infection, they agitated the cultures in a blender to shear off phage parts stuck to the outside of the bacteria, then centrifuged:

  • Heavy bacteria (pellet) went to the bottom of the tube.
  • Lighter phage parts (supernatant) stayed in the liquid above.

The Results

Isotope used Labels... Found in pellet (inside bacteria)? Found in supernatant (outside)?
³²P DNA YES** — most radioactivity in pellet Some in supernatant (unattached phages)
³⁵S Protein No — radioactivity in supernatant YES** — most radioactivity in supernatant

The radioactive phosphorus (DNA) entered the bacteria. The radioactive sulfur (protein) stayed outside. This proved that DNA, not protein, is the genetic material — DNA is what the phage injects into the bacterium to direct new phage production.

Draw this: Draw a simple diagram: a phage (hexagonal head + legs) attached to a bacterium (oval). Show the phage injecting a squiggly line (DNA). On the left, label "³²P-labeled DNA → enters bacteria." On the right, label "³⁵S-labeled protein → stays outside."

Hershey-Chase Experiment (1952): An experiment using radioactive isotopes (³²P for DNA, ³⁵S for protein) to demonstrate that DNA, not protein, is the genetic material of bacteriophages.

2.8 Drawing DNA for IB Exams

You may be asked to draw and label a simple diagram of DNA. Here's exactly what to include:

Step-by-Step Drawing Instructions

  1. Draw two parallel, slightly curved lines going down the page. These are the sugar-phosphate backbones. Label one "Sugar-phosphate backbone." Make them about 2 cm apart. Add an arrow: one strand points "5′" at the top and "3′" at the bottom; the other strand is reversed.
  1. Draw horizontal lines connecting the two backbones. These represent base pairs. Draw 3–4 of them, evenly spaced.
  1. Between the horizontal lines, draw the H-bonds as dotted lines:
  • For A–T: two dotted lines
  • For C–G: three dotted lines
  • Label each base clearly.
  1. Label:
  • Deoxyribose sugar (on the backbone)
  • Phosphate (alternating on the backbone)
  • Phosphodiester bond (between nucleotides)
  • Hydrogen bond (dotted lines between bases)
  • Complementary base pair
  • 3′ end and 5′ end on each strand
  • "Antiparallel strands"
  1. Add dimensions: Label "Diameter = 2 nm" across the helix. Note "One full turn = 3.4 nm (10 base pairs)."

Measurement Value Significance

Distance between base pairs 0.34 nm Constant along the helix
Base pairs per full turn 10 Gives 3.4 nm per turn (10 × 0.34)
Length of one full turn 3.4 nm Pitch of the helix

H-bonds in C–G pair 3 Stronger — CG-rich DNA is more thermally stable

2.10 Worked Examples

Question 1: If one strand of DNA has the sequence 5′-A-T-G-C-C-T-A-3′, write the sequence of the complementary strand, including directionality.

Solution:

Step 1: Apply complementary base pairing rules.

  • A pairs with T
  • T pairs with A
  • G pairs with C
  • C pairs with G

Step 2: Work through each base:

  • A → T, T → A, G → C, C → G, C → G, T → A, A → T

Step 3: The complementary sequence is T-A-C-G-G-A-T.

Step 4: Remember antiparallel direction. If the given strand is 5′ → 3′, the complementary strand runs 3′ → 5′. So the full answer is:

3′-T-A-C-G-G-A-T-5′

Question 2: A sample of DNA contains 30% adenine. What percentage of each other base is present?

Solution:

Step 1: Apply Chargaff's rule: A = T.

If A = 30%, then T = 30%.

Step 2: The remaining percentage: 100% − (A + T) = 100% − 60% = 40%. This 40% is split equally between C and G (C = G).

Step 3: C = 20%, G = 20%.

Answer: A = 30%, T = 30%, C = 20%, G = 20%.

Question 3: A DNA molecule is 10,000 base pairs long. Calculate its length in nanometers and micrometers.

Solution:

Step 1: Recall that each base pair is separated by 0.34 nm along the helix.

Step 2: Length = number of base pairs × distance per base pair

Length = 10,000 × 0.34 = 3,400 nm

Step 3: Convert to µm: 1 µm = 1,000 nm, so 3,400 nm = 3.4 µm.

Answer: 3,400 nm or 3.4 µm.

2.11 Practice Problems

Problem 1 (Paper 1 — Multiple Choice)

What type of bond links nucleotides together in a single DNA strand?

A. Hydrogen bond

B. Peptide bond

C. Phosphodiester bond

D. Glycosidic bond

Answer

C. Phosphodiester bonds link the phosphate of one nucleotide to the 3′ –OH of the next nucleotide's sugar.

Problem 2 (Paper 1 — Multiple Choice)

Which of the following is a correct statement about the DNA double helix?

A. The two strands are parallel (both 5′ → 3′ in the same direction).

B. Adenine pairs with cytosine via three hydrogen bonds.

C. The sugar-phosphate backbone is on the inside of the helix.

D. A purine always pairs with a pyrimidine, maintaining a uniform 2 nm diameter.

Answer

D. Purine (A or G) paired with pyrimidine (T or C) gives a consistent 2 nm width. A is wrong (strands are antiparallel), B is wrong (A pairs with T), C is wrong (backbone is outside).

  2. Bacteriophages with radioactively labeled DNA or protein were allowed to infect bacteria. ✓
  3. After blending and centrifugation, ³²P (DNA) was found inside the bacterial cells (in the pellet), while ³⁵S (protein) remained outside (in the supernatant). ✓
  4. This showed that DNA was the material injected into the bacteria to direct new phage production, proving DNA is the genetic material. ✓

Outline how Chargaff's data and Franklin's X-ray crystallography contributed to Watson and Crick's model of DNA.

Model Answer

  • Chargaff's data showed that A = T and C = G in all DNA samples, which suggested specific base pairing (A with T, C with G). ✓
  • Franklin's X-ray crystallography (Photo 51) revealed that DNA is a helix of uniform 2 nm diameter, with phosphates on the outside and a periodicity of 3.4 nm per turn. ✓

2.12 Common Misconceptions

Misconception The Truth
"Hydrogen bonds hold the backbone together." The backbone is held together by covalent phosphodiester bonds. H-bonds are only between complementary bases.
"A-T pairs are stronger because they have two bonds." C-G pairs have 3 H-bonds and are stronger. AT-rich regions of DNA separate more easily when heated.
"DNA and RNA have the same sugar." DNA has deoxyribose (missing an –OH at 2′). RNA has ribose (with –OH at 2′).
"The two strands are mirror images." They are complementary, not identical. Running antiparallel, they have different sequences that base-pair.
"All organisms have the same A:T and C:G ratios." The ratio (A+T)/(C+G) varies between species. Only A=T and C=G within a species is universal.
