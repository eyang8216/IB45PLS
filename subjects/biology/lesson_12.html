1. What Are Carbohydrates? The Fuel of Life

Imagine you are building a fire. You need fuel — something that burns easily and releases energy quickly. In living organisms, carbohydrates are that fuel. But they are not only fuel; they are also structural scaffolding, identification tags, and information storage molecules.

Chemically, carbohydrates are compounds made of carbon (C), hydrogen (H), and oxygen (O). The name gives it away: carbo- (carbon) + hydrate (water). The general empirical formula for simple carbohydrates is:

$$C_n(H_2O)_n$$

For example, glucose has the formula $$C_6H_{12}O_6$$ — six carbons, six water molecules' worth of H and O. This is why they are sometimes called "hydrates of carbon."

Carbohydrate: An organic compound composed of carbon, hydrogen, and oxygen atoms, generally with the empirical formula $$C_n(H_2O)_n$$. Carbohydrates include sugars, starches, glycogen, and cellulose. They serve as primary energy sources and structural components in living organisms.

Carbohydrates are classified by size into three broad groups:

Group Definition Examples
Monosaccharides Single sugar units (monomers) — cannot be hydrolyzed into simpler sugars Glucose, fructose, galactose, ribose
Disaccharides Two monosaccharides joined by a glycosidic bond Maltose, sucrose, lactose
Polysaccharides Many monosaccharides linked in long chains (polymers) Starch, glycogen, cellulose

2. Monosaccharides: The Building Blocks

Monosaccharides are the simplest carbohydrates. They typically contain 3 to 7 carbon atoms. For IB Biology HL, you must know five specific monosaccharides in detail.

2.1 The Carbonyl Group: Aldoses vs Ketoses

Every monosaccharide contains either an aldehyde group (-CHO) or a ketone group (C=O). This distinction is the first branching point in sugar classification:

  • Aldose: The carbonyl group (C=O) is at the end of the carbon chain (an aldehyde).
  • Ketose: The carbonyl group is on an internal carbon (a ketone).

Analogy: Think of aldoses as a necklace with the clasp at one end, and ketoses as a necklace with the clasp somewhere in the middle.

Aldose: A monosaccharide whose carbonyl group is an aldehyde (at the terminal carbon). Example: glucose.

Ketose: A monosaccharide whose carbonyl group is a ketone (at an internal carbon). Example: fructose.

2.2 Glucose ($$C_6H_{12}O_6$$): The Central Sugar of Metabolism

Glucose is the most important monosaccharide in biology. It is the primary fuel for cellular respiration in nearly all organisms, and it is the monomer from which starch, glycogen, and cellulose are built.

Glucose is a hexose (6-carbon sugar) and an aldose (its carbonyl is an aldehyde at C-1).

#### Drawing Glucose: The Fischer Projection (Straight-Chain Form)

The Fischer projection is a way of drawing sugars in their open-chain form. Here is a step-by-step guide to drawing glucose:

Step 1: Draw a vertical chain of 6 carbon atoms. The most oxidized carbon (the aldehyde carbon, C-1) goes at the top.

Step 2: Attach the functional groups:

  • C-1: Aldehyde (-CHO) — the carbon is double-bonded to O and single-bonded to H.
  • C-2 to C-5: Each carbon has an -OH group and an -H. The orientation of -OH (left or right) determines the identity of the sugar.
  • C-6: A primary alcohol (-CH₂OH).

For glucose (specifically D-glucose, the biologically relevant form), the -OH groups are oriented as follows on the Fischer projection:

```

H-C=O (C-1, aldehyde)

H-C-OH (C-2, -OH on the RIGHT)

HO-C-H (C-3, -OH on the LEFT)

H-C-OH (C-4, -OH on the RIGHT)

H-C-OH (C-5, -OH on the RIGHT)

CH₂OH (C-6)

```

What to draw in your notes: A vertical backbone with H-C-OH and HO-C-H alternating. Label each carbon 1 through 6. Remember: D-glucose = -OH on C-5 is on the RIGHT. This is the defining feature of all D-sugars.

IB Exam Tip: You do NOT need to memorize the full Fischer projection for every monosaccharide, but you SHOULD be able to recognize D-glucose and understand that the ring form is derived from the straight chain. The IB often asks you to draw the ring form (Haworth projection).

#### The Ring Structure: Haworth Projection

In aqueous solution, glucose exists predominantly (over 99%) in its cyclic (ring) form. The ring forms when the carbonyl group (C=1) reacts with a hydroxyl group on C-5. This is an intramolecular reaction — the molecule reacts with itself.

The oxygen from the C-5 hydroxyl attacks the electrophilic carbonyl carbon (C-1), forming a hemiacetal ring. The result is a 6-membered ring containing 5 carbons and 1 oxygen (a pyranose ring).

How to draw the glucose ring from the Fischer projection — the "right → down, left → up" rule:

  1. Draw a hexagon with oxygen at the top-right corner. Number the carbons clockwise starting from the right of the oxygen (C-1) around to C-5, with C-6 as a side chain (-CH₂OH) on C-5.
  2. For D-sugars: the -CH₂OH on C-5 points UP (above the ring).
  3. Place -OH groups: groups that were on the RIGHT in the Fischer projection point DOWN in the Haworth; groups that were on the LEFT point UP.

Following this for D-glucose:

  • C-2 -OH: Right in Fischer → DOWN in Haworth
  • C-3 -OH: Left in Fischer → UP in Haworth
  • C-4 -OH: Right in Fischer → DOWN in Haworth

Now for C-1: the new chiral center created during ring closure. The -OH can be in one of two positions:

  • α-glucose: The -OH on C-1 points DOWN (below the ring plane — opposite side from the C-5 -CH₂OH).
  • β-glucose: The -OH on C-1 points UP (above the ring plane — same side as the C-5 -CH₂OH).

α-glucose: The anomer of glucose where the hydroxyl (-OH) group on carbon 1 is positioned below the plane of the ring (opposite the -CH₂OH on C-5). This is the form found in starch and glycogen.

β-glucose: The anomer of glucose where the -OH on carbon 1 is above the plane of the ring (same side as the -CH₂OH on C-5). This is the form found in cellulose.

Diagram to draw in your notes: Side-by-side Haworth projections of α-glucose and β-glucose, clearly showing the -OH on C-1 pointing DOWN in α and UP in β. Also draw the ring-flipping equilibrium sign (⇌) between them — in solution, α and β interconvert via the open-chain form (mutarotation).

Why do α and β matter so much? Because the orientation of that single -OH on C-1 determines the type of glycosidic bond that can form, which in turn determines whether the resulting polymer is digestible or indigestible, helical or straight, energy storage or structural. This tiny geometric difference is one of the most important concepts in all of biochemistry.

2.3 Fructose ($$C_6H_{12}O_6$$): The Fruit Sugar

Fructose is a structural isomer of glucose — same molecular formula ($$C_6H_{12}O_6$$), different arrangement of atoms. Fructose is a ketose (its carbonyl is a ketone at C-2), whereas glucose is an aldose.

In its ring form, fructose forms a 5-membered ring (a furanose) — 4 carbons and 1 oxygen. This happens because the C-5 hydroxyl reacts with the ketone at C-2.

Key features for your diagram:

  • 5-membered ring (furanose)
  • -CH₂OH side chains at both C-1 and C-6 (two -CH₂OH groups total)
  • The anomeric carbon is C-2 (because that's where the ketone was)
  • Naturally occurring fructose is the β-D-fructofuranose form

Fructose is the sweetest of all natural sugars. It is found in fruit, honey, and high-fructose corn syrup. When combined with glucose, it forms the disaccharide sucrose (table sugar).

2.4 Galactose ($$C_6H_{12}O_6$$): The Milk Sugar Monomer

Galactose is another structural isomer of glucose. It is also a hexose and an aldose, but it differs in the configuration at C-4: in galactose, the -OH on C-4 points UP in the Haworth projection (compared to DOWN in glucose).

What to remember:

  • Galactose = glucose with the C-4 -OH flipped
  • Found in milk as part of the disaccharide lactose (galactose + glucose)
  • Important in glycolipids and glycoproteins of the brain and nervous system

Diagram: Draw galactose's Haworth projection. Label the C-4 -OH pointing UP — this is the only difference from glucose. Add an arrow to highlight "C-4 epimer of glucose."

2.5 Ribose ($$C_5H_{10}O_5$$): The RNA Sugar

Ribose is a pentose (5-carbon sugar) and an aldose. It is the sugar component of RNA (ribonucleic acid), where it forms the backbone by alternating with phosphate groups.

In its ring form, ribose is a furanose (5-membered ring, like fructose). The anomeric carbon (C-1) attaches to a nitrogenous base (adenine, guanine, cytosine, or uracil).

Key features:

  • 5 carbons
  • All -OH groups point DOWN in the β-D-ribofuranose form
  • In RNA, the -OH on C-2 is present and is crucial — it makes RNA less stable than DNA (the 2'-OH can catalyze hydrolysis of the phosphodiester backbone)

Diagram: Draw β-D-ribofuranose: a pentagon with O at the top-right, -CH₂OH pointing UP from C-4, and -OH groups at each position. Label: "C-2 has -OH → this is ribose (RNA)."

2.6 Deoxyribose ($$C_5H_{10}O_4$$): The DNA Sugar

Deoxyribose is ribose with one crucial difference: the -OH on C-2 is replaced with just an -H. This is the "deoxy" (meaning "without oxygen") modification.

Why does this matter? The absence of the 2'-OH makes DNA far more chemically stable than RNA. This stability is essential for DNA's role as the long-term genetic storage molecule. RNA is more reactive and thus suited for short-term roles (messenger RNA, transfer RNA, etc.).

Diagram: Draw deoxyribose next to ribose. Circle the C-2 position and show: ribose has -OH, deoxyribose has -H. Write: "The missing oxygen makes DNA stable."

IB Exam Tip: Be precise when drawing ribose and deoxyribose. Examiners look specifically at C-2. If you draw an -OH on C-2 of deoxyribose, you lose the mark. Always double-check.

3. Disaccharides: Two Sugars Joined Together

A disaccharide forms when two monosaccharides are linked by a glycosidic bond through a condensation reaction. This is also called a dehydration synthesis because a water molecule ($$H_2O$$) is removed.

3.1 The Condensation Reaction (Making the Bond)

Mechanism: The -OH group on the anomeric carbon (C-1) of one monosaccharide reacts with an -OH group on the other monosaccharide. One -OH provides H, the other provides -OH → these combine to form $$H_2O$$, which is released. The two sugar residues are now joined through an oxygen atom: C–O–C. This is the glycosidic bond.

Analogy: Think of two train cars being coupled together. The coupling mechanism is the glycosidic bond, and the "spark" released during coupling is the water molecule.

Condensation reaction (dehydration synthesis): A chemical reaction in which two molecules are joined together with the simultaneous removal of a water molecule. Each reactant contributes part of the water: one donates -H, the other donates -OH.

Glycosidic bond: A covalent bond formed between the anomeric carbon of one sugar and a hydroxyl group of another sugar (or another molecule). The bond type (α or β) depends on the orientation of the -OH on the anomeric carbon of the first sugar.

3.2 Hydrolysis (Breaking the Bond)

Hydrolysis is the reverse of condensation. The glycosidic bond is cleaved by the addition of a water molecule ($$H_2O$$). One sugar receives -H, the other receives -OH, restoring both monosaccharides.

hydro = water, lysis = splitting.

Analogy: Cutting a rope with a water-powered blade. The water molecule is inserted into the bond, splitting the two units apart.

Hydrolysis: A chemical reaction in which a covalent bond is broken by the addition of a water molecule. The water is split: -H attaches to one fragment, -OH attaches to the other.

3.3 Maltose: Glucose + Glucose (α-1,4 Glycosidic Bond)

Maltose is formed from two glucose molecules joined by an α-1,4 glycosidic bond.

The name "α-1,4" tells you:

  • α: The -OH on C-1 of the first glucose is in the α position (DOWN).
  • 1,4: The bond is between C-1 of the first glucose and C-4 of the second glucose.

Diagram to draw:

  1. Draw two glucose rings side by side.
  2. The left glucose: α-glucose (C-1 -OH down).
  3. The right glucose: C-4 -OH is involved in the bond.
  4. Show the oxygen bridge between C-1 (left) and C-4 (right).
  5. Show the water molecule ($$H_2O$$) being removed — the -H from the C-4 -OH and the -OH from C-1.
  6. Label "α-1,4 glycosidic bond."

The second glucose can close its ring with C-1 -OH either α or β — in maltose, the free anomeric carbon can mutarotate.

Where is maltose found? Maltose is produced during the breakdown of starch (specifically amylose) by the enzyme amylase. It is found in germinating seeds (where starch is being mobilized) and malted grains used in brewing.

3.4 Sucrose: Glucose + Fructose (α-1,2 Glycosidic Bond)

Sucrose is common table sugar. It is composed of α-glucose and β-fructose linked by an α-1,2 glycosidic bond.

Key points:

  • The bond is between C-1 of α-glucose and C-2 of β-fructose (C-2 is fructose's anomeric carbon because it is a ketose).
  • Both anomeric carbons are locked in the glycosidic bond. This means sucrose is a non-reducing sugar — it cannot open to the straight-chain form, so it cannot reduce other compounds (unlike maltose or lactose, which are reducing sugars).

Diagram to draw:

  1. α-glucose (6-ring, pyranose) on the left.
  2. β-fructose (5-ring, furanose) on the right.
  3. Show the oxygen bridge between glucose C-1 and fructose C-2.
  4. Label "α-1,2 glycosidic bond."
  5. Note: "Non-reducing sugar — both anomeric carbons involved."

Where is sucrose found? Sugar cane, sugar beet, maple sap, and many fruits. It is the main transport sugar in plants (translocated in phloem).

IB Exam Tip: Students often confuse which sugars make up which disaccharide. A simple mnemonic:

  • Maltose = two Monosaccharides of glucose → glucose + glucose
  • Sucrose = Sweet → glucose + fructose
  • Lactose = Lactating (milk) → glucose + galactose

3.5 Lactose: Glucose + Galactose (β-1,4 Glycosidic Bond)

Lactose is milk sugar. It consists of β-galactose and glucose joined by a β-1,4 glycosidic bond.

Key points:

  • The bond is between C-1 of β-galactose and C-4 of glucose.
  • It is a β linkage because the -OH on C-1 of galactose is in the β position (UP).
  • Lactose is a reducing sugar because the glucose unit has a free anomeric carbon (C-1) that can open to the aldehyde form.

Diagram: Draw β-galactose (C-1 -OH UP) on the left linked to glucose on the right via an oxygen bridge between C-1 (galactose) and C-4 (glucose). Label "β-1,4 glycosidic bond."

Lactose intolerance: Many adults worldwide have reduced levels of lactase, the enzyme that hydrolyzes the β-1,4 bond of lactose. Without lactase, undigested lactose passes to the large intestine where gut bacteria ferment it, producing gas, bloating, and diarrhea. This is the ancestral (normal) state for most mammals after weaning; the ability to digest lactose into adulthood (lactase persistence) is a relatively recent human evolutionary adaptation, common in populations with a long history of dairy farming (e.g., Northern Europeans, some African pastoralist groups).

4. Polysaccharides: Many Sugars, Many Functions

Polysaccharides are polymers of monosaccharides linked by glycosidic bonds. They can contain hundreds to thousands of sugar units. Polysaccharides serve two main roles:

  1. Energy storage (starch in plants, glycogen in animals)
  2. Structural support (cellulose in plant cell walls)

4.1 Starch: The Plant's Energy Reserve

Starch is the primary storage polysaccharide in plants. It is a polymer of α-glucose and exists as a mixture of two forms: amylose and amylopectin.

#### 4.1.1 Amylose (Unbranched, Helical)

Amylose is a linear (unbranched) polymer of α-glucose units connected by α-1,4 glycosidic bonds.

Structure:

  • Glucose units linked α-1,4 only
  • The chain coils into a helix (spiral shape)
  • Each turn of the helix contains approximately 6 glucose residues
  • The interior of the helix is hydrophobic, allowing it to trap iodine molecules

The iodine test: When iodine ($$I_2$$ in KI solution) is added to starch, the iodine molecules fit inside the amylose helix, forming a blue-black complex. This is a specific diagnostic test for starch. Amylopectin alone gives a reddish-purple color with iodine; the deep blue-black color is specifically due to the amylose helix.

Diagram to draw: A long coiled ribbon representing the amylose helix. Show iodine molecules ($$I_3^-$$) nestled inside the coil. Label the α-1,4 linkages along the backbone.

#### 4.1.2 Amylopectin (Branched)

Amylopectin has the same α-1,4 backbone as amylose, but with additional α-1,6 glycosidic bonds creating branch points approximately every 20 glucose units.

Structure:

  • α-1,4 linked glucose backbone (like amylose)
  • α-1,6 branches every ~20 residues
  • The branches themselves are α-1,4 chains
  • The overall shape is more like a tree with many branches than a simple helix

Why branches? Branching creates many terminal glucose residues (non-reducing ends). Enzymes that degrade starch (amylase, phosphorylase) work from these non-reducing ends. More ends = faster mobilization of glucose when energy is needed quickly.

Diagram: Draw a branching tree structure. Show the main chain with α-1,4 bonds and the branches emerging via α-1,6 linkages. Label: "Branch point: α-1,6 glycosidic bond."

4.2 Glycogen: The Animal's Quick-Release Energy

Glycogen is the storage polysaccharide in animals (and fungi). It is structurally similar to amylopectin but with a critical difference: glycogen is more highly branched, with branch points approximately every 10 glucose units (compared to every ~20 in amylopectin).

Key features:

  • Polymer of α-glucose
  • α-1,4 backbone, α-1,6 branches every ~10 residues
  • Stored as granules in liver cells (hepatocytes) and muscle cells (myocytes)
  • The liver stores glycogen to maintain blood glucose levels (releases glucose into bloodstream between meals)
  • Muscle stores glycogen for local energy demands during exercise (muscle lacks glucose-6-phosphatase, so it cannot release free glucose into blood)

Why so many branches? Muscle contraction demands rapid ATP production. The abundance of branch points means glycogen phosphorylase can simultaneously attack many non-reducing ends, releasing glucose-1-phosphate very quickly for glycolysis.

Analogy: If amylopectin is a tree with some branches, glycogen is a dense shrub with branches everywhere. The more branch points, the more ends accessible simultaneously for rapid glucose release.

4.3 Cellulose: The Structural Giant

Cellulose is the most abundant organic polymer on Earth. It forms the structural component of plant cell walls and is a polymer of β-glucose (not α-glucose) linked by β-1,4 glycosidic bonds.

#### 4.3.1 The β-1,4 Difference — Why It Changes Everything

Because the -OH on C-1 of β-glucose points UP, every other glucose residue must be flipped 180° to form the β-1,4 bond. This produces a straight, linear chain with no coiling.

Compare:

  • α-1,4 linkages (starch, glycogen): All glucose units in the same orientation → chain coils → helical → accessible to digestive enzymes
  • β-1,4 linkages (cellulose): Every other glucose flipped → chain is straight → CANNOT be digested by human enzymes

#### 4.3.2 Microfibrils: Strength in Numbers

Individual cellulose chains run parallel to each other. The hydroxyl (-OH) groups on adjacent chains form hydrogen bonds, zipping the chains together into bundles called microfibrils. These microfibrils have enormous tensile strength (resistance to being pulled apart) — comparable to steel of the same diameter.

Multiple microfibrils are further bundled into macrofibrils, which are embedded in a matrix of other polysaccharides (hemicellulose, pectin) in the plant cell wall.

Diagram to draw:

  1. Three parallel cellulose chains, each with alternating flipped glucose units.
  2. Dashed lines between the chains representing hydrogen bonds between -OH groups.
  3. Label: "β-1,4 glycosidic bonds → straight chains → H-bonding → microfibrils → high tensile strength."

#### 4.3.3 Why Can't Humans Digest Cellulose?

The enzyme amylase (in human saliva and pancreatic juice) can hydrolyze α-1,4 glycosidic bonds. It recognizes the specific geometry of α-linked glucose polymers and fits them into its active site.

The enzyme cellulase is required to hydrolyze β-1,4 bonds. Humans do not produce cellulase. Therefore:

  • Starch (α-1,4) → amylase can digest → glucose absorbed → energy
  • Cellulose (β-1,4) → no human enzyme → passes through as dietary fiber → no calories

But: Ruminants (cows, sheep) and termites harbor symbiotic microorganisms (bacteria, protozoa) in their gut that DO produce cellulase. These organisms digest the cellulose, and the host animal absorbs the fermentation products (short-chain fatty acids).

IB Exam Tip: This α vs β distinction is one of the most fundamental concepts in B1.1. Examiners love asking: "Explain why humans can digest starch but not cellulose." The answer always boils down to: starch has α-1,4 linkages that fit the active site of amylase; cellulose has β-1,4 linkages that do not. Mention the structural consequence too — β-1,4 produces straight chains that H-bond into microfibrils, while α-1,4 produces helical chains.

5. Glycoproteins and Glycolipids: Sugar Tags on the Cell Surface

Not all carbohydrates exist as independent molecules. Many are covalently attached to proteins or lipids on the cell surface, where they serve crucial roles in cell recognition, adhesion, and communication.

5.1 Glycoproteins

A glycoprotein is a protein with one or more oligosaccharide (short carbohydrate) chains covalently attached. The carbohydrate is typically attached to the amino acid side chains of asparagine (N-linked) or serine/threonine (O-linked).

Functions:

  • Cell-cell recognition: The carbohydrate chains act like ID tags on the cell surface. The immune system uses these to distinguish "self" from "non-self."
  • Cell adhesion: Glycoproteins help cells stick to each other and to the extracellular matrix.
  • Protection: The carbohydrate coating (glycocalyx) protects the cell membrane from mechanical and chemical damage.
  • Signaling: Many receptor proteins on the cell surface are glycoproteins; the carbohydrate chains can modulate receptor function.

Example — ABO Blood Groups: The ABO blood group system is determined by specific glycoproteins (and glycolipids) on the surface of red blood cells. The "A" and "B" antigens are oligosaccharide chains attached to a base carbohydrate (the H antigen):

  • Type A: Has an additional N-acetylgalactosamine on the H antigen
  • Type B: Has an additional galactose on the H antigen
  • Type AB: Has both A and B antigens
  • Type O: Has only the H antigen (no additional sugar)

These differences are determined by different alleles of a single gene encoding a glycosyltransferase enzyme. Type A allele → transferase that adds N-acetylgalactosamine. Type B allele → transferase that adds galactose. Type O allele → non-functional enzyme, no modification.

The immune system produces antibodies against the blood group antigens you do NOT have (anti-A in type B individuals, anti-B in type A individuals, neither in AB, both in O). This is why blood type compatibility is crucial for transfusions.

5.2 Glycolipids

A glycolipid is a lipid (usually a sphingolipid) with a carbohydrate chain attached to its head group. Like glycoproteins, glycolipids are found on the extracellular face of the plasma membrane and contribute to the glycocalyx — the fuzzy carbohydrate coat surrounding many cells.

Functions:

  • Cell recognition (especially in the nervous system and immune system)
  • Membrane stability
  • Cell-cell adhesion
  • Some glycolipids are receptors for bacterial toxins (e.g., GM1 ganglioside is the receptor for cholera toxin)

6. Worked Examples

Worked Example 1: Identifying Glycosidic Bonds

A student is shown the structure of a disaccharide. The left sugar is α-glucose and the right sugar is galactose. The bond connects C-1 of glucose to C-4 of the galactose. What type of glycosidic bond is this?

Solution:

Step 1: Identify the anomeric carbon of the first sugar. The first sugar is glucose and its anomeric carbon (C-1) is in the α configuration (OH down).

Step 2: Check which carbon of the second sugar is involved. It is C-4 of galactose.

Step 3: Name the bond: α-1,4 glycosidic bond.

Step 4: Note: the identity of the second sugar (galactose vs glucose) does not change the bond name. The bond is named from the first sugar's anomeric configuration and the carbon numbers involved.

Answer: α-1,4 glycosidic bond.

Worked Example 2: Calculating Water Molecules Released During Polymerization

A polysaccharide chain containing 500 glucose monomers is formed from free glucose by condensation reactions. How many water molecules are released?

Solution:

Step 1: Understand the relationship. Each glycosidic bond forms with the release of exactly one water molecule.

Step 2: Calculate the number of bonds. For n monomers linked in a linear chain, there are (n - 1) bonds.

  • For 500 glucose monomers in an unbranched chain: 500 - 1 = 499 glycosidic bonds.

Step 3: Each bond releases one H₂O. Therefore, 499 water molecules are released.

Step 4: Note: If the polymer were branched (e.g., amylopectin), the number of bonds and water molecules would still equal (n - 1), because every branch point is still just one extra glycosidic bond.

Answer: 499 water molecules.

Worked Example 3: The Iodine Test and Starch Structure

A student performs the iodine test on two solutions. Solution A turns a deep blue-black. Solution B turns reddish-purple. What can you deduce about the polysaccharide composition of each solution?

Solution:

The iodine test results are determined by the structure of the polysaccharide:

  • Blue-black color: This is characteristic of amylose. The iodine molecules fit inside the helical structure formed by the α-1,4 linked chains. The helix has approximately 6 glucose residues per turn, and iodine chains ($$I_3^-$$ or $$I_5^-$$) are the right size to slip inside. The charge-transfer complex formed between iodine and the amylose helix absorbs light, producing the blue-black color.
  • Reddish-purple color: This is characteristic of amylopectin or glycogen. These branched molecules have shorter helical segments due to branching (every ~20 residues for amylopectin, ~10 for glycogen), and iodine cannot form the same extended complex. The result is a different color.

Answer: Solution A contains predominantly amylose (unbranched starch). Solution B contains predominantly amylopectin and/or glycogen (branched α-glucose polymers).

Starch is composed of α-glucose units linked by α-1,4 glycosidic bonds (with some α-1,6 branches in amylopectin). The α-linkage causes the polymer chain to coil into a helical structure. Humans produce amylase (in saliva and pancreatic secretions), an enzyme whose active site is complementary to the α-1,4 glycosidic bond. Amylase hydrolyzes these bonds, releasing maltose and glucose, which can be absorbed.

Cellulose is composed of β-glucose units linked by β-1,4 glycosidic bonds. The β-linkage forces every other glucose to be flipped 180°, producing a straight, linear chain. The straight chains pack together via hydrogen bonds to form rigid microfibrils. Humans do NOT produce cellulase, the enzyme needed to hydrolyze β-1,4 bonds. The human digestive system lacks any enzyme with an active site complementary to the β-1,4 linkage. Consequently, cellulose passes through the digestive tract undigested as dietary fiber.

The structural consequence is also important: the β-1,4 straight chains allow extensive inter-chain H-bonding, creating microfibrils that are physically resistant to enzymatic attack. The α-1,4 helical chains are more accessible to enzymes.

Answer 4

For 300 glucose residues with a branch every 20 residues of the main chain:

  • Number of branch points = 300 ÷ 20 = 15 α-1,6 glycosidic bonds.
  • Total glycosidic bonds in a polymer of n monomers = n - 1 = 300 - 1 = 299.
  • Of these 299 bonds, 15 are α-1,6 and the remaining 284 are α-1,4.

Answer: 299 total glycosidic bonds (284 α-1,4 + 15 α-1,6).

Answer 5

Similarities:

  • Both are polymers of α-glucose
  • Both have α-1,4 backbones with α-1,6 branches
  • Both function as energy storage polysaccharides
  • Both are stored as granules in cells

Differences:

Feature Amylopectin Glycogen
Organism Plants Animals and fungi
Branching frequency Every ~20 glucose residues Every ~10 glucose residues
Storage location Starch granules in chloroplasts/amyloplasts Cytoplasmic granules in liver and muscle
Overall structure Moderately branched Highly branched ("bush-like")

Metabolic rationale for difference:

Animals (especially active ones) have higher and more rapidly fluctuating energy demands than plants. Muscle contraction during exercise can increase ATP demand 100-fold within seconds. The higher branching frequency of glycogen provides more non-reducing ends per molecule, allowing glycogen phosphorylase to simultaneously release glucose-1-phosphate from many sites. This enables extremely rapid glucose mobilization.

Plants, being sessile (non-moving) organisms, have lower and more constant metabolic rates. They do not need the same burst capacity, so the moderate branching of amylopectin is sufficient. Additionally, the helical amylose component of starch provides compact, densely packed energy storage that is mobilized more slowly — appropriate for a plant that stores energy for overnight or seasonal use rather than immediate sprinting.

9. Common Misconceptions

Misconception Reality
"All sugars have 6 carbons" Ribose and deoxyribose are pentoses with 5 carbons. Fructose is a hexose but forms a 5-membered furanose ring.
"α and β glucose are just the same thing drawn differently" They are different molecules with different 3D orientations at C-1, leading to profoundly different polymers.
"Glycosidic bonds are always between C-1 and C-4" Sucrose has an α-1,2 bond. Branch points in amylopectin/glycogen use α-1,6 bonds.
"Condensation and hydrolysis are the same thing" They are exact opposites. Condensation removes water to form a bond; hydrolysis adds water to break a bond.
"Cellulose is just another energy source we can't use" Cellulose is structurally and chemically different (β-1,4), not just a hard-to-digest version of starch.
"Glycogen is only stored in the liver" Significant glycogen is also stored in skeletal muscle. Muscle glycogen cannot release free glucose into blood (lacks glucose-6-phosphatase).

10. Key Terms Summary

Term Definition
Monosaccharide A single sugar unit; the simplest carbohydrate (e.g., glucose, fructose)
Disaccharide Two monosaccharides joined by a glycosidic bond (e.g., maltose)
Polysaccharide A polymer of many monosaccharides (e.g., starch, cellulose)
Glycosidic bond The covalent C–O–C linkage between sugar units
Condensation reaction Joining two molecules with the loss of water
Hydrolysis Breaking a bond by adding water
α-1,4 linkage Glycosidic bond where α-configured C-1 links to C-4 of the next sugar
β-1,4 linkage Glycosidic bond where β-configured C-1 links to C-4, requiring alternating flipped residues
Amylose Unbranched α-1,4 glucose polymer; helical; gives blue-black color with iodine
Amylopectin Branched α-glucose polymer with α-1,4 backbone and α-1,6 branches ~ every 20 residues
Glycogen Highly branched α-glucose polymer; branches every ~10 residues; animal energy storage
Cellulose β-1,4 glucose polymer; straight chains; H-bonded into microfibrils; structural
Glycoprotein Protein with covalently attached oligosaccharide chains
Glycolipid Lipid with covalently attached carbohydrate; found on cell surface
