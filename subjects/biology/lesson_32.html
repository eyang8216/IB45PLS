What You'll Learn

Imagine you touch a hot stove. Before you even consciously register the pain, your hand has already jerked away. This lightning-fast response — faster than thought itself — is the work of your nervous system. In this lesson, you'll learn how neurons generate and transmit electrical signals, from the tiny voltage across a resting membrane to the explosive all-or-none action potential that races along your nerves at up to 120 meters per second. By the end, you'll understand:

  • The intricate structure of a neuron and how each part contributes to signal transmission
  • How the resting potential is established and maintained at −70 mV
  • The ionic basis of the action potential — the electrical "spike" that is the fundamental unit of neural communication
  • How myelin enables saltatory conduction, making your nervous system both fast and energy-efficient
  • The molecular dance of synaptic transmission — how one neuron talks to the next
  • Why neonicotinoid pesticides are devastating bee populations — a real-world application of cholinergic synapse biology

1. Neuron Structure: The Cellular Hardware

Neurons are the most morphologically specialized cells in your body. Some extend over a meter in length (from your spinal cord to your toes). Their structure is perfectly adapted for receiving, integrating, and transmitting electrical signals.

1.1 The Parts of a Neuron

Draw and label: A multipolar motor neuron showing all major structures. Use a large diagram with arrows pointing to each component.

Dendrites:

  • Branched, tree-like extensions from the soma (cell body)
  • Function: Receptive surface — they receive signals from other neurons or sensory receptors
  • Covered in tiny protrusions called dendritic spines that increase surface area for synaptic contacts
  • Contain ligand-gated ion channels (postsynaptic receptors) and voltage-gated channels
  • A single neuron may receive input from thousands of other neurons at its dendrites

Soma (Cell Body):

  • Contains the nucleus, ribosomes, ER, Golgi apparatus, and mitochondria
  • Standard cell biology applies: gene transcription → mRNA → protein synthesis
  • Particularly rich in rough ER (called Nissl bodies in neurons) reflecting high rates of protein synthesis
  • The point where signals from dendrites converge — but the "decision" to fire occurs at the axon hillock

Axon Hillock:

  • The cone-shaped region where the axon emerges from the soma
  • This is the integration zone — where all incoming signals (EPSPs and IPSPs) are summed
  • Has the highest density of voltage-gated Na⁺ channels in the neuron
  • If the summed depolarization reaches threshold (~−55 mV), the action potential is initiated here

Axon:

  • A single, long, cylindrical extension (can be over 1 meter)
  • Function: Conducts the action potential from the hillock to the synaptic terminals
  • Contains voltage-gated Na⁺ and K⁺ channels along its length for propagating the signal
  • May or may not be myelinated (see below)
  • Axoplasm (axon cytoplasm) contains microtubules and neurofilaments for structural support and transport

Myelin Sheath:

  • A fatty, insulating wrapping around the axon
  • Formed by Schwann cells in the PNS and oligodendrocytes in the CNS
  • Each Schwann cell wraps around a single segment of one axon; each oligodendrocyte can myelinate multiple axons
  • Increases conduction velocity and reduces metabolic cost
  • Appears white — this is what makes "white matter" white

Nodes of Ranvier:

  • Gaps between adjacent myelin segments where the axon membrane is exposed
  • Extremely high density of voltage-gated Na⁺ channels (thousands per μm²)
  • The only sites where ionic currents flow across the membrane in myelinated axons
  • This is where the action potential is "recharged" during saltatory conduction

Synaptic Terminals (Axon Terminals, Boutons):

  • Branched endings of the axon
  • Each contains synaptic vesicles filled with neurotransmitter
  • Contains voltage-gated Ca²⁺ channels — the critical link between electrical signal and chemical release
  • Mitochondria are abundant here to power vesicle cycling

Draw and label: A second diagram showing the comparison between myelinated axons in CNS vs PNS:

  • Left: CNS — oligodendrocyte sending out processes to wrap multiple axons
  • Right: PNS — individual Schwann cells, each wrapping one segment of one axon
  • Label: "CNS: oligodendrocytes (myelinate multiple axons)" and "PNS: Schwann cells (one segment per axon)"

1.2 Structural Adaptations for Function

Structure Key Adaptation Functional Significance
Dendrites (branched) Large surface area Maximizes synaptic input
Axon hillock High Na⁺ channel density Lowers threshold for AP initiation
Myelin sheath Lipid-rich insulation Increases conduction speed, reduces energy use
Nodes of Ranvier Exposed membrane, high Na⁺ channel density Allows regeneration of action potential

2. The Resting Membrane Potential (~−70 mV)

Every living cell has a voltage across its membrane, but neurons have perfected this property. The resting potential is the foundation upon which all neural signaling is built.

Resting membrane potential — The electrical potential difference across the neuron's plasma membrane when the cell is not transmitting a signal, typically −70 mV (inside negative relative to outside).

2.1 What Creates the Resting Potential?

Imagine two water tanks at different heights connected by a pipe — water flows until the levels equalize. But what if you had a pump constantly moving water back up? The resting potential is like this: a dynamic equilibrium maintained by the interplay of pumps, channels, and impermeant ions.

Factor 1 — The Na⁺/K⁺ Pump (Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase):

This is the active transport pump that sets up the concentration gradients:

  • Pumps 3 Na⁺ out and 2 K⁺ in for each ATP hydrolyzed
  • This is electrogenic — each cycle removes one net positive charge from the cell, contributing a small amount (~−5 to −10 mV) to the resting potential
  • More importantly, it establishes the concentration gradients that drive ion movement through channels:
  • [Na⁺] outside (~140 mM) >> [Na⁺] inside (~10-15 mM)
  • [K⁺] inside (~140 mM) >> [K⁺] outside (~4-5 mM)

Factor 2 — K⁺ Leak Channels (the Dominant Factor):

The membrane at rest is far more permeable to K⁺ than to Na⁺ (about 25-50× more permeable). This is because there are many K⁺ leak channels (always open) and very few open Na⁺ channels.

  • K⁺, driven by its concentration gradient, diffuses out of the cell through leak channels
  • As positive charges leave, the inside becomes more negative
  • This creates an electrical gradient pulling K⁺ back in (opposite charges attract)
  • Equilibrium potential for K⁺ (E_K): The voltage at which the concentration gradient driving K⁺ out exactly balances the electrical gradient pulling K⁺ in. For typical K⁺ gradients, E_K ≈ −90 mV
  • The resting potential (−70 mV) is close to but not exactly at E_K because there is some Na⁺ permeability

Factor 3 — Membrane Impermeability to Na⁺ at Rest:

Most voltage-gated Na⁺ channels are closed at rest. The small number that are open allow a trickle of Na⁺ influx, which makes the resting potential slightly more positive than E_K (−70 mV, not −90 mV). If the membrane were perfectly permeable only to K⁺, the resting potential would be −90 mV.

Factor 4 — Fixed Intracellular Anions:

Inside the cell, there are large, negatively charged molecules — proteins, nucleic acids, organic phosphates — that cannot cross the membrane (they are "fixed"). These contribute to the net negative charge inside.

  • Outside: High Na⁺, low K⁺, many "+" signs
  • Inside: Low Na⁺, high K⁺, many "−" signs (fixed anions), and A⁻ symbols for trapped negative proteins
  • Na⁺/K⁺ pump: Arrows showing 3 Na⁺ out, 2 K⁺ in, with "ATP → ADP + Pᵢ" label
  • K⁺ leak channel: Arrow showing net K⁺ movement OUT (driven by concentration gradient)

2.2 The Nernst Equation (Conceptual Understanding)

For IB HL, you should understand conceptually that the equilibrium potential for an ion depends on its concentration gradient:

$$E_{ion} = \frac{RT}{zF} \ln \frac{[ion]_{out}}{[ion]_{in}}$$

Key points:

  • E_K ≈ −90 mV (K⁺ wants to leave the cell)
  • E_Na ≈ +60 mV (Na⁺ wants to enter the cell)
  • The resting potential (−70 mV) is between these values but closer to E_K because the membrane is more permeable to K⁺

Paper 2 Tip: If asked to "explain how the resting potential is established," always include ALL three factors: (1) Na⁺/K⁺ pump creating gradients, (2) K⁺ leak channels allowing K⁺ efflux, and (3) low Na⁺ permeability at rest. Don't forget the fixed anions. Four factors = four marks.

3. The Action Potential: The Neural "Spike"

The action potential is the all-or-none electrical event that carries information along the axon. It's like a domino falling — once triggered, it goes to completion in a stereotyped, unvarying fashion.

Action potential — A brief, rapid, and large reversal of membrane potential (from −70 mV to about +40 mV and back) that propagates along the axon without decrement. It is all-or-none: either it fires fully or not at all.

Threshold — The membrane potential (typically ~−55 mV) at which voltage-gated Na⁺ channels open in sufficient numbers to trigger a self-sustaining depolarization.

3.1 The Phases of an Action Potential

Draw and label: A graph with membrane potential (mV) on the y-axis (−90 to +50) and time (ms) on the x-axis (0 to ~5 ms). The classic action potential shape should show all phases clearly.

Phase 1 — Resting State (−70 mV):

  • Na⁺/K⁺ pump maintains gradients
  • K⁺ leak channels dominate permeability
  • Voltage-gated Na⁺ channels: closed (activation gate closed, inactivation gate open)
  • Voltage-gated K⁺ channels: closed

Phase 2 — Depolarization to Threshold:

  • An incoming stimulus (EPSP, sensory stimulus, or propagating AP from upstream) depolarizes the membrane slightly
  • Some voltage-gated Na⁺ channels begin to open
  • If the depolarization reaches threshold (~−55 mV), a critical density of Na⁺ channels opens

Phase 3 — Rising Phase (Rapid Depolarization):

  • At threshold, positive feedback kicks in: depolarization → more Na⁺ channels open → more depolarization → even more Na⁺ channels open
  • Voltage-gated Na⁺ channels open rapidly (activation gates swing open)
  • Na⁺ rushes into the cell, driven by both its concentration gradient AND the electrical gradient (both favor entry — [Na⁺] high outside, inside negative)
  • The membrane potential shoots up toward E_Na (+60 mV)
  • It typically reaches about +30 to +40 mV before the peak

Phase 4 — Falling Phase (Repolarization):

  • Two events terminate the rising phase:
  1. Na⁺ channel inactivation: A separate "inactivation gate" (a cytoplasmic loop) swings shut, blocking the channel pore. This occurs automatically ~0.5 ms after opening — it's a built-in timer
  2. K⁺ channel activation: Voltage-gated K⁺ channels open, but they open more slowly than Na⁺ channels. By the time they're fully open, Na⁺ channels are inactivating
  • K⁺ rushes out of the cell (driven by its concentration gradient and now by the positive interior)
  • Outward K⁺ current repolarizes the membrane

Phase 5 — After-Hyperpolarization (Undershoot):

  • Voltage-gated K⁺ channels are slow to close. They remain open after the membrane has returned to −70 mV
  • Excess K⁺ efflux drives the potential below the normal resting value (to about −80 mV)
  • This is the relative refractory period

Phase 6 — Return to Resting:

  • Voltage-gated K⁺ channels eventually close
  • Na⁺/K⁺ pump and leak channels restore normal resting distributions
  • Na⁺ channels reset: inactivation gate opens, activation gate closes → ready for next stimulus

3.2 Ion Channel States During the Action Potential

Understanding channel gating is essential for the IB:

Phase Voltage-Gated Na⁺ Channels Voltage-Gated K⁺ Channels
Rest Closed (activation gate closed, inactivation gate open) → **Ready Closed
Threshold to peak Open (both gates open) → **Activated Opening (slowly)
Peak Inactivated (activation gate open, inactivation gate closed) → **Refractory Open
Falling phase Inactivated → transitioning to closed Open → K⁺ efflux
Hyperpolarization Closed (both gates closed, then reset) Closing (slowly)
Return to rest Closed → Ready again Closed

Crucial distinction: Na⁺ channels have TWO gates (activation and inactivation), while K⁺ channels have only one (activation). The inactivation gate on Na⁺ channels is what ensures the action potential is brief and unidirectional.

3.3 The All-or-None Principle

The action potential is all-or-none:

  • Subthreshold stimuli produce only graded potentials that decay
  • Once threshold is reached, the action potential fires to the same amplitude regardless of stimulus intensity
  • A stronger stimulus does NOT produce a "bigger" action potential — it produces a higher frequency of action potentials (frequency coding)

Think of it like a toilet flush: Pressing the handle harder doesn't make it flush "more" — it either flushes or it doesn't. But you can flush repeatedly.

3.4 Refractory Periods

Absolute refractory period:

  • During the action potential (rising phase to early repolarization)
  • Na⁺ channels are either open or inactivated
  • No stimulus, no matter how strong, can trigger another action potential
  • This ensures action potentials don't overlap and propagate only in one direction

Relative refractory period:

  • During the after-hyperpolarization
  • Na⁺ channels have recovered (closed, ready) but K⁺ channels are still open
  • The membrane is hyperpolarized — further from threshold than normal
  • A stronger-than-normal stimulus CAN trigger an action potential
  • This limits the maximum firing frequency (typically ~200-500 Hz in most neurons)

Draw and label: An action potential with the absolute and relative refractory periods shaded:

  • Shade the rising phase through early repolarization and label: "Absolute refractory period — no AP possible"
  • Shade the hyperpolarization and label: "Relative refractory period — stronger stimulus needed"

4. Propagation: How the Action Potential Travels

Once initiated at the axon hillock, the action potential must travel to the synaptic terminals — sometimes over a meter away.

4.1 Continuous Conduction (Unmyelinated Axons)

In unmyelinated axons, the action potential propagates by local current flow:

  1. At the site of the action potential, the membrane depolarizes (inside becomes positive)
  2. This positive charge spreads passively to adjacent regions of the axon — both forward and backward
  3. The backward spread cannot trigger another AP (that region is refractory), but the forward spread depolarizes the next segment to threshold
  4. A new action potential is generated at that adjacent segment
  5. This process repeats, like a line of falling dominoes

This is slow (~0.5-2 m/s) because every segment of membrane must undergo the full action potential cycle.

4.2 Saltatory Conduction (Myelinated Axons)

Saltatory conduction (saltare = to jump) is the rapid, energy-efficient mode of propagation in myelinated axons.

  • Myelin acts as an electrical insulator — ions cannot cross through the myelin-wrapped membrane
  • The action potential can only be generated at the nodes of Ranvier, where the membrane is exposed and packed with voltage-gated Na⁺ channels
  • The local current from an active node spreads passively and rapidly through the internode (under the myelin) to depolarize the next node
  • At the next node, if threshold is reached, a new action potential is generated, "recharging" the signal
  • The action potential appears to jump from node to node

Advantages of saltatory conduction:

  1. Speed: Conduction velocity is 10-100× faster than in unmyelinated axons of the same diameter (up to 120 m/s vs ~1 m/s)
  2. Energy efficiency: Only the nodes (~0.2% of membrane area in heavily myelinated axons) need to pump ions. The Na⁺/K⁺ pump works far less, conserving ATP
  3. Space efficiency: Myelinated axons can be thinner while maintaining high speed — allowing more axons in the same volume (critical in the spinal cord and optic nerve)

Draw and label: A myelinated axon with 4-5 nodes:

  • At each node: show Na⁺ influx (inward arrows) and the AP being regenerated
  • Between nodes: show the passive current spread under the myelin (dashed arrows)

Paper 1 Tip: Multiple sclerosis (MS) is an autoimmune disease where the immune system attacks myelin in the CNS (oligodendrocytes are targeted). This slows or blocks conduction, causing symptoms like muscle weakness, vision problems, and coordination issues. MS is a classic IB example linking myelin structure to function.

5. Synaptic Transmission: The Neuron-to-Neuron Handoff

The action potential is electrical, but communication between neurons is chemical (with rare exceptions — electrical synapses via gap junctions). The synapse converts an electrical signal into a chemical signal and back again.

5.1 The Synapse: Key Structures

Draw and label: A detailed diagram of a chemical synapse:

Presynaptic terminal (bouton):

  • Synaptic vesicles containing neurotransmitter (small clear circles)
  • Mitochondria (for ATP to power vesicle cycling and ion pumps)
  • Voltage-gated Ca²⁺ channels in the membrane (label these clearly)

Synaptic cleft:

  • The ~20-30 nm gap between pre- and postsynaptic membranes
  • Contains extracellular fluid and sometimes degradative enzymes (e.g., acetylcholinesterase)

Postsynaptic membrane:

  • Contains ligand-gated ion channels (also called ionotropic receptors)
  • These are receptor proteins that open an ion channel when neurotransmitter binds
  • May also contain G-protein-coupled receptors (metabotropic) for slower, modulatory effects

5.2 The Sequence of Synaptic Transmission

This is a step-by-step process that IB expects you to know in precise detail:

Step 1 — Action Potential Arrives:

The action potential propagates down the axon and depolarizes the presynaptic terminal.

Step 2 — Ca²⁺ Influx:

Depolarization opens voltage-gated Ca²⁺ channels in the presynaptic membrane. Ca²⁺ rushes into the terminal, driven by both its concentration gradient ([Ca²⁺] outside ~1-2 mM, inside ~100 nM) and the electrical gradient.

Step 3 — Vesicle Fusion (Exocytosis):

The rise in intracellular [Ca²⁺] triggers synaptic vesicles to fuse with the presynaptic membrane. This involves SNARE proteins (synaptobrevin on vesicles, syntaxin and SNAP-25 on the membrane) that bring the vesicle and membrane together. Ca²⁺ binds to synaptotagmin, which triggers the final fusion step.

Step 4 — Neurotransmitter Release:

The vesicle contents (neurotransmitter) are released into the synaptic cleft by exocytosis. A single vesicle releases a "quantum" of neurotransmitter (typically thousands of molecules).

Step 5 — Diffusion Across the Cleft:

Neurotransmitter molecules diffuse rapidly across the narrow synaptic cleft (~20-30 nm — diffusion is fast over such short distances, typically <1 ms).

Step 6 — Binding to Postsynaptic Receptors:

Neurotransmitter binds to specific ligand-gated ion channels on the postsynaptic membrane. This binding is reversible and specific — like a key fitting a lock. The channel opens, allowing specific ions to flow through.

Step 7 — Postsynaptic Potential:

The ion flow through the opened channels changes the postsynaptic membrane potential:

  • If Na⁺ channels open (e.g., ACh at nicotinic receptors, glutamate at AMPA receptors): Na⁺ enters → depolarization → EPSP (Excitatory Postsynaptic Potential)
  • If Cl⁻ channels open (e.g., GABA at GABA_A receptors): Cl⁻ enters or K⁺ leaves → hyperpolarization → IPSP (Inhibitory Postsynaptic Potential)

Step 8 — Neurotransmitter Removal:

The signal must be terminated! Otherwise, the postsynaptic cell would remain stimulated indefinitely. Three mechanisms:

  1. Enzymatic degradation: e.g., acetylcholinesterase breaks ACh → acetate + choline in the cleft
  2. Reuptake: Neurotransmitter is pumped back into the presynaptic terminal by transporter proteins (e.g., serotonin reuptake, dopamine reuptake, norepinephrine reuptake)
  3. Diffusion: Neurotransmitter simply diffuses away from the synapse

Worked Example: The Cholinergic Synapse

Scenario: Trace the transmission at a neuromuscular junction (a specialized synapse between a motor neuron and a skeletal muscle fiber) from AP arrival to muscle contraction.

Step-by-step:

  1. Motor neuron action potential arrives at the terminal
  2. Voltage-gated Ca²⁺ channels open → Ca²⁺ enters the terminal
  3. Synaptic vesicles containing acetylcholine (ACh) fuse with the membrane → ACh released into the cleft
  4. ACh diffuses across (~50 nm at NMJ) and binds to nicotinic ACh receptors on the muscle fiber membrane (the motor end plate)
  5. Nicotinic receptors are ligand-gated Na⁺ channels → Na⁺ enters → depolarization (end-plate potential)
  6. This depolarization always reaches threshold (the NMJ is a very strong synapse — each AP releases enough ACh to generate ~200 quanta, far exceeding threshold)
  7. Voltage-gated Na⁺ channels in the surrounding muscle membrane open → muscle action potential → muscle contraction
  8. Acetylcholinesterase in the cleft rapidly degrades ACh → signal terminated in ~1 ms

Why this is important: This synapse is the target of many toxins (botulinum toxin blocks ACh release → paralysis; nerve agents like sarin inhibit acetylcholinesterase → ACh accumulates → overstimulation → paralysis). Understanding the mechanism explains the toxicity.

7. Neonicotinoid Pesticides — A Real-World Application

This is a high-yield IB topic that connects synaptic biology to environmental science.

7.1 Mechanism of Action

Neonicotinoids (e.g., imidacloprid, clothianidin) are synthetic insecticides structurally similar to nicotine.

  • They bind irreversibly (or with very high affinity) to nicotinic acetylcholine receptors (nAChRs) in the insect CNS
  • Unlike ACh, they are not broken down by acetylcholinesterase
  • This causes persistent activation of the postsynaptic neuron → constant depolarization
  • The overstimulation leads to paralysis, tremors, and death

7.2 Why Are Bees Particularly Vulnerable?

  • Bees have nAChRs with high affinity for neonicotinoids
  • Neonicotinoids are systemic pesticides — they are absorbed by plants and present in pollen, nectar, and even guttation droplets
  • Sub-lethal doses impair:
  • Navigation and homing: Bees fail to return to the hive
  • Learning and memory: Impairs foraging efficiency
  • Reproduction: Reduced queen survival and colony growth
  • The combination of lethal and sub-lethal effects contributes to colony collapse disorder (CCD)

7.3 Selectivity: Insects vs. Mammals

Neonicotinoids are more toxic to insects than mammals because:

  • Insect nAChRs have higher binding affinity for neonicotinoids
  • Mammalian nAChRs have structural differences that reduce binding
  • However, they are not entirely safe — concerns exist about effects on human neurodevelopment

8. Common Misconceptions

Misconception Reality
"The Na⁺/K⁺ pump directly creates the resting potential." The pump contributes only ~−5 to −10 mV directly. The majority of the resting potential comes from K⁺ efflux through leak channels, driven by the concentration gradient the pump established.
"During the action potential, Na⁺ and K⁺ swap places." They don't swap. Na⁺ enters during the rising phase; K⁺ exits during the falling phase. These are separate fluxes through different channels at different times.
"A stronger stimulus produces a bigger action potential." No. Action potentials are all-or-none. Stronger stimuli produce higher frequency** of APs, not larger amplitude.
"Myelin speeds up the action potential." Partial truth. Myelin enables saltatory conduction, which is faster than continuous conduction. Myelin itself doesn't "speed up" the AP — it changes the mode of propagation.
"All neurotransmitters are excitatory." No. GABA and glycine are inhibitory (hyperpolarize). Even ACh can be inhibitory in the heart (muscarinic receptors → K⁺ channels open → hyperpolarization).
"Neonicotinoids kill insects by blocking ACh receptors." They kill by overstimulating ACh receptors (agonists, not antagonists). The persistent activation causes paralysis and death.
"The synaptic cleft is huge." It's only ~20-30 nm. Diffusion is extremely rapid across this tiny distance.

9. Practice Problems

Problem 1 (Paper 1 Style — Multiple Choice)

During the rising phase of the action potential:

A. Voltage-gated K⁺ channels open and K⁺ leaves the cell.

B. Voltage-gated Na⁺ channels open and Na⁺ enters the cell.

C. The Na⁺/K⁺ pump reverses direction.

D. Voltage-gated Cl⁻ channels open and Cl⁻ enters the cell.

Answer: B. The rising phase is driven by the opening of voltage-gated Na⁺ channels, allowing Na⁺ to rush into the cell down its electrochemical gradient. K⁺ channels open later, during repolarization.

Problem 2 (Paper 1 Style — Multiple Choice)

Which statement correctly describes saltatory conduction?

A. The action potential is continuously regenerated along the entire length of the axon.

B. Myelin increases membrane capacitance, slowing the action potential.

C. The action potential jumps from node to node, where voltage-gated Na⁺ channels are concentrated.

D. Saltatory conduction occurs only in unmyelinated neurons.

Answer: C. Saltatory conduction is the "jumping" of the action potential between nodes of Ranvier in myelinated axons. The nodes have high densities of voltage-gated Na⁺ channels that regenerate the AP.

Problem 3 (Paper 2 Style — Long Answer)

Describe the sequence of events in synaptic transmission, from the arrival of an action potential at the presynaptic terminal to the generation of a postsynaptic potential. [6 marks]

Model Answer:

  1. The action potential depolarizes the presynaptic terminal, causing voltage-gated Ca²⁺ channels to open [1 mark]
  2. Ca²⁺ ions flow into the terminal down their electrochemical gradient (high outside, low inside, inside negative) [1 mark]
  3. The rise in intracellular Ca²⁺ triggers synaptic vesicles containing neurotransmitter to fuse with the presynaptic membrane via SNARE proteins [1 mark]
  4. Neurotransmitter is released into the synaptic cleft by exocytosis and diffuses across the narrow gap (~20-30 nm) [1 mark]
  5. Neurotransmitter binds to specific ligand-gated ion channels (receptors) on the postsynaptic membrane [1 mark]
  6. The ion channels open, allowing specific ions to flow through. For excitatory synapses, Na⁺ enters causing depolarization (EPSP). For inhibitory synapses, Cl⁻ enters or K⁺ exits causing hyperpolarization (IPSP) [1 mark]

[6 marks total — one for each clearly stated step with correct terminology]

(c) Predict how adding acetylcholinesterase to the preparation would affect the results. [1 mark]

Answers:

(a) Both 10 nM and 50 nM imidacloprid increase AP frequency compared to control, with the effect being concentration-dependent (50 nM produces a greater increase than 10 nM). The effect also increases over time (AP frequency at 10 min > at 1 min at both concentrations). [2 marks]

(b) At low concentrations, imidacloprid binds to some nicotinic ACh receptors, causing partial depolarization and increased excitability (higher AP frequency). At high concentrations (100 nM+), it binds to a large proportion of nAChRs. Because neonicotinoids are not degraded by acetylcholinesterase, they cause persistent receptor activation. This leads to sustained depolarization of the postsynaptic neuron. Voltage-gated Na⁺ channels enter the inactivated state and cannot recover, so the neuron becomes inexcitable and goes silent — a phenomenon called depolarization block. [3 marks]

(c) Acetylcholinesterase would have no effect. Neonicotinoids are not substrates for acetylcholinesterase — that's precisely why they're toxic. Adding the enzyme would not break down imidacloprid. [1 mark]

Problem 5 (Paper 2 Style — Application)

A patient has an autoimmune disease that destroys voltage-gated Ca²⁺ channels at the presynaptic terminals of motor neurons. Predict the effects on neuromuscular transmission and explain the symptoms the patient would experience. [4 marks]

Answer:

Without voltage-gated Ca²⁺ channels at the presynaptic terminal:

  1. When the action potential arrives at the terminal, the depolarization cannot trigger Ca²⁺ influx [1 mark]
  2. Without Ca²⁺ entry, synaptic vesicles cannot fuse with the presynaptic membrane [1 mark]
  3. Acetylcholine is not released into the synaptic cleft, so no neurotransmitter binds to postsynaptic receptors on the muscle fiber [1 mark]
  4. No end-plate potential is generated → no muscle action potential → no muscle contraction

The patient would experience muscle weakness or flaccid paralysis (muscles cannot contract). This is similar to the effect of botulinum toxin, which also prevents ACh release (though by a different mechanism — botulinum cleaves SNARE proteins). Breathing could be fatal if respiratory muscles are affected. [1 mark]

10. Key Terms Quick Reference

Term Definition
Resting potential −70 mV across neuron membrane at rest; inside negative relative to outside
Na⁺/K⁺ pump Active transporter: 3 Na⁺ out, 2 K⁺ in per ATP — establishes ion gradients
Threshold ~−55 mV — critical voltage at which sufficient Na⁺ channels open to trigger AP
Depolarization Membrane potential becomes less negative (moves toward zero or positive)
Repolarization Return of membrane potential toward resting after AP peak
Hyperpolarization Membrane potential more negative than resting (undershoot)
Absolute refractory period Period when no stimulus can trigger AP (Na⁺ channels inactivated)
Relative refractory period Period when only stronger-than-normal stimulus can trigger AP (K⁺ channels still open)
Saltaory conduction AP jumps node-to-node in myelinated axons; fast and energy-efficient
Myelin Insulating sheath around axon; Schwann cells (PNS), oligodendrocytes (CNS)
Node of Ranvier Gap in myelin where voltage-gated Na⁺ channels are concentrated
Synaptic vesicle Membrane-bound sac containing neurotransmitter in presynaptic terminal
Exocytosis Fusion of vesicle with membrane, releasing contents to extracellular space
Ligand-gated ion channel Postsynaptic receptor that opens an ion channel when NT binds
EPSP Excitatory Postsynaptic Potential — depolarization bringing cell closer to threshold
IPSP Inhibitory Postsynaptic Potential — hyperpolarization moving cell further from threshold
Neurotransmitter Chemical released at synapse that transmits signal to next cell
Acetylcholinesterase Enzyme in synaptic cleft that breaks down ACh to terminate signal
Neonicotinoid Synthetic pesticide; irreversible agonist of insect nAChRs → overstimulation → paralysis

This lesson covers the structural and functional foundations of neural signaling. The action potential — with its precisely timed opening and closing of ion channels — is one of the most elegant examples of molecular machinery in biology. Master it, and you'll have a powerful framework for understanding everything from reflexes to consciousness.
