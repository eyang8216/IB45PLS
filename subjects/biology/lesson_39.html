1. The Problem: How Does DNA Replicate?

When Watson and Crick published the double helix structure in 1953, they ended their paper with one of the greatest understatements in science: "It has not escaped our notice that the specific pairing we have postulated immediately suggests a possible copying mechanism for the genetic material."

The question: When DNA replicates, what happens to the original strands? Three hypotheses emerged:

Draw and label: Three diagrams showing the three models:

  1. Conservative: The original double helix stays intact. An entirely new double helix is made. After replication: one "old-old" molecule and one "new-new" molecule.
  2. Semiconservative: Each strand separates and serves as a template. Each new double helix contains one original strand and one newly synthesised strand. After replication: two "old-new" hybrid molecules.
  3. Dispersive: The original strands are fragmented, and each new molecule is a patchwork of old and new DNA segments randomly interspersed.

Which one was correct? Enter Meselson and Stahl, 1958.

2. The Meselson-Stahl Experiment: Proving Semiconservative Replication

This is arguably the most beautiful experiment in molecular biology, and IB loves it. Know every detail.

2.1 The Principle: Density Distinguishes Old from New

Nitrogen exists in two stable isotopes:

  • ¹⁴N: The common, "light" isotope (99.6% of natural nitrogen).
  • ¹⁵N: The rare, "heavy" isotope (0.4%).

DNA contains nitrogen (in the nitrogenous bases: A, T, C, G). If you grow bacteria in a medium with ¹⁵N (as NH₄Cl), their DNA will be "heavy." If you grow them in ¹⁴N, their DNA will be "light."

CsCl density gradient centrifugation: When a caesium chloride (CsCl) solution is spun at very high speed (ultracentrifugation, ~50,000 rpm), Cs⁺ ions are so heavy that they form a density gradient — denser at the bottom, lighter at the top. DNA molecules float to the position in the tube where their density matches the surrounding CsCl density. Heavy DNA sinks lower; light DNA floats higher.

Analogy: Imagine dropping different types of wood into a tank where the water gets saltier (denser) as you go deeper. Balsa wood floats near the top; oak sinks deeper. DNA molecules find their "density layer" the same way.

2.2 The Experiment: Step by Step

Step 1: Grow bacteria in ¹⁵N medium for many generations.

  • All DNA is heavy (¹⁵N-¹⁵N).
  • Centrifuge → one band low in the tube (heavy).

Step 2: Transfer bacteria to ¹⁴N medium (the "pulse").

  • Allow exactly ONE round of DNA replication (one generation).
  • Centrifuge → one band at intermediate position (between heavy and light).

This immediately rules out the conservative model. If replication were conservative, you'd see TWO bands: one heavy (original) and one light (new). Instead, you see one intermediate band — meaning each DNA molecule is a hybrid (one heavy strand, one light strand).

But does it rule out dispersive? Not yet. In the dispersive model, after one generation, each molecule would be half-heavy, half-light — also intermediate.

Step 3: Allow a SECOND round of replication in ¹⁴N medium.

  • Centrifuge → TWO bands: one at light position, one at intermediate position.

This is the killer. Let's compare:

Model Expected after 2 generations in ¹⁴N
Semiconservative One light band (¹⁴N-¹⁴N) + one intermediate band (¹⁴N-¹⁵N). Ratio 1:1. ✅
Dispersive One band at ¼-heavy position, but still a single band (all molecules are mixtures of ¼ heavy and ¾ light). ❌
Conservative Already ruled out in Step 2. ❌

The result: Two bands (one light, one intermediate) → semiconservative replication confirmed.

Draw and label: A diagram showing:

  • Test tubes after each generation, with bands at heavy, intermediate, and light positions.
  • Below each tube, a molecular diagram of the DNA: Generation 0 = two red strands (heavy). Generation 1 = one red + one blue strand (intermediate). Generation 2 = two all-blue molecules (light) + two red-blue molecules (intermediate).

Semiconservative replication: Each daughter DNA molecule consists of one strand from the parent molecule and one newly synthesised complementary strand. The parental strands are conserved (not degraded) but separated.

Paper 1 classic: "What result from Meselson and Stahl's experiment ruled out the dispersive model?" Answer: After two generations in ¹⁴N, two bands (light and intermediate) were observed. The dispersive model predicts a single band of continuously changing density.

3. The Replication Fork: A Molecular Machine

DNA replication is carried out by a multi-enzyme complex called the replisome. Let's tour each enzyme's job at the replication fork.

Draw and label: A detailed diagram of a replication fork. Show:

  • The parental DNA double helix being unwound at the fork.
  • Helicase at the fork point.
  • SSBs coating the separated strands.
  • Gyrase ahead of the fork, relieving supercoils.
  • Primase laying down RNA primers.
  • DNA Polymerase III on both strands, with arrows showing synthesis direction (5'→3' on both, but leading is continuous toward fork, lagging is fragmented away from fork).
  • Okazaki fragments on the lagging strand.
  • DNA Polymerase I replacing primers.
  • DNA Ligase sealing nicks.

3.1 Helicase: The Unzipper

Helicase binds at the origin of replication and uses energy from ATP hydrolysis to break the hydrogen bonds between complementary base pairs. It literally unzips the double helix, creating a replication fork — a Y-shaped region where the two strands are separated.

Analogy: Helicase is like a zipper pull moving along a jacket. The "teeth" are hydrogen bonds between base pairs.

3.2 Single-Strand Binding Proteins (SSBs): The Stabilisers

Once separated, the two single-stranded DNA (ssDNA) templates want to snap back together (reanneal) because the complementary bases want to hydrogen-bond. SSBs coat the exposed ssDNA, keeping the strands separated and protecting them from degradation by nucleases.

Analogy: SSBs are like spacers you wedge between pages of a book to keep them apart while you're copying them.

3.3 DNA Gyrase (Topoisomerase): The Tension Reliever

As helicase unwinds the DNA ahead of the fork, the still-helical DNA ahead becomes overwound — it's like twisting a rubber band until it forms supercoils. If not relieved, this tension would stop replication. DNA gyrase (a type II topoisomerase) cuts both strands, allows them to unwind (relieving the tension), and reseals them. This prevents the entire chromosome from tangling.

Analogy: Gyrase is like untwisting a garden hose that's gotten kinked — you cut it, let it untwist, and reconnect it.

Paper 1: "Which enzyme relieves supercoiling tension ahead of the replication fork?" → DNA gyrase (or topoisomerase). Note: in prokaryotes, the specific enzyme is DNA gyrase; in eukaryotes, the general term topoisomerase is used. Both are acceptable unless IB specifies.

3.4 Primase: The Starter

DNA polymerases cannot start a new strand from nothing — they can only add nucleotides to an existing 3'-OH group. Enter primase, which is actually an RNA polymerase. It synthesises a short RNA primer (~10 nucleotides long) complementary to the template. This provides the free 3'-OH that DNA polymerase needs to start.

Analogy: Primase is like laying down a starter track so the main train (DNA polymerase) can begin moving.

3.5 DNA Polymerase III: The Main Builder

DNA Polymerase III is the workhorse of replication. It's a large, multi-subunit enzyme with a sliding clamp that keeps it attached to the DNA. Key properties:

  • Synthesises 5' → 3': It adds deoxyribonucleoside triphosphates (dNTPs: dATP, dTTP, dGTP, dCTP) to the free 3'-OH of the growing strand. The energy comes from cleaving two phosphate groups (pyrophosphate) from the dNTP.
  • Requires a primer: Cannot initiate de novo (unlike primase).
  • Reads template 3' → 5': The template strand is read in the 3' → 5' direction so that the new strand grows 5' → 3'.
  • Proofreading (3' → 5' exonuclease activity): If an incorrect base is inserted, DNA Pol III can detect the mismatch, reverse direction, remove the wrong nucleotide (using its 3'→5' exonuclease), and try again. This reduces the error rate from ~10⁻⁵ to ~10⁻⁹ per base pair.

3.6 Leading vs Lagging Strand

Because DNA polymerase can only synthesise 5' → 3', and the two template strands run antiparallel, the two strands are synthesised differently:

Leading Strand (Continuous):

  • Template runs 3' → 5' into the fork.
  • DNA Pol III synthesises continuously toward the replication fork.
  • Only one RNA primer is needed.
  • Synthesis is smooth and fast.

Lagging Strand (Discontinuous):

  • Template runs 5' → 3' into the fork.
  • DNA Pol III must synthesise away from the fork, in short bursts.
  • This creates Okazaki fragments (~1000-2000 nucleotides in prokaryotes, ~100-200 in eukaryotes).
  • Each fragment needs its own RNA primer.
  • Synthesis is stuttery and slower.

Draw and label: A close-up of the lagging strand showing multiple Okazaki fragments, each starting with an RNA primer (in a different colour). Arrows showing Pol III synthesising each fragment away from the fork.

3.7 DNA Polymerase I: The Cleaner

Once the new DNA is synthesised, the RNA primers are still embedded in it. DNA Polymerase I removes the RNA primers (using its 5'→3' exonuclease activity) and replaces them with DNA nucleotides. Note: this is a different enzyme from Pol III with a different exonuclease direction (5'→3' vs 3'→5').

3.8 DNA Ligase: The Sealer

After Pol I replaces the primers, there are nicks (breaks in the sugar-phosphate backbone) between adjacent Okazaki fragments and at the primer replacement sites. DNA Ligase seals these nicks by forming phosphodiester bonds, creating a continuous DNA strand.

Analogy: If Pol III lays the bricks, Pol I replaces temporary scaffolding with bricks, and Ligase is the mortar that glues everything together.

Summary: Enzymes at the Replication Fork

Enzyme Function Direction/Notes
Helicase Unwinds double helix; breaks H-bonds ATP-dependent
SSBs Stabilise ssDNA; prevent reannealing Coat exposed strands
DNA Gyrase / Topoisomerase Relieves supercoiling ahead of fork Cuts and reseals DNA
Primase Synthesises RNA primer (~10 nt) RNA polymerase
DNA Polymerase III Main DNA synthesis; proofreads 5'→3' synthesis; 3'→5' exonuclease

4. PCR: Polymerase Chain Reaction

PCR is DNA replication in a test tube. It allows you to amplify a specific DNA sequence from a tiny starting amount to billions of copies.

4.1 The Three-Step Cycle

Each cycle consists of three temperature steps, repeated 25-35 times:

Step 1: Denaturation (~95°C, 30 seconds)

  • Heat breaks hydrogen bonds between complementary strands.
  • Double-stranded DNA separates into two single strands.
  • This replaces the need for helicase.

Step 2: Annealing (~50-65°C, 30 seconds)

  • Temperature is lowered to allow primers (short, synthetic DNA oligonucleotides ~18-25 bases) to bind (anneal) to complementary sequences flanking the target region.
  • Two primers: a forward primer (binds to one strand) and a reverse primer (binds to the other strand).
  • This defines the boundaries of the amplified region.

Step 3: Extension (~72°C, 1-2 minutes)

  • Taq polymerase (from the thermophilic bacterium Thermus aquaticus, which lives in hot springs) synthesises new DNA from the 3' end of each primer.
  • Taq is thermostable — it survives the 95°C denaturation step. (E. coli DNA polymerase would be destroyed at this temperature.)
  • Extension at 72°C — Taq's optimal temperature.

4.2 Exponential Amplification

Each cycle doubles the number of DNA molecules in the target region:

$$ \text{Copies} = 2^n $$

where $$n$$ is the number of cycles. After 30 cycles: $$2^{30} \approx 1.07 \times 10^9$$ copies — over a billion.

Draw and label: A diagram showing three cycles of PCR. Cycle 1: 1 dsDNA → 2 dsDNA. Cycle 2: 2 → 4. Cycle 3: 4 → 8. Use different colours for original template vs newly synthesised strands.

PCR Calculation

A forensic sample contains approximately 50 copies of a target DNA sequence. The analyst runs PCR for 28 cycles.

(a) Assuming 100% efficiency, how many copies will be produced?

(b) If the sample is contaminated with 5 copies of a different person's DNA (which also amplifies), what is the ratio of target:contaminant after 28 cycles?

Answers:

(a) $$50 \times 2^{28} = 50 \times 268{,}435{,}456 \approx \mathbf{1.34 \times 10^{10}}$$ copies.

(b) Both amplify by the same factor ($$2^{28}$$). Ratio remains 50:5 = 10:1. PCR amplifies everything equally — it cannot selectively amplify only the target DNA unless the primers are specific to the target.

Paper 2: "Explain why Taq polymerase is used in PCR rather than DNA polymerase from E. coli." → Taq is thermostable; it does not denature at 95°C (the denaturation step). E. coli DNA polymerase would be irreversibly denatured at this temperature, requiring fresh enzyme to be added after each cycle (which is how early PCR was done — expensive and inefficient).

5. Gel Electrophoresis

Once you've amplified DNA (or cut it with restriction enzymes), you need to separate and visualise the fragments. This is the job of gel electrophoresis.

5.1 How It Works

  1. Prepare agarose gel: Agarose (a polysaccharide from seaweed) is poured into a mould and allowed to set, forming a porous matrix. The pore size depends on agarose concentration (higher % = smaller pores = better for separating small fragments).
  1. Load samples: DNA samples mixed with loading dye are pipetted into wells at one end of the gel. A DNA ladder (mixture of fragments of known sizes) is loaded in one well as a reference.
  1. Apply electric field: The gel is placed in a buffer-filled tank with electrodes. The wells are at the negative (cathode) end. DNA is negatively charged (due to phosphate groups in the backbone), so it migrates toward the positive (anode) end.
  1. Separation by size: Smaller fragments move through the gel pores more easily and travel faster/further. Larger fragments are slowed by the gel matrix. After a set time, fragments are separated into bands, each representing a population of DNA molecules of the same size.
  1. Visualisation: The gel is stained with a fluorescent dye (e.g., ethidium bromide or SYBR Safe) that binds to DNA and fluoresces under UV light. Bands appear as glowing lines.

Draw and label: A gel electrophoresis diagram. Show:

  • Wells at the top (negative end).
  • A ladder lane with bands at known sizes (label: "100 bp, 200 bp, 300 bp, 500 bp, 1000 bp").
  • Sample lanes with bands at various positions.
  • An arrow indicating direction of DNA migration (→ toward +).
  • Labels: "Smaller fragments (migrate further)," "Larger fragments (migrate less)."

5.2 Interpreting a Gel

The distance migrated is inversely proportional to the log of the fragment size. By comparing to the ladder, you can estimate the size of unknown fragments.

Paper 1: "Why does DNA move toward the positive electrode?" → DNA has a negative charge due to its phosphate backbone. Opposite charges attract. "Why do smaller fragments move further?" → The agarose gel acts as a molecular sieve; smaller fragments navigate through the pores more easily.

6. DNA Sequencing: Reading the Code

6.1 Sanger (Dideoxy) Sequencing

Developed by Frederick Sanger in 1977 (his second Nobel Prize), this method uses modified nucleotides to determine DNA sequence.

The key trick: dideoxynucleotides (ddNTPs)

Normal dNTPs have a hydroxyl (-OH) group on the 3' carbon of the deoxyribose sugar. This 3'-OH is essential for forming the phosphodiester bond to the next nucleotide.

Dideoxynucleotides (ddNTPs) lack this 3'-OH (they have -H instead). When DNA polymerase incorporates a ddNTP, chain termination occurs — no further nucleotides can be added because there's no 3'-OH to attack the next dNTP.

The procedure:

  1. Four separate reactions, each containing:
  • Template DNA (single-stranded)
  • Primer
  • DNA polymerase
  • All four normal dNTPs
  • A small amount of one ddNTP (ddATP in tube A, ddTTP in tube T, etc.)
  • Each ddNTP is labelled with a different fluorescent dye
  1. In each tube, DNA polymerase synthesises new strands. Occasionally (randomly), a ddNTP is incorporated instead of a dNTP, terminating that particular strand.
  1. The result in each tube: a set of fragments of every possible length, each ending in the specific ddNTP for that tube.
  1. Separation: All four reactions are run in adjacent lanes on a capillary gel (modern version) or polyacrylamide gel (classic). Fragments are separated by size (single-nucleotide resolution).
  1. Reading the sequence: The shortest fragment (terminated at position 1) migrates fastest. Read the bands from bottom to top → this gives the sequence of the synthesised strand (5'→3'). The template strand is complementary.

Analogy: Imagine copying a sentence but occasionally your pen runs out of ink at random positions. By collecting all the partial copies and noting which letter the pen died on, you can reconstruct the entire sentence.

Draw and label: A Sanger sequencing gel with four lanes (A, T, C, G). Show bands at each position. Below the gel, write the sequence read from bottom to top. Label: "ddATP terminates at A positions," etc.

6.2 Next-Generation Sequencing (NGS) — Overview

Sanger sequencing is accurate but can only sequence one fragment at a time. Next-generation sequencing (also called massively parallel sequencing) can sequence millions of fragments simultaneously.

Key features:

  • DNA is fragmented and each fragment is attached to a solid surface.
  • Each fragment is amplified in place (bridge amplification) to create a cluster of identical DNA molecules.
  • Sequencing-by-synthesis: as each nucleotide is added to the growing strand, a fluorescent signal is detected. Different colours for A, T, C, G.
  • A camera records the colour at each cluster after each cycle, building up the sequence for millions of clusters in parallel.

Advantages: Much faster and cheaper per base than Sanger. Enables whole-genome sequencing, metagenomics, and transcriptomics (RNA-Seq).

Feature Sanger Sequencing Next-Generation Sequencing
Throughput One fragment at a time Millions in parallel
Read length Long (800-1000 bp) Short (50-300 bp, but improving)
Accuracy per read Very high Good (but higher error rate)

7. Telomeres and the End-Replication Problem

7.1 What Are Telomeres?

Telomeres are repetitive DNA sequences at the ends of linear chromosomes. In humans, the repeat is TTAGGG, repeated thousands of times. Telomeres don't code for proteins — they are protective caps.

Functions of telomeres:

  • Protect chromosome ends from being recognised as double-strand breaks (which would trigger DNA repair mechanisms that fuse chromosomes).
  • Prevent loss of coding genes during replication (see below).
  • Limit the number of cell divisions (cellular senescence).

7.2 The End-Replication Problem

Linear chromosomes have a problem that circular bacterial chromosomes don't: the very ends cannot be fully replicated.

Here's why, specifically on the lagging strand:

  1. The lagging strand is synthesised as Okazaki fragments, each needing an RNA primer.
  2. After the last Okazaki fragment is made, there is an RNA primer at the very end.
  3. DNA Pol I removes this terminal primer, but it cannot replace it with DNA because there's no 3'-OH upstream for it to extend from. (On internal Okazaki fragments, the adjacent fragment provides the 3'-OH for Pol I and ligase to complete the job.)
  4. Result: after each round of replication, the daughter strand is slightly shorter at the 5' end. The chromosome loses ~50-200 base pairs per replication.

Analogy: Imagine painting a floor starting from the wall, but you can only paint by standing on an unpainted spot. When you reach the far wall, you can't paint the last strip because you'd have to stand on already-painted floor. Each time you repaint, the unpainted strip gets wider.

Draw and label: A diagram showing a chromosome end before and after replication. Before: long telomere (TTAGGG repeats). After one round: the lagging strand daughter molecule is shorter at the 5' end. After multiple rounds: telomere progressively shortens. When telomere is gone, coding genes are lost → cell enters senescence.

7.3 Telomerase: The Solution (in Some Cells)

Telomerase is a reverse transcriptase — it makes DNA from an RNA template. It carries its own built-in RNA molecule (TERC — telomerase RNA component) that serves as a template for synthesising telomeric DNA repeats.

Mechanism:

  1. Telomerase binds to the 3' overhang of the telomere (the G-rich strand is longer than the C-rich strand).
  2. Using its internal RNA template (complementary to TTAGGG), telomerase extends the 3' end by adding TTAGGG repeats.
  3. Telomerase translocates (moves) and repeats — adding many repeats.
  4. Now that the 3' strand is extended, primase can lay down an RNA primer on the extended region, and DNA polymerase can fill in the complementary C-rich strand. The end-replication problem is solved.

Where is telomerase active?

  • Germ cells (sperm, eggs) — so offspring inherit full-length telomeres.
  • Stem cells — to maintain their capacity for many divisions.
  • Cancer cells (~85-90% of cancers have upregulated telomerase) — this gives them unlimited replicative potential (immortality). Without telomerase, cancer cells would eventually senesce after ~50-70 divisions (the Hayflick limit).
  • NOT active in most somatic cells — which is why they have a limited lifespan.

7.4 Telomere Theory of Aging

As somatic cells divide throughout life, their telomeres shorten. When telomeres become critically short, the cell recognises this as DNA damage and enters senescence (irreversible cell cycle arrest) or apoptosis (programmed cell death).

This progressive loss of functional cells in tissues contributes to aging. Evidence:

  • Telomere length correlates with lifespan across species.
  • Individuals with shorter telomeres have higher mortality from age-related diseases.
  • Progeria (premature aging syndromes) often involve defective telomere maintenance.
  • Lifestyle factors (chronic stress, smoking, poor diet, lack of exercise) are associated with accelerated telomere shortening.

However, telomere shortening is one factor among many in aging (others include oxidative damage, mitochondrial decline, and epigenetic changes).

Hayflick limit: The number of times a normal somatic cell can divide before telomere shortening causes senescence (~50-70 divisions for human fibroblasts).

Common Misconceptions

Misconception Reality
"DNA polymerase can start a new strand from scratch." DNA polymerase requires a primer with a free 3'-OH. Only primase (RNA polymerase) can start de novo.
"The leading and lagging strands use different DNA polymerases." Both use DNA Pol III for main synthesis and DNA Pol I for primer replacement. The difference is in how synthesis proceeds (continuous vs fragmented), not the enzyme.
"Okazaki fragments are only on the lagging strand in eukaryotes." Okazaki fragments exist on the lagging strand in ALL organisms — both prokaryotes and eukaryotes. They're just shorter in eukaryotes.
"PCR uses DNA helicase to separate strands." PCR uses heat (95°C) to denature DNA, avoiding the need for helicase.
"The Meselson-Stahl experiment directly showed semiconservative replication after one generation." After one generation, the intermediate band could be explained by BOTH semiconservative and dispersive models. It took two generations to distinguish them.
"Telomerase elongates both strands of the telomere." Telomerase extends only the 3' G-rich strand. The complementary C-rich strand is then filled in by conventional DNA replication machinery.
"Gel electrophoresis separates DNA by charge." All DNA fragments have the same charge-to-mass ratio. Separation is by size — smaller fragments navigate the gel pores faster.

Answers

Answer 1

(a) 0 generations: One band at the heavy (¹⁵N) position. 1 generation: One band at intermediate position (hybrid ¹⁵N-¹⁴N). 2 generations: Two bands — one at the light position and one at the intermediate position (1:1 ratio).

(b) The dispersive model predicts that DNA is fragmented and randomly reassorted. After two generations in ¹⁴N, all molecules would be ¼ heavy and ¾ light — producing a single band at ¼-heavy position. Meselson and Stahl observed two distinct bands, which can only be explained by the semiconservative model where some molecules are entirely light and others are hybrid.

(c) 3 generations: Semi-conservative replication means hybrid molecules (¹⁵N-¹⁴N) separate and each strand serves as a template. The ¹⁵N strand gets a new ¹⁴N complement → hybrid. The ¹⁴N strand gets a new ¹⁴N complement → light. Light molecules (¹⁴N-¹⁴N) replicate to produce two light molecules each. Result: 3 light : 1 intermediate (75% light, 25% intermediate). The intermediate band gets progressively fainter with each generation.

Answer 2

(a) Primase inactivated → no RNA primers. DNA polymerase III cannot initiate synthesis. Replication cannot begin. No new DNA synthesised.

(b) DNA ligase inhibited → nicks not sealed. Okazaki fragments remain unjoined on the lagging strand. The new strand is fragmented and non-functional.

(c) Helicase denatured → DNA not unwound. The replication fork cannot form. Parental strands remain hydrogen-bonded. No replication.

(d) SSBs absent → ssDNA reanneals. The separated template strands immediately re-form hydrogen bonds. DNA polymerase cannot access the template. Replication stalls.

(e) DNA gyrase non-functional → supercoiling builds up. Torsional stress ahead of the fork increases until the DNA physically cannot unwind further. Replication arrests. The chromosome may break.

Answer 3

(a) Components: template DNA (crime scene sample), forward and reverse primers specific to the 400 bp target, Taq polymerase, dNTPs (dATP, dTTP, dGTP, dCTP), buffer with Mg²⁺, thermal cycler.

(b) Primers define the boundaries of the amplified region. If they are not specific, they may bind to other regions of the genome, amplifying unwanted DNA and producing multiple bands (or no band at all if they don't bind). Specificity ensures only the target region is amplified.

(c) The successful amplification should show a single band at 400 bp (migrating to the same position as the 400 bp ladder band). If the amplification worked well, this band should be bright (high concentration of DNA). No other bands should be present.

(d) The negative control lane should show no bands. This confirms that the PCR reagents (primers, Taq, dNTPs, water) are not contaminated with template DNA. If a band appears, the reagents are contaminated and results from sample lanes are unreliable.

Answer 4

(a) On the lagging strand of a linear chromosome, the terminal RNA primer is removed by DNA Pol I but cannot be replaced with DNA (no upstream 3'-OH to extend from). This means the 5' end of the newly synthesised strand is shorter than the template — the chromosome shortens with each replication. Circular chromosomes (bacteria) have no ends, so the lagging strand can be completed all the way around.

(b) Telomerase is a reverse transcriptase with its own RNA template. It binds to the 3' overhang of the G-rich telomere strand and extends it by adding TTAGGG repeats (using the RNA template). After the 3' strand is extended, primase can add a primer and conventional replication machinery fills in the complementary C-rich strand. The net result: telomere length is maintained or even increased.

(c) Cancer cells divide uncontrollably. Without telomerase, they would hit the Hayflick limit after ~50-70 divisions and enter senescence — halting tumour growth. Upregulation of telomerase allows cancer cells to bypass this limit, achieving unlimited replicative potential (a hallmark of cancer). Normal somatic cells silence telomerase, limiting their division capacity as a tumour-suppression mechanism.

(d) Mice have longer telomeres but shorter lifespans — this tells us that telomere length is not the sole determinant of aging. Mice likely die of other causes (predation, cancer, metabolic rate-related oxidative damage) before telomere shortening becomes limiting. The telomere theory is a contributing factor to aging, not the only mechanism.

(c) ddNTPs lack the 3'-OH group (they have a 3'-H instead). The 3'-OH is required for the nucleophilic attack on the incoming dNTP's α-phosphate, which forms the phosphodiester bond. Without 3'-OH, no further nucleotides can be added → chain termination.

Key Takeaways

Concept Summary
Meselson-Stahl ¹⁵N → ¹⁴N shift. 1 gen: one intermediate band (rules out conservative). 2 gen: light + intermediate bands (rules out dispersive → semiconservative confirmed).
Replication enzymes Helicase (unzip), SSBs (stabilise), Gyrase (relieve supercoils), Primase (RNA primer), Pol III (synthesise + proofread 3'→5'), Pol I (replace primers), Ligase (seal nicks).
Leading vs lagging Leading: continuous toward fork, 1 primer. Lagging: Okazaki fragments away from fork, many primers. Both synthesised 5'→3'.
PCR Denature 95°C, Anneal 50-65°C, Extend 72°C with Taq. $$2^n$$ amplification.
Gel electrophoresis DNA (−) → (+) electrode. Smaller = faster/further. Ladder for sizing.
Sanger sequencing ddNTPs (no 3'-OH) terminate chains. Four reactions, separate by size, read sequence.
NGS Massively parallel. Millions of fragments simultaneously. Bridge amplification + sequencing-by-synthesis.
Telomeres TTAGGG repeats. End-replication problem: lagging strand can't replicate to end → progressive shortening.
Telomerase Reverse transcriptase with RNA template. Active in germ cells, stem cells, cancer cells. Extends telomeres → immortality.
