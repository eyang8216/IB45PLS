1. What Evolution Is (and Isn't)

Let's start with the definition because confusion here causes endless problems.

Biological evolution is the change in the heritable characteristics of populations over successive generations. Notice the key words:

  • Heritable: The changes must be genetic — passed through DNA. Acquired traits (building big muscles at the gym) don't count.
  • Populations: Evolution happens to populations, not individuals. An individual organism does not evolve during its lifetime. A population of finches evolves over generations as beak sizes shift.
  • Over generations: Evolution is not instantaneous. It unfolds across reproductive cycles.

Evolution: Change in the heritable characteristics of a population over successive generations. Evolution acts on populations (not individuals), depends on genetic variation, and occurs across generational time scales. The primary mechanism driving adaptive evolution is natural selection.

What evolution is NOT:

  • It is NOT "just a theory" in the colloquial sense. In science, a theory is a well-substantiated explanation supported by massive evidence (like germ theory, atomic theory, or gravitational theory).
  • It does NOT claim "humans came from monkeys." Humans and modern apes share a common ancestor that lived 6–8 million years ago.
  • It does NOT have a direction or goal. Evolution is not "progressing toward perfection" — it produces organisms adapted to their current environment, which may change.

2. Evidence for Evolution: Four Pillars

The evidence for evolution comes from multiple independent fields. When different lines of evidence converge on the same conclusion, confidence in that conclusion is enormously strengthened. This convergence is what makes evolution so robust.

2.1 The Fossil Record

Fossils are the preserved remains, impressions, or traces of organisms from past geological ages. They provide a literal record — a snapshot — of life's history on Earth.

What the fossil record shows:

  1. Change over time: The deepest (oldest) rock layers contain only simple organisms. More complex forms appear in younger layers. You never find a rabbit fossil in Precambrian rock (as J.B.S. Haldane famously quipped when asked what would disprove evolution).
  1. Transitional forms: Fossils that display characteristics intermediate between ancestral and descendant groups. These are the "missing links" that anti-evolutionists claim don't exist — except we have found them, in abundance.

Key transitional fossils:

Fossil Age Intermediate Between Key Features
Tiktaalik roseae ~375 mya Fish → Tetrapods (land vertebrates) Fish-like scales and gills, but also a flat head with eyes on top, a mobile neck, and robust fin bones homologous to the tetrapod limb (a wrist!). Found in exactly the predicted environment (shallow freshwater).
Archaeopteryx lithographica ~150 mya Dinosaurs → Birds Feathers and wings like a bird, but teeth, bony tail, and clawed fingers like a theropod dinosaur. The classic transitional fossil.
Australopithecus afarensis* ("Lucy") ~3.2 mya Apes → Humans Bipedal (walked upright — pelvis and knee joint show this), but small brain (~400 cm³, similar to a chimpanzee). Human below the neck, ape above.
Ambulocetus natans* ("walking whale") ~49 mya Land mammals → Whales Had functional legs for walking on land AND could swim powerfully. Ears adapted for underwater hearing. Nostrils positioned midway on the snout (between the tip, as in land mammals, and the blowhole position of modern whales).

How fossils form: Most organisms don't fossilize. Fossilization requires rapid burial in sediment, mineral-rich water, and lots of time. Hard parts (bones, shells, teeth) fossilize more readily. Soft-tissue fossils are extremely rare and precious. The fossil record is therefore incomplete and biased — but it's still enormously informative.

2.2 Comparative Anatomy

If all organisms share common ancestors, we should see structural similarities that make no sense unless they were inherited and modified from a shared source.

#### Homologous Structures

Homologous structures are anatomical features that share a common evolutionary origin — they are built from the same basic plan because they were inherited from a common ancestor — but they may serve very different functions today.

The classic example is the pentadactyl limb — a five-digit limb structure found across vertebrates:

Organism Structure Function
Human Arm, wrist, five fingers Grasping and manipulation
Whale Flipper Swimming
Bat Wing Flying
Cat Foreleg Walking and running
Mole Forelimb Digging
Bird Wing Flying

Each of these limbs contains the same bone pattern: one bone (humerus) → two bones (radius and ulna) → small bones (carpals/wrist) → five digits (metacarpals and phalanges). The bones have been modified — elongated, shortened, fused, or reduced — but the underlying blueprint is unmistakable.

Why this is evidence for evolution: An intelligent designer could use completely different structures optimized for each function. A whale flipper doesn't need finger bones to be an efficient paddle. The only explanation for the shared pattern is that all these organisms inherited the basic limb structure from a common tetrapod ancestor and it was modified by natural selection for different purposes.

Homologous structures: Anatomical features in different species that share a common evolutionary origin (inherited from a common ancestor), though they may now serve different functions. Homology indicates divergent evolution — related organisms adapting to different environments. Example: the pentadactyl limb in vertebrates.

#### Analogous Structures (Contrast)

For contrast, analogous structures perform similar functions but evolved independently — they do NOT indicate common ancestry.

Example Analogous Structures Function
Bird wing vs. insect wing Both are wings Flying
Shark fin vs. dolphin flipper Both are swimming appendages Swimming
Human eye vs. octopus eye Both are camera-type eyes Vision

Analogous structures indicate convergent evolution — unrelated organisms evolving similar solutions to similar environmental challenges. The shared feature was not present in the common ancestor.

Key distinction for IB: Homology = shared ancestry (same internal structure, different function). Analogy = shared function (same purpose, different evolutionary origin).

#### Vestigial Structures

Vestigial structures are remnants of organs or structures that were functional in an ancestor but are now reduced in size and/or function. They make no sense in terms of design for a current lifestyle — but perfect sense as evolutionary leftovers.

Structure Organism Ancestral Function Evidence of Vestigiality
Appendix Humans Cellulose digestion in herbivorous ancestors Small, narrow pouch; people live normally after appendectomy
Pelvic bones Whales, some snakes Attachment for hind limbs Whales have tiny internal pelvic bones that serve no locomotive function but are homologous to tetrapod pelvic girdles
Wisdom teeth Humans Grinding tough plant material in ancestors with larger jaws Often impacted, frequently removed; some people never develop them
Coccyx (tailbone) Humans Tail in tailed ancestors Fused vertebrae at the base of the spine; occasionally babies born with a small external tail (atavism)
Eyes Blind cave fish Vision in surface-dwelling ancestors Eyes begin developing in embryos but degenerate; covered by skin in adults
Goosebumps Humans Fluffing up fur for insulation/intimidation Tiny arrector pili muscles contract, raising non-existent fur — useless on nearly hairless skin

Vestigial structure: A structure that has lost most or all of its original function through evolution. Vestigial structures are homologous to functional structures in related species and provide evidence of common ancestry and evolutionary change.

2.3 Molecular Evidence

Every living organism uses the same genetic code — DNA made of A, T, C, G (or A, U, C, G in RNA), transcribed into mRNA, translated into proteins using codons that specify the same amino acids. This universality is extraordinarily unlikely unless all life shares a common origin.

Key molecular evidence:

  1. Universal genetic code: With few minor exceptions (some mitochondria, some protists), all organisms use the same codon-to-amino-acid mapping. The code is arbitrary — there's no chemical reason that UUU must code for phenylalanine. A shared arbitrary code implies shared ancestry.
  1. DNA sequence similarity: The more recently two species diverged, the more similar their DNA sequences. Humans and chimpanzees share ~98.8% of their DNA. Humans and mice share ~85%. Humans and fruit flies share ~60%. Even humans and bananas share about ~50% of genes — because fundamental cellular processes (DNA replication, respiration, cell division) were established in a very ancient common ancestor.
  1. Shared pseudogenes: A pseudogene is a non-functional copy of a gene. If humans and chimpanzees share the same mutation that inactivates a pseudogene (e.g., the GULO gene for vitamin C synthesis, which is broken identically in both species), the most parsimonious explanation is that the mutation occurred once in their common ancestor.
  1. Endogenous retroviruses (ERVs): Retroviruses insert their DNA into host genomes. If a retrovirus infects a germline cell, the viral DNA can be passed to offspring and become a permanent genomic fossil — an ERV. Humans and chimpanzees share identical ERV insertions at identical genomic locations. The probability of the same virus inserting at exactly the same position independently in both lineages is vanishingly small — these are molecular "scars" inherited from a common ancestor.

2.4 Biogeography

The geographic distribution of species makes sense only in light of evolution:

  • Island biogeography: Remote islands have unique species that resemble species on the nearest mainland (not species from similar climates elsewhere). The Galápagos finches resemble South American finches, not African finches. This suggests they evolved from mainland colonists, not special creation for each island.
  • Continental drift correlations: Marsupials are concentrated in Australia and South America. These continents were connected via Antarctica when marsupials diversified. Placental mammals later outcompeted marsupials everywhere except where isolation (Australia, offshore South America) protected them.
  • Wallace Line: An invisible biogeographic boundary through Indonesia where Asian fauna (tigers, rhinos, monkeys) abruptly gives way to Australasian fauna (marsupials, cockatoos). This corresponds to deep-water channels that prevented dispersal even when sea levels were lower during ice ages.

3. Darwin's Theory of Natural Selection

Charles Darwin didn't discover evolution — the idea that species change had been proposed before. His revolutionary contribution was identifying the mechanism: natural selection.

3.1 The Logic of Natural Selection

Darwin's argument proceeds through four inescapable observations and three deductions:

Observation 1: Overproduction of offspring

All species produce more offspring than can possibly survive to adulthood. A single oak tree produces thousands of acorns per year. A female cod produces millions of eggs. If all survived, the world would be knee-deep in oak trees and cod within a few generations.

Observation 2: Population stability

Despite this reproductive potential, population sizes remain roughly stable over time. Most offspring die before reproducing.

Deduction 1: There is a struggle for existence.

Resources are limited (food, water, territory, mates). Organisms compete for these resources, and not all can survive.

Observation 3: Variation exists within populations.

No two individuals are exactly alike. They differ in size, speed, coloration, metabolism, disease resistance, and countless other traits. (This is the genetic and environmental variation we studied in Lesson 7.)

Observation 4: Much variation is heritable.

Offspring tend to resemble their parents. Variation has a genetic basis and can be passed on.

Deduction 2: Differential survival and reproduction.

Individuals with variations that are better suited to their environment are more likely to survive and reproduce. Those with less favorable variations are more likely to die or produce fewer offspring. This is natural selection — the environment "selects" which individuals contribute more genes to the next generation.

Deduction 3: Over generations, favorable traits accumulate.

As the process repeats generation after generation, the population becomes progressively better adapted to its environment. This is evolution by natural selection.

3.2 Key Points About Natural Selection

  1. Selection acts on phenotypes, but evolution tracks genotypes. Natural selection "sees" the organism's traits (phenotype), not its genes directly. But for evolution to occur, those traits must have a genetic basis.
  1. Selection is not random; mutation is. Mutations arise randomly. Natural selection is the opposite of random — it is a non-random filtering process that systematically favors beneficial variants.
  1. "Survival of the fittest" means differential reproductive success. "Fitness" in evolutionary biology is measured by the number of viable, fertile offspring an organism produces — not by strength, speed, or longevity.
  1. Natural selection has no foresight. It cannot plan for future environmental changes. It can only work with existing variation to optimize for current conditions.
  1. Natural selection can only work with existing variation. It does not create new traits; it selects among existing variants. Mutation provides the raw material; selection sculpts it.

Natural selection: The differential survival and reproduction of individuals due to differences in phenotype. It is the primary mechanism of adaptive evolution. The process requires: (1) variation in traits, (2) heritability of traits, (3) overproduction of offspring, and (4) differential reproductive success based on those traits.

3.3 A Concrete Example: Darwin's Finches

The Galápagos finches provide a beautifully documented example of natural selection in action, studied for decades by Peter and Rosemary Grant.

The setup: On Daphne Major, a tiny volcanic island in the Galápagos, a population of medium ground finches (Geospiza fortis) eats seeds. Beak depth varies in the population (variation exists, and it's heritable).

The event: In 1977, a severe drought drastically reduced the supply of small, soft seeds. Only large, hard seeds remained.

The selection: Finches with deeper, stronger beaks could crack the large seeds. Finches with smaller beaks could not and starved. Over the course of the drought, the average beak depth in the population increased by about 4%.

The evolution: The surviving finches bred. Because beak depth is heritable, their offspring had deeper beaks on average. The population had evolved — in a single generation — in direct response to environmental change.

The reversal: In 1983, heavy rains favored small seeds again. Finches with smaller beaks had an advantage. Beak depth shifted back. The Grants documented natural selection oscillating in real time.

4. Antibiotic Resistance: Evolution in Real Time

While Darwin's finches show evolution on a scale of years, bacteria show evolution on a scale of hours to days. Antibiotic resistance is the most medically urgent example of natural selection happening right now.

4.1 What Are Antibiotics?

Antibiotics are chemical compounds that kill bacteria (bactericidal) or inhibit their growth (bacteriostatic) without harming the human host (ideally). They target bacterial-specific features:

  • Penicillin: Blocks the enzyme that cross-links peptidoglycan in bacterial cell walls → bacteria burst from osmotic pressure.
  • Tetracycline: Binds to bacterial 70S ribosomes, blocking protein synthesis.
  • Ciprofloxacin: Inhibits bacterial DNA gyrase, preventing DNA replication.

4.2 How Resistance Evolves: Step by Step

Imagine a population of Staphylococcus aureus bacteria infecting a patient. The patient begins taking penicillin.

Step 1: Variation pre-exists.

Before any antibiotic enters the system, random mutations have already occurred in the bacterial population. Among millions of bacteria, a tiny fraction — perhaps just a few cells — happen to carry a mutation that confers some degree of penicillin resistance. This might be a mutation that:

  • Modifies the target protein (penicillin-binding protein) so the antibiotic can't bind.
  • Produces an enzyme (beta-lactamase) that breaks down the penicillin molecule.
  • Increases the number of efflux pumps that eject the antibiotic from the cell.

Step 2: The selective pressure is applied.

When the patient takes penicillin, susceptible bacteria die. The few resistant bacteria survive.

Step 3: Differential reproduction.

With competitors eliminated, the resistant bacteria reproduce rapidly (doubling every 20–30 minutes under ideal conditions), passing their resistance genes to their offspring.

Step 4: The population evolves.

Within days, the infection is dominated by resistant bacteria. A second course of penicillin is now useless.

IB Exam Tip: A common misconception is that antibiotics cause resistance mutations. They don't. The mutations are random and pre-existing. Antibiotics simply select for bacteria that already have resistance. If asked to explain antibiotic resistance evolution, always clarify: "Antibiotics do not cause resistance — they create a selective pressure in which pre-existing resistant variants have a survival advantage."

4.3 Horizontal Gene Transfer Accelerates the Spread

Bacteria don't have to wait for mutations to acquire resistance. They can share resistance genes directly through horizontal gene transfer:

  1. Conjugation: A bacterium with a resistance plasmid (a small circular DNA molecule carrying resistance genes) extends a pilus to a neighboring bacterium and transfers a copy of the plasmid.
  1. Transformation: A bacterium picks up naked DNA (including resistance genes) from the environment — released when other bacteria die and lyse.
  1. Transduction: A bacteriophage (bacterial virus) accidentally packages bacterial DNA (including resistance genes) and transfers it to a new host.

Horizontal gene transfer means resistance can spread not just vertically (parent to offspring) but horizontally — across species boundaries, even between different genera of bacteria. A resistance gene that evolved in E. coli in a pig's gut can end up in Salmonella infecting a human.

4.4 MRSA: A Case Study

MRSA (Methicillin-Resistant Staphylococcus aureus) is one of the most dangerous antibiotic-resistant pathogens.

Staphylococcus aureus is a common bacterium found on the skin and in the noses of about 30% of healthy people. Usually harmless, it can cause serious infections if it enters the body through wounds or during surgery — skin infections, pneumonia, bloodstream infections.

Methicillin was introduced in 1959 specifically to treat penicillin-resistant S. aureus. It is a beta-lactam antibiotic resistant to beta-lactamase enzymes.

How MRSA evolved: S. aureus acquired a gene called mecA, which encodes a modified penicillin-binding protein (PBP2a) that methicillin cannot bind to. The mecA gene is carried on a mobile genetic element called SCCmec (Staphylococcal Cassette Chromosome mec), which can transfer between S. aureus strains.

Timeline:

  • 1960: Methicillin introduced.
  • 1961: First MRSA strains detected (within TWO YEARS of methicillin introduction!).
  • 1980s–1990s: MRSA becomes a major hospital-acquired infection (HA-MRSA).
  • 2000s: Community-associated MRSA (CA-MRSA) emerges — infecting healthy people outside hospitals.

Consequences:

  • MRSA infections are harder and more expensive to treat, requiring "last-resort" antibiotics like vancomycin.
  • Higher mortality rates compared to methicillin-susceptible S. aureus.
  • Longer hospital stays, higher healthcare costs.
  • Some MRSA strains have now developed vancomycin resistance (VRSA) — our last line of defense is crumbling.

4.5 Strategies to Combat Antibiotic Resistance

This is a favorite IB topic — expect questions asking you to "discuss" or "evaluate" strategies.

Strategy Explanation Limitation
Finish the full course** of prescribed antibiotics Stopping early leaves the most resistant bacteria alive to reproduce Patient compliance is difficult; some argue standard course lengths are arbitrary
Reduce unnecessary prescriptions Many antibiotics are prescribed for viral infections (colds, flu) against which they are useless Diagnostic uncertainty — is it bacterial or viral?
Restrict agricultural use ~70% of antibiotics globally are fed to livestock for growth promotion, selecting for resistance Economic pressure; food industry lobbying
Develop new antibiotics Novel drug classes that bypass existing resistance mechanisms Pharmaceutical companies have low financial incentive (antibiotics are taken short-term; resistance makes them obsolete); few new classes in decades
Combination therapy Using multiple antibiotics simultaneously — much harder for bacteria to develop resistance to all at once Increased side effects; more expensive
Phage therapy Using bacteriophages (viruses that specifically kill bacteria) as an alternative Regulatory hurdles; narrow host range; bacterial resistance to phages can also evolve
Improved hygiene and infection control Hand washing, sterilization, isolation of infected patients in hospitals Requires consistent compliance by healthcare workers
Rapid diagnostics Quickly identifying the specific pathogen and its resistance profile to prescribe the right drug the first time Technology still developing; cost

IB Exam Tip: The classic structured question gives you data (a graph or table showing increasing resistance over time) and asks you to (a) describe the trend, (b) explain the evolution of resistance using natural selection, (c) suggest and evaluate strategies to reduce resistance. When evaluating strategies, always give one advantage AND one limitation for each strategy — this shows the "evaluation" skill that distinguishes top-scoring answers.

5. Worked Examples

Worked Example 1: Interpreting Fossil Evidence

A paleontologist discovers a fossil with the following features:

  • Feathers covering the body and forelimbs
  • A long, bony tail with unfused vertebrae
  • Teeth in the jaw (no beak)
  • Claws on the forelimbs
  • A furcula (wishbone)

Question: Identify the likely fossil and explain why it is considered a transitional form. (3 marks)

Step-by-step solution:

  1. The fossil is likely Archaeopteryx. The combination of avian features (feathers, wishbone) and reptilian/dinosaur features (teeth, bony tail, claws) is diagnostic (1 mark).
  1. It is considered a transitional form because it displays a mosaic of characteristics — some shared with ancestral theropod dinosaurs (teeth, unfused tail vertebrae, claws) and some shared with descendant birds (feathers, furcula) (1 mark).
  1. This demonstrates that birds evolved from theropod dinosaurs through a gradual accumulation of avian characteristics, with Archaeopteryx capturing a snapshot of this evolutionary transition (1 mark).

Worked Example 2: Natural Selection in Action

Question: Using Darwin's theory of natural selection, explain how a population of insects could evolve resistance to a pesticide. (5 marks)

Model answer:

  1. Variation exists: Within the insect population, there is genetic variation. Due to random mutation, a small number of insects possess alleles that confer some degree of resistance to the pesticide (1 mark).
  1. Overproduction and struggle: Insects produce many offspring, and resources are limited, so there is competition for survival (1 mark).
  1. Selection pressure: When the pesticide is applied, it acts as a selective agent. Susceptible insects are killed, but insects with resistance alleles survive (1 mark).
  1. Differential reproduction: The surviving resistant insects reproduce, passing their resistance alleles to their offspring. Susceptible insects that died leave fewer or no offspring (1 mark).
  1. Change in population: Over multiple generations, the frequency of resistance alleles in the population increases. The population has evolved resistance. Key point: The pesticide did not cause the resistance mutations — it selected for pre-existing resistant variants (1 mark).

Worked Example 3: Homology vs. Analogy

Classify each pair as homologous or analogous and justify:

a) The wing of a bat and the arm of a human

b) The wing of a butterfly and the wing of a bird

c) The flipper of a dolphin and the flipper of a penguin

Solution:

a) Homologous. Both are derived from the pentadactyl forelimb of a common mammalian ancestor. Same bone structure (humerus, radius, ulna, carpals, digits), adapted for different functions (flight vs. manipulation).

b) Analogous. Both are used for flight but have completely different structural origins. Butterfly wings are outgrowths of the exoskeleton with no bones. Bird wings are modified forelimbs with internal bones. No common winged ancestor — flight evolved independently in insects and vertebrates.

c) Homologous as tetrapod forelimbs (both derived from the pentadactyl limb pattern), but the streamlining and flipper shape are analogous (convergent evolution for aquatic locomotion). In IB, the expected answer would be homologous because both are modified tetrapod forelimbs with the same bone pattern, despite sharing a function.

6. Common Misconceptions

Misconception Reality
"Evolution is 'just a theory'" In science, "theory" means a well-substantiated explanation with overwhelming evidence — like germ theory or the theory of gravity. It does not mean "guess."
"Humans evolved from monkeys" Humans and modern monkeys share a common primate ancestor. We are cousins, not parent-offspring.
"Evolution has a goal or direction" Evolution has no foresight and no goal. It simply favors what works in the current environment.
"Individual organisms evolve" Populations evolve; individuals do not. An individual bacterium does not "become resistant" during its life — it either is resistant (and survives) or isn't (and dies).
"Antibiotics cause resistance mutations" Mutations are random and pre-existing. Antibiotics select for bacteria that already have resistance genes.
"Natural selection creates perfect organisms" Natural selection produces organisms that are "good enough" — adapted to current conditions. Constraints (historical, developmental, genetic) prevent perfection.
"Vestigial organs have no function" Some vestigial structures have acquired minor secondary functions. The human appendix may serve as a "safe house" for gut bacteria — but this doesn't explain its existence; its homology to the functional cecum in herbivores does.
"If evolution is true, why are there still monkeys?" This is like asking "If you descended from your grandparents, why do your cousins still exist?" A common ancestor can produce multiple descendant lineages that coexist.

7. Practice Problems

Problem 1 (Paper 1 style): Which of the following provides the strongest evidence that all life shares a common ancestor?

A. Similarities in embryonic development

B. The universality of the genetic code

C. Homologous structures

D. The fossil record

Click for answer

B. The genetic code is arbitrary — there's no chemical necessity that a particular codon specifies a particular amino acid. That all organisms use the same code (with minor exceptions) is extraordinarily unlikely unless it was inherited from a universal common ancestor. The fossil record, homology, and embryology all support common ancestry within specific groups, but the genetic code's universality points to a single origin of all life.

Problem 2 (Paper 2 style, 4 marks): Explain how the pentadactyl limb provides evidence for evolution.

Click for answer

Model answer:

  • The pentadactyl limb is a homologous structure found across diverse vertebrates — mammals, birds, reptiles, and amphibians (1 mark).
  • Despite serving very different functions (grasping in humans, swimming in whales, flying in bats, running in horses), all pentadactyl limbs share the same basic bone pattern: one bone (humerus) → two bones (radius/ulna) → carpals → metacarpals → five digits (1 mark).
  • This structural similarity despite functional diversity is explained by common ancestry — the basic pattern was inherited from a common tetrapod ancestor and then modified by natural selection for different environments (1 mark).
  • This pattern is difficult to explain through independent design (a designer could use more efficient, function-specific structures) but is exactly what evolution by descent with modification predicts (1 mark).

Problem 3 (Paper 2 style, 6 marks): Describe the process by which antibiotic resistance evolves in bacteria and discuss two strategies to reduce the spread of resistance.

Click for answer

Model answer:

Process (4 marks):

  • Genetic variation exists within bacterial populations due to random mutation; some individuals carry alleles conferring antibiotic resistance (1 mark).
  • When antibiotics are administered, they act as a selective pressure — susceptible bacteria die, while resistant bacteria survive (1 mark).
  • Resistant bacteria reproduce, passing resistance alleles to offspring (vertical transmission) and to other bacteria via horizontal gene transfer (conjugation, transformation, transduction) (1 mark).
  • Over many generations, the frequency of resistance alleles increases, producing a resistant population. Note: antibiotics do NOT cause resistance mutations; they select for pre-existing variants (1 mark).

Strategies (2 marks):

  • Strategy 1: Reduce unnecessary antibiotic prescriptions (e.g., for viral infections). Advantage: Decreases selection pressure, slowing resistance evolution. Limitation: Doctors face diagnostic uncertainty — it's not always clear whether an infection is bacterial or viral; patients often demand antibiotics (1 mark).
  • Strategy 2: Develop new classes of antibiotics. Advantage: Novel mechanisms of action bypass existing resistance. Limitation: Pharmaceutical companies have limited financial incentive because new antibiotics are used sparingly (to preserve efficacy) and resistance evolves quickly, making them poor long-term investments (1 mark).

Problem 4 (Paper 1 style): The human appendix and whale pelvic bones are examples of:

A. Homologous structures

B. Analogous structures

C. Vestigial structures

D. Convergent evolution

Click for answer

C. Both are vestigial structures — reduced remnants of organs that were functional in ancestors. The appendix is a remnant of the large cecum used by herbivorous ancestors for cellulose digestion; whale pelvic bones are remnants of hind limbs.

Problem 5: Tiktaalik is considered a transitional fossil between fish and tetrapods. List three features that support this classification.

Click for answer

Fish-like features:

  1. Scales on body
  2. Fin rays in the pectoral appendages (like fish fins)
  3. Gills for aquatic respiration

Tetrapod-like features:

  1. Flat head with eyes on top (like early tetrapods — suggests shallow-water habitat)
  2. Mobile neck (fish don't have necks; tetrapods do)
  3. Robust internal bones in pectoral fins showing the tetrapod limb pattern: a humerus, radius, and ulna — essentially a "wrist" joint that could support the body in shallow water

Summary

  • Evolution is change in heritable characteristics of populations over generations. It is supported by multiple independent lines of evidence: fossils, comparative anatomy (homologous and vestigial structures), molecular biology, and biogeography.
  • Homologous structures share a common origin (pentadactyl limb); analogous structures share function but not ancestry (bird wing vs. insect wing).
  • Darwin's natural selection requires variation, heritability, overproduction, and differential survival/reproduction. Selection acts on phenotypes; populations evolve.
  • Antibiotic resistance is a textbook example of natural selection: pre-existing resistant variants survive antibiotic treatment, reproduce, and spread resistance genes vertically and horizontally.
  • MRSA illustrates the clinical consequences: resistance to methicillin (and increasingly to vancomycin) forces use of more toxic, more expensive drugs.
