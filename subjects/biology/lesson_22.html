Part 1: Blood Composition — More Than Just Red Liquid

If you centrifuge (spin at high speed) a tube of blood, it separates into three distinct layers. From bottom to top:

  1. Erythrocytes (Red Blood Cells): ~44% of total volume — the dense, dark red layer at the bottom. This percentage is called the hematocrit.
  2. Buffy Coat: <1% — a thin, whitish layer between the RBCs and plasma. Contains leukocytes (white blood cells) and platelets.
  3. Plasma: ~55% — the straw-colored liquid at the top.

Plasma — The Liquid Matrix

Plasma is ~92% water. The remaining ~8% is an incredibly complex mixture:

Plasma Proteins (7% of plasma):

  • Albumin (~60% of plasma proteins): Produced by the liver. Maintains colloid osmotic pressure (oncotic pressure) — it's too large to leave capillaries, so it stays in the blood and draws water in by osmosis. This is crucial for maintaining blood volume. Low albumin (liver disease, malnutrition) → edema (tissue swelling from fluid leaking out).
  • Globulins (~35%): Three types:
  • Alpha and beta globulins: Transport lipids, hormones, and metal ions (e.g., transferrin transports iron, ceruloplasmin transports copper)
  • Gamma globulins (immunoglobulins/antibodies): Produced by plasma cells (activated B lymphocytes). Central to humoral immunity.
  • Fibrinogen (~4%): Soluble precursor of fibrin — the key protein in blood clotting (see Part 2). Produced by the liver.
  • Other: Clotting factors (prothrombin, etc.), complement proteins (immune defense), enzymes.

Other Plasma Components:

  • Nutrients: Glucose, amino acids, fatty acids (bound to albumin), vitamins
  • Electrolytes: Na⁺, K⁺, Ca²⁺, Mg²⁺, Cl⁻, HCO₃⁻, HPO₄²⁻ — maintain osmotic balance, pH (bicarbonate buffer), and membrane potentials
  • Dissolved gases: O₂ (minimal — most is on hemoglobin), CO₂ (as bicarbonate and dissolved), N₂
  • Hormones: Transported from endocrine glands to target cells
  • Waste products: Urea, uric acid, creatinine, bilirubin

Erythrocytes (Red Blood Cells) — The Oxygen Carriers

Structure:

  • Biconcave disc shape — flattened with depressed centers on both sides
  • This shape provides a high surface area to volume ratio for efficient gas exchange
  • It also allows the cell to be flexible — it can fold and squeeze through narrow capillaries
  • No nucleus — ejected during the final stage of development in bone marrow. This creates more room for hemoglobin.
  • No mitochondria — energy is produced exclusively by anaerobic glycolysis. This is clever: if RBCs had mitochondria, they'd consume some of the very O₂ they're supposed to transport!
  • Diameter: ~7-8 μm — slightly larger than typical capillary diameter, ensuring tight contact with the vessel wall

Hemoglobin Content:

Each RBC contains about 250-300 million hemoglobin molecules. Given 4 O₂-binding sites per hemoglobin, one RBC can carry about 1 billion O₂ molecules.

Life Cycle:

  • Production (Erythropoiesis): In the red bone marrow (in adults: sternum, vertebrae, ribs, pelvis, proximal ends of femur and humerus). Stimulated by erythropoietin (EPO) — a hormone produced by the kidneys when they detect hypoxia (low O₂). This is why people (and athletes who blood dope) produce more RBCs at high altitude.
  • Lifespan: ~120 days. Old RBCs become less flexible and get trapped in the spleen.
  • Destruction: In the liver and spleen, macrophages phagocytose old RBCs:
  • Globin (protein part) → broken down to amino acids, recycled
  • Heme → iron removed and recycled (bound to transferrin for transport, stored as ferritin in liver) → remaining porphyrin ring converted to bilirubin (yellow pigment) → excreted in bile → gives feces their brown color (after bacterial conversion to stercobilin)
  • Jaundice (yellow skin/eyes) = excess bilirubin in blood (liver disease, or excessive RBC breakdown)

Leukocytes (White Blood Cells) — The Immune Defenders

Less than 1% of blood volume, but essential. Two broad categories:

Granulocytes (contain cytoplasmic granules, lobed nuclei):

  • Neutrophils (~60-70%): Phagocytic — first responders to bacterial infection. Short-lived, die after engulfing bacteria (form pus).
  • Eosinophils (~2-4%): Attack parasites (worms), involved in allergic responses.
  • Basophils (~0.5-1%): Release histamine and heparin — promote inflammation. Mast cells in tissues have similar function.

Agranulocytes (no prominent granules, round/indented nuclei):

  • Lymphocytes (~25-30%): B cells (antibody production), T cells (cell-mediated immunity), natural killer cells. The cornerstone of adaptive immunity.
  • Monocytes (~3-8%): Largest WBCs. Leave blood, differentiate into macrophages in tissues — powerful phagocytes that also present antigens to T cells.

Platelets (Thrombocytes) — The Clot Formers

Platelets are not cells — they're fragments of cytoplasm pinched off from large bone marrow cells called megakaryocytes. Each megakaryocyte produces thousands of platelets.

  • No nucleus — just cytoplasm with granules containing clotting factors
  • Size: 2-4 μm diameter
  • Lifespan: 8-10 days
  • Function: Central to hemostasis (stopping bleeding) — see Part 2

Diagram to Draw — Centrifuged Blood:

Draw a test tube. Divide into three layers (bottom to top):

  • Bottom layer (~44%): Dark red, labeled "Erythrocytes (hematocrit)"
  • Thin middle line (<1%): White/pale, labeled "Buffy coat — Leukocytes + Platelets"
  • Top layer (~55%): Pale yellow, labeled "Plasma"

Note: This composition can change. High hematocrit = polycythemia (dehydration, EPO doping, high altitude). Low hematocrit = anemia (iron deficiency, B12 deficiency, blood loss).

Part 2: Blood Clotting — The Cascade

Blood clotting (coagulation) must be rapid to prevent blood loss but precisely controlled to prevent inappropriate clotting (thrombosis). The process is a cascade — each step activates the next, providing enormous amplification.

The Sequence

1. Vascular Injury and Vasoconstriction

When a blood vessel is damaged, the smooth muscle in its wall contracts immediately (vasoconstriction) to reduce blood flow. This is a temporary, reflexive response.

2. Platelet Plug Formation (Primary Hemostasis)

  • Damaged endothelial cells expose collagen fibers in the vessel wall
  • Platelets adhere to the exposed collagen via von Willebrand factor (a protein that bridges platelet receptors and collagen)
  • Adherent platelets become activated: they change shape (extend spiky pseudopodia), become sticky, and release the contents of their granules
  • Released substances include ADP, serotonin (enhances vasoconstriction), and thromboxane A₂ (promotes further platelet aggregation)
  • ADP and thromboxane A₂ attract more platelets, which adhere to the already-stuck platelets — platelet aggregation
  • This forms a platelet plug — a temporary seal. It's sufficient for small injuries but fragile for larger ones.

3. The Coagulation Cascade (Secondary Hemostasis)

This is where the protein fibrin forms a permanent, strong clot. The cascade involves a series of clotting factors (numbered I through XIII, mostly serine proteases produced by the liver) that exist in plasma in inactive forms.

The cascade can be triggered by:

  • Extrinsic pathway: Tissue factor (factor III) released from damaged tissue cells — faster, triggered by external trauma
  • Intrinsic pathway: Contact with collagen or negatively charged surfaces — slower, triggered internally

Both pathways converge at the common pathway:

> Prothrombin (factor II) —[activated by factor Xa]→ Thrombin (enzyme)

Thrombin then catalyzes:

> Fibrinogen (soluble plasma protein) —[thrombin]→ Fibrin (insoluble threads)

4. Fibrin Mesh Formation

  • Fibrin monomers polymerize into long, insoluble threads
  • Factor XIII (activated by thrombin) cross-links these threads into a stable mesh
  • The fibrin mesh traps erythrocytes and platelets, forming a solid clot
  • Platelets within the clot contract (using actin-myosin), pulling the edges of the wound together — clot retraction

5. Clot Removal (Fibrinolysis)

Once the vessel heals, the clot must be removed. Plasminogen (inactive, trapped in the clot) is activated to plasmin, which digests fibrin into soluble fragments.

Vitamin K and Clotting

Vitamin K is essential for the synthesis of several clotting factors (II, VII, IX, X) and proteins C and S (anticoagulants) in the liver. Vitamin K acts as a cofactor for the enzyme that adds carboxyl groups to glutamate residues on these proteins, enabling them to bind calcium (Ca²⁺) — which is required for their activity at the platelet surface.

  • Warfarin (a blood thinner/"rat poison") works by inhibiting vitamin K recycling, reducing clotting factor synthesis
  • Newborns receive a vitamin K injection at birth because they have low vitamin K stores

Clotting Cascade Summary:

Injury → collagen exposed → platelets adhere → platelets release ADP, thromboxane A₂ → more platelets aggregate → platelet plug → cascade of clotting factor activations → prothrombin → thrombin → fibrinogen → fibrin → fibrin mesh traps RBCs → solid clot.

Key enzymes: Thrombin (the central enzyme — converts fibrinogen to fibrin, activates platelets, activates factor XIII)

Vitamin K requirement: For synthesis of prothrombin and other clotting factors in the liver.

Part 3: Coronary Heart Disease (CHD)

Atherosclerosis — The Underlying Process

Atherosclerosis is the buildup of fatty plaques (atheromas) in the walls of arteries. It's a chronic inflammatory disease, not just a "plumbing problem."

The process:

  1. Endothelial injury: The endothelium (inner lining) of arteries is damaged by:
  • High blood pressure (mechanical stress)
  • Toxins from cigarette smoke
  • High blood glucose (diabetes)
  • Elevated LDL cholesterol levels
  1. LDL infiltration: Low-density lipoprotein (LDL) cholesterol particles enter the damaged arterial wall. LDL is often called "bad cholesterol" because it delivers cholesterol to tissues — including artery walls. (HDL — high-density lipoprotein — is "good cholesterol" because it removes cholesterol from tissues and returns it to the liver.)
  1. Oxidation and inflammation: LDL in the artery wall becomes oxidized. This triggers an inflammatory response — endothelial cells express adhesion molecules, and monocytes are recruited from the blood.
  1. Foam cell formation: Monocytes enter the artery wall and differentiate into macrophages. They engulf oxidized LDL via scavenger receptors, becoming lipid-laden foam cells. Foam cells accumulate, forming the initial fatty streak.
  1. Plaque development: Smooth muscle cells migrate from the tunica media into the intima and proliferate. They produce collagen and other extracellular matrix, forming a fibrous cap over the lipid core. Calcium deposits may form (calcification). The plaque bulges into the lumen, narrowing it.

Consequences of Atherosclerosis

  • Reduced blood flow: The narrowed lumen restricts blood flow. During exercise, when O₂ demand increases, the inadequate flow causes angina (chest pain) — the heart muscle is ischemic (O₂-starved) but not yet infarcted.
  • Plaque rupture: Plaques can rupture through the fibrous cap, exposing the thrombogenic (clot-promoting) lipid core to blood. This triggers rapid clot formation (thrombosis) at the site.
  • Myocardial infarction (heart attack): If a coronary artery becomes completely blocked by a thrombus (blood clot), the downstream heart muscle is starved of O₂ and begins to die (necrosis) within 20-40 minutes. Dead cardiac muscle is replaced by non-contractile scar tissue, permanently reducing heart function.
  • Stroke: Similar process in arteries supplying the brain.

Risk Factors for CHD

Risk Factor Mechanism
Diet high in saturated fats Raises LDL cholesterol levels
Smoking Damages endothelium, promotes oxidation, increases platelet stickiness, lowers HDL
High blood pressure Mechanical damage to endothelium, accelerates atherosclerosis

IB Exam Tip: When asked about coronary heart disease, always LINK the risk factor to the physiological mechanism. Don't just list risk factors — explain HOW each one contributes to atherosclerosis. For example: "A diet high in saturated fats raises plasma LDL cholesterol levels, which infiltrates damaged arterial endothelium, leading to foam cell formation and plaque development."

Part 4: The Lymphatic System

Not all fluid that leaves capillaries at the arteriole end returns at the venous end. About 10-15% remains in the tissues as excess interstitial fluid. If this fluid accumulated, you'd swell up like a water balloon within hours. The lymphatic system solves this problem.

Structure

The lymphatic system is a network of lymphatic vessels (similar to veins but thinner-walled with more valves) that run alongside blood vessels. The smallest branches — lymphatic capillaries — begin as blind-ended tubes in the tissues. They have overlapping endothelial cells that act as one-way flaps, allowing interstitial fluid (now called lymph) to enter but not exit.

Lymphatic vessels converge into larger trunks and ultimately drain into the thoracic duct (largest lymphatic vessel) and right lymphatic duct, which empty into the subclavian veins near the neck — returning lymph to the blood.

Functions

1. Fluid Balance:

Returns excess interstitial fluid (and any leaked plasma proteins) to the blood. Without this, plasma volume would progressively decrease and tissues would swell (edema).

2. Immune Defense:

  • Lymph nodes are bean-shaped structures along lymphatic vessels that act as filters. They contain dense populations of lymphocytes (B and T cells) and macrophages. Lymph percolates through the node; pathogens and foreign particles are trapped and destroyed.
  • When you have an infection, nearby lymph nodes swell — they're called "swollen glands" (they're nodes, not glands). This is because lymphocytes are proliferating rapidly to fight the infection.
  • Lymph nodes are also sites where antigen-presenting cells (dendritic cells, macrophages) display captured antigens to T lymphocytes, initiating adaptive immune responses.

3. Fat Absorption:

In the small intestine, specialized lymphatic capillaries called lacteals absorb dietary fats. Fats are packaged into chylomicrons (large lipoprotein particles) in intestinal epithelial cells and released into lacteals because they're too large to enter blood capillaries. The lymph carrying these fats (called chyle) is milky white. It eventually reaches the bloodstream via the thoracic duct.

Lymph Movement

Lymph, like venous blood, is under very low pressure. Movement is propelled by:

  • Skeletal muscle pump: Muscles compress lymphatic vessels
  • Respiratory pump: Pressure changes during breathing
  • Smooth muscle in the walls of larger lymphatic vessels (rhythmic contractions)
  • Valves prevent backflow

Elephantiasis (lymphatic filariasis) is a parasitic disease where worms block lymphatic vessels, causing extreme swelling — a dramatic illustration of how critical the lymphatic system is.

Part 5: Transport in Plants — Xylem

Plants face the same distance problem as animals: roots are far from leaves. They need a transport system for water (from roots to leaves) and the products of photosynthesis (from leaves to the rest of the plant). These two tasks are handled by two separate vascular tissues: xylem and phloem.

Xylem — The Water Highway (Up Only)

Xylem transports water and dissolved mineral ions from roots to shoots (leaves, stems, flowers). The flow is always upward and is driven by physical forces, not by a pump.

Cell Types in Xylem:

Tracheids:

  • Long, thin cells with tapered ends
  • Dead at maturity — they're hollow tubes with no cytoplasm
  • Walls contain lignin (a complex polymer), which:
  • Makes the walls rigid and waterproof
  • Provides structural support (wood is mostly lignified xylem)
  • Patterns vary: spiral, annular (rings), reticulate (net-like), pitted
  • Water moves between tracheids through pits — thin areas in the lignified wall where only the primary cell wall remains, allowing lateral water movement

Vessel Elements:

  • Found primarily in angiosperms (flowering plants) — more evolutionarily advanced
  • Shorter and wider than tracheids — larger diameter = less resistance, faster flow
  • Stacked end-to-end to form continuous tubes (vessels)
  • End walls are partially or completely broken down (perforation plates) — this dramatically reduces resistance, creating an open pipe
  • Also dead at maturity with lignified walls

Xylem Parenchyma and Fibers: Living parenchyma cells for storage, and sclerenchyma fibers for additional support.

The Cohesion-Tension Theory

How does water rise 100 meters to the top of a redwood tree? There's no pump. The explanation is the cohesion-tension theory, proposed by Henry Dixon and John Joly in 1894.

The theory has three components working together:

1. Transpiration (the driving force):

  • Water evaporates from the moist cell walls of mesophyll cells into the air spaces of the leaf
  • Water vapor diffuses out through stomata
  • This water loss is transpiration — it creates a water deficit in the leaf
  • The evaporation generates a very negative pressure (tension) at the top of the xylem — pulling water upward

2. Cohesion (water sticks to water):

  • Water molecules are held together by hydrogen bonds — they're strongly attracted to each other
  • This cohesion allows the water column in the xylem to be pulled up as a continuous, unbroken thread
  • Think of it like pulling a chain — you pull one link and the whole chain moves. Similarly, the tension at the top pulls the entire water column up from the roots.
  • The cohesive strength of water is impressive — water columns can withstand tensions of -30 MPa (megapascals), far more than needed for even the tallest trees.

3. Adhesion (water sticks to walls):

  • Water molecules also adhere to the hydrophilic (water-attracting) cellulose and lignin of the xylem walls
  • This adhesion helps counteract gravity and prevents the water column from breaking
  • It also contributes to capillary action — the tendency of water to rise in narrow tubes (capillarity)

Together: Transpiration creates tension at the top → tension is transmitted through the cohesive water column → water is pulled up from the roots.

How does water enter the roots?

  • Root hairs (extensions of epidermal cells) increase surface area for water uptake
  • Water enters roots by osmosis — the soil water has higher water potential (less negative) than root cells, so water moves in
  • Minerals are actively transported into root cells, lowering their water potential and drawing in water
  • Once in the xylem, water is pulled upward by the transpiration stream

Evidence for the Cohesion-Tension Theory

1. Diurnal changes in trunk diameter:

The diameter of tree trunks decreases during the day and increases at night. During the day, high transpiration rates create strong tension in the xylem, which pulls the xylem walls inward (like sucking on a straw — the walls collapse slightly). At night, when transpiration decreases and tension is reduced, the trunk expands again. This demonstrates that significant tension exists in the xylem during transpiration.

2. Breaking the water column:

If a xylem vessel is cut, the water column may break, and air enters (cavitation/embolism). This can be detected as an audible "click" using sensitive microphones. The fact that water columns can be under such tension that they cavitate supports the theory.

3. Measurement of negative pressure:

Using a Scholander pressure bomb, researchers can measure the tension in xylem. A cut leaf or branch is placed in a sealed chamber with the cut end protruding; pressure is applied until water appears at the cut surface. The pressure needed equals the tension the xylem was under — these measurements confirm significant negative pressures.

4. Dye movement:

Colored dyes injected into the base of a cut stem are pulled upward through the xylem — demonstrating the upward flow and the pathway.

Part 6: Transport in Plants — Phloem

Phloem transports organic solutes (mainly sucrose, also amino acids, hormones) from sources (where they're produced or released) to sinks (where they're used or stored). Unlike xylem, phloem transport is bidirectional — up or down depending on where sources and sinks are located.

Sources and Sinks

Sources:

  • Mature leaves (producing sucrose by photosynthesis — the main source)
  • Storage organs releasing stored nutrients (e.g., a potato tuber in spring, sending sugars to growing shoots; a root during regrowth)
  • Germinating seeds (cotyledons releasing stored nutrients)

Sinks:

  • Growing tips (apical meristems — shoot tips, root tips) — need sugars for respiration and growth
  • Developing fruits and seeds — accumulating storage compounds
  • Storage organs filling (e.g., a potato tuber in autumn, receiving sugars from dying leaves; taproots)
  • Any actively respiring, non-photosynthetic tissue

A given organ can be a source at one time and a sink at another. A developing leaf is a sink (importing sugars for growth); once mature, it becomes a source (exporting sugars from photosynthesis).

Phloem Structure

Sieve Tube Elements:

  • Living cells arranged end-to-end to form sieve tubes
  • No nucleus at maturity — it degenerates, creating more space for flow
  • Few organelles — ribosomes, Golgi, and vacuoles are reduced or absent
  • Sieve plates — the end walls between adjacent sieve tube elements, perforated with large pores that allow cytoplasmic continuity and solute flow
  • Cytoplasm is reduced to a thin layer along the wall, with a continuous system of P-protein (phloem protein) that may help seal damaged sieve tubes

Companion Cells:

  • Living cells adjacent to each sieve tube element, connected by numerous plasmodesmata (cytoplasmic channels through the cell wall)
  • Have a nucleus, abundant mitochondria, and ribosomes — fully metabolically active
  • Provide ATP and metabolic support to the sieve tube element — they're the "life support" for the enucleated sieve tube
  • In some plants, companion cells have extensive cell wall ingrowths (transfer cells) to increase surface area for transport

The Pressure-Flow Hypothesis (Münch's Hypothesis)

Proposed by Ernst Münch in 1930, this is the most widely accepted model for phloem translocation:

Step 1: Active loading at the source

  • Sucrose is actively loaded into sieve tube elements at the source (e.g., mesophyll cells → companion cells → sieve tube)
  • This occurs via H⁺-sucrose co-transport (symport): H⁺ is actively pumped out of the sieve tube by H⁺-ATPase, creating a proton gradient. Sucrose is then co-transported back into the sieve tube with H⁺ flowing down its gradient.
  • The high concentration of sucrose in the sieve tube makes the water potential very negative (low ψ).

Step 2: Water enters from xylem

  • The low water potential in the phloem at the source draws water in from the adjacent xylem by osmosis.
  • This influx of water increases the hydrostatic pressure at the source — the phloem is now under high pressure.

Step 3: Pressure-driven bulk flow

  • At the sink, sucrose is actively unloaded (removed) from the sieve tube. This raises the water potential (solute concentration decreases).
  • Water leaves the phloem by osmosis (back into xylem or into sink cells).
  • This decreases the hydrostatic pressure at the sink.

The pressure gradient between source (high pressure) and sink (low pressure) drives the bulk flow of phloem sap — a solution of sucrose, amino acids, and other solutes — through the sieve tubes. No pump is needed; it's entirely passive once the sucrose is loaded/unloaded.

Evidence for the Pressure-Flow Hypothesis:

  1. Aphid stylets: Aphids (small insects) insert their mouthparts (stylets) directly into sieve tubes. If the aphid is cut off, phloem sap continues to exude from the stylet — demonstrating positive pressure in the phloem. The sap can be collected and analyzed, confirming that it contains high concentrations of sucrose.
  1. Radioactive tracers: ¹⁴CO₂ supplied to a leaf is incorporated into sucrose by photosynthesis. Autoradiography shows the radioactive sucrose moving from the source leaf to sink tissues through the phloem.
  1. Pressure measurements: Direct measurements show higher hydrostatic pressure at sources than sinks.
  1. Metabolic inhibitors: Applying inhibitors of respiration (e.g., cyanide) to the source stops active sucrose loading, which stops phloem flow — demonstrating that active transport (requiring ATP) is involved in setting up the pressure gradient, even though the flow itself is passive.

Comparison — Xylem vs. Phloem:

Feature Xylem Phloem
Transports Water + mineral ions Organic solutes (mainly sucrose)

Cells Dead (tracheids, vessel elements) Living (sieve tube elements + companion cells)

Part 7: Xerophyte and Halophyte Adaptations

Xerophytes — Surviving Dry Conditions

Xerophytes are plants adapted to environments with limited water availability (deserts, rocky outcrops, sand dunes). Their adaptations minimize water loss while still allowing gas exchange:

Structural Adaptations:

  • Thick, waxy cuticle on leaves and stems: The cuticle is a waterproof layer of cutin (a waxy polymer) that severely reduces water loss through the epidermis. In extreme xerophytes, the cuticle can be several times thicker than in mesophytes.
  • Sunken stomata: Stomata are located in pits or grooves in the leaf surface. These pits trap a layer of still, humid air, reducing the water vapor concentration gradient between the leaf interior and the external air → slower transpiration.
  • Reduced leaves (spines): In cacti, leaves are reduced to spines. This dramatically reduces surface area for water loss. Photosynthesis is carried out by the green, succulent stem.
  • Rolled leaves: Some grasses and heathers roll their leaves inward, trapping humid air inside a chamber where stomata are located (on the inner surface). The outer surface has a thick cuticle.
  • Extensive root systems: Either very deep (to reach groundwater — e.g., mesquite with roots 50+ m deep) or very wide and shallow (to capture rare rainfall over a large area — e.g., cacti).
  • Succulent water storage: Thick, fleshy leaves or stems with large, thin-walled parenchyma cells that store water (e.g., aloe, agave, cacti).

Physiological Adaptations:

  • CAM photosynthesis (see Lesson 20): Stomata open at night, CO₂ fixed into malic acid, Calvin cycle runs during the day with stomata closed — drastically reduces water loss.
  • Stomatal closure during the hottest part of the day: Even non-CAM xerophytes may close stomata at midday to minimize water loss, though this limits photosynthesis.
  • High solute concentration in cells: Lowers water potential, allowing plants to extract water from drier soil.

Halophytes — Surviving Salty Conditions

Halophytes are plants adapted to high-salinity environments (salt marshes, mangroves, coastal dunes). The osmotic challenge: if soil water has very low water potential (very negative, due to high salt), water tends to leave the plant rather than enter it.

Adaptations:

  • Salt glands: Specialized epidermal structures that actively secrete excess salt onto the leaf surface, where it crystallizes and may be washed off by rain (e.g., mangroves, some grasses).
  • Salt bladders: Modified epidermal cells that accumulate salt and then burst or are shed.
  • Succulent leaves: Dilute the absorbed salt by storing large volumes of water.
  • Selective ion uptake: Roots selectively take up K⁺ while excluding Na⁺.
  • Compartmentalization: Salt is sequestered in vacuoles (away from metabolic enzymes in the cytoplasm) or in old leaves that are eventually shed.
  • Organic solutes (osmolytes): Produce compatible solutes (proline, glycine betaine, sugars) to lower cellular water potential without toxic effects, balancing the low external water potential.

Part 8: Heart Rate Control

The heart has intrinsic rhythmicity — if you remove all nervous connections, it still beats (about 100 bpm, the intrinsic rate of the SA node). But the body modulates this rate through neural and hormonal control.

Intrinsic Conduction System

1. Sinoatrial (SA) Node:

  • Located in the wall of the right atrium, near the entrance of the superior vena cava
  • The pacemaker — spontaneously depolarizes at the fastest rate (~100 times/min intrinsically)
  • Generates electrical impulses that spread through both atria (via gap junctions), causing atrial contraction
  • The SA node rate sets the heart rate because it "resets" all other pacemaker cells before they can fire at their own slower intrinsic rates

2. Atrioventricular (AV) Node:

  • Located at the junction between atria and ventricles (in the interatrial septum, near the tricuspid valve)
  • The only electrical connection between atria and ventricles (the fibrous skeleton of the heart otherwise insulates them)
  • Receives the impulse from the SA node and delays it by ~0.1 seconds
  • This delay is critical — it allows the atria to finish contracting and the ventricles to fill completely before ventricular contraction begins

3. Bundle of His (Atrioventricular Bundle):

  • Conducts the impulse from the AV node through the fibrous skeleton into the interventricular septum
  • Divides into right and left bundle branches

4. Purkinje Fibers:

  • Spread the impulse rapidly through the ventricular walls (both left and right)
  • This ensures coordinated, nearly simultaneous contraction of all ventricular muscle cells — from apex (bottom) upward, efficiently pushing blood toward the outflow valves

Autonomic Nervous System Control

The SA node receives input from both branches of the autonomic nervous system:

Parasympathetic (slows heart rate):

  • Vagus nerve (cranial nerve X)
  • Releases acetylcholine at the SA node
  • Acetylcholine binds muscarinic receptors → increases K⁺ permeability → hyperpolarizes SA node cells → they reach threshold more slowly → decreases heart rate
  • Dominant at rest — keeps resting heart rate at ~70 bpm (below the intrinsic 100 bpm)
  • The phrase "vagal tone" refers to this constant parasympathetic slowing

Sympathetic (speeds heart rate):

  • Cardiac accelerator nerves from the sympathetic chain
  • Releases noradrenaline (norepinephrine) at the SA node (and ventricular muscle)
  • Noradrenaline binds β₁-adrenergic receptors → increases Na⁺ and Ca²⁺ permeability → SA node cells depolarize faster → increases heart rate and increases force of contraction
  • Activated during exercise, stress, fear ("fight or flight")

Hormonal Control

Epinephrine (adrenaline):

  • Released from the adrenal medulla (above the kidneys)
  • Binds the same β₁-adrenergic receptors as noradrenaline
  • Increases both heart rate and contractility
  • Part of the systemic "fight or flight" response

Feedback Control — Chemoreceptors and Baroreceptors

Chemoreceptors:

  • Located in the carotid bodies (at the bifurcation of the carotid artery in the neck) and aortic bodies (in the aortic arch)
  • Detect: ↓pO₂, ↑pCO₂, ↓pH (all signs of increased metabolic demand or inadequate respiration)
  • Signal to the medulla oblongata → increase sympathetic output → ↑ heart rate, ↑ breathing rate

Baroreceptors (pressure receptors):

  • Located in the carotid sinus and aortic arch
  • Detect changes in blood pressure (stretch of the arterial wall)
  • High BP → increased baroreceptor firing → medulla → increase parasympathetic, decrease sympathetic → ↓ heart rate, vasodilation
  • Low BP → decreased baroreceptor firing → increase sympathetic → ↑ heart rate, vasoconstriction
  • This is the baroreceptor reflex — a negative feedback loop maintaining blood pressure

Part 9: The Electrocardiogram (ECG)

The ECG (or EKG) records the electrical activity of the heart from electrodes placed on the skin. It does NOT record mechanical contraction — it records the spread of depolarization and repolarization through the heart. Contraction follows depolarization by milliseconds.

The Normal ECG Waves

P wave:

  • Small, rounded upward deflection
  • Represents atrial depolarization (spread of electrical activity from SA node through both atria)
  • Atrial contraction follows immediately after
  • Duration: ~0.08-0.10 seconds

QRS complex:

  • Q: small downward deflection
  • R: tall, sharp upward deflection
  • S: downward deflection returning to baseline
  • Represents ventricular depolarization (spread through AV node → Bundle of His → Purkinje fibers → ventricular muscle)
  • This large, sharp spike reflects the large muscle mass of the ventricles
  • Atrial repolarization also occurs during this time but is masked by the much larger QRS complex
  • Duration: ~0.06-0.10 seconds (prolonged = conduction problem)

T wave:

  • Broader, rounded upward deflection (normally)
  • Represents ventricular repolarization (ventricles resetting electrically, preparing for the next cycle)
  • Duration: variable (~0.15-0.20 seconds)

PR Interval:

  • From start of P wave to start of QRS complex
  • Represents the time for the impulse to travel from SA node through atria, AV node (the delay), and into the ventricles
  • Normal: 0.12-0.20 seconds

ST Segment:

  • From end of S wave to start of T wave
  • Should be flat (isoelectric) — represents the period when ventricles are depolarized and contracting (plateau phase)
  • Elevation or depression of this segment is clinically significant

QT Interval:

  • From start of QRS to end of T wave
  • Represents total ventricular depolarization and repolarization time
  • Varies with heart rate (shorter at faster rates)

ECG Abnormalities

Prolonged PR Interval (>0.20 s):

  • First-degree heart block — delayed conduction through AV node
  • Can be caused by: increased vagal tone, drugs (beta-blockers, digoxin), ischemia, fibrosis

Missing QRS after P waves (some P waves not followed by QRS):

  • Second-degree heart block — some impulses fail to conduct through AV node

No relationship between P waves and QRS:

  • Third-degree (complete) heart block — atria and ventricles beat independently; ventricles driven by a slow escape pacemaker (~30-40 bpm)
  • Requires a pacemaker

Elevated ST Segment:

  • ST-elevation myocardial infarction (STEMI) — the classic sign of an acute, full-thickness heart attack
  • The elevated ST segment indicates injured myocardium that cannot fully repolarize

Depressed ST Segment:

  • Myocardial ischemia — insufficient O₂ to heart muscle (may occur during angina or a non-STEMI heart attack)

Irregular Intervals Between QRS Complexes:

  • Arrhythmia — irregular heart rhythm
  • Atrial fibrillation: No discernible P waves, irregularly irregular QRS complexes — the atria quiver rather than contract, increasing stroke risk (blood pools and clots)
  • Ventricular fibrillation: Chaotic, no organized QRS — lethal without immediate defibrillation

Diagram to Draw — Normal ECG:

Draw axes: x-axis = time (mark off small squares), y-axis = voltage (mV).

Draw one cardiac cycle:

  1. Small rounded P wave
  2. Brief flat segment (PR segment)
  3. Q wave (small down), R wave (tall up), S wave (down, back to baseline) — the QRS complex
  4. Flat ST segment
  5. Broader rounded T wave

Label: P (atrial depolarization), QRS (ventricular depolarization), T (ventricular repolarization)

Measure PR interval (P start to QRS start) and QT interval (QRS start to T end).

For example: "The elevated ST segment indicates that a region of ventricular muscle is not repolarizing properly, which is characteristic of an acute myocardial infarction — this is a medical emergency requiring immediate intervention."

Practice Problems

Problem 1: Blood Clotting

Describe the role of thrombin in blood clotting, including its substrate, its product, and what happens to the product.

Click for Answer

Thrombin is the central enzyme of the coagulation cascade. It is formed from the inactive precursor prothrombin (factor II) through the action of factor Xa (activated factor X) in the common pathway.

Thrombin catalyzes the conversion of fibrinogen (a soluble plasma protein) into fibrin (insoluble protein threads).

Fibrin monomers spontaneously polymerize into long strands, which are then cross-linked by factor XIIIa (activated by thrombin) to form a stable, insoluble mesh. This fibrin mesh traps erythrocytes and platelets, forming a solid clot that seals the damaged blood vessel.

Additionally, thrombin:

  • Activates platelets (enhances aggregation)
  • Activates factor XIII (for cross-linking)
  • Activates factors V and VIII (positive feedback — amplifies its own production)

Thrombin is thus the key enzyme converting the liquid blood into a solid clot.

Problem 2: Cohesion-Tension Theory

Explain the cohesion-tension theory of water transport in xylem. Include the roles of transpiration, cohesion, and adhesion, and describe one piece of evidence supporting the theory.

Click for Answer

The cohesion-tension theory explains how water rises from roots to leaves against gravity without a pump:

1. Transpiration (the driving force): Water evaporates from moist mesophyll cell walls in the leaf and diffuses out through stomata. This evaporation generates a negative pressure (tension) at the top of the xylem in the leaf. This tension is the "pull."

2. Cohesion: Water molecules are strongly attracted to each other through hydrogen bonds. This cohesion allows the water in the xylem to form a continuous, unbroken column from roots to leaves. When tension is applied at the top, the entire column is pulled upward — like pulling a chain.

3. Adhesion: Water molecules also adhere to the hydrophilic cellulose and lignin of xylem walls. This helps the water column resist gravity and prevents it from breaking.

Evidence:

Diurnal trunk diameter changes: Tree trunks decrease in diameter during the day (when high transpiration creates strong tension, pulling xylem walls inward) and increase at night (when transpiration is low and tension is reduced). This demonstrates that significant negative pressure exists in the xylem during transpiration, consistent with the theory.

Other valid evidence: measurement of negative pressure using pressure bombs, audible cavitation when water columns break, dye tracer experiments showing upward water movement through xylem.

Problem 3: Pressure-Flow Hypothesis

A student states, "Phloem transport is passive because it's driven by a pressure gradient." Critically evaluate this statement.

Click for Answer

The statement is partially correct but incomplete. The flow of phloem sap through sieve tubes is passive — it's driven by the hydrostatic pressure gradient from source (high pressure) to sink (low pressure), which is bulk flow requiring no additional energy.

However, setting up the pressure gradient requires active transport (ATP):

  1. At the source: Sucrose is actively loaded into sieve tubes via H⁺-sucrose co-transport. The H⁺ gradient is maintained by H⁺-ATPase pumps, which directly consume ATP. Without this active loading, the high solute concentration (and resulting low water potential) at the source would not exist.
  1. At the sink: Sucrose is actively unloaded by sink cells (for storage or respiration), also requiring energy. Without unloading, the solute concentration at the sink would rise, reducing the pressure gradient.

So the correct characterization is: Active transport at source and sink establishes the pressure gradient; flow through the phloem is passive bulk flow. Saying "phloem transport is completely passive" is incorrect because it ignores the essential energy-requiring steps at the ends.

This is analogous to water flow in a pipe: once you open a tap at the bottom of a water tower, water flows passively. But energy was required to pump the water up into the tower in the first place.

Problem 4: Heart Rate Control

During a blood donation, a donor loses approximately 500 mL of blood. Explain the body's immediate physiological response to maintain blood pressure, including the roles of baroreceptors and the autonomic nervous system.

Click for Answer

Blood loss → reduced blood volume → decreased venous return → decreased cardiac output → decreased arterial blood pressure.

Detection:

Baroreceptors in the carotid sinus and aortic arch detect reduced stretch of the arterial wall (decreased firing rate). This signal is transmitted to the cardiovascular control center in the medulla oblongata.

Response (coordinated by medulla):

  1. Increased sympathetic output to the SA node → ↑ heart rate (tachycardia)
  2. Increased sympathetic output to ventricular muscle → ↑ force of contraction (increased stroke volume)
  3. Increased sympathetic output to arterioles → vasoconstriction → ↑ total peripheral resistance → ↑ blood pressure
  4. Increased sympathetic output to veins → venoconstriction → ↑ venous return → ↑ cardiac output (by Frank-Starling mechanism)
  5. Decreased parasympathetic (vagal) output to SA node → removes the "brake," further increasing heart rate

Combined effect: Blood pressure is maintained or partially restored despite the volume loss. Heart rate increases, vessels constrict — the donor may feel slightly dizzy but generally maintains adequate perfusion.

Longer-term: Kidneys release renin → angiotensin II → vasoconstriction + aldosterone (Na⁺/water retention) + ADH release → restore blood volume. Bone marrow increases RBC production (erythropoietin).

Problem 5: ECG Interpretation

A patient's ECG shows: (a) no P waves before the QRS complexes, (b) a heart rate of 45 bpm, (c) QRS complexes are wide and abnormally shaped. Interpret these findings and suggest a possible diagnosis.

Click for Answer

(a) No P waves: The atria are not depolarizing normally — the SA node is not driving the rhythm. This could mean the SA node has failed or the atrial impulse is not conducting.

(b) Heart rate of 45 bpm (bradycardia): This is much slower than the normal SA node rate (~70 bpm) or even the intrinsic SA node rate (~100 bpm). This suggests a lower pacemaker has taken over — the AV node (intrinsic rate ~40-60 bpm) or the Purkinje fibers (intrinsic rate ~20-40 bpm).

(c) Wide, abnormally shaped QRS complexes: The impulse is originating in the ventricles (not traveling through the normal, rapid His-Purkinje system). Ventricular muscle depolarization spreads cell-to-cell rather than through the specialized conduction system, which is slower and produces a wider, bizarre QRS.

Diagnosis: Complete (third-degree) heart block with a ventricular escape rhythm.

The SA node impulses are not conducting through the AV node at all (complete block). The atria and ventricles beat independently. The ventricles are being driven by an escape pacemaker somewhere in the ventricles (hence the slow rate and wide QRS). The atria are likely still depolarizing (P waves may be present but hidden in the QRS or T waves, or they may not be visible).

Clinical significance: This is serious — the ventricular rate (45 bpm) may not provide adequate cardiac output. A pacemaker is usually required.

Key Takeaways

  • Blood: ~55% plasma (water, proteins — albumin, globulins, fibrinogen), ~44% erythrocytes (biconcave, no nucleus/mitochondria, hemoglobin, 120-day lifespan), <1% leukocytes and platelets.
  • Clotting cascade: Injury → platelets → clotting factors → prothrombin → thrombin → fibrinogen → fibrin → mesh traps RBCs. Vitamin K required.
  • Atherosclerosis: LDL infiltrates damaged endothelium → oxidized → foam cells → plaque → narrowed lumen, risk of thrombosis → myocardial infarction.
  • Lymphatic system: Returns excess interstitial fluid, filters lymph (immune defense), absorbs dietary fats (lacteals).
  • Xylem: Dead, lignified tubes. Cohesion-tension: transpiration creates tension, cohesion maintains water column, adhesion to walls. Evidence: trunk diameter decreases during day.
  • Phloem: Living sieve tube elements + companion cells. Pressure-flow: active sucrose loading at source → water enters by osmosis → high pressure → bulk flow to sink (low pressure from unloading).
  • Heart rate control: SA node (pacemaker) → AV node (delay) → His-Purkinje system. Sympathetic (noradrenaline) ↑HR. Parasympathetic/vagus (ACh) ↓HR. Epinephrine ↑HR.
  • ECG: P = atrial depolarization, QRS = ventricular depolarization, T = ventricular repolarization. ST elevation = MI. Prolonged PR = heart block.
