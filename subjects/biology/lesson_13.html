1. Polysaccharide Structures — HL Detail

1.1 Amylose: The Helix in Detail

Amylose is the linear component of starch, composed of α-D-glucose units linked exclusively by α-1,4 glycosidic bonds. At the HL level, you need to understand the precise three-dimensional consequence of this linkage pattern.

#### Why Does α-1,4 Produce a Helix?

In an α-1,4 linked polymer, all glucose residues are oriented in the same direction (no flipping). The bond angle at the glycosidic oxygen (approximately 117°) combined with the stereochemistry of the α-linkage causes the polymer chain to adopt a left-handed helical conformation.

Key numbers to memorize:

  • 6 glucose residues per complete turn of the helix
  • The helix has a pitch (rise per turn) of approximately 0.8 nm
  • The external diameter of the helix is about 1.3 nm
  • The internal cavity has a diameter of about 0.5 nm

The Iodine-Starch Complex:

Iodine ($$I_2$$) in potassium iodide ($$KI$$) solution exists as triiodide ions ($$I_3^-$$) and pentaiodide ions ($$I_5^-$$). These linear polyiodide chains fit perfectly inside the hydrophobic interior of the amylose helix. When iodine slips inside, it forms a charge-transfer complex — electrons are partially transferred from the glucose oxygen atoms to the iodine chain. This complex absorbs light in the visible spectrum, producing the characteristic blue-black color (λ_max ≈ 620 nm).

Amylose-iodine complex: A charge-transfer complex formed when polyiodide ions ($$I_3^-$$, $$I_5^-$$) are trapped within the helical cavity of amylose, producing a characteristic blue-black color. This is the basis of the starch-iodine test.

Diagram to draw in your notes:

  1. A ribbon helix showing approximately 2 full turns (12 glucose residues).
  2. The helix should show the hydrophobic interior.
  3. Draw a linear $$I_5^-$$ chain (five iodine atoms in a line) nestled inside the helix.
  4. Label: "~6 glucose residues per turn," "Internal cavity ~0.5 nm diameter," "Polyiodide chain inside → blue-black color."
  5. Annotate: "α-1,4 glycosidic bonds along the backbone."

IB Exam Tip: You may be asked to explain why the iodine test works and what structural feature of amylose is responsible. The answer: the helical conformation of α-1,4 linked amylose creates a cavity of appropriate size for polyiodide ions, and the charge-transfer complex formed absorbs visible light, producing the blue-black color. Amylopectin and glycogen have shorter helical segments due to branching, so they give a different color (reddish-purple/brown).

1.2 Amylopectin: The Branching Pattern

Amylopectin, the branched component of starch, has the same α-1,4 backbone as amylose but incorporates α-1,6 glycosidic bonds at branch points. At the HL level, understand:

Branching pattern:

  • A branch point occurs approximately every 20 glucose residues along the main chain
  • The branch is formed when a terminal glucose C-1 forms an α-glycosidic bond with a C-6 -OH of a glucose in the main chain
  • The branch itself extends as an α-1,4 chain
  • Branching creates a tree-like (dendrimeric) structure

Why this arrangement? Each non-reducing end (the terminal glucose with a free C-4 -OH) is a site where degradative enzymes (β-amylase, starch phosphorylase) can begin cleaving off glucose units. More branches = more non-reducing ends per molecule = faster mobilization of glucose.

Diagram to draw:

  1. A main α-1,4 chain.
  2. Branch emerging from a glucose C-6 via an α-1,6 bond.
  3. The branch extending as an α-1,4 chain.
  4. Circle and label the branch point: "α-1,6 glycosidic bond."
  5. Count and note: "Branch approximately every 20 glucose residues."

1.3 Cellulose: β-1,4 Straight Chains and Microfibrils

This is one of the most important HL topics. The difference between α and β linkages is not just a trivial geometric variation — it has profound structural and biological consequences.

#### The Alternating Flipped Arrangement

In cellulose, each glucose is in the β configuration, meaning the -OH on C-1 points UP (same side as the C-5 -CH₂OH). To form a β-1,4 glycosidic bond between two β-glucose units, the second glucose must be rotated (flipped) 180° relative to the first.

The result: in cellulose, every other glucose residue is flipped upside down relative to its neighbors. The repeating unit in cellulose is actually cellobiose (a disaccharide of two β-1,4 linked glucose units), not glucose.

Diagram to draw:

  1. Draw three β-glucose rings in a row.
  2. The first and third are oriented "normally" (C-5 -CH₂OH pointing UP).
  3. The second (middle) is flipped 180° (C-5 -CH₂OH pointing DOWN).
  4. Show the β-1,4 glycosidic oxygen between each pair.
  5. Label: "Alternating flipped arrangement due to β-1,4 linkage."

#### From Polyglucose Chain to Microfibril

The alternating flipped arrangement produces a polymer that is essentially straight and flat, unlike the helical amylose chain. This straightness is critical because it allows:

  1. Parallel alignment: Multiple cellulose chains can lie side by side in perfect alignment.
  2. Inter-chain hydrogen bonding: The -OH groups on adjacent chains (particularly the -OH on C-3 and the ring oxygen of the neighboring chain, and the -OH on C-6 with the -OH on C-2 of the next chain) form extensive hydrogen bonds.
  3. Microfibril formation: About 60-70 cellulose chains H-bonded together form a microfibril — a crystalline, cable-like structure with extraordinary tensile strength.

Microfibril: A bundle of approximately 60-70 parallel cellulose chains held together by inter-chain hydrogen bonds. Microfibrils provide tensile strength to plant cell walls and are the fundamental structural unit of cellulose.

Diagram to draw in your notes:

  1. Level 1: Individual cellulose chain (alternating flipped β-glucose units, β-1,4 bonds).
  2. Level 2: ~60-70 chains aligned in parallel, with dashed lines between chains representing H-bonds → form a microfibril (roughly rectangular cross-section, ~3-5 nm wide).
  3. Level 3: Multiple microfibrils bundled together → macrofibril.
  4. Level 4: Macrofibrils embedded in matrix of hemicellulose and pectin → plant cell wall.

Why is cellulose so strong? The tensile strength of cellulose microfibrils rivals that of steel (per unit cross-sectional area). This is because:

  • The β-1,4 bonds are covalent and strong.
  • The extensive inter-chain H-bond network creates a crystalline, highly ordered structure.
  • The straight chains allow maximum H-bonding (unlike the helical amylose chains, which cannot pack as tightly).

2. Drawing Condensation Reactions: α-1,4 vs β-1,4 Bond Formation

At HL, you must be able to draw the formation of glycosidic bonds, showing exactly which atoms are removed to form water.

2.1 General Condensation Mechanism

The reaction is a nucleophilic attack: the hydroxyl oxygen of one sugar (the nucleophile) attacks the anomeric carbon of the other sugar (the electrophile). The anomeric -OH leaves as part of a water molecule.

General equation:

$$\text{Sugar}_1\text{–OH} + \text{HO–Sugar}_2 \rightarrow \text{Sugar}_1\text{–O–Sugar}_2 + H_2O$$

The water is formed from the -OH on the anomeric carbon of Sugar₁ and the -H from the hydroxyl group of Sugar₂.

2.2 Drawing α-1,4 Bond Formation (Maltose)

Step-by-step mechanism for your notes:

  1. Draw two α-glucose molecules. On the left glucose, highlight the anomeric -OH on C-1 (pointing DOWN, α configuration). On the right glucose, highlight the -OH on C-4 (pointing DOWN for D-glucose).
  1. Show the anomeric -OH of the left glucose and the H of the C-4 -OH of the right glucose being removed together as $$H_2O$$.
  1. Draw the resulting glycosidic bond: an oxygen atom bridging C-1 (left) and C-4 (right). The C-1 -O- is still in the α orientation (the bond angles preserve the original stereochemistry).
  1. The product is maltose: α-D-glucopyranosyl-(1→4)-D-glucopyranose.

Key point: The anomeric carbon (C-1) of the left glucose is now locked in the α configuration by the glycosidic bond. The right glucose's C-1 is free and can mutarotate (interconvert between α and β in solution).

2.3 Drawing β-1,4 Bond Formation (Lactose / Cellulose)

The mechanism is identical, but the stereochemistry differs:

  1. Draw β-galactose (or β-glucose for cellulose) on the left with -OH on C-1 pointing UP.
  2. Draw glucose on the right with -OH on C-4.
  3. Remove the anomeric -OH (UP, β) and the H from the C-4 -OH as $$H_2O$$.
  4. The resulting bond is a β-1,4 glycosidic bond — the oxygen bridge maintains the β stereochemistry.

This is the critical distinction: the stereochemistry of the anomeric -OH (α = DOWN, β = UP) is preserved in the glycosidic bond and determines whether the resulting polymer is digestible (α) or indigestible (β) to humans.

Worked Example: Identifying Bond Types from a Diagram

A diagram shows two glucose rings linked by a glycosidic bond. The bond connects C-1 of the left ring to C-4 of the right ring. The C-1 of the left ring has the glycosidic oxygen pointing downward (below the ring plane). Identify the type of glycosidic bond and name a polysaccharide that contains this linkage.

Solution:

Step 1: Determine the anomeric configuration. The oxygen on C-1 of the left sugar points downward → this is the α configuration.

Step 2: Identify the carbons involved. The bond is between C-1 and C-4.

Step 3: Name the bond: α-1,4 glycosidic bond.

Step 4: Name a polysaccharide: Both amylose and amylopectin contain α-1,4 linkages. Amylose has α-1,4 exclusively; amylopectin has α-1,4 with α-1,6 branches.

Answer: α-1,4 glycosidic bond. Found in amylose (the unbranched component of starch) and as the backbone of amylopectin and glycogen.

3. Lipids: Fats, Oils, and Beyond

Lipids are a diverse group of hydrophobic (water-insoluble) biomolecules. Unlike carbohydrates, lipids are not defined by a common chemical structure but by their shared property of hydrophobicity. The major classes you must know for IB HL are: triglycerides, phospholipids, and steroids.

4. Triglycerides: The Storage Form of Fat

4.1 Structure: Glycerol + 3 Fatty Acids

A triglyceride (also called a triacylglycerol) consists of:

  • Glycerol: A 3-carbon alcohol. Each carbon bears an -OH group. Formula: $$C_3H_8O_3$$ or more informatively, HO–CH₂–CH(OH)–CH₂–OH.
  • Three fatty acids: Each is a long hydrocarbon chain (typically 14-22 carbons) with a carboxyl group (-COOH) at one end.

The bond: Each fatty acid is linked to glycerol by an ester bond. This is formed by a condensation reaction between the -COOH of the fatty acid and an -OH of glycerol:

$$\text{Fatty acid–COOH} + \text{HO–glycerol} \rightarrow \text{Fatty acid–COO–glycerol} + H_2O$$

For a triglyceride, this happens three times (one for each -OH of glycerol), releasing three water molecules.

Ester bond: A covalent bond formed by a condensation reaction between a carboxylic acid (-COOH) and an alcohol (-OH), releasing water. In triglycerides, ester bonds link each fatty acid to glycerol.

Triglyceride: A lipid composed of a glycerol backbone esterified to three fatty acid molecules. The primary form of fat storage in animals and oil storage in plants.

Diagram to draw in your notes:

  1. Glycerol backbone: three carbons in a row, each with an -OH.
  2. Attached to each glycerol carbon via an ester linkage (–COO–), draw a zigzag hydrocarbon chain (the fatty acid).
  3. Label: "Ester bond" with an arrow pointing to the –COO– linkage.

4.2 Saturated vs Unsaturated Fatty Acids

Fatty acids differ in the presence or absence of carbon-carbon double bonds (C=C) within the hydrocarbon chain.

#### Saturated Fatty Acids

  • No double bonds between carbon atoms — every carbon (except the carboxyl carbon) is bonded to the maximum possible number of hydrogen atoms (hence "saturated" with hydrogen).
  • The hydrocarbon chain is straight and can pack tightly with neighboring chains.
  • Solid at room temperature (e.g., butter, lard, the white fat on meat).
  • Primarily from animal sources (meat, dairy) and some tropical plant oils (coconut oil, palm oil).
  • High dietary intake is associated with increased risk of cardiovascular disease (elevates LDL cholesterol).

Analogy: Saturated fatty acid chains are like uncooked spaghetti sticks — straight and able to pack tightly together.

Example: Stearic acid, $$CH_3(CH_2)_{16}COOH$$ (18 carbons, no double bonds).

#### Unsaturated Fatty Acids

  • One or more C=C double bonds in the hydrocarbon chain.
  • Each double bond introduces a kink (bend) in the chain, preventing tight packing.
  • Liquid at room temperature (oils).
  • Primarily from plant sources (olive oil, sunflower oil, corn oil) and fish oils.

Analogy: Unsaturated fatty acid chains are like cooked spaghetti — bent, kinked, and unable to pack tightly.

Two sub-categories:

  • Monounsaturated: One C=C double bond (e.g., oleic acid in olive oil, 18:1).
  • Polyunsaturated: Two or more C=C double bonds (e.g., linoleic acid, 18:2; α-linolenic acid, 18:3).

4.3 Cis vs Trans Unsaturated Fatty Acids

The double bond in an unsaturated fatty acid can exist in one of two geometric isomers:

#### Cis Configuration

  • The two hydrogen atoms attached to the double-bonded carbons are on the same side of the double bond.
  • This creates a sharp kink (~30° bend) in the hydrocarbon chain.
  • Naturally occurring unsaturated fatty acids are almost exclusively cis.
  • The kink prevents tight packing → low melting point, liquid at room temperature.

#### Trans Configuration

  • The two hydrogen atoms are on opposite sides of the double bond.
  • The chain remains relatively straight (similar to saturated fatty acids) despite the double bond.
  • Rare in nature; produced industrially by partial hydrogenation of vegetable oils (used to make margarine, shortening, and processed foods).
  • Trans fats pack tightly → solid or semi-solid at room temperature.

Health implications of trans fats:

  • Raise LDL (low-density lipoprotein) cholesterol — "bad" cholesterol
  • Lower HDL (high-density lipoprotein) cholesterol — "good" cholesterol
  • This is a doubly harmful combination that significantly increases the risk of atherosclerosis, heart attack, and stroke
  • Saturated fats raise LDL but do not lower HDL — their effect is less harmful than trans fats

Cis fatty acid: An unsaturated fatty acid where the hydrogen atoms on the double-bonded carbons are on the same side of the double bond, producing a kinked chain. Naturally occurring in plant and fish oils.

Trans fatty acid: An unsaturated fatty acid where the hydrogen atoms are on opposite sides of the double bond, producing a relatively straight chain. Produced industrially by partial hydrogenation. Associated with increased cardiovascular risk.

Diagram to draw:

  1. Draw a zigzag hydrocarbon chain with a C=C double bond in the middle.
  2. For cis: both H atoms on the same side, chain kinked. Label: "Cis → kinked → liquid at RT."
  3. For trans: H atoms on opposite sides, chain straight. Label: "Trans → straight → solid at RT → raises LDL, lowers HDL."

IB Exam Tip: When comparing saturated and unsaturated fats, always connect structure to property to function:

  • Structure (no C=C vs C=C, straight vs kinked)
  • Property (solid vs liquid, melting point)
  • Biological/health consequence (membrane fluidity effects, cardiovascular risk)

Also, remember that "polyunsaturated" means multiple C=C bonds, and each additional double bond increases the number of kinks and further lowers the melting point.

5. Phospholipids: The Architects of Membranes

5.1 Structure

A phospholipid is structurally similar to a triglyceride, but with one crucial difference: instead of three fatty acids, it has two fatty acids and a phosphate group attached to glycerol.

The phosphate group is further linked to a polar head group (which can be choline, serine, ethanolamine, inositol, or simply a hydrogen atom).

Structure summary:

  • Glycerol backbone (3 carbons)
  • Two fatty acid tails (hydrophobic, nonpolar) — esterified to C-1 and C-2 of glycerol
  • Phosphate group attached to C-3 of glycerol (via a phosphoester bond)
  • Polar head group attached to the phosphate

5.2 Amphipathic Nature

Phospholipids are amphipathic — they have both hydrophilic (water-loving) and hydrophobic (water-fearing) regions in the same molecule:

  • Hydrophilic "head": The phosphate group and its attached polar group — charged and capable of forming H-bonds with water.
  • Hydrophobic "tails": The two fatty acid hydrocarbon chains — nonpolar and insoluble in water.

Amphipathic: Describing a molecule that contains both a hydrophilic (polar, water-soluble) region and a hydrophobic (nonpolar, water-insoluble) region. Phospholipids and bile salts are classic examples.

Analogy: A phospholipid is like a matchstick. The head (match head) is polar and interacts with water. The tail (wooden stick) is nonpolar and avoids water.

5.3 Spontaneous Bilayer Formation

When phospholipids are placed in an aqueous environment, their amphipathic nature drives them to self-assemble into structures where the hydrophobic tails are shielded from water and the hydrophilic heads are exposed to water.

The most stable arrangement is the lipid bilayer — two layers (leaflets) of phospholipids arranged tail-to-tail:

  • Outer leaflet: heads facing the external aqueous environment
  • Inner region: tails facing each other, forming a hydrophobic core
  • Inner leaflet: heads facing the internal (cytoplasmic) aqueous environment

This is a spontaneous, thermodynamically driven process. No energy input is required — the increase in entropy of water molecules (which would otherwise be ordered around exposed hydrophobic surfaces) drives bilayer formation.

Diagram to draw in your notes:

  1. A double row of phospholipids.
  2. Circles (heads) on top and bottom rows facing outward.
  3. Wavy lines (tails) facing inward, meeting in the middle.
  4. Label: "Hydrophilic heads face aqueous environment," "Hydrophobic tails form internal core," "Spontaneous assembly — entropically driven."

6. Steroids: Cholesterol and Its Relatives

6.1 Cholesterol Structure

Cholesterol is a steroid — a lipid characterized by a distinctive structure of four fused carbon rings (three 6-membered rings and one 5-membered ring).

Key structural features:

  • Steroid nucleus: The four-ring system (labeled A, B, C, D rings)
  • Hydroxyl group (-OH) on C-3 of the A ring → this is the only polar part of the molecule
  • Hydrocarbon tail attached to the D ring
  • The molecule is largely hydrophobic, but the single -OH gives it amphipathic character

Steroid: A lipid characterized by a carbon skeleton of four fused rings (three cyclohexane and one cyclopentane). Examples include cholesterol, testosterone, estrogen, cortisol, and vitamin D.

6.2 Cholesterol's Dual Role

#### Role 1: Membrane Fluidity Buffer

Cholesterol is inserted between phospholipids in animal cell membranes (up to ~50% of membrane lipid in some cells). Its rigid ring system and small polar head group allow it to modulate membrane fluidity:

  • At high temperatures: Cholesterol restrains phospholipid movement (its rigid rings prevent the fatty acid tails from moving too freely) → decreases fluidity, prevents the membrane from becoming too leaky.
  • At low temperatures: Cholesterol prevents the fatty acid tails from packing too tightly and crystallizing → increases fluidity, keeps the membrane functional in the cold.

This dual action makes cholesterol a fluidity buffer — it broadens the temperature range over which the membrane remains functional.

Analogy: Cholesterol is like the thermostat in your house — it keeps things from getting too hot (too fluid) or too cold (too rigid).

#### Role 2: Precursor to Steroid Hormones

Cholesterol is the biosynthetic precursor of all steroid hormones:

Hormone Type Function
Testosterone Androgen (male sex hormone) Development of male secondary sexual characteristics, muscle growth
Estrogen (estradiol) Estrogen (female sex hormone) Development of female secondary sexual characteristics, menstrual cycle regulation
Progesterone Progestogen Preparation of uterus for pregnancy, maintenance of pregnancy
Cortisol Glucocorticoid Stress response, glucose metabolism regulation, anti-inflammatory
Aldosterone Mineralocorticoid Regulation of sodium/potassium balance, blood pressure
Vitamin D (calcitriol) Secosteroid (ring B opened) Calcium absorption from diet, bone mineralization

All of these are synthesized from cholesterol by enzyme-catalyzed modifications (mostly in the adrenal cortex and gonads) that involve hydroxylation and side-chain cleavage.

7. Energy Storage: Lipids vs Carbohydrates

A critical comparison for IB HL: why do organisms use lipids for long-term energy storage rather than carbohydrates?

7.1 Energy Density

Lipids store approximately 2× more energy per gram than carbohydrates:

  • Carbohydrates: ~17 kJ/g (~4 kcal/g)
  • Lipids: ~38 kJ/g (~9 kcal/g)

Two reasons for the higher energy density of lipids:

  1. Lipids are more reduced: Fatty acid hydrocarbon chains are mostly -CH₂- groups (carbon in a relatively reduced state), whereas carbohydrates contain many C-OH groups (carbon more oxidized). The more reduced a carbon compound is, the more energy is released when it is fully oxidized to CO₂ during respiration.
  1. Lipids are stored without associated water: Carbohydrates are hydrophilic and are stored with approximately 2-3 grams of associated water per gram of carbohydrate. Lipids are hydrophobic — they are stored as nearly anhydrous (water-free) droplets. This means a gram of stored lipid represents nearly a gram of pure fuel, whereas a gram of stored carbohydrate (glycogen) represents only about 0.3-0.4 grams of actual fuel due to hydration.

7.2 Functional Comparison

Aspect Carbohydrates (Glycogen/Starch) Lipids (Triglycerides)
Energy per gram ~17 kJ/g ~38 kJ/g
Hydration Hydrated (~2-3 g water per g) Anhydrous (no water)
Accessibility Rapid mobilization (enzyme access at non-reducing ends) Slower mobilization (requires lipases, β-oxidation)
Osmotic effect Significant (many dissolved molecules) Minimal (stored as droplets)
Primary role Short-term energy, rapid response Long-term energy storage
Storage tissue Liver, muscle (glycogen) Adipose tissue (fat cells)
Water solubility Soluble (glycogen granules) Insoluble (stored as lipid droplets)

Analogy: Carbohydrates are the cash in your wallet — immediately accessible for quick purchases. Lipids are the money in your savings account — more of it, stored longer-term, but takes more effort to access.

Worked Example: Calculating Energy Yield

An athlete has 500 g of glycogen stored in her liver and muscles, and 5 kg of triglycerides stored in her adipose tissue. Calculate the total energy available from each storage form and compare.

Solution:

Glycogen:

  • Mass: 500 g
  • Energy density: ~17 kJ/g
  • Total energy: 500 × 17 = 8,500 kJ

Triglycerides:

  • Mass: 5 kg = 5,000 g
  • Energy density: ~38 kJ/g
  • Total energy: 5,000 × 38 = 190,000 kJ

Comparison:

  • Lipid energy ÷ Carbohydrate energy = 190,000 ÷ 8,500 ≈ 22.4× more energy from lipids
  • Even though fat mass is only 10× glycogen mass, the energy yield is over 22× greater due to the 2.2× energy density advantage of lipids combined with the larger absolute mass.

This is why a 70 kg human with ~15% body fat has enough lipid energy to survive weeks without food, but glycogen stores are depleted within ~24 hours of fasting.

Phospholipids are amphipathic — they possess a hydrophilic (polar) phosphate head group and two hydrophobic (nonpolar) fatty acid tails. When placed in water, the hydrophobic tails are thermodynamically unfavorable in contact with water (they disrupt the water's hydrogen bond network). To minimize this energetically unfavorable exposure, phospholipids spontaneously assemble into a bilayer: the hydrophilic heads face outward, interacting with water on both sides, while the hydrophobic tails are sequestered in the interior, away from water. This arrangement is thermodynamically spontaneous because it maximizes the entropy of water molecules and minimizes the exposed hydrophobic surface area.

Answer 4

Cholesterol intercalates between phospholipids in animal membranes. Its rigid, planar ring system acts as a fluidity buffer:

  • At high temperatures: Phospholipids have high kinetic energy and the membrane becomes excessively fluid. Cholesterol's rigid rings restrict phospholipid fatty acid tail movement, decreasing fluidity and preventing the membrane from becoming too permeable.
  • At low temperatures: Phospholipid tails slow down and tend to pack into a crystalline gel state. Cholesterol, with its bulky ring structure, prevents close packing of fatty acid tails, maintaining fluidity and preventing the membrane from solidifying.

Thus, cholesterol broadens the range of temperatures over which the membrane remains in a functional, fluid state.

Answer 5

Amylose:

  • Glycosidic bond: α-1,4
  • 3D conformation: The α-linkage produces a left-handed helix (~6 glucose residues per turn) because all glucose residues are oriented in the same direction and the bond angle induces coiling.
  • H-bonding: Primarily intra-chain H-bonds that stabilize the helix. Inter-chain H-bonding is limited because the helical shape prevents close, parallel packing of chains.
  • Biological role: Energy storage — the helix is accessible to amylase enzymes; the helical cavity can trap iodine (basis of the starch test).

Cellulose:

  • Glycosidic bond: β-1,4
  • 3D conformation: The β-linkage requires every other glucose to be flipped 180°, producing a straight, linear chain.
  • H-bonding: Extensive inter-chain H-bonds between -OH groups on adjacent parallel chains. This zips ~60-70 chains into crystalline microfibrils.
  • Biological role: Structural — the straight chains and inter-chain H-bonding give microfibrils enormous tensile strength for plant cell walls.

Answer 6

The triglyceride with the mixed fatty acids (one saturated + two unsaturated, both cis) would:

  • Have a lower melting point and be more likely liquid at room temperature, because the kinks introduced by the cis double bonds prevent the fatty acid chains from packing tightly together.
  • Be less viscous and more fluid.

The triglyceride with three saturated C18:0 (stearic acid) chains would:

  • Have a higher melting point and be solid at room temperature, because the three straight saturated chains can pack closely together, maximizing van der Waals interactions between chains.
  • The more tightly packed structure requires more thermal energy (higher temperature) to melt.

10. Common Misconceptions

Misconception Reality
"All lipids are fats" "Fat" usually refers to triglycerides. Lipids also include phospholipids, steroids, waxes, and other hydrophobic molecules.
"Saturated fats are always bad" Some saturated fat is essential (e.g., for hormone production). The health risk is associated with chronic excess, not any amount.
"Cholesterol is a harmful molecule" Cholesterol is essential — it is a membrane component and the precursor of all steroid hormones. The problem is excess LDL cholesterol in the blood.
"Phospholipids require energy to form bilayers" Bilayer formation is spontaneous — it is entropically driven and requires no ATP.
"Cellulose is just starch with a different bond" While chemically similar, the β-1,4 linkage produces a structurally and functionally distinct material — not just a harder-to-digest version of starch.
"Trans fats are the same as saturated fats" Trans fats are chemically unsaturated (have C=C) but behave similarly to saturated fats in packing. They are more harmful than saturated fats because they lower HDL in addition to raising LDL.

Cis isomer H atoms on same side of C=C; naturally occurring; kinked
Trans isomer H atoms on opposite sides of C=C; industrially produced; straight; harmful
Phospholipid Glycerol + 2 fatty acids + phosphate + polar head; amphipathic
Amphipathic Molecule with both hydrophilic and hydrophobic regions
Bilayer Two layers of phospholipids arranged tail-to-tail; basis of cell membranes
Cholesterol Steroid with 4 fused rings and -OH group; membrane fluidity buffer; hormone precursor
Microfibril Bundle of ~60-70 cellulose chains H-bonded together; high tensile strength
Polyiodide ions $$I_3^-$$, $$I_5^-$$ — linear ions that fit inside the amylose helix
