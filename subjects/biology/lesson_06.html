6.1 Drawing Cells from Electron Micrographs

In Paper 2 (and sometimes Paper 3), you may be asked to draw cells or organelles as seen in electron micrographs. This is a skill — it requires observation, not memorization.

Guidelines for Drawing from Micrographs

  1. Draw what you see, not what you think should be there. If the micrograph doesn't show ribosomes clearly, don't add them.
  2. Use a sharp pencil. IB drawings should be in pencil, not pen.
  3. Use clear, continuous lines. No sketchy, feathery lines.
  4. Correct proportions. The relative sizes of structures should be approximately accurate.
  5. Label with straight ruled lines. Labels should be written horizontally and lines should point precisely to the structure.
  6. Include a title and scale when given.
  7. No shading or coloring — use stippling (dots) if you need to distinguish regions.

Scale Bars and Magnification Calculations

This is a fundamental quantitative skill that appears regularly.

The formula you must know:

$$M = \frac{I}{A}$$

Where:

  • M = magnification (no units, written as ×500, ×10,000, etc.)
  • I = image size (what you measure on the micrograph)
  • A = actual size (the real size of the specimen)

Magnification (M): The number of times larger an image is than the actual object. M = I/A.

Scale bar: A line on a micrograph whose actual length is labeled, allowing calculation of magnification.

Worked Calculation: From Scale Bar to Magnification

Question: An electron micrograph of a mitochondrion has a scale bar that is 15 mm long, labeled "0.5 µm." Calculate:

a) The magnification of the image

b) The actual length of the mitochondrion if it measures 90 mm in the image

Solution:

a) M = I/A

I (scale bar) = 15 mm = 15,000 µm

A (scale bar) = 0.5 µm

M = 15,000 / 0.5 = 30,000

Magnification = ×30,000

b) A = I/M

A = 90 mm / 30,000 = 0.003 mm

Convert: 0.003 mm = 3 µm

Actual length = 3 µm

Common Unit Conversions You MUST Know

```

1 cm = 10 mm

1 mm = 1,000 µm

1 µm = 1,000 nm

1 nm = 10 Å (angstroms)

```

So: 1 mm = 10⁻³ m, 1 µm = 10⁻⁶ m, 1 nm = 10⁻⁹ m.

IB Exam Tip: Always convert units BEFORE calculating. The most common mistake is mixing mm and µm. Convert both image size and actual size to the SAME unit, then apply M = I/A. Show your working — you can get partial marks even with a calculation error.

6.2 Atypical Cell Structures: Challenging the "Typical Cell"

In Lesson 5, we described the "typical" animal and plant cell. But nature is full of cells that defy these generalizations. The IB syllabus explicitly requires you to know four examples of atypical cells — cells whose structure challenges the idea that "all cells are alike." These examples teach us that cell structure is adapted to function — a core principle of biology.

1. Striated Muscle Fiber

What makes it atypical: Striated (skeletal) muscle fibers are multinucleated — they contain many nuclei in a single, continuous cell. They are also extremely long — up to several centimeters in length (compared to the typical cell size of 10–100 µm).

How this structure arises: During embryonic development, multiple precursor cells called myoblasts fuse together, forming a single long cell (a syncytium) with many nuclei. The nuclei are located at the periphery (near the cell membrane) because the interior is packed with contractile proteins (actin and myosin).

Why this challenges the "typical cell":

  • Typical cells are uninucleate (one nucleus); striated muscle fibers are multinucleated.
  • Typical cells are microscopic; striated muscle fibers can be macroscopic (visible to the naked eye).

Functional significance: Multiple nuclei allow coordinated control of protein synthesis along the entire length of the very long cell. The elongated shape allows contraction over long distances — essential for muscle function.

Draw this: Draw a long, cylindrical cell with stripes (striations) running across it horizontally. Place several oval nuclei along the edges (periphery). Label: "Multiple peripheral nuclei," "Striations (actin + myosin bands)," "Single continuous cytoplasm (syncytium)."

2. Aseptate Fungal Hyphae

What makes it atypical: Most fungi grow as thread-like hyphae. In many fungi (especially in Phylum Zygomycota), the hyphae lack cross-walls called septa (singular: septum). These are aseptate (or coenocytic) hyphae — they consist of a continuous tube of cytoplasm containing many nuclei with no dividing cell walls.

How this structure arises: The hyphae grow by tip extension. Nuclei divide by mitosis, but the cytoplasm does not divide (no cytokinesis). The result is a multinucleated, continuous cytoplasmic tube.

Why this challenges the "typical cell":

  • No division into individual cells — it's one continuous cytoplasm with hundreds or thousands of nuclei.
  • There is no clear "one cell = one nucleus" relationship.

Functional significance: The continuous cytoplasm allows rapid transport of nutrients and organelles along the hypha. This is efficient for a saprophytic or parasitic lifestyle where rapid growth through a substrate (soil, decaying matter, host tissue) is advantageous.

Draw this: Draw a long, branching tube. No internal cross-walls. Scatter many dots (nuclei) throughout the tube. Label: "Continuous cytoplasm," "Multiple nuclei (no septa)," "Aseptate/coenocytic hypha."

Comparison: Septate hyphae (in Ascomycota and Basidiomycota) DO have cross-walls, but the septa have pores that allow some cytoplasmic continuity. Aseptate hyphae have no septa at all.

3. Phloem Sieve Tube Elements

What makes it atypical: Phloem sieve tube elements are plant cells that lose their nucleus at maturity. They are living cells but function without a nucleus. They are arranged end-to-end to form sieve tubes that transport organic compounds (mainly sucrose) throughout the plant.

How this structure arises: During differentiation, the sieve tube element loses its nucleus, vacuole, and most organelles. The end walls (sieve plates) develop pores to allow transport between adjacent elements.

Why this challenges the "typical cell":

  • A typical eukaryotic cell has a nucleus; sieve tube elements lose theirs.
  • They are "living" (metabolically active) without a nucleus — a fascinating exception.

Functional significance: Losing the nucleus and most organelles reduces resistance to flow, maximizing the efficiency of phloem transport. But the cell cannot survive alone — each sieve tube element is supported by a companion cell (a nucleated cell adjacent to it that provides metabolic support and regulates transport via numerous plasmodesmata connections).

Draw this: Draw a vertical stack of elongated cells. Each cell has a perforated end wall (sieve plate — like a sieve with holes). No nuclei inside. Next to the sieve tube, draw a smaller, nucleated cell labeled "Companion cell." Add plasmodesmata (small channels) connecting them. Label: "Sieve plate with pores," "Companion cell (provides nucleus-dependent functions)."

IB Exam Tip: If asked "Explain why phloem sieve tube elements are considered atypical," you must mention BOTH that they lack a nucleus AND that they are supported by companion cells. The companion cell is evidence that the sieve tube element cannot function independently.

4. Red Blood Cells (Erythrocytes)

What makes it atypical: Mammalian red blood cells lack a nucleus entirely at maturity. They also lack mitochondria and most other organelles. They are also biconcave in shape (concave on both sides — like a doughnut without the hole).

How this structure arises: During development in the bone marrow, erythroblasts (precursor cells) actively synthesize hemoglobin and then extrude their nucleus. They also degrade their mitochondria and other organelles.

Why this challenges the "typical cell":

  • No nucleus — cannot divide, cannot transcribe new genes, limited lifespan (~120 days).
  • No mitochondria — they rely entirely on anaerobic glycolysis for ATP.

Functional significance:

  • Maximizes hemoglobin capacity: Without a nucleus, more space is available for hemoglobin — each RBC carries ~270 million hemoglobin molecules.
  • Biconcave shape: Increases surface area-to-volume ratio for efficient gas exchange (O₂ and CO₂ diffusion). Also allows RBCs to deform and squeeze through narrow capillaries.
  • No mitochondria: Oxygen transported by hemoglobin is not consumed by the cell's own respiration — all O₂ is delivered to tissues.

Draw this: Draw a biconcave disc shape (side view: like a figure-8 cross section; top view: circle with a paler center). Label: "Biconcave shape (increased SA:V)," "No nucleus," "Packed with hemoglobin."

6.3 Ribosome Structure: 70S vs. 80S (HL Detail)

In Lesson 5, we introduced 70S (prokaryotic) and 80S (eukaryotic) ribosomes. Now we'll examine their structure in the detail expected for HL.

What Does "S" Mean?

The "S" stands for Svedberg unit — a measure of how fast a particle sediments (sinks) during ultracentrifugation. It depends on mass, shape, and density. S values are NOT additive: a 50S subunit + a 30S subunit = a 70S ribosome (not 80S), because the combined shape affects sedimentation differently than the sum of the parts.

70S Ribosomes (Prokaryotes, Mitochondria, Chloroplasts)

Feature Large subunit Small subunit Whole ribosome
Sedimentation 50S 30S 70S
rRNA molecules 23S rRNA + 5S rRNA 16S rRNA —
Proteins ~34 proteins ~21 proteins ~55 proteins

80S Ribosomes (Eukaryotic Cytoplasm)

Feature Large subunit Small subunit Whole ribosome
Sedimentation 60S 40S 80S
rRNA molecules 28S rRNA + 5.8S rRNA + 5S rRNA 18S rRNA —
Proteins ~49 proteins ~33 proteins ~82 proteins

Key Comparisons

Feature 70S 80S
Location Prokaryotes, mitochondria, chloroplasts Eukaryotic cytoplasm (free and RER-bound)
Size Smaller (~20 nm) Larger (~25 nm)
Antibiotic sensitivity Sensitive (target for streptomycin, tetracycline, chloramphenicol) Insensitive to these antibiotics
Subunits 50S + 30S 60S + 40S

The Functional Sites: A, P, and E Sites

Both 70S and 80S ribosomes have three binding sites for tRNA during translation. These are located at the interface between the large and small subunits:

Site Name Function
A site Aminoacyl site Binds the incoming tRNA carrying the next amino acid to be added
P site Peptidyl site Holds the tRNA carrying the growing polypeptide chain
E site Exit site Where the now-uncharged tRNA (after transferring its amino acid) exits the ribosome

During translation, tRNA molecules move through these sites in order: A → P → E. This is coupled with mRNA movement and peptide bond formation.

Draw this: Draw a simple ribosome as two overlapping shapes (large subunit on top, small on bottom). Between them, draw three slots labeled "E," "P," and "A" from left to right. Show a tRNA molecule in each site: E has an empty tRNA (no amino acid), P has a tRNA with a growing chain of amino acids, A has a tRNA with a single amino acid.

Ribosome: A macromolecular complex of rRNA and proteins that serves as the site of protein synthesis. It consists of a large and small subunit and contains three tRNA binding sites: A (aminoacyl), P (peptidyl), and E (exit).

6.4 The Cytoskeleton

The cytoskeleton is the internal scaffolding of eukaryotic cells — a dynamic network of protein fibers that provides shape, support, and movement. It's not a static skeleton like our bones; it's constantly being remodeled as the cell changes shape, divides, and moves.

Three Types of Cytoskeletal Fibers

Feature Microtubules Microfilaments Intermediate Filaments
Protein Tubulin (α and β dimers) Actin Various (keratin, vimentin, lamin, etc.)
Diameter ~25 nm (largest) ~7 nm (smallest) ~10 nm (intermediate)
Structure Hollow tube of 13 protofilaments Two intertwined actin chains Rope-like cable of coiled-coil fibers
Polarity Yes (plus and minus ends) Yes (plus and minus ends) No polarity
Dynamic? Yes (dynamic instability) Yes (treadmilling) Relatively stable
Motor proteins Dynein, kinesin Myosin None

Microtubules (25 nm)

Structure: Hollow cylinders built from α-tubulin and β-tubulin dimers. The dimers assemble head-to-tail into protofilaments; 13 protofilaments form a tube. Microtubules have polarity: the plus (+) end grows faster, the minus (−) end is anchored at the microtubule organizing center (MTOC) — the centrosome in animal cells.

Functions:

  1. Intracellular transport "tracks": Motor proteins (kinesin and dynein) walk along microtubules, carrying vesicles, organelles, and other cargo. Think of microtubules as railway tracks and motor proteins as trains.
  1. Spindle fibers during cell division: During mitosis and meiosis, microtubules form the mitotic spindle that separates chromosomes.
  1. Cilia and flagella: The core structure (axoneme) of eukaryotic cilia and flagella is a "9+2" arrangement of microtubules — nine doublets surrounding two central singlets. Dynein motor proteins between the doublets cause bending movement.
  1. Cell shape: Microtubules resist compression and help maintain cell shape.

Draw this: Draw a hollow tube made of small circles (tubulin dimers) arranged in a spiral. Label "Protofilament (13 per microtubule)," "α-tubulin / β-tubulin dimer," "Plus end (growing)," "Minus end (at MTOC)."

Microfilaments (7 nm)

Structure: Thin, flexible fibers made of actin (globular/G-actin polymerizes into filamentous/F-actin). Two F-actin chains twist around each other. Like microtubules, they have plus and minus ends.

Functions:

  1. Cell movement: Actin polymerization at the leading edge pushes the plasma membrane forward in crawling cells (e.g., white blood cells, amoebae).
  1. Cytokinesis: During animal cell division, a contractile ring of actin and myosin pinches the cell into two daughter cells.
  1. Muscle contraction: Actin filaments (thin filaments) interact with myosin (motor protein) to produce contraction in muscle cells.
  1. Cell shape and support: Actin filaments form a meshwork (cell cortex) just beneath the plasma membrane, providing mechanical support.
  1. Cytoplasmic streaming: Actin-myosin interactions drive the flow of cytoplasm (cyclosis) in plant cells.

Draw this: Draw two intertwined strands (like a twisted rope) made of small circles (G-actin). Label "F-actin (filamentous)," "G-actin monomers," "Plus end," "Minus end."

Intermediate Filaments (10 nm)

Structure: Rope-like fibers formed from fibrous protein subunits that coil around each other. Unlike microtubules and microfilaments, they have no polarity and are not dynamic — they are relatively stable and permanent.

Functions:

  1. Mechanical strength: Provide tensile strength so cells don't tear apart under stress.
  2. Anchoring: Connect to desmosomes (cell-cell junctions) and hemidesmosomes (cell-matrix junctions), distributing mechanical stress across tissues.
  3. Nuclear lamina: A meshwork of lamin proteins (a type of intermediate filament) lines the inside of the nuclear envelope, providing structural support and organizing chromatin.

Tissue-specific types:

  • Keratin — in epithelial cells (skin, hair, nails)
  • Vimentin — in connective tissue cells
  • Desmin — in muscle cells
  • Neurofilaments — in neurons
  • Lamins — in the nucleus of all animal cells

Draw this: Draw a thick, cable-like structure made of several intertwined coiled fibers. Label "Coiled-coil dimers → tetramers → filament," "No polarity," "Stable (not dynamic)."

6.5 Motor Proteins: Walking on Microtubules

Motor proteins are enzymes that convert chemical energy (from ATP hydrolysis) into mechanical work — movement along cytoskeletal fibers.

Kinesin

Structure: A dimeric protein with two "head" domains (motor domains) that bind microtubules, a coiled-coil stalk, and a tail that binds cargo (vesicles, organelles).

Direction: Kinesin moves toward the plus (+) end of microtubules — typically from the cell center toward the periphery (anterograde transport).

Mechanism: "Hand-over-hand" walking:

  1. One head binds ATP and attaches to the microtubule.
  2. ATP hydrolysis causes a conformational change that swings the other head forward.
  3. The second head binds the microtubule ahead, ATP binds, and the cycle repeats.
  4. Each step covers about 8 nm (one tubulin dimer).

Analogy: Like a person walking on two feet — one foot always stays on the ground while the other swings forward.

Dynein

Structure: A large multi-subunit complex with a motor domain quite different from kinesin's.

Direction: Dynein moves toward the minus (−) end of microtubules — typically from the periphery toward the cell center (retrograde transport).

Functions:

  • Retrograde transport of vesicles and organelles
  • Sliding of microtubule doublets in cilia and flagella (axonemal dynein)
  • Positioning of the Golgi apparatus near the centrosome

The "Highway System" of the Cell

Kinesin and dynein work together as a delivery and retrieval system:

  • Kinesin carries cargo outward (plus-end directed).
  • Dynein carries cargo inward (minus-end directed).
  • Both use ATP to power their movement.
  • Different vesicles carry different "address labels" (Rab proteins, SNAREs) that determine their destination.

Draw this: Draw a microtubule as a long line. Mark the minus end (left, at centrosome) and plus end (right, at cell periphery). Draw kinesin as a two-footed figure walking right (toward plus end) carrying a vesicle. Draw dynein as a different-shaped figure walking left (toward minus end) carrying a different vesicle. Label directions.

IB Exam Tip — HL: The directionality of motor proteins is a classic HL question. Memorize: Kinesin → Katabolic? No, better to remember: Kinesin moves toward the + (plus) end — both "K" and "+" can be remembered as "K+". Dynein moves toward the minus (−) end. Or: Kinesin = outward, Dynein = inward (dynein = "dyne-in").

Question 1 (Atypical Cells): Explain how the structure of aseptate fungal hyphae challenges the concept that cells are always uninucleate compartments. (3 marks)

Solution:

  1. Aseptate fungal hyphae lack septa (cross-walls), resulting in a continuous tube of cytoplasm containing many nuclei. ✓
  2. This challenges the typical concept of a cell as a discrete, uninucleate compartment — there are no cell boundaries dividing nuclei. ✓
  3. The multinucleated, continuous structure is adapted for rapid nutrient transport and growth through substrates. ✓

6.7 Practice Problems

Problem 1 (Paper 1 — Multiple Choice)

In a TEM image, a structure measures 60 mm at a magnification of ×30,000. What is its actual size?

A. 0.5 µm

B. 2 µm

C. 20 µm

D. 200 µm

Answer

B. A = I/M = 60 mm / 30,000 = 0.002 mm = 2 µm.

Problem 2 (Paper 1 — Multiple Choice)

Which motor protein moves toward the minus end of microtubules?

A. Kinesin

B. Dynein

C. Myosin

D. Actin

Answer

B. Dynein moves toward the minus end (retrograde transport). Kinesin moves toward the plus end. Myosin moves on actin, not microtubules.

Problem 3 (Paper 2 — 3 marks)

Explain why mammalian red blood cells are considered atypical eukaryotic cells.

Model Answer

  1. Mature mammalian erythrocytes lack a nucleus — they extrude it during development. ✓
  2. They also lack mitochondria and most other organelles. ✓
  3. These adaptations maximize hemoglobin capacity for oxygen transport; the biconcave shape increases surface area for diffusion. ✓

Problem 4 (Paper 2 — 5 marks)

Outline the structure and function of microtubules in eukaryotic cells.

Model Answer

  • Microtubules are hollow tubes (~25 nm diameter) made of α-tubulin and β-tubulin dimers arranged into 13 protofilaments. ✓
  • They have polarity (plus and minus ends) and exhibit dynamic instability. ✓
  • Functions: (a) Form spindle fibers that separate chromosomes during mitosis/meiosis. ✓
  • (b) Serve as tracks for intracellular transport by motor proteins kinesin (plus-end directed) and dynein (minus-end directed). ✓
  • (c) Form the axoneme (9+2 arrangement) of cilia and flagella, enabling cell motility. ✓
  • (d) Help maintain cell shape by resisting compression. ✓

Problem 5 (Paper 2 — 6 marks)

Compare and contrast the structure and function of 70S and 80S ribosomes.

Model Answer

Similarities:

  • Both are composed of rRNA and proteins. ✓
  • Both have a large and small subunit. ✓
  • Both have A, P, and E sites for tRNA binding during translation. ✓
  • Both are the site of protein synthesis (translation of mRNA into polypeptide). ✓

Differences:

  • 70S ribosomes are smaller and found in prokaryotes, mitochondria, and chloroplasts; 80S ribosomes are larger and found in eukaryotic cytoplasm. ✓
  • 70S subunits are 50S + 30S; 80S subunits are 60S + 40S. ✓
  • 70S ribosomes are sensitive to antibiotics like tetracycline and chloramphenicol; 80S ribosomes are not. ✓

6.8 Common Misconceptions

Misconception The Truth
"All multinucleated cells are abnormal." Striated muscle fibers and aseptate hyphae are perfectly normal, functional cells that are multinucleated by design.
"Red blood cells are dead because they have no nucleus." Mature RBCs are living cells — they carry out glycolysis and maintain ion gradients. They just have a limited lifespan (~120 days) because they can't replace degraded proteins.
"Phloem sieve tubes are dead like xylem." Sieve tube elements are living (though they lack nuclei). Xylem vessel elements are dead at maturity (lignified, hollow).
"Actin and myosin are only in muscle cells." Both are found in nearly all eukaryotic cells. In non-muscle cells, they drive cytokinesis, vesicle transport, and cell movement.
"The Svedberg unit (S) is additive." 50S + 30S = 70S (not 80S). S values depend on sedimentation rate, which is influenced by shape, not just mass.
"Microtubules, microfilaments, and IFs are all dynamic." Microtubules and microfilaments are highly dynamic. Intermediate filaments are relatively stable and permanent.
