1. The Central Dogma of Molecular Biology

Francis Crick proposed the central dogma in 1958: genetic information flows from DNA → RNA → protein. This is the fundamental principle of molecular biology.

DNA stores the information (the master blueprint).

RNA carries the information from the nucleus to the cytoplasm (the working copy).

Protein is the functional product — enzymes, structure, signalling, transport.

Analogy: DNA is like a reference book in a library that cannot be checked out. RNA is like a photocopy of the page you need — you can take it to your lab bench and use it without damaging the original.

Two key processes:

  1. Transcription: DNA → mRNA. Occurs in the nucleus (eukaryotes). The enzyme is RNA polymerase. The DNA sequence of a gene is copied into a complementary mRNA sequence.
  1. Translation: mRNA → polypeptide. Occurs in the cytoplasm on ribosomes. The mRNA sequence is decoded in triplets (codons) to build a chain of amino acids. tRNA molecules act as adaptors.

  • Nucleus: a DNA double helix with an arrow labelled "Transcription (RNA polymerase)" pointing to a single-stranded mRNA.
  • Nuclear pore: mRNA exiting the nucleus.
  • Cytoplasm: mRNA binding to a ribosome. Arrow labelled "Translation" pointing to a folded protein.
  • Add a dashed arrow labelled "Reverse transcription (retroviruses)" going from RNA → DNA to show this is an exception to the strict one-way flow.

Transcription: Synthesis of RNA using DNA as a template.

Translation: Synthesis of a polypeptide using the information encoded in mRNA.

Gene expression: The entire process by which the information in a gene is used to produce a functional product (protein or functional RNA).

2. Transcription: DNA → mRNA

2.1 The Three Steps

Step 1: Initiation

  • RNA polymerase binds to a specific DNA sequence called the promoter.
  • In eukaryotes, the promoter often contains a TATA box (consensus sequence TATAAA) about 25-30 base pairs upstream of the transcription start site.
  • RNA polymerase unwinds the DNA double helix locally (about 10-20 base pairs), creating a transcription bubble.
  • No primer is needed — unlike DNA polymerase, RNA polymerase can start de novo.

Step 2: Elongation

  • RNA polymerase reads the template strand (also called the antisense strand) in the 3' → 5' direction.
  • It synthesises the mRNA strand in the 5' → 3' direction, adding ribonucleoside triphosphates (ATP, UTP, GTP, CTP) complementary to the template.
  • Note: RNA uses uracil (U) instead of thymine (T). So A in DNA template → U in mRNA.
  • As RNA polymerase moves forward, the DNA re-winds behind it.

Step 3: Termination

  • In eukaryotes, RNA polymerase continues past the end of the gene. A specific sequence (often AAUAAA in the mRNA) signals for cleavage. The mRNA is cut free, and RNA polymerase eventually dissociates.
  • In prokaryotes, specific termination sequences (often a hairpin loop followed by a string of U's) cause RNA polymerase to fall off.

2.2 Strand Nomenclature — Important for IB

This causes confusion. Let's clarify:

Strand Name Also Called Role in Transcription
Template strand Antisense strand, non-coding strand Read by RNA polymerase (3'→5'). The mRNA is complementary to this strand.
Coding strand Sense strand, non-template strand NOT read by RNA polymerase. Its sequence is identical to the mRNA (except T→U). This is the strand that "looks like" the gene.

Analogy: If you want to photocopy a page of a book, you put the page you want to copy ("template") face-down on the glass. The copy that comes out looks exactly like the original page you wanted to preserve. The template strand is what the machine reads; the coding strand is what the final product resembles.

Transcription Practice

A section of the template strand of a gene reads: 3'-TAC GGA TCA CCT-5'.

(a) What is the sequence of the coding strand (5'→3')?

(b) What is the sequence of the mRNA produced (5'→3')?

Answers:

(a) Coding strand is complementary and antiparallel to the template:

Template: 3'-TAC GGA TCA CCT-5' → Coding: 5'-ATG CCT AGT GGA-3'

(b) mRNA is complementary to the template (with U replacing T):

Template: 3'-TAC GGA TCA CCT-5' → mRNA: 5'-AUG CCU AGU GGA-3'

Notice: the mRNA sequence is identical to the coding strand sequence, except T→U. (Coding: ATG CCT AGT GGA; mRNA: AUG CCU AGU GGA. ✓)

Paper 1 trick: Always check strand direction. If given 3'→5' template, the mRNA produced is 5'→3'. If the question asks "what is the mRNA sequence" and gives you the coding strand, just replace T with U — same sequence.

3. mRNA Processing in Eukaryotes

In prokaryotes, transcription and translation happen simultaneously — ribosomes start translating mRNA while it's still being transcribed. In eukaryotes, the mRNA undergoes three major processing steps in the nucleus before export to the cytoplasm.

3.1 5' Cap

Shortly after transcription begins, a modified guanine nucleotide — 7-methylguanosine — is added to the 5' end of the mRNA via a 5'-5' triphosphate linkage (an unusual bond).

Functions:

  • Protects the 5' end from degradation by exonucleases.
  • Helps ribosome binding during translation initiation — the small ribosomal subunit recognises the cap.
  • Facilitates export from the nucleus through nuclear pores.

Analogy: The 5' cap is like putting a protective plastic cover on the front of a report — it prevents the pages from getting damaged and signals "this document is ready to be read."

3.2 Poly-A Tail

At the 3' end, an enzyme called poly-A polymerase adds a string of 50-250 adenine nucleotides (the poly-A tail). This is NOT encoded in the DNA — it's added post-transcriptionally.

Functions:

  • Protects the 3' end from degradation by exonucleases.
  • Aids export from the nucleus.
  • Regulates mRNA stability: The longer the poly-A tail, the longer the mRNA persists in the cytoplasm. Gradual shortening of the tail eventually triggers mRNA degradation.

Analogy: The poly-A tail is like the fuse on a bomb — it gets shorter over time, and when it's gone, the mRNA is destroyed.

3.3 Splicing: Removing Introns

Eukaryotic genes contain introns (intervening sequences that don't code for protein) and exons (expressed sequences that do code for protein). Before translation, introns must be removed and exons joined together.

The Spliceosome:

  • A complex of snRNPs (small nuclear ribonucleoproteins, pronounced "snurps") and other proteins.
  • snRNPs contain snRNA (small nuclear RNA) that base-pairs with sequences at intron-exon boundaries.
  • The spliceosome cuts at the 5' splice site (GU at start of intron) and the 3' splice site (AG at end of intron), removes the intron in a lariat (loop) structure, and joins the exons together.
  • The spliced intron is degraded.

Draw and label: A diagram showing a gene with exons (coloured rectangles) and introns (lines between). Show: (a) The primary transcript (pre-mRNA) with both exons and introns. (b) Splicing: the spliceosome binding, intron forming a lariat and being cut out. (c) Mature mRNA with exons joined, 5' cap, and poly-A tail.

Intron: A non-coding sequence within a gene that is transcribed but removed during RNA splicing.

Exon: A coding sequence that is retained in the mature mRNA and contributes to the protein sequence.

Pre-mRNA: The primary transcript before processing — contains both introns and exons.

Mature mRNA: The processed transcript after capping, polyadenylation, and splicing — ready for translation.

3.4 Alternative Splicing

A single gene can produce multiple different proteins through alternative splicing — joining different combinations of exons. This is a major source of proteomic diversity.

Example: The Dscam gene in Drosophila (fruit flies) can produce over 38,000 different proteins through alternative splicing of 95 alternative exons. Humans have ~20,000 protein-coding genes but produce ~100,000+ different proteins — alternative splicing explains much of this discrepancy.

Analogy: Alternative splicing is like editing a film. You shoot many scenes (exons), but different versions of the movie can include or exclude different scenes, creating a director's cut, theatrical release, etc. — all from the same raw footage (gene).

Draw and label: A diagram showing one pre-mRNA with exons 1-2-3-4. Below: "Protein A" uses exons 1-2-3-4 (all exons). "Protein B" uses exons 1-2-4 (exon 3 skipped). "Protein C" uses exons 1-3-4 (exon 2 skipped). Three different proteins from one gene.

4. The Genetic Code

4.1 Structure of the Code

The genetic code is the set of rules by which nucleotide triplets (codons) specify amino acids.

  • 64 possible codons (4³ = 4 × 4 × 4).
  • 61 code for amino acids.
  • 3 stop codons: UAA, UAG, UGA (do not code for any amino acid — signal termination).
  • 1 start codon: AUG, which codes for methionine (Met). Every newly synthesised polypeptide begins with methionine (though it may be cleaved off later).

Draw and label: A codon table (the standard genetic code wheel or grid). Highlight: AUG (start), the three stop codons (UAA, UAG, UGA), and the degenerate codons for leucine (UUA, UUG, CUU, CUC, CUA, CUG — six codons!).

4.2 Key Properties

Property Explanation Example
Triplet code Three nucleotides = one codon = one amino acid AUG = Met, UUU = Phe
Non-overlapping Each nucleotide belongs to exactly one codon. Read sequentially, three at a time. AUG UUU GGC, not AUG UGU UUU
Comma-less No gaps or punctuation between codons. The ribosome reads continuously. No "spacer" nucleotides
Degenerate (redundant) Most amino acids are specified by more than one codon. Leucine = 6 codons; serine = 4 codons
Universal The same code is used by virtually all organisms (with minor exceptions in mitochondria and some protists). AUG = Met in humans, bacteria, plants
Unambiguous Each codon specifies exactly ONE amino acid. No codon codes for two different amino acids. UUU = Phe, always

Why is degeneracy useful? It provides a buffer against mutations. If the third base of a codon changes (a "silent" or synonymous mutation), it often still codes for the same amino acid due to wobble.

4.3 The Wobble Hypothesis (Crick, 1966)

The first two bases of a codon pair strictly with the anticodon (Watson-Crick pairing: A-U, G-C). The third base can "wobble" — meaning non-standard base pairing is allowed between the third codon position and the first anticodon position.

Why wobble exists: It allows fewer tRNA species to decode all 61 codons. In humans, only ~45 different tRNAs exist (instead of 61), because some tRNAs can recognise multiple codons via wobble. For example, a tRNA with anticodon 3'-AAG-5' can pair with both 5'-UUC-3' and 5'-UUU-3' (both code for Phe).

5. Translation: mRNA → Polypeptide

5.1 The Machinery

Ribosomes:

  • Composed of rRNA (ribosomal RNA) and proteins.
  • Prokaryotes: 70S ribosome = 50S (large) + 30S (small) subunits.
  • Eukaryotes: 80S ribosome = 60S (large) + 40S (small) subunits.
  • The ribosome has three tRNA binding sites:
  • A site (aminoacyl): Incoming tRNA with its amino acid enters here.
  • P site (peptidyl): Holds the tRNA with the growing polypeptide chain.
  • E site (exit): The now-empty tRNA exits from here.

Draw and label: A ribosome diagram showing the large and small subunits, the mRNA passing through, and the E, P, and A sites. Show:

  • A tRNA in the P site with a growing polypeptide.
  • A tRNA entering the A site carrying an amino acid.
  • E site with a tRNA about to exit.

tRNA (Transfer RNA):

  • Cloverleaf structure: tRNA folds into a characteristic shape with stems and loops due to intramolecular base pairing.
  • Anticodon: A triplet of three nucleotides on the anticodon loop that is complementary to the mRNA codon. This is how the right tRNA matches the right codon.
  • Amino acid attachment site: The 3' end of tRNA always has the sequence CCA. The amino acid is attached to the 3' adenosine via an ester bond.
  • Each tRNA is specific to one amino acid — it is "charged" by its specific aminoacyl-tRNA synthetase.

Aminoacyl-tRNA Synthetase:

  • This enzyme attaches the correct amino acid to the correct tRNA. There is one synthetase for each amino acid.
  • It has extremely high specificity — it recognises both the amino acid and the tRNA's structure (not just the anticodon).
  • This is sometimes called the "second genetic code" — because the accuracy of translation depends on this enzyme correctly pairing amino acids and tRNAs.

5.2 The Three Stages of Translation

#### Initiation

In eukaryotes:

  1. The small ribosomal subunit (40S) binds to the 5' cap of the mRNA.
  2. The initiator tRNA (tRNA^Met, carrying methionine) binds to the start codon (AUG) with the help of initiation factors.
  3. The small subunit scans the mRNA from 5' to 3' until it finds the AUG start codon (Kozak sequence context helps).
  4. The large ribosomal subunit (60S) joins, forming the complete 80S ribosome. The initiator tRNA^Met is now in the P site. The A site is ready for the next tRNA.
  5. GTP is hydrolysed to provide energy.

In prokaryotes:

  • The small subunit binds to the Shine-Dalgarno sequence (a purine-rich sequence upstream of AUG) in the mRNA — no 5' cap scanning like eukaryotes.

#### Elongation (Repeated Cycle)

Each cycle adds one amino acid and involves three steps:

1. Codon Recognition (A site entry):

  • A "charged" tRNA (with its amino acid) whose anticodon is complementary to the codon in the A site enters.
  • Elongation factors (EF-Tu in prokaryotes, eEF1 in eukaryotes) deliver the tRNA.
  • GTP is hydrolysed.

2. Peptide Bond Formation (Peptidyl Transferase):

  • The polypeptide chain attached to the tRNA in the P site is transferred to the amino acid on the tRNA in the A site.
  • A peptide bond forms between the carboxyl group of the P-site amino acid and the amino group of the A-site amino acid.
  • CRITICAL IB POINT: This reaction is catalysed by peptidyl transferase, which is NOT a protein enzyme — it is an activity of the 23S rRNA (in prokaryotes) or 28S rRNA (in eukaryotes) in the large ribosomal subunit. This is a ribozyme (catalytic RNA) — evidence for the RNA world hypothesis!

3. Translocation:

  • The ribosome moves (translocates) exactly one codon (three nucleotides) along the mRNA in the 5'→3' direction.
  • The tRNA that was in the P site (now empty) moves to the E site and exits.
  • The tRNA with the growing polypeptide (formerly in the A site) moves to the P site.
  • The A site is now empty and ready for the next codon.
  • Elongation factor G (EF-G, or eEF2 in eukaryotes) facilitates translocation.
  • GTP is hydrolysed.

Draw and label: A three-panel diagram showing one elongation cycle:

  • Panel 1: tRNA entering A site (codon recognition).
  • Panel 2: Peptide bond formation — the polypeptide chain from P-site tRNA now attached to A-site tRNA.
  • Panel 3: Translocation — ribosome shifted one codon, empty tRNA exiting E site, A site ready.

#### Termination

  1. A stop codon (UAA, UAG, or UGA) enters the A site.
  2. No tRNA recognises a stop codon. Instead, a release factor protein (eRF1 in eukaryotes, RF1/RF2 in prokaryotes) binds to the A site.
  3. The release factor triggers hydrolysis of the bond between the polypeptide and the tRNA in the P site.
  4. The polypeptide is released. The ribosomal subunits, mRNA, and remaining tRNA dissociate.

Summary of Translation

Stage Key Events Energy
Initiation Small subunit + initiator tRNA^Met bind 5' cap, scan to AUG, large subunit joins. tRNA^Met in P site. GTP
Elongation Cycle: codon recognition (A site) → peptide bond formation → translocation (ribosome moves one codon). Repeat. GTP per cycle
Termination Stop codon enters A site → release factor binds → polypeptide released → ribosome disassembles. GTP

Translation of a Sequence

Given the mRNA: 5'-AUG CCU AGU GGA UAA-3'

(a) Using the genetic code, determine the amino acid sequence.

(b) How many peptide bonds are formed?

(c) How many water molecules are released during the formation of this peptide?

Answers:

(a) AUG = Met (start), CCU = Pro, AGU = Ser, GGA = Gly, UAA = Stop.

Sequence: Met-Pro-Ser-Gly (4 amino acids).

(b) 4 amino acids → 3 peptide bonds (n amino acids = n−1 peptide bonds).

(c) Each peptide bond formation is a condensation reaction that releases one water molecule. 3 peptide bonds → 3 water molecules released.

6. Polysomes (Polyribosomes)

A single mRNA molecule can be translated by multiple ribosomes simultaneously. This structure — an mRNA with multiple ribosomes strung along it, each synthesising a polypeptide — is called a polysome or polyribosome.

Advantages:

  • Efficiency: Multiple copies of the protein are produced from a single mRNA in a short time.
  • Economy: The cell doesn't need to transcribe multiple copies of the gene — one mRNA serves many ribosomes.

Analogy: An mRNA is like a conveyor belt in a sushi restaurant. Multiple chefs (ribosomes) are stationed along the belt, each taking the same ingredients (codons) to assemble the same sushi (protein). The longer the belt, the more chefs can work simultaneously.

Draw and label: A polysome diagram: a long mRNA strand with multiple ribosomes (like beads on a string), each with a growing polypeptide emerging from it. The ribosomes closest to the 5' end have shorter polypeptides (just started); those near the 3' end have longer (almost finished).

7. Protein Sorting: Where Do Proteins Go?

Not all proteins end up in the cytoplasm. Cells must direct proteins to their correct destinations.

Free Ribosomes → Cytosolic Proteins

Proteins synthesised on free ribosomes (floating in the cytoplasm) are destined for:

  • The cytosol (e.g., metabolic enzymes, cytoskeletal proteins).
  • The nucleus (proteins with a nuclear localisation signal).
  • Mitochondria and chloroplasts (organelles with their own genomes, but most of their proteins are nuclear-encoded and imported post-translationally).
  • Peroxisomes.

RER-Bound Ribosomes → Secretory and Membrane Proteins

Proteins destined for secretion, the plasma membrane, lysosomes, or the endomembrane system are synthesised on ribosomes bound to the rough endoplasmic reticulum (RER).

The Signal Peptide Mechanism:

  1. Translation begins on a free ribosome in the cytoplasm.
  2. The first ~20 amino acids form a signal peptide (a short, hydrophobic sequence at the N-terminus).
  3. A Signal Recognition Particle (SRP) binds to the signal peptide and the ribosome, pausing translation.
  4. The SRP-ribosome complex docks onto an SRP receptor on the RER membrane.
  5. Translation resumes. As the polypeptide is synthesised, it is threaded through a translocon (protein channel) into the lumen of the RER.
  6. The signal peptide is usually cleaved off by a signal peptidase.
  7. Inside the RER, the protein folds, may be glycosylated (carbohydrates added), and is packaged into vesicles for transport to the Golgi.

Analogy: The signal peptide is like a postal code on an envelope. The SRP is the mail sorter that reads the code and directs the letter (ribosome + nascent protein) to the correct sorting facility (RER).

Common Misconceptions

Misconception Reality
"RNA polymerase needs a primer." RNA polymerase can start synthesis de novo. DNA polymerase needs a primer — RNA polymerase does not. Don't confuse them.
"The entire DNA strand is transcribed into mRNA." Only specific genes are transcribed. Vast regions of DNA (including intergenic regions, regulatory sequences, repetitive DNA) are not transcribed into mRNA.
"Every codon codes for one unique amino acid." The code is unambiguous — each codon codes for exactly one amino acid. But it's degenerate: multiple codons can code for the SAME amino acid.
"tRNA anticodons are identical to the mRNA codon." The anticodon is complementary and antiparallel to the codon. If codon is 5'-AUG-3', anticodon is 3'-UAC-5'.
"Introns are wasteful junk DNA." Introns enable alternative splicing, increasing protein diversity. They also contain regulatory sequences and may play roles in gene evolution (exon shuffling).
"All proteins start with methionine forever." The initiator methionine is often cleaved off post-translationally. Most mature proteins don't start with Met.
"Transcription and translation happen in the same place in eukaryotes." In eukaryotes: transcription in the nucleus, translation in the cytoplasm (separated by the nuclear membrane). In prokaryotes: they happen simultaneously in the cytoplasm.

Problem 5: Protein Sorting

(a) Describe the mechanism by which a protein destined for secretion is directed to the rough endoplasmic reticulum.

(c) A researcher mutates the signal peptide sequence of insulin (a secreted hormone). Predict the consequence and explain.

Answers

Answer 1

(a) Template strand is complementary and antiparallel to the coding strand:

Coding: 5'-ATG CGT TCA TAG GGC-3'

Template: 3'-TAC GCA AGT ATC CCG-5'

(b) mRNA is complementary to the template (with U instead of T), identical to the coding strand with T→U:

5'-AUG CGU UCA UAG GGC-3'

(c) Translation: AUG (Met/start) → CGU (Arg) → UCA (Ser) → UAG (STOP). Only 3 amino acids are incorporated (Met-Arg-Ser) before the stop codon. GGC is after the stop — it's not translated in this reading frame.

(d) Changing the 5th base (C→T) in the coding strand: the affected codon was originally CGT → becomes TGT. In mRNA, this is CGU → UGU. CGU = Arg, UGU = Cys. This is a missense mutation — a single amino acid change.

Answer 2

(a) 5' cap (7-methylguanosine): Protects the 5' end from exonuclease degradation; aids ribosome binding for translation initiation; facilitates nuclear export.

Poly-A tail: Protects the 3' end from degradation; regulates mRNA stability (longer tail = longer mRNA half-life); aids nuclear export.

Splicing: Removes introns (non-coding) and joins exons (coding) to produce a continuous open reading frame for translation. Without splicing, translation would produce a non-functional protein (introns contain stop codons and frameshifts).

(b) Exon 3 was skipped during alternative splicing — the spliceosome joined exon 2 directly to exon 4. This produces a different protein variant from the same gene. Biological significance: alternative splicing allows one gene to encode multiple proteins with potentially different functions, increasing the proteome's diversity without increasing gene number.

(c) Prokaryotes lack a nucleus — transcription and translation occur simultaneously in the cytoplasm. Prokaryotic genes generally lack introns (they are continuous coding sequences). There's no need for 5' capping, polyadenylation, or splicing because the mRNA is immediately translated and degraded. Additionally, prokaryotic ribosomes bind to the Shine-Dalgarno sequence rather than a 5' cap.

Answer 3

(a) AUG (Met/Start) → GGA (Gly) → UUA (Leu) → CAG (Gln) → UGA (STOP).

The sequence after UGA (CGC) is in the 3' untranslated region and is not translated.

Polypeptide: Met-Gly-Leu-Gln (4 amino acids).

(b) 4 tRNAs are used: one for each amino acid incorporated (Met, Gly, Leu, Gln — each requires a specific charged tRNA). The stop codon does not use a tRNA — a release factor binds instead.

(c) The second amino acid is Gly, codon GGA (5'→3'). The anticodon is complementary and antiparallel: 3'-CCU-5'.

(d) When UGA enters the A site, no tRNA recognises it (there are no tRNAs with anticodons complementary to stop codons). Instead, a release factor (eRF1) binds to the A site. This triggers the hydrolysis of the ester bond linking the polypeptide to the tRNA in the P site. The polypeptide is released, and the ribosomal subunits dissociate.

Answer 4

(a) Peptidyl transferase catalyses the formation of a peptide bond between the carboxyl group of the amino acid attached to the tRNA in the P site and the amino group of the amino acid attached to the tRNA in the A site. This transfers the growing polypeptide chain to the A-site tRNA, elongating it by one amino acid.

(b) The catalytic activity resides in the rRNA of the large ribosomal subunit (23S rRNA in prokaryotes, 28S rRNA in eukaryotes) — not in a protein. This makes the ribosome a ribozyme (an RNA molecule with catalytic activity). This is significant because it shows that RNA can serve both as genetic material AND as a catalyst, challenging the idea that only proteins can be enzymes.

(c) The RNA world hypothesis proposes that early life used RNA as both the genetic material and the catalyst for biochemical reactions (before DNA and proteins evolved). The fact that a fundamental, universally conserved process — protein synthesis — is catalysed by RNA (not protein) suggests that this mechanism originated in an RNA-based world. The ribosome is essentially a "living fossil" from the RNA world.

Answer 5

(a) Insulin is a secreted protein. Its mRNA is translated on free ribosomes. The first ~20 amino acids form a signal peptide (hydrophobic N-terminal sequence). The Signal Recognition Particle (SRP) binds to the signal peptide and pauses translation. The SRP-ribosome complex docks onto the SRP receptor on the RER membrane. Translation resumes through a translocon channel; the growing insulin polypeptide enters the RER lumen. The signal peptide is cleaved. Inside the RER, insulin folds and is packaged into vesicles → Golgi → secretory vesicles → exocytosis.

(b) Free ribosomes → proteins for the cytosol, nucleus, mitochondria, chloroplasts, peroxisomes. These proteins are released directly into the cytoplasm after synthesis.

RER-bound ribosomes → proteins for secretion, plasma membrane, lysosomes, and the endomembrane system (ER, Golgi). These proteins enter the ER lumen and travel through the secretory pathway.

(c) Without a functional signal peptide, the SRP cannot recognise the ribosome-nascent insulin complex. Translation would complete on a free ribosome in the cytoplasm. Insulin would be released into the cytosol instead of the ER lumen. It would not enter the secretory pathway, would not be secreted, and would likely be degraded. The patient would be insulin-deficient (a form of diabetes if this were a genetic mutation).
