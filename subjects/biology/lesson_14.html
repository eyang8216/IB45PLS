1. Why Proteins Matter

Proteins are the most structurally diverse and functionally versatile macromolecules in living organisms. They carry out virtually every task in the cell:

  • Enzymes catalyze chemical reactions (e.g., DNA polymerase, catalase)
  • Transport proteins carry molecules (e.g., hemoglobin carries O₂, albumin carries fatty acids)
  • Structural proteins provide support (e.g., collagen in connective tissue, keratin in hair and nails)
  • Hormones send signals (e.g., insulin regulates blood glucose)
  • Receptors receive signals (e.g., insulin receptor, neurotransmitter receptors)
  • Antibodies defend against pathogens
  • Motor proteins generate movement (e.g., actin and myosin in muscles)

All of this functional diversity arises from variations in the sequence and folding of just 20 building blocks: amino acids.

2. Amino Acids: The Building Blocks of Proteins

2.1 The General Structure

Every amino acid (except proline, which forms a ring) has the same core structure:

```

H

H₂N — C — COOH

R

```

The central carbon is called the α-carbon (alpha carbon). It is a chiral center — bonded to four different groups:

  1. Amino group (-NH₂): Basic; can accept a proton to become -NH₃⁺.
  2. Carboxyl group (-COOH): Acidic; can donate a proton to become -COO⁻.
  3. Hydrogen atom (-H): The smallest substituent.
  4. R-group (side chain): The variable group — this is what makes each amino acid unique.

α-carbon: The central carbon of an amino acid to which the amino group, carboxyl group, hydrogen, and R-group (side chain) are attached. It is chiral (except in glycine where R = H).

R-group (side chain): The variable chemical group attached to the α-carbon of an amino acid. The 20 different R-groups give the 20 standard amino acids their distinct chemical properties (size, polarity, charge, hydrophobicity).

2.2 Chirality and D/L Forms

Except for glycine (where R = H, so the α-carbon has two H atoms and is NOT chiral), all amino acids exist as two optical isomers (enantiomers): the L-form and the D-form.

In biological systems, only L-amino acids are incorporated into proteins. (D-amino acids do exist in nature — e.g., in bacterial cell walls — but are not used in protein synthesis by ribosomes.)

Analogy: L and D amino acids are like your left and right hands — they are mirror images that cannot be superimposed on each other (like gloves, they are chiral).

2.3 The 20 Standard Amino Acids: R-Group Classification

The 20 standard amino acids are grouped by the chemical properties of their R-groups:

#### 2.3.1 Nonpolar (Hydrophobic) R-Groups

These R-groups are composed primarily of hydrocarbons (C and H). They are hydrophobic (water-avoiding) and tend to cluster together in the interior of folded proteins, away from the aqueous environment.

These R-groups contain electronegative atoms (O, N) that can form hydrogen bonds with water, making them hydrophilic. They are often found on the surface of folded proteins.

IB Exam Tip: You do NOT need to memorize all 20 amino acid structures. However, you SHOULD know:

  • The general structure (α-carbon, amino, carboxyl, H, R)
  • That R-groups can be hydrophobic, polar, acidic (-), or basic (+)
  • That cysteine can form disulfide bonds, glycine is the smallest, and proline creates kinks
  • The classification system — you should be able to look at an R-group and classify it

2.4 The Zwitterion: Amino Acids at Physiological pH

At physiological pH (~7.4), the amino group (-NH₂) is protonated to -NH₃⁺ (it acts as a base, accepting H⁺), and the carboxyl group (-COOH) is deprotonated to -COO⁻ (it acts as an acid, donating H⁺).

The result: an amino acid at pH 7 carries both a positive charge (on the amino group) and a negative charge (on the carboxyl group). This dual-charged species is called a zwitterion (from German zwitter, meaning "hybrid" or "hermaphrodite").

Zwitterion: A molecule that carries both a positive and a negative charge but is electrically neutral overall. Amino acids exist predominantly as zwitterions at physiological pH (~7.4), with -NH₃⁺ and -COO⁻ groups.

Diagram to draw:

```

H

⁺H₃N — C — COO⁻

R

```

Label: "Zwitterion form at pH 7.4 — net charge = 0, but has separated + and - charges."

Significance: The zwitterionic nature of amino acids (and proteins) allows them to act as buffers, resisting changes in pH. This is why proteins are effective biological buffers.

2.5 Essential vs Non-Essential Amino Acids

Of the 20 standard amino acids, the human body can synthesize some (non-essential) but must obtain others from the diet (essential).

  • Essential amino acids (9): Histidine, Isoleucine, Leucine, Lysine, Methionine, Phenylalanine, Threonine, Tryptophan, Valine. (Mnemonic: "His Is Lying Low Making People Think There's Value" — H, I, L, L, M, P, T, T, V)
  • Non-essential amino acids (11): The remaining ones, which the body can synthesize from metabolic intermediates.

Note: Some amino acids are conditionally essential — they become essential under certain conditions (e.g., arginine during growth, cysteine if methionine intake is low, tyrosine if phenylalanine intake is low).

3. Peptide Bonds: Linking Amino Acids Together

3.1 The Condensation Reaction

A peptide bond is a covalent bond formed between the carboxyl group (-COOH) of one amino acid and the amino group (-NH₂) of another. Like glycosidic bond formation, this is a condensation reaction — a water molecule is removed.

$$\text{AA}_1\text{–COOH} + \text{H}_2\text{N–AA}_2 \rightarrow \text{AA}_1\text{–CO–NH–AA}_2 + H_2O$$

The resulting –CO–NH– linkage is the peptide bond.

Peptide bond: A covalent amide bond (–CO–NH–) formed by a condensation reaction between the α-carboxyl group of one amino acid and the α-amino group of another. Peptide bonds link amino acids together in proteins.

Diagram to draw:

  1. Draw amino acid 1 (left): H₃N⁺–CHR₁–COO⁻.
  2. Draw amino acid 2 (right): H₃N⁺–CHR₂–COO⁻.
  3. Show the -OH from the carboxyl of AA₁ and -H from the amino of AA₂ being removed as H₂O.
  4. Draw the result: AA₁ and AA₂ joined by the peptide bond: –C(=O)–NH–.
  5. Label: "Peptide bond (amide linkage)," "Water released (condensation)."

3.2 Dipeptides, Oligopeptides, Polypeptides

  • Dipeptide: Two amino acids linked by one peptide bond.
  • Tripeptide: Three amino acids linked by two peptide bonds.
  • Oligopeptide: A short chain of amino acids (typically < 20-30 residues).
  • Polypeptide: A long chain of amino acids. A protein may consist of one or more polypeptide chains.

3.3 Directionality: N-terminus to C-terminus

Every polypeptide chain has a direction (polarity):

  • N-terminus (amino terminus): The end with a free α-amino group (-NH₃⁺).
  • C-terminus (carboxyl terminus): The end with a free α-carboxyl group (-COO⁻).

By convention, amino acid sequences are always written from N-terminus to C-terminus (left to right). This directionality is critical because it reflects the order in which the ribosome synthesizes the protein (N → C).

Example: The dipeptide alanine-glycine:

  • N-terminus: Alanine (free -NH₃⁺)
  • Peptide bond: Ala-CO-NH-Gly
  • C-terminus: Glycine (free -COO⁻)
  • Written as: Ala-Gly or AG

4. Levels of Protein Structure

Protein structure is described at four hierarchical levels. Each level builds upon the previous one.

4.1 Primary Structure (1°): The Amino Acid Sequence

The primary structure is the linear sequence of amino acids in the polypeptide chain, held together by covalent peptide bonds.

Key points:

  • Determined by the DNA sequence of the gene encoding the protein (via transcription and translation).
  • The sequence is unique for each protein.
  • A change of even one amino acid can have profound consequences (e.g., sickle cell anemia: glutamate → valine at position 6 of the β-globin chain).
  • The primary structure ultimately determines all higher levels of structure (as demonstrated by Anfinsen's experiment — see Lesson 15).

Analogy: The primary structure is like the order of letters in a word. Change one letter ("cat" → "bat") and the meaning changes entirely.

Primary structure (1°): The linear sequence of amino acids in a polypeptide chain, held together by peptide bonds. The sequence is determined by the gene's nucleotide sequence and dictates all higher-order folding.

4.2 Secondary Structure (2°): Local Folding Patterns

Secondary structure refers to regular, repeating local conformations of the polypeptide backbone, stabilized primarily by hydrogen bonds between the backbone carbonyl (C=O) and amide (N-H) groups.

The two main types are the α-helix and the β-pleated sheet.

#### 4.2.1 The α-Helix

The α-helix is a right-handed coil (spiral) where the polypeptide backbone forms the inner core and the R-groups project outward.

Key structural features (memorize these numbers):

  • Right-handed helix (clockwise spiral as you look down the axis)
  • 3.6 amino acid residues per complete turn of the helix
  • Hydrogen bonds form between the C=O of residue n and the N-H of residue n + 4 (i.e., 4 residues ahead in the sequence)
  • The H-bonds are parallel to the helix axis — they stabilize the coil
  • R-groups project outward from the helix, minimizing steric clashes
  • The pitch (rise per turn) is 0.54 nm (5.4 Å)

Which amino acids favor/disfavor α-helices?

  • Alanine, leucine, methionine: Strong helix formers
  • Proline: Helix breaker — its ring structure restricts backbone rotation and its N lacks an H for H-bonding
  • Glycine: Too flexible (too much conformational freedom) → rarely found in α-helices
  • Bulky or charged residues near each other: Can destabilize via steric or electrostatic clashes

Diagram to draw in your notes:

  1. A spiral ribbon representing the polypeptide backbone.
  2. Dashed lines between the C=O of residue n and H-N of residue n+4 → label "H-bonds parallel to axis."
  3. R-groups sticking outward from the spiral.
  4. Label: "3.6 residues/turn," "Right-handed," "R-groups project outward."

#### 4.2.2 The β-Pleated Sheet

In a β-pleated sheet, the polypeptide backbone is extended (nearly fully stretched) in a zigzag conformation. Multiple strands (segments of the polypeptide chain) align side by side, and hydrogen bonds form between C=O and N-H of adjacent strands.

Key features:

  • The backbone zigzags like a folded fan — hence "pleated"
  • R-groups alternate above and below the plane of the sheet
  • Strands can run in the same direction (parallel) or opposite directions (antiparallel)
  • Antiparallel sheets have more linear (stronger) H-bonds; parallel sheets have angled (slightly weaker) H-bonds
  • The distance between adjacent strands is ~0.35 nm (for antiparallel) to ~0.325 nm (for parallel)

Parallel vs Antiparallel:

  • Parallel: Adjacent strands run N→C in the same direction. H-bonds are angled (diagonal).
  • Antiparallel: Adjacent strands run N→C in opposite directions. H-bonds are linear (straight), making antiparallel sheets slightly more stable.

Diagram to draw:

  1. Two or three zigzag ribbons running side by side.
  2. Arrows on the ribbons indicating N→C direction.
  3. Dashed lines between strands showing H-bonds between C=O and N-H.
  4. R-groups pointing alternately above and below.
  5. Label: "Parallel" or "Antiparallel."

α-helix: A right-handed coiled secondary structure stabilized by H-bonds between C=O of residue n and N-H of residue n+4, with 3.6 residues per turn.

β-pleated sheet: An extended, zigzag secondary structure where adjacent polypeptide strands (parallel or antiparallel) are stabilized by inter-strand H-bonds between backbone C=O and N-H groups.

4.3 Tertiary Structure (3°): The Overall 3D Shape

Tertiary structure is the complete three-dimensional folding of a single polypeptide chain — how the α-helices, β-sheets, and loop regions are arranged in space to form the final, functional protein shape.

Tertiary structure is stabilized by five types of interactions between R-groups:

#### 1. Hydrophobic Interactions (Most Important)

Nonpolar (hydrophobic) R-groups cluster together in the interior of the protein, away from the aqueous environment. This is NOT a true "bond" — it is an entropically driven effect. Water molecules around exposed hydrophobic groups are highly ordered (low entropy). By burying these groups in the protein core, water molecules are released and entropy increases. This is the hydrophobic effect — the dominant force driving protein folding.

#### 2. Hydrogen Bonds

Form between polar R-groups (e.g., -OH of serine, -OH of tyrosine, -NH₂ of asparagine). These are individually weak (~4-30 kJ/mol) but numerous and collectively significant.

#### 3. Ionic Bonds (Salt Bridges)

Form between positively charged R-groups (Lys⁺, Arg⁺, His⁺) and negatively charged R-groups (Asp⁻, Glu⁻). These are electrostatic attractions and can be quite strong, especially in the hydrophobic protein interior (where there is no water to shield the charges).

#### 4. Disulfide Bridges (Covalent)

A covalent S–S bond forms between the thiol (-SH) groups of two cysteine residues. This is the strongest type of tertiary interaction — it is a true covalent bond.

$$R\text{–SH} + HS\text{–R} \xrightarrow{\text{oxidation}} R\text{–S–S–R} + 2H^+ + 2e^-$$

Disulfide bridges are especially important in:

  • Secreted proteins (e.g., insulin, antibodies) — they stabilize proteins in the harsh extracellular environment
  • Proteins exposed to mechanical stress (e.g., keratin in hair and nails — rich in cysteine)

#### 5. Van der Waals Interactions

Weak, transient attractions between all atoms in close proximity. Individually tiny, but when thousands of atoms pack tightly together, the cumulative effect is significant.

Summary diagram to draw:

Draw a folded protein with the following interactions labeled in different colors:

  • Hydrophobic core (grey cluster in the middle)
  • H-bonds (dashed blue lines between polar R-groups)
  • Ionic bonds (red +/- between charged R-groups)
  • Disulfide bridge (thick yellow S–S bond between two cysteines)
  • Van der Waals (small dots throughout the closely packed interior)

Tertiary structure (3°): The complete three-dimensional folding of a single polypeptide chain, stabilized by hydrophobic interactions, hydrogen bonds, ionic bonds, disulfide bridges, and van der Waals forces between R-groups.

4.4 The Importance of Environment for Proper Folding

Proteins fold correctly only under appropriate conditions:

#### Temperature

  • Moderate heat increases kinetic energy and can disrupt the weak non-covalent interactions (H-bonds, hydrophobic interactions) that maintain tertiary structure.
  • Excessive heat causes denaturation — the protein unfolds and loses its biological function.
  • Each protein has a characteristic melting temperature (Tm) where 50% is denatured.
  • Cold generally does not denature proteins but slows folding and enzymatic activity.

#### pH

  • Changes in pH alter the ionization state of R-groups (especially Asp, Glu, His, Lys, Arg).
  • This can disrupt ionic bonds (salt bridges) critical for tertiary structure.
  • Extreme pH can also affect H-bonding patterns.

Example: The stomach enzyme pepsin functions optimally at pH ~2 (very acidic), while trypsin (small intestine) functions at pH ~8. If you swap their environments, both denature — the ionic bonds that stabilize their tertiary structures are designed for their specific pH.

5. Worked Examples

Worked Example 1: Peptide Bond Counting

A polypeptide contains 127 amino acid residues. How many peptide bonds does it contain? How many water molecules were released during its synthesis?

Solution:

For a linear polypeptide of n amino acids:

  • Number of peptide bonds = n - 1
  • Number of water molecules released = n - 1 (one H₂O per condensation reaction)

For n = 127:

  • Peptide bonds = 127 - 1 = 126
  • Water molecules released = 126

Worked Example 2: Classifying Amino Acid R-Groups

Classify the following amino acids based on their R-group properties at pH 7: glutamate, lysine, phenylalanine, serine.

Solution:

Amino Acid R-Group Property Reason
Glutamate (Glu, E) Charged (acidic, negative) R-group is -CH₂CH₂COO⁻ — deprotonated carboxyl at pH 7
Lysine (Lys, K) Charged (basic, positive) R-group is -(CH₂)₄NH₃⁺ — protonated amino group at pH 7
Phenylalanine (Phe, F) Nonpolar (hydrophobic) R-group is -CH₂-benzene — pure hydrocarbon, no polar atoms
Serine (Ser, S) Polar (uncharged) R-group is -CH₂OH — can H-bond but not ionized at pH 7

Worked Example 3: α-Helix H-Bonding

In an α-helix, the C=O of residue 15 forms a hydrogen bond with the N-H of which residue? The C=O of residue 38 forms an H-bond with which residue?

Solution:

In an α-helix, H-bonds form between C=O of residue n and N-H of residue n + 4.

  • For residue 15: n = 15, so n + 4 = 19. The C=O of residue 15 bonds with N-H of residue 19.
  • For residue 38: n = 38, so n + 4 = 42. The C=O of residue 38 bonds with N-H of residue 42.

Note: At the ends of the helix, some C=O and N-H groups are not H-bonded — these "fray" at the termini. This is why the first four N-H groups and last four C=O groups in an α-helix lack H-bonding partners within the helix.

Answer 4

Proline as a helix breaker:

  • Proline's R-group forms a covalent ring with the backbone nitrogen, creating a rigid, cyclic structure.
  • The backbone N of proline lacks a hydrogen atom (it's part of the ring), so it cannot donate an H-bond to stabilize the α-helix.
  • The ring also restricts the phi (φ) dihedral angle of the backbone to approximately -60°, which is incompatible with the α-helix geometry (φ ≈ -57°).
  • Result: proline introduces a kink or turn, disrupting the regular α-helical pattern.

Glycine rarely found in α-helices:

  • Glycine's R-group is just -H (the smallest possible side chain).
  • This gives glycine far more conformational flexibility than any other amino acid — it can adopt angles that are sterically forbidden for other residues.
  • This flexibility is entropically unfavorable for helix formation: folding glycine into the constrained α-helix conformation costs more entropy than other residues. Glycine is thermodynamically "expensive" to put in an α-helix.

8. Common Misconceptions

Misconception Reality
"Peptide bond formation requires energy from ATP" In protein synthesis, ATP and GTP are used to charge tRNAs and power ribosome movement, but the condensation reaction itself is catalyzed by the ribosome's peptidyl transferase center (an RNA enzyme — a ribozyme).
"The R-group determines everything about an amino acid" The R-group determines the chemical properties, but the backbone α-carbon, amino, and carboxyl groups are identical (except proline) and form the peptide bond.
"Secondary structure is held together by R-group interactions" Secondary structure H-bonds are between backbone C=O and N-H, not R-groups. R-group interactions define tertiary structure.
"Disulfide bonds form spontaneously in the cytoplasm" The cytoplasm is a reducing environment (high glutathione), so disulfide bonds rarely form there. They are primarily found in secreted and extracellular proteins.
"All parts of a protein are α-helix or β-sheet" Many proteins have significant loop and coil regions connecting structured elements. These are not "random" — they are ordered but not repetitive like helices and sheets.
"Hydrophobic interactions are true bonds" The term "hydrophobic interaction" is misleading — it's not a bond but an entropic effect driven by the release of ordered water molecules.
