1. Why Organisms Look Different: The Two Sources of Variation

Walk through any forest or look at a litter of puppies, and one thing is immediately obvious: no two organisms are exactly alike. In IB Biology, we classify all this variation into two fundamental categories — and being able to distinguish them is a classic Paper 1 and Paper 2 skill.

1.1 Genetic Variation: The Blueprint Changes

Genetic variation refers to differences between individuals that are written into their DNA — their hereditary material. Because DNA is passed from parent to offspring, genetic variation is heritable. If a trait is genetic, an organism's offspring can inherit it.

Three processes generate genetic variation:

1. Mutation — A change in the DNA sequence itself. Mutations are the ultimate source of all genetic novelty. Most mutations are neutral or harmful, but occasionally one produces a beneficial change. For example, a single nucleotide substitution in the hemoglobin gene causes sickle-cell disease — harmful in most contexts, but protective against malaria in heterozygous individuals (a classic IB example).

2. Meiosis — The specialized cell division that produces gametes (sperm and eggs). During meiosis, two processes shuffle the genetic deck:

  • Crossing over (prophase I): Homologous chromosomes exchange segments of DNA, creating chromosomes with new combinations of alleles.
  • Independent assortment (metaphase I): When homologous pairs line up at the equator, the maternal and paternal chromosomes of each pair orient randomly. With 23 pairs in humans, this produces 2²³ ≈ 8.4 million possible chromosome combinations per gamete — and that's before crossing over.

3. Sexual reproduction — When two gametes fuse during fertilization, they combine two genetically unique haploid genomes into a new diploid individual. The number of possible offspring genotypes from two human parents exceeds the number of atoms in the observable universe. This is why siblings (except identical twins) are genetically unique.

Genetic variation: Differences between individuals caused by differences in their DNA sequences. These differences are heritable — they can be passed to offspring. Sources include mutation, meiosis (crossing over and independent assortment), and sexual reproduction (fusion of genetically distinct gametes).

1.2 Environmental Variation: The Same Blueprint, Different Expression

Environmental variation refers to differences between individuals that arise from external factors, not from differences in DNA. These traits are not heritable — an organism does not pass its environmentally acquired characteristics to its offspring.

Common environmental factors that cause variation:

Factor Examples
Nutrition Body mass, height (partially), muscle development
Climate/Temperature Fur thickness in mammals, leaf size in plants
Light Skin pigmentation (tanning), plant etiolation (tall, pale growth in darkness)
pH Hydrangea flower color (blue in acidic soil, pink in alkaline)
Water availability Plant height, leaf succulence
Disease/Injury Scarring, stunted growth from childhood illness

Environmental variation: Differences between individuals caused by environmental factors (nutrition, climate, light, etc.). These differences are not heritable — they cannot be passed to offspring through genes.

1.3 The Reality: Most Traits Are Multifactorial

Here's a truth that IB examiners love: very few traits are purely genetic or purely environmental. Most characteristics arise from a combination of both, and this is called multifactorial inheritance.

Human height is the classic example. You inherit a genetic potential for height, but whether you reach that potential depends on nutrition during childhood, illness history, and other environmental factors. Identical twins raised in different nutritional environments often reach different adult heights despite having identical DNA.

Skin color is another — your genotype sets a baseline melanin production capacity, but UV exposure (environment) increases melanin production (tanning).

IB Exam Tip: When asked to determine whether variation is genetic or environmental, ask yourself: "Would this trait be passed on to offspring?" If yes → genetic. If no → environmental. If partially → multifactorial. On Paper 2, you might be given data from twin studies or breeding experiments and asked to draw conclusions. Identical twins raised apart are the gold standard — differences between them are environmental, since their DNA is identical.

1.4 How Scientists Disentangle Genetic from Environmental Causes

Scientists use several approaches:

  1. Twin studies: Compare identical twins (monozygotic — 100% shared DNA) with fraternal twins (dizygotic — ~50% shared DNA). If identical twins are more similar for a trait than fraternal twins, genetics plays a role.
  2. Common garden experiments: Grow organisms from genetically diverse populations in identical environments. Any remaining variation must be genetic.
  3. Reciprocal transplant experiments: Move organisms between different environments. If their phenotype changes to match the new environment, the trait has environmental plasticity.

2. The Binomial Naming System: Every Species Gets a Two-Word Name

Imagine trying to talk about a mountain lion. In different regions, the same animal is called a cougar, puma, panther, or catamount. Worse, "panther" might also refer to a leopard or jaguar. Common names are a disaster for scientific communication. Enter the binomial system.

2.1 History and Principles

Developed by Carl Linnaeus in the 18th century (Systema Naturae, 1735), the binomial system gives every species a unique, universal, two-word Latin name. Latin was chosen because it was the universal scholarly language of Linnaeus's time and — critically — it is a "dead" language that doesn't evolve, so names remain stable.

The rules:

  1. First word = Genus (plural: genera). Always capitalized.
  2. Second word = Species (the "specific epithet"). Always lowercase.
  3. Both words are italicized when typed (or underlined when handwritten).
  4. After the first use, the genus may be abbreviated to its first letter: H. sapiens.

Binomial nomenclature: A system of naming species using two Latin words — the genus name (capitalized) and the species epithet (lowercase). Both are italicized in print or underlined in handwriting. Example: Homo sapiens (humans).

2.2 Worked Examples

Common Name Binomial Name Genus Species Epithet Meaning
Human Homo sapiens Homo sapiens "Wise man"
Lion Panthera leo Panthera leo "Lion" (genus includes tigers, leopards, jaguars)
Wolf Canis lupus Canis lupus "Wolf" (genus includes dogs and coyotes)
Domestic dog Canis familiaris Canis familiaris "Familiar dog" — same genus as wolf!
Maize/Corn Zea mays Zea mays Ancient name for cereal grain
E. coli Escherichia coli Escherichia coli Named after Theodor Escherich; found in colon

Pattern: Notice that closely related species share a genus — the wolf and dog are both Canis, reflecting their close evolutionary relationship and ability to interbreed.

2.3 The Hierarchy Implied by a Name

A binomial name isn't just a label — it tells you about evolutionary relationships. All species in the same genus are more closely related to each other than to species in other genera. For example, Panthera leo (lion), Panthera tigris (tiger), and Panthera pardus (leopard) are close relatives that diverged relatively recently.

2.4 Common Mistakes Students Make

  • Writing the species epithet with a capital letter: ❌ Homo Sapiens → ✅ Homo sapiens
  • Forgetting italics or underlining: ❌ Homo sapiens → ✅ Homo sapiens or Homo sapiens
  • Using quotes around the name: ❌ "Homo sapiens" → ✅ Homo sapiens
  • Abbreviating the species epithet: ❌ H. Sapiens → ✅ H. sapiens

IB Exam Tip: You WILL be tested on binomial nomenclature formatting. Check every letter. If a question says "State the name of the species" and you write it without italics/underlining or with wrong capitalization, you lose the mark. Some mark schemes give the mark only if both formatting AND spelling are perfect.

3. What Even IS a Species? The Biological Species Concept

This seems like a simple question. A dog is a dog, a cat is a cat. But defining "species" precisely turns out to be one of biology's most contentious problems.

3.1 The Definition

The biological species concept, famously articulated by Ernst Mayr in 1942, defines a species as:

> A group of actually or potentially interbreeding natural populations that are reproductively isolated from other such groups.

In simpler terms: if two organisms can mate and produce fertile offspring under natural conditions, they belong to the same species. If they cannot, they belong to different species.

Biological species concept: A species is a group of organisms that can interbreed in nature to produce viable, fertile offspring, but cannot successfully interbreed with members of other groups (they are reproductively isolated).

Key word: "fertile." A horse and a donkey can mate to produce a mule, but mules are sterile. Therefore, horses (Equus ferus caballus) and donkeys (Equus africanus asinus) are separate species.

Key phrase: "under natural conditions." Lions and tigers can produce offspring (ligers and tigons) in captivity, but their ranges don't overlap in the wild, and they would not naturally mate. They are considered separate species.

3.2 The Strengths

The biological species concept is elegant and testable. It provides a clear, objective criterion: reproductive compatibility. It aligns beautifully with population genetics — gene flow holds a species together; reproductive isolation allows divergence.

3.3 The Limitations — Where It Falls Apart

The biological species concept has significant blind spots that IB expects you to know:

1. Asexual organisms: Bacteria, archaea, many protists, and some plants and fungi reproduce entirely by mitosis, binary fission, budding, or vegetative propagation. They don't interbreed at all. How do you apply a definition based on interbreeding to organisms that never breed? You can't.

2. Extinct organisms: Paleontologists studying fossils cannot observe mating behavior. They must classify based on morphology alone.

3. Ring species: A ring species is a connected series of neighboring populations, each of which can interbreed with adjacent populations, but where the "ends" of the ring meet without interbreeding. The classic example is the Ensatina salamander complex in California's Central Valley. Adjacent subspecies interbreed freely, but the subspecies at the southern ends of the ring (E. e. eschscholtzii and E. e. klauberi) overlap without interbreeding. Are they one species or two? The biological species concept cannot give a clear answer.

Visualize a ring species: Imagine a horseshoe-shaped valley. At the open end (north), population A can breed with B, B with C, C with D, D with E. At the closed end (south), populations A and E meet — but they cannot interbreed. Looking at A and E alone, they seem like separate species. But tracing the chain, there's gene flow all around. Where do you draw the line? You can't.

4. Hybridization in nature: Some clearly distinct species hybridize regularly in the wild and produce fertile offspring. The biological species concept predicts they shouldn't, but they do. Examples:

  • Grizzly bears and polar bears produce fertile "pizzly" or "grolar" bears where ranges overlap due to climate change.
  • Many plant species hybridize readily — oaks (Quercus), dandelions (Taraxacum), and many orchids routinely form fertile hybrids.

3.4 Alternative Species Concepts

Because no single concept works for all organisms, biologists have developed alternatives:

Concept Definition Strengths Weaknesses
Morphological Species defined by physical similarities and differences Works for fossils and asexual organisms; intuitive Subjective — how different is "different enough"?; cryptic species look identical
Ecological Species defined by its ecological niche — the resources it uses and its role Highlights functional differences Niches overlap; some species occupy multiple niches
Phylogenetic Species = the smallest monophyletic group on a phylogenetic tree Based on evolutionary history; increasingly used with DNA data Requires phylogenetic data; debate about which genetic markers to use

Cryptic species are worth special mention — these are groups that look identical under a microscope but are genetically distinct and reproductively isolated. DNA sequencing has revealed that many "species" described by morphological criteria are actually complexes of multiple cryptic species.

IB Exam Tip: A common Paper 2 question (often worth 4-7 marks) asks: "Discuss the limitations of the biological species concept" or "Compare and contrast two species concepts." Structure your answer: (1) State the biological species concept clearly; (2) Give at least four limitations with specific examples; (3) Briefly describe at least one alternative concept; (4) Conclude that no single concept works universally — biologists use multiple concepts pragmatically.

4. Chromosome Number: A Species Fingerprint

Every species has a characteristic chromosome number — the total number of chromosomes in a somatic (body) cell. This number is remarkably constant within a species and often differs between species.

4.1 The Human Karyotype

A karyotype is the complete set of chromosomes in a cell, photographed, arranged, and numbered by size and shape. A karyogram is the actual image/arrangement.

To prepare a karyogram:

  1. Cells (usually white blood cells) are cultured and arrested in metaphase of mitosis using colchicine.
  2. The cells are burst open with a hypotonic solution, spreading the chromosomes.
  3. Chromosomes are stained (Giemsa stain produces characteristic G-banding patterns).
  4. A photograph is taken, and chromosomes are digitally paired and arranged.

Human karyotype: 46 chromosomes arranged in 23 pairs.

  • 22 pairs of autosomes (numbered 1–22, roughly by descending size)
  • 1 pair of sex chromosomes (XX in females, XY in males)
  • The karyotype is written as: 46,XX (normal female) or 46,XY (normal male)

Drawing a human karyogram from description: Imagine a grid of 23 columns in 2 rows. The largest chromosomes (pair 1) are on the left, with metacentric centromeres (near the middle). Chromosomes get progressively smaller as you move right. Pairs 13, 14, 15, 21, and 22 are acrocentric — their centromeres are near one end, giving them tiny short arms. The X chromosome is medium-sized and submetacentric. The Y chromosome is very small and acrocentric.

Karyotype: The number and appearance of chromosomes in a eukaryotic cell nucleus. The term also refers to the standardized arrangement of chromosomes by size, centromere position, and banding pattern.

4.2 Variation in Chromosome Number Across Species

Chromosome number varies enormously — and it has nothing to do with organism complexity:

Species Diploid Number (2n)
Myrmecia pilosula* (jack jumper ant) 2 (female), 1 (male)
Drosophila melanogaster* (fruit fly) 8
Arabidopsis thaliana* (thale cress — model plant) 10
Pisum sativum* (garden pea — Mendel's organism) 14
Zea mays* (maize/corn) 20
Homo sapiens* (human) 46
Pan troglodytes* (chimpanzee) 48
Canis familiaris* (dog) 78
Ophioglossum reticulatum* (adder's tongue fern) ~1260

Critical misconception: Students often think "more chromosomes = more complex organism." Absolutely false. The adder's tongue fern has ~1260 chromosomes — it's a small, simple fern. Human chromosome 2 is actually the fusion product of two ancestral ape chromosomes (explaining why we have 46 and chimps have 48 — a classic molecular evidence question).

4.3 Genome Size vs. Chromosome Number

The genome is the complete set of genetic instructions — all the DNA in a cell. Genome size is measured in base pairs (bp) or picograms (pg) of DNA. Genome size, like chromosome number, does NOT correlate with complexity:

Organism Genome Size (million bp)
E. coli* (bacterium) ~4.6
Saccharomyces cerevisiae* (yeast) ~12
Drosophila melanogaster* (fruit fly) ~180
Homo sapiens* (human) ~3,200
Paris japonica* (Japanese canopy plant) ~149,000
Protopterus aethiopicus* (marbled lungfish) ~130,000

The C-value paradox: Why does a lungfish have 40× more DNA than a human? Most of the lungfish genome is non-coding DNA — repetitive sequences, transposons, and introns. Humans have more sophisticated gene regulation, not more genes.

5. Atypical Chromosome Inheritance

Not all organisms follow the standard diploid-with-two-sex-chromosomes pattern. Nature is far more creative.

5.1 Polyploidy: Having Extra Complete Chromosome Sets

Polyploidy refers to an organism having more than two complete sets of chromosomes. The terminology:

Term Chromosome Sets Notation Example
Haploid 1 set n Gametes
Diploid 2 sets 2n Most animals, many plants
Triploid 3 sets 3n Seedless watermelons, bananas
Tetraploid 4 sets 4n Durum wheat (pasta), cotton, potatoes
Hexaploid 6 sets 6n Bread wheat (Triticum aestivum)
Octoploid 8 sets 8n Strawberries

How polyploidy arises:

  • Autopolyploidy: Duplication of chromosome sets within a single species. A failure of meiosis produces diploid (2n) gametes instead of haploid (n). When a 2n gamete fuses with a normal n gamete → triploid (3n) offspring. When two 2n gametes fuse → tetraploid (4n).
  • Allopolyploidy: Hybridization between two different species followed by chromosome doubling. The hybrid is initially sterile (chromosomes from different parents can't pair in meiosis), but if spontaneous chromosome doubling occurs, each chromosome now has a homologous partner, and the organism becomes fertile. This is how bread wheat evolved — it's an allohexaploid from three ancestral grass species.

Polyploidy in plants is common, beneficial, and agriculturally important. Over 70% of flowering plant species are polyploid. Polyploid plants often have larger cells, larger organs, and greater vigor (the "gigas effect"). Many of our most important crops are polyploid:

  • Bread wheat (Triticum aestivum): hexaploid (2n = 6x = 42)
  • Strawberry (Fragaria × ananassa): octoploid (2n = 8x = 56)
  • Cotton (Gossypium hirsutum): tetraploid (2n = 4x = 52)
  • Potato (Solanum tuberosum): tetraploid (2n = 4x = 48)

Polyploidy in animals is rare. Why? In animals, sex determination often depends on chromosome ratios (e.g., X:autosome ratio in Drosophila). Polyploidy disrupts this balance and usually causes lethal developmental abnormalities. However, some animals are polyploid:

  • Some fish (salmonids, some carp)
  • Some amphibians (certain frogs in the genus Xenopus)
  • Parthenogenetic lizards and insects
  • Flatworms (some planarians)

IB Exam Tip: If you're asked "Why are polyploid plants often sterile with odd ploidy levels?", here's the answer: During meiosis I, homologous chromosomes must pair and separate. In an odd-ploid organism (e.g., triploid, 3n), chromosomes cannot form equal pairs — they form trivalents (groups of three), leading to unbalanced segregation and aneuploid gametes. This is why triploid bananas and seedless watermelons are seedless — they cannot produce viable gametes. This is actually commercially useful!

5.2 Sex Determination Systems

Not all organisms use the mammalian XX/XY system. The diversity is stunning:

1. XX/XY system (mammals, including humans, some insects)

  • Females: XX (homogametic — all gametes carry X)
  • Males: XY (heterogametic — half carry X, half carry Y)
  • The SRY gene on the Y chromosome triggers male development. Without SRY, the default developmental pathway is female.
  • During meiosis in males, X and Y chromosomes pair only at small pseudoautosomal regions (PARs) — the rest of the Y doesn't recombine.

2. ZZ/ZW system (birds, some reptiles, butterflies, some fish)

  • Females: ZW (heterogametic)
  • Males: ZZ (homogametic)
  • It's the "reverse" of XY — the female determines offspring sex via which sex chromosome she contributes.
  • In birds, the DMRT1 gene on the Z chromosome (present in double dose in males) triggers male development.

3. Haplodiploidy (bees, wasps, ants — order Hymenoptera)

  • Females (workers and queens): Diploid — develop from fertilized eggs (2n)
  • Males (drones): Haploid — develop from unfertilized eggs (n) via parthenogenesis
  • This means males have no father and can produce no sons (only grandsons via their daughters)
  • A bizarre consequence: a drone has grandfathers but no father, and sisters are more closely related to each other (sharing 75% of genes) than to their own offspring (50%). This genetic asymmetry is thought to explain the evolution of eusociality (worker sterility) in Hymenoptera.

4. Environmental sex determination (some reptiles, especially turtles and crocodilians)

  • Sex is determined by incubation temperature during a critical period of embryonic development, not by chromosomes.
  • Turtles: Cool temperatures → males; warm → females (some species reverse this)
  • Crocodilians: Cool (<30°C) → females; intermediate (31–33°C) → males; hot (>34°C) → females
  • The mechanism involves temperature-sensitive enzymes that convert testosterone to estradiol. Aromatase, the key enzyme, is more active at certain temperatures.
  • This has alarming implications for climate change — skewed sex ratios could drive populations extinct.

5. Other systems:

  • XO system (grasshoppers, some insects): Females XX, males XO (single X, no second sex chromosome)
  • UV system (some algae, mosses): The haploid stage determines sex — U-bearing individuals are female, V-bearing are male
  • Sequential hermaphroditism: An individual changes sex during its life — clownfish start male and become female (protandry); wrasses start female and become male (protogyny)

6. Worked Examples

Worked Example 1: Distinguishing Variation Types

A student measures the body mass of 100 rabbits from the same litter raised in different cages with different food. She also measures ear length.

Data: Body mass varies widely (1.5–3.2 kg). Ear length is nearly identical across all rabbits (±2 mm).

Question: Which trait shows primarily genetic variation and which shows primarily environmental variation? Explain.

Step-by-step solution:

  1. These rabbits are from the same litter, meaning they share parents and are genetically similar (though not identical — they are siblings, not clones).
  1. Body mass varies widely despite similar genetics. Since the rabbits were raised with different food, the most likely explanation is that nutritional differences (environmental) caused the variation. Body mass in this case shows primarily environmental variation.
  1. Ear length is nearly identical despite different environments. This suggests ear length is strongly genetically determined and shows little environmental plasticity. The trait shows primarily genetic variation.
  1. IB mark scheme check: To get full marks, explicitly state that (a) identical or similar environments with different outcomes suggest genetic variation, while (b) identical or similar genetics with different environments suggest environmental variation. Use the words "heritable" and "non-heritable."

Worked Example 2: Correcting Binomial Nomenclature Errors

A student writes the following names. Identify and correct each error:

a) Canis Lupus

b) Homo sapiens

c) panthera leo

d) E. Coli

Solution:

a) Species epithet must be lowercase: Canis lupus

b) Must be italicized or underlined: Homo sapiens

c) Genus must be capitalized and both words italicized: Panthera leo

d) Species epithet must be lowercase, even after abbreviation: E. coli

Worked Example 3: Analyzing a Karyogram

A karyogram shows 54 chromosomes arranged in 27 pairs. All pairs appear identical in both sexes.

Question: Identify the sex determination system, the diploid number, and suggest what type of organism this might be.

Solution:

  1. Diploid number (2n) = 54 chromosomes.
  2. No heteromorphic (differently-shaped) sex chromosomes are visible — all 27 pairs look identical in both sexes. This is not XY or ZW.
  3. Possible explanations: (a) The species could use environmental sex determination (all chromosomes appear identical). (b) It could be monoecious/hermaphroditic. (c) The sex chromosomes could be homomorphic (similar in size — this occurs in some reptiles and fish).
  4. Given 2n = 54, this could be a crocodilian, turtle, or fish species.

7. Common Misconceptions

Misconception Reality
"Acquired characteristics can be inherited" No — this was Lamarck's discredited theory. Lifting weights doesn't mean your children will be born muscular. (Epigenetic inheritance is a limited, controversial exception — not the rule.)
"All variation within a species is genetic" Much variation is environmental. Identical twins are not perfectly identical in all traits.
"Binomial names are in English" They're in Latin or latinized form. Always.
"Two organisms that look different must be different species" Phenotypic plasticity and sexual dimorphism mean one species can produce very different-looking individuals.
"The biological species concept is the only species concept" It's the most famous, but many alternatives exist and are regularly used by scientists.
"More chromosomes = more advanced organism" Chromosome number is unrelated to complexity. Ferns have >1000 chromosomes.
"Polyploidy is always harmful" In plants, polyploidy is often beneficial and is a major driver of speciation. Most flowering plants are polyploid.
"Sex is always determined by chromosomes" In many reptiles, temperature determines sex. In some fish, social cues trigger sex change.

8. Practice Problems

Problem 1 (Paper 1 style): The binomial name for the lion is Panthera leo. Which statement is correct?

A. Panthera is the species and leo is the genus

B. Panthera is the genus and must be written lowercase

C. The name Panthera leo indicates the lion cannot interbreed with Panthera tigris

D. Panthera is the genus and leo is the species epithet

Click for answer

D. Panthera is the genus (capitalized, first word) and leo is the species epithet (lowercase, second word). A is backwards. B is wrong because genus must be capitalized. C is wrong because binomial names indicate classification, not reproductive compatibility — many closely related Panthera species can hybridize in captivity.

Problem 2 (Paper 2 style, 4 marks): Explain why the biological species concept cannot be applied to bacteria.

Click for answer

Model answer (4 marks):

  1. The biological species concept defines a species as a group of organisms that can interbreed to produce fertile offspring (1 mark).
  2. Bacteria reproduce asexually by binary fission — they do not breed sexually (1 mark).
  3. Since there is no interbreeding, the concept of reproductive isolation cannot be applied (1 mark).
  4. Additionally, bacteria engage in horizontal gene transfer (conjugation, transformation, transduction), exchanging genes across species boundaries, further blurring species lines (1 mark).

Problem 3 (Paper 2 style, 6 marks): Compare polyploidy in plants and animals.

Click for answer

Model answer (6 marks):

Similarities (any 2 for 2 marks):

  • Both involve having more than two complete sets of chromosomes
  • Both can arise from failure of meiosis producing diploid gametes
  • Hybridization followed by chromosome doubling can produce allopolyploids in both

Differences (any 4 for 4 marks):

  • Polyploidy is common in plants (>70% of flowering plants) but rare in animals
  • Polyploid plants are often larger, more vigorous (gigas effect); animal polyploids usually have developmental abnormalities
  • Polyploidy is a major speciation mechanism in plants; rare in animals
  • Polyploidy disrupts sex chromosome-based sex determination in animals, which is usually lethal
  • Many agriculturally important crops are polyploid (wheat, cotton, potato); few domesticated animals are polyploid
  • Odd-ploid plants (3n, 5n) are often sterile but commercially useful (seedless fruits); odd-ploid animals rarely survive

Problem 4: A population of turtles on a beach produces 95% female hatchlings as sand temperatures rise due to climate change. Explain the mechanism and predict the long-term consequence. (3 marks)

Click for answer

  1. These turtles use environmental sex determination, where incubation temperature determines sex (1 mark).
  2. Higher temperatures during the thermosensitive period increase aromatase activity, converting androgens to estrogens, promoting female development (1 mark).
  3. Long-term consequence: The extreme female-biased sex ratio will reduce effective population size, decreasing genetic diversity. Eventually, too few males will exist for successful reproduction, leading to population decline and possible local extinction (1 mark).

Summary

  • Variation comes from genetic sources (mutation, meiosis, sex) and environmental sources (nutrition, climate, etc.). Most traits are multifactorial.
  • The binomial system gives every species a two-part Latin name: Genus species. Formatting matters.
  • The biological species concept defines species by reproductive compatibility but fails for asexual/extinct organisms, ring species, and natural hybrids.
  • Chromosome number is species-characteristic. The human karyotype is 46,XX or 46,XY with 23 pairs.
  • Polyploidy is common and beneficial in plants, rare and usually harmful in animals.
  • Sex determination systems include XX/XY, ZZ/ZW, haplodiploidy, and environmental (temperature-dependent).
