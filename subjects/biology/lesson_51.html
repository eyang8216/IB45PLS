1. What Is Homeostasis?

Homeostasis is the maintenance of a relatively constant internal environment within narrow limits, despite changes in the external environment. It is a dynamic equilibrium — not a fixed state, but continuous adjustments around a set point.

> Analogy: Think of homeostasis as a thermostat in a room. When the temperature drops below the set point (e.g., 21°C), the thermostat detects the deviation and turns on the heater. When the temperature rises above the set point, the heater turns off. The temperature oscillates slightly around 21°C — never perfectly constant, but always within an acceptable range. The body works the same way but for dozens of variables simultaneously.

Key Variables Under Homeostatic Control

Variable Normal Range / Set Point Why It Must Be Controlled
Core body temperature ~36.5–37.5°C Enzymes denature >40°C; metabolic reactions too slow <35°C
Blood pH 7.35–7.45 Enzyme function is pH-dependent; acidosis/alkalosis disrupts cellular processes
Blood glucose ~90 mg/100 mL (~5 mM) Brain depends almost entirely on glucose; hypoglycemia → coma; hyperglycemia → osmotic damage
Blood CO₂ ~40 mmHg partial pressure CO₂ + H₂O → carbonic acid → affects blood pH (respiratory acidosis/alkalosis)
Water balance (osmolality) ~285–295 mOsm/kg Cells shrink (crenation) in hypertonic solutions; swell and burst (lysis) in hypotonic solutions
Blood O₂ ~95–100% saturation Cellular respiration requires O₂ as final electron acceptor

2. Negative Feedback: The Universal Control Mechanism

The Negative Feedback Loop

Negative feedback is a control mechanism in which a change from the set point triggers a response that counteracts (reverses) the change, returning the system to the set point.

> Draw and label: A circular diagram of a negative feedback loop: (1) Stimulus — change from set point, (2) Receptor/Sensor — detects the change, (3) Signal — nerve impulse or hormone, (4) Integrator/Control Centre — processes information (often hypothalamus), (5) Effector — carries out the response (muscle or gland), (6) Response — counteracts the original stimulus, (7) Return to set point — feedback is "negative" because response opposes stimulus.

The Five Components

Component Role Examples
Stimulus Deviation from the set point Rise in blood glucose after a meal; drop in body temperature in cold weather
Receptor/Sensor Detects the deviation β-cells in pancreatic islets (detect high glucose); thermoreceptors in skin and hypothalamus
Signal Communicates deviation to integrator Nerve impulse (action potential); hormone (e.g., insulin released into blood)
Integrator/Control Centre Processes information and coordinates response Hypothalamus (temperature, osmolality); pancreas (glucose)
Effector Carries out the corrective response Skeletal muscle (shivering); sweat glands; liver cells (glycogen synthesis/breakdown)

Positive Feedback: The Exception

In positive feedback, the response amplifies the original stimulus rather than counteracting it. This is used for processes that must be completed rapidly and definitively.

Process Positive Feedback Mechanism
Childbirth Baby's head presses on cervix → nerve signal to hypothalamus → oxytocin release → stronger uterine contractions → more pressure on cervix → more oxytocin → cycle continues until birth
Blood clotting Damaged vessel releases clotting factors → platelets adhere and release MORE factors → attract MORE platelets → plug forms rapidly
Action potential Depolarisation opens voltage-gated Na⁺ channels → Na⁺ enters → further depolarisation → opens MORE Na⁺ channels → explosive depolarisation
Lactation Baby suckles → oxytocin release → milk ejection → baby continues suckling

3. Thermoregulation

Endotherms vs Ectotherms

Feature Endotherms (e.g., mammals, birds) Ectotherms (e.g., reptiles, amphibians, fish)
Heat source Metabolic heat production (internal) External environment (behavioural)
Metabolic rate High (5–10× higher than ectotherms of same size) Low
Mitochondria density High (generate more metabolic heat) Lower
Brown adipose tissue Present (non-shivering thermogenesis) Absent
Thermoneutral zone Range of ambient temperatures where metabolic rate is minimal N/A (metabolic rate varies with temperature)
Behavioural thermoregulation Supplementary (seeking shade, huddling) Primary (basking, shade-seeking, burrowing)
Energy cost High (need more food) Low
Activity in cold Can remain active Sluggish or inactive

Brown adipose tissue (BAT) contains thermogenin (UCP1 — uncoupling protein 1) in the inner mitochondrial membrane. UCP1 allows protons to leak back across the membrane without passing through ATP synthase → energy from the proton gradient is released as heat rather than used for ATP synthesis. This is non-shivering thermogenesis.

Temperature Receptors

  • Peripheral thermoreceptors: In skin — detect environmental (shell) temperature
  • Central thermoreceptors: In hypothalamus — detect core blood temperature
  • The hypothalamus integrates signals from both and compares them to the set point (~37°C)

Responses to Heat (Hyperthermia)

Response Mechanism
Vasodilation Arterioles in skin dilate → more warm blood flows near skin surface → heat lost by radiation and convection
Sweating Sweat glands secrete water + salts onto skin → evaporation absorbs latent heat of vaporisation (~2.4 kJ/g water) → cooling
Panting** (dogs, birds) Rapid shallow breathing → evaporation from respiratory surfaces → cooling (dogs lack sweat glands over most of body)
Behavioural Seeking shade, stretching out (increased surface area for heat loss), reduced activity, drinking cold water
Piloerection reduced Hair lies flat → less insulation → more heat loss

Responses to Cold (Hypothermia)

Response Mechanism
Vasoconstriction Arterioles in skin constrict → blood shunted away from skin to core → heat conserved
Shivering thermogenesis Skeletal muscles contract rapidly and involuntarily → ATP hydrolysis + muscle activity generate heat (can increase heat production 5×)
Non-shivering thermogenesis Brown adipose tissue → UCP1 uncouples oxidative phosphorylation → energy released as heat (especially important in infants and hibernating animals)
Piloerection Arrector pili muscles contract → hairs stand up → traps insulating layer of air (goosebumps — minimal effect in humans but significant in furry mammals)
Behavioural Curling up (reduced surface area), huddling, seeking shelter, putting on clothing, increased voluntary activity

Countercurrent Heat Exchange

In limbs of endotherms in cold environments (e.g., arctic foxes, penguins, whales), arteries carrying warm blood to the extremity run adjacent to veins carrying cool blood back to the core.

> Draw and label: An artery (red, warm) running parallel to a vein (blue, cool) in a limb. Show heat transfer arrows from artery to vein along the length. At the extremity, arterial blood has been cooled (conserving core heat) while venous blood has been warmed (preventing cold blood from cooling the core). Label: "Countercurrent heat exchange — arterial blood warms venous return, conserving core temperature."

This arrangement means that heat from arterial blood is transferred to venous blood before it reaches the extremity, so:

  • The extremity can be much cooler than the core (reducing heat loss to the environment)
  • The core stays warm because venous return has been pre-warmed

4. Blood Glucose Regulation

The Glucose Set Point

Fasting blood glucose: ~90 mg/100 mL (~5 mmol/L). After a meal, it rises temporarily; between meals, it must not fall too low because the brain depends almost entirely on glucose for energy (cannot metabolise fatty acids).

The Pancreatic Islets

The pancreas contains clusters of endocrine cells called islets of Langerhans:

Cell Type Hormone Secreted Stimulus Effect
α-cells (alpha) Glucagon Low blood glucose ↑ blood glucose
β-cells (beta) Insulin High blood glucose ↓ blood glucose

> Draw and label: A cross-section of the pancreas showing an islet of Langerhans surrounded by exocrine acinar cells. Label α-cells (periphery of islet), β-cells (centre of islet), and blood capillary.

Insulin Signalling Cascade

When blood glucose rises (after a meal):

  1. Glucose enters β-cells via GLUT2 transporters (not insulin-dependent).
  2. Increased glucose metabolism → ↑ ATP in β-cell.
  3. ATP closes K⁺-ATP channels → membrane depolarises.
  4. Voltage-gated Ca²⁺ channels open → Ca²⁺ enters.
  5. Ca²⁺ triggers exocytosis of insulin-containing vesicles.
  6. Insulin travels in blood to target cells (muscle, liver, adipose tissue).

At the target cell:

  1. Insulin binds to insulin receptor (a receptor tyrosine kinase).
  2. Intracellular signalling cascade (phosphorylation events).
  3. GLUT4 vesicles translocate to the cell membrane → glucose uptake.
  4. Activation of glycogen synthase → glycogen synthesis (glycogenesis).
  5. Activation of glycolysis → glucose broken down for energy.

Glucagon Signalling

When blood glucose falls (between meals, fasting, exercise):

  1. α-cells detect low glucose → secrete glucagon.
  2. Glucagon binds to receptors on liver cells.
  3. Activates adenylyl cyclase → cAMP → protein kinase A cascade.
  4. Activates glycogen phosphorylase → breaks down glycogen → glucose-1-phosphate → glucose released into blood (glycogenolysis).
  5. Promotes gluconeogenesis — synthesis of glucose from non-carbohydrate sources (amino acids, lactate, glycerol).

> Analogy: Insulin is the "storage manager" — after a meal, it tells cells to take up glucose and store it as glycogen. Glucagon is the "emergency supply officer" — when glucose is low, it tells the liver to break down glycogen and release glucose. Together, they maintain glucose within a tight range.

5. Diabetes Mellitus

Diabetes is a disorder of blood glucose regulation characterised by chronic hyperglycemia (high blood glucose).

Type I Diabetes

Feature Description
Cause Autoimmune destruction** of pancreatic β-cells by the body's own immune system (autoantibodies against β-cell antigens)
Contributing factors Genetic susceptibility (certain HLA genes) + environmental trigger (viral infection suspected — e.g., coxsackievirus, rubella)
Onset Usually childhood/adolescence (hence formerly "juvenile diabetes") — rapid onset
Insulin production Little or none — β-cells destroyed
Treatment Insulin injections** (subcutaneous), insulin pump (continuous subcutaneous insulin infusion), islet cell transplantation (experimental)
Without treatment Hyperglycemia → glucose in urine (glycosuria), osmotic diuresis, dehydration, weight loss (cells starving despite high blood glucose), ketoacidosis (fat breakdown → ketone bodies → blood pH drops → life-threatening)

Type II Diabetes

Feature Description
Cause Insulin resistance** — target cells (muscle, liver, adipose) do not respond properly to insulin; β-cells initially compensate by producing MORE insulin (hyperinsulinemia), but eventually become exhausted
Risk factors Obesity (especially visceral/abdominal fat), sedentary lifestyle, high-sugar/high-fat diet, genetic predisposition, age, ethnicity
Onset Usually adulthood (increasingly in children due to obesity epidemic) — gradual onset
Treatment progression 1. Diet modification + exercise (improves insulin sensitivity); 2. Oral medications (e.g., metformin — reduces liver glucose production, increases insulin sensitivity); 3. Insulin injections (if β-cells eventually fail)

Consequences of Chronic Hyperglycemia

  • Damage to blood vessels (microvascular and macrovascular)
  • Diabetic retinopathy → blindness
  • Diabetic nephropathy → kidney failure
  • Diabetic neuropathy → loss of sensation, especially in feet → injuries unnoticed → infections → amputation
  • Increased risk of cardiovascular disease and stroke

Hypoglycemia

Excessively low blood glucose (<70 mg/100 mL) → brain starved of glucose → confusion, shakiness, sweating, loss of consciousness, coma, death if severe and prolonged. Can result from too much insulin (diabetic overdose), prolonged fasting, or insulin-secreting tumours.

6. Osmoregulation and the Kidney

The Nephron: Functional Unit

Each kidney contains ~1 million nephrons. A nephron is a microscopic tubule that filters blood and modifies the filtrate to produce urine.

> Draw and label: A nephron showing: (1) Renal corpuscle — glomerulus (capillary knot) + Bowman's capsule, (2) Proximal convoluted tubule (PCT), (3) Loop of Henle — descending limb (thin) and ascending limb (thick), (4) Distal convoluted tubule (DCT), (5) Collecting duct. Show associated blood vessels: afferent arteriole (into glomerulus), efferent arteriole (out), peritubular capillaries.

1. Glomerular Filtration

Blood enters the glomerulus via the afferent arteriole (wider) and exits via the efferent arteriole (narrower). This creates high hydrostatic pressure in the glomerular capillaries.

  • What passes through: Water, ions (Na⁺, K⁺, Cl⁻, etc.), glucose, amino acids, urea, creatinine — all small molecules
  • What is retained: Red blood cells, white blood cells, platelets, large plasma proteins (albumin, globulins) — too large for filtration slits
  • Filtrate composition: ~Plasma minus proteins
  • Glomerular filtration rate (GFR): ~125 mL/min (~180 L/day — entire plasma volume filtered ~60× per day!)

2. Proximal Convoluted Tubule (PCT)

The PCT reabsorbs ~70% of the filtrate volume:

Substance Mechanism
Glucose Co-transport with Na⁺ (SGLT proteins) → all reabsorbed (healthy individual)
Amino acids Co-transport with Na⁺ → all reabsorbed
Na⁺ Active transport (Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase on basolateral membrane) → most reabsorbed
Water Follows solutes by osmosis (PCT walls highly permeable to water)
Cl⁻ Follows Na⁺ electrochemically

The PCT cells have microvilli (brush border) → huge surface area for reabsorption, and many mitochondria → ATP for active transport.

3. Loop of Henle: The Countercurrent Multiplier

The loop of Henle creates a concentration gradient in the kidney medulla (increasing from cortex → deep medulla), which allows production of concentrated urine.

Limb Permeability to Water Permeability to Ions What Happens
Descending limb High** (many aquaporins) Low Water leaves by osmosis (drawn out by high medullary solute concentration) → filtrate becomes increasingly concentrated
Ascending limb** (thin) Low Passive Na⁺/Cl⁻ efflux NaCl diffuses out down concentration gradient
Ascending limb** (thick) Impermeable Active transport** of Na⁺, K⁺, Cl⁻ out NaCl actively pumped into medullary interstitium → maintains high solute concentration in medulla

> Why "countercurrent multiplier"? The descending and ascending limbs run in opposite directions and are close together. NaCl pumped out of the ascending limb increases the interstitial solute concentration, which draws water out of the descending limb, further concentrating the filtrate entering the ascending limb, which provides more NaCl to pump out. The effect multiplies down the loop.

4. Distal Convoluted Tubule (DCT)

Fine-tuning of Na⁺ (reabsorption), K⁺ (secretion), and pH regulation (H⁺ secretion or HCO₃⁻ reabsorption). Controlled by aldosterone (increases Na⁺ reabsorption and K⁺ secretion).

5. Collecting Duct and ADH

The collecting duct's permeability to water is controlled by antidiuretic hormone (ADH, also called vasopressin):

  • High blood solute concentration (dehydration): Osmoreceptors in hypothalamus detect high osmolality → hypothalamus signals posterior pituitary → ADH released → ADH binds to receptors on collecting duct cells → aquaporin-2 (AQP2) vesicles inserted into apical membrane → water channels open → water reabsorbed from collecting duct into hypertonic medulla → small volume of concentrated urine.
  • Low blood solute concentration (overhydration): Osmoreceptors detect low osmolality → ADH secretion suppressed → aquaporins removed from membrane → collecting duct impermeable to water → water stays in filtrate → large volume of dilute urine.

> Draw and label: Collecting duct cell: (1) Without ADH — aquaporins stored in cytoplasmic vesicles. (2) With ADH — ADH binds to receptor → cAMP signalling cascade → vesicles fuse with apical membrane → aquaporins inserted → water enters cell → exits via basolateral aquaporins.

7. Leptin and Appetite Control

Leptin: The Satiety Hormone

  • Produced by: Adipose (fat) tissue — more body fat → more leptin
  • Target: Hypothalamus (arcuate nucleus)
  • Effect: Suppresses appetite, increases energy expenditure
  • Function: Long-term signal of energy reserves — tells the brain how much fat is stored
  • Normal physiology: Weight gain → more leptin → less appetite → weight loss. Weight loss → less leptin → more appetite → weight gain. This is a negative feedback loop for body weight.

The ob/ob Mouse

A landmark discovery: mice with a mutation in the ob gene (now known as the Lep gene) cannot produce functional leptin. These ob/ob mice:

  • Have no leptin signal → brain thinks body is starving
  • Eat voraciously (hyperphagia)
  • Become massively obese
  • Have low metabolic rate
  • When injected with leptin → lose weight dramatically

Leptin Resistance in Humans

Most obese humans have high (not low) leptin levels — the problem is leptin resistance, analogous to insulin resistance in Type II diabetes. The hypothalamus does not respond to the leptin signal, so appetite remains high despite adequate fat stores.

Ghrelin: The Hunger Hormone

  • Produced by: Stomach (when empty)
  • Effect: Stimulates appetite
  • Levels rise before meals and fall after eating
  • Works in opposition to leptin (short-term meal initiation vs long-term energy balance)

8. Melatonin and Circadian Rhythms

The Circadian Clock

Circadian rhythms are ~24-hour biological cycles that govern sleep-wake patterns, hormone secretion, body temperature, and metabolism. The master clock is the suprachiasmatic nucleus (SCN) in the hypothalamus.

Melatonin Synthesis and Secretion

  • Produced by: Pineal gland (a small endocrine gland deep in the brain)
  • Synthesis pathway: Tryptophan → serotonin → N-acetylserotonin → melatonin
  • Pattern: Synthesis INCREASES in darkness, DECREASES in light → melatonin is the "hormone of darkness"
  • Peak: ~2–4 AM in humans
  • Function: Signals darkness to the body → promotes sleep onset, regulates timing of sleep-wake cycle

The Light Detection Pathway

  1. Light detected by intrinsically photosensitive retinal ganglion cells (ipRGCs) in the retina — these contain the photopigment melanopsin (distinct from rod and cone opsins).
  2. Signal travels via the retinohypothalamic tract to the SCN.
  3. SCN signals to the pineal gland via sympathetic nervous system.
  4. In light: SCN inhibits melatonin synthesis. In darkness: inhibition removed → melatonin produced.

> Key point: This is NOT the same pathway as vision. Even blind people with retinal damage may retain functional ipRGCs and maintain circadian entrainment.

Circadian Rhythm Disruption

Disruption Mechanism Consequences
Jet lag Rapid travel across time zones → internal clock out of sync with local light-dark cycle Fatigue, insomnia, digestive issues, impaired cognitive performance (takes ~1 day per time zone to adjust)
Shift work Working at night, sleeping during day → chronic misalignment of circadian clock with behaviour Increased risk of cardiovascular disease, metabolic disorders (obesity, diabetes), depression, certain cancers (IARC classifies shift work as "probably carcinogenic")
Blue light exposure at night Screens emit blue-rich light → ipRGCs activated → SCN interprets as daylight → melatonin suppressed → difficulty sleeping Sleep onset delayed, reduced sleep quality

9. Common Misconceptions

Misconception Reality
"Homeostasis means keeping everything constant" It means keeping variables within a tolerable range — there is continuous oscillation around the set point
"Positive feedback is 'bad' feedback" Positive feedback is essential for specific physiological processes (childbirth, clotting, action potentials). It is not inherently pathological
"Endotherms are 'warm-blooded' and ectotherms are 'cold-blooded'" An ectotherm basking in the sun may have a higher body temperature than an endotherm. The distinction is about the SOURCE of heat, not the temperature itself
"Sweating cools because the sweat is cold" Sweating cools through evaporative cooling — heat is absorbed as water changes from liquid to gas
"All glucose is reabsorbed in the kidney" In a healthy person, yes — glucose should not appear in urine. In diabetics, blood glucose exceeds the renal threshold (~180 mg/100mL) → glucose appears in urine (glycosuria)
"ADH makes you produce more urine" ADH = anti-diuretic hormone — it REDUCES urine volume by increasing water reabsorption
"Melatonin makes you sleep" Melatonin does not induce sleep directly — it signals that it is nighttime, facilitating the transition to sleep and synchronising the circadian clock

Practice Problems

Problem 1 (Negative Feedback)

A student measures her body temperature at rest (37.1°C). She then exercises vigorously for 30 minutes and her core temperature rises to 38.2°C. Describe the negative feedback responses that will return her temperature to normal, naming the receptor, integrator, and effectors involved.

Answer

Stimulus: Core body temperature rises above set point (~37°C).

Receptor: Central thermoreceptors in the hypothalamus detect elevated blood temperature; peripheral thermoreceptors in the skin also send signals.

Integrator: Hypothalamus (thermoregulatory centre) compares input to set point and coordinates response.

Effectors and Responses:

  1. Vasodilation — arterioles in skin dilate → more blood flows near skin surface → heat lost by radiation and convection.
  2. Sweating — sweat glands secrete sweat onto skin surface → evaporation absorbs latent heat → cooling.
  3. Behavioural — reduced activity, seeking cooler environment, removing clothing.

Outcome: Heat loss increases, body temperature falls back toward set point → negative feedback (response counteracts original stimulus).

Problem 2 (Diabetes)

Compare and contrast Type I and Type II diabetes mellitus. Include: cause, age of onset, insulin levels, and treatment for each.

Answer

Feature Type I Diabetes Type II Diabetes
Cause Autoimmune destruction of pancreatic β-cells Insulin resistance in target cells; eventual β-cell exhaustion
Contributing factors Genetic susceptibility + environmental trigger (viral) Obesity, sedentary lifestyle, high-sugar diet, genetics
Typical age of onset Childhood/adolescence Adulthood (increasingly in children)
Insulin levels at diagnosis Very low or absent (β-cells destroyed) Normal or high (β-cells compensating for resistance)
Treatment Insulin injections/pump (essential for survival) Diet/exercise → metformin (oral) → insulin (if β-cells fail)
Body weight Often normal or underweight at diagnosis Often overweight/obese

Key contrast: Type I = not enough insulin produced; Type II = cells don't respond to insulin (resistance).

Problem 3 (Nephron Function)

A drug inhibits the Na⁺/K⁺/2Cl⁻ co-transporter in the thick ascending limb of the loop of Henle. Predict the effect on: (a) the medullary osmotic gradient, (b) water reabsorption from the collecting duct, and (c) urine volume and concentration.

Answer

(a) Medullary osmotic gradient: The thick ascending limb actively transports Na⁺, K⁺, and Cl⁻ out of the filtrate into the medullary interstitium, which is essential for creating and maintaining the high solute concentration in the medulla. Inhibiting this transporter would reduce or abolish the medullary osmotic gradient.

(b) Water reabsorption in collecting duct: Water reabsorption from the collecting duct depends on the hypertonic medullary interstitium drawing water out by osmosis. Without this gradient, even if ADH is present and aquaporins are inserted, water reabsorption would be greatly reduced because there is no osmotic driving force.

(c) Urine: Large volume of dilute urine — similar to the effect of loop diuretics like furosemide, which target this exact transporter.

Problem 4 (ADH)

A student drinks 1 litre of water rapidly. Explain the sequence of events that leads to production of a large volume of dilute urine. Include the roles of osmoreceptors, the hypothalamus, posterior pituitary, ADH, aquaporins, and the collecting duct.

Answer

  1. Drinking 1L water → water absorbed from gut into blood → blood solute concentration decreases (blood becomes more dilute, osmolality falls).
  2. Osmoreceptors in the hypothalamus detect the decrease in blood osmolality.
  3. Hypothalamus signals the posterior pituitary to reduce/cease ADH secretion.
  4. Low ADH → aquaporin-2 (AQP2) channels are removed from the apical membrane of collecting duct cells (internalised into vesicles).
  5. Collecting duct becomes impermeable to water.
  6. Water remains in the filtrate rather than being reabsorbed → large volume of dilute urine produced.
  7. Excess water is eliminated → blood osmolality returns to normal → negative feedback.

Problem 5 (Thermoregulation)

An arctic fox has short ears, a thick fur coat, and countercurrent heat exchange in its legs. Explain how each of these adaptations helps the fox conserve heat in its extremely cold environment.

Answer

Short ears: Ears have a large surface area-to-volume ratio, making them sites of significant heat loss. Short ears reduce surface area → less heat lost to the environment.

Thick fur coat: Fur traps a layer of still air next to the skin. Air is a poor conductor of heat (good insulator). The trapped air reduces heat loss by convection and conduction. Additionally, piloerection can fluff the fur to trap more air.

Countercurrent heat exchange in legs: Arteries carrying warm blood to the feet run adjacent to veins carrying cool blood back to the body. Heat transfers from the warm arterial blood to the cool venous blood along the length of the limb:

  • Blood reaching the feet is already cooled → less heat lost to the cold ground (smaller temperature gradient between foot and environment).
  • Blood returning to the body has been pre-warmed → prevents cold venous blood from cooling the core.

This system allows the fox's feet to be much cooler than its core (sometimes near 0°C) while the core stays warm (~38°C), dramatically reducing heat loss from extremities.
