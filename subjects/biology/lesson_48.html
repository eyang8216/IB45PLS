What You'll Learn

Plants don't just passively grow — they actively reproduce using structures that are as sophisticated as anything in the animal kingdom. From the intricate double fertilization unique to flowering plants to the ability to measure night length with molecular precision, plant reproduction reveals nature's ingenuity at every level. This lesson explores flower structure, pollination, seed development, and the remarkable photoperiodic control of flowering.

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to:

  • Identify and describe the functions of flower structures (sepals, petals, stamens, carpels)
  • Explain pollination, distinguishing self-pollination from cross-pollination
  • Describe double fertilization and its outcomes (zygote + endosperm)
  • Outline seed and fruit development and dispersal mechanisms
  • Distinguish between short-day, long-day, and day-neutral plants
  • Explain the role of phytochrome (Pr and Pfr) in photoperiodism

1. Flower Structure: The Reproductive Organs

Flowers are the reproductive structures of angiosperms (flowering plants). A complete flower contains four whorls of modified leaves.

1.1 The Four Whorls

Whorl Structure Function
Calyx Sepals** (usually green) Protect the developing flower bud before it opens
Corolla Petals** (often brightly colored) Attract pollinators (insects, birds) with color, pattern, scent, and nectar guides
Androecium Stamens** (male reproductive organs) Produce and release pollen (male gametophyte)
Gynoecium Carpels/Pistil** (female reproductive organs) Receive pollen; house ovules; develop into fruit

Draw and label a longitudinal section of a flower: Start from the bottom — receptacle (flower base). Going inward: sepals (outermost), petals, then stamens surrounding the central pistil. Label each structure clearly.

1.2 The Stamen: Male Reproductive Organ

Each stamen consists of:

Part Structure Function
Anther Usually two-lobed, four pollen sacs Produces pollen grains** through meiosis of microspore mother cells → microspores → pollen grains (male gametophytes). Each pollen grain contains a tube nucleus and a generative nucleus (which divides to form two sperm cells).
Filament Slender stalk Positions the anther for effective pollen dispersal (to contact pollinators or release into wind)

1.3 The Carpel/Pistil: Female Reproductive Organ

The gynoecium may consist of one carpel or multiple fused carpels. A single pistil or each carpel has:

Part Structure Function
Stigma Sticky tip at the top Receives pollen** during pollination. Sticky surface or feathery (wind-pollinated) to trap pollen grains.
Style Elongated stalk Connects stigma to ovary; pollen tube grows through the style.
Ovary Swollen base Contains ovules. After fertilization, the ovary develops into the fruit.
Ovules Within the ovary Each ovule contains the female gametophyte (embryo sac) with the egg cell and two polar nuclei. After fertilization, each ovule develops into a seed.

Draw and label the internal structure of an ovule: Draw the ovule attached to the ovary wall by the funiculus. Label: "Integuments" (outer layers → become seed coat), "Micropyle" (small opening for pollen tube entry), "Embryo sac," "Egg cell," "Two polar nuclei" (central cell), "Synergids" (flanking the egg).

2. Pollination: Getting Pollen to the Stigma

Pollination is the transfer of pollen grains from the anther to the stigma. It is NOT the same as fertilization — pollination is just the delivery; fertilization happens later when the sperm and egg fuse.

2.1 Types of Pollination

Type Description
Self-pollination Pollen from a flower's anther lands on the same flower's stigma or another flower on the same plant
Cross-pollination Pollen from the anther of one plant lands on the stigma of a different plant of the same species

2.2 Pollination Vectors

Vector Flower Adaptations Examples
Insects** (bees, butterflies, moths, beetles, flies) Brightly colored petals (often UV patterns visible to insects), scent, nectar guides, nectar reward Roses, orchids, sunflowers
Birds** (hummingbirds) Red/orange tubular flowers, abundant dilute nectar, no strong scent (birds have poor smell) Trumpet vine, fuchsia
Wind Small, inconspicuous petals or none, large feathery stigmas, abundant lightweight pollen, anthers hanging outside flower Grasses, wheat, maize, oak, birch
Water Pollen released underwater and drifts to female flowers Seagrasses, some aquatic plants
Bats Large, pale, night-blooming flowers, strong fruity scent, abundant nectar Some cacti, baobab

2.3 Promoting Cross-Pollination

Many plants have mechanisms to avoid self-pollination and promote cross-pollination:

Mechanism How It Works Example
Dioecy Separate male and female plants (individual plants are either male or female) Holly, asparagus, date palm
Dichogamy Male and female parts of the same flower mature at different times Protandry (anthers mature first): many Asteraceae; Protogyny (stigma matures first): some grasses
Self-incompatibility Biochemical mechanism prevents pollen from fertilizing ovules of the same plant — pollen tube growth is blocked if pollen and stigma share the same self-incompatibility (S) alleles Many fruit trees (apple, cherry), Brassicas
Heterostyly Different flower morphs have different style lengths (pin vs. thrum) → promotes cross-pollination between morphs Primula (primrose)

IB Exam Tip (Paper 1): Be clear that cross-pollination (not self-pollination) promotes genetic variation. Self-pollination is essentially a form of inbreeding — it reduces genetic diversity. Mechanisms that promote cross-pollination are adaptations to increase genetic variation in offspring.

3. Double Fertilization: Unique to Angiosperms

Double fertilization is the hallmark of flowering plants. Watch the numbers — they're specific and testable.

3.1 Pollen Tube Growth

  1. When a compatible pollen grain lands on the stigma, it absorbs water and germinates.
  2. The tube nucleus directs the growth of a pollen tube that grows down the style, through the micropyle, into the embryo sac.
  3. During pollen tube growth, the generative nucleus divides by mitosis to produce two sperm cells (male gametes).

3.2 The Two Fertilization Events

When the pollen tube reaches the embryo sac and discharges its contents:

Fertilization Event What Fuses Product Ploidy
First fertilization One sperm (n) + Egg cell (n) Zygote (2n) → develops into the embryo Diploid (2n)
Second fertilization One sperm (n) + Two polar nuclei (n + n) Endosperm nucleus (3n) → develops into endosperm Triploid (3n)

Draw and label double fertilization: Draw the embryo sac inside the ovule. Show the pollen tube entering through the micropyle. Label: "Pollen tube," "Two sperm cells," "Egg cell (n)," "Two polar nuclei (n + n)." Below: "Sperm + Egg → Zygote (2n) → Embryo. Sperm + 2 polar nuclei → Endosperm (3n) — nutrient tissue."

Double fertilization: A unique feature of angiosperms where two fertilization events occur in a single embryo sac: one sperm fertilizes the egg to form the diploid zygote, and the second sperm fuses with the two polar nuclei to form the triploid endosperm.

3.3 Why Triploid Endosperm?

The endosperm is a nutrient-rich tissue that nourishes the developing embryo. It can be thought of as "packed lunch" for the embryo.

Having 3n endosperm means:

  • More copies of genes for nutrient production → more efficient synthesis of starch, proteins, and lipids.
  • The triploid nature may contribute to the vigor and abundance of the endosperm.

In some seeds (e.g., maize, wheat, coconut), the endosperm persists and is the main nutrient store. In others (e.g., beans, peas), the embryo absorbs the endosperm during development and stores nutrients in the cotyledons (seed leaves).

4. Seed and Fruit Development

4.1 From Ovule to Seed

After fertilization:

Ovule Structure Develops Into...
Zygote** (2n) Embryo** — consists of:
— Plumule: embryonic shoot (develops into stem and leaves)
— Radicle: embryonic root
— Cotyledon(s): seed leaves (monocots: 1; dicots: 2) — absorb or store nutrients
Endosperm** (3n) Nutrient tissue for the embryo (may be absorbed by cotyledons in some species)
Integuments Seed coat (testa)** — tough, protective outer layer
Micropyle Small pore in seed coat for water uptake during germination

Draw and label a seed (e.g., bean): Draw a kidney-shaped seed. Label: "Testa (seed coat)," "Micropyle" (tiny hole), "Hilum" (scar where seed was attached to ovary), "Embryo" showing "Plumule," "Radicle," and "Cotyledons."

4.2 From Ovary to Fruit

The fruit is a mature ovary. Its primary function is to protect seeds and aid in their dispersal.

Fruit Type Description Examples
Fleshy fruits Ovary wall (pericarp) becomes soft, often sweet and colorful — attracts animals for dispersal Tomato, apple, peach, cucumber
Dry dehiscent fruits Dry ovary that splits open at maturity to release seeds Pea pod, poppy capsule
Dry indehiscent fruits Dry ovary that does NOT split open; seed is dispersed with the fruit Achene (sunflower), nut (acorn), grain/ caryopsis (maize — fruit wall fused to seed coat)

4.3 Seed Dispersal

Plants have evolved diverse mechanisms to disperse seeds:

Method Adaptations Examples
Wind Lightweight seeds, wings (maple samara), or plumes/parachutes (dandelion pappus) Maple, dandelion, orchid, sycamore
Animal (external) Hooks or barbs that attach to fur/feathers Burdock, cocklebur
Animal (internal) Fleshy, edible fruit; seeds pass through digestive tract unharmed (scarification may even aid germination) Tomato, blackberry, apple
Water Buoyant, waterproof fruits or seeds Coconut, water lily
Explosive/mechanical Tension builds in drying fruit walls → sudden release flings seeds Touch-me-not (Impatiens), gorse, pea pods

5. Photoperiodism: How Plants Measure Day Length

Photoperiodism is the response of plants to the relative lengths of day and night. It controls flowering in many species, ensuring that flowering occurs at the appropriate season.

5.1 Critical Discovery: It's Night Length, Not Day Length

Early researchers thought plants measured day length. However, experiments showed that it is actually the uninterrupted dark period (night length) that matters.

Classic experiment:

  • Short-day plant (chrysanthemum): given a long night (14 hours dark), it flowers. If a brief flash of light interrupts the long night, it does NOT flower.
  • Long-day plant (spinach): given a short night (8 hours dark), it flowers. Adding a brief interruption of the night with light promotes flowering even more.

Conclusion: Plants measure the length of the uninterrupted dark period, not the light period. A night interruption (night break) with just a few minutes of light cancels the effect of the long night.

5.2 Categories of Plants

Category When They Flower Night Requirement Examples
Short-day plants (SDP) Flower when the night is LONG (exceeds a critical length) — typically autumn/winter Night must be longer than a critical duration Chrysanthemum, poinsettia, soybean, rice, strawberry, tobacco (some varieties)
Long-day plants (LDP) Flower when the night is SHORT (less than a critical length) — typically spring/summer Night must be shorter than a critical duration Spinach, clover, lettuce, wheat (spring varieties), Arabidopsis
Day-neutral plants Flower regardless of day/night length No night length requirement Tomato, cucumber, maize, many weeds

IB Exam Tip (Paper 1): Don't be fooled by the names — short-day plants actually need long nights to flower. The name is historical (they flower when days are short), but the mechanism is night-length dependent. "Short-day plant" = "long-night plant." "Long-day plant" = "short-night plant."

5.3 Practical Application: Controlling Flowering

Commercial growers manipulate photoperiod to control flowering:

  • Chrysanthemums (SDP — naturally flower in autumn): To produce flowers year-round for the cut flower market, growers artificially extend nights using blackout curtains during long summer days. To prevent flowering (keep plants vegetative), they interrupt the long night with a brief flash of light.
  • Poinsettias (SDP — naturally flower in winter): Commercial growers provide artificially long nights (~14 hours) by covering plants with black cloth in late afternoon. Even a brief light interruption can ruin the crop.

6. The Phytochrome System: The Molecular Mechanism

Plants measure night length using phytochrome, a photoreceptor protein. Phytochrome exists in two interconvertible forms:

6.1 The Two Forms

Form Name Absorption Peak Biological Activity
Pr Phytochrome red Absorbs red light at ~660 nm Biologically **inactive
Pfr Phytochrome far-red Absorbs far-red light at ~730 nm Biologically **active

6.2 Interconversion

The two forms are photoconvertible:

  • Red light (660 nm) converts Pr → Pfr
  • Far-red light (730 nm) converts Pfr → Pr
  • In darkness, Pfr slowly and spontaneously reverts to Pr over several hours

Draw and label: Draw a cycle diagram. Pr on one side, Pfr on the other. Arrow labeled "Red light (660 nm)" going from Pr → Pfr. Arrow labeled "Far-red light (730 nm)" going from Pfr → Pr. Arrow labeled "Dark reversion (slow)" going from Pfr → Pr. Label: "Pr — inactive form," "Pfr — active form."

6.3 How Phytochrome Measures Night Length

The key insight: Pfr is the biologically active form, and it slowly reverts to Pr in the dark. By measuring how much Pfr remains at the end of the night, the plant knows how long the night has been.

During the day: Sunlight is rich in red light → most phytochrome is converted to Pfr. The plant has high Pfr levels.

During the night: No light → Pfr slowly reverts to Pr (dark reversion). The longer the night, the more Pfr reverts to Pr.

At dawn: The plant measures the Pfr/Pr ratio:

  • Short night (e.g., 8 hours): Some Pfr reverted, but significant Pfr remains → high Pfr/Pr ratio at dawn.
  • Long night (e.g., 14 hours): Most Pfr has reverted to Pr → low Pfr/Pr ratio at dawn.

6.4 Pfr and Flowering Response

Plant Type Effect of High Pfr (at dawn) Flowering Response
Short-day plant (SDP) Pfr INHIBITS flowering High Pfr (short night) → NO flowering; Low Pfr (long night) → FLOWERING
Long-day plant (LDP) Pfr PROMOTES flowering High Pfr (short night) → FLOWERING; Low Pfr (long night) → NO flowering

6.5 The Night Break Experiment — Explained

SDP during a long night, given a brief flash of red light in the middle:

  1. Before the flash: Pfr has been reverting to Pr in the dark.
  2. Red light flash: Pr is rapidly converted back to Pfr.
  3. After the flash: Pfr starts reverting again, but the night is now effectively "short" — not enough time for all Pfr to revert.
  4. End of night: Significant Pfr remains → plant "thinks" it was a short night → SDP does NOT flower.

What if you follow the red flash with far-red light?

  • Red flash: Pr → Pfr
  • Far-red flash immediately after: Pfr → Pr (reverses the effect)
  • Result: The far-red cancels the red light effect → plant "thinks" it's still a long night → SDP flowers.

This red/far-red reversibility is the classic proof that phytochrome is the photoreceptor.

Worked Example: Explaining Experimental Results

A researcher grows chrysanthemums (short-day plants) under the following light regimes:

Treatment Light Regime Result
A 10 hours light, 14 hours dark Flowering
B 10 hours light, 14 hours dark with 5-minute red light in middle of dark period No flowering
C 10 hours light, 14 hours dark with 5-minute red light, then 5-minute far-red light in middle Flowering

Explain each result:

A: 14 hours of uninterrupted dark = long night. Most Pfr reverts to Pr → low Pfr at dawn. In SDP, low Pfr promotes flowering → flowering occurs. ✓

B: The red light flash in the middle of the night rapidly converts remaining Pr → Pfr. The night is "interrupted" — only two short dark periods (e.g., 7 hours each) instead of one long one. Not enough time for all Pfr to revert → significant Pfr at dawn. High Pfr inhibits flowering in SDP → no flowering. ✓

C: The red flash creates Pfr, but the immediately following far-red flash converts it back to Pr. The far-red cancels the effect of the red light. The plant "perceives" an uninterrupted long night → low Pfr at dawn → flowering. ✓

IB Exam Tip (Paper 1 & 2): The red/far-red reversibility experiment is classic. You must know: (1) Red light = promotes Pfr formation = the effect depends on the plant type (inhibits SDP flowering, promotes LDP flowering). (2) Far-red light reverses the effect of red light. (3) If you're asked to design an experiment about photoperiodism, include a red light treatment and a red + far-red treatment to demonstrate phytochrome involvement.

Process Key Structures Key Events

Pollen tube growth Stigma → Style → Ovary → Micropyle Tube nucleus guides growth; generative nucleus divides → 2 sperm
Double fertilization Embryo sac (egg + polar nuclei) Sperm + Egg → Zygote (2n); Sperm + 2 polar nuclei → Endosperm (3n)
Seed development Ovule Zygote → Embryo; Integuments → Testa; Endosperm → nutrient tissue
Fruit development Ovary Mature ovary → Fruit; aids seed dispersal
Photoperiodism Leaves (phytochrome) Pr ⇄ Pfr; measures night length; controls flowering time

8. Common Misconceptions

Misconception Reality
"Pollination is the same as fertilization" Pollination = transfer of pollen to stigma. Fertilization = fusion of sperm and egg. They are separate events that may be separated by hours or days.
"The pollen grain is the male gamete" The pollen grain is the male gametophyte — it contains the tube nucleus and a generative nucleus, which divides to produce two sperm (the actual male gametes).
"Double fertilization produces two embryos" No — it produces one embryo (2n) and one endosperm (3n). The endosperm is nutritive tissue, not an embryo.
"The fruit is just the ovary wall" In true fruits, yes — but some "fruits" (like apples and strawberries) include accessory tissue (receptacle) — these are "false fruits" or accessory fruits.
"Short-day plants flower when days are short" They flower when nights are long. The mechanism detects night length, not day length. The name is misleading.
"Plants measure day length to flower" Plants measure the length of the uninterrupted dark period using phytochrome.
"Phytochrome is in the flowers" Phytochrome is located in the leaves, where light is perceived. The flowering signal (florigen) is then transmitted to the shoot apical meristem.
"All plants are photoperiodic" Many plants are day-neutral and flower based on other cues (age, temperature, developmental stage) rather than photoperiod.

Answer 2

(a) The pollen tube grows into the embryo sac and discharges two sperm cells [1]. One sperm fertilizes the egg cell (n + n) → diploid zygote (2n) → develops into embryo [1]. The second sperm fuses with the two polar nuclei (n + n + n) → triploid endosperm nucleus (3n) → develops into endosperm (nutrient tissue). [1]

(b) The endosperm is triploid because one haploid sperm (n) fuses with the two haploid polar nuclei (n + n), giving n + n + n = 3n [1]. The triploid nature may provide metabolic advantages for efficient nutrient synthesis to support the developing embryo. [1]

(c) Integuments → seed coat (testa), which protects the embryo [1]. Ovary → fruit, which protects seeds and aids dispersal [1].

Answer 3

(a) Cross-pollination increases genetic variation in the offspring [1]. This produces populations with a wider range of genotypes, increasing the chance that some individuals can survive environmental changes or disease outbreaks. Self-pollination reduces genetic diversity — essentially inbreeding. [1]

(b) Any two of the following (2 marks each):

  • Dioecy: Male and female flowers are on separate plants, so pollen must travel from a male plant to a female plant — self-pollination is impossible.
  • Dichogamy: Anthers and stigmas of the same flower mature at different times. Protandry: anthers release pollen before the stigma is receptive. Protogyny: stigma is receptive before anthers release pollen.
  • Self-incompatibility: A genetic mechanism where pollen tube growth is blocked if the pollen grain and stigma share the same self-incompatibility (S) alleles. This prevents self-fertilization.
  • Heterostyly: Different flower morphs have different style lengths (pin-eyed vs. thrum-eyed), physically separating anthers and stigmas and promoting cross-pollination between morphs.

(a) Fleshy fruits are typically brightly colored and sweet/nutritious when ripe, attracting animals (birds, mammals) to eat them [1]. The seeds pass through the animal's digestive tract unharmed (often with the seed coat scarified, which may aid germination) and are deposited at a distance from the parent plant in feces. [1]

(b) Advantages of seed dispersal [1 each]:

  • Reduces competition: Seedlings are less likely to compete with the parent plant or each other for light, water, and nutrients.
  • Colonization of new habitats: Seeds may reach favorable environments where the species can establish.
  • Reduces predation: Seed and seedling predators (herbivores, pathogens) concentrated near the parent plant are avoided by dispersed offspring.
  • Gene flow: Dispersal mixes genes between populations, maintaining genetic diversity.
