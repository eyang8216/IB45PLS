1.1 The Water Molecule: Why Water Is Special

The Basics: Atomic Structure of H₂O

Every water molecule — H₂O — consists of one oxygen atom covalently bonded to two hydrogen atoms. Let's unpack that sentence from first principles.

An atom is the smallest unit of an element. Oxygen (O) and hydrogen (H) are two different elements. In a water molecule, these atoms are held together by covalent bonds — bonds where atoms share electrons. Think of electrons as the "glue" between atoms. In a covalent bond, each atom contributes one electron to the shared pair, and both atoms feel like they "own" that pair.

In H₂O, the oxygen atom shares one pair of electrons with each hydrogen atom. That gives us two O–H covalent bonds.

Electronegativity and Polarity

Now here's the crucial detail: not all atoms pull on shared electrons equally. This pull is called electronegativity. Oxygen is far more electronegative than hydrogen — oxygen pulls the shared electrons closer to itself.

Imagine a tug-of-war where one person (oxygen) is much stronger than the other (hydrogen). The shared electrons spend more time near the oxygen nucleus than near the hydrogen nuclei. The result? The oxygen end of the molecule becomes slightly negative (δ⁻, pronounced "delta negative"), and each hydrogen end becomes slightly positive (δ⁺).

This separation of charge — one end slightly negative, the other slightly positive — is called a dipole, and the molecule is described as polar. Water is a polar molecule.

Polar molecule: A molecule with an uneven distribution of electrical charge, resulting in partial positive (δ⁺) and partial negative (δ⁻) regions. Water is the classic example because oxygen's high electronegativity pulls electrons away from hydrogen.

The Bent Shape Matters

You might ask: if water has two δ⁺ hydrogens on one side and a δ⁻ oxygen on the other, doesn't that cancel out? It doesn't — because water is not linear. The two O–H bonds form an angle of about 104.5°, giving the molecule a bent, V-like shape. This means the two bond dipoles add together rather than cancel. The oxygen side of the V is the negative pole, and the hydrogen side is the positive pole.

1.2 Hydrogen Bonding: The Key to Everything

What Is a Hydrogen Bond?

Because water molecules are polar, the δ⁺ hydrogen of one water molecule is electrically attracted to the δ⁻ oxygen of a different water molecule. This attraction is a hydrogen bond (H-bond).

A hydrogen bond is not a covalent bond. It is much weaker — about 1/20th the strength of an O–H covalent bond. But it is strong enough to matter enormously when millions of them act together.

Hydrogen bond: A weak intermolecular attraction between a slightly positive hydrogen atom (δ⁺) on one polar molecule and a slightly negative atom (δ⁻, usually oxygen or nitrogen) on another polar molecule.

Key distinction: Covalent bonds = within a molecule (intramolecular). Hydrogen bonds = between molecules (intermolecular). Water molecules are held to each other by H-bonds; the atoms within each water molecule are held by covalent bonds.

Draw this: Draw three water molecules with dotted lines between the H of one and the O of another. Label the dotted lines as "hydrogen bond" and the solid O–H lines as "covalent bond".

Why Hydrogen Bonding Is Central to IB Biology

Every property of water that makes life possible — cohesion, adhesion, high specific heat capacity, high latent heat of vaporization, solvent ability, ice density — arises directly from water's ability to form hydrogen bonds. The IB exam will repeatedly ask you to trace a biological phenomenon back to "because of hydrogen bonding between water molecules." Memorize this chain of reasoning:

> Polarity → Hydrogen bonding → Property → Biological significance

1.3 Cohesion, Adhesion, and Surface Tension

Cohesion

Cohesion is the tendency of water molecules to stick to each other via hydrogen bonds. Within liquid water, each molecule is constantly forming, breaking, and re-forming H-bonds with its neighbors. The collective effect is that water "pulls together."

Cohesion: The attraction between molecules of the same substance. In water, this is due to hydrogen bonding between water molecules.

Biological example — Transpiration Pull in Plants:

This is one of the most important IB examples. Water moves from the roots to the leaves of a plant through xylem vessels. The driving force is transpiration — the evaporation of water from stomata (pores) in the leaves. As water evaporates from leaf cell walls, it creates a tension (negative pressure) that pulls water up the xylem. This works because:

  1. Water molecules are cohesive — they stick to each other via H-bonds.
  2. So when one water molecule is pulled at the top of the column, it pulls the next one, and the next one, all the way down to the roots.
  3. The continuous column of water in the xylem does not break because of cohesion.

Think of a chain of people holding hands. If you pull the first person, the whole chain moves because they are all holding on to each other. That's cohesion in action.

Adhesion

Adhesion is the tendency of water molecules to stick to other polar surfaces (not other water molecules). The δ⁺ and δ⁻ charges on water can form H-bonds with polar groups on other substances, such as the cellulose in plant cell walls.

Adhesion: The attraction between molecules of different substances, such as water molecules and the polar surfaces of xylem vessel walls (cellulose).

Biological example — Capillary Action in Xylem:

As water is pulled up the xylem by transpiration, it also adheres to the hydrophilic (water-loving) cellulose walls of the xylem vessels. This adhesion helps counteract gravity and keeps the water column in contact with the vessel walls. Together, cohesion and adhesion produce capillary action — the ability of water to rise in narrow tubes against gravity.

Draw this: Draw a long vertical tube (xylem vessel). Inside it, draw small circles (water molecules) touching each other (cohesion) and touching the walls of the tube (adhesion). Draw arrows pointing upward. Label "Transpiration pull from leaves" at the top.

Surface Tension

At the surface of water (the air-water interface), water molecules experience an unbalanced pull. Molecules in the bulk liquid are pulled in all directions equally by H-bonds with neighbors. But molecules at the surface have no water molecules above them — only air. So they are pulled sideways and downward more strongly than upward.

The result is that the surface behaves like an elastic skin — this is surface tension.

Surface tension: The property of a liquid's surface that allows it to resist an external force, caused by the cohesion (H-bonding) between liquid molecules at the surface.

Biological example — Pond Skaters (Gerridae):

Pond skater insects can walk on the surface of ponds without sinking because their weight is distributed over long legs, and the surface tension of the water supports them. The H-bonds at the surface resist being broken. The insect's feet dent the surface but don't break through.

1.4 Thermal Properties of Water

High Specific Heat Capacity

Specific heat capacity is the amount of energy required to raise the temperature of 1 g of a substance by 1°C. Water has an exceptionally high specific heat capacity: approximately 4.2 J g⁻¹ °C⁻¹.

Why? When you heat water, much of the energy goes into breaking hydrogen bonds rather than increasing the kinetic energy (motion) of the molecules. Temperature measures average kinetic energy. Because energy is diverted into breaking H-bonds, the kinetic energy — and thus the temperature — rises slowly.

Specific heat capacity: The energy (in joules) required to raise the temperature of 1 gram of a substance by 1°C. Water has a high value (~4.2 J g⁻¹ °C⁻¹) compared to most other substances.

Biological significance:

  1. Thermal stability of aquatic habitats: Oceans, lakes, and rivers change temperature very slowly. This provides a stable environment for aquatic organisms, which are often adapted to narrow temperature ranges. A sudden temperature swing could denature their enzymes.
  1. Thermal stability of organisms: Since organisms are mostly water (~70% of cell mass), their internal temperature resists rapid changes. This is especially important for endothermic (warm-blooded) animals maintaining a constant body temperature.
  1. Coastal climates: Large bodies of water moderate the climate of nearby land, keeping coastal areas cooler in summer and warmer in winter than inland areas.

IB Tip: The exam may ask, "Explain why water's high specific heat capacity is important for living organisms." Your answer must include (a) the reason (energy used to break H-bonds rather than increase kinetic energy) and (b) a specific biological consequence (e.g., stable aquatic temperatures).

High Latent Heat of Vaporization

Latent heat of vaporization is the energy required to convert 1 g of liquid water into water vapor (gas) at boiling point — without changing its temperature. For water, this is very high: about 2260 J g⁻¹.

Why? To turn liquid water into a gas, you must break all the hydrogen bonds holding water molecules together in the liquid state. That requires a lot of energy.

Latent heat of vaporization: The heat energy required to change 1 gram of a liquid into a gas at constant temperature. Water's value is exceptionally high due to the need to break all hydrogen bonds.

Biological significance — Cooling by Sweating and Transpiration:

When you sweat, water on your skin evaporates. To evaporate, each water molecule must absorb a large amount of energy from your skin to break its H-bonds. This draws heat away from your body, cooling you down. The same principle applies to transpiration in plants — evaporation of water from leaves cools the leaf surface.

This is an extremely efficient cooling mechanism. Think about it: a small amount of sweat can remove a large amount of heat because each gram of evaporating water takes 2260 J with it.

Worked Example:

Question: An athlete produces 10 g of sweat during exercise. How much heat energy is removed from the athlete's body when all this sweat evaporates? (Latent heat of vaporization of water = 2260 J g⁻¹)

Solution:

Step 1: Identify what you know.

  • Mass of sweat: m = 10 g
  • Latent heat of vaporization: L = 2260 J g⁻¹

Step 2: Use the formula: Energy (Q) = m × L

Step 3: Q = 10 × 2260 = 22,600 J

Answer: 22,600 J (or 22.6 kJ) of heat energy is removed from the athlete's body. To put this in perspective, that's enough energy to raise the temperature of about 5.4 L of water by 1°C.

High Latent Heat of Fusion

Water also has a high latent heat of fusion — the energy that must be removed to freeze water. This means water resists freezing. For organisms living in cold environments, the water inside their cells does not freeze easily, providing a buffer against cold temperatures.

1.5 The Density Anomaly: Why Ice Floats

The Strange Behavior of Water at 4°C

Most substances become denser as they cool (molecules move less, pack closer together). Water follows this rule — until it reaches about 4°C. Below 4°C, water begins to expand. When it freezes at 0°C, it expands by about 9%, making ice less dense than liquid water. That's why ice floats.

Why does this happen? In liquid water, molecules are constantly moving and H-bonds are constantly breaking and re-forming. As water cools to 4°C, molecules slow down and pack more tightly — density increases. But below 4°C, the H-bonds start to lock into a rigid, crystalline lattice. In this lattice (ice), each water molecule is H-bonded to four neighbors in a tetrahedral arrangement. The lattice has a lot of open space — it's more "spread out" than the random arrangement of liquid water. Hence, ice is less dense.

Draw this: Draw a hexagonal (honeycomb-like) arrangement of water molecules for ice, showing the open spaces. Next to it, draw a more random, closer-packed arrangement for liquid water. Label both "Ice (hydrogen-bonded lattice, less dense)" and "Liquid water (more dense)".

Biological Significance: Pond Insulation

When a pond freezes in winter:

  1. The surface water cools, becomes denser, and sinks (until 4°C).
  2. As the surface continues to cool below 4°C, it becomes less dense and stays at the top.
  3. Ice forms on the surface and floats.

The ice layer acts as an insulator, preventing the water below from freezing. This allows aquatic organisms (fish, amphibians, invertebrates) to survive the winter in the liquid water beneath the ice. If ice were denser than water, ponds would freeze from the bottom up, killing all aquatic life.

IB Exam Tip: "Explain why ice floats and why this is biologically significant" is a classic Paper 2 question worth 3–4 marks. Your answer should hit:

  1. In ice, water molecules form a crystalline lattice held by H-bonds (1 mark)
  2. The lattice has more open space, so ice is less dense than liquid water (1 mark)
  3. Ice forms on the surface of water bodies, insulating the water below and allowing aquatic life to survive in winter (1 mark)

Don't just say "ice is less dense" — you must explain why (the H-bonded lattice).

1.6 Water as a Solvent: The Universal Solvent

How Water Dissolves Polar and Ionic Substances

Water is often called the "universal solvent" — it dissolves more substances than any other liquid. This is, again, because of its polarity.

Dissolving ionic compounds (e.g., NaCl — table salt):

When NaCl crystals are placed in water:

  1. The δ⁺ hydrogens of water molecules are attracted to the Cl⁻ ions.
  2. The δ⁻ oxygens of water molecules are attracted to the Na⁺ ions.
  3. Water molecules surround each ion, forming a hydration shell.
  4. The attraction between Na⁺ and Cl⁻ is weakened because each ion is now more attracted to water molecules than to each other.
  5. The ions separate and become dissolved.

Draw this: Draw an Na⁺ ion surrounded by water molecules with their δ⁻ oxygen ends pointing toward the positive ion. Draw a Cl⁻ ion surrounded by water molecules with their δ⁺ hydrogen ends pointing toward the negative ion.

Dissolving polar molecules (e.g., glucose, amino acids):

Polar molecules have δ⁺ and δ⁻ regions (like the –OH hydroxyl groups on glucose). Water forms H-bonds with these polar groups, pulling the molecules into solution.

Hydrophilic vs. Hydrophobic:

  • Hydrophilic ("water-loving"): Substances that dissolve easily in water because they are polar or charged (e.g., glucose, ions, amino acids).
  • Hydrophobic ("water-fearing"): Substances that do not dissolve in water because they are nonpolar (e.g., lipids, oils, hydrocarbons). They cannot form H-bonds with water.

Solvent: A liquid in which other substances (solutes) dissolve. Water is a powerful solvent for polar and ionic substances because its dipole can interact with and surround charged particles.

Biological Significance

  1. Blood plasma (which is ~92% water) transports dissolved substances throughout the body: glucose, amino acids, ions (Na⁺, K⁺, Ca²⁺, Cl⁻), hormones, urea, and dissolved gases (O₂, CO₂).
  1. Cytoplasm and tissue fluid are water-based solutions where all metabolic reactions occur. Enzymes, substrates, and products are dissolved.
  1. Xylem sap carries dissolved mineral ions from roots to leaves.
  1. Excretion: Urea (a polar nitrogenous waste) dissolves in water to form urine, allowing its removal from the body.

1.7 Water in Metabolism: Reactant and Product

Water is not just the medium where reactions happen — it is often a direct participant in biochemical reactions.

Water as a Reactant: Hydrolysis

Hydrolysis (hydro = water, lysis = splitting) is a reaction where water is used to break a covalent bond in a larger molecule, splitting it into two smaller molecules.

General reaction: A–B + H₂O → A–OH + H–B

Biological examples:

Reaction Substrate Products Enzyme
Digestion of starch Starch (polysaccharide) Maltose (disaccharide) Amylase
Digestion of proteins Polypeptide Amino acids Proteases
Digestion of lipids Triglyceride Fatty acids + glycerol Lipases
ATP hydrolysis ATP ADP + Pi (+ energy) ATPase

In each case, a water molecule is consumed. The –OH group attaches to one fragment and the –H to the other fragment.

Water as a Product: Condensation

Condensation (also called dehydration synthesis) is the reverse of hydrolysis. Two smaller molecules join to form a larger molecule, and a water molecule is released.

General reaction: A–OH + H–B → A–B + H₂O

Biological examples:

Reaction Monomers Polymer Water produced?
Polypeptide synthesis Amino acids Polypeptide (protein) Yes — from –OH of carboxyl and –H of amine
Polysaccharide synthesis Glucose Starch / Glycogen Yes — from –OH groups on adjacent glucose
Polynucleotide synthesis Nucleotides DNA / RNA Yes — from –OH groups
Triglyceride synthesis Glycerol + 3 fatty acids Triglyceride Yes — 3 water molecules (one per ester bond)

Draw two amino acids side by side. On the left one, circle the –OH of the carboxyl group. On the right one, circle the –H of the amine group. Remove these and show them forming H₂O. Show the remaining parts joined by a peptide bond (–CO–NH–). Label "Condensation reaction — water is produced."

Photosynthesis and Respiration: The Grand Water Reactions

Water is central to both of the most important metabolic pathways in biology:

  • Photosynthesis (summary): 6CO₂ + 6H₂O → C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂

Water is a reactant. In the light-dependent reactions, water is split (photolysis): 2H₂O → 4H⁺ + 4e⁻ + O₂. The electrons replace those lost by chlorophyll, and O₂ is released as a waste product.

  • Aerobic respiration (summary): C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂ → 6CO₂ + 6H₂O

Water is a product. At the end of the electron transport chain, oxygen accepts electrons and combines with H⁺ to form water: ½O₂ + 2H⁺ + 2e⁻ → H₂O.

Notice the beautiful symmetry: water is consumed in photosynthesis and produced in respiration.

IB Exam Tip — NOS (Nature of Science): The IB loves linking water's properties to scientific methodology. You might be asked: "Explain how the properties of water make it a suitable medium for the first living organisms." Link each property to a specific need:

  • Solvent → allows dissolved nutrient transport
  • High specific heat → stable thermal environment
  • Ice floats → protection from freezing
  • Cohesion → capillary action in primitive transport systems

1.8 Comparison of Methane and Water

The IB syllabus sometimes asks you to compare water with methane (CH₄) to highlight the importance of polarity and H-bonding. Here's the key contrast:

Property Water (H₂O) Methane (CH₄)
Molecular mass 18 Da 16 Da
Polarity Polar (O is electronegative) Nonpolar (C–H bonds are nonpolar)
Hydrogen bonding? Yes No
State at room temperature Liquid Gas
Boiling point 100°C −161.5°C
Specific heat capacity High (~4.2 J g⁻¹ °C⁻¹) Low
Solvent for polar substances? Excellent Poor

Despite having a very similar molecular mass to methane (18 vs 16 daltons), water is a liquid at room temperature while methane is a gas. The difference is entirely due to hydrogen bonding — water molecules are "sticky" and require much more energy to separate into gas.

1.9 Practice Problems

Problem 1 (Paper 1 — Multiple Choice)

Which property of water explains its ability to act as a coolant in sweating?

A. High specific heat capacity

B. High latent heat of vaporization

C. Cohesion

D. Surface tension

Answer

B. High latent heat of vaporization. When sweat evaporates, it requires a large amount of energy (latent heat) to break the H-bonds holding water molecules together. This energy is drawn from the skin, cooling the body.

A is tempting but wrong: Specific heat capacity relates to temperature stability, not evaporative cooling.

Problem 2 (Paper 2 — Structured)

Explain how the cohesive properties of water contribute to the transport of water in plants. (3 marks)

Answer — Model Response

  1. Water molecules are cohesive because of hydrogen bonding between the δ⁺ hydrogen of one water molecule and the δ⁻ oxygen of another. ✓
  2. As water evaporates from the leaf via transpiration, tension (negative pressure) is created at the top of the xylem. ✓
  3. Due to cohesion, the entire column of water is pulled upward as one continuous stream without breaking, from roots to leaves. ✓

Problem 3 (Paper 2 — Data-based)

The specific heat capacity of water is 4.2 J g⁻¹ °C⁻¹, while that of ethanol is 2.4 J g⁻¹ °C⁻¹. Suggest why water has a higher specific heat capacity than ethanol. (2 marks)

Answer

  1. Water can form hydrogen bonds; although ethanol also has an –OH group, water forms a more extensive H-bond network because each water molecule can form up to 4 H-bonds (two from its hydrogens, two accepted by its oxygen). ✓
  2. More energy is needed to break the more extensive H-bond network in water, so less energy goes into raising kinetic energy (temperature). ✓

Problem 4 (Paper 2 — Essay-style)

Outline the biological significance of the fact that ice is less dense than liquid water. (3 marks)

Answer — Model Response

  1. In ice, water molecules form a crystalline lattice held by hydrogen bonds, which has more open space than liquid water, making ice less dense. ✓
  2. Ice therefore floats on the surface of ponds, lakes, and oceans. ✓
  3. The ice layer insulates the liquid water below, preventing it from freezing completely and allowing aquatic organisms to survive in winter. ✓

1.10 Common Misconceptions

Misconception The Truth
"Hydrogen bonds are strong like covalent bonds." Hydrogen bonds are weak intermolecular forces, about 1/20th the strength of covalent bonds. Their collective effect is strong.
"Water's polarity means it's electrically charged." Water is electrically neutral overall. It has partial charges (δ⁺ and δ⁻), not full charges like ions.
"Ice is denser than water because solids are denser than liquids." Water is an exception. The H-bonded lattice of ice is more open, so ice is less dense.
"Specific heat capacity and latent heat are the same thing." Specific heat capacity is about raising temperature; latent heat is about changing state without changing temperature.
"Hydrolysis is the same as dissolving in water." Hydrolysis is a chemical reaction that breaks covalent bonds using water. Dissolving is a physical process — no covalent bonds are broken.
