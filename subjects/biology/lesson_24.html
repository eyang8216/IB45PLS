What Is an Adaptation?

In everyday language, "adaptation" might mean any adjustment you make — wearing a coat when it's cold, learning to use a new phone. In biology, the term is much more specific:

Adaptation (biological): A heritable trait — structural, physiological, or behavioral — that enhances the survival and reproductive success (fitness) of an organism in a particular environment. Adaptations are the product of natural selection acting over generations.

Three critical features distinguish an adaptation:

  1. It is heritable — encoded in genes and passed to offspring. A bodybuilder's large muscles are not an adaptation (they're acquired, not inherited). A cheetah's streamlined body is (it's genetic).
  1. It enhances fitness — it makes the organism more likely to survive and reproduce in its specific environment. Not every trait is an adaptation; some are byproducts or neutral.
  1. It is specific to an environment — what's adaptive in one context may be maladaptive in another. Thick fur is adaptive in the Arctic, maladaptive in the Sahara.

Adaptation vs. Acclimatization — A Crucial Distinction

Feature Adaptation Acclimatization
Timescale Generations (evolutionary) Within an organism's lifetime
Heritability Yes — genetic No — physiological adjustment
Reversibility Not reversible within an individual Reversible (usually)
Example: High Altitude Tibetan populations have EPAS1 gene variant for lower hemoglobin (prevents thickening of blood). This is genetic, inherited. A lowlander visiting altitude increases RBC count over weeks. This is acclimatization — temporary, not passed to children.
Example: Temperature Arctic foxes have thick fur, small ears (genetic). A human in winter develops more brown fat, shivers less — this acclimatization disappears in summer.

IB Exam Tip: The adaptation vs. acclimatization distinction appears frequently in exams. Always state: "An adaptation is heritable and results from natural selection over generations, while acclimatization is a short-term physiological adjustment within an organism's lifetime that is NOT inherited." Give clear examples for each.

Part 1: Types of Adaptations

Adaptations can be categorized into three broad types, though many organisms use combinations:

Structural Adaptations — Physical Features

These are anatomical or morphological traits — the physical "hardware" of the organism.

The Camel — A Desert Survivor:

The camel is a masterwork of structural (and physiological) adaptation to the desert:

  • Hump stores fat, not water: This is one of the most common misconceptions. The hump is a deposit of adipose tissue — up to 36 kg of fat. This fat serves as an energy reserve when food is scarce. Critically, when fat is metabolized, it yields metabolic water (about 1.1 g of water per gram of fat oxidized). This water contributes to the camel's water balance, but the fat is primarily an energy store.
  • Concentrated urine: Camel kidneys are extraordinarily efficient at water conservation. They produce urine that is highly concentrated (up to 2-3× more concentrated than human urine), and the urine can become almost syrupy. The loops of Henle in the nephrons are exceptionally long, enabling maximum water reabsorption.
  • Dry feces: Camels reabsorb so much water in the large intestine that their feces are so dry they can be used as fuel for fires immediately upon excretion.
  • Nasal countercurrent heat exchanger: The camel's nasal passages are long and convoluted. During exhalation, warm, moist air from the lungs passes over the cool nasal surfaces, where water vapor condenses and is reabsorbed. During inhalation, dry desert air is warmed and humidified as it passes over the same surfaces. This recovers a significant fraction of respiratory water loss.
  • Long eyelashes and slit-like nostrils: Protect against blowing sand and reduce water loss. The nostrils can be closed completely during sandstorms.
  • Wide, padded feet: Spread the camel's weight, preventing it from sinking into sand (like snowshoes).
  • Variable body temperature: Unlike most mammals which tightly regulate body temperature, camels can allow their body temperature to fluctuate 6-7°C over the course of a day (from ~34°C at night to ~41°C by late afternoon). This reduces the need for evaporative cooling (sweating), saving enormous amounts of water. They essentially "store" heat during the day and dissipate it at night.

The Cactus — A Plant Survivor:

  • Succulent stem: The thick, fleshy, green stem stores water in large, thin-walled parenchyma cells. It also performs photosynthesis (since leaves are reduced to spines). The stem can expand like an accordion when water is available and shrink during drought.
  • Spines instead of leaves: Leaves are reduced to spines, which:
  • Dramatically reduce surface area for water loss
  • Provide shade and create a boundary layer of still air around the stem, reducing convective water loss
  • Protect against herbivores
  • CAM photosynthesis: (Covered in Lesson 20) — stomata open at night, CO₂ fixed into malic acid, Calvin cycle runs during the day with stomata closed.
  • Extensive shallow root system: Cactus roots spread widely (up to 15 meters from the plant) just below the soil surface, allowing rapid absorption of even small amounts of rainfall before it evaporates. Some cacti also have a taproot for anchorage.
  • Thick, waxy cuticle: A waterproof coating on the stem epidermis drastically reduces evaporative water loss.
  • Ribbed or fluted shape: The ribs provide shade (self-shading), allow the stem to expand and contract with water content, and channel dew and rainwater toward the roots.

Physiological Adaptations — Internal Processes

These involve metabolic, biochemical, or homeostatic processes within the organism.

Osmoregulation — The Water Balance Challenge:

Every aquatic organism faces a fundamental problem: its internal solute concentration may differ from that of the surrounding water, creating an osmotic gradient that drives water in or out.

Freshwater Fish (e.g., carp, trout):

The problem: A freshwater fish's body fluids are hyperosmotic (higher solute concentration, lower water potential) compared to the surrounding freshwater. Water constantly enters the fish's body by osmosis (through the gills and skin). Meanwhile, salts tend to diffuse out (down their concentration gradient).

If unchecked, the fish would swell like a water balloon and lose essential ions.

Solutions (adaptations):

  • Produce copious amounts of very dilute urine: The kidneys excrete large volumes of water while retaining salts. Freshwater fish urine can be 10× more dilute than their blood.
  • Active uptake of ions by the gills: Specialized chloride cells (ionocytes) in the gill epithelium actively transport Na⁺ and Cl⁻ from the water into the blood, against their concentration gradients. This requires ATP.
  • Relatively impermeable skin and scales: Reduces water influx.
  • Don't drink water: Unlike marine fish, freshwater fish do not drink — they already have excess water.

Marine Bony Fish (e.g., cod, tuna):

The problem: A marine fish's body fluids are hypoosmotic (lower solute concentration, higher water potential) compared to seawater. Water constantly leaves the fish's body by osmosis. If unchecked, the fish would dehydrate.

Solutions (adaptations):

  • Drink seawater: Marine fish actively drink significant amounts of seawater to replace water lost by osmosis.
  • Excrete excess salt via the gills: Chloride cells in the gills actively transport Na⁺ and Cl⁻ out of the blood into the seawater. This is energetically expensive but essential.
  • Produce small volumes of concentrated urine: The kidneys conserve water by excreting minimal, highly concentrated urine (mostly Mg²⁺ and SO₄²⁻, which are the ions the gills don't excrete).
  • Relatively impermeable skin.

Cartilaginous Fish (sharks, rays):

Sharks use a different strategy. They retain urea in their blood (at concentrations that would be toxic to other vertebrates), making their body fluids slightly hyperosmotic to seawater. This means water enters them slightly by osmosis (like freshwater fish), so they don't face the dehydration problem of bony marine fish. They also have trimethylamine oxide (TMAO) to counteract the destabilizing effects of urea on proteins.

Summary — Osmoregulation:

Feature Freshwater Fish Marine Bony Fish
Body vs. Environment Hyperosmotic (body more concentrated) Hypoosmotic (body less concentrated)
Water Movement Water enters by osmosis Water leaves by osmosis
Drinking Does not drink Drinks seawater
Urine Copious, very dilute Small volume, concentrated
Ion Transport at Gills Active uptake of Na⁺/Cl⁻ Active excretion of Na⁺/Cl⁻
Main Challenge Excess water, ion loss Dehydration, excess ions

Behavioral Adaptations — What Organisms Do

These are actions or patterns of behavior that enhance survival.

Migration:

Seasonal movement of populations from one region to another, typically to exploit resources or avoid harsh conditions.

  • Arctic terns: Migrate ~70,000 km annually from Arctic breeding grounds to Antarctic waters and back — the longest migration of any animal. They experience two summers each year, maximizing feeding opportunities.
  • Wildebeest: Over 1.5 million wildebeest migrate in a circular route through the Serengeti-Mara ecosystem, following seasonal rainfall and new grass growth.
  • Monarch butterflies: Multi-generational migration — it takes 3-4 generations to complete the round trip from Mexico to Canada and back.

Hibernation:

A state of prolonged torpor (greatly reduced metabolic activity) during winter, allowing animals to survive periods of cold and food scarcity.

  • Physiological changes during hibernation:
  • Metabolic rate drops to 2-5% of normal
  • Body temperature decreases (often to near ambient, but maintained above freezing)
  • Heart rate drops dramatically (e.g., ground squirrel: from 200-300 bpm to 3-10 bpm)
  • Breathing slows significantly
  • The animal relies on stored fat reserves accumulated during summer/autumn
  • Examples: Ground squirrels, bats, hedgehogs, some bears (though bear hibernation is less deep — their body temperature drops only ~5°C, not to near-freezing)

Estivation (Aestivation):

Summer equivalent of hibernation — a state of torpor during hot, dry periods.

  • Lungfish: When their pond dries up, lungfish burrow into the mud and secrete a mucus cocoon. They estivate for months or even years, breathing air through a small opening, until rains return.
  • Desert snails: Seal themselves to rocks with dried mucus and remain dormant for months or years until rain.

Part 2: Thermoregulation — Endotherms and Ectotherms

Endotherms ("Warm-Blooded")

Endotherms generate most of their body heat internally through metabolic processes. They maintain a relatively constant, high body temperature regardless of environmental temperature (within limits).

Examples: Mammals, birds

Advantages:

  • Can remain active in cold environments (enzymes function optimally at stable temperatures)
  • Can sustain high levels of aerobic activity (high metabolic rate)
  • Can exploit a wide range of habitats

Disadvantages:

  • Enormous energy cost — endotherms must consume much more food than ectotherms of similar size
  • A significant proportion of energy intake goes to maintaining body temperature

Mechanisms of thermoregulation in endotherms:

In cold environments:

  • Vasoconstriction in skin (reduces heat loss)
  • Piloerection (raising fur/feathers — traps insulating layer of air)
  • Shivering (involuntary muscle contractions produce heat)
  • Non-shivering thermogenesis (brown adipose tissue — specialized fat with abundant mitochondria containing uncoupling protein/thermogenin that allows H⁺ to leak across the inner mitochondrial membrane, generating heat instead of ATP)
  • Behavioral: Huddling, seeking shelter, curling up (reduces surface area)

In hot environments:

  • Vasodilation in skin (increases heat loss)
  • Sweating (evaporative cooling — water evaporating from skin carries away heat). Each gram of evaporated sweat removes ~2.4 kJ of heat.
  • Panting (rapid shallow breathing increases evaporative heat loss from the respiratory tract)
  • Behavioral: Seeking shade, being active at cooler times (dawn/dusk), spreading out (increases surface area)

Ectotherms ("Cold-Blooded")

Ectotherms rely primarily on external sources of heat to regulate body temperature. Their body temperature fluctuates with the environment.

Examples: Most fish, amphibians, reptiles, all invertebrates

Advantages:

  • Low energy requirement — an ectotherm of the same size as an endotherm needs only ~10% of the food energy
  • Can survive long periods without food
  • More of consumed energy goes to growth and reproduction

Disadvantages:

  • Activity limited by environmental temperature (sluggish when cold)
  • Cannot sustain prolonged high-intensity activity
  • Restricted to environments with adequate external heat

Behavioral thermoregulation in ectotherms:

Ectotherms aren't purely passive — many behaviorally regulate their temperature:

  • Basking in the sun (lizards on rocks, butterflies with wings spread) — increases heat absorption
  • Changing body orientation (perpendicular to sun for maximum heating, parallel for minimum)
  • Seeking shade or burrowing (when too hot)
  • Changing color (some reptiles can darken to absorb more heat)
  • Swimming to different water depths (fish)
  • Evaporative cooling (some reptiles gape/mouth-breathe when hot)

Part 3: Convergent Evolution

Convergent evolution occurs when unrelated species independently evolve similar traits because they face similar environmental pressures. The resulting structures are called analogous structures (similar function, different evolutionary origin).

This is one of the most powerful pieces of evidence for natural selection — similar challenges lead to similar solutions, even in completely different lineages.

Classic Examples of Convergent Evolution

1. Wings — Flight Evolved Independently Multiple Times:

  • Bird wings: Modified forelimbs with feathers (evolved from theropod dinosaurs)
  • Bat wings: Modified forelimbs with skin membrane stretched between elongated finger bones (mammals)
  • Insect wings: Outgrowths of the exoskeleton, not modified limbs (evolved independently at least twice within insects — Pterygota)
  • Pterosaur wings: Extinct flying reptiles — skin membrane stretched from an enormously elongated fourth finger to the body

All four groups evolved flight independently. The wings are analogous (same function — flight) but have fundamentally different developmental origins and structures. They are NOT homologous (homologous = same evolutionary origin, may have different functions — e.g., human arm, whale flipper, bat wing all derive from the same ancestral tetrapod forelimb).

2. Streamlined Body Shape — Fast Swimming:

  • Dolphins (mammals)
  • Sharks (cartilaginous fish)
  • Ichthyosaurs (extinct marine reptiles)
  • Tuna (bony fish)

All have a torpedo-shaped body, dorsal fin, and powerful tail for efficient swimming — but they belong to entirely different vertebrate classes. The shape is analogous, shaped by the physics of moving through water.

3. Camera-Type Eyes:

  • Vertebrate eyes (retina inverted — light passes through neurons before reaching photoreceptors)
  • Cephalopod eyes (squid, octopus — retina is the right way around, more "sensible" design)

Both groups independently evolved complex, lens-based camera eyes — extremely similar in overall structure but with different embryological origins and fine details.

4. Specialized Plant Defenses:

  • Euphorbia (Africa) and Cactaceae (Americas): Both are succulent, spiny, drought-adapted plants that look nearly identical. But they belong to completely different plant families and evolved on different continents — their similarities are convergent.

5. Antifreeze Proteins:

  • Evolved independently in Arctic and Antarctic fish, and in some insects and plants. Different protein structures, same function — preventing ice crystal formation in body fluids.

Part 4: Adaptations to Extreme Environments

Deep Sea

The deep ocean (below ~1000 m) is one of Earth's most extreme habitats: near-freezing temperatures (2-4°C), crushing pressure, total darkness, extreme food scarcity.

Adaptations:

  • Bioluminescence: The most common adaptation in the deep sea. Organisms produce light through chemical reactions (luciferin + luciferase). Uses include:
  • Attracting prey (anglerfish lure)
  • Counter-illumination camouflage (light organs on the belly match the faint light from above, eliminating silhouette)
  • Mate attraction
  • Confusing predators
  • Large mouths and expandable stomachs: Food is so scarce that when a meal is encountered, it must not be lost. Many deep-sea fish (e.g., gulper eel, black swallower) have enormous mouths and highly distensible stomachs, allowing them to swallow prey larger than themselves.
  • Chemosynthesis at hydrothermal vents: Instead of photosynthesis (no light), entire ecosystems are based on chemosynthesis. Bacteria oxidize hydrogen sulfide (H₂S) from the vents to produce organic molecules:

$$6CO_2 + 6H_2O + 3H_2S \rightarrow C_6H_{12}O_6 + 3H_2SO_4$$

These bacteria form the base of a food chain that supports giant tube worms, clams, shrimp, and fish — an ecosystem completely independent of solar energy.

  • Reduced or absent eyes: Many deep-sea species have tiny or non-functional eyes. Others (that live in the twilight zone, 200-1000 m) have enormous, highly sensitive eyes.
  • Slow metabolism, infrequent reproduction: Energy conservation is paramount.
  • Reduced calcified skeletons: High pressure and low carbonate availability make heavy skeletons impractical. Many deep-sea organisms are gelatinous.

High Altitude

As altitude increases, atmospheric pressure (and thus pO₂) decreases. At 4,000 m, pO₂ is about 60% of sea level. At 8,848 m (Everest summit), it's ~33%.

Acclimatization (short-term, non-heritable):

  • Increased breathing rate and depth (hyperventilation — driven by carotid body chemoreceptors sensing low pO₂)
  • Increased heart rate and cardiac output
  • Increased erythropoietin (EPO) production by kidneys → increased RBC production → higher hematocrit
  • Increased 2,3-BPG in RBCs → right-shifts hemoglobin dissociation curve → easier O₂ unloading in tissues
  • Increased capillary density in muscles (over weeks to months)

Adaptations (genetic, heritable) in high-altitude human populations:

Tibetan populations:

  • EPAS1 gene variant: This gene (encoding HIF-2α, a transcription factor involved in hypoxia response) has a variant that results in lower hemoglobin levels at altitude compared to acclimatized lowlanders. This seems paradoxical but is adaptive: excessively high hematocrit makes blood viscous, increasing strain on the heart and risk of thrombosis. Tibetans maintain adequate O₂ delivery with less thick blood.
  • Higher resting ventilation and larger lung volumes
  • Higher levels of nitric oxide (NO) — a vasodilator — improving blood flow

Andean populations (Quechua, Aymara):

  • Different genetic adaptations — higher hemoglobin concentrations than Tibetans, suggesting different evolutionary solutions to the same problem
  • Larger lung volumes, barrel chests
  • Higher myoglobin concentrations in muscle

Other high-altitude animals:

  • Bar-headed goose: Migrates over the Himalayas at altitudes exceeding 9,000 m. Adaptations include: hemoglobin with higher O₂ affinity, exceptionally efficient lungs with cross-current gas exchange, dense capillary networks in flight muscles, and mitochondria positioned close to the sarcolemma to reduce O₂ diffusion distance.
  • Yak: Thick fur, large lungs and heart, fetal-type hemoglobin (higher O₂ affinity) persists into adulthood.

Desert

Covered in detail earlier (camel, cactus), but additional adaptations:

  • Nocturnal activity: Many desert animals are active only at night, avoiding peak heat and reducing evaporative water loss.
  • Kangaroo rat: Never drinks water — obtains all water from metabolic water (from seeds it eats) and produces extremely concentrated urine. Nasal countercurrent system recovers respiratory water.
  • Fennec fox: Enormous ears — not just for hearing, but for thermoregulation. The large surface area, richly supplied with blood vessels, allows heat dissipation (like a car radiator).

Part 5: Ecological Niches

What Is a Niche?

Ecological niche: The role of a species within its ecosystem — encompassing all the biotic and abiotic conditions it needs to survive, reproduce, and maintain a viable population. This includes: what it eats, what eats it, where it lives, when it's active, its temperature and moisture requirements, its reproductive strategy, and all its interactions with other species.

A niche is often described as a species' "profession" within the ecosystem, while its habitat is its "address."

Fundamental Niche vs. Realized Niche

This is one of the most important concepts in ecology:

Fundamental niche: The full range of environmental conditions and resources a species could theoretically occupy and use in the absence of competition, predation, or other biotic constraints. It's the "potential" niche — limited only by abiotic (physical/chemical) factors.

Realized niche: The actual range of conditions and resources a species occupies in nature, constrained by biotic interactions — particularly competition, but also predation, parasitism, and mutualism. The realized niche is always a subset of the fundamental niche.

Analogy: Imagine you're capable of playing any position on a soccer team (fundamental niche). But on your actual team, the striker position is already taken by a better player, so you end up playing midfield (realized niche). Your fundamental niche includes striker; your realized niche doesn't, because of competition.

Diagram to Draw — Fundamental vs. Realized Niche:

Draw two overlapping or nested rectangles.

The larger rectangle represents the Fundamental Niche — all conditions the species could theoretically use. Label it broadly.

Inside it, draw a smaller, irregular shape — the Realized Niche — "Actual niche when competitors/predators are present."

Add an arrow showing "Competition, predation, etc." compressing the fundamental niche into the realized niche.

Below, add a real example:

Barnacles on a rocky shore:

  • Chthamalus (small barnacle): Fundamental niche = entire intertidal zone. Realized niche = upper zone only (excluded from lower zone by competition with Semibalanus, which outcompetes it but cannot survive desiccation in the upper zone).
  • Semibalanus (large barnacle): Realized niche = lower intertidal zone (cannot survive in upper zone due to desiccation — an abiotic limit).

Joseph Connell's classic experiment demonstrated this: when he removed Semibalanus from the lower zone, Chthamalus happily colonized it — proving competition was compressing its realized niche.

Part 6: The Competitive Exclusion Principle

Competitive Exclusion Principle (Gause's Principle): Two species with identical niches cannot coexist indefinitely in the same habitat. One will inevitably outcompete the other, driving it to local extinction (extirpation) or forcing it to shift its niche.

If two species coexist, their niches must differ in some significant way.

Gause's Experiments with Paramecium

Georgy Gause, a Russian ecologist, provided the classic experimental demonstration of competitive exclusion in the 1930s. He grew different species of Paramecium (single-celled ciliates) in laboratory cultures with a constant food supply.

Experiment 1: P. aurelia vs. P. caudatum

  • When grown separately, each species thrived, producing a logistic (S-shaped) growth curve.
  • When grown together in the same culture (same food source), P. caudatum was driven to extinction. P. aurelia didn't directly attack P. caudatum — it was simply more efficient at utilizing the limited food, reproducing faster, and gradually starving out its competitor.

Key result: Two species with essentially identical niches (same food, same conditions) could not coexist — competitive exclusion.

Experiment 2: P. aurelia vs. P. bursaria

  • When grown together, these two species coexisted.
  • Why? P. bursaria tends to feed near the bottom of the culture (on bacteria associated with settled debris), while P. aurelia feeds in the water column. Their niches were sufficiently different to allow coexistence — an example of niche partitioning.

Conditions for Coexistence

For two competing species to coexist, their niches must differ in at least one of the following:

  1. Resource type: They eat different things (e.g., different seed sizes)
  2. Spatial habitat: They use different locations (e.g., different heights in a tree)
  3. Temporal activity: They're active at different times (e.g., nocturnal vs. diurnal)
  4. Some other niche dimension

Part 7: Niche Partitioning — The Evidence from Nature

Niche partitioning (also called resource partitioning) is the process by which competing species evolve differences in resource use, allowing them to coexist. It reduces the intensity of competition.

MacArthur's Warblers

Robert MacArthur (1958) studied five species of warbler (small insect-eating birds) that all lived in the same spruce forests in New England. At first glance, they seemed to have identical niches — all eating insects in the same trees. How did five species coexist?

MacArthur's meticulous observations revealed a beautiful pattern: each species concentrated its feeding in a different zone of the tree:

  • Cape May warbler: Fed mostly in the crown (top) and on outer branches
  • Blackburnian warbler: Fed mostly in the crown but on inner branches near the trunk
  • Black-throated green warbler: Fed in the middle zone, mostly inner branches
  • Bay-breasted warbler: Fed in the middle zone, mostly outer branches
  • Myrtle warbler: Fed in the lower zone and often on the ground

Additionally, they had slightly different feeding behaviors (hovering vs. gleaning vs. fly-catching), different preferred insect prey sizes, and different nesting times.

This spatial partitioning of the tree allowed all five species to coexist without competitive exclusion.

Darwin's Finches

The famous Galápagos finches demonstrate niche partitioning through beak morphology:

  • Large ground finch: Large, deep beak → cracks large, hard seeds
  • Medium ground finch: Medium beak → medium seeds
  • Small ground finch: Small beak → small, soft seeds
  • Cactus finch: Long, probing beak → feeds on cactus flowers and fruits
  • Warbler finch: Thin, pointed beak → catches insects

Each beak is adapted to a specific food type, reducing dietary overlap. When drought reduces seed availability, the differences become even more critical — Peter and Rosemary Grant documented natural selection in action, with beak depth changing measurably between drought and wet years.

Anolis Lizards

In the Caribbean, multiple Anolis lizard species coexist on the same islands by partitioning their habitat vertically and structurally:

  • Trunk-crown anoles: Live on the upper trunk and in the canopy
  • Trunk-ground anoles: Live on the lower trunk and on the ground
  • Twig anoles: Live on narrow twigs at the periphery of trees
  • Grass-bush anoles: Live in grass and low bushes

Each "ecomorph" has characteristic body proportions, limb lengths, and toe pad sizes adapted to its specific microhabitat. Remarkably, similar sets of ecomorphs evolved independently on different islands (convergent evolution at the community level).

Part 8: Keystone Species

Keystone species: A species whose impact on its ecosystem is disproportionately large relative to its abundance. Removal of a keystone species triggers a cascade of changes that dramatically alter community structure and ecosystem function.

The term comes from architecture: the keystone is the central stone at the top of an arch that, if removed, causes the entire arch to collapse.

Classic Examples of Keystone Species

1. Sea Otters (Enhydra lutris) — Pacific Kelp Forests

Sea otters are a textbook keystone species:

  • Sea otters eat sea urchins, which are herbivores that graze on kelp
  • When sea otters were hunted to near-extinction for their fur in the 18th-19th centuries, sea urchin populations exploded
  • Urchins overgrazed the kelp, creating "urchin barrens" — vast areas of bare rock where lush kelp forests once stood
  • Kelp forests provide habitat and food for hundreds of species (fish, invertebrates, marine mammals)
  • The collapse of kelp forests caused a trophic cascade — effects rippling down through the food web
  • When sea otters were reintroduced (e.g., in parts of Alaska and California), kelp forests recovered, and biodiversity rebounded

What makes sea otters a keystone species: They're not particularly abundant, but by controlling urchin populations, they maintain the entire kelp forest ecosystem. Remove them, and the ecosystem collapses.

2. Pisaster Starfish — Rocky Intertidal Zone

Robert Paine's (1966) classic experiment on the Washington coast:

  • Pisaster ochraceus is a predatory starfish that feeds on mussels, barnacles, limpets, and chitons
  • Paine removed Pisaster from experimental plots and left control plots unchanged
  • Within months, the removed plots were dominated by mussels (Mytilus californianus), which outcompeted and crowded out nearly all other species
  • Species diversity in removal plots plummeted from ~15 species to ~3-4 species
  • In control plots, Pisaster preferentially ate the dominant competitor (mussels), preventing any single species from monopolizing space and maintaining high biodiversity

This demonstrates: A predator can be a keystone species not because it's abundant, but because it prevents competitive exclusion by the dominant competitor, thereby maintaining diversity.

3. Wolves in Yellowstone

Gray wolves were exterminated from Yellowstone National Park in the 1920s. They were reintroduced in 1995. The effects were profound and surprising:

  • Without wolves: Elk populations grew unchecked. Elk overbrowsed willow, aspen, and cottonwood saplings along streams. This reduced habitat for beavers (which need trees for dams and food). Fewer beaver dams meant altered stream flow, increased erosion, and degraded fish habitat. Songbird populations dependent on riparian vegetation declined.
  • With wolves reintroduced: Wolves reduced elk numbers and changed elk behavior (elk avoided areas where they were vulnerable — stream corridors). Vegetation recovered along waterways. Beavers returned. Beaver dams created ponds that supported fish, amphibians, and waterfowl. Stabilized stream banks reduced erosion. Even the courses of rivers changed — more meandering, less straight-channel erosion.

This is a trophic cascade — a top predator influencing species at multiple lower trophic levels all the way down to the physical structure of the ecosystem.

4. Other Keystone Examples:

  • African elephants: "Ecosystem engineers" — they knock down trees, creating grasslands and maintaining savanna habitats. Without elephants, many savannas would become closed woodlands.
  • Beavers: Build dams that create wetlands — entire ecosystems that wouldn't otherwise exist. A single beaver family can transform a stream into a pond ecosystem supporting dozens of species.
  • Prairie dogs: Their burrows aerate soil and provide habitat for other species (burrowing owls, black-footed ferrets, snakes). Their grazing maintains short-grass prairie.
  • Figs (as keystone mutualists): In tropical forests, figs fruit year-round and sustain frugivores (fruit-eating animals) during seasons when other fruits are unavailable. Remove figs, and many animal species would starve during lean periods.

IB Exam Tip: When describing a keystone species, always (1) identify the species and its role, (2) describe the ecosystem effects when it is present (high biodiversity, stable community), (3) describe what happens when it is removed (trophic cascade, reduced biodiversity, ecosystem shift), and (4) explain why its effect is disproportionate to its abundance. Use specific named examples (Pisaster, sea otters, wolves) — named examples earn marks.

Part 9: Using Niche Concepts to Predict Species Distribution

Ecological niche modeling (also called species distribution modeling) uses data about where a species currently lives to predict where it could live, where it might spread under climate change, or where conservation efforts should be focused.

Climate Envelope Models

A climate envelope is the range of climatic conditions (temperature, precipitation, seasonality) within which a species currently occurs. By mapping where those climate conditions will exist in the future (under climate change scenarios), scientists can predict:

  • Range shifts: Where species may need to move to track their climate envelope
  • Range contractions: Areas that will become unsuitable
  • Invasive species risk: Where an introduced species might establish

Limitations: Climate envelope models don't account for:

  • Biotic interactions (competition, predation, mutualism — a species might not be able to colonize a climatically suitable area if a strong competitor is already there)
  • Dispersal ability (can the species actually get there?)
  • Evolutionary adaptation (species may evolve to tolerate new conditions)
  • Microhabitats (a species may find local refuges even if the regional climate changes)

Common Misconceptions

Myth #1: "A camel stores water in its hump."

Reality: The hump stores fat — an energy reserve. The metabolic water produced when this fat is oxidized does contribute to water balance, but the primary purpose is energy storage, not water storage. Camels conserve water through concentrated urine, dry feces, nasal countercurrent exchange, and variable body temperature — not through a "water tank" in the hump.

Myth #2: "Adaptation and acclimatization are the same thing."

Reality: Adaptation is genetic, heritable, and occurs over generations through natural selection. Acclimatization is a reversible physiological adjustment within an organism's lifetime. An Inuit person and a person from tropical Africa placed in the Arctic at birth would not differ in cold tolerance (assuming similar clothing/shelter) — cold adaptation in human populations is largely acclimatization and cultural, not genetic.

Myth #3: "If two species coexist, they must have different fundamental niches."

Reality: They may have similar fundamental niches but different realized niches — they coexist because competitive interactions have forced niche differentiation (or one species is restricted to a subset of its fundamental niche).

Myth #4: "Keystone species are always predators."

Reality: While many classic examples are predators (sea otters, Pisaster, wolves), keystone species can also be herbivores (elephants), ecosystem engineers (beavers, prairie dogs), mutualists (figs), or even plants (foundation species that create habitat for others).

Myth #5: "The competitive exclusion principle means competing species can never coexist."

Reality: It means species with identical niches cannot coexist. Species with similar but not identical niches can coexist through resource partitioning — and this is exactly what we observe in nature.

Practice Problems

Problem 1: Adaptation vs. Acclimatization

A student claims that a person who moves from sea level to a high-altitude city and develops a higher red blood cell count has "adapted" to altitude. Critically evaluate this claim.

Click for Answer

The student is using "adapted" in the everyday sense, but in biological terms, this is acclimatization, not adaptation.

Why it's acclimatization:

  • The increased RBC count develops within weeks — it's a physiological adjustment within the individual's lifetime
  • It's reversible — if the person moves back to sea level, their RBC count returns to baseline
  • It is not heritable — the person's children, if born at sea level, will not inherit an elevated RBC count
  • The mechanism: reduced pO₂ is sensed by the kidneys → increased EPO production → stimulates bone marrow to produce more RBCs

Why it's NOT an adaptation:

  • It's not encoded in genes and passed to offspring
  • It does not result from natural selection acting over generations

A true adaptation to high altitude would be genetic and heritable — for example, the EPAS1 gene variant in Tibetan populations that results in lower (not higher) hemoglobin levels at altitude, which is more adaptive in the long term (avoids the complications of excessively viscous blood).

Summary: The RBC increase is acclimatization (short-term, reversible, physiological, not heritable). Genetic differences in high-altitude populations are adaptations (heritable, result of natural selection).

Problem 2: Osmoregulation

Compare and contrast the osmoregulatory challenges and solutions of a freshwater bony fish and a marine bony fish.

Click for Answer

Freshwater Bony Fish:

Challenge: Body fluids are hyperosmotic to the environment (higher solute concentration than freshwater). Water constantly enters by osmosis through gills/skin. Ions tend to diffuse out (down concentration gradient).

Solutions:

  • Does not drink water
  • Produces copious volumes of very dilute urine to eliminate excess water
  • Actively transports ions (Na⁺, Cl⁻) into the blood at the gills (chloride cells)
  • Relatively impermeable skin/scales

Marine Bony Fish:

Challenge: Body fluids are hypoosmotic to the environment (lower solute concentration than seawater). Water constantly leaves by osmosis. The fish is in constant danger of dehydration. Ions tend to diffuse in.

Solutions:

  • Drinks seawater to replace lost water
  • Produces small volumes of concentrated urine to conserve water
  • Actively transports excess ions (Na⁺, Cl⁻) out of the blood at the gills
  • Relatively impermeable skin/scales

Key contrast: The gills do opposite jobs — active ion uptake in freshwater fish vs. active ion excretion in marine fish. The kidneys also work oppositely: high-volume dilute urine vs. low-volume concentrated urine. Both require significant energy expenditure (ATP for active transport).

Problem 3: Competitive Exclusion and Niche Partitioning

(a) State the competitive exclusion principle.

(b) Describe Gause's experiment with P. aurelia and P. caudatum. What was the result and what does it demonstrate?

(c) Explain how MacArthur's warblers avoid competitive exclusion.

Click for Answer

(a) The competitive exclusion principle states that two species with identical niches cannot coexist indefinitely in the same habitat. One will outcompete the other, leading to its local extinction or forcing a niche shift.

(b) Gause's experiment: Gause grew P. aurelia and P. caudatum in separate cultures and in mixed culture with limited food (bacteria). In separate cultures, both thrived. In mixed culture, P. aurelia outcompeted P. caudatum, which was driven to extinction. P. aurelia did not attack P. caudatum — it was simply more efficient at utilizing the shared food resource, reproducing faster and starving out its competitor. This demonstrated the competitive exclusion principle experimentally.

However, when Gause grew P. aurelia with P. bursaria, they coexisted because P. bursaria fed near the bottom of the culture while P. aurelia fed in the water column — their niches were sufficiently different (niche partitioning).

(c) MacArthur's warblers: Five warbler species coexisted in the same spruce forests by partitioning the tree spatially. Each species concentrated its feeding in a different zone: Cape May (crown, outer), Blackburnian (crown, inner), Black-throated green (middle, inner), Bay-breasted (middle, outer), Myrtle (lower zone and ground). By specializing in different parts of the tree and having slightly different feeding behaviors and prey sizes, they reduced direct competition, allowing all five species to coexist. This is resource/niche partitioning — each species' realized niche is a subset of its fundamental niche, constrained by competition.

Problem 4: Keystone Species

Explain why the sea otter is considered a keystone species. Include the concept of a trophic cascade and describe the consequences of otter removal and reintroduction.

Click for Answer

Sea otters as a keystone species:

Sea otters feed on sea urchins. Sea urchins are herbivores that graze on kelp. This creates a simple food chain: kelp → urchins → otters.

When otters are present (healthy ecosystem):

  • Sea otter predation keeps urchin populations in check
  • Kelp forests thrive, providing habitat and food for hundreds of species (fish, invertebrates, marine mammals)
  • High biodiversity, complex three-dimensional habitat

When otters are removed (e.g., by hunting):

  • Sea urchin populations explode (release from predation)
  • Urchins overgraze kelp, creating "urchin barrens" — bare rock where kelp once grew
  • Kelp forest ecosystem collapses → fish and invertebrate populations crash → biodiversity plummets
  • This is a trophic cascade — removal of a top predator causes effects that cascade down through lower trophic levels to the base of the food web

Reintroduction:

  • Where sea otters have been reintroduced, urchin populations decline, kelp forests recover, and biodiversity rebounds

Why otters are a keystone species:

They are not particularly abundant, but their effect is disproportionate — they maintain the entire kelp forest ecosystem by controlling urchin populations. Without them, the ecosystem fundamentally shifts (from kelp forest to urchin barren). This disproportionate effect relative to abundance is the defining characteristic of a keystone species.

Other keystone examples: Pisaster starfish (controls mussels → maintains intertidal biodiversity), wolves in Yellowstone (control elk → allow vegetation recovery → changed river courses → cascade through entire ecosystem).

Problem 5: Convergent Evolution

Distinguish between homologous and analogous structures. Use the wings of birds and the wings of insects to illustrate your answer. Explain why analogous structures provide evidence for evolution by natural selection.

Click for Answer

Homologous structures: Structures that share a common evolutionary origin (derived from the same ancestral structure) but may have different functions in different species. They reflect divergent evolution from a common ancestor.

  • Example: The forelimbs of mammals — human arm, whale flipper, bat wing, horse foreleg. All share the same basic bone arrangement (humerus, radius, ulna, carpals, metacarpals, phalanges) — homologous structures with different functions.

Analogous structures: Structures that have similar function but different evolutionary origins. They arise through convergent evolution — independent evolution of similar traits in unrelated lineages facing similar selective pressures.

  • Example: Bird wings vs. insect wings. Both are used for flight, but:
  • Bird wings are modified forelimbs with an internal skeleton (bones, joints), covered with feathers
  • Insect wings are outgrowths of the exoskeleton (cuticle), not modified limbs, with no internal bones
  • The last common ancestor of birds and insects did not have wings; wings evolved independently in each lineage
  • Their developmental origins, genetic basis, and structural materials are entirely different

Why analogous structures support evolution by natural selection:

Analogous structures demonstrate that similar environmental challenges lead to similar adaptive solutions, even in completely unrelated organisms. The fact that streamlined body shapes evolved independently in dolphins (mammals), sharks (fish), and ichthyosaurs (reptiles) suggests that natural selection, constrained by the physics of moving through water, predictably shapes similar forms. This pattern of convergence is exactly what we would expect if natural selection acts on heritable variation to optimize organisms for their environments.

Key Takeaways

  • Adaptation = heritable trait enhancing fitness, product of natural selection. Acclimatization = short-term, reversible physiological adjustment within lifetime.
  • Structural adaptations: Camel (fat hump, nasal countercurrent, concentrated urine), cactus (succulent stem, spines, CAM, shallow roots).
  • Physiological adaptations: Freshwater fish (dilute urine, active ion uptake) vs. marine fish (drink seawater, excrete ions, concentrated urine).
  • Endotherms generate internal heat (high energy cost, constant activity). Ectotherms rely on external heat (low energy cost, activity limited by temperature).
  • Convergent evolution → analogous structures (similar function, different origin): bird/bat/insect wings, dolphin/shark/ichthyosaur body shape.
  • Fundamental niche = potential range (abiotic limits only). Realized niche = actual range (constrained by competition, predation).
  • Competitive exclusion principle: Two species with identical niches cannot coexist. Demonstrated by Gause: P. aurelia excluded P. caudatum.
  • Niche partitioning allows coexistence: MacArthur's warblers (different tree zones), Darwin's finches (different beak/seed sizes), Anolis lizards (different microhabitats).
  • Keystone species: Disproportionately large effect relative to abundance. Sea otters → control urchins → protect kelp. Pisaster → controls mussels → maintains biodiversity. Wolves → trophic cascade in Yellowstone.
