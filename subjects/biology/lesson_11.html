1. What Is Biodiversity?

Biodiversity — a contraction of "biological diversity" — refers to the variety of life on Earth at all levels, from genes to ecosystems. It is not just "the number of species." IB requires you to understand biodiversity at three distinct levels.

1.1 Genetic Diversity

Genetic diversity is the total variety of alleles (gene variants) within a species or population.

Why it matters: Genetic diversity is the raw material for evolution and adaptation. A population with high genetic diversity has a broader range of traits. When the environment changes — a new disease emerges, the climate shifts — some individuals will likely possess alleles that allow them to survive and reproduce. A population with low genetic diversity is vulnerable: if no individual carries the right allele, the entire population may perish.

Example — Cheetahs (Acinonyx jubatus): Cheetahs have notoriously low genetic diversity. About 10,000–12,000 years ago, cheetahs passed through a severe population bottleneck (possibly reduced to a handful of individuals). As a result, modern cheetahs are genetically almost identical — they can accept skin grafts from unrelated individuals without immune rejection (something normally only possible between identical twins). This lack of genetic diversity makes them highly susceptible to diseases and reduces reproductive success (high rates of abnormal sperm, low cub survival).

Example — Irish Potato Famine: In the 1840s, Ireland's potato crop was almost entirely a single genetic variety (the "Lumper" potato). When the oomycete pathogen Phytophthora infestans (potato blight) arrived, the genetically uniform crop had no resistance. The blight destroyed the harvest, and approximately one million people starved. This is a tragic demonstration of why monocultures — crops with low genetic diversity — are dangerous.

Measuring genetic diversity: Scientists look at:

  • Heterozygosity: The proportion of individuals that are heterozygous at a given locus. Higher heterozygosity = higher genetic diversity.
  • Allelic richness: The number of different alleles at a locus.
  • Nucleotide diversity: The average number of nucleotide differences per base pair between individuals.

Genetic diversity: The variety of alleles (gene variants) within the gene pool of a species or population. It provides the capacity for populations to adapt to environmental changes and resist diseases. Low genetic diversity increases extinction risk.

1.2 Species Diversity

Species diversity has two components:

  1. Species richness: The number of different species in a community. A rainforest with 200 tree species has higher species richness than a boreal forest with 5 tree species.
  1. Species evenness (relative abundance): How equally individuals are distributed among species. A community where each species has roughly equal numbers has high evenness. A community dominated by one species with a few rare ones has low evenness — even if the species count is the same.

Why both matter: Consider two forests, each with 100 trees and 4 species:

Forest A Forest B
Species 1 25 trees 91 trees
Species 2 25 trees 3 trees
Species 3 25 trees 3 trees
Species 4 25 trees 3 trees
Richness 4 4
Evenness High Low

Both forests have the same species richness (4 species), but Forest A is far more diverse because individuals are evenly distributed. In Forest B, a single species dominates; if a disease affects Species 1, the entire forest ecosystem collapses. Forest A is more resilient.

1.3 Ecosystem Diversity

Ecosystem diversity refers to the variety of habitats, communities, and ecological processes in a region. It encompasses the diversity of:

  • Terrestrial ecosystems: Tropical rainforests, temperate grasslands, deserts, tundra, boreal forests, Mediterranean shrublands.
  • Aquatic ecosystems: Coral reefs, mangroves, kelp forests, freshwater wetlands, deep-sea hydrothermal vents, estuaries.

Why it matters: Different ecosystems support different species assemblages and provide different ecosystem services. A region with high ecosystem diversity (e.g., Costa Rica, with rainforests, cloud forests, dry forests, mangroves, and coral reefs in a small area) supports far more total biodiversity than a region dominated by a single ecosystem type.

Biodiversity: The variety of life at all levels of organization — genetic diversity (within species), species diversity (richness + evenness of communities), and ecosystem diversity (variety of habitats). These three levels are interconnected: genetic diversity enables species survival, species interactions create ecosystems, and diverse ecosystems provide the conditions for speciation.

2. Simpson's Diversity Index: Measuring Biodiversity

Simpson's diversity index is the mathematical tool IB requires you to master. It combines species richness AND evenness into a single number, allowing quantitative comparison of biodiversity between communities.

2.1 The Formula

The Simpson's reciprocal index is:

$$D = \frac{1}{\sum \left(\frac{n}{N}\right)^2}$$

Or, more commonly in IB, the Simpson's index of diversity is expressed as:

$$D = 1 - \sum \left(\frac{n}{N}\right)^2$$

Where:

  • n = total number of individuals of a particular species
  • N = total number of individuals of ALL species
  • Σ = sum across all species
  • (n/N)² = the proportion of individuals belonging to a species, squared

The term Σ(n/N)² is called "Simpson's dominance index." It represents the probability that two randomly selected individuals from the community belong to the same species.

  • If D = 0 → absolutely no diversity (only one species; Σ(n/N)² = 1, so 1 − 1 = 0).
  • As D approaches 1 → infinite diversity (many species, all equally abundant; Σ(n/N)² → 0).

Which formula does IB use? The IB uses both, but D = 1 − Σ(n/N)² is the standard one for "Simpson's diversity index." Always check which formula your question or dataset expects. The key insight is the same: larger values = greater diversity.

Simpson's diversity index: A mathematical measure of species diversity that accounts for both species richness (number of species) and species evenness (relative abundance). Formula: D = 1 − Σ(n/N)². Values range from 0 (no diversity) to nearly 1 (very high diversity). Higher D indicates greater biodiversity.

2.2 Worked Examples

Worked Example 1: Simple Calculation

Two meadows are surveyed for wildflower diversity. Calculate Simpson's index for each and determine which has higher diversity.

Meadow A:

Species Count (n) (n/N) (n/N)²
Daisy 20 20/100 = 0.20 0.0400
Buttercup 20 0.20 0.0400
Clover 20 0.20 0.0400
Dandelion 20 0.20 0.0400
Thistle 20 0.20 0.0400
Total N = 100 Σ = 0.2000

Dₐ = 1 − 0.2000 = 0.8000

Meadow B:

Species Count (n) (n/N) (n/N)²
Daisy 80 0.80 0.6400
Buttercup 5 0.05 0.0025
Clover 5 0.05 0.0025
Dandelion 5 0.05 0.0025
Thistle 5 0.05 0.0025
Total N = 100 Σ = 0.6500

D_b = 1 − 0.6500 = 0.3500

Conclusion: Meadow A (D = 0.80) has much higher diversity than Meadow B (D = 0.35), despite both having the same 5 species. This is because Meadow A has high evenness (equal numbers of each species), while Meadow B is dominated by daisies.

IB Application: This is a common data-based question — you're given species counts from multiple sites, asked to calculate Simpson's index, and then asked to interpret the results in terms of environmental quality.

IB Exam Tip: Simpson's index calculations are a guaranteed practical skill. Common pitfalls: (1) Forgetting to square n/N — students sometimes just sum n/N, which gives 1.0 every time. (2) Confusing n and N — n is per species, N is the grand total. (3) Reporting D as a percentage — D is a proportion between 0 and 1 (though you can multiply by 100 for a percentage if the question specifically asks). (4) Claiming that D = 0.8 is "80% diverse" — this is meaningless. Instead say: "The index of 0.8 indicates high species diversity with high evenness."

3. Threats to Biodiversity

Biodiversity is declining at rates unprecedented in human history. Scientists estimate current extinction rates are 100–1,000 times higher than the natural "background" rate. The threats fall into five major categories, easily remembered with the acronym HIPCO:

3.1 Habitat Loss (H)

Habitat loss is the single greatest threat to biodiversity. When habitats are destroyed, fragmented, or degraded, the species that depend on them lose their homes, food sources, and breeding sites.

Causes:

Cause Example Impact
Deforestation Amazon rainforest cleared for cattle ranching and soy agriculture (~17% lost in 50 years) Loss of ~137 plant, animal, and insect species EVERY DAY (WWF estimate). Disruption of rainfall patterns.
Urbanization Cities expanding into surrounding natural areas Habitat fragmentation — remaining patches are too small and isolated to support viable populations
Agriculture Conversion of grasslands, wetlands, and forests to cropland The single largest driver. 40% of Earth's ice-free land is used for agriculture.
Wetland drainage Draining marshes and swamps for development/farming Wetlands are among the most biodiverse ecosystems; 87% of global wetlands have been lost since 1700
Dam construction Three Gorges Dam (China), Belo Monte Dam (Brazil) Floods upstream habitats, alters downstream flow, blocks fish migration

Habitat fragmentation deserves special mention. Even when some habitat remains, splitting it into small, isolated patches creates problems:

  • Edge effects: The boundaries of fragments experience different conditions (more wind, more light, lower humidity), changing the habitat. Interior species (that need deep forest) lose their preferred conditions.
  • Reduced population sizes: Small patches support fewer individuals → higher extinction risk from stochastic events.
  • Barriers to dispersal: Roads, farmland, and development prevent movement between fragments → reduced gene flow → inbreeding and loss of genetic diversity.

3.2 Invasive Species (I)

An invasive species is a non-native organism that, when introduced to a new ecosystem, spreads rapidly and causes harm to native species, ecosystems, or economies.

Why are invasives so damaging? Native species evolved together over millennia, developing checks and balances (predators, parasites, competitors). When a species is moved to a new ecosystem, it often leaves its natural enemies behind. Freed from these constraints, it can explode in population.

Key Examples:

Invasive Species Introduction Impact
Cane toad (Rhinella marina)** — Australia Introduced in 1935 to control sugar cane beetles. Failed to control beetles but thrived spectacularly. Cane toads secrete a potent toxin (bufotoxin) from glands on their shoulders. Native Australian predators (quolls, goannas, snakes, crocodiles) that eat toads die — they have no evolutionary history with this toxin. Populations of northern quolls have declined by >75% in toad-colonized areas.
Zebra mussel (Dreissena polymorpha)** — North American Great Lakes Arrived in ballast water from European ships in the 1980s. Extraordinary filtration capacity — each mussel filters ~1 liter of water per day. Massive populations clear the water, allowing sunlight to penetrate deeper → algal blooms. They outcompete native mussels for food and space. They clog water intake pipes, causing billions in industrial damage.
Kudzu vine (Pueraria montana)** — Southeastern USA Introduced from Japan in 1876 for erosion control and as an ornamental. Grows up to 30 cm per DAY, smothering trees, buildings, and anything in its path. Known as "the vine that ate the South."
Brown tree snake (Boiga irregularis)** — Guam Accidentally introduced via military cargo from the South Pacific after WWII. Guam had no native snakes. The brown tree snake has extirpated 10 of 12 native forest bird species. It also causes frequent power outages by climbing on electrical lines.
Nile perch (Lates niloticus)** — Lake Victoria Introduced in the 1950s to boost commercial fishing. The large predatory fish devastated Lake Victoria's native cichlid population — ~200 of ~500 endemic cichlid species are now extinct or critically endangered, representing one of the largest vertebrate mass extinction events in recent history.

3.3 Pollution (P)

Pollution degrades habitats and directly harms organisms through toxicity, oxygen depletion, and habitat alteration.

Major types:

1. Eutrophication: The enrichment of water bodies with nutrients (nitrates and phosphates), primarily from agricultural fertilizers and sewage discharge.

The eutrophication cascade:

  1. Nutrients (especially nitrogen and phosphorus) enter water bodies via runoff.
  2. Nutrients stimulate explosive growth of algae and cyanobacteria (algal bloom). The water turns green and turbid.
  3. The dense algal bloom blocks sunlight from reaching submerged aquatic plants, which die.
  4. When algae die, they sink and are decomposed by aerobic bacteria. The bacterial decomposition consumes enormous amounts of dissolved oxygen.
  5. Oxygen levels crash (hypoxia or anoxia), creating "dead zones" where fish and other aerobic organisms cannot survive.

Example: The Gulf of Mexico "dead zone" — an area of up to 22,000 km² (larger than Wales) where oxygen levels are too low to support most marine life, caused by nutrient runoff from the Mississippi River watershed.

2. Acid rain: Sulfur dioxide (SO₂) and nitrogen oxides (NOₓ) from burning fossil fuels react with water vapor in the atmosphere to form sulfuric and nitric acids. This falls as acid rain (pH < 5.6):

  • Acidifies lakes and streams → kills fish and amphibians (many cannot reproduce below pH 5.0).
  • Leaches aluminum from soil → toxic to plant roots.
  • Damages forests directly (the "waldsterben" — forest death — in Central Europe in the 1970s–80s).
  • Erodes buildings and monuments made of limestone/marble.

3. Plastic pollution: An estimated 8 million tonnes of plastic enter the oceans annually. Effects:

  • Entanglement: Marine mammals, turtles, and seabirds become tangled in discarded fishing nets and plastic rings → drowning, starvation, amputation.
  • Ingestion: Animals mistake plastic for food. Sea turtles eat plastic bags (resembling jellyfish). Albatross chicks are fed plastic by their parents → stomachs fill with indigestible material → starvation. Over 90% of seabirds have plastic in their stomachs.
  • Microplastics: Plastic fragments <5 mm. Absorbed by filter feeders, entering the food chain. They adsorb and concentrate toxic pollutants. Found in human blood, placentas, and breast milk.

4. Biomagnification: The concentration of persistent pollutants increases at each trophic level. Example — DDT (dichlorodiphenyltrichloroethane):

  • DDT is a persistent insecticide used widely mid-20th century.
  • It is fat-soluble — organisms store it in fatty tissues rather than excreting it.
  • At each trophic level, a predator eats many prey organisms, concentrating the DDT from all of them.
  • Top predators (e.g., peregrine falcons, bald eagles) accumulated DDT at levels causing eggshell thinning → eggs broke during incubation → catastrophic population declines.
  • DDT was banned in most countries by the 1970s-80s; raptor populations have recovered dramatically.

3.4 Overexploitation (O — but "overharvesting" or "overexploitation" to complete HIPCO in various orders)

Overexploitation is the harvesting of wild organisms at rates that exceed the population's capacity to recover. It includes hunting, fishing, logging, and collecting.

Examples:

Case Detail
Overfishing ~90% of global fish stocks are fully exploited, overexploited, or collapsed. Atlantic cod (Gadus morhua) off Newfoundland — one of the world's richest fisheries — collapsed in 1992 due to decades of overfishing. ~40,000 people lost jobs. The cod population has still not recovered 30+ years later.
Bushmeat Hunting wild animals for food in tropical forests. In Central Africa, an estimated 5 million tonnes of bushmeat are harvested annually — unsustainable levels for many primate, antelope, and rodent species. The "empty forest syndrome" — forest stands intact but is ecologically empty of large animals.
Poaching Illegal hunting, typically for high-value animal products. Rhinoceros poached for horn (keratin — medically useless but valued in traditional medicine). African elephant poached for ivory. Tigers poached for bones, skins, and organs. Pangolins — the world's most trafficked mammal — poached for scales and meat.
Illegal logging Particularly of high-value tropical hardwoods (mahogany, rosewood, ebony). Destroys habitat, often in protected areas.
Wildlife trade The illegal wildlife trade is estimated at $7–23 billion annually, making it the fourth-largest illegal trade after drugs, counterfeiting, and human trafficking.

3.5 Climate Change (C)

Climate change is a threat multiplier — it amplifies the effects of habitat loss, invasive species, and other pressures.

Key mechanisms:

  1. Range shifts: As temperatures warm, species must move toward the poles or to higher elevations to stay within their temperature tolerance. Species that cannot move fast enough (trees, many amphibians) or that have nowhere to go (species already at mountain tops or poleward coastlines) face extinction.
  1. Phenological mismatch: The timing of biological events (flowering, migration, breeding) shifts at different rates for different species. Example: European great tits (Parus major) time their breeding to coincide with peak caterpillar abundance to feed chicks. Caterpillar emergence is cued by temperature and has advanced by ~2 weeks. Great tit egg-laying is cued by photoperiod and has advanced less. The mismatch means chicks hatch when fewer caterpillars are available → reduced chick survival.
  1. Coral bleaching: Corals have a mutualistic relationship with zooxanthellae (photosynthetic algae living in their tissues). When water temperatures exceed a threshold (typically ~1–2°C above normal summer maximum for sustained periods), corals expel their zooxanthellae → the coral turns white ("bleaches"). Without their symbionts, corals starve. If temperatures return to normal quickly, corals can recover. If not, they die.
  • 2016–2017 Great Barrier Reef bleaching: Back-to-back bleaching events killed ~50% of shallow-water corals across the reef's 2,300 km length.
  1. Ocean acidification: About 30% of anthropogenic CO₂ is absorbed by the oceans, where it reacts with water to form carbonic acid (H₂CO₃), lowering pH. Since pre-industrial times, ocean pH has dropped from ~8.2 to ~8.1 (a 30% increase in acidity on the logarithmic pH scale). Acidification reduces the availability of carbonate ions (CO₃²⁻) needed by calcifying organisms (corals, mollusks, some plankton) to build their calcium carbonate skeletons and shells. By 2100, some projections suggest corals may be unable to build skeletons faster than they dissolve.
  1. Sea level rise: Melting ice sheets and thermal expansion of seawater are causing sea levels to rise (~3.3 mm/year currently, accelerating). This threatens coastal ecosystems — mangroves, salt marshes, sea turtle nesting beaches, and low-lying island ecosystems.

IB Exam Tip: When asked to "discuss threats to biodiversity," avoid listing them as a bullet-point shopping list. Structure your answer around HIPCO with at least one specific, named example for each threat. For the highest marks, explain the mechanism by which the threat reduces biodiversity, not just that it does. For example, don't just say "climate change threatens biodiversity" — say "climate change causes coral bleaching by disrupting the coral-zooxanthellae mutualism, leading to coral mortality and the collapse of reef ecosystems that support ~25% of marine biodiversity."

4. The IUCN Red List

The International Union for Conservation of Nature (IUCN) maintains the Red List of Threatened Species — the world's most comprehensive inventory of the conservation status of species.

4.1 The Categories

The IUCN categories, from most to least threatened:

```

Extinct (EX)

Extinct in the Wild (EW)

Critically Endangered (CR)

Endangered (EN)

Vulnerable (VU)

Near Threatened (NT)

Least Concern (LC)

Data Deficient (DD)

Not Evaluated (NE)

```

The three "threatened" categories — Critically Endangered, Endangered, and Vulnerable — are defined by quantitative criteria.

4.2 The Criteria

A species is assigned to a threat category based on any ONE of five criteria:

Criterion What It Measures Example Threshold for "Endangered"
A: Population reduction Rate of decline over time ≥50% decline over 10 years or 3 generations
B: Geographic range Extent of occurrence or area of occupancy Extent of occurrence <5,000 km², severely fragmented or known from ≤5 locations
C: Small population + decline Population size and ongoing decline <2,500 mature individuals + continuing decline
D: Very small population Restricted population <250 mature individuals
E: Quantitative analysis Extinction probability ≥20% probability of extinction within 20 years or 5 generations

Species IUCN Status Key Reason
Mountain gorilla (Gorilla beringei beringei) Endangered Population ~1,000; restricted range; habitat loss; poaching; disease
Giant panda (Ailuropoda melanoleuca) Vulnerable (downlisted from Endangered in 2016!) Population ~1,800 in wild; habitat fragmentation; but recovering due to intense conservation
Vaquita (Phocoena sinus) Critically Endangered World's most endangered marine mammal — ~10 individuals remain; bycatch in illegal gillnets

Panda conservation success story: The giant panda was downlisted from Endangered to Vulnerable in 2016 — a rare conservation victory. This was achieved through: (1) massive expansion of protected reserves in China, (2) strict anti-poaching enforcement, (3) captive breeding and reintroduction programs, and (4) bamboo corridor restoration to reconnect fragmented populations. It demonstrates that conservation efforts CAN work when sufficiently resourced.

5. Conservation Approaches

Conservation strategies fall into two broad categories: protecting species in their natural habitats (in situ) and protecting them outside their natural habitats (ex situ).

5.1 In Situ Conservation

In situ (Latin: "in place") conservation protects species within their natural ecosystems. This is generally preferred because it preserves not just the species but the ecological relationships, evolutionary processes, and ecosystem services of the entire habitat.

Types of in situ conservation:

Approach Description Examples
National Parks Legally protected areas where human activity is restricted Yellowstone (USA, 1872 — world's first national park); Serengeti (Tanzania — protects wildebeest migration); Galápagos National Park (Ecuador — 97% of the archipelago is protected)
Wildlife Reserves / Nature Reserves Similar to national parks but often with less strict protection; may allow limited sustainable resource use Sian Ka'an Biosphere Reserve (Mexico — allows ecotourism and some traditional use)
Marine Protected Areas (MPAs) Ocean areas with restrictions on fishing, mining, and other extractive activities Great Barrier Reef Marine Park (Australia); Papahānaumokuākea Marine National Monument (Hawaii — largest protected area on Earth at 1.5 million km²)
Community-based conservation Local communities manage and benefit from protecting biodiversity on their lands Namibia's communal conservancies — local communities manage wildlife and receive income from ecotourism and sustainable hunting

Advantages of in situ:

  • Protects the entire ecosystem, not just target species.
  • Maintains ecological and evolutionary processes (predator-prey dynamics, nutrient cycling, natural selection).
  • Generally cheaper per species in the long term.
  • Preserves ecosystem services (watershed protection, carbon sequestration, pollination).

Challenges:

  • Requires large areas — difficult in densely populated regions.
  • Poaching and illegal resource extraction remain problems even in "protected" areas.
  • Political and economic pressures to open protected areas to resource extraction.
  • Climate change may render protected areas unsuitable for the species they were designed to protect (species shift ranges; park boundaries don't).

5.2 Ex Situ Conservation

Ex situ (Latin: "out of place") conservation protects species outside their natural habitats. It is used when in situ conservation is impossible or as a last resort when a species faces imminent extinction in the wild.

Types of ex situ conservation:

Approach Description Examples
Zoos Captive populations maintained for breeding, research, education, and potential reintroduction California condor — declined to 27 individuals by 1987; all captured for captive breeding; now >500 individuals, with >300 flying free in the wild. Przewalski's horse — extinct in the wild by 1969; captive breeding from 12 surviving individuals; now reintroduced to Mongolia.
Botanical Gardens Living plant collections for conservation, research, and education Royal Botanic Gardens, Kew (UK) — maintains ~30,000 plant species, including the world's rarest. The "living dead" — some species survive only in botanical gardens, extinct in the wild.
Seed Banks Seeds stored under cold, dry conditions to preserve genetic diversity for centuries/millennia Svalbard Global Seed Vault** (Norway) — the "Doomsday Vault" buried in permafrost, storing >1 million seed samples from nearly every country. Millennium Seed Bank (UK) — aims to bank seeds from 25% of the world's plant species by 2020 (target achieved).
Captive Breeding Programs Coordinated breeding across multiple zoos to maintain genetic diversity and produce individuals for reintroduction Species Survival Plans (SSPs) in accredited zoos — genetic studbooks track relatedness; breeding pairs recommended to maximize genetic diversity and minimize inbreeding.
Gene Banks Storage of genetic material — sperm, eggs, embryos, tissue samples, DNA Frozen Zoo (San Diego Zoo) — cryopreserved cell lines from >10,000 animals of >1,000 species, including extinct species like the po'ouli (Hawaiian honeycreeper).

Advantages of ex situ:

  • Can prevent immediate extinction when wild populations are critically small.
  • Allows controlled breeding to maximize genetic diversity.
  • Enables research that would be impossible in the wild.
  • Educates the public and generates funding for in situ conservation.

Challenges:

  • Very expensive per individual.
  • Limited capacity — only a tiny fraction of threatened species can be maintained.
  • Captive populations lose genetic diversity over generations (genetic drift in small populations).
  • Reintroduction is difficult: Captive-bred animals may lack survival skills (hunting, avoiding predators, migration routes — whooping cranes had to be taught to migrate by following ultralight aircraft!). Habitats must be restored before reintroduction. Captive-bred animals may introduce diseases to wild populations.
  • Does not protect the habitat. A species cannot be reintroduced if its habitat is destroyed.

In situ conservation: Protection of species within their natural habitats (national parks, reserves, marine protected areas). Preserves the entire ecosystem and its processes.

Ex situ conservation: Protection of species outside their natural habitats (zoos, seed banks, botanical gardens, captive breeding). Used when in situ conservation is insufficient or impossible; serves as insurance against extinction.

6. Conservation Prioritization: Choosing Where to Act

Conservation resources are limited. We cannot save everything. How do we decide what to protect?

6.1 Flagship Species

A flagship species is a charismatic species chosen to serve as an ambassador or symbol for a conservation cause. They are used to raise public awareness and funds.

Characteristics: Large, attractive, often mammals or birds. They appeal to human emotions.

Example: The giant panda is the logo of the World Wildlife Fund (WWF). The panda's appeal generates millions in donations — money that funds conservation not just for pandas but for the entire forest ecosystems they inhabit (and all the less-charismatic species that share those forests).

Caveat: Focusing on flagship species can distort conservation priorities. The panda receives vastly more funding than equally endangered but less cute species (e.g., the Chinese giant salamander, the world's largest amphibian and Critically Endangered).

6.2 Keystone Species

A keystone species is one whose impact on its ecosystem is disproportionately large relative to its abundance. Removing a keystone species causes a trophic cascade — a series of dramatic changes throughout the food web.

Example — Sea otter (Enhydra lutris):

  • Sea otters eat sea urchins.
  • Sea urchins eat kelp.
  • When sea otters were hunted nearly to extinction for their fur (18th–19th centuries), sea urchin populations exploded.
  • Urchins overgrazed kelp forests → "urchin barrens" — bare rock where lush kelp forests once stood.
  • Kelp forests provide habitat and food for hundreds of species (fish, crabs, snails). Their disappearance caused a collapse of the entire coastal ecosystem.
  • Protection of sea otters in the 20th century allowed populations to recover → urchins controlled → kelp forests regrew → biodiversity rebounded.

Other keystone examples:

  • Gray wolf in Yellowstone (see rewilding section below).
  • African elephant: A "ecosystem engineer" — pushes over trees, creating grassland patches that benefit grazing species; digs water holes used by many species.
  • Prairie dogs: Their burrows provide habitat for >150 other species; their grazing stimulates plant growth; they are prey for black-footed ferrets, hawks, and badgers.
  • Fig trees in tropical forests: Produce fruit year-round, sustaining frugivores (fruit-eating animals) during seasons when other fruits are scarce.

Keystone species: A species that has a disproportionately large effect on ecosystem structure and function relative to its abundance. Removal triggers significant changes (often a trophic cascade) throughout the ecosystem. Conservation of keystone species provides broad ecosystem benefits.

6.3 Biodiversity Hotspots

A biodiversity hotspot is a region that meets two strict criteria:

  1. It contains at least 1,500 species of endemic vascular plants (plants found nowhere else on Earth).
  2. It has lost at least 70% of its original primary vegetation (i.e., it is highly threatened).

The concept: Conservation International identified 36 biodiversity hotspots worldwide (as of the most recent update). Together, these hotspots:

  • Cover only 2.4% of Earth's land surface.
  • Contain ~50% of all vascular plant species and ~43% of all terrestrial vertebrate species as endemics.

By concentrating conservation resources on hotspots, we protect the maximum number of unique species per dollar spent.

Examples of hotspots:

Hotspot Key Features Threats
Tropical Andes** (Venezuela to Bolivia) Richest hotspot — ~15,000 endemic plant species, spectacled bear, mountain tapir, Andean condor Deforestation for agriculture, mining, climate change (species must move upslope but run out of mountain)
Madagascar & Indian Ocean Islands ~90% of species found nowhere else — lemurs (all 100+ species endemic), baobab trees, fossa Slash-and-burn agriculture, illegal logging of rosewood, hunting
Indo-Burma** (Southeast Asia) Freshwater biodiversity epicenter — giant catfish, Irrawaddy dolphin, Siamese crocodile Dam construction, wetland drainage, agricultural conversion
Mediterranean Basin ~13,000 endemic plants; cork oak forests; monk seal Urbanization (coastal development), tourism, fires, climate change
Caribbean Islands Highest concentration of endemic amphibians in the world; unique bird species like todies and todies Deforestation, invasive species (mongoose, cats, rats), hurricanes intensified by climate change

7. International Conservation Frameworks

7.1 CITES

CITES (the Convention on International Trade in Endangered Species of Wild Fauna and Flora) is an international agreement signed by 184 parties (countries + the EU) that regulates international trade in endangered species.

How CITES works: Species are listed in three appendices:

Appendix Protection Level Examples
Appendix I Species threatened with extinction. International trade is prohibited except for non-commercial purposes (e.g., scientific research). Giant panda, all great apes, tigers, rhinos, sea turtles, all orchids
Appendix II Species not necessarily threatened with extinction but trade must be controlled to ensure sustainability. Trade allowed with export permits. Most cacti and succulents, all seahorses, queen conch, mahogany, many parrots
Appendix III Species protected in at least one country that has asked other CITES parties for assistance in controlling trade. Walrus (Canada), two-toed sloth (Costa Rica)

Strengths: CITES has been effective for high-profile species. Elephant ivory trade bans, rhino horn trade bans, and tiger product bans have reduced some poaching pressures.

Limitations: Enforcement is difficult — illegal wildlife trade persists. Domestic trade is not regulated. Species must be individually listed — many newly threatened species are unprotected. Some argue that trade bans can backfire by driving trade underground and increasing prices (creating black market incentives).

7.2 Rewilding and Ecosystem Restoration

Rewilding is a conservation approach focused on restoring natural processes and, where appropriate, reintroducing species that have been lost from ecosystems — particularly keystone species.

The Yellowstone Wolf Reintroduction — the most famous rewilding success story:

  • Background: Gray wolves were extirpated from Yellowstone by the 1920s. Without wolves, elk populations exploded. Elk overgrazed willows, aspen, and cottonwood seedlings along streams, preventing forest regeneration. Beavers declined (they need willows for food and dam-building). Without beaver dams, streams eroded, and wetland habitat declined.
  • Reintroduction: In 1995–1996, 31 wolves were reintroduced to Yellowstone from Canada.
  • The trophic cascade:
  1. Wolves predated elk and changed elk behavior (elk avoided open stream corridors where they were vulnerable).
  2. With reduced browsing pressure, willows and aspen regenerated along streams.
  3. Beavers returned — beaver colonies increased from 1 (pre-wolf) to 9 (post-wolf). Beaver dams created ponds and wetlands.
  4. Riparian vegetation stabilized stream banks, reducing erosion.
  5. Songbird diversity and abundance increased as riparian habitat recovered.
  6. Wolves provided carrion for scavengers — ravens, eagles, coyotes, bears.
  7. Wolf-killed elk carcasses provided food for grizzly bears, supplementing their diet.
  • Conclusion: The reintroduction of a single keystone species triggered a cascade of ecological restoration affecting dozens of other species — a powerful demonstration that rewilding can restore not just species but entire ecosystem functions.

Worked Example: Simpson's Index with Interpretation

A student surveys tree diversity in two forest plots of equal area:

Plot X (primary forest):

Species Count
Dipterocarp A 12
Dipterocarp B 10
Fig 8
Ironwood 8
Meranti 7
Kempas 5
Total 50

Plot Y (logged forest, 10 years post-logging):

Species Count
Macaranga (pioneer) 35
Dipterocarp A 5
Fig 4
Ironwood 3
Meranti 2
Kempas 1
Total 50

Task: Calculate Simpson's index for each plot. Explain what the values reveal about the impact of logging on biodiversity. (5 marks)

Solution:

Plot X:

Σ(n/N)² = (12/50)² + (10/50)² + (8/50)² + (8/50)² + (7/50)² + (5/50)²

= 0.0576 + 0.0400 + 0.0256 + 0.0256 + 0.0196 + 0.0100

= 0.1784

D_x = 1 − 0.1784 = 0.8216

Plot Y:

Σ(n/N)² = (35/50)² + (5/50)² + (4/50)² + (3/50)² + (2/50)² + (1/50)²

= 0.4900 + 0.0100 + 0.0064 + 0.0036 + 0.0016 + 0.0004

= 0.5120

D_y = 1 − 0.5120 = 0.4880

Interpretation (3 marks):

  1. Plot X (D = 0.82) has substantially higher tree species diversity than Plot Y (D = 0.49), indicating that logging has reduced biodiversity (1 mark).
  1. Both plots have 6 species (same richness), but Plot X has relatively even distribution while Plot Y is heavily dominated by Macaranga (70% of all trees), reducing evenness — Simpson's index captures this difference (1 mark).
  1. The dominance of Macaranga — a pioneer species that colonizes disturbed habitats — is a classic indicator of post-logging ecological succession. Primary forest dipterocarps have been largely replaced by this early-successional species. Without further disturbance, biodiversity may gradually recover through succession, but dipterocarp species are slow-growing and may take centuries to re-establish (1 mark).

9. Common Misconceptions

Misconception Reality
"Biodiversity just means the number of species" Biodiversity has three levels: genetic, species, and ecosystem. All three are interconnected and must be conserved.
"A community with 10 species is always more diverse than one with 5" Not necessarily — if the 10-species community is dominated by one species (low evenness), a 5-species community with high evenness could have a higher Simpson's index.
"Ex situ conservation is better because animals are safe from poachers" In situ is preferred whenever possible because it preserves ecological relationships and evolutionary processes. Ex situ is a last resort or supplementary measure.
"Flagship species conservation is just about saving cute animals" Flagship species serve as "umbrella species" — protecting their large habitat requirements also protects many other species in the same ecosystem.
"Once a protected area is established, the biodiversity is safe" Protected areas face ongoing threats: poaching, encroachment, climate change shifting species ranges outside park boundaries, inadequate funding for enforcement.
"CITES has stopped the illegal wildlife trade" CITES regulates legal trade and has reduced some poaching, but illegal wildlife trade is still enormous — estimated at $7–23 billion annually.
"Climate change is a future threat — it's not affecting biodiversity yet" Climate change is affecting biodiversity NOW: coral bleaching, range shifts, phenological mismatches are all well-documented current phenomena.
"Seed banks can preserve biodiversity forever" Seeds lose viability over time (though this can be centuries under optimal conditions). Seed banks don't preserve ecological interactions. Recalcitrant seeds (e.g., many tropical trees, oaks) cannot be dried and frozen — they must be conserved as living collections.

Problem 1 (Paper 1 style): Which of the following is an example of an ex situ conservation method?

A. Establishing a marine protected area

B. Creating a national park

Problem 2 (Paper 2 style, 4 marks): Explain how a keystone species differs from a flagship species, giving a named example of each.

Click for answer

Model answer:

  • A flagship species is a charismatic species used as a conservation symbol to raise public awareness and funds. Example: the giant panda (WWF logo) — its appeal generates donations that fund broader conservation efforts (1 mark for definition, 1 mark for example).
  • A keystone species has a disproportionately large effect on ecosystem structure relative to its abundance; its removal triggers trophic cascades. Example: the sea otter — when sea otters were hunted, sea urchin populations exploded, destroying kelp forests and the biodiversity they supported (1 mark for definition, 1 mark for example).
  • Key distinction: Flagship status is about human perception (charisma, fundraising value); keystone status is about ecological function (impact on the ecosystem). A species can be both (elephants are both keystone — ecosystem engineers — and flagship — charismatic megafauna) (this distinction would strengthen the answer).

Species Abundance

Problem 4 (Paper 2 style, 6 marks): Discuss the relative advantages and disadvantages of in situ and ex situ conservation.

Click for answer

Model answer:

In situ advantages (any 3):

  • Protects entire ecosystems, not just target species.
  • Maintains ecological processes (predator-prey dynamics, nutrient cycling, pollination).
  • Preserves evolutionary processes (natural selection continues).
  • Generally more cost-effective per species in the long term.
  • Protects ecosystem services (watershed protection, carbon storage).

In situ disadvantages (any 2):

  • Requires large areas — difficult in regions with high human population density.
  • Protected areas still face poaching, encroachment, and inadequate enforcement.
  • Climate change may shift suitable habitats outside protected area boundaries.

Ex situ advantages (any 2):

  • Can prevent immediate extinction of critically endangered species.
  • Allows controlled breeding to maximize genetic diversity.
  • Enables research and public education.

Ex situ disadvantages (any 2):

  • Very expensive per individual; only a tiny fraction of species can be maintained.
  • Captive populations lose genetic diversity; reintroduction is often unsuccessful.
  • Does not protect the habitat — a species cannot be reintroduced if its habitat is destroyed.

Conclusion: In situ is generally preferred as the primary strategy; ex situ serves as a critical backup for species facing imminent extinction and for supplementing wild populations.

Problem 5 (Paper 1 style): The Simpson's diversity index for a forest is calculated as D = 0.92. This value indicates:

A. Low species richness and low evenness

B. High species richness and high evenness

C. Low species richness but high evenness

D. An error in calculation, as D cannot exceed 0.9

Click for answer

B. D = 0.92 is very high (close to the maximum of 1.0), indicating both high species richness (many species) AND high evenness (individuals roughly equally distributed among species). D can approach but never reach 1.0 (unless there are infinite species all with equal abundance).

Summary

  • Biodiversity exists at three levels: genetic (allele variety), species (richness + evenness), and ecosystem (habitat variety). All three are interdependent.
  • Simpson's diversity index (D = 1 − Σ(n/N)²) quantifies species diversity, combining richness and evenness into a single value (0 = no diversity, approaching 1 = high diversity).
  • Threats follow HIPCO: Habitat loss (primary threat), Invasive species, Pollution (eutrophication, acid rain, plastic, biomagnification), Climate change (coral bleaching, range shifts, ocean acidification), and Overexploitation (overfishing, poaching, bushmeat).
  • The IUCN Red List categorizes extinction risk using quantitative criteria. Categories range from Least Concern to Extinct.
  • In situ conservation protects species in their habitats (national parks, marine protected areas); ex situ protects them outside (zoos, seed banks, captive breeding). In situ is preferred; ex situ is a critical backup.
  • Flagship species (panda) are charismatic fundraisers; keystone species (sea otter, wolf) have disproportionate ecological impact. Biodiversity hotspots prioritize regions with high endemism and high threat.
  • CITES regulates international trade in endangered species. Rewilding restores ecosystem function by reintroducing keystone species.
