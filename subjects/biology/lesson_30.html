1. The Z-Scheme: Why Two Photosystems?

The Z-scheme is the name given to the complete electron transport pathway of oxygenic photosynthesis. It gets its name because when the redox potentials of each carrier are plotted against the reaction sequence, the line traces a shape resembling the letter "Z" — electrons go up (excited by light), down (losing energy through the ETC), up again (excited by second photon), then down to NADP⁺.

Redox Potentials Along the Chain

Draw and label: The Z-scheme diagram with the y-axis labelled "Redox Potential (E⁰′, volts)" ranging from −1.2 V (top, most reducing) to +1.0 V (bottom, most oxidising). Plot:

Component Approximate E⁰′ (V) Event
H₂O / ½O₂ +0.82 Water splitting (oxidising)
P680 ~+1.0 Ground state
P680 ~−0.7 Excited by photon 1 — becomes strong reductant
Pheophytin ~−0.6 Primary e⁻ acceptor
QA / QB ~−0.1 to 0.0 Plastoquinones
Cytochrome b6f +0.1 to +0.3 H⁺ pumping
Plastocyanin (PC) +0.37 Cu protein
P700 ~+0.4 Ground state
P700 ~−1.0 Excited by photon 2 — becomes very strong reductant
Ferredoxin (Fd) ~−0.43 Fe-S protein
NADP⁺ / NADPH −0.32 Final acceptor

The key insight: Electrons from water (E⁰′ = +0.82 V) cannot spontaneously reduce NADP⁺ (E⁰′ = −0.32 V) — that would require electrons to flow uphill by over 1 V. A single photon of visible light (~1.8 eV for 680 nm) cannot bridge this gap efficiently. Two photosystems working in series solve the problem: each photon excites electrons to a sufficiently negative potential to drive part of the overall reaction.

The Z-scheme: The complete electron transport pathway of oxygenic photosynthesis from H₂O to NADP⁺, requiring two photochemical events (PSII and PSI) working in series. The name derives from the characteristic shape of the redox potential diagram.

Analogy: Imagine you need to lift a heavy box 2 metres onto a shelf. You can't reach that high in one go. Instead, you lift the box onto a 1-metre table (PSII — first photon), then from the table onto the 2-metre shelf (PSI — second photon). The two-stage lift makes an impossible task possible.

2. Photosystem Architecture: Antenna to Reaction Centre

Photosystems are among the most sophisticated protein-pigment complexes in nature. Each is a masterpiece of molecular engineering.

The Antenna Complex (Light-Harvesting Complex)

Draw and label: A photosystem showing the antenna complex surrounding the reaction centre. Draw ~200–300 pigment molecules (chlorophyll a, chlorophyll b, carotenoids) arranged in a protein scaffold. Show energy arrows hopping from peripheral pigments toward the reaction centre.

Antenna complex (LHC): An array of 200–300 chlorophyll and carotenoid molecules bound to transmembrane proteins. Its function is to increase the effective cross-section for photon capture — the reaction centre alone would catch very few photons. The antenna acts like a satellite dish, collecting light energy over a large area and funneling it to the reaction centre.

#### Resonance Energy Transfer (Förster Resonance)

Energy absorbed by an antenna pigment is transferred to neighbouring pigments via resonance energy transfer — NOT by electron transfer.

Förster resonance energy transfer (FRET): A non-radiative (no photon emitted) transfer of excitation energy from a donor pigment to an acceptor pigment through dipole-dipole coupling. Efficiency depends on: (1) distance between pigments (∝ 1/r⁶ — must be within ~10 nm), (2) spectral overlap (donor emission must overlap acceptor absorption), and (3) orientation of dipoles.

Analogy: Imagine a row of closely-spaced tuning forks. When you strike the first one, it vibrates. Its vibrations cause the next tuning fork to vibrate, which causes the next to vibrate, and so on. The energy travels from fork to fork without any physical object (electron) moving between them. This is exactly how resonance energy transfer works — energy hops from pigment to pigment like a hot potato being passed along a line.

Key distinction:

  • Resonance energy transfer: Energy moves, not electrons. This is how the antenna funnels energy to the reaction centre.
  • Electron transfer: An actual electron is donated and accepted (redox chemistry). This happens at the reaction centre.

At the reaction centre, the accumulated excitation energy drives charge separation — an electron is physically transferred from the special pair to the primary electron acceptor.

Photosystem II: Structural Detail

Draw and label: A detailed diagram of PSII showing:

  • Antenna (LHCII): Peripheral and core antenna chlorophylls, plus carotenoids
  • Reaction centre core: D1 and D2 proteins that bind P680, pheophytin, QA, and QB
  • P680: Special pair of chlorophyll a molecules
  • Pheophytin (Pheo): Primary electron acceptor — chlorophyll without Mg²⁺
  • QA: Tightly-bound plastoquinone (one-electron carrier)
  • QB: Loosely-bound plastoquinone — after 2 electrons, picks up 2H⁺ from stroma → QH₂, detaches
  • Oxygen-Evolving Complex (OEC): Mn₄CaO₅ cluster on the lumenal side, connected to P680 via a redox-active tyrosine residue (YZ)

#### The D1 Protein and Photoinhibition

The D1 protein of PSII is vulnerable to damage from excess light. At high light intensities, the rate of D1 damage exceeds the rate of repair. This is photoinhibition — a reduction in photosynthetic efficiency caused by PSII damage. Plants respond by rapidly degrading damaged D1 and synthesising new copies (the PSII repair cycle).

Photosystem I: Structural Detail

Draw and label: A detailed diagram of PSI showing:

  • Antenna (LHCI): Core antenna chlorophylls
  • P700: Special pair of chlorophyll a molecules
  • A₀: Primary electron acceptor (chlorophyll)
  • A₁: Phylloquinone (vitamin K₁)
  • FX, FA, FB: Three [4Fe-4S] iron-sulfur clusters
  • Ferredoxin docking site: On the stromal side, where Fd binds to accept electrons from FB

Unlike PSII, PSI is not susceptible to photoinhibition. Its reaction centre is more robust and operates at a lower redox potential.

3. The Oxygen-Evolving Complex: The Kok Cycle

For HL, you should appreciate the elegance of the OEC mechanism. The Mn₄CaO₅ cluster cycles through five oxidation states — S₀ to S₄ — each triggered by the absorption of one photon by PSII.

Draw and label: The Kok cycle as a circle with five states (S₀→S₁→S₂→S₃→S₄→S₀). At each S-state transition, one electron is removed from the Mn cluster by P680⁺ (via YZ). After four photons (S₄ state), the cluster has accumulated enough oxidising power to split two water molecules in one concerted step, releasing O₂ and returning to S₀.

  • S₀ → S₁: 1st photon, 1st electron removed
  • S₁ → S₂: 2nd photon, 2nd electron removed
  • S₂ → S₃: 3rd photon, 3rd electron removed
  • S₃ → S₄ → S₀: 4th photon, 4th electron removed; 2H₂O bind, O₂ released, Mn cluster returns to S₀

The S₁ state is the most stable in the dark — after prolonged darkness, the OEC relaxes to S₁. This means that in practice, only three flashes of light are needed to produce the first O₂ molecule.

4. The Calvin Cycle: Complete Detail

The Calvin cycle is the "business end" of photosynthesis — where CO₂ is actually fixed into carbohydrate. It occurs in the stroma and consumes the ATP and NADPH produced by the light reactions.

The Three Phases

Draw and label: A complete Calvin cycle diagram showing all three phases. Label each intermediate and the ATP/NADPH consumed at each step.

Phase What Happens Inputs per 3 CO₂ Outputs
1. Carboxylation CO₂ + RuBP → 2 × 3-PGA 3 CO₂ + 3 RuBP 6 × 3-PGA
2. Reduction 3-PGA → G3P 6 ATP + 6 NADPH 6 × G3P (1 exits)
3. Regeneration 5 G3P → 3 RuBP 3 ATP 3 RuBP

Net per 3 CO₂: 9 ATP + 6 NADPH consumed, 1 G3P exported (→ glucose synthesis).

Phase 1: Carboxylation

Worked Example: Calvin Cycle Carbon Accounting

Question: For 6 molecules of CO₂ entering the Calvin cycle, how many molecules of RuBP are consumed and regenerated? How many G3P molecules exit the cycle?

Step-by-step:

  1. Carboxylation: 6 CO₂ + 6 RuBP → 12 × 3-PGA. Each CO₂ is fixed onto one RuBP (5C), forming an unstable 6C intermediate that splits into two 3-PGA. So 6 CO₂ + 6 RuBP (30C) → 12 × 3-PGA (36C). The 6 extra carbons come from the 6 CO₂.
  1. Reduction: 12 × 3-PGA + 12 ATP + 12 NADPH → 12 × G3P. All 12 molecules of 3-PGA are converted to 12 G3P (36C total).
  1. Export: 2 G3P exit the cycle (2 × 3C = 6C). These 6 carbons represent the net fixation of 6 CO₂. The remaining 10 G3P (30C) stay in the cycle.
  1. Regeneration: 10 G3P (30C) + 6 ATP → 6 RuBP (30C). The 10 G3P are rearranged through transketolase/transaldolase reactions and phosphorylated to regenerate exactly the 6 RuBP consumed in step 1.

Check: 6 RuBP consumed (step 1) = 6 RuBP regenerated (step 4). Carbon balance: 30C (RuBP) + 6C (CO₂) = 36C (12 G3P) = 6C (2 G3P exported) + 30C (6 RuBP). ✓

#### The Reaction

$$3\text{ CO}_2 + 3\text{ RuBP (5C)} \xrightarrow{\text{RuBisCO}} 6 \times 3\text{-phosphoglycerate (3C)}$$

Three molecules of CO₂ react with three molecules of ribulose-1,5-bisphosphate (RuBP), a 5-carbon sugar with phosphates on carbons 1 and 5. The enzyme RuBisCO (ribulose-1,5-bisphosphate carboxylase/oxygenase) catalyses this reaction.

Each RuBP is carboxylated to form an unstable 6-carbon intermediate that immediately splits into two molecules of 3-phosphoglycerate (3-PGA). So 3 RuBP × 2 = 6 × 3-PGA (18 carbons total: 3 from CO₂ + 15 from RuBP).

#### RuBisCO: Structure and Properties

RuBisCO (ribulose-1,5-bisphosphate carboxylase/oxygenase): The enzyme that catalyses the first step of the Calvin cycle — the fixation of CO₂ onto RuBP. It is:

  • The most abundant protein on Earth (~30–50% of soluble leaf protein)
  • Composed of 8 large subunits (L, ~55 kDa each, encoded by chloroplast DNA, catalytic) and 8 small subunits (S, ~15 kDa each, encoded by nuclear DNA, structural/regulatory)
  • A remarkably slow enzyme — turnover number ~3 CO₂ per second per active site (compare to carbonic anhydrase: ~10⁶ s⁻¹). Plants compensate by making enormous quantities.
  • Capable of both carboxylase (CO₂ + RuBP → 2 × 3-PGA) and oxygenase (O₂ + RuBP → 3-PGA + 2-phosphoglycolate) activities

Why is RuBisCO so slow? The reaction it catalyses is chemically difficult. CO₂ is a stable, linear molecule. Attaching it to RuBP requires activating CO₂ (which RuBisCO does by binding a Mg²⁺ ion and a carbamylated lysine residue). This activation step is slow and contributes to RuBisCO's low catalytic rate.

Phase 2: Reduction

This phase converts 3-PGA (a carboxylic acid) into G3P (an aldehyde), using ATP and NADPH.

#### Step 1: Phosphorylation

$$6 \times \text{3-PGA} + 6\text{ ATP} \xrightarrow{\text{phosphoglycerate kinase}} 6 \times \text{1,3-bisphosphoglycerate} + 6\text{ ADP}$$

ATP consumed: 6 ATP (per 3 CO₂)

This is the same enzyme that works in reverse during glycolysis. In the Calvin cycle, it phosphorylates 3-PGA at the expense of ATP, creating a high-energy acyl phosphate.

#### Step 2: Reduction

$$6 \times \text{1,3-BPG} + 6\text{ NADPH} + 6\text{ H}^+ \xrightarrow{\text{GAPDH}} 6 \times \text{glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate (G3P)} + 6\text{ NADP}^+ + 6\text{ P}_i$$

NADPH consumed: 6 NADPH (per 3 CO₂)

Enzyme: Glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate dehydrogenase (GAPDH) — again, the same enzyme as in glycolysis but running in the opposite direction (reductive rather than oxidative).

The result: 6 molecules of G3P are produced. Each is a 3-carbon sugar phosphate.

#### The One That Gets Away

One G3P molecule exits the cycle — this is the net product of carbon fixation. It can be used to synthesise:

  • Glucose (2 G3P → glucose-6-phosphate)
  • Starch (for storage in chloroplast)
  • Sucrose (for export to the rest of the plant)

The remaining 5 G3P molecules stay in the cycle for regeneration.

Phase 3: Regeneration of RuBP

Five G3P molecules (5 × 3C = 15 carbons) must be rearranged to form three RuBP molecules (3 × 5C = 15 carbons). This is a complex series of reactions — essentially the reductive pentose phosphate pathway running backwards (or forwards, depending on your reference frame).

The key steps:

  1. Isomerisation and condensation: G3P and DHAP interconvert, then condense to form fructose-1,6-bisphosphate (6C) and sedoheptulose-1,7-bisphosphate (7C)
  2. Dephosphorylation: F1,6BP → F6P; S1,7BP → S7P
  3. Carbon shuffling: Transketolase and transaldolase rearrange carbon skeletons to produce ribulose-5-phosphate (Ru5P)
  4. Phosphorylation: Ru5P + ATP → RuBP (catalysed by phosphoribulokinase)

$$3\text{ Ru5P} + 3\text{ ATP} \xrightarrow{\text{phosphoribulokinase}} 3\text{ RuBP} + 3\text{ ADP}$$

ATP consumed: 3 ATP (per 3 CO₂)

IB Exam Tip (Paper 2): You do NOT need to memorise every intermediate of the regeneration phase. You DO need to know: (1) the starting point is 5 G3P, (2) the endpoint is 3 RuBP, (3) it costs 3 ATP, and (4) the final enzyme is phosphoribulokinase. The total energy cost per 3 CO₂ is 9 ATP + 6 NADPH.

Calvin Cycle: Complete Accounting per 3 CO₂

Phase Input Output ATP NADPH
Carboxylation 3 CO₂ + 3 RuBP (15C) 6 × 3-PGA (18C) 0 0
Reduction (phosphorylation) 6 × 3-PGA 6 × 1,3-BPG 6 0
Reduction (reduction) 6 × 1,3-BPG 6 × G3P (18C) 0 6

5. RuBisCO Regulation

RuBisCO must be active in the light (when ATP and NADPH are available) and inactive in the dark (to avoid futile cycling). Several mechanisms ensure this:

5.1 pH Activation

In the light, H⁺ are pumped into the thylakoid lumen, raising the stromal pH from ~7.0 to ~8.0. RuBisCO's optimal pH is ~8.0 — it is more active at the higher pH that prevails during photosynthesis.

5.2 Mg²⁺ Activation

As H⁺ enter the thylakoid lumen, Mg²⁺ ions move from the lumen into the stroma (to balance charge). The increased stromal [Mg²⁺] (from ~1–2 mM to ~5 mM) promotes RuBisCO activation. Mg²⁺ is required for the carbamylation of a specific lysine residue (Lys-201) in the active site — without it, RuBisCO cannot bind CO₂ as an activator.

5.3 Thioredoxin Activation

In the light, electrons from PSI reduce ferredoxin, which reduces thioredoxin (via ferredoxin-thioredoxin reductase). Reduced thioredoxin then reduces disulfide bonds in several Calvin cycle enzymes (including RuBisCO, GAPDH, phosphoribulokinase, and sedoheptulose-1,7-bisphosphatase), converting them from an inactive to an active conformation. In the dark, these disulfide bonds re-form (oxidation), inactivating the enzymes.

Thioredoxin: A small (~12 kDa) protein with a redox-active disulfide group. It acts as a "light switch" — when reduced by ferredoxin in the light, it activates Calvin cycle enzymes by reducing their regulatory disulfide bonds. This couples enzyme activity directly to the light reactions.

5.4 RuBisCO Activase

RuBisCO has a tendency to bind sugar phosphates (especially RuBP) in its inactive form, blocking the active site. RuBisCO activase is an ATP-dependent chaperone that physically removes these inhibitory molecules, allowing carbamylation and activation to proceed. RuBisCO activase itself is regulated by the ATP/ADP ratio — high ATP (light conditions) activates it; low ATP (dark) inhibits it.

IB Exam Tip (Paper 1): RuBisCO activase is often tested as an example of an ATP-dependent regulatory mechanism. Remember: it uses ATP to change RuBisCO's conformation and release tightly-bound sugar phosphates from the active site. This is distinct from the ATP consumed in the Calvin cycle itself.

6. Photorespiration: When RuBisCO Makes a Mistake

The Oxygenase Reaction

RuBisCO is not perfectly specific for CO₂. It can also use O₂ as a substrate:

$$\text{RuBP (5C)} + \text{O}_2 \xrightarrow{\text{RuBisCO (oxygenase)}} \text{3-PGA (3C)} + \text{2-phosphoglycolate (2C)}$$

The 2-phosphoglycolate (2-PG) is toxic to the plant — it inhibits several Calvin cycle enzymes and must be salvaged.

The Photorespiratory Salvage Pathway (PCO Cycle)

Draw and label: A diagram showing the three-organelle salvage pathway: chloroplast → peroxisome → mitochondrion → peroxisome → chloroplast.

This is also called the photorespiratory carbon oxidation (PCO) cycle or C2 cycle:

  1. Chloroplast: 2-phosphoglycolate → glycolate + Pi (phosphoglycolate phosphatase)
  2. Peroxisome: Glycolate → glyoxylate (glycolate oxidase, produces H₂O₂ → broken down by catalase); glyoxylate → glycine (transamination)
  3. Mitochondrion: 2 glycine → serine + CO₂ + NH₃ (glycine decarboxylase + serine hydroxymethyltransferase). This is where CO₂ is lost — previously fixed carbon is released!
  4. Peroxisome: Serine → hydroxypyruvate → glycerate
  5. Chloroplast: Glycerate → 3-PGA (glycerate kinase, costs 1 ATP)

The Cost of Photorespiration

For every 2 molecules of 2-phosphoglycolate entering the salvage pathway:

  • 1 CO₂ is lost (in the mitochondrion) — this is carbon that had been fixed by RuBisCO
  • ATP and reducing power are consumed (glycerate kinase, glycine decarboxylation)
  • NH₃ is released and must be re-assimilated (costs more ATP)

Net: Photorespiration reduces photosynthetic efficiency by 25–50% at 25°C in C3 plants. This is a staggering waste — imagine a factory that loses a quarter of its output due to a design flaw.

Why Does Photorespiration Happen?

It's not really RuBisCO's "fault" — the enzyme evolved over 3 billion years ago, when Earth's atmosphere had virtually no O₂ and much higher CO₂. Under those conditions, oxygenase activity was negligible. RuBisCO's active site chemistry inherently cannot perfectly discriminate between CO₂ and O₂ because both are small, linear, uncharged molecules that bind in a similar geometry.

Why Does Photorespiration Get Worse at Higher Temperatures?

Two reasons:

  1. RuBisCO specificity (τ = V_c/V_o × [O₂]/[CO₂]) decreases with temperature: The ratio of carboxylation to oxygenation rates changes — more oxygenase activity relative to carboxylase at higher temperatures.
  1. Stomatal closure: At high temperatures, stomata close to reduce water loss. This traps O₂ inside the leaf (from photosynthetic water splitting) while CO₂ entry is restricted. The [O₂]/[CO₂] ratio in the leaf rises, favouring the oxygenase reaction.

7. C4 Plants: Spatial Carbon Concentration

C4 plants have evolved a mechanism to concentrate CO₂ around RuBisCO, suppressing photorespiration. They separate the initial CO₂ fixation (in mesophyll cells) from the Calvin cycle (in bundle sheath cells).

The C4 Pathway (Hatch-Slack Pathway)

Draw and label: A diagram showing a mesophyll cell and an adjacent bundle sheath cell connected by plasmodesmata. Show:

Mesophyll cell:

  1. CO₂ enters → converted to HCO₃⁻ by carbonic anhydrase
  2. PEP + HCO₃⁻ → Oxaloacetate (4C), catalysed by PEP carboxylase
  3. OAA → Malate (or aspartate, depending on C4 subtype)

Bundle sheath cell:

  1. Malate imported via plasmodesmata
  2. Malate → Pyruvate + CO₂ (decarboxylation, releases CO₂)
  3. CO₂ enters Calvin cycle → Rubisco fixes it
  4. Pyruvate → back to mesophyll cell
  5. Pyruvate + ATP + Pi → PEP + AMP + PPi (catalysed by pyruvate phosphate dikinase)

PEP carboxylase: The enzyme that catalyses the first step of C4 photosynthesis: PEP + HCO₃⁻ → oxaloacetate + Pi. Unlike RuBisCO, PEP carboxylase has no oxygenase activity and has a much higher affinity for its substrate (Kₘ for HCO₃⁻ ~0.2 mM vs RuBisCO Kₘ for CO₂ ~10–20 μM). It is not inhibited by O₂.

Why the C4 Pathway Works

PEP carboxylase fixes HCO₃⁻ (not CO₂ directly) into a 4-carbon acid (hence "C4"). The 4C acid is transported to bundle sheath cells where it is decarboxylated, releasing CO₂ at concentrations 10–100 times higher than atmospheric. This high [CO₂] saturates RuBisCO and out-competes O₂, virtually eliminating photorespiration.

Kranz Anatomy

Draw and label: A transverse section of a C4 leaf showing Kranz anatomy:

  • Mesophyll cells arranged in a concentric ring around bundle sheath cells
  • Bundle sheath cells have thick cell walls impregnated with suberin (a waxy, hydrophobic polymer) — this prevents CO₂ leakage after it has been concentrated
  • Chloroplasts in bundle sheath cells are often larger and contain more starch grains
  • Chloroplasts in mesophyll cells have well-developed grana (PSII-rich); bundle sheath chloroplasts often have reduced grana (PSII-poor, PSI-rich — adapted for cyclic photophosphorylation to generate extra ATP)

Kranz anatomy (German: Kranz = wreath): The distinctive anatomical arrangement of C4 leaves where bundle sheath cells are surrounded by a concentric layer of mesophyll cells. The suberised walls of bundle sheath cells create a diffusion barrier that traps CO₂, allowing high concentrations to build up around RuBisCO.

C4 Subtypes

There are three biochemical subtypes of C4 photosynthesis, differing in the decarboxylation enzyme used:

Subtype Decarboxylating Enzyme Main 4C Acid Transported Example
NADP-ME NADP-malic enzyme Malate Maize, sugarcane, sorghum
NAD-ME NAD-malic enzyme Aspartate Millet, amaranth
PEP-CK PEP carboxykinase Aspartate Some grasses

For IB HL, you only need to know that variants exist — not the details of each subtype.

Energy Cost of C4

The C4 pathway requires extra ATP to regenerate PEP:

Pyruvate + ATP + Pi → PEP + AMP + PPi

Since AMP is produced (not ADP), and PPi is hydrolysed to 2 Pi, this reaction costs the equivalent of 2 ATP. So the total cost per CO₂ fixed is:

Pathway ATP per CO₂ NADPH per CO₂
C3 (Calvin cycle alone) 3 2
C4 (PEP regeneration + Calvin cycle) 5 2

C4 plants pay 5 ATP per CO₂ instead of 3 — a higher energy cost. But under hot, dry conditions, the elimination of photorespiration more than compensates for this extra cost. In cool, wet conditions, C3 plants have the advantage (lower energy cost with minimal photorespiration).

IB Exam Tip (Paper 2): When comparing C3 and C4, always mention the trade-off: C4 costs more ATP (5 vs 3 per CO₂) but avoids photorespiration, giving a net advantage in hot/dry conditions. C3 is more efficient in cool/moist conditions where photorespiration is low.

8. CAM Plants: Temporal Carbon Concentration

CAM (Crassulacean Acid Metabolism) plants separate CO₂ fixation and the Calvin cycle in time, not space. Both processes occur in the same cell, but at different times of day.

Draw and label: A diagram showing the same mesophyll cell at night vs day:

Night (stomata OPEN):

  1. CO₂ enters through open stomata
  2. PEP carboxylase fixes HCO₃⁻ → OAA → Malate
  3. Malate is pumped into the vacuole and stored as malic acid (vacuolar pH drops to ~3–4)
  4. Starch is broken down to provide PEP

Day (stomata CLOSED):

  1. Malate is released from vacuole
  2. Malate is decarboxylated → CO₂ + pyruvate (or PEP)
  3. CO₂ enters Calvin cycle (RuBisCO)
  4. ATP and NADPH from light reactions drive Calvin cycle
  5. Pyruvate (or PEP) → starch (for the next night)

Key Features of CAM

Feature CAM
Stomata open Night** (reduces water loss in hot, dry conditions)
CO₂ fixation enzyme PEP carboxylase (night)
Storage form Malic acid in vacuole (night)
Decarboxylation Day (malate → CO₂ + pyruvate)
Calvin cycle Day (uses stored CO₂)
Anatomy No Kranz anatomy — same cell does everything

Water Use Efficiency

CAM plants have the highest water use efficiency of all photosynthetic types:

CAM > C4 > C3

This is because CAM plants open their stomata at night when temperatures are lower and humidity is higher, minimising transpirational water loss. Some CAM plants can survive in extreme deserts where C3 and even C4 plants cannot.

Water use efficiency (WUE): The ratio of carbon fixed (photosynthesis) to water lost (transpiration). CAM plants achieve WUE values 3–6 times higher than C3 plants.

Facultative CAM

Some plants (e.g., Mesembryanthemum crystallinum, the common ice plant) can switch between C3 and CAM depending on environmental conditions — using C3 when water is abundant and switching to CAM under drought or salinity stress. This is an example of remarkable metabolic plasticity.

10. Photorespiration and Crop Improvement

Photorespiration is a major target for improving crop yields. Several strategies exist:

  1. C4 engineering: Scientists are attempting to introduce C4 biochemistry into rice (the C4 Rice Project). This requires introducing Kranz anatomy, cell-specific gene expression, and the full suite of C4 enzymes — a monumental challenge.
  1. RuBisCO engineering: Attempts to create a RuBisCO with higher CO₂/O₂ specificity. Evolution has had 3 billion years to optimise RuBisCO; there may be a fundamental trade-off between specificity and catalytic rate.
  1. Alternative carbon fixation: Synthetic biology approaches to introduce more efficient CO₂-fixing pathways (e.g., from bacteria).
  1. Algal CO₂-concentrating mechanisms: Algae concentrate CO₂ using pyrenoid structures; transferring this to higher plants is an active area of research.

IB Exam Tip (Paper 2): The "applications" section of Paper 2 may ask about the significance of photorespiration for agriculture. You could discuss: (1) C4 crops (maize, sugarcane) outperform C3 crops (wheat, rice) in hot climates, (2) rising global temperatures may favour C4 weeds over C3 crops, (3) genetic engineering aims to reduce photorespiration in C3 crops.

Common Misconceptions

Misconception Reality
"The Z-scheme is named after its discoverer" It's named for the Z-shape formed when redox potentials are plotted — not a person!
"Each photosystem absorbs one wavelength only" P680 and P700 are named for their absorption maxima, but they absorb a range of wavelengths via their antenna complexes
"Photorespiration is just 'respiration in the light'" Photorespiration is a wasteful salvage pathway triggered by RuBisCO oxygenase activity — completely distinct from mitochondrial respiration, which produces ATP
"C4 plants don't do the Calvin cycle" C4 plants DO run the Calvin cycle — they just do it in bundle sheath cells with concentrated CO₂
"CAM plants fix CO₂ at night and do Calvin cycle during the day" This is CORRECT — but students often confuse this with "C4 plants do Calvin cycle at night" (wrong — C4 plants do everything during the day, just in different cells)
"C4 and CAM use different enzymes" Both use PEP carboxylase for initial fixation and RuBisCO for the Calvin cycle. The difference is spatial (C4) vs temporal (CAM).
"RuBisCO is only found in plants" RuBisCO is found in all photosynthetic organisms — plants, algae, cyanobacteria, and some chemoautotrophic bacteria
"The Calvin cycle produces glucose" It produces G3P (triose phosphate). Glucose is synthesised from G3P in separate reactions.

Problem 1: Z-Scheme Redox Potentials

Explain why two photosystems are required for oxygenic photosynthesis. In your answer, refer to the redox potentials of the H₂O/½O₂ couple (+0.82 V) and the NADP⁺/NADPH couple (−0.32 V), and the energy of a single photon of visible light.

Problem 2: Calvin Cycle Accounting

For the fixation of 6 molecules of CO₂ in the Calvin cycle, calculate:

(a) The number of RuBP molecules consumed

(b) The total number of G3P molecules produced

(c) The number of G3P molecules that exit the cycle

(d) The total ATP consumed

(e) The total NADPH consumed

Problem 3: RuBisCO Regulation

Describe three mechanisms by which RuBisCO is activated in the light and inactivated in the dark. For each mechanism, explain how it is linked to the light reactions.

Problem 4: Photorespiration

(a) Explain why photorespiration occurs in C3 plants.

(b) Describe the fate of 2-phosphoglycolate, naming the organelles involved in its salvage.

(c) Explain why photorespiration increases at higher temperatures.

Problem 5: C4 and CAM Comparison

Compare C4 and CAM photosynthesis under the following headings:

(a) The mechanism by which CO₂ is concentrated around RuBisCO

(b) The anatomical adaptations involved

(c) The ecological conditions under which each is advantageous

(d) The energy cost relative to C3 photosynthesis

Answers

Answer 1

Two photosystems are required because a single photon cannot bridge the redox gap between water and NADP⁺.

  • The H₂O/½O₂ couple has E⁰′ = +0.82 V — electrons from water are at a relatively oxidising (positive) potential.
  • The NADP⁺/NADPH couple has E⁰′ = −0.32 V — to reduce NADP⁺, electrons must reach a reducing (negative) potential.
  • The gap is over 1.1 V.
  • A single photon of red light (λ ≈ 680 nm) has energy E = hc/λ ≈ 1.8 eV. While this is theoretically enough, in practice, photochemical energy conversion is never 100% efficient. Using two photosystems in series, each photon excites electrons by ~1 eV, making the overall transfer thermodynamically favourable with reasonable quantum efficiency.
  • PSII (P680 excited to ~−0.7 V) bridges from water oxidation to the ETC.
  • PSI (P700 excited to ~−1.0 V) bridges from plastocyanin to ferredoxin/NADP⁺ reduction.

Without two photosystems, the energy of a single photon would be insufficient to drive electrons from the oxidising potential of water to the reducing potential needed for NADP⁺.

Answer 2

For 6 CO₂ (two "turns" of the 3-CO₂ cycle):

(a) RuBP consumed: 6 RuBP (one per CO₂ fixed)

(b) Total G3P produced: 6 CO₂ → 12 × 3-PGA → 12 × G3P (after reduction phase)

(c) G3P exiting the cycle: 12 − 10 = 2 G3P exit (for every 3 CO₂, 1 G3P exits; for 6 CO₂, 2 G3P exit; the remaining 10 G3P = 30C → 6 RuBP = 30C)

(d) ATP consumed:

  • Reduction phase: 12 ATP (2 ATP per CO₂ fixed)
  • Regeneration: 6 ATP (1 ATP per CO₂ for the 6 RuBP regenerated from 10 G3P)
  • Total: 18 ATP

(e) NADPH consumed: 12 NADPH (2 NADPH per CO₂ fixed)

Answer 3

Mechanism 1: pH activation

  • In the light, H⁺ are pumped into the thylakoid lumen. Stromal pH rises from ~7.0 to ~8.0.
  • RuBisCO's pH optimum is ~8.0 — it is more active at the higher pH that prevails during photosynthesis.
  • In the dark, H⁺ leak back into the stroma, pH drops to ~7.0, and RuBisCO activity decreases.

Mechanism 2: Mg²⁺ activation

  • As H⁺ enter the thylakoid lumen in the light, Mg²⁺ ions are released into the stroma to maintain charge balance.
  • The increased stromal [Mg²⁺] promotes carbamylation of a specific lysine residue at the RuBisCO active site — this carbamylation is essential for catalytic activity and requires Mg²⁺.
  • In the dark, Mg²⁺ returns to the lumen, [Mg²⁺] in stroma drops, and RuBisCO loses its activating carbamylation.

Mechanism 3: Thioredoxin-mediated reduction

  • In the light, electrons from PSI reduce ferredoxin → ferredoxin-thioredoxin reductase reduces thioredoxin → reduced thioredoxin reduces regulatory disulfide bonds on RuBisCO (and other Calvin cycle enzymes), converting them to their active conformations.
  • In the dark, thioredoxin is no longer reduced; regulatory disulfide bonds re-oxidise, inactivating the enzymes.
  • This directly couples enzyme activity to the output of the light reactions.

Answer 4

(a) Why photorespiration occurs: RuBisCO can use either CO₂ (carboxylase activity) or O₂ (oxygenase activity) as a substrate. Both molecules are small, linear, and uncharged — RuBisCO's active site cannot perfectly discriminate between them. When O₂ reacts with RuBP, the product is 3-PGA + 2-phosphoglycolate (instead of 2 × 3-PGA). The 2-phosphoglycolate is toxic and must be salvaged via the photorespiratory pathway.

(b) Fate of 2-phosphoglycolate — organelles involved:

  1. Chloroplast: 2-PG → glycolate (dephosphorylation)
  2. Peroxisome: Glycolate → glyoxylate → glycine (oxidation + transamination; H₂O₂ produced and broken down by catalase)
  3. Mitochondrion: 2 glycine → serine + CO₂ + NH₃ (this is where previously fixed CO₂ is lost)
  4. Peroxisome: Serine → hydroxypyruvate → glycerate
  5. Chloroplast: Glycerate → 3-PGA (costs 1 ATP)

(c) Why photorespiration increases at higher temperatures:

  • RuBisCO's specificity factor (τ = ratio of carboxylation to oxygenation) decreases with temperature — the enzyme becomes relatively more oxygenase-active.
  • At high temperatures, stomata close to reduce water loss, trapping O₂ inside the leaf (from water splitting) while CO₂ entry is restricted. The [O₂]/[CO₂] ratio in the leaf increases, favouring oxygenase activity.
  • The solubility of CO₂ decreases faster than O₂ with increasing temperature, further raising the O₂/CO₂ ratio in solution.

Answer 5

(a) CO₂ concentration mechanism:

  • C4: Spatial separation. Mesophyll cells fix CO₂ via PEP carboxylase → 4C acid transported to bundle sheath cells → decarboxylated → high [CO₂] around RuBisCO in bundle sheath. CO₂ concentrated 10–100× atmospheric.
  • CAM: Temporal separation. Same cell fixes CO₂ at night (PEP carboxylase → malate stored in vacuole) and releases CO₂ during the day (malate decarboxylated → CO₂ enters Calvin cycle).

(b) Anatomical adaptations:

  • C4: Kranz anatomy — mesophyll cells form a concentric ring around bundle sheath cells. Bundle sheath cells have thick, suberised walls that prevent CO₂ leakage.
  • CAM: No specialised anatomy — both fixation and Calvin cycle occur in the same mesophyll cells. However, CAM cells typically have large vacuoles for malic acid storage.

(c) Ecological conditions:

  • C4: Advantageous in hot, high-light environments with moderate water availability (tropical and subtropical grasslands). Photorespiration is suppressed, and C4 plants outperform C3 at temperatures above ~30°C.
  • CAM: Advantageous in arid, water-limited environments (deserts). By opening stomata at night, CAM plants minimise water loss while still fixing CO₂. CAM plants are also common as epiphytes (plants growing on other plants, with limited water access).

(d) Energy cost:

  • C4: ~5 ATP + 2 NADPH per CO₂ fixed (vs C3: 3 ATP + 2 NADPH). The extra 2 ATP equivalent is for pyruvate phosphate dikinase (PEP regeneration).
  • CAM: Similar to C4 (~5 ATP + 2 NADPH per CO₂) — the extra cost is for PEP regeneration and the energy required to pump malate into the vacuole at night.
  • Both pay a higher energy price per CO₂ but avoid the ~25–50% loss from photorespiration, giving a net advantage in their respective environments.

Summary

  • Z-scheme: Two photosystems bridge the ~1.1 V redox gap between H₂O oxidation (+0.82 V) and NADP⁺ reduction (−0.32 V). Each photon excites electrons to sufficiently negative potentials.
  • Photosystem architecture: Antenna complex (200–300 pigments, funnel energy via FRET) → reaction centre (special pair chlorophyll a, charge separation, electron transfer)
  • OEC (Kok cycle): Mn₄CaO₅ cluster cycles through 5 oxidation states; 4 photons → 2H₂O → O₂ + 4H⁺ + 4e⁻
  • Calvin cycle: 3 phases — carboxylation (3 CO₂ + 3 RuBP → 6 × 3-PGA, RuBisCO), reduction (6 ATP + 6 NADPH → 6 G3P), regeneration (5 G3P + 3 ATP → 3 RuBP). Net: 9 ATP + 6 NADPH per 3 CO₂; 1 G3P exported.
  • RuBisCO: Most abundant protein on Earth; 8 large + 8 small subunits; slow enzyme (3 CO₂/s); activated by pH ~8, Mg²⁺, thioredoxin, RuBisCO activase
  • Photorespiration: RuBisCO oxygenase → 2-phosphoglycolate → salvage across chloroplast, peroxisome, mitochondrion; costs fixed carbon, ATP, reducing power; ~25–50% efficiency loss
  • C4 plants: Spatial CO₂ concentration; PEP carboxylase in mesophyll, Calvin cycle in bundle sheath; Kranz anatomy; 5 ATP per CO₂; maize, sugarcane
  • CAM plants: Temporal CO₂ concentration; stomata open at night, malate stored in vacuole, Calvin cycle during day; highest water use efficiency; cactus, pineapple
