- Polyploidy is the condition of having more than two complete sets of chromosomes (e.g., 3n = triploid, 4n = tetraploid) ✔
- Autopolyploidy: all chromosome sets are derived from the same species; arises through errors in meiosis (e.g., nondisjunction of all chromosomes producing diploid gametes) ✔
- Allopolyploidy: chromosome sets are derived from two or more different species; arises through hybridisation followed by chromosome doubling ✔

**(b)** Award [3 max]:

- Flowering plants are often hermaphroditic (have both male and female reproductive organs on the same individual), so self-fertilisation allows a polyploid to reproduce even without a polyploid mate ✔
- Plants can also reproduce vegetatively (asexually), allowing polyploids to persist and spread until sexual reproduction with another polyploid is possible ✔
- Many plant species have evolved post-polyploidisation mechanisms (e.g., diploidisation) that restore normal meiotic pairing ✔

**(c)** Award [2 max]:
- Hybridisation between two different diploid species produces a sterile hybrid (because chromosomes from the two parent species cannot pair properly during meiosis) ✔
- If chromosome doubling occurs (e.g., through meiotic nondisjunction), the hybrid becomes a fertile allopolyploid because each chromosome now has a homologous partner to pair with during meiosis ✔
- This creates a reproductively isolated new species in a single generation because the allopolyploid cannot interbreed with either parent species ✔

**In situ conservation — advantages:**
- Species are conserved in their natural habitat, maintaining ecological relationships (predator–prey, pollination, seed dispersal) ✔

- Larger populations can be maintained than in captivity ✔

- Habitats may be too degraded or fragmented to sustain viable populations ✔
- Poaching, invasive species, and climate change may continue to threaten species despite protected area status ✔

- Provides a safety net against extinction when wild populations are critically low; captive breeding can increase numbers (e.g., California condor increased from 27 to >400 individuals) ✔

- Does not preserve the ecological context; species may lose natural behaviours needed for survival upon reintroduction ✔
- Captive populations are small, leading to genetic bottlenecks, inbreeding depression, and loss of genetic diversity ✔
- Expensive and technically demanding; only feasible for a small fraction of threatened species ✔

- This triggers a trophic cascade: overgrazing reduces plant diversity and habitat complexity, negatively affecting many other species ✔
- Species diversity declines overall as competitively dominant prey species outcompete others, and habitat simplification eliminates niches ✔
- Ecosystem stability decreases: the system becomes more vulnerable to perturbations (disease, invasive species, environmental fluctuations) because redundancy and resilience are reduced ✔
- Real-world example: removal of sea otters (keystone predator) led to sea urchin population explosions and destruction of kelp forest ecosystems ✔
