**Q1.** Which statement best describes the effect of an enzyme on the activation energy of a biochemical reaction?

**Answer: B**

**Explanation:** Enzymes are biological catalysts that lower the activation energy (Eₐ) by providing an alternative reaction pathway involving the enzyme–substrate complex. This allows the reaction to proceed more rapidly at physiological temperatures.  
- **A is wrong:** Enzymes *lower*, not raise, activation energy.  
- **C is wrong:** Enzymes do not eliminate activation energy — a certain Eₐ always remains.  
- **D is wrong:** Enzymes do not increase kinetic energy of substrates; they stabilize the transition state.

**Q2.** Methotrexate is a drug that closely resembles the structure of folic acid and binds irreversibly to the active site of dihydrofolate reductase. This is an example of:

**Answer: C**

**Explanation:** A suicide inhibitor (also called a mechanism-based inhibitor) is a competitive inhibitor that binds irreversibly to the active site. Methotrexate competes with folic acid for the active site (competitive aspect) but binds irreversibly (suicide aspect).  
- **A is wrong:** Non-competitive inhibitors bind at an allosteric site, not the active site.  
- **B is wrong:** Feedback inhibition is a regulatory mechanism where the end product inhibits an earlier enzyme; it is not typically irreversible.  
- **D is wrong:** Allosteric regulation involves binding at a site other than the active site and is reversible.

**Q3. (HL)** The Lineweaver–Burk plot pattern shown is characteristic of:

**Answer: A**

**Explanation:** In competitive inhibition, the lines on a Lineweaver–Burk plot intersect at the y-axis (same V_max, different K_m). The steeper line (with inhibitor) has a higher apparent K_m, but both lines share the same y-intercept (1/V_max).  
- **B is wrong:** Non-competitive inhibition shows lines intersecting at the x-axis (same K_m, different V_max).  
- **C is wrong:** Uncompetitive inhibition shows parallel lines.  
- **D is wrong:** Feedback inhibition is a regulatory mechanism, not a kinetic pattern on a Lineweaver–Burk plot.

> *Note: The description "same x-intercept" in the question sketch means the lines share the same 1/V_max (y-intercept), supporting competitive inhibition.*

**Q4.** During glycolysis… correct net yield per glucose molecule?

**Answer: B**

**Explanation:** Glycolysis produces a **gross** yield of 4 ATP (by substrate-level phosphorylation) but consumes 2 ATP in the energy investment phase. The **net** yield is therefore **2 ATP** and **2 NADH** (from the oxidation of glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate).  
- **A is wrong:** 4 is the gross, not the net, ATP yield.  
- **C is wrong:** NADH is produced; the value is not zero.  
- **D is wrong:** Both the net ATP and NADH values are incorrect.

**Q5.** Cyanide binds to cytochrome c oxidase… most immediate consequence?

**Answer: C**

**Explanation:** Cyanide blocks Complex IV (cytochrome c oxidase), preventing the transfer of electrons to O₂. This halts the ETC, meaning protons are no longer pumped across the inner membrane, the proton gradient dissipates, and ATP synthase stops producing ATP.  
- **A is wrong:** Glycolysis is cytosolic and can continue without the ETC (anaerobically).  
- **B is wrong:** The Krebs cycle produces NADH and FADH₂; if they cannot be re-oxidized, the cycle will slow/halt, not accelerate.  
- **D is wrong:** NADH would accumulate, and the Krebs cycle *would* halt — but this is a *secondary* consequence. The most *immediate* effect is the collapse of the proton gradient.

**Q6.** In the light-dependent reactions of photosynthesis, the Z-scheme describes:

**Answer: B**

This is called the "Z-scheme" because the redox potential changes in a Z-shaped pattern when plotted. Water is the electron donor and NADP⁺ is the final electron acceptor.  
- **A is wrong:** Cyclic electron flow involves PSI only, not the Z-scheme.  
- **C is wrong:** Regeneration of RuBP occurs in the Calvin cycle (light-independent reactions).  
- **D is wrong:** Absorption spectra measure light absorbed by pigments, not electron flow.

**Q8. (HL)** A peptide hormone binds… correct sequence?

This enzyme converts ATP to cAMP (the second messenger), which activates protein kinase A (PKA), leading to a phosphorylation cascade.

**Q9.** During an action potential, the rising (depolarization) phase is primarily caused by:

**Answer: C**

**Explanation:** When the membrane reaches threshold potential (≈ –55 mV), voltage-gated Na⁺ channels open, allowing Na⁺ to rush into the axon down its electrochemical gradient. This rapid influx of positive charge depolarizes the membrane toward the Na⁺ equilibrium potential (+30 to +40 mV).  
- **A is wrong:** K⁺ efflux through voltage-gated K⁺ channels causes repolarization, not depolarization.  
- **B is wrong:** Na⁺ channel *inactivation* occurs at the peak; K⁺ influx does not occur (K⁺ flows *out*).  
- **D is wrong:** The Na⁺/K⁺ ATPase maintains resting potential but is too slow to drive the action potential.

**Q10. (HL)** LTP involves:

**Answer: B**

**Explanation:** Long-term potentiation (LTP) at glutamatergic synapses requires activation of both AMPA and NMDA receptors. At resting potential, NMDA receptors are blocked by Mg²⁺. Strong depolarization (via AMPA receptor activation) expels the Mg²⁺ block, allowing Ca²⁺ to enter through NMDA receptors. Ca²⁺ influx triggers intracellular cascades that increase postsynaptic AMPA receptor number and sensitivity.  
- **A is wrong:** LTP involves an *increase*, not decrease, in AMPA receptors.  
- **C is wrong:** Na⁺ channels are not permanently inactivated; the refractory period is temporary.  
- **D is wrong:** Hyperpolarization would inhibit, not facilitate, LTP.

- **C is wrong:** pH regulation involves H⁺ secretion but is not primarily in response to ADH; ADH acts on the collecting duct.

**Q14.** As N approaches K in the logistic model…

**Answer: C**

**Explanation:** The logistic growth equation is dN/dt = rN × (K – N)/K. As N → K, (K – N)/K → 0, so dN/dt → 0. The population stops growing and stabilizes at K.  
- **A is wrong:** Per capita growth rate = r × (K – N)/K, which approaches zero as N → K, not *r*.  
- **B is wrong:** The lag phase occurs at the *beginning* of population growth, not near K.  
- **D is wrong:** In the basic logistic model, the population approaches K asymptotically without overshooting.

**Q15.** In a grassland food chain… percentage of energy fixed by grass available to snake?

**Answer: C**

**Explanation:** Approximately 10% of energy is transferred between each trophic level. Grass (producer) → Grasshopper (1° consumer) = 10% → Frog (2° consumer) = 1% → Snake (3° consumer) = 0.1%. The snake is three trophic levels above the producer, so: 10% × 10% × 10% = **0.1%**.  
- **A is wrong:** 10% is the energy available to the grasshopper (one transfer).  
- **B is wrong:** 1% is the energy available to the frog (two transfers).  
- **D is wrong:** 0.01% would be four trophic levels (e.g., hawk).

**Q17. (HL)** *Cell Respiration — Extended Response*

**(a)** Outline the fate of acetyl-CoA from the link reaction through the Krebs cycle. [3]

**Model answer:**
1. Acetyl-CoA (2C) enters the Krebs cycle by combining with oxaloacetate (4C) to form **citrate** (6C), catalyzed by citrate synthase.
2. Citrate undergoes a series of oxidation, decarboxylation, and rearrangement reactions, regenerating **oxaloacetate** (4C) to continue the cycle.
3. **Per acetyl-CoA**, the cycle yields: 2 CO₂ (released as waste), 3 NADH, 1 FADH₂, and 1 ATP (GTP) by substrate-level phosphorylation.

> *Mark scheme:* 1 mark for joining with oxaloacetate to form citrate; 1 mark for identifying products (CO₂, reduced coenzymes, ATP/GTP); 1 mark for regeneration of oxaloacetate.

---

**(b)** Explain ETC coupling to ATP synthesis (chemiosmosis). [4]

**Model answer:**
1. NADH and FADH₂ donate electrons to the electron transport chain (ETC) on the **inner mitochondrial membrane**.
2. As electrons pass through ETC complexes (I → IV), the energy released is used to pump **protons (H⁺) from the matrix into the intermembrane space**, creating an electrochemical (proton) gradient.
3. This gradient represents **potential energy** (proton motive force). Protons can only flow back into the matrix through **ATP synthase** (Complex V), a membrane protein.
4. The flow of protons through ATP synthase drives the **rotation** of the enzyme, coupling the energy of the proton gradient to the **phosphorylation of ADP → ATP** (oxidative phosphorylation). O₂ acts as the **terminal electron acceptor**, forming H₂O.

> *Mark scheme:* 1 mark for NADH/FADH₂ donating electrons to ETC; 1 mark for proton pumping and gradient formation; 1 mark for protons flowing through ATP synthase; 1 mark for ATP synthesis coupled to proton flow + O₂ as terminal acceptor.

---

**(c)** Describe β-oxidation and entry into aerobic respiration. [3]

**Model answer:**
1. β-oxidation occurs in the **mitochondrial matrix**, where fatty acids are broken down in a cyclical process removing **2-carbon acetyl-CoA units** from the fatty acid chain.
2. Each round of β-oxidation also produces **1 NADH and 1 FADH₂**.
3. The acetyl-CoA produced enters the **Krebs cycle** directly, while NADH and FADH₂ donate electrons to the **ETC** for oxidative phosphorylation.

> *Mark scheme:* 1 mark for location (mitochondrial matrix) and removal of 2C acetyl-CoA units; 1 mark for production of reduced coenzymes (NADH + FADH₂); 1 mark for acetyl-CoA entering Krebs cycle and reduced coenzymes feeding into ETC.

**Q18.** *Photosynthesis — Data-Based Question*

The limiting factor is **CO₂ concentration**.

At high light intensities (1200–1600 µmol m⁻² s⁻¹) with 0.2% NaHCO₃, **CO₂ concentration becomes the limiting factor**. The photosynthetic machinery (Calvin cycle enzymes, particularly RuBisCO) is operating at its maximum rate for the available CO₂. Increasing light intensity further does not increase the rate of the light-independent reactions, so the overall rate of photosynthesis plateaus.

**(c)** Outline thin-layer chromatography (TLC) for photosynthetic pigments. Include R_f. [3]

**Model answer:**
1. **Method:** Pigments are extracted from spinach leaves by grinding with propanone (acetone). The extract is spotted onto a TLC plate (silica gel). The plate is placed in a solvent (e.g., petroleum ether:propanone mixture) in a sealed container.
2. **Separation:** As solvent rises by capillary action, pigments partition between the stationary phase (silica) and mobile phase (solvent) based on their solubility and polarity.
3. **R_f calculation:** R_f = (distance moved by pigment spot) ÷ (distance moved by solvent front). Each pigment (chlorophyll a, chlorophyll b, carotenes, xanthophylls) has a characteristic R_f value under given conditions, allowing identification by comparison with known standards.

> *Mark scheme:* 1 mark for extraction and spotting method; 1 mark for separation principle; 1 mark for R_f formula + identification by comparison.

**(d) (HL)** Explain the Z-scheme generation of ATP and NADPH. [4]

**Model answer:**
1. **Photosystem II (PSII):** Light energy excites electrons in the reaction center (P680). Electrons are passed to the primary electron acceptor. PSII splits water (photolysis): 2H₂O → 4H⁺ + 4e⁻ + O₂, replacing lost electrons.
2. **Electron transport:** Excited electrons travel from PSII to PSI via an ETC (plastoquinone, cytochrome b₆f, plastocyanin). The energy released pumps **protons into the thylakoid lumen**, creating a proton gradient.
3. **ATP synthesis:** Protons flow back into the stroma through **ATP synthase**, driving photophosphorylation of ADP → ATP.
4. **Photosystem I (PSI):** Light re-excites electrons at P700. These electrons are passed via ferredoxin to ferredoxin-NADP⁺ reductase, which reduces **NADP⁺ → NADPH**. The overall products are **ATP, NADPH, and O₂**.

> *Mark scheme:* 1 mark for PSII photolysis of water; 1 mark for electron flow through ETC and proton pumping; 1 mark for chemiosmotic ATP synthesis; 1 mark for PSI and NADP⁺ reduction.

**(b)** Glucagon signaling cascade in hepatocytes. [4]

**Q20. (HL)** *Neural Signaling — Action Potential Graph Interpretation*

**(a)** Identify the membrane potential values. [2]

**Model answer:**

- **Threshold potential:** approximately –55 mV
- **Peak of action potential:** approximately +40 mV

> *Mark scheme:* 2 marks for all three correct; 1 mark for two correct.

**(b)** Describe the ionic events. [6]

**(b)(i) Phase A (RMP to threshold): [2]**

Phase A represents a **graded (generator/receptor) potential**. A stimulus causes **ligand-gated Na⁺ channels** to open, allowing a small influx of Na⁺. This causes partial depolarization. If the depolarization reaches **threshold** (≈ –55 mV), an action potential is triggered.

> *Mark scheme:* 1 mark for graded depolarization; 1 mark for Na⁺ influx through ligand-gated channels reaching threshold.

**(b)(ii) Phase B (rising phase): [2]**

Once threshold is reached, **voltage-gated Na⁺ channels** open rapidly. Na⁺ floods into the axon down its electrochemical gradient, causing rapid **depolarization** toward the Na⁺ equilibrium potential (+30 to +40 mV). This is a positive feedback process: depolarization opens more Na⁺ channels.

> *Mark scheme:* 1 mark for voltage-gated Na⁺ channel opening; 1 mark for Na⁺ influx causing depolarization toward positive values.

**(b)(iii) Phase C (falling phase/hyperpolarization): [2]**

At the peak, **voltage-gated Na⁺ channels inactivate** (ball-and-chain mechanism). **Voltage-gated K⁺ channels** (delayed rectifiers) are fully open. K⁺ efflux repolarizes the membrane. Because K⁺ channels close slowly, the membrane **hyperpolarizes** below resting potential (to approx. –80 mV) before the Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase restores the RMP.

> *Mark scheme:* 1 mark for Na⁺ channel inactivation and K⁺ channel opening; 1 mark for K⁺ efflux causing repolarization and hyperpolarization.

---

**(c)** Absolute refractory period and Na⁺ channel states. [2]

**Model answer:**
The **absolute refractory period** is the time during which a second action potential **cannot be initiated**, regardless of stimulus strength. This occurs because **voltage-gated Na⁺ channels are inactivated** (the inactivation gate is closed) and cannot reopen until the membrane repolarizes. This ensures: (i) unidirectional propagation of action potentials, and (ii) discrete signals with a maximum firing frequency.

> *Mark scheme:* 1 mark for definition (cannot fire second AP); 1 mark for linking to Na⁺ channel inactivation.

---

**(d)** Temporal vs. spatial summation. [2]

**Model answer:**
- **Temporal summation:** Multiple EPSPs arrive from the **same** presynaptic neuron in **rapid succession** (high frequency) at a single synapse. The postsynaptic potentials add together before the first decays, potentially reaching threshold.
- **Spatial summation:** EPSPs arrive **simultaneously** from **multiple different** presynaptic neurons synapsing at different locations on the same postsynaptic neuron. The combined depolarization may reach threshold at the axon hillock.

> *Mark scheme:* 1 mark for temporal (same neuron, rapid succession); 1 mark for spatial (multiple neurons, simultaneous).

**Q21.** *Integration of Body Systems — Homeostasis*

**(a)** Define homeostasis and explain its importance for enzyme function. [2]

**Model answer:**
**Homeostasis** is the maintenance of a relatively constant internal environment within narrow limits, despite changes in external conditions.

**Importance for enzymes:** Enzymes have optimal temperature and pH ranges. Deviations from these optima reduce enzyme activity (by denaturation or altered ionization of active site residues), disrupting metabolism. Homeostasis maintains conditions (e.g., 37°C, pH 7.4) within the optimal range for enzymatic reactions.

> *Mark scheme:* 1 mark for definition; 1 mark for linking to enzyme temperature/pH optima and metabolic consequences.

**(b)** Hypothalamus and pituitary in thermoregulation (body temperature rising). [3]

**Model answer:**
1. **Thermoreceptors** in the skin and hypothalamus detect increased core temperature and send signals to the **hypothalamus** (thermoregulatory center).
2. The hypothalamus initiates cooling mechanisms via the **autonomic nervous system**: vasodilation of skin arterioles (increases heat loss by radiation/convection) and activation of **sweat glands** (evaporative cooling).
3. The **pituitary gland** is involved in regulating metabolic rate via **TSH → thyroxine**: when temperature rises, TSH secretion is reduced, lowering thyroxine and thus decreasing metabolic heat production.

> *Mark scheme:* 1 mark for detection by thermoreceptors and hypothalamus; 1 mark for vasodilation and sweating; 1 mark for pituitary–thyroid axis reduction.

**(c) (HL)** Countercurrent multiplier in the loop of Henle. [4]

**Model answer:**
1. The loop of Henle creates an **osmotic gradient** in the medulla of the kidney, from ~300 mOsm in the cortex to ~1200 mOsm in the inner medulla.
2. The **descending limb** is permeable to water but not to Na⁺/Cl⁻. Water exits by osmosis into the hypertonic medulla, concentrating the filtrate.
3. The **ascending limb** is impermeable to water but actively transports **Na⁺ and Cl⁻** out (via Na⁺-K⁺-2Cl⁻ cotransporters in the thick ascending limb). This increases medullary interstitial osmolarity.
4. The **countercurrent flow** (filtrate flowing in opposite directions in the two limbs) **multiplies** this effect: the active transport in the ascending limb creates a gradient that draws water from the descending limb, which further concentrates the filtrate entering the ascending limb, amplifying the medullary gradient. This enables the collecting duct to produce **concentrated urine** under ADH influence.

> *Mark scheme:* 1 mark for osmotic gradient description; 1 mark for descending limb water permeability; 1 mark for ascending limb active Na⁺/Cl⁻ transport; 1 mark for countercurrent multiplication concept and concentrated urine production.

**Q22. (HL)** *Defence Against Disease — HIV Data Analysis*

**(a)** Relationship between HIV RNA and CD4⁺ count in the first year. [2]

**Model answer:**
During the first year: HIV RNA levels are **very high** (acute viremia), while CD4⁺ T-cell counts **decline sharply**. After the peak of viremia (as the immune response begins to control the infection), HIV RNA levels drop and CD4⁺ counts partially **recover**, though not to pre-infection levels.

> *Mark scheme:* 1 mark for noting inverse relationship (high viral load, low CD4⁺); 1 mark for describing partial recovery of CD4⁺ as viral load drops.

**(b)** Why anti-HIV antibodies not detectable until several weeks. [2]

**Model answer:**
After initial infection, there is a **lag phase** before the adaptive immune response can produce detectable antibodies. This is because:
1. The immune system must first recognize the virus, requiring **antigen presentation** by dendritic cells to T-helper cells.
2. T-helper cells must then activate **B-lymphocytes** specific to HIV antigens, which undergo **clonal expansion and differentiation** into plasma cells. Plasma cells then produce antibodies. This process typically takes **2–6 weeks** (the "window period" before seroconversion).

> *Mark scheme:* 1 mark for describing the lag phase of adaptive immunity; 1 mark for linking to antigen presentation, clonal selection, and plasma cell differentiation.

**(c)** HIV entry and replication in CD4⁺ T-helper cells. [3]

**Model answer:**

**(d) (HL)** Role of nitrifying bacteria in the nitrogen cycle. [4]

**Model answer:**
1. **Nitrification** is a two-step aerobic process carried out by chemolithotrophic bacteria in the soil:
   - *Nitrosomonas* oxidizes **ammonium (NH₄⁺)** to **nitrite (NO₂⁻)**.
   - *Nitrobacter* oxidizes **nitrite (NO₂⁻)** to **nitrate (NO₃⁻)**.
2. This is essential because plants can absorb nitrogen primarily as **nitrate (NO₃⁻)** through their roots (and to a lesser extent as ammonium).
3. Without nitrification, ammonium from ammonification (decomposition) would accumulate, and nitrogen would be unavailable to most plants in a usable form, limiting plant growth and primary production.
4. Nitrate is also highly soluble and mobile in soil water, making it readily accessible for root uptake and assimilation into amino acids and nucleotides.

> *Mark scheme:* 1 mark for *Nitrosomonas* → NH₄⁺ to NO₂⁻; 1 mark for *Nitrobacter* → NO₂⁻ to NO₃⁻; 1 mark for nitrate as the plant-available form; 1 mark for link to plant growth and primary production.

**(e)** Seasonal oscillations in the Keeling curve. [2]

**Model answer:**
The seasonal oscillations (sawtooth pattern) reflect **photosynthesis and respiration in the Northern Hemisphere** (which has more land mass and vegetation):

- In **spring/summer**: increased photosynthesis by terrestrial plants fixes CO₂, causing atmospheric CO₂ to **decline**.
- In **autumn/winter**: photosynthesis decreases while respiration (decomposition of leaf litter) continues, releasing CO₂ and causing atmospheric CO₂ to **rise**.

The Northern Hemisphere dominates this pattern because it has significantly more land mass (and thus more terrestrial vegetation) than the Southern Hemisphere.

> *Mark scheme:* 1 mark for linking decline to spring/summer photosynthesis; 1 mark for linking rise to autumn/winter respiration and Northern Hemisphere dominance.

**Q25. (HL)** *Integrative Question — Theme C*

**(a)** Hormonal response to falling blood glucose. [5]

**Model answer:**
1. **Detection:** α-cells in the **islets of Langerhans (pancreas)** detect low blood glucose and secrete **glucagon** into the bloodstream.
2. **Receptor binding:** Glucagon binds to **G-protein coupled receptors** on hepatocyte membranes.
3. **Second messenger cascade:** Receptor activation → G-protein → adenylyl cyclase → **cAMP** (second messenger) → **protein kinase A (PKA)** activation.
4. **Glycogenolysis:** PKA phosphorylates and activates **glycogen phosphorylase**, which breaks down glycogen → glucose-1-phosphate → glucose-6-phosphate.
5. **Gluconeogenesis and release:** PKA also promotes **gluconeogenesis** (synthesis of glucose from lactate, amino acids, glycerol). Glucose-6-phosphate is converted to free **glucose** (by glucose-6-phosphatase) and released into the blood, raising blood glucose toward normal.

> *Mark scheme:* 1 mark for α-cells detecting low glucose and secreting glucagon; 1 mark for G-protein coupled receptor and adenylyl cyclase; 1 mark for cAMP as second messenger; 1 mark for glycogenolysis via phosphorylase cascade; 1 mark for gluconeogenesis and glucose release.

**(b)** Thermoregulatory mechanisms and conflict with osmoregulation. [4]

**Model answer:**
**Thermoregulatory mechanisms to dissipate heat:**
1. **Vasodilation** of skin arterioles — increases blood flow to skin surface, promoting heat loss by radiation and convection.
2. **Sweating** — sweat glands secrete fluid onto skin; evaporation of sweat removes latent heat from the body surface, cooling the skin and underlying blood.

**Conflict with osmoregulation:**
Prolonged sweating results in significant **water loss** from the body, increasing **plasma osmolality** (as detected by osmoreceptors in the hypothalamus). This triggers **ADH release** from the posterior pituitary to promote water reabsorption in the kidney collecting duct, reducing urine output. However, if the athlete continues to sweat for thermoregulation, there is **competing demand** — the body must choose between water conservation (osmoregulation) and heat dissipation (thermoregulation). Dehydration can result if water loss exceeds intake.

> *Mark scheme:* 1 mark for vasodilation mechanism; 1 mark for sweating/evaporative cooling; 1 mark for water loss increasing plasma osmolality; 1 mark for ADH response and competing demands explanation.

**(c)** Anaerobic pathway when oxygen is limiting. [3]

**Model answer:**
1. When O₂ delivery is insufficient, the ETC and oxidative phosphorylation slow, NADH accumulates, and NAD⁺ becomes scarce. Glycolysis would stop without NAD⁺ regeneration.
2. **Anaerobic glycolysis (lactate pathway):** Pyruvate is reduced to **lactate** by **lactate dehydrogenase**, oxidizing NADH back to **NAD⁺**. This allows glycolysis (substrate-level phosphorylation) to continue, producing a net **2 ATP per glucose**.
3. **Fate of lactate:** Lactate is transported via the blood to the **liver**, where it is oxidized back to pyruvate and then converted to glucose via **gluconeogenesis** (the Cori cycle). Alternatively, lactate can be used directly as a fuel by cardiac muscle.

> *Mark scheme:* 1 mark for need to regenerate NAD⁺; 1 mark for pyruvate → lactate via lactate dehydrogenase; 1 mark for fate of lactate (Cori cycle/liver).

**(d)** Detection of increased plasma osmolality and role of ADH. [3]

**Model answer:**
1. **Detection:** **Osmoreceptors** in the **hypothalamus** detect the increase in plasma osmolality (caused by water loss from sweating).
2. **ADH secretion:** The hypothalamus signals the **posterior pituitary gland** to release **antidiuretic hormone (ADH)** into the bloodstream.
3. **Action of ADH:** ADH binds to receptors on the **collecting duct** cells of the kidney, triggering insertion of **aquaporin-2** water channels into the apical membrane via cAMP-mediated vesicle fusion. This increases the permeability of the collecting duct to water, allowing more water to be reabsorbed into the hypertonic medulla, producing **small volumes of concentrated urine** and restoring plasma osmolality to normal.

> *Mark scheme:* 1 mark for osmoreceptors in hypothalamus; 1 mark for ADH release from posterior pituitary; 1 mark for aquaporin insertion in collecting duct and water reabsorption.
