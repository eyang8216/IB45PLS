**Q16.** **(B1.1 — Carbohydrates)**

**(a)** α-1,4-glycosidic bonds (for linear chains) and α-1,6-glycosidic bonds (at branch points). [1]

**(b)** [2 marks]
- Glycogen has more frequent branching (every ~10 residues) compared to amylopectin (every ~20 residues). This provides more terminal glucose units that can be simultaneously cleaved by glycogen phosphorylase.
- The highly branched structure allows rapid mobilization of glucose because multiple enzymes can act on different branch termini at the same time, releasing glucose-1-phosphate more quickly to meet the high metabolic demands of animal tissues (especially muscle and liver).

**(c)** [3 marks]
- **Starch:** Contains α-glucose monomers linked by α-1,4-glycosidic bonds, which produce a helical/coiled structure. Human digestive enzymes (amylase) can recognize and hydrolyze α-1,4 bonds, making starch digestible.
- **Cellulose:** Contains β-glucose monomers linked by β-1,4-glycosidic bonds, producing straight, linear chains. The β-linkage means every other glucose is inverted, allowing extensive inter-chain hydrogen bonding.
- Humans lack cellulase (the enzyme that hydrolyzes β-1,4 bonds), so cellulose passes through the digestive tract as insoluble fiber. In contrast, starch's α-linkages are substrate-compatible with human α-amylase and maltase.

**(d)** Any one of: [1 mark]
- Long, straight, parallel chains of β-glucose allow extensive hydrogen bonding between adjacent cellulose chains.
- Bundling of 60–70 cellulose chains into microfibrils, which are further bundled into macrofibrils.
- The alternating orientation of glucose monomers (each rotated 180°) enables close packing and maximization of inter-chain H-bonds.

**Q17.** **(B1.2 and B3.1 — Data-based)**

**(a)** As pH decreases, hemoglobin's affinity for oxygen decreases (the oxygen dissociation curve shifts to the right). [1]

**(b)** At pO₂ = 4 kPa: pH 7.6 saturation ≈ 75%, pH 7.2 saturation ≈ 35%. Difference = 75 − 35 = 40% [1]

**(c)** [3 marks]
- Actively respiring tissues produce CO₂, which dissolves in blood plasma to form carbonic acid (H₂CO₃), dissociating to H⁺ and HCO₃⁻, lowering pH.
- The Bohr shift (rightward shift of the dissociation curve) means that at any given pO₂, hemoglobin's oxygen saturation is lower at lower pH.
- This promotes **unloading of oxygen** precisely where it is most needed — in tissues with high respiratory rates. CO₂ produced by respiration facilitates its own removal by promoting O₂ delivery, creating a physiologically efficient feedback loop.

**(d) (HL)** [2 marks]

- The lack of sigmoidicity indicates **no cooperative binding** (myoglobin is a monomer with a single heme group). This means it holds onto O₂ tightly until extreme hypoxia, making it ideal for storage rather than transport (which hemoglobin's sigmoidal cooperativity is better suited for).

**Q18.** **(Fick's Law Calculation — B3.1)**

**(a)** [2 marks]

$$\text{Rate} = k \times \frac{A \times \Delta C}{d}$$

$$\text{Rate} = (2.0 \times 10^{-5}) \times \frac{(7.0 \times 10^{-5}) \times (8.0 \times 10^{-2})}{5.0 \times 10^{-7}}$$

Numerator of fraction: (7.0 × 10⁻⁵) × (8.0 × 10⁻²) = 5.6 × 10⁻⁶

Fraction: (5.6 × 10⁻⁶) / (5.0 × 10⁻⁷) = 11.2

Rate = (2.0 × 10⁻⁵) × 11.2 = **2.24 × 10⁻⁴ mol s⁻¹** (or 2.2 × 10⁻⁴ mol s⁻¹ to 2 significant figures)

Award [1] for correct substitution and [1] for correct answer with units.

**(b)** [2 marks]

New d = 2.0 × 10⁻⁶ m

$$\text{New rate} = (2.0 \times 10^{-5}) \times \frac{(7.0 \times 10^{-5}) \times (8.0 \times 10^{-2})}{2.0 \times 10^{-6}}$$

Fraction: (5.6 × 10⁻⁶) / (2.0 × 10⁻⁶) = 2.8

New rate = (2.0 × 10⁻⁵) × 2.8 = **5.6 × 10⁻⁵ mol s⁻¹**

Percentage = (5.6 × 10⁻⁵ / 2.24 × 10⁻⁴) × 100 = **25%**

Award [1] for correct new rate and [1] for correct percentage.

**(c)** [2 marks]
- In emphysema, the alveolar walls break down, reducing the total surface area available for gas exchange (the alveoli coalesce into fewer, larger sacs).
- According to Fick's Law, a reduced surface area (A) directly reduces the rate of diffusion, even if diffusion distance (d) remains normal.
- Additionally, breakdown of elastic fibers in alveolar walls reduces passive recoil during expiration, impairing ventilation and thus reducing the concentration gradient (ΔC) for O₂ and CO₂ exchange.

**Q19.** **(B2.1 and B2.2 — Structured)**

**(a)** [3 marks]
- The membrane consists of a phospholipid bilayer with hydrophilic phosphate heads facing outward (toward aqueous environments on both sides) and hydrophobic fatty acid tails facing inward, forming a selectively permeable barrier.
- Proteins are embedded within or associated with the bilayer: integral proteins span the membrane, while peripheral proteins are attached to one surface.
- The "fluid" aspect refers to the lateral movement of phospholipids and many proteins within the leaflet; the "mosaic" aspect refers to the patchwork of different proteins, glycoproteins, and glycolipids distributed throughout. Cholesterol (in animal cells) modulates fluidity.

**(b)** [3 marks]
- The Na⁺/K⁺ pump actively transports 3 Na⁺ ions out of the cell and 2 K⁺ ions into the cell per ATP hydrolyzed, against their concentration gradients.
- This creates an electrochemical gradient: high [K⁺] inside and high [Na⁺] outside. The net export of one positive charge per cycle makes the pump electrogenic.
- The unequal distribution of ions, combined with differential membrane permeability (more K⁺ leak channels than Na⁺ leak channels), establishes the resting membrane potential (approximately −70 mV in neurons).

**(c) (HL)** [2 marks]
- **Receptor-mediated endocytosis:** Specific molecules (ligands) bind to receptor proteins clustered in clathrin-coated pits on the plasma membrane. The membrane invaginates and pinches off to form a coated vesicle containing the ligand-receptor complexes. Highly selective.
- **Phagocytosis:** The cell extends pseudopodia to engulf large, solid particles (e.g., bacteria, cell debris) into a large vesicle called a phagosome. Less selective; typically performed by specialized cells such as macrophages and neutrophils. Involves actin polymerization-driven membrane protrusion.
- [Also accept: receptor-mediated endocytosis involves clathrin-coated vesicles; phagocytosis involves actin-driven engulfment; receptor-mediated is highly specific while phagocytosis is triggered by general recognition of foreign particles.]

**(d)** [1 mark]
- The lumen (interior) of a transport vesicle is derived from the lumen of the donor organelle (e.g., ER or Golgi), which is topologically equivalent to the extracellular space because during vesicle budding, the inner leaflet of the donor membrane becomes the inner leaflet of the vesicle membrane. Thus, secreted proteins never actually cross a membrane — they remain within the lumenal/extracellular compartment throughout.

**Q23.** **(Extended Response — B3.2)**

**(a)** [4 marks]
- **Atrial systole:** Both atria contract, increasing atrial pressure. Blood flows passively and then actively through the open AV valves (tricuspid and bicuspid) into the relaxed ventricles. Semilunar valves remain closed.
- **Ventricular systole (isovolumetric contraction):** Ventricles begin contracting. Ventricular pressure rises rapidly and exceeds atrial pressure, causing the AV valves to snap shut (preventing backflow). Semilunar valves are still closed because ventricular pressure is below aortic/pulmonary pressure. Volume is constant.
- **Ventricular systole (ejection):** Ventricular pressure exceeds aortic (~16 kPa) and pulmonary artery pressures, forcing the semilunar valves open. Blood is ejected into the aorta and pulmonary artery. Ventricular volume decreases.
- **Diastole:** Ventricles relax. Ventricular pressure drops below aortic/pulmonary pressure, causing semilunar valves to close (preventing backflow from arteries). When ventricular pressure drops below atrial pressure, AV valves open and passive ventricular filling begins.

**(b) (HL)** [3 marks]
- The **sinoatrial (SA) node** in the wall of the right atrium acts as the pacemaker. It spontaneously generates action potentials (due to pacemaker potential), initiating atrial depolarization and contraction (atrial systole).
- The electrical impulse spreads across both atria (causing contraction) and converges at the **atrioventricular (AV) node**, located at the junction of the atria and ventricles.
- The AV node delays the impulse (~0.1 s) to allow complete atrial contraction and ventricular filling before the impulse is conducted rapidly through the **Bundle of His**, down the interventricular septum via **left and right bundle branches**, and through **Purkinje fibers**, causing coordinated ventricular contraction from the apex upward.

**(c)** [3 marks]
- **Vasoconstriction and platelet plug:** Damaged blood vessel walls trigger vasoconstriction. Platelets adhere to exposed collagen in the damaged endothelium and release substances (e.g., thromboxane A₂, serotonin) that attract more platelets, forming a temporary platelet plug.
- **Coagulation cascade:** A cascade of clotting factors (serine proteases, including Factor X, prothrombin, fibrinogen) is activated via the intrinsic and/or extrinsic pathways, converging on the activation of Factor X.
- **Fibrin formation:** Factor Xa converts **prothrombin** to **thrombin** (with Factor Va as a cofactor). Thrombin then converts soluble **fibrinogen** into insoluble **fibrin** monomers, which polymerize and are cross-linked by Factor XIIIa to form a stable meshwork that traps blood cells, forming a stable clot.

**Q24.** **(B4.1 and B4.2 — Synthesis)**

**(a)** [2 marks]
- **Fundamental niche:** The full range of environmental conditions (abiotic factors) and resources under which a species can potentially survive, grow, and reproduce in the absence of competition, predation, or other biotic constraints.
- **Realized niche:** The actual range of conditions and resources a species occupies in nature, which is typically narrower than the fundamental niche due to biotic interactions such as competition, predation, and parasitism.

**(b)** [2 marks]
- **Fundamental niche of *Chthamalus*:** The entire intertidal zone, from the upper shore to the lower shore (as demonstrated by its ability to survive and grow in the lower zone when *Semibalanus* is removed).
- **Realized niche of *Chthamalus*:** Restricted to the upper intertidal zone only, because *Semibalanus* competitively excludes it from the lower zone.

**(c)** [3 marks]
- The experiment demonstrates **competitive exclusion**: when both species coexist, *Semibalanus* outcompetes *Chthamalus* in the lower intertidal zone (likely through faster growth or undercutting), excluding *Chthamalus* from this region.
- When *Semibalanus* is removed, *Chthamalus* expands into the lower zone, confirming that its fundamental niche includes the entire shore and its realized niche is limited by interspecific competition.
- **Niche partitioning** is demonstrated by the spatial separation: *Chthamalus* occupies the upper zone (where it tolerates desiccation better), while *Semibalanus* occupies the lower zone (where it is a superior competitor). This reduces direct competition, allowing coexistence.

**(d) (HL)** [3 marks]
- Sea otters are a keystone species because their effect on ecosystem structure is disproportionately large relative to their biomass.
- Sea otters prey on sea urchins, which are herbivores that consume kelp. In the presence of sea otters, sea urchin populations are controlled, allowing kelp forests to thrive and support a diverse community.
- **Trophic cascade upon otter removal:** Otter decline → sea urchin population explosion → overgrazing of kelp → destruction of kelp forest habitat → collapse of the diverse community dependent on kelp (fish, invertebrates, other species) → formation of "urchin barrens." This cascade demonstrates how a top predator controls ecosystem structure via indirect effects across multiple trophic levels.

**Q25.** **(Integrated Analysis — B1.1, B1.2, B2.1, B2.2)**

7. **Plasma membrane** — exocytosis, releasing the mature glycoprotein hormone into the bloodstream

[Any 4 of the above in correct sequence for full marks. Must include nucleus/genetic information → RER → Golgi → secretion.]

**(b)** [5 marks]
**(i) Rough endoplasmic reticulum:** [2]
- Provides the site of protein synthesis for the hormone's polypeptide chains via bound ribosomes.
- Within the RER lumen, signal peptidase cleaves the signal peptide, chaperone proteins (e.g., BiP) assist with proper folding, and enzymes catalyze the formation of disulfide bonds (between cysteine residues) that link the α and β subunits.

**(ii) Golgi apparatus:** [2]
- Modifies the N-linked oligosaccharides added in the RER — trimming mannose residues and adding galactose and N-acetylglucosamine residues (glycan processing) to produce mature glycoprotein.
- Sorts and packages the completed hormone into secretory vesicles at the trans-Golgi network, directing it for regulated secretion in response to cellular signals.

**(iii) Chaperone proteins:** [1]
- Chaperones (e.g., BiP/GRP78, calnexin, calreticulin) assist in the proper folding of nascent polypeptide chains in the ER, preventing misfolding and aggregation. They recognize exposed hydrophobic patches and facilitate correct tertiary structure formation. Calnexin/calreticulin specifically assist in folding of glycosylated proteins.

**(c)** [2 marks]
- Mannose, galactose, and N-acetylglucosamine belong to the class of biomolecules called **carbohydrates** (specifically, they are **monosaccharides**).
- They are linked together by **glycosidic bonds** (formed by condensation reactions between the hydroxyl groups of adjacent monosaccharide residues).

**(d)** [2 marks]
- **Hydrophilic carbohydrate groups:** The hydroxyl (−OH) groups on the carbohydrate residues readily form hydrogen bonds with water molecules in blood plasma, increasing the hormone's solubility and preventing aggregation/precipitation. The carbohydrate coating also provides steric protection against proteolytic degradation.
- **Disulfide bonds:** These covalent cross-links between cysteine residues stabilize the tertiary and quaternary structure of the hormone (linking α and β subunits). The strong covalent nature of disulfide bonds resists denaturation in the extracellular environment, maintaining the hormone's biologically active conformation during transport.
