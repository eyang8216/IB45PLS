**Q12.** **(HL)** **Answer: C**

Dideoxynucleotides (ddNTPs) are chain-terminating nucleotides. These fragments are separated by capillary electrophoresis to determine the sequence. Option D describes pyrosequencing or Illumina sequencing by synthesis, not Sanger sequencing.

**Q16.** **DNA Replication & PCR — Mark Scheme**

**(a)** Explain the function of **two** PCR components. [4 marks]

Award **2 marks per component** (1 mark for basic identification, 1 mark for explanation).

- **Taq DNA polymerase**: A thermostable DNA polymerase isolated from *Thermus aquaticus* ✓ that synthesizes new DNA strands by adding dNTPs to the 3′-OH end of primers; it survives the 95 °C denaturation step without denaturing itself, eliminating the need to add fresh enzyme after each cycle ✓.
- **Primers (forward and reverse)**: Short single-stranded oligonucleotides (~18–25 nucleotides) ✓ that are complementary to the sequences flanking the target region; they provide free 3′-OH groups for Taq polymerase to extend and define the boundaries of the DNA fragment to be amplified ✓.
- **dNTPs (dATP, dTTP, dCTP, dGTP)**: Free deoxynucleoside triphosphates that serve as the building blocks ✓ for the new DNA strand; Taq polymerase incorporates them according to complementary base pairing with the template strand ✓.
- **Buffer with Mg²⁺**: Mg²⁺ ions act as essential cofactors for Taq polymerase ✓; they affect enzyme activity, processivity, and fidelity; insufficient Mg²⁺ reduces yield, while excess Mg²⁺ increases non-specific amplification ✓.
- **Template DNA**: The double-stranded DNA containing the target sequence to be amplified ✓; it provides the sequence information that determines which dNTPs are incorporated at each position ✓.

**(b)** Predict outcome of 72 °C-only protocol. [3 marks]

- **Prediction**: No amplified product (no band on gel) OR only primer-dimers visible ✓.
- **Justification 1**: At 72 °C, double-stranded template DNA does **not denature** (95 °C is required to break hydrogen bonds between complementary strands) ✓, so primers cannot access their binding sites on separated strands.
- **Justification 2**: Even if some primers accidentally find partially single-stranded regions, the annealing temperature of primers is typically ~55 °C; at 72 °C, primers cannot stably anneal to template ✓. Without denaturation and annealing, exponential amplification cannot occur.

Award partial credit for recognizing that Taq polymerase is active at 72 °C (extension temperature) but cannot overcome the lack of denaturation and annealing.

**(c)** Explain the ~900 bp additional band. [3 marks]

- The ~900 bp band is approximately **twice** the expected product size → suggests a **dimer/concatemer** of the 450 bp amplicon ✓.
- **Formation mechanism**: In later PCR cycles, when primers become depleted or at low concentration, complementary single-stranded 450 bp products from previous cycles can **anneal to each other** at their overlapping terminal sequences ✓. Taq polymerase then extends these partially annealed products, creating a 900 bp double-stranded product that serves as template in subsequent cycles ✓.
- This is more common in positive controls because the higher initial template concentration produces more product more quickly, exhausting primers earlier; alternatively, it can indicate that the primers have complementary sequences at their 5′ ends facilitating concatemerization.

**Q17.** **(HL)** **Epigenetics & Gene Expression — Mark Scheme**

**(a)** DNA methylation and discordant diabetes. [4 marks]

- DNA methylation involves the addition of methyl (–CH₃) groups to cytosine bases, typically at **CpG dinucleotides** (CpG islands) in promoter regions ✓.
- Hypermethylation of the *INSR* (insulin receptor) promoter in the diabetic twin → methyl groups protrude into the major groove of DNA, **blocking transcription factor binding** OR recruiting methyl-CpG-binding domain (MBD) proteins that promote chromatin condensation ✓.
- This results in **transcriptional silencing** of the *INSR* gene → fewer insulin receptor proteins produced on target cell membranes ✓.
- Consequence: despite normal or elevated insulin levels, target cells (muscle, adipose, liver) are less responsive → **insulin resistance** → impaired glucose uptake → hyperglycemia → type 2 diabetes ✓.
- The healthy twin has normal (unmethylated) *INSR* promoter → normal transcription factor access → normal insulin receptor expression → normal glucose homeostasis ✓.
- Link to twin discordance: epigenetic marks can diverge over time due to **differential environmental exposures** (diet, stress, smoking) despite identical DNA sequences ✓.

Award up to 4 marks for any combination of the above points.

**(b)** Histone acetylation and cancer risk. [3 marks]

- Histone acetylation: acetyl groups added to **lysine residues** on histone tails by histone acetyltransferases (HATs); this neutralizes the positive charge on lysine ✓ → reduced electrostatic attraction between histones and negatively charged DNA → **relaxed chromatin** (euchromatin) → genes accessible for transcription ✓.
- Chemicals in cigarette smoke (e.g., carcinogens in tar) can inhibit HATs or activate histone deacetylases (HDACs), leading to **histone deacetylation** at tumor suppressor gene loci (e.g., *TP53*, *BRCA1*, *CDKN2A*) ✓.
- Deacetylated histones → chromatin condensation → **transcriptional silencing of tumor suppressor genes** ✓.
- Loss of tumor suppressor protein function → cells with DNA damage bypass cell cycle checkpoints and evade apoptosis → accumulation of mutations → **increased cancer risk** ✓.

Award up to 3 marks.

**(c)** Alternative splicing of *PPARG*. [3 marks]

- Alternative splicing: during pre-mRNA processing, different combinations of exons are joined together while introns and **some exons are excised** ✓. This is mediated by spliceosomes and regulated by splicing enhancers and silencers that respond to cell-type-specific proteins ✓.
- The *PPARG* gene can produce multiple mRNA variants (e.g., PPARγ1, PPARγ2) that differ in their 5′ exons, leading to protein isoforms with **different N-terminal domains** ✓.
- Functional consequence: PPARγ2 has an additional 30 amino acids at its N-terminus compared to PPARγ1, which affects its **ligand-independent activation domain** → PPARγ2 is more active in promoting **adipocyte differentiation** and is predominantly expressed in adipose tissue, while PPARγ1 is expressed more broadly ✓. Different isoform ratios in the twins could lead to different adipokine secretion profiles, affecting systemic insulin sensitivity and explaining discordant metabolic phenotypes ✓.

**Q18.** **CRISPR-Cas9 & Ethics — Mark Scheme**

**(a)** CRISPR-Cas9 mechanism. [3 marks]

- A **guide RNA (gRNA)** is designed with ~20 nucleotides complementary to the target DNA sequence ✓.
- The gRNA forms a complex with the **Cas9 endonuclease**; this complex scans the genome for a **protospacer adjacent motif (PAM)** — typically 5′-NGG-3′ for *Streptococcus pyogenes* Cas9 ✓.
- When the gRNA base-pairs with the complementary DNA sequence adjacent to a PAM, Cas9 undergoes a conformational change and cleaves both DNA strands **3–4 nucleotides upstream of the PAM**, generating a **double-strand break (DSB)** with blunt ends ✓.
- The cell repairs the DSB by non-homologous end joining (NHEJ, error-prone, can cause indels) or homology-directed repair (HDR, if a repair template is provided) ✓.

Award up to 3 marks.

**(b)** Off-target edits despite specific gRNA. [2 marks]

- The 20-nucleotide gRNA may have **partial complementarity** (e.g., 15–17 out of 20 bases) to other genomic loci; Cas9 tolerates a limited number of mismatches, especially at positions distal (far) from the PAM ✓.
- The PAM sequence (NGG) is very common in the genome, occurring approximately every **8 base pairs** in the human genome → many off-target sites have a suitable PAM adjacent to a partially complementary sequence ✓.

**(c)** Ethical evaluation of human embryo editing. [5 marks]

Arguments **against** (award up to 3 marks):

- **Germline modification**: edits to embryos are **heritable** — they will be passed to all future generations. The long-term consequences on the human gene pool are unknown and impossible to predict ✓.
- **Lack of consent**: the embryo cannot consent to genetic modification; future generations inherit changes they did not choose ✓.
- **Off-target mutations**: the data show 3–7 off-target edits per embryo. These could disrupt tumor suppressor genes or essential genes, causing cancer or developmental abnormalities in the child, or creating new disease alleles for future generations ✓.
- **Slippery slope to enhancement**: once germline editing for disease is accepted, it becomes difficult to draw a line between therapy (correcting *CFTR*) and enhancement (selecting for height, intelligence, eye color) → exacerbates social inequality ✓.
- **Alternative options exist**: preimplantation genetic diagnosis (PGD) can already select embryos without the *CFTR* mutation without any genetic modification ✓.

Arguments **for** (award up to 2 marks):

- **Prevention of severe suffering**: CF is a devastating disease causing chronic lung infections, pancreatic insufficiency, and reduced life expectancy (~40 years). Correcting the mutation eliminates a lifetime of suffering ✓.
- **Reproductive autonomy**: parents who are both carriers and for whom PGD is not feasible or acceptable (e.g., religious objections to embryo selection) should have the option to have a healthy biological child ✓.
- **Somatic vs. germline distinction**: somatic editing (editing lung epithelial cells in an existing CF patient) is **not heritable** and is ethically less contentious; several clinical trials for somatic CRISPR therapies are already underway (e.g., sickle cell disease) ✓.

**Conclusion**: Most scientific and bioethics bodies (WHO, Nuffield Council) currently consider germline editing for reproductive purposes premature and ethically unacceptable due to unknown risks and lack of societal consensus, while supporting continued research and somatic applications ✓.

Award 5 marks for a balanced evaluation with multiple points on both sides.

**(d)** Cohesion–tension under saline conditions. [3 marks]

- **Cohesion**: water molecules form hydrogen bonds with each other → creates a continuous, unbroken column of water from roots to leaves within xylem vessels ✓. This allows tension generated at the leaves to be transmitted all the way down to the roots.
- **Adhesion**: water molecules adhere to the hydrophilic **lignin-lined walls** of xylem vessels, helping to counteract gravity and maintain the water column ✓. The narrow diameter of xylem tracheids and vessels also contributes via capillarity.
- **Tension**: transpiration from mesophyll cell walls in leaves generates **negative pressure (tension)** at the air–water interfaces in the leaf; this tension pulls the entire water column upward ✓.
- **Under saline conditions**: the more negative soil water potential means the water potential gradient between soil and root is **reduced** → less water uptake. However, the cohesion–tension mechanism can still function as long as the leaf water potential is **more negative** than the soil water potential. The plant may adapt by accumulating compatible solutes (e.g., proline) in root cells to lower ψₛ further, maintaining the gradient ✓.
- Lignified xylem walls are strong and **resist collapse** under the significant negative pressures (as low as −2 MPa) generated by transpiration ✓.

Award up to 3 marks for explanation of the mechanism with reference to water properties and xylem structure.

**Q20.** **(HL)** **Flowering & Photoperiodism — Mark Scheme**

**(a)** Control by night length. [3 marks]

- Evidence that **night length**, not day length, controls flowering:
  - Regime 1 vs. Regime 3: both have **10 hours of light**, but SDP flowers in regime 1 (uninterrupted 14h dark) and does **not** flower in regime 3 (14h dark interrupted by 15 min red light) ✓. If day length controlled flowering, both regimes should produce the same result.
  - Similarly, LDP does not flower in regime 1 (long night) but **does** flower in regime 3 (long night broken by red light) even though day length is the same (10h) ✓.
- Conclusion: SDPs flower when the **uninterrupted dark period exceeds a critical length** (long-night plants); LDPs flower when the uninterrupted dark period is **shorter than a critical length** (short-night plants) ✓. The night break with red light effectively makes a long night appear "short" to the plant.

**(b)** Phytochrome mechanism. [4 marks]

- Phytochrome exists in two interconvertible forms: **Pᵣ** (red-light-absorbing, inactive form, absorbs at ~660 nm) and **P_fr** (far-red-light-absorbing, active form, absorbs at ~730 nm) ✓.
- **Red light (R)** converts Pᵣ → P_fr; **far-red light (FR)** converts P_fr → Pᵣ. In darkness, P_fr **slowly reverts** to Pᵣ over several hours (dark reversion) ✓.
- **Regime 3 interpretation (SDP)**: The red light night break rapidly converts Pᵣ → P_fr → high P_fr levels during what would otherwise be a long dark period. SDPs require **low P_fr** (achieved by long uninterrupted darkness allowing P_fr → Pᵣ dark reversion) to flower. High P_fr from the night break **inhibits flowering** in SDPs ✓.
- **Regime 4 interpretation**: FR light after R converts P_fr back to Pᵣ → low P_fr restored → SDP perceives it as an uninterrupted long night → **flowers**. Conversely, LDP requires high P_fr → FR reduces P_fr → **no flowering** ✓.
- This R/FR reversibility is the classic phytochrome experiment demonstrated by Borthwick and Hendricks.

**(c)** Adaptive significance for SDP at 45°N. [3 marks]

- At 45°N latitude, days shorten in late summer/autumn (nights lengthen beyond ~12 h). An SDP will flower when nights exceed the critical length, ensuring flowering occurs in **late summer or early autumn** ✓.
- Adaptive value: ensures that seeds develop and are dispersed **before winter frost** kills the plant; seedlings emerge in favorable spring conditions ✓.
- Synchronizes flowering within the population → maximizes **cross-pollination** success; ensures pollinator availability (many insect pollinators are also seasonal) ✓.
- Avoids flowering in midsummer when competition for pollinators is highest or when drought stress is maximal ✓.
- If the plant migrated from lower latitudes, the photoperiodic response may be calibrated to its **original latitude**, ensuring it flowers at the appropriate calendar time at the new latitude ✓.

Award up to 3 marks.

**Q22.** **(HL)** **Recombination Frequency & Gene Mapping — Mark Scheme**

**(a)** Parental genotypes. [1 mark]

The two most abundant genotypes are the parental (non-recombinant) chromosomes:

**X Y Z** (1102) and **x y z** (1118) ✓

**(b)** Determine gene order. [3 marks]

- Identify **double-recombinant (DCO)** genotypes: the **least abundant** classes. These are **x Y Z** (8) and **X y z** (12) ✓.
- Compare DCO with parental genotypes:

  | Parental | DCO     |
  |----------|---------|
  | X Y Z    | x Y Z   |
  | x y z    | X y z   |

- In a double crossover, the **middle gene's allele** is the one that is "swapped" relative to the two flanking markers (which remain in their parental combination) ✓.
- Comparing **X Y Z** (parental) with **x Y Z** (DCO): genes Y and Z remain the same (both dominant), but **X changes from dominant to recessive**. Therefore, **X is the middle gene** ✓.
- Gene order: **Y — X — Z** (or equivalently **Z — X — Y**) ✓.

**(c)** Recombination frequencies and genetic map. [4 marks]

**RF (Y–X)**: Single crossovers between Y and X + DCOs.

SC in region I (recombinant for Y–X, parental for X–Z):
- x Y z (142) and X y Z (138)
- Plus DCO: x Y Z (8) and X y z (12)

Total recombinants Y–X = 142 + 138 + 8 + 12 = **300** ✓

RF (Y–X) = 300 / 3200 × 100 = **9.375%** ≈ **9.4 cM** ✓

**RF (X–Z)**: Single crossovers between X and Z + DCOs.

SC in region II (recombinant for X–Z, parental for Y–X):
- x y Z (338) and X Y z (342)
- Plus DCO: x Y Z (8) and X y z (12)

Total recombinants X–Z = 338 + 342 + 8 + 12 = **700** ✓

RF (X–Z) = 700 / 3200 × 100 = **21.875%** ≈ **21.9 cM** ✓

**Genetic map**:

```
Y ———— 9.4 cM ———— X ———————— 21.9 cM ———————— Z
```

Award marks for correct identification of recombinants in each interval and correct calculations.

**(d)** Coefficient of coincidence and interference. [2 marks]

Expected frequency of double crossovers:
= RF(Y–X) × RF(X–Z) = 0.09375 × 0.21875 = **0.02051** ✓

Expected number of DCOs = 0.02051 × 3200 = **65.6**

Observed DCOs = 8 + 12 = **20**

**Coefficient of coincidence (C)** = Observed / Expected = 20 / 65.6 = **0.305** ✓

**Interference (I)** = 1 − C = 1 − 0.305 = **0.695** (or 69.5%) ✓

**Interpretation**: Interference of 0.695 means that **~70% of expected double crossovers did not occur**. A crossover in one region physically interferes with (reduces the probability of) a crossover in the adjacent region. This is typical for genes that are relatively close together (<~30 cM apart) and reflects the mechanism of chiasma formation during prophase I of meiosis ✓.

**Q23.** **(HL)** **Hardy–Weinberg Equilibrium — Mark Scheme**

**(a)** Frequency of recessive allele *c*. [2 marks]

Pale moths = homozygous recessive (*cc*) = 108

Frequency of *cc* genotype = *q*² = 108 / 1200 = **0.09** ✓

*q* = √0.09 = **0.30** ✓

Frequency of allele *c* = 0.30 (30%)

**(b)** Frequency of dominant allele *C*. [1 mark]

*p* = 1 − *q* = 1 − 0.30 = **0.70** ✓

**(c)** Expected numbers under Hardy–Weinberg equilibrium. [3 marks]

|----------|-----------|-------------------|-----------------|
| *CC*     | *p*²      | (0.70)² = 0.49    | 0.49 × 1200 = **588** ✓ |

**(d)** Expected percentage of melanic moths in 2010. [2 marks]

2010: *q* = 0.60, so *p* = 1 − 0.60 = **0.40** ✓

Melanic moths = *CC* + *Cc* = *p*² + 2*pq*
= (0.40)² + 2(0.40)(0.60)
= 0.16 + 0.48

**(e)** Explanation of allele frequency shift. [2 marks]

- Between 1970 and 2010, the frequency of the *c* (typica/pale) allele increased from 0.30 to 0.60, while the *C* (carbonaria/melanic) allele decreased from 0.70 to 0.40 ✓.
- This represents **directional selection** where the selective advantage **reversed** due to environmental change:
  - **1970 (polluted)**: tree bark darkened by soot → melanic moths camouflaged from bird predators → *C* allele favored by directional selection → high *p* ✓.
  - **2010 (clean air)**: clean-air legislation reduced soot; lichens recolonized tree bark → pale moths now camouflaged → *c* allele favored → directional selection in the opposite direction → *q* increased ✓.
- This is a classic example of natural selection in action (Kettlewell's peppered moth studies), showing that the direction of selection depends on environmental conditions.

**Q24.** **Succession & Intermediate Disturbance — Mark Scheme**

**(a)** Primary vs. secondary succession. [2 marks]

- **Primary succession**: colonization of a **previously unvegetated, sterile substrate** lacking soil (e.g., bare rock after volcanic eruption, glacial retreat, newly formed sand dunes). Pioneer species (lichens, mosses) must create soil through weathering and organic matter accumulation ✓.
- **Secondary succession**: recolonization of a disturbed area where **soil remains intact** (e.g., after fire, logging, hurricane). The seed bank and soil organisms speed up recovery ✓.
- The island post-eruption is undergoing **primary succession** because the eruption "sterilizes the soil" — all organic matter and soil biota are destroyed ✓.

**(b)** Trend and explanation. [3 marks]

The graph would show a roughly **sigmoidal (S-shaped) curve**:

Species richness rises slowly from 0 to ~5 (years 0–50), then **steeply increases** from 5 to 24 (years 50–150), then **levels off or slightly declines** (24 → 22, years 150–200) ✓.

**Explanation**:
- **Early stage (0–50 years)**: only hardy pioneer species (lichens, mosses, small herbs) can tolerate the harsh, nutrient-poor conditions. Species richness is low. Soil formation is slow ✓.
- **Middle stage (50–150 years)**: soil depth and nutrient content increase; shrubs and fast-growing trees colonize; more niches available → rapid increase in species richness. Facilitation (early species modify the environment, making it suitable for later species) drives succession ✓.
- **Late stage (150–200 years)**: the community approaches a **climax state**; competitive exclusion by dominant tree species reduces species richness slightly. The slight decline from 24 to 22 suggests a mature forest where a few competitive species outcompete others for light and nutrients ✓.

**(c)** Intermediate disturbance hypothesis. [3 marks]

- The **intermediate disturbance hypothesis (IDH)** states that species richness is maximized at **moderate levels of disturbance** — not too rare (where competitive exclusion dominates) and not too frequent (where only disturbance-tolerant species survive) ✓.
- The hurricane at year 100 is a **moderate-intensity disturbance**: it removes dominant tree species (reducing competitive exclusion) but leaves soil intact ✓.
- **Prediction**: Species richness should **increase above 24** in the following 50 years (year 100–150) because:
  - Gaps created by tree removal allow **colonization by early- and mid-successional species** that were previously excluded by dominant competitors ✓.
  - The remaining patches of mature forest provide refugia for late-successional species → **patch dynamics** create a mosaic of habitats at different successional stages → higher overall diversity ✓.
- After year 150, as the forest canopy closes again and competitive exclusion resumes, richness may decline toward the pre-disturbance level ✓.

**(d)** Tipping point, resilience, resistance. [2 marks]

- **Tipping point**: a critical threshold beyond which an ecosystem undergoes an **abrupt, often irreversible shift** to an alternative stable state. In coral reefs: combined thermal stress (bleaching) + ocean acidification (reduced calcification) → mass coral mortality → **phase shift from coral-dominated to macroalgae-dominated** state ✓. Once macroalgae dominate, they prevent coral larval settlement (feedback loop), making recovery to the coral state extremely difficult even if conditions improve.
- **Resistance**: the ability of an ecosystem to **withstand disturbance without changing** structure or function. A resistant coral reef would not bleach despite elevated temperatures (e.g., heat-tolerant *Symbiodinium* strains).
- **Resilience**: the **speed of recovery** after a disturbance. A resilient coral reef bleaches but rapidly recolonizes with corals once temperatures return to normal ✓.

**Q25.** **Climate Change — Keeling Curve & Evidence — Mark Scheme**

**(a)** Biological cause of seasonal CO₂ oscillation. [3 marks]

- Atmospheric CO₂ shows an annual **sawtooth pattern** with an amplitude of ~6 ppm superimposed on the long-term rising trend ✓.
- The oscillation is driven primarily by **photosynthesis and respiration in terrestrial ecosystems of the Northern Hemisphere** ✓, which has significantly more land mass and vegetation than the Southern Hemisphere.
- **Northern Hemisphere spring/summer (May–September)**: photosynthesis exceeds respiration → **net CO₂ uptake** by vegetation → atmospheric CO₂ declines (the downward leg of each sawtooth) ✓.
- **Northern Hemisphere autumn/winter (October–April)**: leaves fall, photosynthesis declines, respiration and decomposition continue → **net CO₂ release** → atmospheric CO₂ rises (the upward leg) ✓.
- This creates the annual minimum in ~September/October and the annual maximum in ~May of each year ✓.

**(b)** Rate of CO₂ increase vs. glacial–interglacial fluctuations. [3 marks]

- Glacial–interglacial CO₂ changes (180 → 280 ppm) occurred over **~50,000–100,000 years** → rate of change was approximately **0.001–0.002 ppm year⁻¹** ✓.
- The current rate of increase is approximately **2–3 ppm year⁻¹** → **over 1,000× faster** than natural glacial–interglacial transitions ✓.
- **Implications for ecosystems**:
  - Organisms and ecosystems had **millennia to adapt, migrate, or evolve** during glacial–interglacial cycles. Current rapid change outpaces the adaptive capacity of most species → range shifts cannot keep pace ✓.
  - The current CO₂ level (414 ppm) **far exceeds** anything experienced in at least 800,000 years (and likely 3–5 million years) → many organisms face CO₂ conditions for which they have no evolutionary precedent ✓.
  - Rapid climate velocity means sessile organisms (corals, trees) and species with long generation times are particularly vulnerable ✓.
  - The simultaneous rate of warming (~0.2 °C per decade) compounds the stress from direct CO₂ effects (acidification) ✓.

**(c)** Dual impact of rising CO₂ on coral reefs. [4 marks]

**1. Ocean warming → coral bleaching** (award up to 2 marks):

- Elevated atmospheric CO₂ enhances the greenhouse effect → **rising sea surface temperatures (SSTs)** ✓.
- Corals have a **symbiotic relationship with zooxanthellae** (dinoflagellate algae of the genus *Symbiodinium*) that live within coral tissues and provide up to 90% of the coral's energy through photosynthesis ✓.
- When SST exceeds the coral's thermal tolerance threshold (typically 1–2 °C above the summer maximum for several weeks), the zooxanthellae produce **reactive oxygen species (ROS)** that damage coral cells → the coral **expels the zooxanthellae** → **bleaching** (the white calcium carbonate skeleton becomes visible through the transparent tissue) ✓.
- Without zooxanthellae, the coral loses its primary energy source → if temperatures do not return to normal within weeks, the coral starves and dies → loss of **reef structure and habitat** for ~25% of marine biodiversity ✓.

**2. Ocean acidification → reduced calcification** (award up to 2 marks):

- ~30% of anthropogenic CO₂ is absorbed by the oceans: CO₂ + H₂O ⇌ H₂CO₃ ⇌ **H⁺ + HCO₃⁻** ✓.
- The excess H⁺ reacts with carbonate ions: **H⁺ + CO₃²⁻ ⇌ HCO₃⁻**, reducing the **carbonate ion concentration [CO₃²⁻]** in seawater ✓.
- Corals need CO₃²⁻ to combine with Ca²⁺ to precipitate their **aragonite (CaCO₃) skeletons**. The saturation state (Ω) of aragonite decreases as [CO₃²⁻] falls; when Ω < 1, CaCO₃ dissolves rather than precipitates ✓.
- Reduced calcification rates → **slower growth, weaker skeletons** → corals are more susceptible to bioerosion and physical damage from storms; reefs cannot accrete fast enough to keep pace with **sea-level rise** ✓.

**Synergistic effect**: Even corals that survive bleaching events grow more slowly due to acidification → reduced reef resilience → longer recovery times between bleaching events → increased likelihood of ecosystem collapse as bleaching frequency increases with climate change ✓.
