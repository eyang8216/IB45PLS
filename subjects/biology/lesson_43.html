What You'll Learn

Every cell in your body—except your gametes—was created by mitosis, a process so fundamental that it's happening in your body right now, producing millions of new cells every second. In this lesson, you'll learn how a single cell duplicates its entire contents and splits into two genetically identical daughter cells, the regulation that ensures this process doesn't go wrong, and what happens when that regulation fails.

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to:

  • Describe each phase of the cell cycle, including what happens in interphase (G₁, S, G₂) and M phase
  • Draw and label each stage of mitosis: prophase, metaphase, anaphase, telophase
  • Compare cytokinesis in animal and plant cells
  • Explain how checkpoints, cyclins, and CDKs control the cell cycle
  • Distinguish between oncogenes and tumor suppressor genes
  • Link failures in cell cycle regulation to cancer development and treatment

1. The Cell Cycle: A Cellular Clock

1.1 The Big Picture

Imagine a photocopier that not only copies a document but builds a second, identical photocopier around that copy. That's essentially what the cell cycle accomplishes. It divides the life of a cell into distinct phases, each with a specific job, all leading to the production of two daughter cells from one parent cell.

The cell cycle has two broad phases:

Phase Duration (typical) What Happens
Interphase ~23 hours Cell grows, replicates DNA, prepares for division
M Phase** (mitosis + cytokinesis) ~1 hour Nucleus divides, then cytoplasm divides

Key insight for IB: The ~1 hour that mitosis takes is dwarfed by the ~23 hours of interphase. Most of a cell's "life" is spent not visibly dividing. When you look at a typical tissue section under the microscope, you'll see very few cells in M phase.

1.2 Interphase: Getting Ready to Divide

Interphase is divided into three sub-phases. Think of them as three stages of preparing for a marathon:

#### G₁ Phase (Gap 1): "The Growth Phase"

After the previous division, the cell is small. G₁ is when the cell:

  • Grows in size: Increases its volume by synthesizing cytoplasm, organelles, and membranes
  • Synthesizes proteins: Produces enzymes, structural proteins, and the proteins needed for DNA replication
  • Carries out its normal function: A liver cell does liver things; a neuron does neuron things
  • Monitors conditions: The cell "asks" whether conditions are right for division

Draw and label: Sketch a small circle labeled "G₁ cell." Draw arrows pointing inward showing: amino acids → protein synthesis; glucose → respiration → ATP. Label: "Cell growth, protein synthesis, normal metabolic activity."

#### S Phase (Synthesis): "DNA Replication"

This is the critical moment. Every chromosome must be copied once and only once:

  • Each of the 46 chromosomes (in humans) is replicated
  • The result: each chromosome now consists of two identical sister chromatids
  • Sister chromatids are held together at a region called the centromere
  • The cell now has twice the normal amount of DNA (4C instead of 2C, where C = amount of DNA in a haploid set)

Sister chromatids: Two identical copies of a chromosome, formed by DNA replication during S phase, held together at the centromere. Each will become a separate chromosome after separation in anaphase.

Draw and label: Draw a chromosome before S phase as a single line. Below it, draw the same chromosome after S phase: two parallel lines (sister chromatids) joined by a dot labeled "centromere." Label: "Before S phase: 1 chromosome, 1 DNA molecule. After S phase: 1 chromosome, 2 sister chromatids (2 DNA molecules)."

Critical IB distinction: Before anaphase, the two sister chromatids count as ONE chromosome with TWO chromatids. After anaphase separation, each chromatid becomes an independent chromosome. So 1 chromosome → 2 chromosomes after separation, but the DNA content doesn't change.

#### G₂ Phase (Gap 2): "Final Preparations"

The cell now has duplicated DNA. G₂ is about:

  • Continued growth and protein synthesis
  • Centrosome duplication completes (in animal cells, centrosomes organize spindle fibers)
  • Synthesis of microtubules needed for the spindle apparatus
  • Final check: Is all DNA replicated? Is there any damage?

Draw and label: Sketch a cell in G₂. Draw duplicated centrosomes near the nucleus, and label them: "Two centrosomes — will become spindle poles."

1.3 M Phase: The Main Event (~1 Hour)

M phase includes mitosis (nuclear division) and cytokinesis (cytoplasmic division). Mitosis itself is divided into four stages: prophase, metaphase, anaphase, and telophase (PMAT — a useful mnemonic).

2. The Stages of Mitosis

2.1 Prophase: "Setting the Stage"

Prophase is the longest phase of mitosis. During prophase:

  1. Chromatin condenses: The diffuse, thread-like chromatin coils and supercoils, becoming visible as distinct chromosomes. Each chromosome is now visible as two sister chromatids held at the centromere.
  1. Nuclear membrane breaks down: The nuclear envelope fragments into small vesicles, dispersing into the cytoplasm. This allows spindle fibers to access the chromosomes.
  1. Nucleolus disappears: The nucleolus, site of ribosome synthesis, disassembles.
  1. Spindle formation: The two centrosomes (each with a pair of centrioles in animal cells) move to opposite poles of the cell. Microtubules radiate from them, forming the mitotic spindle.

Draw and label:

  • Draw the cell as a circle
  • At opposite sides, draw two centrosomes with microtubules extending toward the center
  • In the center, draw condensed chromosomes (X-shaped, showing two sister chromatids)
  • Label: "Condensed chromosomes," "Nuclear membrane breaking down," "Spindle fibers extending from centrosomes at poles," "Centrosomes"

IB Exam Tip (Paper 1): You may be shown electron micrographs of cells in different stages. In prophase, look for: condensed chromosomes visible as dark bodies, fragments of nuclear membrane, and the beginning of spindle formation. The key identifier is that chromosomes are condensed but NOT yet aligned in a single line.

2.2 Metaphase: "Alignment at the Equator"

Metaphase is when chromosomes are most visible and most easily counted:

  1. Chromosomes align: Each chromosome (still as two sister chromatids) moves to the metaphase plate — the "equator" of the cell, halfway between the two poles.
  1. Spindle fiber attachment: Microtubules from each pole attach to kinetochores — protein structures assembled on the centromere of each sister chromatid. Each sister chromatid faces one pole.
  1. Tension is established: The spindle fibers from opposite poles pull the chromosomes with equal force, holding them at the metaphase plate.

Draw and label:

  • Draw the cell as a circle with spindle fibers extending from poles
  • Along the equator, draw chromosomes as X-shapes lined up in a row
  • Label each chromosome's centromere/kinetochore and show spindle fibers attached
  • Label: "Metaphase plate (equator)," "Kinetochore," "Spindle fibers (microtubules)"

Kinetochore: A protein complex assembled on the centromere of each chromatid. It is the attachment point for spindle microtubules. Each sister chromatid has its own kinetochore facing opposite poles.

IB Exam Tip (Paper 2): When asked to identify metaphase in a diagram: look for chromosomes aligned in a single file across the middle. This is the most diagnostic feature. Metaphase is also when karyotypes are made because chromosomes are maximally condensed and visible.

2.3 Anaphase: "The Great Separation"

Anaphase is the shortest but most dramatic phase:

  1. Centromeres split: The cohesin proteins holding sister chromatids together are cleaved by the enzyme separase.
  1. Sister chromatids separate: Once the centromere splits, each sister chromatid becomes an independent chromosome. They are pulled toward opposite poles.
  1. Mechanism of movement: The spindle microtubules shorten at the kinetochore end (depolymerization), pulling chromosomes poleward. Simultaneously, the spindle poles move further apart (polar microtubules lengthen and slide).
  1. V-shape appearance: As chromosomes are pulled centromere-first, they take on a characteristic V-shape, with the centromere leading and chromosome arms trailing.

Draw and label:

  • Draw chromosomes at two distinct groups, each group moving toward one pole
  • Show chromosomes as V-shaped, with centromeres pointing toward poles
  • Label: "Sister chromatids now = individual chromosomes," "Shortening microtubules pull chromosomes," "Poles moving apart"

2.4 Telophase: "Return to Normal"

Telophase is essentially prophase in reverse:

  1. Nuclear membranes reform: Vesicles from the old nuclear envelope reassemble around each group of chromosomes, forming two new nuclei.
  1. Chromosomes decondense: The tightly coiled chromosomes unwind back into diffuse chromatin.
  1. Nucleoli reappear: Ribosome synthesis resumes.
  1. Spindle disassembles: Microtubules depolymerize.

Draw and label:

  • Draw a dumbbell-shaped cell (beginning to pinch)
  • At each end, draw a nucleus with chromatin inside
  • Label: "Nuclear membrane reforming," "Chromosomes decondensing," "Cleavage furrow beginning"

3. Cytokinesis: Splitting the Cytoplasm

Cytokinesis begins during late telophase and divides the cytoplasm, resulting in two separate daughter cells. The mechanism differs significantly between animal and plant cells.

3.1 Animal Cells: Cleavage Furrow

In animal cells, cytokinesis occurs through a cleavage furrow:

  1. A ring of actin and myosin microfilaments forms just beneath the plasma membrane at the equator.
  1. The actin-myosin ring contracts (similar to muscle contraction), pulling the membrane inward like a drawstring.
  1. The furrow deepens until the cell is pinched into two.

Analogy: Imagine pulling the drawstring of a bag closed — the plasma membrane is pinched inward until the two sides meet and separate.

Draw and label: Draw a series of three cells: one with slight indentation, one deeply pinched, and one nearly separated. Label: "Actin-myosin contractile ring," "Cleavage furrow."

3.2 Plant Cells: Cell Plate Formation

Plant cells cannot use a cleavage furrow because of their rigid cell walls. Instead:

  1. Golgi vesicles containing cell wall materials (pectin, cellulose precursors) accumulate at the equator of the cell during telophase.
  1. These vesicles fuse to form a cell plate, which grows outward from the center.
  1. The membrane of the vesicles becomes the new plasma membrane for each daughter cell.
  1. The contents of the vesicles form the middle lamella (pectin-rich layer between cells).
  1. Each daughter cell then deposits cellulose to build its own primary cell wall.

Draw and label: Draw a rectangular plant cell with a line forming across the middle. Label: "Golgi vesicles fusing → cell plate," "New cell wall forming," "Middle lamella."

3.3 Comparison Table: Animal vs. Plant Cytokinesis

Feature Animal Cells Plant Cells
Mechanism Cleavage furrow (actin-myosin ring) Cell plate (Golgi vesicle fusion)
Direction Furrow pinches inward from outside Plate grows outward from center
Key molecules Actin, myosin Pectin, cellulose precursors
Reason for difference No cell wall Rigid cell wall prevents pinching

4. Regulation of the Cell Cycle

The cell cycle is tightly controlled. Uncontrolled division is the hallmark of cancer.

4.1 Checkpoints: The Quality Control System

Think of checkpoints as border crossings where a cell's "passport" is checked before it's allowed to proceed to the next phase.

Checkpoint When? What's Checked? Outcome if Failed
G₁ Checkpoint End of G₁ Is cell large enough? Is DNA undamaged? Are nutrients and growth factors present? Cell enters G₀ (quiescent state) or apoptosis
G₂ Checkpoint End of G₂ Has all DNA been replicated? Is replicated DNA undamaged? Cell pauses to repair DNA; if irreparable → apoptosis
M Checkpoint** (Spindle checkpoint) Metaphase→Anaphase Are all chromosomes attached to spindle fibers at kinetochores? Anaphase delayed until all chromosomes properly attached

The G₀ state: If conditions are unfavorable at the G₁ checkpoint, cells can exit the cell cycle into G₀ — a non-dividing state. Some cells (neurons, skeletal muscle) are permanently in G₀. Others can re-enter the cycle given the right signals.

IB Exam Tip (Paper 1 & 2): You should know what happens at each checkpoint. A common question: "What would happen if the G₁ checkpoint failed?" Answer: Damaged DNA would be replicated in S phase, potentially passing mutations to daughter cells → cancer.

4.2 Cyclins and CDKs: The Molecular Oscillators

The cell cycle is driven by cyclin-dependent kinases (CDKs) and their regulatory partners, cyclins.

Analogy: Imagine a security key card system. The CDK is the card reader (always present, but inactive without a card). The cyclin is the key card (made only at specific times). Only when the key card is inserted does the reader activate and open a specific door (trigger a phase transition).

#### Key Principles:

  1. CDK levels are constant throughout the cell cycle — they are always present.
  1. Cyclin levels oscillate — they rise and fall rhythmically:
  • Synthesized just before they're needed
  • Rapidly degraded by proteolysis after they've done their job
  1. Activation: When a cyclin binds its partner CDK, it forms an active cyclin-CDK complex.
  1. Mechanism: The active complex phosphorylates specific target proteins (adds phosphate groups), changing their activity and triggering events like:
  • Chromosome condensation
  • Nuclear envelope breakdown
  • Spindle assembly

#### Different Cyclins for Different Phases:

Cyclin Type CDK Partner Phase Triggered
G₁ cyclins** (cyclin D) CDK4, CDK6 G₁ → S transition (commitment to divide)
G₁/S cyclins** (cyclin E) CDK2 Initiation of DNA replication (S phase entry)
S cyclins** (cyclin A) CDK2 Progression through S phase
Mitotic cyclins** (cyclin B) CDK1 G₂ → M transition (entry into mitosis)

Draw and label: Sketch a graph showing time on the x-axis (G₁ → S → G₂ → M). Plot CDK concentration as a flat horizontal line. Plot cyclin concentration as a curve that rises during each phase and drops sharply at phase transition. Label: "CDK — constant," "Cyclin — oscillates," "Active complex = cyclin + CDK."

Cyclin-dependent kinase (CDK): A protein kinase that is only enzymatically active when bound to its partner cyclin. CDKs phosphorylate target proteins to drive cell cycle progression.

Worked Example: How mitotic cyclin triggers mitosis

  1. During G₂, mitotic cyclin (cyclin B) is synthesized and accumulates.
  2. Cyclin B binds CDK1, forming the M-phase promoting factor (MPF).
  3. MPF phosphorylates:
  • Histone H1 → chromosome condensation
  • Nuclear lamins → nuclear envelope breakdown
  • Proteins regulating microtubule dynamics → spindle assembly
  1. At the end of mitosis, cyclin B is tagged with ubiquitin and destroyed by the proteasome.
  2. CDK1 becomes inactive → cell exits mitosis.

This explains the oscillation: cyclin levels must fall for the cell to exit one phase and prepare for the next.

5. Cancer: When Regulation Fails

Cancer is fundamentally a disease of uncontrolled cell division caused by failures in the regulatory mechanisms described above.

5.1 Tumor Formation

When cells divide uncontrollably, they form a mass of cells called a tumor:

  • Benign tumors: Remain localized, do not invade surrounding tissue, usually not life-threatening (though they can cause problems by pressing on organs).
  • Malignant tumors (cancer): Invade surrounding tissue, can spread to other body parts via the blood or lymphatic system (metastasis).

5.2 Oncogenes and Tumor Suppressor Genes

Cancer arises from mutations in two broad categories of genes:

#### Oncogenes: "The Accelerator Stuck Down"

Proto-oncogenes are normal genes that promote cell division (like the accelerator pedal in a car). A mutation that causes a gain of function converts a proto-oncogene into an oncogene.

  • Mutation effect: The gene product becomes overactive or is produced in excess.
  • Genetic nature: Mutation is dominant — mutation in just ONE copy of the gene is sufficient to promote cancer.
  • Analogy: A car's accelerator pedal getting stuck in the "pressed down" position.

Classic Example — Ras:

Ras is a G-protein (GTPase) that acts as a molecular switch in cell signaling pathways:

  • Normal Ras: Binds GTP → active → transmits growth signal → hydrolyzes GTP to GDP → inactive (signal stops). It's a self-limiting switch.
  • Oncogenic Ras: Mutation prevents GTP hydrolysis → Ras is permanently "on" (GTP-bound) → constantly sends growth signals even in the absence of growth factors.
  • Result: Continuous cell division.

Ras mutations are found in about 25–30% of all human cancers.

#### Tumor Suppressor Genes: "The Brakes Cut"

Tumor suppressor genes normally inhibit cell division or promote apoptosis (like the brake pedal). Loss-of-function mutations in these genes remove this inhibition.

  • Mutation effect: The gene product is inactivated or lost.
  • Genetic nature: Mutation is recessive — BOTH copies of the gene must be inactivated for cancer to develop (the "two-hit hypothesis").
  • Analogy: A car with both brake lines cut.

Classic Example — p53: "The Guardian of the Genome":

p53 is arguably the most important tumor suppressor:

  • Normal p53 function: When DNA damage is detected:
  1. p53 is stabilized and activated
  2. p53 acts as a transcription factor, activating genes that:
  • Arrest the cell cycle (via p21, which inhibits CDKs) — giving time for DNA repair
  • Trigger apoptosis (programmed cell death) if damage is too severe — via Bax and other pro-apoptotic proteins
  • Mutated p53: Cannot perform either function → cells with damaged DNA continue to divide → mutations accumulate → cancer.
  • Clinical significance: p53 is mutated in more than 50% of all human cancers. It is the single most frequently mutated gene in cancer.

The Two-Hit Hypothesis (Knudson's hypothesis): For a tumor suppressor gene to contribute to cancer, both alleles (copies) must be inactivated. The first "hit" may be inherited (familial cancer) or acquired. The second "hit" is typically a somatic mutation. This explains why inherited cancer syndromes (like BRCA1/BRCA2) are dominant in inheritance pattern but recessive at the cellular level — you inherit one mutated copy and only need one more "hit" in any cell.

Feature Proto-oncogene/Oncogene Tumor Suppressor Gene
Normal function Promotes cell division Inhibits cell division / promotes apoptosis
Effect of mutation Gain of function (overactivation) Loss of function (inactivation)
Type of mutation Dominant (one copy sufficient) Recessive (both copies needed)
Analogy Accelerator stuck Brakes cut
Example Ras p53

5.3 Metastasis: When Cancer Spreads

Metastasis is what makes cancer so deadly. The process:

  1. Cancer cells break away from the primary tumor.
  2. They secrete enzymes (matrix metalloproteinases) to digest the basement membrane and extracellular matrix.
  3. They enter the bloodstream or lymphatic system (intravasation).
  4. They survive in circulation and exit at a distant site (extravasation).
  5. They establish a new blood supply (angiogenesis) and form a secondary tumor.

Analogy: Think of dandelion seeds — a single plant produces seeds that blow away in the wind and establish new plants far from the original. Similarly, a primary tumor can "seed" secondary tumors throughout the body.

5.4 Causes of Cancer

Cancer is multifactorial. Causes (carcinogens) can be categorized:

Category Examples Mechanism
Chemical carcinogens Tobacco smoke (benzopyrene), asbestos, aflatoxin (from moldy peanuts) Direct DNA damage, adduct formation
Radiation UV light (sunlight), X-rays, gamma rays, radon gas DNA strand breaks, thymine dimer formation (UV)
Biological agents HPV** (human papillomavirus → cervical cancer), Hepatitis B/C (→ liver cancer), Helicobacter pylori (→ stomach cancer) Viral oncogenes (HPV E6/E7 inactivate p53 and Rb); chronic inflammation
Inherited mutations BRCA1/BRCA2** mutations → breast/ovarian cancer; APC mutation → familial colorectal cancer; Rb mutation → retinoblastoma Inherit one defective copy of tumor suppressor; second copy lost somatically

HPV and Cervical Cancer — A Key IB Example:

  • HPV types 16 and 18 cause ~70% of cervical cancers.
  • The virus produces two oncoproteins: E6 (binds and degrades p53) and E7 (binds and inactivates Rb, another tumor suppressor).
  • This effectively knocks out two key tumor suppressors in one infection.
  • The HPV vaccine prevents infection with high-risk HPV types.

5.5 Approaches to Cancer Treatment

Treatment How It Works Limitations / Side Effects
Surgery Physical removal of the tumor Only effective for localized tumors; can't remove all cancer cells if spread
Chemotherapy Drugs that target rapidly dividing cells (e.g., disrupt DNA replication, mitosis, or microtubule function) Kills ALL rapidly dividing cells — not just cancer cells: hair follicles (→ hair loss), gut epithelial cells (→ nausea, vomiting), bone marrow (→ reduced blood cell production, immunosuppression)
Radiation therapy Focused ionizing radiation to damage DNA of cancer cells in a specific area Damage to surrounding healthy tissue; DNA damage to normal cells
Targeted therapy Drugs that specifically target molecular features of cancer cells Only works for cancers with the specific target; cancer may develop resistance

Targeted Therapy Example — Herceptin (trastuzumab):

  • ~20–25% of breast cancers overexpress HER2 (a cell surface receptor in the EGF receptor family).
  • HER2 overexpression → excessive growth signaling.
  • Herceptin is a monoclonal antibody that binds HER2, blocking its signaling and marking cells for immune destruction.
  • It is only effective in HER2-positive breast cancers (tested before prescribing).

7. Common Misconceptions

Misconception Reality
"Mitosis makes gametes" No — mitosis makes somatic cells (growth/repair). Meiosis makes gametes.
"DNA replication happens in prophase" DNA replicates in S phase of interphase, well before mitosis starts.
"Chromosomes are always visible" Chromosomes are only visible as distinct bodies during mitosis. In interphase, DNA is in the form of chromatin (thin, diffuse).
"In anaphase, centromeres split and chromatids become chromosomes" — the term After separation, each chromatid IS a chromosome. So yes — what were "sister chromatids" become individual "chromosomes."
"Cytokinesis is part of mitosis" Cytokinesis is part of M phase but not part of mitosis. Mitosis = nuclear division. Cytokinesis = cytoplasmic division.
"All cells divide constantly" Many cells enter G₀ and rarely or never divide (neurons, skeletal muscle).
"Cancer is a single disease" Cancer is hundreds of different diseases, each with different molecular causes.
"If you inherit a BRCA1 mutation, you will definitely get cancer" No — it increases risk substantially, but is not a 100% guarantee.

  • No visible nuclear membrane
  • Chromosomes aligned in a single row across the middle of the cell
  • Spindle fibers visible

(a) Explain why CDK levels remain constant throughout the cell cycle while their activity oscillates. [2 marks]

(b) Predict what would happen if mitotic cyclin were not degraded at the end of mitosis. [2 marks]

(a) Distinguish between oncogenes and tumor suppressor genes. [3 marks]

(b) Explain why mutations in p53 are the most common genetic change found across all human cancers. [2 marks]

(c) Explain why inherited mutations in tumor suppressor genes show a dominant inheritance pattern even though the mutation is recessive at the cellular level. [2 marks]

(a) Compare and contrast cytokinesis in animal and plant cells. [4 marks]

9. Answers

Answer 1

(a) Metaphase. [1]

(b) Chromosomes aligned at the equator / metaphase plate in a single row [1]. The absence of a nuclear membrane and the visibility of spindle fibers are consistent with mitosis; the alignment specifically identifies metaphase. [1]

(c) 16 chromosomes. DNA content doubles during S phase (each chromosome gets 2 chromatids), but the chromosome number does not change until anaphase. [1]

Answer 2

(a) CDK proteins are constitutively expressed (always present) [1]. Their activity oscillates because they require cyclin binding to become active, and cyclin levels oscillate — cyclins are synthesized and then rapidly degraded at specific points in the cell cycle. [1]

(b) If mitotic cyclin were not degraded, the cyclin-CDK complex would remain active [1]. The cell would be unable to exit mitosis — chromosomes might stay condensed, nuclear membranes would not reform, and the cell could not proceed to G₁. This could lead to cell cycle arrest or aberrant division. [1]

Answer 3

(a) Oncogenes: mutated proto-oncogenes with gain of function; promote cell division; dominant (one mutated copy sufficient). [1] Tumor suppressor genes: loss of function mutations needed; normally inhibit cell division or promote apoptosis; recessive (both copies must be inactivated). [1] Proto-oncogenes become oncogenes through overactivation; tumor suppressors contribute to cancer when their inhibitory function is lost. [1]

(b) p53 is the "guardian of the genome" — it arrests the cell cycle in response to DNA damage and triggers apoptosis if damage is irreparable. [1] If p53 is inactivated, cells with DNA damage continue to divide, accumulating mutations that lead to cancer. This makes p53 mutation a "gateway" that enables other oncogenic mutations to persist. [1]

(c) At the organismal level, the inheritance pattern appears dominant because inheriting one mutated copy means every cell already has one "hit." [1] Over a lifetime, the probability that any given cell will acquire a second "hit" (somatic mutation in the other allele) is high, so cancer frequently develops. In contrast, someone with two normal alleles would need two rare somatic mutations in the same cell. [1]

Answer 4

(a) Similarities: Both occur after telophase to divide the cytoplasm; both result in two separate daughter cells. [1]

Differences: Animal cells form a cleavage furrow through contraction of an actin-myosin ring, pinching the cell from outside inward. [1] Plant cells form a cell plate from Golgi-derived vesicles that fuse at the equator and grow outward. [1] Plant cytokinesis also involves synthesis of a new cell wall (middle lamella, primary walls). [1]

(b) Plant cells have a rigid cellulose cell wall [1]; a cleavage furrow mechanism cannot physically pinch through the rigid wall.

Answer 5

Without a functional G₁ checkpoint, the cell would enter S phase even if DNA were damaged [1]. Replication of damaged DNA would "fix" the mutations into both daughter strands [1]. These mutations could affect oncogenes or tumor suppressor genes, and subsequent cell divisions would produce a population of cells with accumulating mutations [1]. If mutations inactivate both copies of a tumor suppressor (e.g., p53) or activate an oncogene (e.g., Ras), uncontrolled division ensues → cancer [1].
