# Lesson 57: Atom 1 — Atomic Structure and Rutherford Scattering

## What You'll Learn

Two thousand years ago, Greek philosophers argued that matter was made of tiny, indivisible particles they called "atoms." For most of human history, that was just a guess — there was no experimental evidence. In the late 19th and early 20th centuries, a series of brilliant experiments revealed not just that atoms exist, but what they look like inside. This lesson tells that story.

You'll learn about the experiments that discovered the electron, how we know atoms are mostly empty space, why Ernest Rutherford said discovering the nucleus was "like firing a 15-inch shell at a piece of tissue paper and it came back and hit you," and how scientists figured out the basic structure of every atom in the universe.

---

## 1. What Is an Atom? The Problem Before 1897

### Why This Matters

If you don't know where we started, you can't appreciate the genius of the experiments that followed. Before 1897, atoms were hypothetical. Some scientists didn't even believe they existed. The idea that atoms have internal structure — that they're made of even smaller pieces — was considered radical and, to many, absurd. After all, the word "atom" comes from the Greek *atomos*, meaning "uncuttable."

### The Key Ideas

**The ancient idea.** Democritus (~400 BCE) proposed that if you cut a piece of matter in half, then in half again, and kept going, you'd eventually reach a particle so small it couldn't be divided further. He called it *atomos*. This was pure philosophy — no experiments, no predictions, no mathematics. Aristotle rejected the idea, and for 2000 years, most scholars agreed with Aristotle.

**The chemical atom.** In the early 1800s, John Dalton revived the atomic idea with experimental evidence. He noticed that elements combine in fixed ratios by mass — for example, 1 gram of hydrogen always combines with exactly 8 grams of oxygen to make water. Dalton proposed that each element consists of identical atoms, and that compounds form when atoms of different elements combine in small whole-number ratios. This was a huge advance, but Dalton's atoms were still indivisible spheres — no internal structure.

**The problem.** By the 1890s, evidence was piling up that atoms were NOT indivisible. Cathode ray tubes (the ancestors of old TV picture tubes) produced mysterious "rays" when high voltage was applied across a vacuum. These rays could be deflected by electric and magnetic fields. Whatever they were, they came from inside atoms. The atom had structure.

---

## 2. Discovery of the Electron — J.J. Thomson (1897)

### Why This Matters

The electron was the first subatomic particle discovered. Its discovery proved that atoms are NOT indivisible — they contain smaller pieces. This opened the door to all of atomic and nuclear physics.

### The Key Ideas

**Cathode rays.** In a glass tube with most of the air evacuated, two metal electrodes are connected to a high voltage. The negative electrode (cathode) emits invisible rays that travel to the positive electrode (anode), causing the glass to glow where they hit. These are cathode rays.

**What were they?** Some scientists thought cathode rays were a new kind of electromagnetic wave (like light). Others thought they were streams of particles. The debate was resolved by J.J. Thomson at Cambridge University.

**Thomson's experiment.** Thomson applied both electric and magnetic fields to cathode rays. He observed that:

1. The rays were deflected by both fields — waves wouldn't be deflected by an electric field.
2. The direction of deflection showed the particles carried NEGATIVE charge.
3. By balancing the electric and magnetic forces so the beam went straight, he could measure the velocity of the particles.
4. By measuring the deflection with only the magnetic field, he could find the charge-to-mass ratio (e/m) of the particles.

**The charge-to-mass ratio.** Thomson found e/m ≈ 1.76 × 10¹¹ C/kg. This was enormous — about 1800 times larger than the e/m value for a hydrogen ion (a proton, though protons weren't known yet). This meant either the particle's charge was huge, or its mass was tiny.

**The conclusion.** Either way, these particles were not atoms. They were subatomic — pieces of atoms. Thomson had discovered the **electron**.

**The significance.** If electrons come from atoms, and atoms are electrically neutral, then atoms must also contain positive charge. The "uncuttable" atom had been cut. Something inside held positive charge to balance the negative electrons. But what was it, and how was it arranged?

---

## 3. Thomson's "Plum Pudding" Model (1904)

### Why This Matters

The first model of atomic structure is almost always wrong in science. But a wrong model, if it's specific enough to make predictions that can be tested, drives progress. Thomson's model was wrong — but it was specific enough to be tested, and testing it led to the correct model.

### The Key Ideas

**The model.** Thomson proposed that an atom is a sphere of uniform positive charge (the "pudding"), with negatively charged electrons embedded throughout it (the "plums"). The electrons could vibrate within this positive sphere, and their vibration frequencies would correspond to the frequencies of light emitted by the atom. The total positive charge exactly balanced the total negative charge, making the atom neutral.

**Why it seemed reasonable.** This model explained several things:
- Atoms are electrically neutral (equal positive and negative charge).
- Electrons are much lighter than atoms, so most of the mass must be in the positive part.
- Atoms can emit light — the vibrating electrons would produce electromagnetic radiation.

**What it predicted.** If Thomson's model was correct, then firing a beam of heavy, positively charged particles at a thin metal foil should result in small, gradual deflections. The positive charge is spread out, so no single encounter would produce a large deflection. Each alpha particle would pass through, experiencing many small nudges from the distributed positive charge, and emerge with its direction changed by at most a degree or two.

This prediction was about to be tested in the most famous physics experiment of the early 20th century.

---

## 4. The Rutherford (Geiger-Marsden) Experiment (1909-1911)

### Why This Matters

This experiment changed our picture of matter forever. It showed that the atom is mostly empty space with a tiny, massive, positively charged nucleus at its center. Every model of the atom since — Bohr, quantum mechanical, everything — builds on this result.

### The Key Ideas

**The experimental setup.** Hans Geiger and Ernest Marsden, working under Ernest Rutherford at the University of Manchester, fired a beam of **alpha particles** at an extremely thin gold foil (only about 1000 atoms thick). Alpha particles are helium nuclei — they have a charge of +2e and a mass about 7300 times that of an electron. They are heavy, fast, and positively charged. The experimenters used a zinc sulfide screen that produced a tiny flash of light (a "scintillation") when struck by an alpha particle. By moving this screen around the foil, they could count particles scattered at every angle.

**What Thomson's model predicted.** Almost all alpha particles should pass straight through or be deflected by tiny angles (less than 1°). The distributed positive charge simply couldn't produce a strong enough electric force to turn a heavy alpha particle around.

**What actually happened.** Most alpha particles DID pass straight through — that was consistent with Thomson. But:

1. A small fraction were deflected by large angles (more than 90°).
2. About 1 in 8000 alpha particles BOUNCED BACK — they were scattered through angles greater than 90°, some almost 180°.

Rutherford's famous reaction: "It was quite the most incredible event that has ever happened to me in my life. It was almost as incredible as if you fired a 15-inch shell at a piece of tissue paper and it came back and hit you."

### The Inference: The Nuclear Atom

Rutherford realized that to reverse the direction of a heavy, fast alpha particle, the positive charge of the atom must be concentrated in an extremely small volume. A distributed positive charge (Thomson's pudding) could never produce a strong enough electric field.

**The nuclear model:**
- The atom's positive charge and nearly all of its mass are concentrated in a tiny central **nucleus**.
- The nucleus has a radius of about 10⁻¹⁵ m to 10⁻¹⁴ m.
- The electrons occupy the rest of the atom, which has a radius of about 10⁻¹⁰ m.
- Between the nucleus and the electrons is empty space.

**The scale.** If the nucleus were the size of a marble (1 cm), the atom would be about 100 m across — the size of a football field. The electrons would be specks of dust somewhere in that vast space. Matter, the solid stuff you touch every day, is 99.9999999999999% empty space. (That's 15 nines.)

**Why we don't fall through the floor.** If atoms are mostly empty space, why doesn't your hand pass through a table? Because the electrons in the atoms of your hand are repelled by the electrons in the atoms of the table. The electromagnetic force between the electron clouds is enormous at close range, creating the sensation of solidity. You never actually "touch" anything — you just feel the electromagnetic repulsion between electron clouds.

### Closest Approach and Nuclear Size

Rutherford derived a formula for the distance of closest approach of an alpha particle to the nucleus. Consider an alpha particle (charge +2e) heading directly toward a nucleus (charge +Ze, where Z is the atomic number). As it approaches, it slows down because the electrostatic repulsion converts its kinetic energy into potential energy. At the point of closest approach, all its initial kinetic energy has been converted to electrostatic potential energy:

**½ m v² = (1 / 4πε₀) × (2e)(Ze) / d**

where d is the distance of closest approach. Solving for d:

**d = (1 / 4πε₀) × (4Ze²) / (m v²)**

For gold (Z = 79), with alpha particles of energy about 5 MeV, d ≈ 4.5 × 10⁻¹⁴ m. This is an upper limit on the nuclear radius — the nucleus must be smaller than this. Modern measurements give the gold nucleus a radius of about 7 × 10⁻¹⁵ m.

## 5. The Nuclear Model: Structure and Notation

### Why This Matters

The Rutherford experiment established that atoms have nuclei. But what's inside the nucleus? The complete picture requires two more particles, and a system of notation that every physicist uses.

### The Key Ideas

**The proton.** By 1919, Rutherford had discovered the **proton** by bombarding nitrogen with alpha particles and detecting hydrogen nuclei as products. The proton has:
- Charge: +e = +1.60 × 10⁻¹⁹ C
- Mass: 1.6726 × 10⁻²⁷ kg, defined as approximately 1 atomic mass unit (1 u)
- It is a hydrogen nucleus

**The neutron.** A problem emerged: the mass of atoms (except hydrogen) was roughly twice their atomic number. For example, helium has Z = 2 but a mass number of about 4. There must be another particle in the nucleus, roughly as massive as a proton but with no charge. James Chadwick discovered the **neutron** in 1932 by bombarding beryllium with alpha particles. The neutron has:
- Charge: 0
- Mass: 1.6749 × 10⁻²⁷ kg ≈ 1 u (slightly more massive than a proton)

**Nuclear notation.** A nucleus is specified by its **atomic number** Z (number of protons) and **mass number** A (number of protons + neutrons). The standard notation is:

$$^{A}_{Z}\text{X}$$

where X is the chemical symbol. For example:
- $${}^{4}_{2}\text{He}$$: helium-4, with 2 protons and 2 neutrons
- $${}^{238}_{92}\text{U}$$: uranium-238, with 92 protons and 146 neutrons

The number of neutrons is N = A − Z.

**Isotopes.** Atoms of the same element (same Z) can have different numbers of neutrons (different N, thus different A). These are called **isotopes**. For example, carbon has three naturally occurring isotopes: ¹²C (6 protons, 6 neutrons), ¹³C (6 protons, 7 neutrons), and ¹⁴C (6 protons, 8 neutrons). They're chemically identical (same number of electrons) but have different nuclear masses.

### Common Misconceptions

**"The nucleus contains protons and electrons."** This was a pre-Chadwick idea: since the mass number A is roughly twice Z, and protons provide the charge, maybe the nucleus contains A protons and (A − Z) electrons to adjust the charge. This model has serious problems: electrons are too light to account for nuclear mass, and quantum mechanics shows that electrons cannot be confined to a nuclear volume. The neutron solves all these problems.

**"Isotopes are different elements."** No. Isotopes have the same number of protons (same Z), so they are the same element. They differ only in neutron number, which affects nuclear properties but not chemical behavior.

**"The atomic mass unit is the mass of a proton."** Approximately, but not exactly. The atomic mass unit (u) is defined as exactly 1/12 of the mass of a carbon-12 atom. This makes 1 u = 1.6605 × 10⁻²⁷ kg. A proton has a mass of 1.0073 u; a neutron has a mass of 1.0087 u.

---

### The Chart of Nuclides

The **chart of nuclides** plots all known nuclei with neutron number N on the x-axis and proton number Z on the y-axis. Stable nuclei form a narrow "valley of stability." Nuclei too far from this valley are radioactive and will decay toward stability:

- **Above the valley (too many protons):** Undergo β⁺ decay or electron capture to reduce Z.
- **Below the valley (too many neutrons):** Undergo β⁻ decay to increase Z.
- **Far above the valley (very heavy, Z > 83):** Undergo α decay to reduce both Z and N.

The valley curves upward for heavy nuclei — this is the N > Z requirement for stability in heavy elements, which you'll explore further with the binding energy curve in Lesson 66. 

### Why Isotope Stability Matters for Dating

Radioactive isotopes with half-lives comparable to the age of what you want to date are the most useful. If the half-life is too short, the isotope decays away before you can measure it. If too long, too little decay has occurred to measure accurately. This is why ¹⁴C (5730 years) is perfect for archaeology, ⁴⁰K (1.25 billion years) for early Earth rocks, and ²³⁸U (4.47 billion years) for the age of the Earth itself. Each "clock" is suited to a different time scale. This concept returns in Lesson 65.

### Rutherford's Other Contribution: Transmutation

In 1919, Rutherford achieved the first artificial nuclear transmutation. He bombarded nitrogen gas with alpha particles and detected hydrogen nuclei (protons) as products:

$${}^{4}_{2}\text{He} + {}^{14}_{7}\text{N} \rightarrow {}^{17}_{8}\text{O} + {}^{1}_{1}\text{H}$$

This was the first time anyone had intentionally changed one element into another — the alchemists' dream, achieved through nuclear physics. It also confirmed that the hydrogen nucleus (proton) is a constituent of all atomic nuclei.

## Worked Examples

### Example 1: Alpha Particle Scattering

An alpha particle with kinetic energy 5.50 MeV approaches a gold nucleus (Z = 79) head-on. Calculate the distance of closest approach.

**Approach:** At closest approach, all kinetic energy is converted to electrostatic potential energy. Use energy conservation.

**Step 1:** Convert energy to joules.
K = 5.50 MeV = 5.50 × 10⁶ × 1.60 × 10⁻¹⁹ = 8.80 × 10⁻¹³ J

**Step 2:** Set kinetic energy equal to potential energy at closest approach.
K = (1/4πε₀) × q₁q₂ / d
where q₁ = 2e, q₂ = 79e.

**Step 3:** Solve for d.
d = (1/4πε₀) × (2e × 79e) / K
(1/4πε₀) = 8.99 × 10⁹ N·m²/C²
d = (8.99 × 10⁹) × (2 × 79 × (1.60 × 10⁻¹⁹)²) / (8.80 × 10⁻¹³)
d = (8.99 × 10⁹) × (2 × 79 × 2.56 × 10⁻³⁸) / (8.80 × 10⁻¹³)
d = (8.99 × 10⁹) × (4.045 × 10⁻³⁶) / (8.80 × 10⁻¹³)
d = (3.636 × 10⁻²⁶) / (8.80 × 10⁻¹³)
d = 4.13 × 10⁻¹⁴ m

**Why this makes sense:** This is on the order of 10⁻¹⁴ m, which is larger than the actual nuclear radius (~7 × 10⁻¹⁵ m for gold). The alpha particle never actually touches the nucleus; it's turned back by electrostatic repulsion before contact.

---

### Example 2: Nuclear Notation

An atom has 26 protons, 30 neutrons, and 26 electrons. (a) What is the chemical symbol? (b) Write it in nuclear notation. (c) Identify this isotope. (d) Calculate A and Z.

**Approach:** Z = number of protons. The element is identified by Z. A = protons + neutrons.

**Step 1:** Identify the element.
Z = 26 → iron (Fe).

**Step 2:** Calculate mass number.
A = 26 + 30 = 56.

**Step 3:** Nuclear notation.
$${}^{56}_{26}\text{Fe}$$

**Step 4:** This is iron-56, the most common isotope of iron.

---

### Example 3: Nuclear Size (Challenge)

Use the formula for nuclear radius R = R₀ A^(1/3), where R₀ = 1.20 × 10⁻¹⁵ m and A is the mass number, to calculate the radius of a gold-197 nucleus (Z = 79, A = 197).

**Approach:** Simply substitute into the empirical formula.

**Step 1:** R = R₀ A^(1/3) = 1.20 × 10⁻¹⁵ × (197)^(1/3)
197^(1/3) ≈ 5.82
R = 1.20 × 10⁻¹⁵ × 5.82 = 6.98 × 10⁻¹⁵ m

**Why this makes sense:** This is about 10⁻¹⁴ m, which is smaller than the distance of closest approach calculated in Example 1 (4.13 × 10⁻¹⁴ m), confirming that the alpha particle doesn't hit the nucleus. The nuclear radius formula shows that nuclear volume is proportional to A, meaning nuclear density is approximately constant across all nuclei.

---

### Example 4: Scale of the Atom

A hydrogen atom has a nuclear radius of about 1.0 × 10⁻¹⁵ m and an atomic radius of about 5.3 × 10⁻¹¹ m. If the nucleus were scaled up to the size of a tennis ball (radius ~3.2 cm), how far away would the electron be?

**Approach:** Use the ratio of atomic radius to nuclear radius, then apply that ratio to the scaled nucleus.

**Step 1:** Ratio = atomic radius / nuclear radius = 5.3 × 10⁻¹¹ / 1.0 × 10⁻¹⁵ = 53,000.

**Step 2:** Scaled distance = 3.2 cm × 53,000 = 169,600 cm = 1696 m ≈ 1.7 km.

**Why this makes sense:** If the nucleus were a tennis ball, the electron's orbit would be nearly 2 km away. The atom is overwhelmingly empty space. This dramatic scale explains why most alpha particles pass straight through gold foil — the probability of hitting a nucleus is tiny.

---

### Example 5: Interpreting Rutherford's Data

In a Rutherford scattering experiment, 1.00 × 10⁶ alpha particles are fired at a gold foil. 800 are detected at an angle of 60°. Using Rutherford's formula, the predicted ratio for 90° scattering relative to 60° is about 1:8. Approximately how many alpha particles would be detected at 90°?

**Approach:** Use the given ratio. The scattering rate drops rapidly with increasing angle.

**Step 1:** If the ratio of 90° to 60° counts is 1:8, then:
Counts at 90° = (1/8) × counts at 60° = (1/8) × 800 = 100.

**Why this makes sense:** Rutherford scattering follows a 1/sin⁴(θ/2) dependence. The probability of large-angle scattering is very small, which is consistent with a tiny, concentrated nucleus.

## Answers

### Answer 1
(a) Thomson crossed electric and magnetic fields perpendicular to the beam. When the electric force eE balanced the magnetic force evB, the beam was undeflected, giving v = E/B. Then, with only the magnetic field, he measured the radius of curvature r of the beam path: evB = mv²/r, so e/m = v/(Br) = E/(B²r). All quantities on the right could be measured, giving e/m.

(b) m_e = e / (e/m) = 1.60 × 10⁻¹⁹ / (1.76 × 10¹¹) = 9.09 × 10⁻³¹ kg. (The small difference from 9.11 × 10⁻³¹ kg comes from the precision of Thomson's measurement.)

(c) m_H / m_e = 1.67 × 10⁻²⁷ / 9.09 × 10⁻³¹ ≈ 1837. The electron is about 1800 times lighter than hydrogen, the lightest atom. This proves electrons are subatomic — they are pieces of atoms, not atoms themselves. Atoms must contain something much heavier to account for their mass.

### Answer 2
(a) K = 7.70 × 10⁶ × 1.60 × 10⁻¹⁹ = 1.232 × 10⁻¹² J.
d = (8.99 × 10⁹) × (2 × 29 × (1.60 × 10⁻¹⁹)²) / (1.232 × 10⁻¹²)
= (8.99 × 10⁹) × (1.485 × 10⁻³⁶) / (1.232 × 10⁻¹²)
= 1.084 × 10⁻¹⁴ m.

(b) Both the alpha particle and nucleus are positively charged, so they repel. As the alpha particle approaches, it slows down as its kinetic energy converts to electrostatic potential energy. At the point of closest approach, it momentarily stops, then the repulsive force accelerates it backward. The alpha particle never physically contacts the nucleus.

(c) Smaller for gold. Gold has a higher Z (79 vs 29), so the repulsive force is stronger. At the same incident energy, the alpha particle cannot get as close to a gold nucleus before being turned around.

### Answer 3
(a) Alpha decay reduces Z by 2 and A by 4. So parent Z = 86 + 2 = 88, parent A = 222 + 4 = 226.

(b) Z = 88 is radium (Ra). The parent is radium-226.

(c) Neutrons = A − Z = 226 − 88 = 138.

### Answer 4
In Thomson's model, the positive charge is spread uniformly throughout the atom. An alpha particle passing through this distributed charge would experience small forces from all directions, partially canceling. The net deflection would be at most 1-2 degrees. A deflection >90° would require the cumulative effect of many small deflections all in the same direction, which is statistically impossible.

In Rutherford's nuclear model, the positive charge is concentrated in a tiny nucleus. If an alpha particle passes close to this nucleus, it experiences an enormous Coulomb force that can reverse its direction in a single encounter. This naturally explains the rare large-angle scattering events. The nucleus is small, so most alpha particles miss it entirely and pass through with little or no deflection.

### Answer 5
(a) R = 1.20 × 10⁻¹⁵ × (12)^(1/3) = 1.20 × 10⁻¹⁵ × 2.29 = 2.75 × 10⁻¹⁵ m.

(b) R = 1.20 × 10⁻¹⁵ × (238)^(1/3) = 1.20 × 10⁻¹⁵ × 6.20 = 7.44 × 10⁻¹⁵ m.

(c) Volume ∝ R³, so ratio = (7.44/2.75)³ = (2.71)³ = 19.8 ≈ 20. The volume ratio equals the mass number ratio: 238/12 = 19.8.

(d) Nuclear density is approximately constant. The volume is proportional to A, which means the number of nucleons per unit volume is the same for all nuclei. Nuclear matter is essentially incompressible. This is very different from atoms, where adding electrons increases the atomic radius.

### Answer 6
(a) An alpha particle is a helium nucleus, consisting of 2 protons and 2 neutrons. It has a charge of +2e and a mass of approximately 4 u.

(b) (i) The vast majority of alpha particles passed straight through the foil with little or no deflection.
(ii) A very small fraction (about 1 in 8000) were deflected through angles greater than 90°, with some bouncing almost straight back.

(c) Most alpha particles passing through with no deflection means the atom is mostly empty space. The rare large-angle deflections mean the positive charge (which repels the positively charged alpha particles) must be concentrated in an extremely small volume — if it were spread out, large Coulomb forces could never occur. The fact that even the heaviest alpha particles could be turned around means the nucleus contains most of the atom's mass (conservation of momentum: a light nucleus couldn't reverse a heavy alpha particle). Rutherford therefore proposed that the atom consists of a small, dense, positively charged nucleus containing almost all the mass, surrounded by electrons at relatively large distances.

(d) K = 4.80 × 10⁶ × 1.60 × 10⁻¹⁹ = 7.68 × 10⁻¹³ J.
d = (8.99 × 10⁹) × (2 × 79 × (1.60 × 10⁻¹⁹)²) / (7.68 × 10⁻¹³)
= (8.99 × 10⁹) × (4.045 × 10⁻³⁶) / (7.68 × 10⁻¹³)
= 4.74 × 10⁻¹⁴ m.

(e) Air molecules would scatter the alpha particles, so they would lose energy and change direction before reaching the gold foil. An evacuated chamber ensures the alpha particles travel in straight lines and reach the foil with their full energy. Also, the zinc sulfide screen scintillations are faint and would be harder to see if air scattered the light.

## Key Takeaways

1. J.J. Thomson discovered the electron in 1897 by measuring its charge-to-mass ratio using crossed electric and magnetic fields, proving atoms have internal structure.

2. Thomson's "plum pudding" model (1904) proposed atoms as spheres of uniform positive charge with electrons embedded within. This model predicted only small-angle scattering of alpha particles.

3. The Rutherford/Geiger-Marsden experiment (1909-1911) fired alpha particles at thin gold foil. Most passed through, but ~1 in 8000 bounced back — impossible under Thomson's model.

4. Rutherford concluded that the atom has a tiny, dense, positively charged nucleus (radius ~10⁻¹⁵ m) containing nearly all the mass, with electrons occupying the much larger atomic volume (radius ~10⁻¹⁰ m). The atom is overwhelmingly empty space.

5. The nucleus contains Z protons and N neutrons, where A = Z + N. Isotopes have the same Z but different N.

6. The nuclear radius follows R ≈ R₀ A^(1/3) with R₀ ≈ 1.20 × 10⁻¹⁵ m, meaning nuclear density is approximately constant for all nuclei.
