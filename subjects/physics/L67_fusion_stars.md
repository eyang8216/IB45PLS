# Lesson 67: Fusion and Stars — Energy in the Cosmos

## What You'll Learn

Every second, the Sun converts about 600 million tonnes of hydrogen into helium, releasing energy equivalent to billions of nuclear bombs. It has been doing this for 4.6 billion years and will continue for another 5 billion. The process that powers the Sun — nuclear fusion — is the same process that lights up every star in the universe, forged all the elements heavier than hydrogen and helium, and, if harnessed on Earth, could provide virtually limitless clean energy.

This lesson explains what fusion is, why it releases energy (connecting back to the binding energy curve from Lesson 66), how quantum tunneling makes fusion possible in stars at temperatures far below what classical physics requires, how stars evolve from birth to death, and how the elements that make up your body were created in the cores of stars that exploded billions of years ago.

## 1. What Is Nuclear Fusion?

### Why This Matters

Fusion is the energy source of the universe. Every star, including the Sun, is a giant fusion reactor. The light and heat that make life on Earth possible come from the fusion of hydrogen into helium in the Sun's core. Understanding fusion is understanding why the sky is full of stars.

### The Key Ideas

**Definition.** **Nuclear fusion** is the process in which two light atomic nuclei combine to form a heavier nucleus, releasing energy. For fusion to occur, the nuclei must overcome their mutual electrostatic repulsion (the Coulomb barrier) and approach within about 10⁻¹⁵ m, where the attractive strong nuclear force takes over and binds them together.

**Why fusion releases energy.** Return to the binding energy curve (Lesson 66). Light nuclei have relatively low binding energy per nucleon. When light nuclei fuse, the product has a HIGHER BE/A, meaning nucleons become more tightly bound. The difference in binding energy is released.

For example: fusing hydrogen into helium:
- 4 protons (BE/A = 0) → ²He (BE/A ≈ 7.1 MeV/nucleon)
- Total energy released: ~26.7 MeV per ²He nucleus formed

**The Coulomb barrier problem.** Two protons approaching each other experience enormous electrostatic repulsion. At a separation of 10⁻¹⁵ m, the Coulomb potential energy is:

$$U = \frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0} \frac{e^2}{r} \approx \frac{(9 \times 10^9)(1.6 \times 10^{-19})^2}{10^{-15}} \approx 2.3 \times 10^{-13} \text{ J} \approx 1.4 \text{ MeV}$$

The kinetic energy of protons in the Sun's core (T ≈ 15 × 10⁶ K) is about kT ≈ 1.3 keV — a THOUSAND times less than the Coulomb barrier. Classically, fusion should be impossible.

### The Resolution: Quantum Tunneling

As you learned in Lesson 63, quantum tunneling allows particles to pass through barriers they classically cannot surmount. Protons in the Sun's core tunnel through the Coulomb barrier. The tunneling probability is extremely small for any given collision — but there are so many protons colliding so often (~10³⁰ collisions per second in the Sun's core) that the net fusion rate is enormous.

**The Gamow peak.** The fusion rate depends on two competing factors:
1. The Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution of proton speeds: higher speeds are exponentially rarer.
2. The tunneling probability: higher speeds mean higher energy, thinner effective barrier, exponentially more tunneling.

The product of these two exponential factors produces a peak — the **Gamow peak** — at an energy well above the average thermal energy but well below the Coulomb barrier height. Most fusion reactions occur in this narrow energy window.

This is why the Sun burns slowly and steadily. If fusion required classical barrier crossing, stars couldn't exist. If tunneling were easy, stars would explode. The specific tunneling probability sets the rate, giving the Sun its 10-billion-year lifetime.

## 2. Fusion in the Sun: The Proton-Proton Chain

### Why This Matters

The pp-chain is the dominant fusion process in stars like the Sun. Understanding its steps shows you how fusion actually works in nature, why it produces neutrinos (which we can detect on Earth), and why it's so hard to replicate in a laboratory.

### The Key Ideas

The proton-proton chain fuses four protons into one helium-4 nucleus. The net reaction:

$$4 \, ^{1}\text{H} \rightarrow {}^{4}\text{He} + 2e^+ + 2\nu_e + 2\gamma$$

This happens in three main steps:

**Step 1: p + p → deuterium.** Two protons fuse to form deuterium (²H), a positron, and a neutrino:
$$p + p \rightarrow {}^{2}\text{H} + e^+ + \nu_e$$
This is the bottleneck of the entire chain. It's extremely slow because it requires a proton to convert to a neutron via the weak interaction DURING the collision. The average proton in the Sun's core waits about 10 billion years before undergoing this reaction. This step determines the Sun's lifetime.

**Step 2: Deuterium burning.** The deuterium fuses with another proton:
$${}^{2}\text{H} + p \rightarrow {}^{3}\text{He} + \gamma$$
This step is fast — deuterium doesn't accumulate because it's consumed almost immediately.

**Step 3: Helium-3 fusion.** Two ³He nuclei fuse:
$${}^{3}\text{He} + {}^{3}\text{He} \rightarrow {}^{4}\text{He} + 2p$$
This is the most common termination of the pp chain (there are branches, but this is the dominant one in the Sun).

**Net result:** 4 protons → ²He + 2 positrons + 2 neutrinos + 26.7 MeV.

**The solar neutrino problem.** The pp-chain produces neutrinos, which stream out of the Sun's core and can be detected on Earth. In the 1970s-1990s, experiments detected only about 1/3 of the expected solar neutrinos. This "solar neutrino problem" was eventually resolved by the discovery that neutrinos have mass and oscillate between three flavors (electron, muon, tau). The early detectors were only sensitive to electron neutrinos — the others had "disappeared." This was a major discovery in particle physics, confirming physics beyond the Standard Model.

## 3. Stellar Nucleosynthesis: How Elements Are Made

### Why This Matters

The Big Bang produced only hydrogen, helium, and trace amounts of lithium. Every other element in the universe — the carbon in your DNA, the oxygen you breathe, the iron in your blood, the silicon in your computer — was forged by nuclear fusion inside stars and scattered across space when those stars died. You are, quite literally, made of stardust.

### The Key Ideas

**Hydrogen burning (main sequence).** Stars spend ~90% of their lives fusing hydrogen into helium in their cores. This is the "main sequence" phase. For the Sun, this lasts about 10 billion years.

**Helium burning (red giant phase).** When the core hydrogen is exhausted, the core contracts and heats up. At about 100 million K, helium fusion begins:

$$3 \, {}^{4}\text{He} \rightarrow {}^{12}\text{C} + \gamma$$

This is the **triple-alpha process**. It requires three helium nuclei to collide nearly simultaneously — an extremely rare event. The reaction rate is proportional to (density)³, making it very sensitive to core conditions. This process creates carbon-12, the basis of organic chemistry.

**Carbon and oxygen burning.** In stars more massive than about 8 solar masses, the core continues to contract and heat after helium is exhausted:
- Carbon burning (~5 × 10⁸ K): ¹²C + ¹²C → ²⁰Ne, ²³Na, ²⁴Mg + ...
- Neon burning (~1.2 × 10⁹ K): photodisintegration of ²⁰Ne
- Oxygen burning (~1.5 × 10⁹ K): ¹⁶O + ¹⁶O → ²⁸Si, ³²S + ...
- Silicon burning (~3 × 10⁹ K): a complex network of reactions producing nuclei up to iron-56.

**The iron limit.** Fusion stops at iron-56 because iron sits at the peak of the binding energy curve. Fusing iron into heavier elements would CONSUME energy rather than release it. The star's core, now made of iron, has no more nuclear fuel. The core collapses, triggering a supernova.

**Supernova nucleosynthesis.** The collapsing core rebounds, creating a shock wave that blasts the outer layers of the star into space. During the explosion, the enormous flux of neutrons produces elements heavier than iron through two processes:

- **s-process (slow neutron capture):** Occurs in red giants. Neutrons are captured slowly, giving unstable isotopes time to beta-decay before the next capture. Produces elements up to bismuth (Z = 83).
- **r-process (rapid neutron capture):** Occurs during supernovae (and neutron star mergers). Neutrons are captured so rapidly that isotopes beta-decay only after the neutron flux ends. Produces about half the elements heavier than iron, including gold, platinum, and uranium.

**You are stardust.** The carbon, nitrogen, and oxygen in your body were forged by helium and carbon burning in the cores of stars that lived and died before the Sun formed. The calcium in your bones came from oxygen and silicon burning. The iron in your blood is the nuclear "ash" of stellar fusion. The gold in jewelry was likely created in neutron star mergers. Every element heavier than helium has a cosmic origin story.

## 4. Stellar Evolution: The Life Cycle of Stars

### Why This Matters

Stars are born, live, and die. The path a star takes depends almost entirely on its mass. Understanding stellar evolution connects nuclear physics (what we've studied for the last five lessons) to astronomy and cosmology.

### The Key Ideas

**Star formation.** Stars form from giant molecular clouds — cold, dense regions of gas and dust. When a region of a cloud reaches a critical density (the Jeans criterion), gravity overcomes internal pressure, and the region collapses. The collapsing gas heats up, and when the core temperature reaches ~10 million K, hydrogen fusion ignites. A star is born.

**Low-mass stars (like the Sun, M < 8 M_☉).**
1. **Main sequence:** Hydrogen fusion in the core. ~10 billion years for the Sun.
2. **Red giant:** Core hydrogen exhausted. Hydrogen fusion continues in a shell around the inert helium core. The star swells to ~100× its original radius.
3. **Helium flash:** Core reaches ~100 million K. Helium fusion ignites explosively in the core (for stars below ~2 M_☉).
4. **Horizontal branch / red clump:** Stable helium burning in the core, hydrogen burning in a shell.
5. **AGB (Asymptotic Giant Branch):** Core helium exhausted. Helium and hydrogen burn in concentric shells. The star is unstable and pulsates, losing its outer layers.
6. **Planetary nebula:** The outer layers are ejected, creating a glowing shell of ionized gas.
7. **White dwarf:** The remaining core — a sphere of carbon and oxygen, about the size of Earth, supported by **electron degeneracy pressure** (a quantum mechanical effect from the Pauli exclusion principle). No further fusion occurs. It slowly cools over billions of years.

**High-mass stars (M > 8 M_☉).**
1. **Main sequence:** Hydrogen fusion. Much shorter lifetime (millions, not billions of years).
2. **Successive fusion stages:** Helium → carbon → neon → oxygen → silicon burning. Each stage creates a nested "onion" structure with the heaviest elements in the core.
3. **Iron core formation:** The core becomes iron. Fusion stops. The core is supported by electron degeneracy pressure.
4. **Core collapse:** The iron core exceeds the Chandrasekhar limit (~1.4 M_☉). Electron degeneracy pressure fails. The core collapses in less than a second.
5. **Supernova:** The collapsing core rebounds, creating a shock wave that blasts the star apart. The explosion outshines an entire galaxy for a few weeks.
6. **Remnant:** Either a **neutron star** (~12 km radius, supported by neutron degeneracy pressure) or a **black hole** (if the remnant exceeds ~3 M_☉).

### Common Misconceptions

**"The Sun will explode as a supernova."** No. The Sun is too low-mass. It will end as a white dwarf after passing through the red giant and planetary nebula phases. Only stars above ~8 solar masses can produce core-collapse supernovae.

**"All elements are made in stars."** Hydrogen, most helium, and trace lithium were made in the Big Bang — not in stars. Elements from carbon to iron are made by fusion in stellar cores. Elements heavier than iron are made primarily by neutron capture processes (s-process in red giants, r-process in supernovae and neutron star mergers).

## 5. Fusion on Earth: Toward a Clean Energy Source

### Why This Matters

If we could replicate the Sun's fusion process on Earth, we'd have a virtually limitless, carbon-free energy source using fuel (deuterium from seawater, lithium for tritium breeding) that would last millions of years. The challenge is enormous — but progress is being made.

### The Key Ideas

**Why fusion on Earth is hard.** To fuse nuclei, you need to overcome the Coulomb barrier. In the Sun, this happens because of the enormous gravitational pressure — the core density is ~150 g/cm³ (15× lead). On Earth, we can't achieve such densities. Instead, we must achieve much HIGHER temperatures: about 100-200 million K — ten times hotter than the Sun's core.

**The Lawson criterion.** For a fusion reactor to produce net energy, the product of plasma density (n), confinement time (τ), and temperature (T) must exceed a critical value. For deuterium-tritium (D-T) fusion:

$$n \tau T > 3 \times 10^{21} \text{ keV·s/m}^3$$

This is extremely demanding. Two main approaches exist:

**Magnetic confinement (tokamak).** A toroidal (doughnut-shaped) magnetic field confines the hot plasma. The leading project is ITER (International Thermonuclear Experimental Reactor) in France, aiming to produce 500 MW of fusion power from 50 MW of input — a Q = 10 energy gain. The fuel: deuterium and tritium.

**Inertial confinement.** High-powered lasers or particle beams compress a tiny fuel pellet to extreme densities and temperatures. The leading facility is the National Ignition Facility (NIF) in the US. In December 2022, NIF achieved fusion ignition — more energy out of the fuel than was delivered by the lasers (though not net energy considering the laser efficiency).

**The reaction of choice: D-T fusion.**

$${}^{2}\text{H} + {}^{3}\text{H} \rightarrow {}^{4}\text{He} + n + 17.6 \text{ MeV}$$

D-T has the lowest ignition temperature and highest reaction rate of any fusion reaction. The 14.1 MeV neutron carries most of the energy and would heat a "blanket" to produce steam. The tritium would be bred from lithium in the blanket: n + ⁶Li → ²He + ³H.

### Common Misconceptions

**"Cold fusion is a proven technology."** The 1989 claims of "cold fusion" (Fleischmann and Pons) — fusion at room temperature in a tabletop electrochemical cell — have not been replicated with the reliability and consistency required by science. Mainstream physics considers cold fusion unproven. Hot fusion (magnetic and inertial confinement) remains the scientifically validated approach.

**"Fusion produces no radioactive waste."** D-T fusion produces high-energy neutrons that activate the reactor structure, creating radioactive waste (though with much shorter half-lives than fission waste — hundreds of years rather than tens of thousands). The fusion process itself produces only helium (safe), but neutron activation of the reactor walls is a waste management challenge. Advanced fuels (like D-³He) produce fewer neutrons but require much higher temperatures.

## Worked Examples

### Example 1: Energy from the pp-chain

The net reaction of the pp-chain is 4 ¹H → ²He + 2e⁺ + 2ν_e + 2γ. Given the masses: ¹H = 1.007825 u, ²He = 4.002603 u, e = 0.000549 u, and assuming neutrinos have negligible mass, calculate the energy released per ²He nucleus formed.

**Approach:** Total mass before − total mass after = Δm. E = Δm c².

**Step 1:** Mass before: 4 × 1.007825 = 4.031300 u.
Mass after: 4.002603 + 2 × 0.000549 = 4.002603 + 0.001098 = 4.003701 u.
Δm = 4.031300 − 4.003701 = 0.027599 u.

**Step 2:** E = 0.027599 × 931.5 = 25.71 MeV.

**Why this makes sense:** The accepted value is ~26.7 MeV. The small discrepancy is because we didn't account for the mass of the two neutrinos (including their kinetic energy) and the gamma-ray energy. The 25.7 MeV accounts for the mass defect; the remaining ~1 MeV is the neutrino kinetic energy.

---

### Example 2: Solar Luminosity and Mass Loss

The Sun's luminosity is 3.846 × 10²⁶ W. Each pp-chain completion releases 26.7 MeV. (a) Calculate the number of pp-chain completions per second in the Sun. (b) Calculate the mass converted to energy per second. (c) Estimate the Sun's mass loss over its 10-billion-year main sequence lifetime.

**Approach:** Energy per reaction → reactions per second. Then mass from E = mc².

**Step 1:** E per reaction = 26.7 MeV = 4.272 × 10⁻¹² J.
Reactions/s = 3.846 × 10²⁶ / (4.272 × 10⁻¹²) = 9.00 × 10³⁷ s⁻¹.

**Step 2:** Mass loss per reaction = Δm = 0.0276 u × 1.6605 × 10⁻²⁷ kg/u = 4.58 × 10⁻²⁹ kg.
Mass loss/s = 9.00 × 10³⁷ × 4.58 × 10⁻²⁹ = 4.12 × 10⁹ kg/s ≈ 4 million tonnes/s.

**Step 3:** Over 10¹⁰ years: t = 10¹⁰ × 3.156 × 10⁷ = 3.156 × 10¹⁷ s.
Total mass loss = 4.12 × 10⁹ × 3.156 × 10¹⁷ = 1.30 × 10²⁷ kg.
Fraction of Sun's mass (2 × 10³⁰ kg): ~0.065%.

**Why this makes sense:** The Sun loses about 4 million tonnes per second to fusion. Over its lifetime, this is less than 0.1% of its total mass. The Sun's mass loss is primarily from the solar wind (~1-2 million tonnes/s), not from fusion. The fusion mass-to-energy conversion is remarkably small — even over 10 billion years, only a tiny fraction of the Sun's mass is used.

### Example 3: The Triple-Alpha Process

Calculate the energy released in the triple-alpha process: 3 ²He → ¹²C. Masses: ²He = 4.002603 u, ¹²C = 12.000000 u.

**Approach:** Mass before − mass after = Δm.

**Step 1:** Mass before: 3 × 4.002603 = 12.007809 u.
Δm = 12.007809 − 12.000000 = 0.007809 u.

**Step 2:** E = 0.007809 × 931.5 = 7.27 MeV.

**Why this makes sense:** The triple-alpha process releases about 7.3 MeV, far less than the pp-chain's 26.7 MeV. This is because helium-4 is already quite tightly bound (BE/A ≈ 7.1 MeV/nucleon), so the gain from fusing three heliums into carbon is modest. This limited energy release is one reason why the red giant phase is shorter than the main sequence — the fuel (helium) releases less energy per reaction.

### Example 4: D-T Fusion Energy

Calculate the energy released in D-T fusion: ²H + ³H → ²He + n. Masses: ²H = 2.014102 u, ³H = 3.016049 u, ²He = 4.002603 u, n = 1.008665 u.

**Step 1:** Mass before: 2.014102 + 3.016049 = 5.030151 u.
Mass after: 4.002603 + 1.008665 = 5.011268 u.
Δm = 5.030151 − 5.011268 = 0.018883 u.

**Step 2:** E = 0.018883 × 931.5 = 17.59 MeV ≈ 17.6 MeV.

**Why this makes sense:** This is the accepted value. The energy is shared: the alpha particle gets ~3.5 MeV, the neutron gets ~14.1 MeV (momentum conservation — the lighter neutron gets more of the kinetic energy).

### Example 5: Temperature Required for Fusion

Estimate the temperature needed for deuterium-tritium fusion based on the Coulomb barrier. The nuclear separation for fusion is about 2 × 10⁻¹⁵ m. Compare this with the temperatures required in practice (~10⁸ K) and explain the difference.

**Approach:** Set kT = Coulomb potential energy at the nuclear separation.

**Step 1:** For D-T, each has Z = 1:
U = (1/4πε₀)(e²/r) = (9 × 10⁹)(1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹)² / (2 × 10⁻¹⁵) = 2.304 × 10⁻¹³ / (2 × 10⁻¹⁵) = 1.152 × 10⁻¹³ J.

kT = 1.152 × 10⁻¹³ → T = 1.152 × 10⁻¹³ / (1.38 × 10⁻²³) = 8.35 × 10⁹ K.

This is about 8 billion K — 80× higher than the ~100 million K actually needed.

**Step 2:** Explanation: Quantum tunneling allows nuclei to fuse at temperatures well below the classical Coulomb barrier. The Gamow peak means most reactions occur not at the average thermal energy (~10 keV at 10⁸ K) but in the high-energy tail of the Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution (~30-50 keV), where the tunneling probability is much higher. The combination of thermal distribution and tunneling probability determines the practical fusion temperature, which is about 10⁸ K for D-T.

**Why this makes sense:** Without tunneling, fusion reactors (and stars) would be impossible at achievable temperatures. Tunneling reduces the required temperature by nearly two orders of magnitude.

### Answer 3
(a) Iron-56 has the highest binding energy per nucleon of any nucleus (~8.79 MeV/nucleon). Fusing iron into heavier nuclei would DECREASE binding energy per nucleon, consuming rather than releasing energy. The star can't extract nuclear energy from iron, so fusion stops. The iron core is "dead" — no more fusion is possible.

(b) Heavier-than-iron elements have lower BE/A than iron. Creating them by fusion (adding charged particles) would consume energy and faces enormous Coulomb barriers. Instead, they're created by neutron capture: neutrons have no charge, so they're not repelled by the nucleus. The s-process (slow neutron capture in red giants) and r-process (rapid neutron capture in supernovae and neutron star mergers) build up heavy nuclei one neutron at a time, with beta decays converting neutrons to protons as needed.

(c) The Sun is not massive enough. To fuse carbon and heavier elements, the core must reach temperatures of ~5 × 10⁸ K. The Sun's core will only reach about 10⁸ K during the helium burning phase. Without sufficient gravitational compression, the core never gets hot enough to ignite carbon. Only stars above ~8 solar masses achieve the core temperatures needed for advanced fusion stages leading to iron.

### Answer 4
Three errors:

1. **The Sun is not "burning" chemically.** Chemical burning (like a campfire) is a chemical reaction where atoms rearrange and electrons change energy levels, releasing a few eV per reaction. The Sun produces energy through NUCLEAR FUSION — nuclei combine, releasing millions of eV per reaction. The energy source and mechanism are completely different.

2. **The Sun is not using oxygen.** Chemical fires require oxygen as an oxidizer. The Sun's fusion has nothing to do with oxygen — it fuses hydrogen nuclei (protons) into helium. No oxygen is involved in the primary energy generation. (Oxygen exists in the Sun from previous generations of stars, but it's not a participant in the energy-producing reaction.)

3. **The Sun is not "on fire."** Fire is a rapid exothermic chemical oxidation reaction producing heat, light, and flames (combustion products). The Sun is a plasma — a hot, ionized gas — in which nuclear fusion occurs in the core. There is no flame, no chemical combustion, no oxygen consumption. The light we see from the Sun's surface (photosphere) is thermal radiation from hot plasma (~5800 K), not flames.

Each ²He produced has mass: 4.002603 u × 1.6605 × 10⁻²⁷ = 6.646 × 10⁻²⁷ kg.
Number of pp-chain completions over 10¹⁰ years:
Energy per completion = 26.7 MeV = 4.272 × 10⁻¹² J.
Total energy = 3.8 × 10²⁶ × 3.156 × 10¹⁷ = 1.20 × 10⁴⁴ J.
Number of completions = 1.20 × 10⁴⁴ / (4.272 × 10⁻¹²) = 2.81 × 10⁵⁵.
Mass of helium produced = 2.81 × 10⁵⁵ × 6.646 × 10⁻²⁷ = 1.87 × 10²⁹ kg.
Mass converted to energy = 4.22 × 10⁹ × 3.156 × 10¹⁷ = 1.33 × 10²⁷ kg.
Total hydrogen consumed = mass of helium + mass converted ≈ 1.87 × 10²⁹ kg.
(This represents about 9% of the Sun's total mass, though only the core ~10% is hot enough for fusion, so effectively the core hydrogen would be consumed.)

(iii) Assumption: The Sun's luminosity remains constant at 3.8 × 10²⁶ W over its entire main-sequence lifetime. In reality, the Sun's luminosity gradually increases (it's about 30% brighter now than when it formed). This means actual hydrogen consumption has been somewhat less in the past and will be somewhat more in the future. The estimate gives an approximate order of magnitude.

(e) When the Sun exhausts its core hydrogen, it will leave the main sequence and become a red giant. The core will contract and heat up until helium fusion ignites (at ~10⁸ K). The outer layers will expand enormously, possibly engulfing Mercury and Venus. After helium is exhausted in the core, the Sun will pulsate and shed its outer layers, forming a planetary nebula. The remaining core will become a white dwarf — a hot, dense sphere of carbon and oxygen about the size of Earth — which will gradually cool over billions of years. The Sun is not massive enough to go supernova.

## Key Takeaways

1. Nuclear fusion is the combining of light nuclei into heavier ones, releasing energy because the products have higher binding energy per nucleon (moving toward iron-56 on the BE/A curve).

2. The proton-proton chain (4 ¹H → ²He + 2e⁺ + 2ν_e + 26.7 MeV) powers the Sun. Quantum tunneling allows fusion at ~15 million K despite a ~1.4 MeV Coulomb barrier.

3. Stellar nucleosynthesis: hydrogen burning → helium → carbon (triple-alpha) → oxygen → ... → iron. The iron limit exists because iron has the highest BE/A.

4. Elements heavier than iron are created by neutron capture (s-process in red giants, r-process in supernovae/neutron star mergers), not by fusion.

5. Low-mass stars (<8 M_☉) end as white dwarfs after red giant and planetary nebula phases. High-mass stars (>8 M_☉) produce supernovae, leaving neutron stars or black holes.

6. Fusion on Earth (D-T: ~17.6 MeV) requires ~100 million K. Two approaches: magnetic confinement (tokamaks like ITER) and inertial confinement (lasers like NIF). The Lawson criterion must be satisfied for net energy gain.
