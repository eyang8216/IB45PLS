## Solution 1 — Rutherford Scattering [P1, 1 mark]

**Answer: B**

Rutherford's experiment showed that most alpha particles passed straight through the gold foil (A is true but does not directly indicate a nucleus — it indicates that atoms are mostly empty space). The critical observation was that **a very small fraction of alpha particles were deflected through angles greater than 90°**, which could only be explained by the existence of a small, dense, positively charged nucleus capable of exerting a strong repulsive Coulomb force.

- **A** ✗ — This observation indicates that most of the atom is empty space, but it does not by itself reveal the existence of a nucleus.
- **B** ✓ — Large-angle deflections require the alpha particle to approach a concentration of positive charge and mass (the nucleus). This was the key evidence.
- **C** ✗ — Alpha particles were not absorbed; almost all passed through.
- **D** ✗ — Only a small fraction were deflected at all; most passed through undeflected.

## Solution 3 — Strong Nuclear Force [P1, 1 mark]

**Answer: C**

The strong nuclear force is a short-range force (~1–3 fm) that acts between all nucleons (not just protons). At separations of about 1 fm it is strongly attractive, binding the nucleus together against Coulomb repulsion. At very short separations (below ~0.5 fm) it becomes **repulsive**, preventing nucleons from collapsing into each other.

- **A** ✗ — The strong force does not act equally at all separations; it is strongly range-dependent and negligible beyond ~3 fm.
- **B** ✗ — It is not attractive at *all* separations; it becomes repulsive at very short range.
- **C** ✓ — This correctly describes the attractive-then-repulsive character of the strong force.
- **D** ✗ — The strong force acts between all nucleons (p–p, n–n, p–n), not only between protons.

## Solution 7 — Photoelectric Effect Concepts [P1, 1 mark]

**Answer: B**

According to Einstein's photoelectric theory, electron emission occurs only if the incident photon has energy greater than or equal to the work function $\phi$ of the metal: $hf \geq \phi$. This depends on the **frequency** (or wavelength) of the light, not its intensity.

- **A** ✗ — Intensity affects the *number* of photoelectrons emitted (if $f$ is above the threshold), not *whether* they are emitted at all.
- **B** ✓ — Frequency determines photon energy $E = hf$; emission requires $hf \geq \phi$.
- **C** ✗ — The photoelectric effect is instantaneous (no time delay); duration of exposure is irrelevant if frequency is below threshold.
- **D** ✗ — Polarization does not affect the photoelectric effect.

## Solution 10 — Uncertainty Principle [P1, 1 mark]

**Answer: B**

The Heisenberg uncertainty principle states:

$$\Delta x \cdot \Delta p \geq \frac{h}{4\pi}$$

The product of uncertainties has a minimum value. Therefore, if $\Delta x$ (position uncertainty) is **reduced**, $\Delta p$ (momentum uncertainty) must **increase** to satisfy the inequality.

- **A** ✗ — This would violate the uncertainty principle; position and momentum uncertainties cannot both be arbitrarily small.
- **B** ✓ — Reducing $\Delta x$ forces $\Delta p$ to increase.
- **C** ✗ — This is incorrect; the uncertainties are inversely related.
- **D** ✗ — $\Delta p$ cannot become zero because $\Delta x \cdot 0 = 0 < h/4\pi$, which would violate the principle.

## Solution 17 — Controlled Chain Reaction [P1, 1 mark]

**Answer: B**

For a steady, controlled chain reaction (the critical condition, $k = 1$), each fission must produce, on average, exactly one neutron that goes on to induce another fission.

- **A** ✗ — Absorbing all neutrons would stop the chain reaction entirely ($k = 0$).
- **B** ✓ — $k = 1$ is the critical condition for a steady chain reaction.
- **C** ✗ — Reactor-grade uranium is typically enriched to only 3–5% U-235; 90% enrichment is weapons-grade.
- **D** ✗ — Thermal (slow) neutrons are needed for U-235 fission; fast neutrons are less likely to be captured.

## Solution 18 — Nuclear Reactor Components [P2, 5 marks]

**(a) Function of the moderator** **[1 mark]**

The moderator slows down fast neutrons produced by fission into **thermal neutrons** (low kinetic energy, $\sim 0.025\ \mathrm{eV}$), which have a much higher probability of inducing further fission in uranium-235. [1 mark]

**(b) Water as moderator and coolant** **[2 marks]**

- **Moderator**: Water contains hydrogen atoms, whose mass is close to that of a neutron. This makes elastic collisions very efficient at transferring kinetic energy from neutrons to hydrogen nuclei, rapidly slowing the neutrons. [1 mark]
- **Coolant**: Water has a high specific heat capacity and can be pumped through the reactor core to remove the thermal energy produced by fission, transporting it to a heat exchanger to generate steam. [1 mark]

**(c) Control rods** **[2 marks]**

- **Function**: Control rods absorb excess neutrons, allowing operators to adjust the rate of the chain reaction and maintain the reactor at criticality ($k = 1$). They can also be fully inserted to shut down the reactor. [1 mark]
- **Common materials**: Cadmium, boron (or boron carbide), or hafnium — all of which have high neutron absorption cross-sections. [1 mark]

## Solution 19 — Mass Defect [P1, 1 mark]

**Answer: B**

The mass of a stable nucleus is always **less than** the sum of the masses of its individual constituent protons and neutrons. This "missing mass," called the mass defect, is converted into binding energy according to $E = \Delta m c^2$, and it is what holds the nucleus together.

- **A** ✗ — This would imply no binding energy and the nucleus would not be stable.
- **B** ✓ — The mass defect is a fundamental property of all stable nuclei.
- **C** ✗ — A nucleus cannot be heavier than its constituents and still be bound.
- **D** ✗ — The mass of a nucleus (without electrons) is different from the mass of the neutral atom.

## Solution 24 ★ — HL: Nuclear Radius & Electron Diffraction [P2, 8 marks]

**(c) Consistency check for gold-197** **[2 marks]**

For ${}^{197}\mathrm{Au}$, $A = 197$:

$$A^{1/3} = 197^{1/3} = 5.819$$

Predicted radius:

$$R_{\text{pred}} = R_0 \, A^{1/3} = (1.19 \times 10^{-15}) \times 5.819 = 6.93 \times 10^{-15}\ \mathrm{m}$$

[1 mark] for correct prediction.

Compare with measured value of $7.0 \times 10^{-15}\ \mathrm{m}$:

$$R_{\text{pred}} = 6.93 \times 10^{-15}\ \mathrm{m} \approx 7.0 \times 10^{-15}\ \mathrm{m}$$

The predicted value differs from the measured value by only ~1%, which is well within experimental uncertainty. Therefore the measurement is **consistent** with the $R = R_0 A^{1/3}$ relationship. [1 mark]

$$\boxed{\text{Consistent};\ R_{\text{pred}} = 6.93 \times 10^{-15}\ \mathrm{m} \approx 7.0 \times 10^{-15}\ \mathrm{m}}$$

**(d) Why electrons are preferred over protons** **[2 marks]**

- Electrons are **leptons** and do not experience the **strong nuclear force**. They interact with nuclei only via the electromagnetic (Coulomb) force, so the diffraction pattern depends purely on the nuclear charge distribution — giving a clean measurement of nuclear size. [1 mark]
- Protons, being hadrons, experience the strong nuclear force in addition to the electromagnetic force. This complicates the interpretation of the diffraction pattern, as both strong and electromagnetic scattering contribute. The analysis would be far more complex and ambiguous. [1 mark]

*Also acceptable: electrons can be accelerated to the required de Broglie wavelength ($\sim 10^{-15}\ \mathrm{m}$) with relatively modest accelerating voltages compared to protons.*

## Solution 25 ★ — HL: Nucleosynthesis & Binding Energy [P2, 9 marks]

**(a) Energy release in fission and fusion** **[3 marks]**

The binding energy per nucleon (BE/A) curve rises steeply from low $A$, peaks at $A \approx 56$ (iron), and then decreases gradually for higher $A$.

- **(i) Fission of heavy nuclei**: Heavy nuclei such as uranium ($A \approx 235$) have relatively low BE/A ($\sim 7.4\ \mathrm{MeV}$). When they split into two medium-mass daughter nuclei, each daughter has a higher BE/A (closer to the peak at $\sim 8.5\ \mathrm{MeV}$). The increase in binding energy per nucleon corresponds to a net release of energy. [1.5 marks]

- **(ii) Fusion of light nuclei**: Light nuclei such as hydrogen ($A = 1$) have very low BE/A (zero for a free proton). When they fuse to form heavier nuclei (e.g. He-4, $A = 4$), the BE/A increases significantly (to $\sim 7.1\ \mathrm{MeV}$). Again, this increase in binding energy per nucleon is released as energy. [1.5 marks]

In both cases, energy is released because the product nuclei are closer to the **peak of the binding energy per nucleon curve** at $A \approx 56$. For $A < 56$, fusion is exothermic; for $A > 56$, fission is exothermic.

**(b) Iron peak — fusion limit in stellar cores** **[2 marks]**

Iron-56 has the **highest binding energy per nucleon** (~8.79 MeV) of any nuclide. Any fusion reaction that would produce an element heavier than iron would result in a **decrease** in binding energy per nucleon — the product nuclei would be *less* tightly bound than the reactants. Such a reaction would be **endothermic** (absorb energy rather than release it) and therefore cannot sustain the star against gravitational collapse. [2 marks]

**(c) s-process vs. r-process** **[2 marks]**

| Feature | s-process (slow) | r-process (rapid) |
|---|---|---|
| Neutron capture rate | Slow — each capture is followed by $\beta^-$ decay before the next capture | Rapid — many neutrons captured before $\beta^-$ decay can occur |
| Environment | Occurs in AGB stars (asymptotic giant branch) over long timescales | Occurs in supernova explosions or neutron star mergers on timescales of seconds |
| Nuclides produced | Stable and moderately neutron-rich isotopes up to bismuth-209 | Very neutron-rich, unstable isotopes; produces elements heavier than bismuth including uranium and thorium |

[1 mark] for explaining the difference in capture rates relative to $\beta^-$ decay.  
[1 mark] for identifying the astrophysical environments.

**(d) Mass equivalent of supernova energy** **[2 marks]**

Using $E = mc^2$:

$$m = \frac{E}{c^2} = \frac{1.0 \times 10^{46}}{(3.00 \times 10^8)^2}$$

$$m = \frac{1.0 \times 10^{46}}{9.00 \times 10^{16}} = 1.11 \times 10^{29}\ \mathrm{kg}$$

[1 mark] for correct mass equivalent.

As a percentage of the Sun's mass:

$$\frac{m}{M_\odot} \times 100\% = \frac{1.11 \times 10^{29}}{2.0 \times 10^{30}} \times 100\% = 5.6\%$$

[1 mark] for correct percentage.

$$\boxed{m \approx 1.1 \times 10^{29}\ \mathrm{kg};\ \text{approximately } 5.6\% \text{ of the Sun's mass}}$$
