## Section 1: Simple Harmonic Motion

### Solution 1 [3 marks]

**(a)** Angular frequency:

$$\omega = \sqrt{\frac{k}{m}} = \sqrt{\frac{200}{0.50}} = \sqrt{400} = 20\ \text{rad s}^{-1}$$

> **[1 mark]** $\checkmark$ $\omega = 20\ \text{rad s}^{-1}$

**(b)** Maximum speed:

$$v_{\text{max}} = \omega A = 20 \times 0.040 = 0.80\ \text{m s}^{-1}$$

> **[1 mark]** $\checkmark$ $v_{\text{max}} = 0.80\ \text{m s}^{-1}$

**(c)** Total mechanical energy (two equivalent methods):

$$E = \frac{1}{2}kA^2 = \frac{1}{2} \times 200 \times (0.040)^2 = \frac{1}{2} \times 200 \times 0.0016 = 0.16\ \text{J}$$

$$\text{or}\quad E = \frac{1}{2}mv_{\text{max}}^2 = \frac{1}{2} \times 0.50 \times (0.80)^2 = 0.16\ \text{J}$$

> **[1 mark]** $\checkmark$ $E = 0.16\ \text{J}$

### Solution 2 [1 mark — Paper 1 MCQ]

The period of a simple pendulum is:

$$T = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{L}{g}}$$

On Earth: $T = 2\pi\sqrt{\dfrac{L}{10}}$

On the planet ($g = 2.5\ \text{m s}^{-2}$):

$$T' = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{L}{2.5}} = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{4L}{10}} = 2 \times 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{L}{10}} = 2T$$

**Correct answer: D ($2T$)**

**Why the distractors are wrong:**
- **A ($T/4$):** Would require $g$ to be multiplied by 16, i.e. $g = 160\ \text{m s}^{-2}$. A common inversion error: dividing by 4 instead of taking the square root of the factor.
- **B ($T/2$):** Would require $g$ to be multiplied by 4. The student may have forgotten the square root in the period formula.
- **C ($T$):** Would require $g$ to be unchanged. Ignores the dependence of period on $g$.

### Solution 3 [3 marks]

**(a)** Angular frequency and maximum acceleration:

$$\omega = \frac{2\pi}{T} = \frac{2\pi}{0.80} = 2.5\pi \approx 7.85\ \text{rad s}^{-1}$$

$$a_{\text{max}} = \omega^2 A = (2.5\pi)^2 \times 0.12 = 6.25\pi^2 \times 0.12 \approx 7.4\ \text{m s}^{-2}$$

> **[1 mark]** $\checkmark$ $a_{\text{max}} = 7.4\ \text{m s}^{-2}$ (accept $7.40\ \text{m s}^{-2}$ or $6.25\pi^2 \times 0.12$)

**(b)** At $t = 0$, $x = 0$ and moving in the $+x$ direction $\implies$ sine function with zero phase constant:

$$x(t) = A\sin(\omega t) = 0.12\sin(2.5\pi\,t)$$

> **[1 mark]** $\checkmark$ $x = 0.12\sin(2.5\pi\,t)$ (accept any equivalent form)

**(c)** Displacement at $t = 0.30\ \text{s}$:

$$x = 0.12\sin(2.5\pi \times 0.30) = 0.12\sin(0.75\pi)$$

$$\sin(0.75\pi) = \sin\left(\frac{3\pi}{4}\right) = \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2} \approx 0.7071$$

$$x = 0.12 \times 0.7071 = 0.0849 \approx 0.085\ \text{m}$$

> **[1 mark]** $\checkmark$ $x = 0.085\ \text{m}$ (accept $0.085\ \text{m}$ or $8.5 \times 10^{-2}\ \text{m}$)

### Solution 4 [1 mark — Paper 1 MCQ]

For SHM, the defining equation is $a = -\omega^2 x$. This is the equation of a straight line through the origin with gradient $-\omega^2$.

Therefore the magnitude of the gradient is $\omega^2$.

**Correct answer: B ($\omega^2$)**

**Why the distractors are wrong:**
- **A ($\omega$):** Confuses the gradient of the $a$–$x$ graph ($\omega^2$) with the angular frequency itself. In SHM, $a \propto x$ with proportionality constant $\omega^2$, not $\omega$.
- **C ($1/\omega$):** This is the reciprocal; has no physical meaning in SHM kinematics.
- **D ($1/\omega^2$):** Also a reciprocal error; perhaps confusing $a = -\omega^2 x$ with $x = -(1/\omega^2)a$.

> **[1 mark]** $\checkmark$ $\lambda = 0.50\ \text{m}$

### Solution 9 [1 mark — Paper 1 MCQ]

For a point source, intensity obeys the inverse-square law: $I \propto \dfrac{1}{r^2}$.

At $r = 6.0\ \text{m}$:

$$I' = I \times \left(\frac{2.0}{6.0}\right)^2 = I \times \left(\frac{1}{3}\right)^2 = \frac{I}{9}$$

**Correct answer: C ($I/9$)**

**Why the distractors are wrong:**
- **A ($I/3$):** Used $I \propto 1/r$ instead of $I \propto 1/r^2$.
- **B ($I/6$):** May have reasoned $6.0\ \text{m}$ is 3 times farther, but then used $I' = I/(6/2) = I/3$ and confused further.
- **D ($I/36$):** Squared the distance ratio but used $6.0/2.0 = 3$, then squared 6 directly. May have calculated $(6)^2 = 36$ without using the ratio properly.

## Section 3: Wave Phenomena (Interference & Diffraction)

### Solution 11 [1 mark — Paper 1 MCQ]

Fringe separation in double-slit: $\Delta y = \dfrac{\lambda D}{d}$

If $d$ is doubled: $\Delta y' = \dfrac{\lambda D}{2d} = \dfrac{1}{2}\Delta y$

**Correct answer: A (halves)**

**Why the distractors are wrong:**
- **B (doubles):** Confuses the inverse relationship; thinks $\Delta y \propto d$ instead of $\Delta y \propto 1/d$.
- **C (remains unchanged):** Thinks fringe separation is independent of slit separation, perhaps confusing with the *existence* of interference rather than the fringe spacing.
- **D (quadruples):** Thinks $\Delta y \propto d^2$, which is not correct.

### Solution 12 [4 marks]

**(a)** Fringe separation:

$$\Delta y = \frac{\lambda D}{d} = \frac{(600 \times 10^{-9})(1.5)}{0.30 \times 10^{-3}}$$

$$= \frac{9.0 \times 10^{-7}}{3.0 \times 10^{-4}} = 3.0 \times 10^{-3}\ \text{m} = 3.0\ \text{mm}$$

> **[2 marks]** $\checkmark$ Correct substitution [1], correct answer with unit [1]

**(b)** When slits are moved further apart (increasing $d$):
1. **Fringe separation decreases** — since $\Delta y \propto 1/d$, larger $d$ gives smaller $\Delta y$, so fringes become closer together.
2. **Number of fringes visible increases** — with narrower spacing, more fringes fit within the same screen width.

> **[2 marks]** $\checkmark$ Two correct changes with valid explanations (1 mark each)

### Solution 13 [4 marks]

**(a)** Grating spacing:

$$d = \frac{1}{\text{lines per metre}} = \frac{1}{600 \times 10^3} = \frac{1}{6.00 \times 10^5} = 1.667 \times 10^{-6}\ \text{m}$$

> **[1 mark]** $\checkmark$ $d = 1.67 \times 10^{-6}\ \text{m}$

**(b)** First-order maximum ($m = 1$):

$$d\sin\theta_1 = \lambda \quad\Rightarrow\quad \sin\theta_1 = \frac{\lambda}{d} = \frac{550 \times 10^{-9}}{1.667 \times 10^{-6}} = 0.3300$$

$$\theta_1 = \sin^{-1}(0.3300) = 19.3^\circ$$

> **[1 mark]** $\checkmark$ $\theta_1 = 19.3^\circ$

**(c)** For the highest order, $\sin\theta \leq 1$:

$$m_{\text{max}} \leq \frac{d}{\lambda} = \frac{1.667 \times 10^{-6}}{550 \times 10^{-9}} = 3.03$$

Since $m$ must be an integer, $m_{\text{max}} = 3$.

> **[2 marks]** $\checkmark$ Sets $\sin\theta = 1$ / uses $m \leq d/\lambda$ [1], obtains $m = 3$ with reasoning [1]

### Solution 14 [1 mark — Paper 1 MCQ]

For single-slit diffraction, the first minima occur at $\sin\theta = \lambda/b$, where $b$ is the slit width. For small angles, $\theta \approx \lambda/b$.

The central maximum width $W \propto 1/b$. If $b$ is halved, $W$ doubles.

**Correct answer: D ($2W$)**

**Why the distractors are wrong:**
- **A ($W/4$):** Used $W \propto b^2$ — this would be the case if $W$ depended on the *area* of the slit.
- **B ($W/2$):** Used $W \propto b$ instead of $W \propto 1/b$.
- **C ($W$):** Thinks the central maximum width is independent of slit width. It is not; it arises *because* of diffraction by the slit.

> **[1 mark]** $\checkmark$ $v = 400\ \text{m s}^{-1}$

### Solution 17 [3 marks]

**(a)** For a pipe open at both ends, the fundamental has $L = \lambda/2$:

$$L = \frac{\lambda}{2} = 1.0\ \text{m}$$

$$f_2 = 2f_1 = 2 \times 170 = 340\ \text{Hz}$$

> **[1 mark]** $\checkmark$ $f_2 = 340\ \text{Hz}$

**(c)** With one end closed, the pipe now has a node at the closed end and an antinode at the open end. The fundamental satisfies $L = \lambda/4$:

> **[1 mark]** $\checkmark$ $f = 85\ \text{Hz}$

## Section 5: Doppler Effect

### Solution 21 [3 marks]

**(a)** Source moving **toward** a stationary observer:

$$f' = f\left(\frac{v}{v - v_s}\right) = 800 \times \frac{340}{340 - 30} = 800 \times \frac{340}{310}$$

$$f' = 800 \times 1.0968 = 877.4 \approx 877\ \text{Hz}$$

> **[2 marks]** $\checkmark$ Correct formula [1], correct numerical answer [1]

**(b)** Source moving **away** from a stationary observer:

$$f' = f\left(\frac{v}{v + v_s}\right) = 800 \times \frac{340}{340 + 30} = 800 \times \frac{340}{370}$$

$$f' = 800 \times 0.9189 = 735.1 \approx 735\ \text{Hz}$$

> **[1 mark]** $\checkmark$ $f' = 735\ \text{Hz}$

### Solution 22 [1 mark — Paper 1 MCQ]

A shift toward longer wavelengths means the observed wavelength $\lambda_{\text{obs}} > \lambda_{\text{source}}$. This is a **redshift**, which occurs when the source is **moving away** from the observer (recession).

**Correct answer: C** — moving away from Earth, and the light is redshifted.

**Why the distractors are wrong:**
- **A:** Interstellar dust causes dimming and can scatter blue light preferentially (making light appear redder), but it does **not** systematically shift spectral lines. The *lines themselves* shift, not the continuum.
- **B:** Moving **toward** Earth would produce a **blueshift** (shorter wavelengths), not a redshift.
- **D:** Moving away produces redshift, not blueshift. This option has the correct motion but the wrong spectral description.

## Section 6: HL Extension

### Solution 23 [4 marks]

**(a)** Phase changes on reflection:

- **(i) Air–oil interface:** Light travels from air ($n = 1.00$) to oil ($n = 1.40$). Reflection off a medium of **higher** refractive index → **phase change of $\pi$ radians** (equivalent to a path difference of $\lambda/2$).

- **(ii) Oil–water interface:** Light travels from oil ($n = 1.40$) to water ($n = 1.33$). Reflection off a medium of **lower** refractive index → **no phase change**.

> **[1 mark]** $\checkmark$ Both correct

**(b)** The two reflected rays experience a **net phase change of $\pi$** from reflections (one $\pi$, one $0$). The path difference due to traveling through the film (down and back) is $2nt$.

For **constructive** interference with a net $\pi$ phase change from reflections:

$$2nt = \left(m + \tfrac{1}{2}\right)\lambda,\qquad m = 0, 1, 2, \ldots$$

For minimum thickness, $m = 0$:

$$2nt = \frac{\lambda}{2} \quad\Rightarrow\quad t = \frac{\lambda}{4n}$$

$$t = \frac{560 \times 10^{-9}}{4 \times 1.40} = \frac{560 \times 10^{-9}}{5.60} = 1.00 \times 10^{-7}\ \text{m}$$

$$t = 100\ \text{nm}$$

> **[3 marks]** $\checkmark$ Recognises net phase change and sets up $2nt = (m+\frac{1}{2})\lambda$ [1], uses $m=0$ for minimum [1], correct answer $t = 100\ \text{nm}$ [1]

### Solution 24 [3 marks]

**(a)** **Rayleigh criterion:** Two point sources are said to be **just resolved** when the central maximum of the diffraction pattern of one source coincides with the first minimum of the diffraction pattern of the other. For a circular aperture of diameter $b$:

$$\theta = \frac{1.22\lambda}{b}$$

where $\theta$ is the minimum angular separation in radians.

> **[1 mark]** $\checkmark$ Any correct statement of the criterion

**(b)** Minimum resolvable angular separation:

$$\theta = \frac{1.22\lambda}{b} = \frac{1.22 \times 550 \times 10^{-9}}{4.0 \times 10^{-3}}$$

$$= \frac{6.71 \times 10^{-7}}{4.0 \times 10^{-3}} = 1.6775 \times 10^{-4}\ \text{rad}$$

For small angles, $\theta \approx \dfrac{\text{separation}}{\text{distance}} = \dfrac{s}{D}$:

$$D = \frac{s}{\theta} = \frac{1.2}{1.6775 \times 10^{-4}} = 7150\ \text{m}$$

$$D \approx 7.2 \times 10^3\ \text{m}\ \ (7.2\ \text{km})$$

> **[2 marks]** $\checkmark$ Correct calculation of $\theta$ [1], correct distance [1]

### Solution 25 [3 marks]

**(a)** When **unpolarized** light passes through an ideal polarizer, the intensity is reduced by half (each polarization component contributes equally, and only one is transmitted):

$$I_1 = \frac{I_0}{2}$$

> **[1 mark]** $\checkmark$ $I_1 = I_0/2$

**(b)** The light emerging from the first filter is now **linearly polarized** along the transmission axis of the first filter. When it encounters the second filter at angle $\theta = 60^\circ$, **Malus's Law** applies:

$$I_2 = I_1 \cos^2\theta = \frac{I_0}{2} \cos^2(60^\circ)$$

$$\cos(60^\circ) = \frac{1}{2},\qquad \cos^2(60^\circ) = \frac{1}{4}$$

$$I_2 = \frac{I_0}{2} \times \frac{1}{4} = \frac{I_0}{8}$$

> **[2 marks]** $\checkmark$ Applies Malus's Law with correct $\theta$ and $I_1$ [1], correct final answer $I_0/8$ [1]
