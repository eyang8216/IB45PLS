Lesson 22: Polar Form and Euler's Formula

What Are Complex Numbers in Polar Form?

The "What" — A New Way to Describe Complex Numbers

Until now, every complex number has been written in Cartesian form: $z = a + bi$, where $a$ and $b$ are real numbers. The number $a$ tells us how far left or right to go on the complex plane. The number $b$ tells us how far up or down. This is exactly the same idea as $(x, y)$ coordinates on a graph.

There is another way to describe the position of a point on the complex plane. Instead of saying "go right by $a$ units and up by $b$ units," we can say:

  • - Start at the origin.
  • - Face the point from the origin (this gives a direction, measured as an angle from the positive real axis).
  • - Walk a certain distance in that direction.

    • This is polar form. A complex number in polar form is specified by two things:

  • - $r$ — the modulus, which is the distance from the origin to the point. It is always a non-negative real number.
  • - $\theta$ — the argument, which is the angle from the positive real axis to the line connecting the origin to the point. It is measured in radians (or degrees), usually between $-\pi$ and $\pi$.

    • The polar form of a complex number $z$ is written as:

    $$z = r(\cos\theta + i\sin\theta)$$

    The shorthand $r\operatorname{cis}\theta$ means exactly the same thing: $r(\cos\theta + i\sin\theta)$.

    The "Why" — Multiplication Becomes Simple

    Multiplying complex numbers in Cartesian form requires expanding brackets (FOIL), collecting terms, and remembering that $i^2 = -1$. Division in Cartesian form requires multiplying numerator and denominator by the complex conjugate.

    Polar form turns multiplication into a much simpler operation: multiply the moduli, add the arguments. Division becomes: divide the moduli, subtract the arguments. This geometric insight — that multiplying complex numbers corresponds to rotating and scaling — is one of the most powerful ideas in complex analysis.

    1. From Cartesian to Polar Form

    Any non-zero complex number $z = a + bi$ can be converted to polar form by finding its modulus and argument.

    The Modulus

    The modulus of $z = a + bi$ is the distance from the point $(a, b)$ to the origin $(0, 0)$. By the Pythagorean theorem:

    $$r = |z| = \sqrt{a^2 + b^2}$$

    The modulus is always a non-negative real number. It is never negative because it represents a distance.

    The Argument

    The argument of $z$, written $\arg(z)$, is the angle $\theta$ that the line from the origin to $(a, b)$ makes with the positive real axis. The tangent of this angle is $\frac{b}{a}$. However, using $\theta = \arctan\left(\frac{b}{a}\right)$ directly is dangerous — the arctan function on a calculator only returns angles between $-\frac{\pi}{2}$ and $\frac{\pi}{2}$, which means it cannot distinguish between Quadrant I and Quadrant III, or between Quadrant II and Quadrant IV.

    A common misconception: Many students compute $\arctan\left(\frac{b}{a}\right)$ and accept whatever the calculator gives them. This is wrong. You must always check which quadrant the point $(a, b)$ lies in and adjust the angle accordingly:

  • - If $a > 0$ and $b \geq 0$: Quadrant I, $\theta = \arctan\left(\frac{b}{a}\right)$.
  • - If $a < 0$: Quadrant II or III, $\theta = \arctan\left(\frac{b}{a}\right) + \pi$ (or $\arctan\left(\frac{b}{a}\right) - \pi$, whichever keeps the answer in the principal range).
  • - If $a > 0$ and $b < 0$: Quadrant IV, $\theta = \arctan\left(\frac{b}{a}\right)$ (which will be negative).
  • - If $a = 0$ and $b > 0$: $\theta = \frac{\pi}{2}$.
  • - If $a = 0$ and $b < 0$: $\theta = -\frac{\pi}{2}$.

    • Converting from Polar to Cartesian

    To go back from polar form $z = r(\cos\theta + i\sin\theta)$ to Cartesian form $z = a + bi$, compute:

    $$a = r\cos\theta, \quad b = r\sin\theta$$

    This follows directly from the definition of sine and cosine in a right triangle with hypotenuse $r$.

    Worked Example 1: Cartesian to Polar

    Problem: Express $z = -1 + i\sqrt{3}$ in polar form $r(\cos\theta + i\sin\theta)$.

    Approach: To put a complex number in polar form, we need two numbers: the modulus $r$ and the argument $\theta$. The modulus comes from the Pythagorean theorem. The argument comes from $\arctan$ with a quadrant check.

    Step 1 — Find the modulus:

    $$r = \sqrt{(-1)^2 + (\sqrt{3})^2} = \sqrt{1 + 3} = \sqrt{4} = 2$$

    Step 2 — Find the basic angle:

    $$\tan\theta = \frac{b}{a} = \frac{\sqrt{3}}{-1} = -\sqrt{3}$$

    The reference angle whose tangent is $\sqrt{3}$ (ignoring the negative sign for now) is $\frac{\pi}{3}$, because $\tan\frac{\pi}{3} = \sqrt{3}$.

    Step 3 — Adjust for quadrant: Since $a = -1$ (negative) and $b = \sqrt{3}$ (positive), the point lies in Quadrant II. In Quadrant II, the argument is $\pi$ minus the reference angle:

    $$\theta = \pi - \frac{\pi}{3} = \frac{2\pi}{3}$$

    Answer:

    $$z = 2\left(\cos\frac{2\pi}{3} + i\sin\frac{2\pi}{3}\right)$$

    Why this makes sense: The modulus is $2$, meaning the point is $2$ units from the origin. The argument is $\frac{2\pi}{3}$, which is $120^\circ$ — this points into the second quadrant, exactly where $(-1, \sqrt{3})$ should be.

    Worked Example 2: Polar to Cartesian

    Problem: Express $z = 4\left(\cos\frac{5\pi}{6} + i\sin\frac{5\pi}{6}\right)$ in Cartesian form $a + bi$.

    Approach: We need to evaluate $\cos\frac{5\pi}{6}$ and $\sin\frac{5\pi}{6}$, then multiply each by the modulus $4$.

    Step 1 — Evaluate the trigonometric functions: The angle $\frac{5\pi}{6}$ is in Quadrant II (it is $150^\circ$). The reference angle is $\frac{\pi}{6}$.

    $$\cos\frac{5\pi}{6} = -\cos\frac{\pi}{6} = -\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}$$
    $$\sin\frac{5\pi}{6} = \sin\frac{\pi}{6} = \frac{1}{2}$$

    Step 2 — Multiply by the modulus:

    $$a = 4\left(-\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}\right) = -2\sqrt{3}$$
    $$b = 4\left(\frac{1}{2}\right) = 2$$

    Answer: $z = -2\sqrt{3} + 2i$

    Why this makes sense: The modulus $4$ and argument $\frac{5\pi}{6}$ place the point in the second quadrant, with $a$ negative and $b$ positive. Our Cartesian form has $a = -2\sqrt{3} \approx -3.46$ (negative) and $b = 2$ (positive), which fits.

    2. Euler's Formula

    What It Is

    One of the most important formulas in all of mathematics links exponential functions with trigonometric functions:

    $$e^{i\theta} = \cos\theta + i\sin\theta$$

    This is called Euler's formula. The number $e$ is the mathematical constant approximately equal to $2.71828$ (the base of natural logarithms). The letter $i$ is the imaginary unit. The letter $\theta$ is any real number, representing an angle in radians.

    Why It Matters

    Euler's formula turns complex number multiplication into exponent arithmetic. Instead of manipulating sines and cosines, you can use the familiar rules of exponents:

  • - $e^{i\theta_1} \cdot e^{i\theta_2} = e^{i(\theta_1 + \theta_2)}$ — multiplying adds the angles.
  • - $(e^{i\theta})^n = e^{in\theta}$ — raising to a power multiplies the angle.

    • Using Euler's formula, we can write any complex number in exponential form:

    $$z = re^{i\theta}$$

    where $r$ is the modulus and $\theta$ is the argument. This is exactly equivalent to the polar form $r(\cos\theta + i\sin\theta)$.

    Special Values to Memorize

    Certain complex numbers appear so frequently that you should know their exponential forms without calculation:

  • - $i = e^{i\pi/2}$ (modulus $1$, argument $\frac{\pi}{2}$)
  • - $-1 = e^{i\pi}$ (modulus $1$, argument $\pi$)
  • - $-i = e^{-i\pi/2}$ (modulus $1$, argument $-\frac{\pi}{2}$)
  • - $1 + i = \sqrt{2}\,e^{i\pi/4}$ (modulus $\sqrt{2}$, argument $\frac{\pi}{4}$)
  • - $1 - i = \sqrt{2}\,e^{-i\pi/4}$ (modulus $\sqrt{2}$, argument $-\frac{\pi}{4}$)
  • - $\sqrt{3} + i = 2e^{i\pi/6}$ (modulus $2$, argument $\frac{\pi}{6}$)
  • - $1 + i\sqrt{3} = 2e^{i\pi/3}$ (modulus $2$, argument $\frac{\pi}{3}$)

    • 3. Multiplication in Polar and Exponential Form

    If $z_1 = r_1(\cos\theta_1 + i\sin\theta_1)$ and $z_2 = r_2(\cos\theta_2 + i\sin\theta_2)$, then their product is:

    $$z_1 z_2 = r_1 r_2 \bigl(\cos(\theta_1 + \theta_2) + i\sin(\theta_1 + \theta_2)\bigr)$$

    In exponential form, this becomes even more compact:

    $$z_1 z_2 = r_1 e^{i\theta_1} \cdot r_2 e^{i\theta_2} = r_1 r_2\, e^{i(\theta_1 + \theta_2)}$$

    The rule: When multiplying two complex numbers, multiply their moduli and add their arguments. This is the geometric meaning of complex multiplication — it is a scaling (by the product of moduli) combined with a rotation (by the sum of the angles).

    Worked Example 3: Multiplication in Exponential Form

    Problem: Given $z_1 = 2e^{i\pi/4}$ and $z_2 = 3e^{i\pi/3}$, find $z_1 z_2$ in both exponential and Cartesian forms.

    Approach: For exponential form, multiply the moduli and add the arguments directly. For Cartesian form, convert the result using Euler's formula.

    Step 1 — Multiply in exponential form:

    $$z_1 z_2 = 2 \cdot 3 \cdot e^{i(\pi/4 + \pi/3)} = 6e^{i\cdot 7\pi/12}$$

    Step 2 — Convert to Cartesian form:

    $$z_1 z_2 = 6\left(\cos\frac{7\pi}{12} + i\sin\frac{7\pi}{12}\right)$$

    This is an acceptable answer. If numerical values were needed, we could compute $\cos\frac{7\pi}{12}$ and $\sin\frac{7\pi}{12}$ using known angle formulas, but leaving it in this form is often preferred.

    Answer: Exponential: $6e^{i\cdot 7\pi/12}$. Cartesian: $6\left(\cos\frac{7\pi}{12} + i\sin\frac{7\pi}{12}\right)$.

    Why this makes sense: The product has modulus $6$, which is the product of $2$ and $3$. Its argument is $\frac{7\pi}{12}$, which is the sum of $\frac{\pi}{4}$ and $\frac{\pi}{3}$. Both results align with the multiplication rule.

    4. Division in Polar and Exponential Form

    If $z_1 = r_1 e^{i\theta_1}$ and $z_2 = r_2 e^{i\theta_2}$ (with $z_2 \neq 0$), then:

    $$\frac{z_1}{z_2} = \frac{r_1}{r_2}\, e^{i(\theta_1 - \theta_2)}$$

    The rule: When dividing two complex numbers, divide their moduli and subtract the argument of the denominator from the argument of the numerator.

    5. Why Multiplying by $i$ Rotates by $90^\circ$

    The number $i$ can be written in polar form. Its modulus is $|i| = \sqrt{0^2 + 1^2} = 1$. Its argument is $\frac{\pi}{2}$ because it lies on the positive imaginary axis. Therefore:

    $$i = 1 \cdot e^{i\pi/2}$$

    When we multiply any complex number $z = re^{i\theta}$ by $i$:

    $$i \cdot z = e^{i\pi/2} \cdot re^{i\theta} = r e^{i(\theta + \pi/2)}$$

    The modulus stays the same (multiplying by $1$). The argument increases by $\frac{\pi}{2}$ radians, which is exactly $90^\circ$ counterclockwise. This explains geometrically why multiplying by $i$ rotates a complex number by $90^\circ$.

    Practice Problems

    Problem 1: Express the complex number $z = 4 - 4i$ in polar form $r(\cos\theta + i\sin\theta)$ and in exponential form $re^{i\theta}$.

    Problem 2: Convert the complex number $z = 5e^{i\cdot 3\pi/4}$ to Cartesian form $a + bi$. Give exact values, not decimal approximations.

    Problem 3: Let $z_1 = 2e^{i\pi/6}$ and $z_2 = 4e^{-i\pi/3}$. Find the product $z_1 z_2$ in both exponential form and Cartesian form.

    Problem 4: Express the complex number $z = -\sqrt{2} - i\sqrt{2}$ in exponential form $re^{i\theta}$.

    Problem 5 (IB-style): A complex number $z$ is given by $z = 1 - i\sqrt{3}$.

    (a) Find the modulus and argument of $z$. [2 marks]

    (b) Hence, or otherwise, write $\frac{1}{z}$ in the form $r(\cos\theta + i\sin\theta)$, where $r > 0$ and $-\pi < \theta \leq \pi$. [3 marks]

    Problem 6: Two non-zero complex numbers $z_1$ and $z_2$ have moduli $|z_1| = 3$ and $|z_2| = 2$. Their arguments are $\arg(z_1) = \frac{\pi}{3}$ and $\arg(z_2) = \frac{5\pi}{6}$. Find the modulus and argument of the quotient $\frac{z_1}{z_2}$.

    Answers

    Answer 1:

    We have $a = 4$ and $b = -4$.

    The modulus is $r = \sqrt{4^2 + (-4)^2} = \sqrt{16 + 16} = \sqrt{32} = 4\sqrt{2}$.

    For the argument: $\tan\theta = \frac{-4}{4} = -1$. Since $a > 0$ and $b < 0$, the point is in Quadrant IV. In Quadrant IV, the argument equals the arctan value (which is negative): $\theta = -\frac{\pi}{4}$.

    Polar form: $z = 4\sqrt{2}\left(\cos\left(-\frac{\pi}{4}\right) + i\sin\left(-\frac{\pi}{4}\right)\right)$.

    Exponential form: $z = 4\sqrt{2}\,e^{-i\pi/4}$.

    Common pitfall: Many students give $\theta = \frac{7\pi}{4}$ instead of $-\frac{\pi}{4}$. While $\frac{7\pi}{4}$ represents the same direction, the principal argument is typically given in the range $(-\pi, \pi]$, so $-\frac{\pi}{4}$ is the standard choice.

    Answer 2:

    We use Euler's formula: $e^{i\cdot 3\pi/4} = \cos\frac{3\pi}{4} + i\sin\frac{3\pi}{4}$.

    The angle $\frac{3\pi}{4}$ is in Quadrant II. The reference angle is $\frac{\pi}{4}$.

    $\cos\frac{3\pi}{4} = -\frac{\sqrt{2}}{2},\quad \sin\frac{3\pi}{4} = \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}$

    Multiply by the modulus $5$:

    $z = 5\left(-\frac{\sqrt{2}}{2} + i\frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}\right) = -\frac{5\sqrt{2}}{2} + \frac{5\sqrt{2}}{2}i$

    Answer 3:

    In exponential form, we multiply the moduli and add the arguments:

    $z_1 z_2 = 2 \cdot 4 \cdot e^{i(\pi/6 + (-\pi/3))} = 8e^{-i\pi/6}$

    For Cartesian form, we use Euler's formula with $\theta = -\frac{\pi}{6}$:

    $\cos\left(-\frac{\pi}{6}\right) = \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2},\quad \sin\left(-\frac{\pi}{6}\right) = -\frac{1}{2}$

    $z_1 z_2 = 8\left(\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2} - i\frac{1}{2}\right) = 4\sqrt{3} - 4i$

    Why this makes sense: The argument $-\frac{\pi}{6}$ places the result in Quadrant IV, meaning the real part should be positive and the imaginary part negative. Indeed, $4\sqrt{3} > 0$ and $-4 < 0$, so the quadrant is correct.

    Answer 4:

    We have $a = -\sqrt{2}$ and $b = -\sqrt{2}$, both negative, so the point is in Quadrant III.

    Modulus: $r = \sqrt{(-\sqrt{2})^2 + (-\sqrt{2})^2} = \sqrt{2 + 2} = \sqrt{4} = 2$.

    For the argument: $\tan\theta = \frac{-\sqrt{2}}{-\sqrt{2}} = 1$. The reference angle whose tangent is $1$ is $\frac{\pi}{4}$.

    Since the point is in Quadrant III, the argument is $-\pi + \frac{\pi}{4} = -\frac{3\pi}{4}$. (Alternatively, $\pi + \frac{\pi}{4} = \frac{5\pi}{4}$, but the principal argument is typically $-\frac{3\pi}{4}$.)

    Exponential form: $z = 2e^{-i3\pi/4}$.

    Answer 5:

    (a) Modulus: $|z| = \sqrt{1^2 + (-\sqrt{3})^2} = \sqrt{1 + 3} = \sqrt{4} = 2$.

    For the argument: $\tan\theta = \frac{-\sqrt{3}}{1} = -\sqrt{3}$. The reference angle is $\frac{\pi}{3}$. Since $a = 1 > 0$ and $b = -\sqrt{3} < 0$, the point is in Quadrant IV, so $\theta = -\frac{\pi}{3}$.

    Thus $|z| = 2$ and $\arg(z) = -\frac{\pi}{3}$.

    (b) We can write $z = 2e^{-i\pi/3}$. Then:

    $\frac{1}{z} = \frac{1}{2e^{-i\pi/3}} = \frac{1}{2}e^{i\pi/3}$

    The modulus of $\frac{1}{z}$ is $\frac{1}{2}$. The argument is $\frac{\pi}{3}$.

    In the form $r(\cos\theta + i\sin\theta)$:

    $\frac{1}{z} = \frac{1}{2}\left(\cos\frac{\pi}{3} + i\sin\frac{\pi}{3}\right)$

    Answer 6:

    For division, we divide the moduli and subtract the arguments:

    $\left|\frac{z_1}{z_2}\right| = \frac{|z_1|}{|z_2|} = \frac{3}{2}$

    $\arg\!\left(\frac{z_1}{z_2}\right) = \arg(z_1) - \arg(z_2) = \frac{\pi}{3} - \frac{5\pi}{6}$

    To subtract, find a common denominator:

    $\frac{\pi}{3} = \frac{2\pi}{6}$, so $\frac{2\pi}{6} - \frac{5\pi}{6} = -\frac{3\pi}{6} = -\frac{\pi}{2}$.

    The modulus is $\frac{3}{2}$ and the argument is $-\frac{\pi}{2}$.
