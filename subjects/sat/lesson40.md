# Lesson 40: Words in Context (Vocabulary)

## What You'll Learn
SAT vocabulary questions don't test obscure words you've never seen. They test whether you can use context clues to determine the meaning of a word you probably already know — just not in the way you're used to. This lesson teaches you the context-based approach that makes these questions predictable and solvable.

## SAT Context
Words in Context questions appear 2-4 times per module. Almost all tested words are familiar — the challenge is that they're being used in an unfamiliar sense. The SAT tests precision: can you identify exactly which meaning fits the tone, logic, and syntax of the passage?

---

### The Context Clue Method

When you encounter a vocabulary-in-context question, follow these steps:

**Step 1: Read the sentence containing the word, plus one sentence before and after.**
Vocabulary meaning is always local — you rarely need the full passage.

**Step 2: Cover up the target word and predict what word should go there.**
Your prediction gives you a yardstick to measure the answer choices against.

**Step 3: Identify what kind of context clue is present.**
There are three main types:

---

### Context Clue Type 1: Definition or Explanation

The passage literally tells you what the word means.

**Signal words:** "that is," "in other words," "which means," "i.e.," "or"
Also watch for: appositives set off by commas, dashes, or parentheses.

**Example:**
"The politician engaged in prevarication — that is, deliberately misleading statements — to avoid answering the question."
→ "Prevarication" means lying or being evasive, directly defined by the dashes.

**Example:**
"The substance was found to be deleterious, or harmful, to the developing embryos."
→ "Deleterious" means harmful, signaled by "or."

---

### Context Clue Type 2: Contrast Signals

The passage tells you what the word does NOT mean, and you infer the opposite.

**Signal words:** "but," "however," "although," "whereas," "unlike," "in contrast," "on the other hand," "not...but," "rather than," "instead of"

**Example:**
"Unlike her gregarious sister, who thrived at parties, Maria was reserved and preferred solitude."
→ "Gregarious" contrasts with "reserved" and "preferred solitude," so it means sociable or outgoing.

**Example:**
"The critic's review was surprisingly laudatory, whereas his previous reviews had been harsh and dismissive."
→ "Laudatory" contrasts with "harsh and dismissive," so it means full of praise.

> **🔍 PATTERN RECOGNITION:** Contrast clues are the most common type on the SAT. When you see contrast signals (but, however, unlike), ask: "What's the opposite idea here?" The vocabulary word means the opposite of whatever comes after the contrast signal.

---

### Context Clue Type 3: Example or Illustration

The passage gives examples that illustrate the word's meaning.

**Signal words:** "such as," "including," "for example," "for instance," "like"

**Example:**
"The store sold an eclectic assortment of goods, including vintage clothing, imported spices, and handmade musical instruments."
→ "Eclectic" is illustrated by the diverse, unrelated items, so it means varied or from many sources.

---

### Multiple-Meaning Words: The SAT's Favorite Trick

The SAT loves words that have a common meaning and a less-common secondary meaning. The question will use the secondary meaning, but the common meaning will be among the answer choices.

**Common SAT multiple-meaning words:**

| Word | Common Meaning | SAT-Preferred Meaning |
|------|---------------|----------------------|
| striking | hitting | noticeable, remarkable |
| determined | having decided | discovered, established ("the study determined...") |
| critical | disapproving | extremely important, decisive |
| just | fair | exactly, barely ("just above") |
| simple | easy | mere, nothing more than |
| charged | billed money | filled with (emotion/energy) |
| sustained | maintained | suffered ("sustained an injury") |
| temper | anger | moderate, soften |
| champion | winner | advocate for, support |
| want | desire | lack ("for want of") |

> **🚨 SAT TRAP ALERT:** The most common definition of a word will almost always appear as a WRONG answer choice. Students who don't read context grab the familiar definition. ALWAYS test the word in the sentence — if the common meaning feels slightly off, the SAT is testing a secondary meaning.

### Academic Vocabulary Worth Knowing

These words appear frequently across SAT passages. You don't need to memorize obscure vocabulary, but knowing these high-frequency academic words will speed you up:

- **analyze:** examine in detail
- **constitute:** make up, form
- **derive:** obtain from a source
- **establish:** set up, prove, demonstrate
- **indicate:** point out, show, suggest
- **occur:** happen
- **role:** function or part
- **significant:** important, meaningful
- **factor:** element contributing to a result
- **context:** circumstances surrounding something
- **explicit:** clearly stated
- **implicit:** implied but not stated
- **phenomenon:** observable fact or event
- **valid:** well-founded, logically sound
- **contend:** argue, assert

---

### Tone and Logic: The Final Check

After narrowing down answer choices using context clues, apply the tone-and-logic test:

**Tone check:** Does the word choice match the passage's tone? A scientific passage won't use emotional language. An opinion piece might.

**Logic check:** Does this meaning make logical sense in the sentence? Read it back. If it sounds forced or awkward, it's wrong.

> **🔍 PATTERN RECOGNITION:** The correct answer almost always fits BOTH the semantic meaning AND the tone of the surrounding text. A synonym that's technically correct but too informal or too formal for the passage's tone is usually wrong.

---

### Worked Example 1

**Question:**
As used in the text, "simple" most nearly means:
"The discovery that the complex protein structure was composed of a simple chain of amino acids surprised the research team."

A) easy
B) mere
C) basic in design
D) unintelligent

**Answer:** B
**Explanation:** In this scientific context, "simple" contrasts with "complex protein structure" and modifies "chain." The point is that the structure was nothing more than a chain — "mere" captures this. Option A (easy) is the most common meaning and thus a trap. Option C is close but doesn't capture the surprising contrast ("basic in design" doesn't convey the same sense of "nothing more than"). Option D is a meaning that applies to people, not chains.

---

### Worked Example 2

**Question:**
As used in the text, "charged" most nearly means:
"The atmosphere in the negotiating room was charged with tension as both sides refused to compromise."

A) billed a fee
B) formally accused
C) filled with
D) rushed forward

**Answer:** C
**Explanation:** "Charged with tension" means the atmosphere was filled with or saturated with tension. The context — negotiating room, both sides refusing to compromise — supports a meaning of emotional intensity. Option A (billed a fee) and B (formally accused) are common meanings that don't fit. Option D is a verb meaning, but "charged" here functions as an adjective modifying "atmosphere."

---

### Worked Example 3

**Question:**
As used in the text, "wrought" most nearly means:
"The Industrial Revolution wrought profound changes in the structure of society, from urbanization to new class divisions."

A) made of metal
B) caused
C) twisted
D) destroyed

**Answer:** B
**Explanation:** "Wrought" is an archaic past tense of "work" — here it means "brought about" or "caused." The context tells us the Industrial Revolution caused profound changes. Option A relates to "wrought iron" and is a common association. Option C is a physical meaning. Option D is the opposite of what happens — the changes happened, they weren't destroyed.

## Practice Problems

### Problem 1
As used in the text, "determined" most nearly means:
"The archaeological team determined that the artifacts dated back to 3000 BCE through carbon dating analysis."

A) decided firmly
B) discovered
C) controlled
D) resolved

### Problem 2
As used in the text, "critical" most nearly means:
"While the researcher's work was innovative, her reliance on a small sample size proved critical to the study's ultimate rejection by the journal."

A) expressing disapproval
B) extremely important
C) at a crisis point
D) analytical

### Problem 3
As used in the text, "sustained" most nearly means:
"The athlete sustained a career-ending injury during what seemed like a routine play."

A) maintained over time
B) suffered
C) supported
D) endured

### Problem 4
As used in the text, "provincial" most nearly means:
"The diplomat's provincial attitudes, shaped entirely by his upbringing in a small rural town, prevented him from understanding the complexity of international relations."

A) relating to a province
B) narrow-minded
C) governmental
D) regional

### Problem 5
As used in the text, "temper" most nearly means:
"The judge decided to temper her ruling with leniency, acknowledging the defendant's difficult circumstances."

A) anger
B) harden
C) moderate
D) adjust the pitch of

### Problem 6
As used in the text, "want" most nearly means:
"For want of sufficient evidence, the case against the corporation was dismissed."

A) desire
B) need
C) lack
D) poverty

### Problem 7
As used in the text, "fixed" most nearly means:
"The researchers kept the temperature fixed at 37 degrees Celsius throughout the experiment to ensure consistent conditions."

A) repaired
B) arranged dishonestly
C) constant
D) attached

---

## Answers

### Problem 1
**Answer: B (discovered)**
**Explanation:** In scientific/archaeological contexts, "determined" often means "found out" or "discovered" through investigation. The team discovered (determined) the age of the artifacts through carbon dating. Option A (decided firmly) is the most common meaning but doesn't fit — you don't "decide firmly" a date through scientific testing; you discover it. Option C (controlled) and D (resolved) don't fit the context of archaeological findings.

### Problem 2
**Answer: B (extremely important)**
**Explanation:** The sentence tells us the small sample size was "critical to the study's ultimate rejection" — meaning it was the crucial or decisive factor. "Extremely important" captures this sense of being the deciding factor. Option A (expressing disapproval) is the most common meaning but doesn't fit — sample sizes don't express disapproval. Option C (at a crisis point) is close but too medical-emergency in tone. Option D (analytical) doesn't fit the context of causing rejection.

### Problem 3
**Answer: B (suffered)**
**Explanation:** The athlete "sustained" a career-ending injury — this is the common medical/formal usage meaning "suffered" or "received." Option A (maintained over time) is the most common meaning and the trap — injuries aren't "maintained over time." Option C (supported) and D (endured) don't fit: you don't "support" or "endure" an injury in this context; you suffer it.

### Problem 4
**Answer: B (narrow-minded)**
**Explanation:** The diplomat's "provincial" attitudes are explained by his upbringing "in a small rural town" and prevented him from understanding international relations. The context tells us this is a limitation — the attitudes are narrow, unsophisticated, limited in perspective. Option A (relating to a province) is the literal meaning but doesn't capture the pejorative sense. Option C (governmental) and D (regional) miss the judgmental tone.

### Problem 5
**Answer: C (moderate)**
**Explanation:** The judge tempered her ruling "with leniency," meaning she softened or moderated the severity of the ruling. "Temper" as a verb means to moderate or soften. Option A (anger) is the noun trap. Option B (harden) means the opposite. Option D (adjust the pitch of) is a musical meaning that doesn't fit here.

### Problem 6
**Answer: C (lack)**
**Explanation:** "For want of sufficient evidence" means "for lack of sufficient evidence" — the case was dismissed because evidence was lacking. Option A (desire) is the most common meaning and the trap. Option B (need) is close but "for want of" is an idiomatic phrase meaning "for lack of." Option D (poverty) is related but too extreme — it refers to lack of resources, not specifically material poverty.

### Problem 7
**Answer: C (constant)**
**Explanation:** The temperature was "fixed" at 37 degrees — meaning it was kept constant, unchanging. In scientific contexts, "fixed" often means held steady. Option A (repaired) is the most common meaning — you can't "repair" a temperature. Option B (arranged dishonestly) applies to things like fixed races or fixed elections. Option D (attached) is a physical meaning that doesn't apply to temperature.

---

**Quick Recap:**
- Always read 1 sentence before and after the target word
- Predict what word should go there before looking at answers
- Look for definition signals, contrast signals, or example signals
- Beware of the most common definition — it's often a trap
- The right answer fits both meaning AND tone
