Lesson 28: Verb Tense, Mood & Aspect

What You'll Learn

Verbs do more than show action — they convey *when* something happened (tense), *how* the speaker views the action (aspect), and the speaker's *attitude* toward the action (mood). The SAT tests your ability to maintain consistent tenses, correctly use perfect tenses for time sequencing, and apply the subjunctive mood in formal English. By the end of this lesson, you'll know when to use "had gone," "has gone," and "went" — and why "if I was" is usually wrong.

SAT Context

Verb tense, mood, and aspect questions appear in the Standard English Conventions domain. They often require you to look beyond the individual sentence to the paragraph context — the SAT will test whether you can maintain tense consistency across sentences. Expect 2-4 questions per test on these skills.

Tense Consistency: Don't Shift Without Reason

The core rule: once you establish a time frame in a passage, maintain that tense unless there's a logical reason to shift.

**WRONG:** When the scientist **conducted** the experiment, she **observes** an unexpected reaction.

(Shifted from past "conducted" to present "observes" — no reason.)

**RIGHT:** When the scientist **conducted** the experiment, she **observed** an unexpected reaction.

(Both past tense — consistent.)

**RIGHT:** The scientist **conducts** experiments every day, and she **observes** the results carefully.

(Both present tense — describing a habit.)

When Shifting IS Correct

Shift tenses when the *time relationship* requires it:

**RIGHT:** The scientist **believes** (present) that the experiment **was** (past) successful.

**RIGHT:** She **had finished** (past perfect) the analysis before the deadline **arrived** (past).

The Perfect Tenses: Sequencing Past Events

Perfect tenses are formed with forms of "have" + past participle. They show how one action relates to another in time.

Present Perfect: has/have + past participle

Use for: (1) actions that started in the past and continue to the present, or (2) past actions with present relevance.

  • She **has lived** in Chicago since 2010. (still lives there)
  • They **have completed** the project. (recently finished, relevant now)
  • I **have never visited** Japan. (life experience up to now)

**Signal words:** since, for, yet, already, ever, never, so far, recently

Past Perfect: had + past participle

Use for: an action that happened BEFORE another past action. The past perfect is the "past of the past."

  • She **had already eaten** when I arrived. (eating happened before arriving)
  • The team **had practiced** for months before the tournament began.
  • He realized he **had left** his keys at home.

> **🚨 SAT TRAP ALERT:** The SAT loves to test whether you recognize when past perfect is needed. Two past events → the earlier one gets "had." If only ONE past event is described, simple past is correct. Don't use "had" just because something happened in the past.

**WRONG:** Yesterday, I **had gone** to the store. (Only one past event — use simple past: "I went")

**RIGHT:** I **had gone** to the store before I realized I forgot my wallet. (Two past events: going to the store happened first)

Future Perfect: will have + past participle

Use for: an action that will be completed before a specific future time.

  • By December, she **will have graduated**.
  • They **will have finished** the construction by next summer.

Future: Will vs. Would

"Will" — for actual future events

  • The concert **will begin** at 8 PM.
  • Scientists predict that sea levels **will rise**.

"Would" — for hypotheticals, conditionals, and past-future

  • If I had more time, I **would travel** more. (hypothetical)
  • She said she **would call** me later. (past tense of "will" — reporting what someone said)
  • I knew it **would rain**. (past perspective on a then-future event)

> **🔍 PATTERN RECOGNITION:** In "if...then" sentences: "If [past], [would]." / "If [had + past participle], [would have + past participle]."

The Subjunctive Mood

The subjunctive mood expresses wishes, hypotheticals, demands, and suggestions. It uses unusual verb forms that often sound wrong but are grammatically correct.

Subjunctive for Hypotheticals: "If I WERE..."

Use **were** (not was) for hypothetical/unreal conditions with all subjects:

  • **RIGHT:** If I **were** rich, I'd travel the world. (NOT "If I was")
  • **RIGHT:** If she **were** here, she'd agree. (NOT "If she was")
  • **RIGHT:** I wish it **were** summer. (NOT "wish it was")

> **🚨 SAT TRAP ALERT:** "If I was" is common in casual speech, but the SAT expects "If I were" for hypothetical situations. However, if the "if" clause expresses something that might actually be true (not hypothetical), use the indicative: "If I **was** rude yesterday, I apologize."

Subjunctive for Demands/Suggestions: The Bare Infinitive

After verbs like *suggest, recommend, insist, demand, request, propose, require*, use the base form of the verb (bare infinitive) — NOT the third-person singular form:

  • **RIGHT:** I suggest that he **go** to the doctor. (NOT "goes")
  • **RIGHT:** The teacher recommended that she **study** more. (NOT "studies")
  • **RIGHT:** The rules require that each participant **arrive** on time. (NOT "arrives")
  • **RIGHT:** It is essential that the package **be** delivered today. (NOT "is delivered")

This applies even when the subject is singular third-person!

> **🔍 PATTERN RECOGNITION:** When you see "suggest/recommend/insist/demand/require/request/propose that..." the next verb MUST be in base form — no -s, no -ed, just the bare verb.

Worked Example 1

**Question:** By the time the rescue team arrived, the hikers __________ without food or water for nearly three days.

A) go

B) went

C) had gone

D) have gone

**Answer:** C — "had gone"

**Explanation:** Two past events are being sequenced: (1) the hikers going without food/water and (2) the rescue team arriving. The hikers' deprivation came first and continued up to the arrival. Past perfect "had gone" correctly shows that this action preceded the arrival.

Worked Example 2

**Question:** The museum director insisted that every artifact __________ handled with extreme care during the transportation process.

A) is

B) was

C) be

D) will be

**Answer:** C — "be"

**Explanation:** After the verb "insisted," the subjunctive mood is required. The subjunctive uses the bare infinitive form "be" (not "is," "was," or "will be"). This applies even though "artifact" is singular.

Worked Example 3

**Question:** If I __________ aware of the traffic conditions, I would have taken an alternate route to the airport.

A) was

B) were

C) had been

D) would be

**Answer:** C — "had been"

**Explanation:** This is a past hypothetical ("would have taken"). The "if" clause for a past hypothetical uses past perfect: "had been." Option B ("were") would be correct for a present hypothetical: "If I were aware...I would take..." But since the result clause uses "would have taken" (past), the condition needs "had been."

Practice Problems

Problem 1

After the company __________ its new policy, employee satisfaction improved significantly.

A) implements

B) has implemented

C) implemented

D) had implemented

Problem 2

The professor recommended that each student __________ the primary source documents before writing the final essay.

A) reads

B) read

C) has read

D) reading

Problem 3

If the experiment __________ conducted under more controlled conditions, the results might have been more reliable.

A) is

B) was

C) were

D) had been

Problem 4

Last summer, my family __________ to three national parks, and we plan to visit two more next year.

A) traveled

B) had traveled

C) has traveled

D) travels

Problem 5

The architect __________ the blueprints for over a year before construction finally began.

A) has refined

B) had been refining

C) refined

D) will have refined

Problem 6

It is crucial that the medical team __________ prepared for any complications that may arise during the procedure.

A) is

B) was

C) be

D) will be

Answers

Problem 1 — Answer: D ("had implemented")

**Why D is correct:** Two past events: (1) the company implementing the policy and (2) employee satisfaction improving. The policy implementation came first, so past perfect "had implemented" correctly sequences the earlier action.

**Why others are wrong:**

  • A ("implements"): Present tense — doesn't match the past context of "improved."
  • B ("has implemented"): Present perfect — sets up a connection to the present, but the sentence is about past events.
  • C ("implemented"): Simple past — this would suggest the two events happened simultaneously or in an unspecified order. Past perfect clarifies the sequence.

Problem 2 — Answer: B ("read")

**Why B is correct:** After "recommended that," the subjunctive mood requires the bare infinitive (base form). "Read" is the base form of the verb (pronounced "reed" here, but spelled the same). Singular "student" does NOT trigger an -s ending in the subjunctive.

**Why others are wrong:**

  • A ("reads"): Third-person singular present — not the subjunctive form.
  • C ("has read"): Present perfect — not the subjunctive form.
  • D ("reading"): Gerund/present participle — grammatically incorrect after "recommended that [subject]..."

Problem 3 — Answer: D ("had been")

**Why D is correct:** The result clause "might have been" signals a past hypothetical. For past hypotheticals, the "if" clause requires past perfect: "had been conducted."

**Why others are wrong:**

  • A ("is"): Present tense — doesn't match the hypothetical/past context.
  • B ("was"): Simple past indicative — this would suggest the experiment actually was conducted that way, not a hypothetical.
  • C ("were"): Subjunctive "were" is for present hypotheticals ("If it were conducted now, the results might be..."). The past hypothetical requires "had been."

Problem 4 — Answer: A ("traveled")

**Why A is correct:** "Last summer" signals a completed past time period. Simple past "traveled" is the correct tense for a completed past action. There's only one past event being described, so past perfect is not needed.

**Why others are wrong:**

  • B ("had traveled"): Past perfect is unnecessary — there's no second past event that needs sequencing.
  • C ("has traveled"): Present perfect — but "last summer" is a finished time period, which can't be used with present perfect.
  • D ("travels"): Present tense — contradicts "last summer."

Problem 5 — Answer: B ("had been refining")

**Why B is correct:** Two past events: (1) the architect refining blueprints and (2) construction beginning. The refining happened continuously over a year before construction began. "Had been refining" (past perfect progressive) emphasizes the ongoing duration of the earlier action.

**Why others are wrong:**

  • A ("has refined"): Present perfect — doesn't match the past context.
  • C ("refined"): Simple past — doesn't show that refining came before construction.
  • D ("will have refined"): Future perfect — contradicts the past context.

Problem 6 — Answer: C ("be")

**Why C is correct:** "It is crucial that..." triggers the subjunctive mood, which requires the bare infinitive "be" regardless of the subject. Similar to "recommend that," "suggest that," etc.

**Why others are wrong:**

  • A ("is"): Indicative mood — not subjunctive. "It is crucial that the team is prepared" is common in speech but wrong on the SAT.
  • B ("was"): Past tense — doesn't match the subjunctive requirement.
  • D ("will be"): Future — not the subjunctive form.
