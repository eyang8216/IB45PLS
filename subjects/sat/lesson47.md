# Lesson 47: Inferences from Text

## What You'll Learn
Inference questions ask you to read between the lines — but only slightly. You'll learn the SAT's strict definition of "reasonable inference," how to separate valid inferences from wild guesses, and a reliable elimination strategy that works on the hardest inference questions.

## SAT Context
Inference questions appear 3-5 times per module and are among the most challenging question types. Many students overthink them or bring in outside assumptions. The SAT's inference standard is rigorous: the correct answer must be the MOST REASONABLE conclusion based ONLY on the text.

---

### What Is a Valid SAT Inference?

A valid inference is a conclusion that is NOT directly stated in the passage but follows LOGICALLY from what IS stated.

**Key characteristics of a valid SAT inference:**
1. It can be logically derived from stated facts in the passage
2. It requires minimal assumptions — ideally zero outside knowledge
3. It is the MOST reasonable conclusion among the options
4. It does NOT contradict anything in the passage

**What an inference is NOT:**
- A direct restatement of what the passage says (that's a detail question)
- A wild guess based on one word or phrase
- Your personal opinion about the topic
- Something that "could be true" but isn't well-supported

> **🚨 SAT TRAP ALERT:** "This could be true" is NOT the standard. The SAT requires that the inference "is most reasonably drawn" — meaning it MUST be true given the information provided, or at least be MORE reasonable than any alternative. If you can imagine the passage being true and your inference being false, it's not a valid inference.

---

### The Elimination-First Strategy

For inference questions, elimination is more reliable than trying to construct the correct answer from scratch.

**Step 1: Read the question carefully.**
Note exactly what is being asked. "It can be inferred that the author believes..." is different from "It can be inferred about the character that..."

**Step 2: For each answer choice, apply the PROVE test.**
Can you PROVE this answer must be true using ONLY evidence from the passage?
- If NO → eliminate
- If MAYBE → keep for now, but compare against others
- If YES → this is likely the answer

**Step 3: Compare the survivors.**
Among answers that pass the "maybe" test, which one requires the fewest assumptions?

**Step 4: Do a negative check.**
Could the passage be true and this inference be false? If yes, eliminate.

---

### The Assumption Spectrum

All inferences require some assumptions. The question is: how much?

**Minimal assumption (GOOD inference):**
Passage: "Maria studied for six hours and still failed the exam."
Inference: "The exam was difficult."
→ Requires one small assumption: studying a lot + failing = difficulty.

**Moderate assumption (USUALLY wrong on SAT):**
Passage: "Maria studied for six hours and still failed the exam."
Inference: "Maria has poor study habits."
→ Requires assuming her studying was ineffective, which isn't stated. Maybe she studied brilliantly but the exam was impossibly hard.

**Heavy assumption (ALWAYS wrong on SAT):**
Passage: "Maria studied for six hours and still failed the exam."
Inference: "Maria will never succeed academically."
→ Requires multiple unjustified leaps.

> **🔍 PATTERN RECOGNITION:** The correct inference almost always stays VERY close to the text. It's often a logical consequence of combining two stated facts, or recognizing the implication of a single stated fact. If you find yourself thinking "well, it makes sense because in real life...," you're making an outside-knowledge assumption.

---

### Inference Signal Phrases

Learn to recognize these question stems — they all signal inference questions:

| Question Stem | What's Being Asked |
|---------------|-------------------|
| "It can be reasonably inferred that..." | Logical conclusion from stated facts |
| "The passage suggests that..." | Implication not directly stated |
| "The author implies that..." | What the author hints at |
| "The passage most strongly suggests..." | What the preponderance of evidence points to |
| "Based on the passage, it is reasonable to conclude..." | Logical endpoint of the argument |

**IMPORTANT:** "Suggests" and "implies" are inference signals. "States," "indicates," "According to the passage" are detail signals. Don't confuse them.

---

### Combining Facts for Inference

Many inference questions require you to combine TWO separate facts from the passage to reach a conclusion.

**Example:**
Fact 1: "All members of the council voted in favor of the proposal."
Fact 2: "Dr. Chen is a member of the council."

Inference: Dr. Chen voted in favor of the proposal.

This is a deductive inference — it's logically guaranteed by the two facts combined. The SAT's "most reasonable" standard actually approaches deductive certainty.

---

### The Negative Test

The most powerful tool for eliminating wrong inference answers:

**For each answer choice, ask: "Could the passage be entirely true, and yet this statement be false?"**

If the answer is YES, eliminate the choice. A valid inference must be true whenever the passage is true.

**Example:**
Passage: "The mayor proposed a new tax. The city council voted 5-4 against the proposal."

Could the passage be true and yet "The mayor is unpopular" be false? YES — a popular mayor can have a proposal rejected.
Could the passage be true and yet "The proposal did not receive majority support in the council" be false? NO — 5-4 against means it didn't get majority support.

The second survives the negative test.

---

### Inference vs. Over-Inference

The most common mistake: going one step too far.

**Example:**
Passage: "Since switching to the new manufacturing process, the company's defect rate has fallen from 3.2% to 0.8%."

Valid inference: The new process is associated with a lower defect rate. (Modest, directly supported)
Over-inference: The new process is the best manufacturing method in the industry. (Comparative, goes beyond data)
Over-inference: The company will now dominate the market. (Predictive, requires assumptions about competitors)
Over-inference: The old process was terrible. (Evaluative, goes beyond what's stated)

**The rule of thumb:** If the inference contains a value judgment (good, bad, best, worst), make sure that judgment is clearly implied by the passage. If the inference makes a prediction or comparison beyond the scope of the passage, it's likely wrong.

### Worked Example 1

**Passage:**
"Despite the widespread belief that the library's collection was comprehensive, Dr. Patel discovered that nearly 40% of the 19th-century medical journals cited in academic literature were absent from the library's holdings."

**Question:** It can be reasonably inferred from the passage that:
A) Dr. Patel is the head librarian at the institution
B) the library's collection of 19th-century medical journals is incomplete
C) most academic researchers are unaware of the library's deficiencies
D) 19th-century medical journals are no longer relevant to modern research

**Answer:** B
**Explanation:** If 40% of cited journals are absent, the collection is factually incomplete. This is almost a restatement — the only inference is that "absent from holdings" = "incomplete." Option A is unsupported — Dr. Patel discovered this, but their job title isn't given. Option C is plausible (the passage mentions "widespread belief") but goes further than the text supports — we don't know what "most" researchers know. Option D contradicts the passage, which indicates these journals ARE cited (thus relevant).

---

### Worked Example 2

**Passage:**
"The city's new bike-sharing program has been operational for six months. During that period, ridership has increased every month, from 12,000 trips in January to 34,000 in June. However, the program's costs have also risen steadily, and it currently operates at a deficit of approximately $200,000 per month."

**Question:** The passage most strongly suggests that:
A) the bike-sharing program will be shut down within the next year
B) residents prefer bike-sharing to public transit
C) the program's popularity is growing, but its financial viability is uncertain
D) the city should have invested in roads instead of bike-sharing

**Answer:** C
**Explanation:** Two facts combine: ridership is increasing (growing popularity) AND the program operates at a deficit (financial viability uncertain). Option A is an over-inference — the passage doesn't predict shutdown. Option B is unsupported — no comparison to public transit is made. Option D is an opinion outside the passage's scope.

---

### Worked Example 3

**Passage:**
"The experiment was designed to test whether plants grow faster when exposed to classical music. After eight weeks, the plants in the music group averaged 14.3 cm in height, while the control group averaged 14.1 cm. The researchers noted that this difference was not statistically significant."

**Question:** It can be reasonably inferred that:
A) the researchers conducted the experiment poorly
B) classical music has no effect on plant growth under any circumstances
C) the results do not provide compelling evidence that classical music affects plant growth
D) the plants in the music group would have grown faster if the experiment had lasted longer

**Answer:** C**
**Explanation:** The researchers explicitly say the difference "was not statistically significant." From this, we can infer the results don't provide compelling evidence. Option A is an unsupported value judgment. Option B overgeneralizes — the study tested specific conditions. Option D speculates about hypotheticals beyond the data.

## Practice Problems

### Problem 1
"In 1960, women made up approximately 6% of medical school graduates in the United States. By 2020, that figure had risen to just over 50%."

It can be reasonably inferred from the passage that:
A) medical schools now discriminate against male applicants
B) the absolute number of female medical school graduates increased substantially between 1960 and 2020
C) women are now better doctors than men
D) medical schools reduced their admission standards to accommodate more women

### Problem 2
"When the company announced its decision to move manufacturing overseas, the factory's 800 workers received notice that their positions would be eliminated within six months. The town's unemployment rate, already at 9%, was expected to rise sharply."

The passage most strongly suggests that:
A) the company was losing money on its domestic manufacturing operations
B) all 800 workers will find new jobs within a year
C) the factory closure will have a significant negative impact on the local economy
D) the company made the decision primarily to avoid paying union wages

### Problem 3
"The new software update was designed to fix the security vulnerability that had been exploited in the previous quarter's data breach. However, within days of the update's release, independent security researchers identified three new vulnerabilities in the patched code."

It can be reasonably inferred that:
A) the company should fire its software developers
B) the software is now completely secure
C) the update successfully addressed the original vulnerability
D) fixing one security problem can sometimes introduce new ones

### Problem 4
"Maya submitted her manuscript to twelve literary agents over the course of eighteen months. She received form rejection letters from eleven. The twelfth agent requested the full manuscript and, after three weeks of deliberation, offered representation."

Based on the passage, it is reasonable to conclude that:
A) Maya's manuscript was poorly written
B) finding a literary agent for one's manuscript can require persistence
C) Maya will become a bestselling author
D) the eleventh agent almost offered representation but changed their mind

### Problem 5
"The archaeological team had been excavating the site for three weeks with nothing to show for their efforts but pottery shards and rusted nails. On the twenty-second day, a graduate student's trowel struck something solid. The team would later describe what emerged from the earth as the most significant Roman mosaic discovered in Britain in over fifty years."

The passage suggests that:
A) the graduate student was more skilled than the senior archaeologists
B) the mosaic was made of solid gold
C) significant archaeological discoveries can occur after long periods of unremarkable findings
D) the team should have started excavating in a different location

### Problem 6
"A 2022 survey found that 68% of remote workers reported being satisfied with their work-life balance, compared to 54% of in-office workers. However, 41% of remote workers also reported feeling isolated from their colleagues, compared to 18% of in-office workers."

It can be reasonably inferred from the passage that:
A) remote work is superior to in-office work in all respects
B) remote work involves trade-offs, with certain advantages and certain drawbacks
C) all remote workers prefer working from home
D) in-office workers are more productive than remote workers

---

## Answers

### Problem 1
**Answer: B**
**Explanation:** If the percentage went from 6% to ~50%, the absolute number of female graduates almost certainly increased substantially (unless total medical school enrollment crashed, which is far less reasonable to assume). This is a minimal, logical inference. Option A is an unsupported claim. Option C is a value judgment with no textual support. Option D is an unsupported assumption about admissions standards.

### Problem 2
**Answer: C**
**Explanation:** The passage states 800 jobs will be eliminated and the unemployment rate (already 9%) was "expected to rise sharply." This directly suggests significant negative impact. Option A is plausible in reality but unstated. Option B contradicts "expected to rise sharply." Option D is an unsupported assumption about motivation.

### Problem 3
**Answer: D**
**Explanation:** The update fixed one vulnerability (the one from the data breach) but introduced ("identified...in the patched code") three new ones. This illustrates exactly what D describes. Option A is a value judgment. Option B contradicts the passage. Option C — we know the update was DESIGNED to fix it, but we don't know if it SUCCEEDED.

### Problem 4
**Answer: B**
**Explanation:** Maya submitted to twelve agents over eighteen months before finding one who offered representation. This demonstrates persistence. Option A is an unsupported value judgment — many excellent manuscripts get rejected. Option C is a prediction far beyond the text. Option D is pure speculation — we know nothing about what the eleventh agent thought.

### Problem 5
**Answer: C**
**Explanation:** Three weeks of unremarkable finds (pottery shards, nails) followed by a "most significant...discovery in over fifty years." This maps directly to option C. Option A is unsupported — the grad student happened to find it; skill isn't mentioned. Option B is nonsensical — mosaics aren't made of gold. Option D is an unsupported suggestion.

### Problem 6
**Answer: B**
**Explanation:** The passage presents both a positive (better work-life balance: 68% vs 54%) and a negative (more isolation: 41% vs 18%). This is a clear trade-off. Option A is contradicted by the isolation data. Option C is contradicted — 32% of remote workers are NOT satisfied, and 41% feel isolated. Option D is unsupported — productivity isn't mentioned.

---

**Quick Recap:**
- Valid inference = logically follows from stated facts, minimal assumptions
- Eliminate answers that fail the "must this be true?" test
- Combine multiple stated facts to reach conclusions
- The negative test: "Could the passage be true and this be false?"
- Stay close to the text — the right answer often feels modest or obvious
- Beware of over-inference: value judgments, predictions, and broad generalizations
