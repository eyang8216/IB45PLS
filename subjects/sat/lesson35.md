# Lesson 35: Frequently Confused Words & Idioms

## What You'll Learn
English is full of word pairs that sound alike but have different meanings (homophones) and words that look similar but serve different grammatical functions. The SAT tests your ability to distinguish between these commonly confused words. You'll also learn fixed prepositional idioms — combinations like "preoccupied with" and "different from" — that follow no logical rule but must be memorized. By the end of this lesson, you'll never confuse "affect" and "effect," "fewer" and "less," or "lie" and "lay" again.

## SAT Context
Frequently confused words appear in the Standard English Conventions domain and sometimes in Expression of Ideas questions. These are pure knowledge questions — either you know the distinction or you don't. The SAT loves to test affect/effect, its/it's (see Lesson 33), lie/lay, and preposition idioms. Expect 2-4 questions per test on word choice and idioms.

## Part 1: Commonly Confused Words

---

### Affect vs. Effect

This is the most commonly tested word pair on the SAT.

**Affect** (verb) = to influence or produce a change in something
- The weather **affects** my mood.
- The new policy **affected** thousands of employees.
- How does caffeine **affect** your sleep?

**Effect** (noun) = the result or consequence of an action
- The **effect** of the medication was immediate.
- The new law had unintended **effects**.
- What **effect** did the speech have on the audience?

**Effect** (verb, rare) = to bring about or cause (formal)
- The new CEO hopes to **effect** change. (bring about change)

> **🔍 PATTERN RECOGNITION:** 95% of the time on the SAT: **affect = verb, effect = noun.** Only worry about "effect" as a verb if "affect" clearly doesn't work in context.

---

### Lie vs. Lay

This is the hardest pair because the past tense forms overlap.

| | Present | Past | Past Participle | Meaning |
|---|---------|------|-----------------|---------|
| **Lie** | lie | lay | lain | to recline (no direct object) |
| **Lay** | lay | laid | laid | to place something (takes object) |

**Lie** (recline — intransitive, no object):
- I need to **lie** down. (present)
- Yesterday, I **lay** down for a nap. (past — NOTE: "lay" is the past of "lie"!)
- I have **lain** on this couch for hours. (past participle)

**Lay** (place — transitive, takes an object):
- **Lay** the book on the table. (present — place something)
- She **laid** the book on the table. (past)
- She has **laid** the groundwork. (past participle)

**Test:** If you can replace the verb with "place/placed," use "lay/laid." If you can replace it with "recline/reclined," use "lie/lay/lain."

**WRONG:** I am going to **lay** down. (Are you going to place something down? No — you're going to recline.)
**RIGHT:** I am going to **lie** down.

---

### Fewer vs. Less

**Fewer** = for countable nouns (things you can count individually)
**Less** = for uncountable/mass nouns (things measured as a whole)

- **Fewer** students, **fewer** cars, **fewer** mistakes, **fewer** opportunities
- **Less** water, **less** time, **less** money, **less** information

**WRONG:** There were **less** people at the concert. (People are countable.)
**RIGHT:** There were **fewer** people at the concert.

**WRONG:** I have **fewer** patience than I used to. (Patience is uncountable.)
**RIGHT:** I have **less** patience than I used to.

> **🚨 SAT TRAP ALERT:** The SAT especially loves testing "less" vs. "fewer" with words that seem uncountable but are countable, like "people," "items," "reasons," and "opportunities."

---

### Who vs. Which vs. That

**Who** = people (and sometimes animals with personality)
- The student **who** won the award

**Which** = things, animals, ideas (nonrestrictive, usually with commas)
- The book, **which** was published last year

**That** = people or things, but restrictive (no commas, essential info)
- The book **that** I read yesterday
- The student **that** sits next to me (acceptable, though "who" is more formal for people)

On the SAT:
- Use **who** for people
- Use **which** for nonrestrictive clauses about things (with commas)
- Use **that** for restrictive clauses about things (no commas)

---

### Between vs. Among

**Between** = for two entities
**Among** = for three or more entities

- The secret is **between** you and me. (two people)
- The inheritance was divided **among** the four siblings. (four people)

---

### Than vs. Then

**Than** = used for comparisons
- She is taller **than** her brother.
- I'd rather study **than** fail.

**Then** = refers to time/sequence
- We studied, **then** we took a break.
- If it rains, **then** we'll stay inside.

---

### Accept vs. Except

**Accept** = to receive or agree to something
- I **accept** your offer.
- She was **accepted** to three colleges.

**Except** = excluding, but not
- Everyone **except** John came to the party.
- I like all vegetables **except** Brussels sprouts.

---

### Principle vs. Principal

**Principle** = a fundamental truth, rule, or belief
- She has strong moral **principles**.
- The **principle** of gravity.

**Principal** = main, primary, or the head of a school; also the original sum of money
- The **principal** of the school. (head)
- The **principal** reason for the decision. (main)
- Pay off the **principal** on the loan. (original sum)

---

### Compliment vs. Complement

**Compliment** = an expression of praise
- She gave me a nice **compliment** about my presentation.

**Complement** = something that completes or goes well with something else
- The wine **complements** the cheese.
- The two teammates' skills **complement** each other.

### More Quick Hits

| Pair | Distinction |
|------|-------------|
| **Allusion** / **Illusion** | reference / false perception |
| **Elicit** / **Illicit** | draw out / illegal |
| **Emigrate** / **Immigrate** | leave a country / enter a country |
| **Farther** / **Further** | physical distance / metaphorical degree |
| **Imply** / **Infer** | suggest / deduce |
| **Precede** / **Proceed** | come before / go forward |

## Part 2: Prepositional Idioms

Some words must be followed by specific prepositions. These follow no logic — you just need to know them.

### Common SAT Idioms

| Correct | Incorrect |
|---------|-----------|
| **preoccupied with** | preoccupied by |
| **consistent with** | consistent to |
| **different from** | different than (SAT prefers "from") |
| **capable of** | capable to |
| **interested in** | interested about |
| **associated with** | associated to |
| **responsible for** | responsible of |
| **familiar with** | familiar to (different meaning) |
| **similar to** | similar with |
| **prefer X to Y** | prefer X over Y |
| **regard X as Y** | regard X to be Y |
| **consider X Y** (no "as") | consider X as Y |
| **prohibit X from Y** | prohibit X to Y |
| **forbid X to Y** | forbid X from Y |

> **🚨 SAT TRAP ALERT:** The SAT tests idioms by changing the preposition. If something sounds slightly "off" — "preoccupied by" instead of "preoccupied with" — the preposition is likely the problem.

---

### Worked Example 1
**Question:** The new regulations are expected to __________ thousands of small businesses across the country.

A) effect
B) affect
C) impact on
D) have an effect

**Answer:** B — "affect"

**Explanation:** The sentence needs a verb meaning "influence." "Affect" is the verb form. "Effect" is primarily a noun. "Impact on" is unnecessarily wordy and the sentence structure ("are expected to ___") calls for a direct verb. "Have an effect" would require "on" after it: "have an effect on thousands."

---

### Worked Example 2
**Question:** After the long hike, Maria needed to __________ down for a while before she could continue.

A) lay
B) laid
C) lie
D) lain

**Answer:** C — "lie"

**Explanation:** Maria needs to recline (no object being placed). The present tense of "to recline" is "lie." The test: can you replace it with "recline"? Yes → "lie." Can you replace it with "place"? "Maria needed to place down" → No. So "lie" is correct.

---

### Worked Example 3
**Question:** The researcher was preoccupied __________ the implications of her findings rather than the methodology itself.

A) by
B) with
C) about
D) for

**Answer:** B — "with"

**Explanation:** The correct idiom is "preoccupied with." There's no logical rule — it's just the convention. "Preoccupied by" is commonly misused but incorrect on the SAT.

## Practice Problems

### Problem 1
The medication had a calming __________ on the patient, reducing anxiety within thirty minutes.

A) affect
B) effect
C) affecting
D) effective

### Problem 2
Because the survey had __________ respondents this year than last, the researchers questioned the reliability of the results.

A) less
B) fewer
C) least
D) few

### Problem 3
The professor asked the students to __________ their essays on the desk before leaving the classroom.

A) lie
B) lay
C) laid
D) lain

### Problem 4
The committee's decision was __________ with the organization's stated goals and values.

A) consistent to
B) consistent with
C) consistent for
D) consistent about

### Problem 5
The architect's design for the new wing was meant to __________ the existing structure without mimicking it exactly.

A) compliment
B) complement
C) comply
D) complete

### Problem 6
The study found that students who read for pleasure performed better academically __________ those who did not.

A) then
B) than
C) from
D) as

---

## Answers

### Problem 1 — Answer: B ("effect")

**Why B is correct:** The sentence needs a noun — "a calming ___ on the patient." "Effect" is the noun form meaning "result" or "influence." The article "a" before the blank confirms we need a noun.

**Why others are wrong:**
- A ("affect"): "Affect" is a verb, not a noun. "A calming affect" is incorrect.
- C ("affecting"): This is a verb form (present participle), not a noun.
- D ("effective"): This is an adjective, not the noun needed here.

---

### Problem 2 — Answer: B ("fewer")

**Why B is correct:** "Respondents" (people who responded) are countable. For countable nouns, use "fewer." "Fewer respondents this year than last."

**Why others are wrong:**
- A ("less"): "Less" is for uncountable nouns. Respondents are countable.
- C ("least"): "Least" is the superlative form and would need "the" ("the least respondents"), and it doesn't fit the comparative structure "___ than last."
- D ("few"): "Few" is positive, but the comparison "___ than" requires a comparative form ("fewer").

---

### Problem 3 — Answer: B ("lay")

**Why B is correct:** The students are placing their essays on the desk. "To place" = "to lay" (transitive — takes an object: "essays"). The present tense of "to lay" is "lay." Test: "The professor asked the students to place their essays..." ✓

**Why others are wrong:**
- A ("lie"): "Lie" means to recline. "To lie their essays" — doesn't make sense.
- C ("laid"): "Laid" is the past tense of "lay." "To laid" is grammatically incorrect — "to" is followed by the base form.
- D ("lain"): "Lain" is the past participle of "lie." "To lain" is incorrect.

---

### Problem 4 — Answer: B ("consistent with")

**Why B is correct:** The correct idiom is "consistent with" — meaning in agreement or harmony with. This is a fixed prepositional idiom.

**Why others are wrong:**
- A ("consistent to"): Not the correct idiom.
- C ("consistent for"): Not the correct idiom.
- D ("consistent about"): Not the correct idiom.

---

### Problem 5 — Answer: B ("complement")

**Why B is correct:** The design is meant to go well with the existing structure — to complete or enhance it. "Complement" means something that completes or goes well with something else.

**Why others are wrong:**
- A ("compliment"): "Compliment" means to praise. You can't "praise" the existing structure architecturally.
- C ("comply"): "Comply" means to follow rules — doesn't fit the context.
- D ("complete"): Close but not quite right — "complete the existing structure" suggests it was unfinished. "Complement" better captures the idea of harmonious addition.

---

### Problem 6 — Answer: B ("than")

**Why B is correct:** The sentence makes a comparison: students who read perform better ____ those who don't. "Than" is the word used for comparisons.

**Why others are wrong:**
- A ("then"): "Then" refers to time or sequence, not comparison.
- C ("from"): "Different from" is an idiom, but "better from" is not.
- D ("as"): "Better as" is not a comparison structure. The correct structure with "as" would be "as good as," not "better as."
