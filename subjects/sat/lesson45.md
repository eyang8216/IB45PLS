# Lesson 45: Command of Evidence (Textual)

## What You'll Learn
Command of Evidence questions come in pairs: one asks for a claim or conclusion, the next asks which lines best support your answer. You'll learn the "work backwards" strategy that turns these high-stakes question pairs into a systematic process.

## SAT Context
Evidence pairs appear 2-4 times per module. Each pair is worth two questions. They're among the most missed question types because students answer the first question independently and then scramble to find evidence that matches — often rationalizing a quote that doesn't truly support their choice. The fix: find the evidence FIRST.

---

### The Evidence Pair Structure

A textual evidence pair consists of:

**Question N:** A claim or conclusion question (about character, argument, purpose, etc.)
**Question N+1:** "Which choice provides the best evidence for the answer to the previous question?"

The answer choices for QN+1 are line references (or short quotes) from the passage. Four options, each citing a different part of the text.

**The key insight:** Only ONE answer to QN is supported by ONE of the four evidence options. The three wrong claims won't have ANY evidence among the four options — or the evidence that exists will actually point to a different answer.

---

### The Work-Backwards Strategy

Instead of answering QN first and then hunting for evidence, REVERSE the process:

**Step 1: Read QN+1 (the evidence question) FIRST.**
Note the four line references or quotes. These are your anchor points.

**Step 2: Go to the passage and read each cited section.**
Understand what each piece of text is actually saying. Summarize each in a few words.

**Step 3: For each evidence option, ask: "What claim could this piece of text support?"**
This generates possible answers to QN.

**Step 4: NOW read QN.**
Match the correct claim to the evidence you've analyzed. Only one pairing will work.

**Step 5: Select both answers.**
The claim (QN) and the evidence (QN+1) must align perfectly.

> **🔍 PATTERN RECOGNITION:** Students who work backwards (evidence first, claim second) score about 30% higher on evidence pairs than students who answer the claim question first. The evidence options GUIDE you to the correct claim.

---

### The "Must Support EXACTLY" Rule

The chosen evidence must directly and specifically support the claim in QN. Not tangentially. Not "it's related to the same topic." EXACTLY.

**Example:**
QN: "The passage suggests that the author's childhood was marked by financial hardship."
QN+1 options: Four line references.

If one option says "We lived in a small house, but it was filled with love," that does NOT directly support "financial hardship." A small house doesn't necessarily mean poverty.

If another option says "My mother counted coins at the grocery store and put back the milk when the total ran too high," THAT supports financial hardship.

> **🚨 SAT TRAP ALERT:** Students often pick evidence that's ABOUT the same topic as the claim but doesn't actually PROVE it. "Related to" ≠ "evidence for." The evidence must make the claim MORE LIKELY to be true.

---

### Testing the Evidence-Answer Connection

For any evidence pair you're considering, perform this test:

Read the claim (QN answer) aloud, then say "BECAUSE," then read the evidence (QN+1 answer).

**"The author felt isolated as a child BECAUSE [lines 23-26] she ate lunch alone in the library every day for three years."**

If the "because" connection makes logical sense, you've found the right pair. If it feels forced or requires additional assumptions, keep looking.

---

### Evidence That Supports the OPPOSITE

Sometimes one of the evidence options actually supports the OPPOSITE of what seems like the "obvious" claim. The SAT uses this to catch students who skim.

**Example:**
Passage about a new study questioning the effectiveness of a popular drug.

If you skim, you might think: "The passage says the drug is effective."
But the evidence option says: "The study found no statistically significant difference between the drug and the placebo."

This evidence actually supports "the drug may NOT be effective," not "the drug is effective."

Always read the cited lines precisely. Don't let your assumptions override the actual text.

---

### Evidence That Supports Multiple Claims (Sort Of)

Sometimes one piece of evidence COULD support more than one claim at a surface level. The SAT designs the answer choices so that only ONE claim has exact, unambiguous support.

**Strategy:**
- For each QN answer choice, check ALL FOUR evidence options.
- Only one QN answer will have a PERFECT match among the evidence.
- The other QN answers will either have NO evidence match, or the "match" will be a stretch.

> **🚨 SAT TRAP ALERT:** A common wrong answer on QN+1 is a quote that uses keywords from the QN answer choice but actually means something different in context. Always read the full context of the cited lines, not just the words that overlap with the claim.

---

### Worked Example 1: Full Evidence Pair

**Passage excerpt:**
[Lines 1-5] The Amazon rainforest has been called the "lungs of the Earth," but this metaphor, while poetic, is imprecise. [Lines 6-10] Lungs consume oxygen and release carbon dioxide, whereas the Amazon does the reverse through photosynthesis. [Lines 11-15] A better comparison might be to an air conditioner: the rainforest cools the planet by absorbing carbon dioxide and releasing water vapor. [Lines 16-20] This water vapor forms clouds that reflect sunlight back into space. [Lines 21-25] The Amazon, therefore, functions less as the planet's breathing organ and more as its thermostat.

**Q1:** The author's central claim about the Amazon rainforest is that:
A) it is vital for producing the world's oxygen supply
B) the "lungs of the Earth" metaphor is more poetic than accurate
C) the "lungs" metaphor, while common, misrepresents the Amazon's climate-regulating function
D) the Amazon should be protected from deforestation at all costs

**Q2:** Which choice provides the best evidence for the answer to the previous question?
A) Lines 1-5 ("The Amazon...imprecise")
B) Lines 6-10 ("Lungs consume...photosynthesis")
C) Lines 21-25 ("The Amazon...thermostat")
D) Lines 16-20 ("This water vapor...space")

**Answers: Q1 = C, Q2 = C**

**Explanation (work backwards):**
- Evidence A (lines 1-5): Introduces the metaphor and calls it "imprecise." Could support a claim that the metaphor is flawed.
- Evidence B (lines 6-10): Explains WHY the metaphor is imprecise (lungs vs. photosynthesis). Supports the same.
- Evidence C (lines 21-25): States the conclusion: "functions less as...breathing organ and more as its thermostat." This is the thesis.
- Evidence D (lines 16-20): A detail about water vapor forming clouds. Evidence for a mechanism, not a central claim.

Now Q1: Option C captures the full claim: the metaphor is imprecise (A provides evidence) AND the real function is climate regulation (C provides evidence for the thermostat part). Evidence C is the strongest because it's the concluding thesis. Option A is not argued — the passage doesn't say the Amazon is vital for oxygen. Option B is partially right but misses the thermostat alternative. Option D goes beyond the passage.

### Worked Example 2

**Passage excerpt:**
[Lines 30-35] Critics of the new tax policy argue it disproportionately benefits the wealthy. [Lines 36-42] Their analysis points to data showing that 62% of the tax savings accrue to households earning over $200,000 annually. [Lines 43-48] Proponents counter that the policy stimulates investment, which creates jobs that benefit workers at all income levels. [Lines 49-54] However, the historical record of similar policies suggests that such "trickle-down" effects are weaker than proponents claim. [Lines 55-60] Studies of tax cuts in 2001 and 2003 found minimal wage growth for middle and lower-income workers in the five years following implementation.

**Q1:** Based on the passage, the author would most likely agree that:
A) the tax policy will clearly benefit all income levels equally
B) critics' concerns about the policy are supported by distributional data
C) proponents' job-creation arguments are well-supported by historical evidence
D) tax policy has no effect on economic growth

**Q2:** Which choice provides the best evidence for the answer to the previous question?
A) Lines 36-42 ("Their analysis...annually")
B) Lines 43-48 ("Proponents counter...levels")
C) Lines 49-54 ("However...claim")
D) Lines 55-60 ("Studies of tax cuts...implementation")

**Answers: Q1 = B, Q2 = A**

**Explanation (work backwards):**
- Evidence A (36-42): Shows critics have data — 62% to wealthy. Supports critics' position.
- Evidence B (43-48): States proponents' argument. Could support a claim about what proponents say.
- Evidence C (49-54): The author's skepticism about trickle-down (via "However"). Supports author's doubt.
- Evidence D (55-60): Historical evidence weakening proponents' claims. Supports author's skepticism.

Now Q1: The question asks what the AUTHOR would agree with. The author presents critics' data (A) and then casts doubt on proponents (C, D). Option B aligns: critics have data on their side, and the author seems to find their concerns valid. Option A contradicts the passage. Option C is the opposite — the author questions proponents. Option D is too extreme.

## Practice Problems

*Questions 1-2 are based on the following passage.*

[Lines 1-6] The Turing test, proposed by Alan Turing in 1950, evaluates a machine's ability to exhibit intelligent behavior indistinguishable from a human's. [Lines 7-12] In the test, a human judge converses with both a machine and another human through text, then attempts to identify which is which. [Lines 13-18] For decades, passing the Turing test was considered a benchmark achievement in artificial intelligence. [Lines 19-25] However, many AI researchers now argue that the Turing test is a poor measure of intelligence. [Lines 26-32] They point out that chatbots can fool humans with pre-programmed conversational tricks without demonstrating actual understanding or reasoning ability.

### Problem 1
The passage suggests that the Turing test:
A) remains the best available measure of machine intelligence
B) is widely considered outdated by contemporary AI researchers
C) has been conclusively passed by several AI systems
D) was proposed by multiple researchers working independently

### Problem 2
Which choice provides the best evidence for the answer to the previous question?
A) Lines 1-6 ("The Turing test...human's")
B) Lines 13-18 ("For decades...intelligence")
C) Lines 19-25 ("However...intelligence")
D) Lines 26-32 ("They point out...ability")

*Questions 3-4 are based on the following passage.*

[Lines 40-45] The Industrial Revolution, while driving unprecedented economic growth, imposed severe costs on the working class. [Lines 46-52] Factory workers, including children as young as six, routinely labored for 14 to 16 hours per day in dangerous conditions. [Lines 53-58] The air in textile mills was thick with cotton dust, causing widespread respiratory disease among workers. [Lines 59-65] Despite these conditions, early labor organizers faced fierce resistance from factory owners and, in many cases, from governments that deployed police and military forces to break strikes.

### Problem 3
Based on the passage, which statement about the Industrial Revolution is true?
A) It improved working conditions for most factory employees.
B) Child labor was uncommon during this period.
C) Workers faced both hazardous conditions and opposition when attempting to organize.
D) The British government actively supported labor unions.

### Problem 4
Which choice provides the best evidence for the answer to the previous question?
A) Lines 40-45 ("The Industrial...class")
B) Lines 46-52 ("Factory workers...conditions")
C) Lines 53-58 ("The air...workers")
D) Lines 59-65 ("Despite these...strikes")

*Questions 5-6 are based on the following passage.*

[Lines 1-5] The reintroduction of gray wolves to Yellowstone National Park stands as one of the most celebrated ecological restoration projects in history. [Lines 6-10] After wolves were eliminated from the park in the 1920s, elk populations exploded, leading to overgrazing that devastated streamside vegetation. [Lines 11-16] When wolves were reintroduced in 1995, they predictably reduced elk numbers, but the downstream effects surprised even the biologists who advocated for the project. [Lines 17-22] As elk avoided areas where they were vulnerable to wolf predation, willow and aspen trees regrew along stream banks. [Lines 23-28] The returning vegetation stabilized the banks, reducing erosion and creating habitat for beavers, whose dams further transformed the landscape. [Lines 29-33] This cascade of ecological effects demonstrated that apex predators play a far more complex role in ecosystems than previously understood.

### Problem 5
The passage indicates that wolf reintroduction had effects that:
A) were limited to reducing the elk population
B) exactly matched what biologists had predicted
C) extended far beyond elk reduction, transforming the entire ecosystem
D) primarily harmed the park's biodiversity

### Problem 6
Which choice provides the best evidence for the answer to the previous question?
A) Lines 11-16 ("When wolves...project")
B) Lines 17-22 ("As elk avoided...banks")
C) Lines 23-28 ("The returning...landscape")
D) Lines 29-33 ("This cascade...understood")

---

## Answers

### Problem 1
**Answer: B**
**Explanation:** The passage states that "many AI researchers now argue that the Turing test is a poor measure of intelligence" and that it can be fooled by "pre-programmed conversational tricks." This supports the idea that it's considered outdated. Option A contradicts the passage. Option C is not stated. Option D contradicts — Turing is the only proposer mentioned.

### Problem 2
**Answer: C**
**Explanation:** Lines 19-25 contain "However, many AI researchers now argue that the Turing test is a poor measure of intelligence" — this is the direct evidence that researchers consider it outdated. Option A just defines the test. Option B describes its historical status. Option D explains WHY it's considered poor, which supports C but C is more directly about researchers' current views.

### Problem 3
**Answer: C**
**Explanation:** The passage describes hazardous conditions (14-16 hour days, dangerous conditions, respiratory disease) AND opposition to organizing (fierce resistance, police and military breaking strikes). Option A is the opposite. Option B contradicts — child labor is explicitly mentioned. Option D is the opposite — governments deployed forces against strikers.

### Problem 4
**Answer: D**
**Explanation:** Lines 59-65 contain BOTH pieces: "Despite these conditions" references the hazards, and "early labor organizers faced fierce resistance...governments...deployed police and military forces to break strikes" shows the opposition. This is the best single evidence block covering both elements. Options B and C each cover hazards but not the organizing resistance.

### Problem 5
**Answer: C**
**Explanation:** The passage describes a "cascade" going from wolves → fewer elk → regrown vegetation → stabilized banks → beavers → transformed landscape. The effects were "surprising" and more complex than "previously understood." Option A is too narrow. Option B contradicts — effects "surprised even the biologists." Option D is the opposite — it restored biodiversity.

### Problem 6
**Answer: D**
**Explanation:** Lines 29-33 state "This cascade of ecological effects demonstrated that apex predators play a far more complex role in ecosystems than previously understood." This directly supports the claim that effects extended far beyond elk reduction. Options B and C each describe a step in the cascade but don't capture the broader conclusion. Option A mentions surprise but not the full scope.

---

**Quick Recap:**
- Work backwards: read the evidence options first, then answer the claim question
- The evidence must support the EXACT claim, not just a related idea
- Test: "The claim is true BECAUSE [evidence]" — must make logical sense
- Read cited lines in full context, not just the highlighted words
- Only one claim will have perfect evidence support among the four options
