Lesson 31: Comma Usage — Complete Guide

What You'll Learn

The comma is the most frequently used — and most frequently misused — punctuation mark in English. The SAT tests comma usage extensively, and the rules aren't about "pausing" or "taking a breath." They're about grammatical structure. By the end of this lesson, you'll know exactly when a comma is required, when it's forbidden, and when it's optional. Master the comma, and you'll gain points on 5-8 questions per SAT.

SAT Context

Comma questions dominate the Standard English Conventions domain. They appear in list punctuation, joining clauses, setting off nonrestrictive elements, and separating introductory phrases. The SAT also loves to test comma splices — one of the most common errors. Expect 5-8 comma-related questions on every SAT.

Rule 1: Commas in Lists

Use commas to separate three or more items in a series:

RIGHT: I bought apples, bananas, and oranges.

RIGHT: I bought apples, bananas and oranges. (Oxford comma omitted — both are acceptable on the SAT)

The Oxford comma (the comma before "and" in a list) is optional on the SAT, but you MUST be consistent. If the passage uses Oxford commas, don't remove one. If it doesn't use them, don't add one.

WRONG: I bought apples, bananas, and, oranges. (only one comma before "and")

WRONG: I bought apples bananas, and oranges. (missing comma between first two items)

When List Items Already Contain Commas

Use semicolons instead (see Lesson 32), but this is rarely tested on the SAT.

Rule 2: FANBOYS — Comma Before Coordinating Conjunctions

When joining two independent clauses (complete sentences that can stand alone), use a comma BEFORE the coordinating conjunction:

FANBOYS: For, And, Nor, But, Or, Yet, So

RIGHT: I wanted to go to the concert, but I had to study.

(Both "I wanted to go to the concert" and "I had to study" are complete sentences.)

RIGHT: She studied for weeks, so she felt confident on test day.

WRONG: I wanted to go to the concert but I had to study.

(Missing comma before "but" joining two independent clauses.)

> 🚨 SAT TRAP ALERT: The SAT will show you "and" or "but" joining what LOOKS like two complete sentences — but one side might not actually be a complete thought. If what follows a FANBOYS word is NOT an independent clause (no subject + verb), do NOT use a comma.

RIGHT (no comma): I wanted to go to the concert but had to study.

("Had to study" has no subject — it shares "I" from the first clause. Not an independent clause → no comma.)

RIGHT (comma): I wanted to go to the concert, but I had to study.

("I had to study" has its own subject "I" — it's an independent clause → comma needed.)

> 🔍 PATTERN RECOGNITION: The test: look on both sides of the FANBOYS word. If BOTH sides have their own subject and verb (and can stand alone as sentences), you NEED the comma. If the second side shares the subject from the first, you DON'T use the comma.

Rule 3: Nonrestrictive vs. Restrictive Clauses

Nonrestrictive (Use Commas)

A nonrestrictive clause adds extra, non-essential information. If you remove it, the sentence still makes complete sense and the meaning of the main noun doesn't change. Set it off with commas.

  • My brother, who lives in Boston, is a doctor.
  • "Who lives in Boston" is extra info. I (presumably) have only one brother. Remove it: "My brother is a doctor" — still makes sense.
  • The Eiffel Tower, which was built in 1889, is a global icon.
  • "Which was built in 1889" is extra. There's only one Eiffel Tower.

Restrictive (NO Commas)

A restrictive clause is essential to identify which person or thing you mean. If you remove it, the meaning changes or becomes unclear. Do NOT use commas.

  • The person who called me was my mother.
  • "Who called me" identifies WHICH person. Without it: "The person was my mother" — which person? Unclear. No commas.
  • Students who study regularly perform better on exams.
  • "Who study regularly" specifies which students. Not all students — only those who study. No commas.

The Which/That Rule (SAT Favorite!)

  • Which = usually nonrestrictive (commas, extra info)
  • That = restrictive (no commas, essential info)

RIGHT: The book, which I bought yesterday, is fascinating. (extra info, commas)

RIGHT: The book that I bought yesterday is fascinating. (identifies which book, no commas)

However, "which" CAN be used restrictively in British English. On the SAT, "which" with commas and "that" without commas is the safest pattern, but don't assume "which" always needs commas.

> 🚨 SAT TRAP ALERT: The SAT loves to test restrictive vs. nonrestrictive with people's names and unique entities. "My mother, who lives in Florida" — commas because you only have one mother. "The student who scored highest" — no commas because it identifies which student out of many.

Rule 4: Commas After Introductory Phrases

Use a comma after an introductory clause, phrase, or word that comes before the main subject:

RIGHT: After the movie ended, we went out for dinner.

RIGHT: Having finished her homework, Sarah went to bed.

RIGHT: In the middle of the night, the phone rang.

RIGHT: However, the results were inconclusive.

RIGHT: In 2020, the pandemic changed everything.

When the Introductory Element Is Short

For very short introductory elements (2-3 words), the comma is sometimes optional:

  • RIGHT: In the morning I like to jog.
  • RIGHT: In the morning, I like to jog.

Both are acceptable on the SAT.

Rule 5: Appositives

An appositive is a noun or noun phrase that renames another noun right next to it. If the appositive is nonrestrictive (provides extra information about a noun that is already identified), wrap it in commas:

RIGHT: Barack Obama, the 44th president, was born in Hawaii.

RIGHT: The novel *Beloved*, a masterpiece by Toni Morrison, won the Pulitzer Prize.

If the appositive is restrictive (essential to identify the noun), no commas:

RIGHT: My friend John is a lawyer. (I have multiple friends; "John" identifies which one.)

RIGHT: The artist Monet painted many water lilies. (Identifies which artist.)

Rule 6: Commas With Transitional Words

Set off transitional words and phrases with commas:

RIGHT: The evidence, therefore, supports the hypothesis.

RIGHT: Furthermore, the study has been replicated.

RIGHT: The results, however, were surprising.

Rule 7: Where NOT to Use Commas

Never Between Subject and Verb

WRONG: The student who studies hardest, usually succeeds.

RIGHT: The student who studies hardest usually succeeds.

Never Between Verb and Object

WRONG: She enjoys, swimming, running, and biking.

RIGHT: She enjoys swimming, running, and biking.

Never Before a Prepositional Phrase (Unless It's Nonrestrictive)

WRONG: The book, on the table is mine.

RIGHT: The book on the table is mine.

The Comma Splice — SAT's #1 Trap

A comma splice occurs when two independent clauses are joined with ONLY a comma (no FANBOYS word). This is one of the most commonly tested errors on the SAT.

WRONG: I love pizza, it is delicious.

(Two complete sentences joined by just a comma.)

WRONG: The experiment was a success, the results were published immediately.

Four Ways to Fix a Comma Splice:

  • Period: I love pizza. It is delicious.
  • Semicolon: I love pizza; it is delicious.
  • Comma + FANBOYS: I love pizza, and it is delicious.
  • Subordinating conjunction: Because I love pizza, I eat it often.

    • > 🚨 SAT TRAP ALERT: The SAT creates comma splices that are easy to miss because the two clauses feel closely related. Don't rely on "how it sounds." Always check: can each side stand alone as a complete sentence? If yes, a comma alone CANNOT join them.

    Worked Example 1

    Question: The Nobel Prize-winning author __________ gave a lecture at the university last night.

    A) who writes about social justice,

    B) who writes about social justice

    C) , who writes about social justice,

    D) , who writes about social justice

    Answer: C — ", who writes about social justice,"

    Explanation: The phrase "who writes about social justice" is nonrestrictive — it provides extra information about an author who is already identified (by being a Nobel Prize winner). Nonrestrictive clauses must be set off with commas on BOTH sides. The clause is inserted between the subject ("The Nobel Prize-winning author") and the verb ("gave"), so commas are needed both before and after.

    Worked Example 2

    Question: The team worked overtime to meet the deadline __________ they were still unable to complete the project on time.

    A) , but

    B) but

    C) , and

    D) and

    Answer: A — ", but"

    Explanation: There are two independent clauses: "The team worked overtime to meet the deadline" and "they were still unable to complete the project on time." When joining two independent clauses with a coordinating conjunction (FANBOYS), you need a comma before the conjunction. "But" is correct here because it shows contrast.

    Worked Example 3

    Question: The musician played three instruments during the concert: the piano, the guitar __________.

    A) , and the violin

    B) and the violin

    C) , and, the violin

    D) and, the violin

    Answer: A — ", and the violin" (with Oxford comma) or B — "and the violin" (without)

    Explanation: Both are acceptable on the SAT. The list has three items: "the piano," "the guitar," and "the violin." The comma before "and" (Oxford comma) is optional. Option C has an extra comma after "and," which is wrong. Option D has the comma in the wrong place. In context, the SAT passage's existing style would determine which is correct.

    Practice Problems

    Problem 1

    The scientist __________ discovered the new element received international recognition for her groundbreaking work.

    A) who

    B) , who

    C) who,

    D) , who,

    Problem 2

    The conference was scheduled for July, __________ many attendees were unable to participate due to prior commitments.

    A) but

    B) , but

    C) however

    D) , however,

    Problem 3

    Walking through the ancient ruins, __________.

    A) the history of the civilization came alive for the tourists

    B) the tourists felt the history of the civilization come alive

    C) the civilization's history felt alive to the tourists

    D) the feeling of history coming alive was experienced by the tourists

    Problem 4

    My favorite author Toni Morrison wrote extensively about the African American experience, and her work continues to inspire readers worldwide.

    A) NO CHANGE

    B) My favorite author, Toni Morrison wrote

    C) My favorite author, Toni Morrison, wrote

    D) My favorite author Toni Morrison, wrote

    Problem 5

    The company announced record profits for the third quarter, the stock price surged immediately after the announcement.

    A) NO CHANGE

    B) quarter; the stock price

    C) quarter, and the stock price

    D) both B and C are correct

    Problem 6

    The research paper which was published in a prestigious journal has been cited over a thousand times.

    A) NO CHANGE

    B) paper, which was published in a prestigious journal, has

    C) paper which, was published in a prestigious journal, has

    D) paper, which was published in a prestigious journal has

    Problem 7

    After graduating from college __________ she moved to New York to pursue a career in finance.

    A) ,

    B) ;

    C) .

    D) no punctuation needed

    Problem 8

    Students, who complete the optional review sessions, tend to perform better on the final exam than those who do not.

    A) NO CHANGE

    B) Students who complete the optional review sessions tend

    C) Students, who complete the optional review sessions tend

    D) Students who complete the optional review sessions, tend

    Answers

    Problem 1 — Answer: A ("who")

    Why A is correct: "Who discovered the new element" is a restrictive clause — it identifies WHICH scientist we're talking about. Without it: "The scientist received international recognition" — which scientist? Unclear. Restrictive clauses do NOT get commas.

    Why others are wrong:

    • B (", who"): Adding a comma before "who" makes it nonrestrictive (suggesting the scientist is already identified by something else), which is wrong here.
    • C ("who,"): A single comma after "who" is never correct.
    • D (", who,"): Two commas make it nonrestrictive, but this clause is essential to identify the scientist.

    Problem 2 — Answer: B (", but")

    Why B is correct: Two independent clauses are being joined: "The conference was scheduled for July" and "many attendees were unable to participate due to prior commitments." Joining two independent clauses requires a comma + FANBOYS conjunction. "But" shows the contrast.

    Why others are wrong:

    • A ("but"): Missing the required comma before the conjunction joining independent clauses.
    • C ("however"): "However" is not a FANBOYS conjunction — using it alone creates a comma splice.
    • D (", however,"): "However" with commas on both sides still doesn't fix the comma splice; "however" is a transitional adverb, not a conjunction.

    Problem 3 — Answer: B

    Why B is correct: "Walking through the ancient ruins" is an introductory participial phrase. The word after the comma must be the thing doing the walking — "the tourists." Option B correctly places "the tourists" right after the comma. (See also Lesson 29 on modifiers.)

    Why others are wrong:

    • A: "The history" wasn't walking through the ruins.
    • C: "The civilization's history" wasn't walking.
    • D: "The feeling" wasn't walking.

    Problem 4 — Answer: C

    Why C is correct: "Toni Morrison" is an appositive renaming "My favorite author." Since you can only have one "favorite author," the name is nonrestrictive — it provides extra information. Nonrestrictive appositives must be set off with commas on BOTH sides.

    Why others are wrong:

    • A: Missing commas around the appositive "Toni Morrison."
    • B: Only has an opening comma but missing the closing comma.
    • D: Only has a comma after "Toni Morrison" but missing the opening comma.

    Problem 5 — Answer: D ("both B and C are correct")

    Why D is correct: The original sentence is a comma splice — two independent clauses joined by only a comma. Both B and C fix this correctly: B uses a semicolon to join the two independent clauses, and C uses a comma + FANBOYS ("and"). Both are grammatically correct solutions.

    Why others are wrong:

    • A: Comma splice — can't join independent clauses with only a comma.

    Problem 6 — Answer: B

    Why B is correct: The clause "which was published in a prestigious journal" provides extra, non-essential information about the research paper. (We already know which paper — "the research paper.") Nonrestrictive clauses must be set off with commas on both sides.

    Why others are wrong:

    • A: Missing commas around the nonrestrictive clause.
    • C: Comma is in the wrong place (after "which" instead of before it).
    • D: Missing the closing comma after "journal."

    Problem 7 — Answer: A (",")

    Why A is correct: "After graduating from college" is an introductory dependent clause. Introductory phrases and clauses should be followed by a comma before the main clause begins.

    Why others are wrong:

    • B (";"): A semicolon is used between two independent clauses. "After graduating from college" is not an independent clause — it can't stand alone.
    • C ("."): A period would create a sentence fragment: "After graduating from college." is incomplete.
    • D (no punctuation): While very short introductory elements can sometimes omit the comma, "After graduating from college" is long enough to require one. On the SAT, the comma after an introductory dependent clause is expected.

    Problem 8 — Answer: B

    Why B is correct: "Who complete the optional review sessions" is a restrictive clause — it identifies WHICH students tend to perform better (only those who attend review sessions). Without it, the meaning changes to "all students tend to perform better." Restrictive clauses must NOT have commas.

    Why others are wrong:

    • A: Commas make the clause nonrestrictive, implying ALL students complete the review sessions — which changes the meaning.
    • C: Single comma after "Students" — a comma here would need a matching comma after "sessions" to set off a nonrestrictive clause.
    • D: Single comma after "sessions" — no matching opening comma, and even if there were, the clause should be restrictive.
