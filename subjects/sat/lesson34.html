Lesson 34: Sentence Boundaries (Fragments, Run-ons, Comma Splices)

What You'll Learn

Every sentence in formal English must have exactly one independent clause — no more, no less — unless properly joined. When sentences have less than one complete thought, you get a fragment. When they have more than one joined incorrectly, you get a run-on or comma splice. This lesson teaches you to identify all three boundary errors and fix them with confidence. These are the most fundamental grammar skills on the SAT; master these, and you'll be equipped to tackle a wide range of questions.

SAT Context

Sentence boundary questions are among the most important on the SAT. They appear in the Standard English Conventions domain and test your ability to recognize fragments, fix run-ons, and eliminate comma splices. Combined with comma and semicolon questions, these form the backbone of the punctuation/conventions section. Expect 3-5 sentence boundary questions per test.

What Makes a Complete Sentence?

A complete sentence (independent clause) must have:

  • **A subject** (who or what does the action)
  • **A verb** (the action or state of being)
  • **A complete thought** (can stand alone and make sense)

    • **COMPLETE:** The student studied.

    • Subject: "student" ✓ / Verb: "studied" ✓ / Complete thought: ✓

    **INCOMPLETE:** Because the student studied.

    • Subject: "student" ✓ / Verb: "studied" ✓ / Complete thought: ✗ (starts with "because" — leaves you hanging)

    **INCOMPLETE:** The student who studied all night.

    • Subject: "student" ✓ / Verb: "studied" ✓ / Complete thought: ✗ (what about the student? No main verb for "student.")

    Error Type 1: Sentence Fragments

    A fragment is an incomplete sentence — it's missing a subject, verb, or complete thought, but is punctuated as if it were a full sentence.

    Dependent Clause Alone (Most Common Fragment)

    A dependent clause starts with a subordinating conjunction (*although, because, since, while, if, when, unless, after, before, until, as, whereas, even though*) or a relative pronoun (*who, which, that*). It has a subject and verb but can't stand alone.

    **FRAGMENT:** Although she studied all night.

    **FIXED:** Although she studied all night, she failed the test.

    **FIXED:** She studied all night. (Remove "although")

    **FRAGMENT:** Because the experiment was flawed.

    **FIXED:** Because the experiment was flawed, the results were discarded.

    **FRAGMENT:** While walking to school.

    **FIXED:** While walking to school, I saw a rainbow.

    Missing Subject or Verb

    **FRAGMENT:** Hoping to arrive before sunset.

    (No main subject or verb — just a participial phrase.)

    **FIXED:** Hoping to arrive before sunset, we drove faster.

    **FRAGMENT:** The author of several best-selling novels and a renowned public speaker.

    (Subject "the author" has no verb — just a long description.)

    **FIXED:** The author of several best-selling novels and a renowned public speaker **gave** a lecture.

    > **🚨 SAT TRAP ALERT:** The SAT creates fragments that are LONG — stuffed with descriptive phrases so they *look* like sentences. Don't be fooled by length! A 30-word fragment is still a fragment. Always check: does this group of words have a subject, a main verb, and a complete thought?

    > **🔍 PATTERN RECOGNITION:** To test for a fragment, read the "sentence" aloud and ask: "Can this stand alone? Does it feel complete?" If you're waiting for more information, it's likely a fragment.

    Error Type 2: Run-on Sentences

    A run-on (or fused sentence) occurs when two or more independent clauses are joined with **no punctuation at all**.

    **RUN-ON:** I love pizza it is delicious.

    **FIXED:** I love pizza. It is delicious. (period)

    **FIXED:** I love pizza; it is delicious. (semicolon)

    **FIXED:** I love pizza, and it is delicious. (comma + FANBOYS)

    **FIXED:** I love pizza because it is delicious. (subordinating conjunction)

    **RUN-ON:** The experiment was a success the researchers published their findings immediately.

    **RUN-ON:** She applied to six colleges she was accepted to four of them.

    Error Type 3: Comma Splices

    A comma splice is a run-on where the two independent clauses are joined by **only a comma** — no FANBOYS conjunction.

    **COMMA SPLICE:** I love pizza, it is delicious.

    **COMMA SPLICE:** The concert was sold out, we couldn't get tickets.

    A comma splice is just a run-on with a comma in the middle. Same fixes apply.

    > **🚨 SAT TRAP ALERT:** Comma splices can be hard to spot when the two clauses feel closely related: "The weather was perfect, the view was spectacular." This FEELS like a list, but it's two independent clauses. Check: can each side stand alone as a sentence? If yes, a comma alone cannot join them.

    Four Ways to Fix Boundary Errors

    Given two independent clauses (IC), here are your four options:

    Option 1: Period → Two Sentences

    **IC. IC.**

    • I love pizza. It is delicious.

    Option 2: Semicolon → One Sentence

    **IC; IC.**

    • I love pizza; it is delicious.

    Option 3: Comma + FANBOYS

    **IC, FANBOYS IC.**

    • I love pizza, and it is delicious.
    • I love pizza, but I don't eat it every day.

    Option 4: Subordinating Conjunction

    **Subordinating Conjunction + IC, IC.** or **IC + Subordinating Conjunction + IC.**

    • Because I love pizza, I eat it often.
    • I eat pizza often because I love it.

    Common Subordinating Conjunctions

    Memorize these — they turn independent clauses into dependent clauses:

    | Common | Less Common |

    |--------|-------------|

    | after | as long as |

    | although | as soon as |

    | as | even though |

    | because | in order that |

    | before | now that |

    | if | provided that |

    | since | so that |

    | unless | though |

    | until | whereas |

    | when | wherever |

    | while | |

    Worked Example 1

    **Question:** The company's profits increased significantly last quarter, the CEO attributed the growth to the new marketing strategy.

    A) NO CHANGE

    B) quarter; the CEO

    C) quarter the CEO

    D) quarter, and the CEO

    **Answer:** B or D would both be correct.

    **Explanation:** The original sentence is a comma splice — two independent clauses ("The company's profits increased significantly last quarter" and "the CEO attributed the growth to the new marketing strategy") joined by only a comma. Both B (semicolon) and D (comma + FANBOYS "and") correctly fix the error. Option C is a run-on (fused sentence). On the SAT, only one correct answer would be presented among the choices.

    Worked Example 2

    **Question:** Although the film received mixed reviews from critics __________.

    A) it became a commercial success

    B) , it became a commercial success

    C) ; it became a commercial success

    D) and it became a commercial success

    **Answer:** B — ", it became a commercial success"

    **Explanation:** "Although the film received mixed reviews from critics" is a dependent clause. The main clause "it became a commercial success" follows. A comma separates the introductory dependent clause from the main clause. Options A and D would create run-ons/fragments. Option C is wrong because a semicolon can only join two independent clauses, and the first part is dependent.

    Worked Example 3

    **Question:** The researcher who conducted the groundbreaking study on memory retention __________ received the prestigious award.

    A) , she

    B) . She

    C) ;

    D)

    **Answer:** D — no punctuation needed

    **Explanation:** "The researcher who conducted the groundbreaking study on memory retention" is the subject. "Received the prestigious award" is the predicate (verb + object). There's only one independent clause here, and the subject should not be separated from its verb by a period, semicolon, or comma. "The researcher...received" — the subject and verb flow together. "Who conducted the groundbreaking study on memory retention" is a restrictive relative clause modifying "researcher."

    Practice Problems

    Problem 1

    The symposium featured presentations by scholars from around the world, the keynote address was delivered by a Nobel laureate.

    A) NO CHANGE

    B) world; the keynote address

    C) world the keynote address

    D) world: the keynote address

    Problem 2

    Because the city implemented a comprehensive recycling program, landfill waste decreased by nearly forty percent.

    A) NO CHANGE

    B) program; landfill waste

    C) program landfill waste

    D) program. Landfill waste

    Problem 3

    The artist's latest exhibition attracting visitors from across the country and generating widespread media attention.

    A) NO CHANGE

    B) exhibition, attracting

    C) exhibition attracted

    D) exhibition; attracting

    Problem 4

    The debate team prepared extensively, they felt confident going into the national tournament.

    A) NO CHANGE

    B) extensively; and they

    C) extensively, and they

    D) extensively they

    Problem 5

    While the initial results of the clinical trial were promising.

    A) NO CHANGE

    B) promising, further

    C) promising; further

    D) promising further

    Problem 6

    The documentary, which explored the effects of climate change on coastal communities and featured interviews with leading environmental scientists.

    A) NO CHANGE

    B) communities, and featured

    C) communities, featured

    D) communities; featured

    Problem 7

    The startup secured major funding from investors it expanded its operations to three new cities within a year.

    A) NO CHANGE

    B) investors, it expanded

    C) investors, and it expanded

    D) investors; but it expanded

    Answers

    Problem 1 — Answer: B ("world; the keynote address")

    **Why B is correct:** The original is a comma splice — two independent clauses joined by only a comma. A semicolon correctly joins them. "The symposium featured presentations by scholars from around the world" and "the keynote address was delivered by a Nobel laureate" are both complete sentences.

    **Why others are wrong:**

    • A: Comma splice — two independent clauses joined by only a comma.
    • C: Run-on — two independent clauses with no punctuation at all.
    • D: Colon — while a colon CAN join independent clauses when the second explains the first, the relationship here is more sequential than explanatory. The semicolon (B) is the better choice.

    Problem 2 — Answer: A ("NO CHANGE")

    **Why A is correct:** This sentence is perfectly correct as written. "Because the city implemented a comprehensive recycling program" is a dependent clause. "Landfill waste decreased by nearly forty percent" is the independent clause. A comma correctly separates the introductory dependent clause from the main clause. This is not a fragment or a run-on — it's a correctly punctuated complex sentence.

    **Why others are wrong:**

    • B: A semicolon would incorrectly join a dependent clause ("Because...program") with an independent clause. Semicolons only join two independent clauses.
    • C: Removing the comma creates a run-on or at least confusing structure.
    • D: A period would create a fragment: "Because the city implemented a comprehensive recycling program." — this cannot stand alone.

    Problem 3 — Answer: C ("exhibition attracted")

    **Why C is correct:** The original is a fragment — there's no main verb for "The artist's latest exhibition." "Attracting" and "generating" are participles, not finite verbs. By changing "attracting" to "attracted" (finite verb), the sentence becomes complete: "The artist's latest exhibition attracted visitors from across the country and generated widespread media attention."

    **Why others are wrong:**

    • A: Fragment — no finite verb. "Attracting" is a participle, not a main verb.
    • B: Adding a comma doesn't fix the fragment — "attracting" is still a participle.
    • D: Semicolon doesn't fix the fragment — "attracting" is still not a main verb.

    Problem 4 — Answer: C ("extensively, and they")

    **Why C is correct:** The original is a comma splice — two independent clauses joined by only a comma. Adding "and" after the comma creates a comma + FANBOYS structure, which correctly joins the two independent clauses.

    **Why others are wrong:**

    • A: Comma splice.
    • B: Semicolon + FANBOYS is incorrect — you use semicolon alone OR comma + FANBOYS, never semicolon + FANBOYS.
    • D: Run-on — two independent clauses with no punctuation.

    Problem 5 — Answer: B ("promising, further")

    **Why B is correct:** The original is a fragment — "While the initial results of the clinical trial were promising" is only a dependent clause. Adding a comma and a main clause ("further research is needed" or similar — the context would complete the sentence) fixes it by attaching the dependent clause to an independent clause.

    (Note: In a real SAT question, the full sentence would be provided. The key is that option B makes the dependent clause introductory to a main clause, which is correct.)

    **Why others are wrong:**

    • A: Fragment — dependent clause standing alone.
    • C: Semicolon can only join independent clauses, and a "while" clause is dependent.
    • D: Without the comma, "promising further research" would be ungrammatical.

    Problem 6 — Answer: C ("communities, featured")

    **Why C is correct:** The original is a fragment. "The documentary" is the subject, but there's no main verb for it — only the relative clause "which explored...and featured..." Removing "and" and making "featured" the main verb (with the "which" clause as a modifier) makes the sentence complete: "The documentary, which explored the effects of climate change on coastal communities, featured interviews with leading environmental scientists."

    **Why others are wrong:**

    • A: Fragment — no main verb for "The documentary."
    • B: Still a fragment — "and featured" is still part of the "which" clause, not a main verb.
    • D: Semicolon would require independent clauses on both sides, which isn't the case here.

    Problem 7 — Answer: C ("investors, and it expanded")

    **Why C is correct:** The original is a run-on — two independent clauses with no punctuation. Adding a comma + "and" correctly joins the two independent clauses: "The startup secured major funding from investors, and it expanded its operations to three new cities within a year."

    **Why others are wrong:**

    • A: Run-on (fused sentence).
    • B: Comma splice — just adding a comma without a FANBOYS word doesn't fix the run-on.
    • D: Semicolon + FANBOYS is incorrect — semicolon replaces the need for FANBOYS, so using both is redundant.
