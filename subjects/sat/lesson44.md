# Lesson 44: Specific Details & Literal Comprehension

## What You'll Learn
Some SAT questions simply ask: "What does the passage say?" These detail questions reward careful, literal reading. You'll learn to locate exact answers in the text, avoid bringing in outside knowledge, and recognize how the SAT paraphrases correct answers.

## SAT Context
Detail questions appear 3-5 times per module. They're often the most straightforward questions on the test — which makes them dangerous. Students rush through them, answer from memory (incorrectly), and lose points they should have secured. Slow down and find the proof.

---

### The Cardinal Rule: Answer Only From the Text

This cannot be overstated. Detail questions test your ability to find and VERIFY information in the passage — not your general knowledge, not your opinions, not what "feels right."

**"According to the passage..."** → The answer must be explicitly stated.
**"The passage indicates..."** → The answer must be in the text, though possibly paraphrased.
**"Based on the passage..."** → The answer must be derivable from text evidence.

> **🚨 SAT TRAP ALERT:** The SAT loves to offer answer choices that are factually true in the real world but never stated in the passage. Your outside knowledge is IRRELEVANT. If it's not in the passage, it's wrong. Period.

---

### The Hunt-and-Verify Method

For every detail question, follow this exact process:

**Step 1: Identify the keyword(s) in the question.**
What specific person, place, concept, event, or date is the question about?

**Step 2: Scan the passage for that keyword.**
Your eyes should move quickly through the text, hunting for the keyword or a close synonym.

**Step 3: Read 2-3 lines before and after the keyword.**
Context matters. The answer is usually in the same sentence or the next one.

**Step 4: Match the passage text to an answer choice.**
The correct answer will be a direct restatement or close paraphrase of what's in the passage.

**Step 5: Verify by eliminating the other three choices.**
Each wrong answer must have a specific, articulable reason it's wrong.

---

### Line References: Your Best Friend

Some detail questions give you line or paragraph references. When they do, USE them.

**"In lines 12-15, the author suggests that..."**

**Strategy:**
1. Go to lines 12-15.
2. Also read lines 10-11 (before) and 16-17 (after) for context.
3. Find the answer that matches those lines.

The SAT isn't trying to trick you — the answer really is in the referenced lines. But you must read them carefully.

---

### Paraphrasing: How Correct Answers Disguise Themselves

The correct answer to a detail question rarely quotes the passage word-for-word. Instead, it PARAPHRASES — expressing the same idea in different language.

**Passage says:** "The species experienced a precipitous decline in population during the 20th century."
**Correct answer says:** "The species' numbers fell sharply in the 1900s."

"Precipitous decline" → "fell sharply"
"during the 20th century" → "in the 1900s"

> **🔍 PATTERN RECOGNITION:** When you find the sentence in the passage that contains the answer, underline it. Then translate it into plain English in your head. Your plain-English version will almost exactly match one of the answer choices.

---

### Common Wrong Answer Patterns for Detail Questions

**1. Plausible but unstated (most common trap):**
The answer "makes sense" or aligns with your knowledge, but the passage never actually says it.

**2. Distorted detail:**
A fact from the passage is slightly altered — a date changed, a name swapped, a relationship reversed.

**3. True in the passage, but for the wrong thing:**
The passage says X about dogs and Y about cats. The answer says X about cats.

**4. Uses passage words but creates a new meaning:**
The answer uses vocabulary from the passage but arranges it to mean something the passage never said.

**5. Too extreme:**
The passage says "some researchers believe" — the answer says "all scientists agree."

---

### When Outside Knowledge Attacks

This is worth repeating: outside knowledge is the enemy on detail questions.

**Example:**
Passage: "Marie Curie was awarded the Nobel Prize in Chemistry in 1911 for her discovery of radium and polonium."

Question: According to the passage, Marie Curie:
A) was the first woman to win a Nobel Prize → TRUE in real life, NOT STATED in the passage. WRONG.
B) won the Nobel Prize in Chemistry in 1911 → STATED in the passage. CORRECT.
C) worked with her husband Pierre on radiation research → TRUE in real life, NOT STATED in the passage. WRONG.
D) discovered radium and polonium → STATED in the passage. WAIT — this is also stated. In this case, both B and D are correct, but the question will be written so only one is right.

The key insight: When two statements are true, go back to the question and the passage and figure out which one the question is actually asking about.

---

### Specific Detail vs. Inference

A common point of confusion: the difference between a detail question and an inference question.

**Detail:** The answer is STATED in the passage. You can point to the exact sentence.
**Inference:** The answer is IMPLIED by the passage. You can't point to a sentence that says it directly, but you can show the logical path.

**Detail question signal phrases:** "According to the passage," "The passage states," "The author notes," "The passage indicates"

**Inference question signal phrases:** "It can be inferred," "The passage suggests," "The author implies," "The passage most strongly suggests"

### Worked Example 1

**Passage excerpt:**
"The Voynich manuscript, housed at Yale University's Beinecke Library, has baffled cryptographers and linguists since its rediscovery in 1912. The 240-page codex, carbon-dated to the early 15th century, is written in an unknown script that no one has successfully deciphered. Its illustrations of unidentifiable plants and astronomical diagrams provide the only clues to its mysterious contents."

**Question:** According to the passage, the Voynich manuscript:
A) was written by a 15th-century monk
B) contains illustrations that may offer clues to its meaning
C) was discovered at Yale University
D) has been partially deciphered by modern cryptographers

**Answer:** B
**Explanation:** The passage states that "illustrations...provide the only clues to its mysterious contents." This is a direct match. Option A is plausible but unstated — no monk is mentioned. Option C distorts the detail — it's "housed" at Yale, not discovered there (it was "rediscovered in 1912"). Option D contradicts the passage — "no one has successfully deciphered" it.

---

### Worked Example 2

**Passage excerpt:**
"Urban heat islands occur when cities replace natural land cover with dense concentrations of pavement, buildings, and other surfaces that absorb and retain heat. This effect can raise urban temperatures by 1.8 to 5.4 degrees Fahrenheit compared to surrounding rural areas during the day, and by as much as 22 degrees at night."

**Question:** The passage indicates that urban heat islands:
A) affect temperatures more during the day than at night
B) can result in nighttime temperatures up to 22 degrees higher than in rural areas
C) are caused primarily by vehicle emissions
D) have been decreasing as cities become more densely built

**Answer:** B
**Explanation:** The passage directly states nighttime temperatures can be elevated "by as much as 22 degrees at night" — B is a close paraphrase. Option A contradicts the passage: the nighttime difference (22°) is much larger than the daytime difference (1.8-5.4°). Option C is plausible outside knowledge but unstated. Option D is the opposite of what the passage describes.

---

### Worked Example 3

**Passage excerpt:**
"The Harlem Renaissance of the 1920s and 1930s marked a flowering of African American literature, music, and visual art. While centered in the Harlem neighborhood of New York City, the movement's influence extended to cities across the United States and even to Paris. Writers like Langston Hughes and Zora Neale Hurston gained national prominence, while jazz musicians such as Duke Ellington and Louis Armstrong achieved international fame."

**Question:** According to the passage, which of the following is true of the Harlem Renaissance?
A) It was limited exclusively to the Harlem neighborhood of New York City.
B) It encompassed multiple art forms, including literature and music.
C) Langston Hughes was primarily a visual artist.
D) It began in Paris before spreading to the United States.

**Answer:** B
**Explanation:** The passage says the movement involved "literature, music, and visual art" — that's multiple art forms. Option A contradicts the passage ("the movement's influence extended to cities across the United States"). Option C miscategorizes Hughes — he's listed among "writers." Option D reverses the direction — influence extended "to Paris," not from it.

## Practice Problems

### Problem 1
"Estuaries — areas where rivers meet the sea — are among the most productive ecosystems on Earth. The mixing of fresh and salt water creates a nutrient-rich environment that supports diverse populations of fish, shellfish, and migratory birds. More than two-thirds of the fish and shellfish consumed by humans spend at least part of their life cycle in estuarine habitats."

According to the passage, estuaries are productive ecosystems primarily because:
A) they are protected from ocean storms by barrier islands
B) the mixing of fresh and salt water creates nutrient-rich conditions
C) they are free from pollution caused by human activity
D) migratory birds introduce nutrients from inland areas

### Problem 2
"The Gothic novel emerged as a distinct literary genre in England during the late 18th century. Horace Walpole's The Castle of Otranto (1764) is widely considered the first Gothic novel, establishing many of the genre's enduring conventions: a medieval setting (often a castle or monastery), supernatural occurrences, a brooding atmosphere of mystery and terror, and a persecuted heroine."

According to the passage, which of the following is a convention of the Gothic novel established by The Castle of Otranto?
A) a contemporary urban setting
B) a comedic tone that undercuts the horror
C) a persecuted heroine
D) a scientific explanation for supernatural events

### Problem 3
"Coral bleaching occurs when corals, stressed by elevated water temperatures, expel the symbiotic algae living in their tissues. These algae, called zooxanthellae, provide corals with both their vibrant colors and up to 90% of their energy through photosynthesis. Without them, the corals turn white and, if conditions do not improve, eventually starve."

The passage indicates that zooxanthellae:
A) cause elevated water temperatures that stress corals
B) provide corals with energy through photosynthesis
C) are a type of coral found in tropical waters
D) are responsible for the structural growth of coral reefs

### Problem 4
"The Antikythera mechanism, recovered from a Roman-era shipwreck in 1901, is an ancient Greek astronomical calculator of extraordinary sophistication. Dating to approximately 100 BCE, the device used a complex system of bronze gears to predict astronomical positions and eclipses. Its technological complexity would not be matched until the development of astronomical clocks in 14th-century Europe, a gap of roughly 1,400 years."

According to the passage, the Antikythera mechanism:
A) was discovered in a Greek temple
B) used bronze gears to perform astronomical calculations
C) was invented in 14th-century Europe
D) could predict weather patterns and ocean currents

### Problem 5
"In 1977, marine scientists exploring the Galápagos Rift made a startling discovery: thriving ecosystems on the deep ocean floor, far beyond the reach of sunlight. These communities, clustered around hydrothermal vents spewing superheated, mineral-rich water, derive their energy not from photosynthesis but from chemosynthesis — bacteria convert chemicals from the vents into organic matter, forming the base of a food chain that supports tube worms, clams, and other unique organisms."

According to the passage, the ecosystems around hydrothermal vents:
A) depend on sunlight for energy
B) were first discovered in 1977 at the Galápagos Rift
C) consist entirely of bacterial life forms
D) are located in shallow coastal waters

### Problem 6
"Morse code, developed by Samuel Morse and Alfred Vail in the 1830s, revolutionized long-distance communication by encoding letters and numbers as sequences of dots and dashes. The code was designed so that the most frequently used letters in English — E and T — received the shortest codes (a single dot and a single dash, respectively), while less common letters received longer sequences."

The passage indicates that in Morse code:
A) all letters were assigned codes of equal length
B) the letter E is represented by a single dot because it is the most frequently used letter
C) Samuel Morse invented the telegraph independently of any collaborators
D) numbers were encoded using a system different from that used for letters

## Answers

### Problem 1
**Answer: B**
**Explanation:** The passage directly states: "The mixing of fresh and salt water creates a nutrient-rich environment" — this is the stated reason estuaries are productive. Option A is plausible but unstated. Option C is not mentioned and likely false in reality. Option D introduces a mechanism (birds introducing nutrients) that the passage never describes.

### Problem 2
**Answer: C**
**Explanation:** The passage lists "a persecuted heroine" among the conventions established by The Castle of Otranto. Option A contradicts the passage, which specifies "a medieval setting." Option B contradicts "a brooding atmosphere of mystery and terror." Option D is the opposite — the passage mentions "supernatural occurrences," not scientific explanations.

### Problem 3
**Answer: B**
**Explanation:** The passage states zooxanthellae "provide corals with both their vibrant colors and up to 90% of their energy through photosynthesis." Option A reverses causality — temperature causes bleaching, not the algae. Option C miscategorizes — they're algae, not coral. Option D is plausible but unstated — the passage doesn't mention structural growth.

### Problem 4
**Answer: B**
**Explanation:** The passage says "the device used a complex system of bronze gears to predict astronomical positions and eclipses." Option B closely paraphrases this. Option A distorts — it was from a "shipwreck," not a temple. Option C confuses the mechanism with 14th-century astronomical clocks. Option D goes beyond — only astronomical predictions are mentioned.

### Problem 5
**Answer: B**
**Explanation:** The passage's first sentence states: "In 1977, marine scientists exploring the Galápagos Rift made a startling discovery: thriving ecosystems on the deep ocean floor." Option A contradicts — they derive energy from chemosynthesis, not photosynthesis. Option C is overly narrow — tube worms, clams, and other organisms are mentioned. Option D contradicts — they're on the "deep ocean floor."

### Problem 6
**Answer: B**
**Explanation:** The passage says: "the most frequently used letters in English — E and T — received the shortest codes (a single dot and a single dash, respectively)." E gets a single dot because it's frequent. Option A contradicts the passage. Option C is false — Vail is mentioned as a collaborator. Option D — the passage says "letters and numbers" were encoded, implying the same system, not a different one.

---

**Quick Recap:**
- Answer only from the text — outside knowledge is irrelevant
- Hunt for keywords, then read 2-3 lines before and after
- Use line references — the answer lives there
- Correct answers often paraphrase, not quote
- Common traps: plausible but unstated, distorted detail, wrong subject, too extreme
