Lesson 37: Transitions & Logical Sequence

What You'll Learn

Transitions are the glue that connects ideas in a passage. The SAT tests your ability to choose the right transition word based on the logical relationship between sentences and paragraphs. This isn't about "what sounds good" — it's about analyzing whether the second sentence continues, contrasts, explains, or results from the first. By the end of this lesson, you'll have a systematic method for identifying the logical relationship and selecting the correct transition every time.

SAT Context

Transition questions appear in the Expression of Ideas domain (rhetoric/composition). On the digital SAT, these are common and appear as questions that ask you to choose the best transition word or phrase to connect two sentences or ideas. These questions test logical reasoning as much as vocabulary. Expect 3-5 transition questions per test.

The Core Strategy

When you see a transition question, ask yourself:

"What is the relationship between the ideas on either side of the blank?"

Don't read the answer choices first. Determine the relationship FIRST, then find the answer that matches. This prevents the SAT from tricking you with a word that "sounds good" but has the wrong logical meaning.

Transition Categories

1. Addition (Continuing the Same Direction)

These transitions indicate that the second idea adds to or reinforces the first. The two ideas point in the same direction.

| Transition | Example Use |

|------------|-------------|

| furthermore | formal addition |

| moreover | formal addition |

| additionally | adding another point |

| also | simple addition |

| in addition | adding a related point |

| similarly | showing similarity |

| likewise | showing parallel situations |

| not only...but also | paired addition |

Example: The new policy reduced costs. Furthermore, it improved employee satisfaction.

(Both ideas are positive — the second adds to the first.)

2. Contrast (Changing Direction)

These transitions indicate that the second idea contradicts, opposes, or qualifies the first. The two ideas point in opposite directions.

| Transition | Example Use |

|------------|-------------|

| however | direct contrast |

| nevertheless | despite what was said |

| nonetheless | same as nevertheless |

| on the other hand | alternative perspective |

| conversely | opposite relationship |

| in contrast | direct comparison |

| although / though | subordinating contrast |

| despite / in spite of | contrast with concession |

| whereas / while | comparing differences |

| yet | contrast (conjunction) |

| but | direct contrast (conjunction) |

| still | despite everything |

Example: The new policy reduced costs. However, it led to lower employee morale.

(First idea is positive, second is negative — contrast.)

3. Cause and Effect

These transitions indicate that the second idea is a result or consequence of the first.

| Transition | Example Use |

|------------|-------------|

| therefore | logical conclusion |

| consequently | as a result |

| as a result | direct consequence |

| thus | formal conclusion |

| hence | formal result |

| accordingly | appropriate response |

| so | simple cause-effect (conjunction) |

| for this reason | explicit causal link |

Example: The company failed to innovate. As a result, it lost market share.

(The first caused the second.)

4. Emphasis

These transitions highlight or strengthen the point being made.

| Transition | Example Use |

|------------|-------------|

| indeed | confirming and strengthening |

| in fact | providing stronger evidence |

| certainly | expressing confidence |

| of course | stating the obvious |

| undoubtedly | without doubt |

| especially | singling out |

| particularly | highlighting specificity |

Example: The experiment was a success. In fact, the results exceeded all expectations.

5. Exemplification (Giving Examples)

| Transition | Example Use |

|------------|-------------|

| for example | introducing an example |

| for instance | same as above |

| specifically | narrowing to a case |

| to illustrate | providing an illustration |

| in particular | highlighting a specific case |

| namely | naming specifically |

Example: Many countries have adopted renewable energy. For instance, Denmark now generates over 40% of its electricity from wind.

6. Sequence / Time

| Transition | Example Use |

|------------|-------------|

| first / second / third | ordering points |

| then / next | sequencing events |

| finally | concluding a sequence |

| subsequently | after that |

| meanwhile | at the same time |

| previously | before this point |

| afterward | after an event |

7. Summary / Conclusion

| Transition | Example Use |

|------------|-------------|

| in conclusion | wrapping up |

| in summary | summarizing |

| overall | taking a broad view |

| ultimately | final outcome |

| in the end | final result |

The SAT's Trickiest Transition Distinctions

However vs. Therefore

These are the most commonly tested pair. They are OPPOSITES:

  • However = contrast (different direction)
  • Therefore = cause-effect (same direction logically)

Test: The study was flawed. __________, its conclusions are questionable.

  • Is the second idea contrasting the first? No — the second follows logically from the first. → Therefore

Test: The study had a large sample size. __________, its conclusions are questionable.

  • Is the second idea contrasting the first? Yes — large sample should mean good conclusions, but they're questionable. → However

Furthermore vs. However

  • Furthermore = same direction (addition)
  • However = different direction (contrast)

For example vs. Specifically

  • For example = introduces one instance among many possible
  • Specifically = narrows down to precise detail about what was already mentioned

Transition Traps

> 🚨 SAT TRAP ALERT #1: A transition that "sounds good" but has the wrong logical meaning. The SAT fills the answer choices with plausible-sounding words that don't match the actual relationship. Always analyze the logical direction first.

> 🚨 SAT TRAP ALERT #2: Confusing subordinating conjunctions (although, because, while, since) with transition adverbs (however, therefore, nonetheless). Subordinating conjunctions make a clause dependent; transition adverbs don't. The punctuation rules are different, but the SAT may test your ability to use the right word regardless.

> 🚨 SAT TRAP ALERT #3: Two answer choices that mean the same thing. The SAT rarely makes you choose between true synonyms (like "furthermore" and "moreover"). If two choices mean the same thing, look more closely — one might have a subtle nuance issue, or both might be wrong.

Worked Example 1

Question: The researchers hypothesized that the new drug would reduce symptoms of the disease. __________, the clinical trial showed no significant difference between the treatment group and the control group.

A) Therefore,

B) Furthermore,

C) However,

D) For example,

Answer: C — "However,"

Explanation: Analyze the two sentences. Sentence 1: researchers expected the drug to work. Sentence 2: the trial showed it didn't work. These ideas are in opposition — the result contradicts the expectation. That's a contrast relationship. "However" signals contrast. "Therefore" (cause-effect) and "Furthermore" (addition) would both be wrong because sentence 2 doesn't follow from or add to sentence 1.

Worked Example 2

Question: The city implemented a bike-sharing program in 2019. __________, the number of cyclists on downtown streets increased by 60 percent within the first year.

A) In contrast,

B) As a result,

C) Nevertheless,

D) On the other hand,

Answer: B — "As a result,"

Explanation: Sentence 1: city implemented a program. Sentence 2: cycling increased. These ideas have a cause-effect relationship — the program appears to have caused the increase. "As a result" captures this logical connection. The other choices all signal contrast, which doesn't fit.

Worked Example 3

Question: Studying for the SAT requires consistent practice over time. __________, cramming the night before the test is unlikely to produce strong results.

A) Moreover,

B) For instance,

C) In other words,

D) Specifically,

Answer: C — "In other words,"

Explanation: Sentence 2 isn't adding a new point (not "Moreover"), giving an example (not "For instance"), or narrowing a detail (not "Specifically"). It's restating the first idea in a different way: consistent practice is needed = cramming won't work. "In other words" signals this restatement.

Practice Problems

Problem 1

The proposed legislation would reduce carbon emissions by 40% over the next decade. __________, it would create thousands of new jobs in the renewable energy sector.

A) However,

B) In contrast,

C) Additionally,

D) As a result,

Problem 2

The initial reviews of the film were overwhelmingly negative. __________, it went on to become one of the highest-grossing movies of the year.

A) For example,

B) Therefore,

C) Nevertheless,

D) Specifically,

Problem 3

The researchers collected data from over 10,000 participants across five continents. __________, the study is one of the most comprehensive investigations of sleep patterns ever conducted.

A) However,

B) For instance,

C) Thus,

D) In contrast,

Problem 4

Many people assume that creativity is an innate talent that cannot be developed. __________, research suggests that creative thinking can be significantly improved through targeted exercises and practice.

A) Moreover,

B) In other words,

C) For example,

D) However,

Problem 5

The company invested heavily in employee training programs. __________, productivity rose and turnover rates declined significantly.

A) Nevertheless,

B) Consequently,

C) Meanwhile,

D) In contrast,

Problem 6

The Great Wall of China stretches over 13,000 miles. __________, it is not a single continuous wall but a series of fortifications built by different dynasties.

A) For example,

B) As a result,

C) Therefore,

D) However,

Problem 7

The new software interface was designed to be more intuitive and user-friendly. __________, many longtime users found the changes confusing and disruptive to their workflow.

A) Furthermore,

B) In addition,

C) In other words,

D) On the other hand,

Problem 8

Climate scientists have documented rising global temperatures for decades. __________, 2023 was the hottest year on record, with average temperatures surpassing all previous measurements.

A) In other words,

B) For instance,

C) Nevertheless,

D) Therefore,

Answers

Problem 1 — Answer: C ("Additionally,")

Why C is correct: Sentence 1 describes a positive outcome of the legislation (reducing emissions). Sentence 2 describes another positive outcome (creating jobs). These are two benefits that point in the same direction — this is an addition relationship. "Additionally" correctly signals that another good thing also happens.

Why others are wrong:

  • A ("However,"): Contrast — but both sentences describe positive effects.
  • B ("In contrast,"): Contrast — same issue.
  • D ("As a result,"): Cause-effect — but the second sentence isn't a result of the first; it's a separate benefit.

Problem 2 — Answer: C ("Nevertheless,")

Why C is correct: Sentence 1: reviews were negative (bad). Sentence 2: the film was very successful (good). These are opposite directions — contrast. "Nevertheless" means "despite that" and correctly signals that the second idea contradicts what you'd expect from the first.

Why others are wrong:

  • A ("For example,"): Would mean the success is an example of negative reviews — illogical.
  • B ("Therefore,"): Would mean the success is a result of negative reviews — illogical.
  • D ("Specifically,"): Would narrow the first statement, which doesn't match the relationship.

Problem 3 — Answer: C ("Thus,")

Why C is correct: Sentence 1 describes a study with massive scope (10,000+ participants, 5 continents). Sentence 2 concludes that it's one of the most comprehensive studies. The second statement logically follows from the first — cause-effect. "Thus" signals this conclusion.

Why others are wrong:

  • A ("However,"): Contrast — but the second statement follows naturally from the first.
  • B ("For instance,"): Would make the comprehensiveness an example of data collection, which is backwards.
  • D ("In contrast,"): Contrast — doesn't fit.

Problem 4 — Answer: D ("However,")

Why D is correct: Sentence 1: people assume creativity can't be developed. Sentence 2: research shows it CAN be developed. These are opposing ideas — the second contradicts the first. "However" correctly signals contrast.

Why others are wrong:

  • A ("Moreover,"): Addition — but the second idea contradicts, not adds to, the first.
  • B ("In other words,"): Restatement — but the second idea is opposite, not a restatement.
  • C ("For example,"): Would make the research an example of creativity being innate — illogical.

Problem 5 — Answer: B ("Consequently,")

Why B is correct: Sentence 1: company invested in training. Sentence 2: productivity rose and turnover dropped. The second is a result of the first — cause-effect. "Consequently" means "as a result" and fits perfectly.

Why others are wrong:

  • A ("Nevertheless,"): Contrast — but the second follows logically from the first.
  • C ("Meanwhile,"): Time sequence — suggests simultaneous unrelated events, not cause-effect.
  • D ("In contrast,"): Contrast — doesn't fit.

Problem 6 — Answer: D ("However,")

Why D is correct: Sentence 1: The Great Wall stretches over 13,000 miles (suggesting one continuous wall). Sentence 2: it's actually not one continuous wall. This is a correction or qualification — a contrast to what the first sentence implies. "However" signals this contrast/correction.

Why others are wrong:

  • A ("For example,"): The second sentence isn't an example of the wall's length.
  • B ("As a result,"): The wall being non-continuous isn't a result of its length.
  • C ("Therefore,"): Same issue — no cause-effect relationship.

Problem 7 — Answer: D ("On the other hand,")

Why D is correct: Sentence 1: software was designed to be intuitive (positive intent). Sentence 2: users found it confusing (negative outcome). These are contrasting perspectives — the designers' intent vs. the users' experience. "On the other hand" signals this alternative perspective.

Why others are wrong:

  • A ("Furthermore,"): Addition — but the second idea contrasts, not adds.
  • B ("In addition,"): Addition — same issue.
  • C ("In other words,"): Restatement — but the second isn't restating the first; it's opposing it.

Problem 8 — Answer: B ("For instance,")

Why B is correct: Sentence 1: general statement about rising temperatures over decades. Sentence 2: a specific example — 2023 as the hottest year. This is a general-to-specific relationship. "For instance" introduces a concrete example of the general trend.

Why others are wrong:

  • A ("In other words,"): Would mean 2023 being hottest is a restatement of "rising temperatures" — it's not; it's a specific case.
  • C ("Nevertheless,"): Contrast — but the specific example supports, not contradicts, the general statement.
  • D ("Therefore,"): Cause-effect — but 2023's record isn't caused by decades of documentation; it's evidence of the trend.
