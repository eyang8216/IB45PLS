# Lesson 41: Text Structure, Purpose & Tone

## What You'll Learn
Every SAT passage has a structure, a purpose, and a tone. Recognizing these quickly lets you answer "primary purpose," "overall structure," and "paragraph function" questions without getting lost. This lesson teaches you to identify the architecture of any passage.

## SAT Context
Structure and purpose questions represent 3-5 questions per module. They reward readers who see the big picture rather than getting bogged down in details. Tone questions test your sensitivity to the author's attitude — a skill that transcends the SAT and applies to all critical reading.

---

### The Five Most Common Text Structures

SAT passages follow predictable organizational patterns. Identifying the structure helps you predict where information will appear and what the author is doing at each point.

#### 1. Problem-Solution
**Pattern:** A problem is introduced → causes or effects are discussed → a solution is proposed → the solution is evaluated.

**Signal phrases:** "One major challenge facing..." / "To address this issue..." / "A potential remedy..."

**Most common in:** Social science passages, policy discussions

#### 2. Cause-Effect
**Pattern:** An event or phenomenon is described → its causes are explored → its consequences are traced.

**Signal phrases:** "This led to..." / "As a result..." / "The primary cause was..." / "Consequently..."

**Most common in:** Science passages, historical analysis

#### 3. Compare-Contrast
**Pattern:** Two things, ideas, or approaches are examined → similarities are noted → differences are highlighted → a conclusion about the comparison is drawn.

**Signal phrases:** "Similarly..." / "In contrast..." / "Whereas..." / "Both X and Y..." / "Unlike X, Y..."

**Most common in:** Humanities passages, literary analysis

#### 4. Chronological / Sequential
**Pattern:** Events or steps are presented in time order.

**Signal phrases:** "First..." / "Subsequently..." / "By the 19th century..." / "Eventually..."

**Most common in:** History passages, process descriptions

#### 5. Claim-Evidence
**Pattern:** A claim is stated → evidence is provided → the evidence is analyzed → the claim is reinforced or refined.

**Signal phrases:** "Research has shown..." / "For example..." / "This demonstrates that..." / "The data suggest..."

**Most common in:** Science passages, argumentative texts

> **🔍 PATTERN RECOGNITION:** Most SAT science passages follow claim-evidence structure. Most SAT history/social science passages follow either cause-effect or problem-solution. Knowing this lets you anticipate the passage's flow before you even start reading.

---

### Identifying Overall Structure

When the question asks "The passage is primarily organized by..." or "The overall structure of the passage can best be described as...", you're being tested on the big-picture organization.

**Strategy:**
1. Read the first sentence of each paragraph
2. Ask: what is the author DOING at each paragraph?
3. Map it: Intro → Evidence → Analysis → Conclusion (or whatever pattern emerges)

**Wrong answers typically:**
- Describe a structure that's present but not the PRIMARY one
- Reverse the order ("cause, then effect" when the passage goes "effect, then cause")
- Insert a structure that's not there at all

---

### Paragraph Function Questions

"The function of paragraph 2 is to..." — These questions ask what ROLE a specific paragraph plays in the overall argument.

**Common paragraph functions:**
- **Introduce** the topic or problem
- **Provide background** or context
- **Present evidence** (data, study, example)
- **Analyze** or interpret evidence
- **Refute** a counterargument
- **Transition** between ideas
- **Summarize** or conclude
- **Illustrate** a point with an example
- **Qualify** or limit a previous claim

**Strategy:** Read the paragraph, then ask: "What does this paragraph DO for the passage as a whole?" Your answer should be a verb: it introduces, it supports, it challenges, it elaborates.

> **🚨 SAT TRAP ALERT:** A common trap is describing what the paragraph SAYS rather than what it DOES. For example, "It describes the process of photosynthesis" is a description of content, not function. The correct answer is more like "It provides a detailed example that illustrates the main argument."

---

### Author's Purpose

Purpose questions ask WHY the author wrote the passage. The answer is always an infinitive verb.

**The five main purposes:**

| Purpose | Key Question | Common in... |
|---------|-------------|--------------|
| **To inform** | Does the passage teach facts without taking a side? | Science, neutral reporting |
| **To persuade** | Does the passage argue for a position? | Opinion pieces, editorials |
| **To analyze** | Does the passage break down a complex topic? | Academic passages, literary criticism |
| **To describe** | Does the passage paint a picture without arguing? | Fiction excerpts, travel writing |
| **To entertain** | Is the primary goal amusement/enjoyment? | Fiction, humorous essays |

> **🚨 SAT TRAP ALERT:** Choosing a purpose that's too broad ("to discuss a topic") or too narrow ("to describe a single experiment mentioned in paragraph 3"). The purpose must capture the ENTIRE passage, not just one section.

### Tone Identification

Tone = the author's attitude toward the subject. SAT tone words fall on a spectrum.

**Common SAT tone words, organized by attitude:**

**Positive tones:**
- Admiring — showing respect and approval
- Celebratory — praising enthusiastically
- Optimistic — hopeful about the future
- Reverent — deeply respectful

**Neutral/Objective tones:**
- Neutral — no personal opinion
- Analytical — examining logically without emotion
- Scholarly — academic and research-oriented
- Matter-of-fact — straightforward and unemotional

**Negative/Critical tones:**
- Skeptical — doubtful, questioning
- Critical — finding fault
- Dismissive — treating as unworthy of consideration
- Condescending — talking down to the subject

**Nuanced tones:**
- Nostalgic — longing for the past
- Ironic — saying the opposite of what's meant
- Ambivalent — having mixed feelings
- Urgent — insisting on immediate action
- Cautious — careful and qualified

> **🔍 PATTERN RECOGNITION:** When a passage includes words like "perhaps," "may," "suggests," or "potentially," the tone is cautious, not confident. When a passage uses words like "undoubtedly," "clearly," or "must," the tone is confident. Match your tone answer to these language cues.

### Worked Example 1: Structure

**Passage summary:**
"Studies show that urban green spaces reduce stress levels in residents. Researchers at the University of Tokyo measured cortisol levels in participants who walked through both forested parks and city streets. Cortisol dropped 12% after park walks but only 2% after street walks. This suggests that even brief exposure to greenery has measurable physiological benefits."

**Question:** The overall structure of the passage can best be described as:
A) a problem presented, followed by competing solutions
B) a claim stated, followed by evidence that supports it
C) a chronological account of changing attitudes toward urban planning
D) a comparison of two cities' approaches to green space design

**Answer:** B
**Explanation:** The passage opens with a claim (green spaces reduce stress), provides specific evidence (cortisol measurements), and ends by reinforcing the claim. This is classic claim-evidence structure. Option A is wrong because there's only one solution discussed. Option C is wrong because there's no chronology. Option D is wrong because no cities' approaches are compared.

---

### Worked Example 2: Tone

**Passage excerpt:**
"It is perhaps tempting to attribute the company's success to visionary leadership. However, a closer examination reveals that favorable market conditions, combined with a series of fortunate acquisitions, may have played a more significant role than any individual's strategic brilliance."

**Question:** The tone of the passage is best described as:
A) admiring
B) skeptical
C) celebratory
D) nostalgic

**Answer:** B
**Explanation:** The author pushes back against the "tempting" explanation (visionary leadership) and suggests alternative causes (market conditions, fortunate acquisitions). The words "perhaps," "may have," and "closer examination reveals" signal doubt about the conventional narrative — that's skepticism. Option A (admiring) is the opposite. Option C (celebratory) doesn't fit the questioning stance. Option D (nostalgic) involves looking back fondly, which isn't present.

---

### Worked Example 3: Paragraph Function

**Question:** The function of paragraph 2 is to:
[Paragraph 2 provides a detailed description of a 2019 study in which 500 participants were divided into control and experimental groups to test a new teaching method.]

A) introduce the main argument of the passage
B) provide evidence that supports the claims made in paragraph 1
C) summarize the implications of the research discussed
D) present a counterargument to the position in paragraph 1

**Answer:** B
**Explanation:** The paragraph describes a study — its function is to provide evidence. The verb "provides evidence" captures function, not just content. Option A would be the function of paragraph 1 typically. Option C would be the function of a concluding paragraph. Option D is wrong because the study supports, not challenges, the preceding claims.

## Practice Problems

### Problem 1
Passage excerpt: "The invention of the mechanical clock in medieval Europe transformed not just timekeeping but the very structure of daily life. Before clocks, people organized their days around natural rhythms — sunrise, sunset, the changing seasons. After clocks became widespread in town squares, work began and ended at specific hours, punctuality became a virtue, and the concept of 'wasted time' entered the cultural vocabulary. This shift, historians argue, laid the groundwork for the efficiency-obsessed modern world."

The overall structure of this passage can best be described as:
A) a problem identified, followed by a proposed solution
B) a comparison of two different inventions
C) a cause-and-effect analysis of a historical development
D) a chronological listing of medieval inventions

### Problem 2
Passage excerpt: "Critics of renewable energy often point to the intermittent nature of solar and wind power. The sun does not always shine, they note, nor does the wind always blow. However, advances in battery storage technology have begun to address this limitation. Grid-scale batteries can now store excess energy generated during peak production and release it during periods of low generation, effectively smoothing out the intermittency problem."

The tone of this passage is best described as:
A) skeptically questioning renewable energy
B) neutrally describing both sides of an issue and then presenting a counterargument
C) enthusiastically promoting battery technology
D) ironically mocking critics of renewable energy

### Problem 3
[Paragraph 2 of a passage about the printing press]
"The Gutenberg press did not, however, immediately revolutionize reading habits. For the first several decades after its invention, printed books remained luxury items accessible only to the wealthy. Most printed works were religious texts in Latin, a language ordinary people could not read. It was not until the rise of vernacular printing — books in everyday languages like German, French, and English — that literacy began to spread beyond the elite."

The function of this paragraph is primarily to:
A) describe how the printing press worked mechanically
B) qualify the claim that the printing press immediately transformed society
C) provide statistical evidence about literacy rates
D) introduce a completely new topic unrelated to the printing press

### Problem 4
"To call Moby-Dick merely a novel about a whale is to miss the point entirely. While Melville's masterpiece does center on Captain Ahab's obsessive hunt, the white whale functions as far more than a plot device. It serves as a canvas onto which each character — and each reader — projects their own fears, ambitions, and questions about fate."

The author's purpose is primarily to:
A) inform readers about the plot of Moby-Dick
B) persuade readers that Moby-Dick is deeper than a simple adventure story
C) entertain with a humorous take on classic literature
D) analyze the economic conditions of the whaling industry

### Problem 5
"The data from the clinical trial are, to put it charitably, less than definitive. The sample size was a mere 47 participants, the control group was poorly matched to the experimental group, and the authors' own statistical analysis acknowledges a margin of error that makes their conclusions tentative at best. One might reasonably ask whether the study was worth publishing at all."

The tone of this passage is best described as:
A) admiring of the researchers' honesty
B) neutral and objective
C) dismissive and critical
D) cautiously optimistic

### Problem 6
[Passage about the introduction of coffee to Europe in the 17th century]
Paragraph 1: Describes how coffee was first introduced and how coffeehouses quickly became popular.
Paragraph 2: Explains how coffeehouses became centers of intellectual and political discussion, earning the nickname "penny universities."
Paragraph 3: Discusses how authorities in several countries attempted to shut down coffeehouses, viewing them as hotbeds of sedition.

The overall structure of this passage can best be described as:
A) a phenomenon introduced, its effects discussed, and the reaction to it described
B) a problem presented and multiple solutions evaluated
C) a comparison of coffee culture in different European countries
D) a claim made and then refuted by historical evidence

---

## Answers

### Problem 1
**Answer: C**
**Explanation:** The passage traces how the mechanical clock (cause) transformed society (effects): work schedules, punctuality, the concept of wasted time, and the efficiency-obsessed modern world. This is classic cause-and-effect structure. Option A fails because no solution is proposed. Option B fails because only one invention is discussed. Option D fails because it's not a list — it's an analysis of one invention's impact.

### Problem 2
**Answer: B**
**Explanation:** The passage begins by acknowledging the critics' point (intermittency), signaled by "Critics...often point out." Then "However" signals the turn to the counterargument (battery storage). The tone is measured and balanced, neither overly enthusiastic nor mocking. Option A misses the "however" shift. Option C overstates — "enthusiastically promoting" is too strong for the neutral description. Option D is wrong — there's no irony or mockery.

### Problem 3
**Answer: B**
**Explanation:** The paragraph begins with "did not, however, immediately revolutionize" — the word "however" signals it's qualifying (limiting or modifying) a previous claim. The paragraph explains that the press didn't immediately spread literacy because early books were expensive and in Latin. "Qualify" means to limit or add nuance to a claim. Option A is wrong — no mechanical description appears. Option C is wrong — no statistics. Option D is wrong — it's clearly related to the printing press.

### Problem 4
**Answer: B**
**Explanation:** The author argues against the view that Moby-Dick is "merely a novel about a whale" and makes a case that it's much deeper. The phrase "is to miss the point entirely" signals persuasion — the author is trying to change the reader's view. Option A is too narrow — the author isn't just informing about plot. Option C is wrong — there's no humor. Option D is wrong — economics isn't mentioned.

### Problem 5
**Answer: C**
**Explanation:** The author systematically dismantles the study's credibility: small sample size, poor control matching, wide margin of error. The phrase "to put it charitably" drips with sarcasm, and "One might reasonably ask whether the study was worth publishing at all" is outright dismissive. Option A misses the sarcasm. Option B is wrong — this isn't neutral. Option D is the opposite of the actual tone.

### Problem 6
**Answer: A**
**Explanation:** Paragraph 1 introduces coffee and its popularity (phenomenon introduced). Paragraph 2 discusses the intellectual effects (effects discussed). Paragraph 3 covers the authorities' attempts to shut down coffeehouses (reaction to it). This maps perfectly to option A. Option B fails — no problem is presented up front, and no solutions are evaluated. Option C fails — no country-by-country comparison. Option D fails — no claim is refuted.

---

**Quick Recap:**
- Identify structure by looking at what each paragraph DOES
- Paragraph function = the ROLE it plays (introduce, support, challenge, etc.)
- Purpose = WHY the author wrote (inform, persuade, analyze, describe, entertain)
- Tone = the author's ATTITUDE, detected through word choice
- The correct answer captures the WHOLE passage, not just one part
