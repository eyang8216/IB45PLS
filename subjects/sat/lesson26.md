# Lesson 26: Subject-Verb Agreement

## What You'll Learn
Subject-verb agreement is the foundation of grammatically correct English. You'll learn to ensure every verb matches its subject in number (singular/plural), even when the SAT deliberately hides the true subject behind distracting phrases. By the end of this lesson, you'll never be tricked by prepositional phrases, inverted sentences, or tricky indefinite pronouns again.

## SAT Context
On the digital SAT, subject-verb agreement questions appear in the **Standard English Conventions** domain (formerly "Writing and Language"). They're among the most frequently tested grammar concepts, appearing in roughly 15-20% of conventions questions. The SAT *loves* to bury the subject inside complex sentence structures so you lose track of whether it's singular or plural. Master this, and you lock in easy points.

---

## The Core Rule

Every sentence has a subject (who or what does the action) and a verb (the action or state of being). They must agree in number:

- **Singular subject → singular verb** (usually ends in -s in present tense)
- **Plural subject → plural verb** (no -s ending in present tense)

Compare:
- The **dog barks**. (singular subject "dog" + singular verb "barks")
- The **dogs bark**. (plural subject "dogs" + plural verb "bark")

### The S-Curve Rule
In present tense, the letter **-s** goes on either the subject OR the verb, but not both:
- One dog **barkS** → -s on the verb because "dog" has no -s
- Two dog**S** bark → -s on the subject, so the verb drops it

This "one -s rule" will save you on test day. If you see "The dogs barks," your ear should immediately twitch—two -s endings, which is wrong.

---

## Intervening Phrases Don't Change Number

The SAT's favorite trick: stuff a long phrase between the subject and verb so you forget what the actual subject is. 

> **🚨 SAT TRAP ALERT:** When you see a prepositional phrase (starting with *of, in, on, for, with, about, to, from, by*), cross it out mentally. It NEVER contains the subject.

**WRONG:** The box of chocolates **are** on the table.
**RIGHT:** The box of chocolates **is** on the table.

"Box" is the subject (singular). "Of chocolates" is just a prepositional phrase telling you what's in the box. Ignore it.

More examples:
- The **team** of engineers **was** working late. (subject: team, singular)
- The **pages** in the book **are** yellowed. (subject: pages, plural)
- A **collection** of rare stamps **was** auctioned. (subject: collection, singular)
- The **results** of the experiment **confirm** the hypothesis. (subject: results, plural)

### 🔍 PATTERN RECOGNITION: When you see a prepositional phrase between a subject and a verb on the SAT, physically cover it with your finger. Read the sentence without it: "The box...IS on the table." Always correct.

Singular prepositional phrase distractors:
- along with, together with, as well as, in addition to, accompanied by

**WRONG:** The mayor, along with her advisors, **are** attending.
**RIGHT:** The mayor, along with her advisors, **is** attending.

Phrases like "along with" are NOT the same as "and." Only "and" makes a compound subject plural.

## Compound Subjects

### With "and" → Plural (usually)
Two singular subjects joined by "and" create a plural subject:
- **The cat and the dog are** sleeping.
- **My brother and I were** at the store.

**Exception:** When "and" joins two things that form a single unit or idea, use singular:
- **Peanut butter and jelly is** my favorite sandwich. (one sandwich type)
- **Macaroni and cheese was** served. (one dish)
- **Research and development requires** significant funding. (one department)

### With "or" / "nor" → Closest Subject Rules
When subjects are joined by *or*, *either...or*, *neither...nor*, the verb agrees with the subject **closest to it**:

- **Neither the students nor the teacher WAS** present.
  - "Teacher" is closest → singular → "was"
- **Neither the teacher nor the students WERE** present.
  - "Students" is closest → plural → "were"
- **Either my parents or my sister IS** picking me up.
  - "Sister" is closest → singular → "is"

> **🔍 PATTERN RECOGNITION:** On SAT questions, look at the word RIGHT BEFORE the verb when you see "or/nor." That's your subject. Ignore everything before "or/nor."

---

## Collective Nouns

Collective nouns refer to groups: *team, jury, family, committee, audience, crowd, class, staff, band, government, company.*

**American English rule (what the SAT uses):** Treat collective nouns as **singular** when the group acts as one unit.

- The team **is** practicing. (one unit, all practicing together)
- The jury **has** reached a verdict. (one decision)
- The committee **meets** every Tuesday. (one meeting)

**Exception — but RARE on the SAT:** If individuals in the group act separately, use plural. This almost never happens on the test. When in doubt, go singular.

---

## Inverted Sentences

In questions and sentences starting with "there" or "here," the subject comes AFTER the verb. Don't let this confuse you.

- **There IS a problem** with the car. (subject: "problem" — singular)
- **There ARE problems** with the car. (subject: "problems" — plural)
- **Here IS the book** you wanted. (subject: "book")
- **Here ARE the books** you wanted. (subject: "books")

> **🚨 SAT TRAP ALERT:** The SAT knows students instinctively want to make the verb agree with "there." Don't. "There" is never the subject. Find the real subject after the verb.

Also watch for inverted sentences beginning with prepositional phrases:
- **In the drawer IS** a key. (subject: key)
- **Among the candidates WAS** a former governor. (subject: governor)

## Indefinite Pronouns — The Tricky Ones

These pronouns don't refer to a specific person or thing, and each has a fixed number:

### Always Singular (Memorize These!)
Each, every, everyone, everybody, someone, somebody, anyone, anybody, no one, nobody, either, neither, one, nothing, anything, something

- **Everyone IS** going to the party. (NOT "are")
- **Each of the students HAS** a locker. (NOT "have")
- **Nobody KNOWS** the answer. (NOT "know")
- **Either of the options IS** acceptable. (NOT "are")

### Always Plural
Both, few, many, several, others

- **Both WERE** correct. 
- **Few HAVE** finished the exam.
- **Many ARE** called but few are chosen.

### Depends on Context
All, any, most, none, some — these can be singular or plural depending on what they refer to:
- **All of the pie IS** gone. (pie = singular mass)
- **All of the cookies ARE** gone. (cookies = plural count)
- **None of the work WAS** finished. (work = singular)
- **None of the players WERE** injured. (players = plural — SAT now accepts both singular and plural for "none")

### Worked Example 1
**Question:** The collection of rare artifacts, which were discovered during the excavation of an ancient tomb in the northern region of the country, __________ to be the most significant archaeological find of the decade.

A) appear
B) appears
C) are appearing
D) have appeared

**Answer:** B — "appears"

**Explanation:** The subject is "collection," which is singular. Strip away the prepositional phrase "of rare artifacts" and the nonrestrictive clause "which were discovered during the excavation of an ancient tomb in the northern region of the country." The sentence core is: "The collection...appears to be..." Singular subject needs singular verb.

---

### Worked Example 2
**Question:** Neither the marketing team nor the sales representatives __________ satisfied with the new pricing structure.

A) is
B) was
C) are
D) has been

**Answer:** C — "are"

**Explanation:** With "neither...nor," the verb agrees with the subject closest to it. "Sales representatives" is plural and closest to the verb, so we need a plural verb: "are."

---

### Worked Example 3
**Question:** Each of the applicants who submitted their materials before the deadline __________ to receive an interview invitation.

A) expect
B) expects
C) are expecting
D) have expected

**Answer:** B — "expects"

**Explanation:** The subject is "Each," which is always singular. "Of the applicants...deadline" is a long prepositional phrase and relative clause that you can strip away. "Each...expects."

## Practice Problems

### Problem 1
The sharp increase in housing prices, especially in major metropolitan areas, __________ many young families to reconsider relocating to suburban communities.

A) have led
B) are leading
C) lead
D) has led

### Problem 2
Either the director or the actors __________ responsible for the miscommunication that delayed the production schedule.

A) is
B) was
C) were
D) has been

### Problem 3
There __________ several factors that researchers believe contribute to the decline in bee populations worldwide.

A) is
B) was
C) are
D) has been

### Problem 4
The committee, despite differing opinions among its members about the proposed budget, __________ to reach a unanimous decision by the end of the meeting.

A) are hoping
B) hope
C) hopes
D) have hoped

### Problem 5
Every one of the participants who completed the study __________ a follow-up survey six months after the initial assessment.

A) receive
B) receives
C) are receiving
D) have received

### Problem 6
The data collected from the satellite imagery __________ that deforestation rates have accelerated over the past decade.

A) suggest
B) are suggesting
C) suggests
D) have suggested

### Problem 7
Not only the CEO but also the board members __________ in favor of the merger, which surprised industry analysts.

A) was
B) is
C) has been
D) were

---

## Answers

### Problem 1 — Answer: D ("has led")

**Why D is correct:** The subject is "increase," which is singular. "In housing prices" and "especially in major metropolitan areas" are prepositional phrases. Strip them away: "The increase...has led." Singular subject needs singular verb.

**Why others are wrong:**
- A ("have led"): Plural verb, but the subject "increase" is singular.
- B ("are leading"): Plural, present progressive — wrong number and unnecessarily continuous.
- C ("lead"): Plural present tense. "Increase" is singular.

---

### Problem 2 — Answer: C ("were")

**Why C is correct:** With "either...or," the verb agrees with the subject closest to it. "The actors" is plural, so we need a plural verb. "Were" is the plural past tense form.

**Why others are wrong:**
- A ("is"): Singular present, but "actors" is plural.
- B ("was"): Singular past, but "actors" is plural.
- D ("has been"): Singular present perfect, but "actors" is plural.

---

### Problem 3 — Answer: C ("are")

**Why C is correct:** In sentences beginning with "There," the subject comes after the verb. The subject is "factors," which is plural. So: "There are several factors."

**Why others are wrong:**
- A ("is"): Singular verb, but "factors" is plural.
- B ("was"): Singular past tense, wrong number.
- D ("has been"): Singular present perfect, wrong number.

---

### Problem 4 — Answer: C ("hopes")

**Why C is correct:** "Committee" is a collective noun treated as singular when the group acts as a unit. The committee as a whole hopes to reach a decision. Ignore the interrupting phrase "despite...budget."

**Why others are wrong:**
- A ("are hoping"): Plural present progressive; "committee" is singular here.
- B ("hope"): Plural present tense; "committee" is singular.
- D ("have hoped"): Plural present perfect; wrong number.

---

### Problem 5 — Answer: B ("receives")

**Why B is correct:** The subject is "Every one" (an indefinite pronoun that is always singular). "Of the participants who completed the study" is a prepositional phrase with a relative clause. Strip it: "Every one...receives."

**Why others are wrong:**
- A ("receive"): Plural present tense form. "Every one" is singular.
- C ("are receiving"): Plural present progressive. Wrong number.
- D ("have received"): Plural present perfect. Wrong number.

---

### Problem 6 — Answer: C ("suggests")

**Why C is correct:** "Data" is technically plural (singular: datum), but in modern usage—and on the SAT—it's typically treated as a singular mass noun, like "information." The singular verb "suggests" is preferred. (Note: The SAT has moved toward accepting "data" as singular in most contexts, treating it like "information.")

**Why others are wrong:**
- A ("suggest"): Plural verb; SAT prefers singular for "data."
- B ("are suggesting"): Plural present progressive; SAT prefers singular.
- D ("have suggested"): Plural present perfect; SAT prefers singular.

---

### Problem 7 — Answer: D ("were")

**Why D is correct:** "Not only...but also" functions like "either...or" — the verb agrees with the nearest subject. "Board members" is plural, so: "were." The relative clause "which surprised industry analysts" is unrelated to the agreement.

**Why others are wrong:**
- A ("was"): Singular past tense; "board members" is plural.
- B ("is"): Singular present tense; wrong number.
- C ("has been"): Singular present perfect; wrong number.
