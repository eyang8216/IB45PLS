Lesson 42: Cross-Text Connections (Paired Passages)

What You'll Learn

Paired passages present two texts on the same topic — but with different perspectives, emphasis, or conclusions. You'll learn to analyze each passage independently and then compare them, answering questions about agreement, disagreement, and how each author would respond to the other.

SAT Context

Paired passages appear in roughly 1 out of every 3-4 SAT modules. They typically include 3-5 questions about the individual passages and 2-3 cross-text questions. The cross-text questions are where most points are lost — but with the right strategy, they're entirely manageable.

Why Paired Passages Exist

The SAT pairs passages to test synthesis — your ability to hold two perspectives in mind simultaneously and understand the relationship between them. This is a genuine academic skill: in college, you'll constantly read multiple sources and compare their arguments.

Common pairings:

  • Two scientists interpreting the same data differently
  • Two historians offering competing explanations for the same event
  • A traditional view and a revisionist challenge to that view
  • Two authors with different values emphasizing different aspects of the same phenomenon
  • A general theory and a specific counterexample or exception

The Two-Pass Strategy

The most efficient approach to paired passages:

Step 1: Read Passage 1 thoroughly.

  • Identify: Main argument, tone, key evidence
  • Answer all Passage 1-only questions immediately

Step 2: Read Passage 2 thoroughly.

  • Identify: Main argument, tone, key evidence
  • Note how it DIFFERS from Passage 1 (this is crucial)
  • Answer all Passage 2-only questions immediately

Step 3: Tackle the cross-text questions.

  • Now you have both arguments fresh in mind
  • For each cross-text question, re-examine the relevant portions of both passages

> 🔍 PATTERN RECOGNITION: Students who read Passage 1 → Passage 2 → then answer all questions perform significantly better than students who read both passages first and then face all questions. The segmentation keeps each author's argument distinct in your mind.

Identifying Each Author's Argument

Before you can compare passages, you must nail down each author's position independently.

For each passage, answer these four questions:

  • What is the author's main claim?
  • What evidence or reasoning does the author offer?
  • What is the author's tone (critical, supportive, skeptical, neutral)?
  • What assumptions does the author make?

    • Write it down mentally (or on scratch paper):

    • Passage 1: Argues X because of Y. Tone: Z.
    • Passage 2: Argues A because of B. Tone: C.
    • Relationship: They disagree on [specific point] because [reason].

    Cross-Text Question Type 1: Agreement

    "Both passages share which concern?"

    "The authors of both passages would most likely agree that..."

    "Which statement is supported by both passages?"

    Strategy:

    • Eliminate any answer that one passage supports but the other doesn't mention or contradicts
    • The correct answer must be explicitly or implicitly supported by BOTH texts
    • Focus on the common ground — what do both authors accept as true, even if they interpret it differently?

    > 🚨 SAT TRAP ALERT: Beware of answers that one passage clearly supports and the other doesn't address at all. "Not addressed" is NOT the same as "agrees." Both passages must affirmatively support the statement.

    Cross-Text Question Type 2: Disagreement

    "The author of Passage 2 would most likely disagree with which claim from Passage 1?"

    "On which point would the authors most likely disagree?"

    Strategy:

    • Find the point of conflict between the two arguments
    • The correct answer must be something Passage 1 asserts and Passage 2 contradicts (explicitly or implicitly)
    • Again: if one passage doesn't address it, they can't disagree on it

    Cross-Text Question Type 3: Response/Reaction

    "How would the author of Passage 1 most likely respond to the argument in Passage 2?"

    "The author of Passage 2 would most likely view Passage 1's argument as..."

    Strategy:

    • This is essentially: "What would Author 1 say about Author 2's argument?"
    • Base your answer on what Author 1 believes and values
    • The response should be consistent with Author 1's established position and tone
    • These questions are answered from within the worldview of one author, not from your own opinion

    Cross-Text Question Type 4: Relationship

    "Which best describes the relationship between the two passages?"

    "Passage 2 relates to Passage 1 in which way?"

    Common relationships:

    • Passage 2 challenges/refutes Passage 1
    • Passage 2 provides a specific example of Passage 1's general principle
    • Passage 2 offers an alternative explanation
    • Passage 2 extends or elaborates on Passage 1's argument
    • Passage 2 draws a different conclusion from similar evidence

    Worked Example 1

    Passage 1:

    The widespread adoption of electric vehicles (EVs) represents the most promising path to reducing transportation emissions. Unlike hydrogen fuel cells, which require an entirely new fueling infrastructure, EVs can leverage the existing electrical grid. Battery costs have fallen 89% since 2010, and ranges now exceed 300 miles on many models, eliminating "range anxiety" for most drivers.

    Passage 2:

    While electric vehicles have undeniable benefits, focusing exclusively on EVs overlooks fundamental limitations. The electrical grid in many regions still relies heavily on fossil fuels, meaning EVs often run on coal-generated electricity. Moreover, lithium mining for batteries causes significant environmental damage, and battery disposal poses unresolved recycling challenges. A diversified approach — including hydrogen, improved public transit, and urban redesign — is more prudent than betting everything on electrification.

    Question 1: Both authors would most likely agree that:

    A) electric vehicles should be abandoned in favor of hydrogen fuel cells

    B) electric vehicles contribute to reducing transportation emissions

    C) battery technology has advanced sufficiently to address all concerns

    D) lithium mining is the most serious environmental problem facing the planet

    Answer: B

    Explanation: Passage 1 explicitly says EVs are "the most promising path to reducing transportation emissions." Passage 2 says EVs "have undeniable benefits" — the context makes clear these benefits include emission reduction. Options A and C are positions only Passage 1 would take. Option D is an exaggeration of Passage 2's concern.

    Question 2: How would the author of Passage 2 most likely respond to Passage 1's claim that EVs "can leverage the existing electrical grid"?

    A) by agreeing that the existing grid is sufficient to support widespread EV adoption

    B) by pointing out that the existing grid often relies on fossil fuels, undercutting EVs' environmental benefits

    C) by arguing that hydrogen fuel cells are technologically superior in every respect

    D) by suggesting that the electrical grid should be dismantled entirely

    Answer: B

    Explanation: Passage 2 directly states that "the electrical grid in many regions still relies heavily on fossil fuels, meaning EVs often run on coal-generated electricity." This is the exact counterargument Author 2 would make to Author 1's grid-based optimism. Option A has Author 2 agreeing when they clearly don't. Option C overstates (Author 2 doesn't say hydrogen is superior in every respect). Option D is extreme and unsupported.

    Worked Example 2

    Passage 1:

    Social media platforms have democratized public discourse. Anyone with an internet connection can now share their views with a global audience, bypassing traditional gatekeepers like newspaper editors and television producers. This has amplified marginalized voices and enabled grassroots movements that would have been impossible in the pre-internet era.

    Passage 2:

    The narrative that social media democratizes discourse ignores the role of algorithms, which determine what users actually see. These algorithms, designed to maximize engagement, promote sensational and divisive content while burying nuanced discussion. Far from democratizing discourse, social media platforms have created new, less visible gatekeepers whose decisions are driven by profit, not public interest.

    Question 3: The relationship between Passage 1 and Passage 2 can best be described as:

    A) Passage 2 provides additional evidence supporting Passage 1's main argument

    B) Passage 2 challenges Passage 1's central claim by introducing a consideration Passage 1 overlooks

    C) Passage 2 and Passage 1 reach the same conclusion through different reasoning

    D) Passage 2 discusses a completely unrelated aspect of social media

    Answer: B

    Explanation: Passage 1 claims social media democratizes discourse. Passage 2 directly challenges this by introducing the role of algorithms — a consideration Passage 1 doesn't address. Options A and C are wrong because the passages disagree, not agree. Option D is wrong because both passages discuss social media's effect on public discourse.

    Practice Problems

    *Questions 1-3 refer to the following paired passages.*

    Passage 1:

    Remote work, accelerated by the pandemic, has been widely celebrated as a permanent improvement to work-life balance. Employees report higher satisfaction, save hours previously lost to commuting, and enjoy greater flexibility. Companies benefit from reduced real estate costs and access to a global talent pool. The evidence suggests that remote work is not merely a temporary adjustment but a structural shift that should be embraced.

    Passage 2:

    The enthusiasm for remote work overlooks significant drawbacks that emerge over time. Informal collaboration — the spontaneous conversations that spark innovation — diminishes when colleagues interact only through scheduled video calls. Junior employees lose mentorship opportunities that naturally occur in shared workspaces. And the blurring of work-life boundaries often leads to longer hours, not greater freedom, as the office follows workers into their homes.

    Problem 1

    Both passages share which concern?

    A) whether companies will reduce real estate costs

    B) whether remote work is a temporary or permanent change

    C) the effects of remote work on employees' professional lives

    D) whether employees commute too far to their workplaces

    Problem 2

    The author of Passage 2 would most likely characterize Passage 1's argument as:

    A) entirely correct and well-supported

    B) fundamentally mistaken about the definition of work-life balance

    C) overly optimistic because it fails to account for important negative consequences

    D) deliberately misleading and factually inaccurate

    Problem 3

    How would the author of Passage 1 most likely respond to Passage 2's claim about "informal collaboration"?

    A) by conceding that remote work eliminates all forms of collaboration

    B) by arguing that digital tools can facilitate collaboration and that the benefits of remote work outweigh this concern

    C) by insisting that collaboration is not important for most jobs

    D) by agreeing that everyone should return to offices immediately

    *Questions 4-6 refer to the following paired passages.*

    Passage 1:

    Standardized testing provides an objective metric that can cut through the inconsistencies of high school grading. A student with a 4.0 GPA from one school may be far less prepared than a student with a 3.5 from a more rigorous institution. Tests like the SAT offer colleges a common yardstick, and without them, admissions would rely even more heavily on factors correlated with socioeconomic advantage, such as extracurricular activities and personal essays polished by expensive consultants.

    Passage 2:

    The claim that standardized tests are objective ignores the well-documented fact that test scores correlate strongly with family income. Wealthy families can afford test preparation courses, private tutors, and multiple test attempts — advantages unavailable to lower-income students. Far from leveling the playing field, standardized tests reinforce existing inequalities under the guise of meritocracy. Colleges should move toward holistic review processes that consider students in the context of their opportunities.

    Problem 4

    On which point would the authors of Passage 1 and Passage 2 most likely DISAGREE?

    A) high school grades can vary significantly in their meaning across different schools

    B) socioeconomic factors affect college admissions

    C) standardized tests are objective and reduce inequality in admissions

    D) colleges need some method of evaluating applicants

    Problem 5

    The relationship between the two passages can best be described as:

    A) Passage 2 offers an alternative interpretation of the same evidence Passage 1 uses

    B) Passage 2 completely agrees with Passage 1 but proposes a different solution

    C) Passage 2 discusses a topic wholly unrelated to Passage 1's concerns

    D) Passage 2 accepts Passage 1's premise about objectivity but draws a different conclusion

    Problem 6

    The author of Passage 1 would most likely respond to Passage 2's argument about test preparation access by:

    A) conceding that standardized tests should be abolished

    B) arguing that removing tests would make inequality worse because admissions would depend even more on advantages wealth can buy

    C) claiming that test preparation has no effect on scores

    D) suggesting that only wealthy students should take standardized tests

    Answers

    Problem 1

    Answer: C

    Explanation: Both passages discuss how remote work affects employees. Passage 1 focuses on positive effects (satisfaction, less commuting, flexibility). Passage 2 focuses on negative effects (lost collaboration, mentorship, blurred boundaries). Both are concerned with effects on employees' professional lives. Option A is only Passage 1's concern. Option B — Passage 1 says it's permanent, Passage 2 doesn't address permanence specifically. Option D is only Passage 1's concern.

    Problem 2

    Answer: C

    Explanation: Passage 2 explicitly says "the enthusiasm for remote work overlooks significant drawbacks" — this maps directly to "overly optimistic because it fails to account for important negative consequences." Option A is wrong — Passage 2 disagrees. Option B overreaches — Passage 2 doesn't say Passage 1 is "fundamentally mistaken" about the definition. Option D is too harsh — Passage 2 doesn't accuse Passage 1 of deliberate deception.

    Problem 3

    Answer: B

    Explanation: Passage 1 argues remote work should be "embraced" and emphasizes benefits like flexibility and satisfaction. Author 1 would likely acknowledge the collaboration concern but argue that digital tools address it and that the overall balance favors remote work. Option A is too extreme — Author 1 wouldn't concede "all forms." Option C contradicts Passage 1's pro-remote stance (it doesn't dismiss collaboration). Option D directly contradicts Passage 1.

    Problem 4

    Answer: C

    Explanation: Passage 1 argues tests ARE objective and help reduce inequality (by providing a common yardstick). Passage 2 argues tests are NOT truly objective because they correlate with income and REINFORCE inequality. This is the core disagreement. Option A — both seem to agree grades vary across schools. Option B — both acknowledge socioeconomic factors affect admissions. Option D — both presumably agree colleges need evaluation methods; they just disagree on what methods.

    Problem 5

    Answer: A

    Explanation: Both passages use the same evidence — that grading is inconsistent, that socioeconomic factors matter — but interpret it differently. Passage 1 sees tests as the solution; Passage 2 sees tests as part of the problem. This is "alternative interpretation of the same evidence." Option B is wrong (they disagree). Option C is wrong (same topic). Option D is close but not quite right — Passage 2 rejects Passage 1's premise of objectivity.

    Problem 6

    Answer: B

    Explanation: Passage 1 explicitly argues that without tests, "admissions would rely even more heavily on factors correlated with socioeconomic advantage, such as extracurricular activities and personal essays polished by expensive consultants." Author 1 would argue that removing tests makes things worse, not better. Option A contradicts Passage 1. Option C is unsupported by either passage. Option D is absurd and unsupported.

    Quick Recap:

    • Read Passage 1 → answer its questions → read Passage 2 → answer its questions → cross-text questions
    • Identify each author's argument, evidence, and tone separately before comparing
    • For agreement questions: both passages must affirmatively support the answer
    • For disagreement questions: one must assert, the other must contradict
    • For response questions: answer from within the author's established worldview
    • Common pairings: competing interpretations, traditional vs. revisionist, optimistic vs. skeptical
