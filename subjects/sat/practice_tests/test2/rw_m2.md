> In 1935, the linguist George Kingsley Zipf observed a striking statistical regularity in natural language: the frequency of any word is inversely proportional to its rank in the frequency table. The most common word occurs about twice as often as the second most common, three times as often as the third, and so on. This "Zipf's law" has since been observed far beyond linguistics—in city populations, income distributions, internet traffic, and even the sizes of craters on the moon. Despite its ubiquity, no single explanation for Zipf's law has achieved consensus. Some researchers attribute it to preferential attachment processes, others to the principle of least effort in communication, and still others argue it is a mathematical inevitability of certain random processes, requiring no causal explanation at all.
