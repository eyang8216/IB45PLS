**Questions 21–23 are based on the following passage.**

> The concept of affordances, introduced by psychologist James J. Gibson in the 1970s, refers to the possibilities for action that an environment offers to an organism. A chair, for instance, affords sitting to a human adult; a doorknob affords turning. Crucially, affordances are relational properties—they exist not in the object alone nor in the perceiver alone, but in the relationship between the two. A tree branch that affords perching to a sparrow may not afford the same to a hawk, and a staircase affords climbing to a typically-abled person but may not afford the same to a wheelchair user. This relational conception has been enormously influential in design theory, where it has encouraged designers to think not in terms of what an object "is" but in terms of what it "offers" to its intended users.

> Physicist Richard Feynman was known not only for his scientific contributions but also for his philosophy of understanding. He famously insisted that true understanding meant being able to explain a concept in simple terms to a non-specialist. "If you can't explain it to a first-year student," he reportedly said, "you don't really understand it."
