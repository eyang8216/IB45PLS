# Lesson 27: Pronoun Agreement & Ambiguity

## What You'll Learn
Pronouns replace nouns to avoid repetition, but they must clearly and correctly refer back to their antecedents (the nouns they replace). In this lesson, you'll master pronoun-antecedent agreement in number and gender, eliminate ambiguous pronoun references, and properly use who/whom. These skills are essential for the SAT's Standard English Conventions questions.

## SAT Context
Pronoun agreement and clarity are tested on roughly 10-15% of SAT Standard English Conventions questions. The SAT tests three main things: (1) whether a pronoun matches its antecedent in number, (2) whether the antecedent is clear, and (3) correct usage of who vs. whom. The digital SAT also now accepts singular "they," an important shift from previous versions of the test.

---

## Core Rule: Pronoun-Antecedent Agreement

A **pronoun** (he, she, it, they, him, her, them, his, hers, its, their) must match its **antecedent** (the noun it replaces) in two ways:

1. **Number:** singular antecedent → singular pronoun; plural antecedent → plural pronoun
2. **Gender:** match logical gender (he/she/it)

**RIGHT:** The **student** brought **her** book. (student → singular feminine → her)
**RIGHT:** The **students** brought **their** books. (students → plural → their)
**WRONG:** The **student** brought **their** book. (Traditional SAT rule — though this is now changing, see below)

---

## Indefinite Pronouns and Agreement

Indefinite pronouns are the SAT's favorite trap. Many sound plural but are grammatically singular.

### Always Singular
*each, every, everyone, everybody, someone, somebody, anyone, anybody, no one, nobody, either, neither, one*

- **RIGHT:** Each of the students must bring **his or her** own supplies.
- **RIGHT (modern SAT):** Each of the students must bring **their** own supplies.
- **WRONG:** Each of the students must bring **their** own supplies. (Only wrong on the OLD SAT!)

> **🚨 SAT TRAP ALERT — THE BIG ONE:** The digital SAT now **accepts singular "they"** as a pronoun for singular antecedents, especially when the gender is unknown. However, you may still see "he or she" as an alternative correct answer. The key: if the SAT gives you BOTH "he or she" and "they" as options for a singular antecedent, look for other grammar issues to break the tie.

### Always Plural
*both, few, many, several, others*

- Everyone was invited, and **both** brought **their** friends.
- **Many** expressed **their** concerns.

## Pronoun Ambiguity

A pronoun is **ambiguous** when it's unclear which noun it refers to. The SAT tests this frequently.

**WRONG:** When Sarah visited Maria, **she** made dinner.
(Who made dinner? Sarah or Maria? Ambiguous.)

**RIGHT:** When Sarah visited Maria, **Sarah** made dinner.
**RIGHT:** When Sarah visited Maria, **the host** made dinner.

**WRONG:** The company told the employees that **they** would receive bonuses.
(Who receives bonuses — the company or the employees?)

**RIGHT:** The company told the employees that **the employees** would receive bonuses.

**WRONG:** The book fell off the shelf and **it** broke.
(Did the book break or did the shelf break?)

**RIGHT:** The book fell off the shelf and **the book's spine** broke.

> **🔍 PATTERN RECOGNITION:** When you see a pronoun (it, they, he, she) on the SAT, trace it back to its antecedent. If the sentence has two possible antecedents, the pronoun is ambiguous and wrong. The correct answer will replace the ambiguous pronoun with a specific noun.

## Who vs. Whom

This is the most feared grammar rule in English, but it's actually simple:

### The He/Him Test
Replace "who/whom" with "he" or "him" (rearrange the clause if needed):
- If **he** fits → use **who** (subject)
- If **him** fits → use **whom** (object)

**Example 1:** The person _____ called me was rude.
- Test: **He** called me. → "He" works → **who**

**Example 2:** The person _____ I called was rude.
- Test: I called **him**. → "Him" works → **whom**

**Example 3:** To _____ should I address this letter?
- Test: I should address this letter to **him**. → "Him" → **whom**

### Preposition Rule
After a preposition (to, for, from, with, by, about), always use **whom**:
- To **whom** it may concern.
- The candidate for **whom** I voted.
- The professor with **whom** I studied.

> **🔍 PATTERN RECOGNITION:** If you see "to ____" or "for ____" or "with ____" immediately before the blank, the answer is "whom" — every single time.

## Pronoun Consistency

When you start a sentence with a certain pronoun perspective, maintain it:

**WRONG:** When **one** studies for the SAT, **you** should take breaks.
(Shift from "one" to "you" — inconsistent)

**RIGHT:** When **one** studies for the SAT, **one** should take breaks.
**RIGHT:** When **you** study for the SAT, **you** should take breaks.

**WRONG:** **A person** should always follow **their** dreams.
(Traditionally: singular "person" with plural "their" — but now accepted!)

**RIGHT (traditional):** **A person** should always follow **his or her** dreams.
**RIGHT (modern):** **A person** should always follow **their** dreams. (SAT now OK with this)

## Possessive Pronouns — No Apostrophes!

A quick but important reminder:
- **its** = possessive (The dog wagged **its** tail.)
- **it's** = it is ( **It's** time to go.)
- **their** = possessive (**Their** house is big.)
- **they're** = they are (**They're** coming over.)
- **whose** = possessive (**Whose** book is this?)
- **who's** = who is (**Who's** coming to dinner?)

---

### Worked Example 1
**Question:** The orchestra members took __________ seats before the conductor arrived.

A) his or her
B) its
C) their
D) his

**Answer:** C — "their"

**Explanation:** The antecedent is "members," which is plural. We need a plural possessive pronoun. "Their" correctly refers to the plural "members." "His or her" is singular, "its" is singular and neuter (for things, not people), and "his" is singular masculine.

---

### Worked Example 2
**Question:** The biologist told the research assistant that __________ would present the findings at the conference.

A) he
B) she
C) they
D) the assistant

**Answer:** D — "the assistant"

**Explanation:** Options A, B, and C are all ambiguous — it's unclear whether "he/she/they" refers to the biologist or the research assistant. Only D removes the ambiguity by naming the specific person.

---

### Worked Example 3
**Question:** The award was given to the student __________ the faculty nominated for the scholarship.

A) who
B) whom
C) which
D) whose

**Answer:** B — "whom"

**Explanation:** Apply the he/him test: "The faculty nominated **him**." Since "him" fits, we need "whom" — the object form. "Who" is for subjects, "which" is for things (not people), and "whose" is possessive.

## Practice Problems

### Problem 1
Anyone who wants to participate in the charity event should submit __________ registration form by Friday.

A) his or her
B) their

D) our

### Problem 2
After the manager spoke with the client, __________ expressed appreciation for the thorough presentation.

A) she
B) he
C) they
D) the client

### Problem 3
The architect, __________ designs have won numerous awards, was selected to lead the museum renovation project.

A) who
B) whom
C) whose
D) which

### Problem 4
Neither of the candidates has announced __________ official platform for the upcoming election.

A) their
B) his or her
C) its
D) our

### Problem 5
The researchers, __________ the university recruited from leading institutions worldwide, brought diverse expertise to the department.

A) who
B) whom
C) which
D) whose

### Problem 6
If a student wants to improve __________ SAT score, consistent practice is essential.

A) their
B) his or her
C) its
D) our

---

## Answers

### Problem 1 — Answer: B ("their") — and A is also acceptable on current SAT

**Why B is correct:** On the digital SAT, singular "they/their" is now accepted for indefinite pronouns like "anyone." "Their" matches the singular antecedent "anyone" in modern usage.

**Why others are wrong:**
- A ("his or her"): Actually ALSO correct on the SAT! Both A and B would be acceptable, making this a question where you'd need to look for other differences.
- C ("its"): "Its" is for things/animals, not people ("anyone").
- D ("our"): "Our" is first-person plural; "anyone" is third-person singular.

(Note: On the actual SAT, you won't get two grammatically correct options — so one of these would be eliminated by context. In this case, if forced to pick between A and B on an older test, A is the traditionally "correct" answer; on the modern digital SAT, B is accepted.)

---

### Problem 2 — Answer: D ("the client")

**Why D is correct:** Options A, B, and C all create ambiguity — we can't tell whether the pronoun refers to "the manager" or "the client." D replaces the ambiguous pronoun with a specific noun, eliminating the confusion.

**Why others are wrong:**
- A ("she"): Ambiguous — could refer to either person.
- B ("he"): Ambiguous — could refer to either person.
- C ("they"): Ambiguous — could refer to either person.

---

### Problem 3 — Answer: C ("whose")

**Why C is correct:** We need a possessive pronoun to modify "designs" — the designs belong to the architect. "Whose" is the possessive form meaning "of whom."

**Why others are wrong:**
- A ("who"): Subject form; cannot modify a noun possessively.
- B ("whom"): Object form; cannot show possession.
- D ("which"): Refers to things, not people.

---

### Problem 4 — Answer: A ("their") — and B is also acceptable

**Why A is correct:** "Neither" is singular, but the modern SAT accepts singular "their." 

**Why others are wrong:**
- B ("his or her"): Also technically correct. On a real SAT question, context would eliminate one.
- C ("its"): Refers to things, not people ("candidates").
- D ("our"): First-person plural; doesn't match "neither" (third-person singular).

---

### Problem 5 — Answer: B ("whom")

**Why B is correct:** Apply the he/him test: "The university recruited **him**." Since "him" works, we need the object form "whom." The researchers are the objects of the verb "recruited."

**Why others are wrong:**
- A ("who"): Subject form, but the researchers are the object of "recruited."
- C ("which"): Refers to things, not people.
- D ("whose"): Possessive form, but we're not showing possession here.

---

### Problem 6 — Answer: A ("their") — and B is also acceptable

**Why A is correct:** "A student" is a singular antecedent of unknown gender. The digital SAT now accepts singular "their" in this context.

**Why others are wrong:**
- B ("his or her"): Also correct in traditional grammar.
- C ("its"): "Its" is for things, not people.
- D ("our"): "Our" is first-person; "a student" is third-person.

(Note: If this appeared on the SAT with both A and B, look for other grammar issues. In isolation, both are viable under current rules.)
