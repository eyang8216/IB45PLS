# Lesson 33: Apostrophes & Possessives

## What You'll Learn
Apostrophes have exactly three jobs in English: (1) forming possessives, (2) forming contractions, and (3) nothing else. Yet they're among the most commonly misused punctuation marks. The SAT tests apostrophes relentlessly — especially the its/it's distinction, which appears on virtually every test. This lesson covers every apostrophe rule you need, plus the homophone traps (their/they're/there, your/you're, whose/who's) that the SAT uses to separate careful readers from careless ones.

## SAT Context
Apostrophe and possessive questions appear in the Standard English Conventions domain on every SAT. The its/it's trap alone is one of the most frequently tested items. Combined with possessive formation rules and homophone distinctions, expect 2-4 apostrophe-related questions per test. These are some of the quickest points you can earn if you know the rules cold.

---

## Apostrophe Rule 1: Singular Possessives

For singular nouns, add **'s**:

- The **student's** book (the book of one student)
- The **dog's** leash (the leash of one dog)
- **James's** car (the car belonging to James — yes, even when the name ends in s)
- The **boss's** office (the office of the boss)
- The **class's** average (the average of one class)

> **Note on words ending in -s:** Both "James's" and "James'" are acceptable. The SAT accepts both. Be consistent with what the passage uses.

---

## Apostrophe Rule 2: Plural Possessives

For regular plural nouns (ending in -s), add only an apostrophe after the s:

- The **students'** books (the books of multiple students)
- The **dogs'** leashes (the leashes of multiple dogs)
- The **teachers'** lounge (the lounge for multiple teachers)
- The **Smiths'** house (the house of the Smith family)

For irregular plural nouns (not ending in -s), add **'s**:

- The **children's** playground
- The **men's** restroom
- The **women's** team
- The **people's** choice
- The **mice's** nest

---

## Apostrophe Rule 3: Joint vs. Separate Possession

**Joint possession** (two people share one thing): add **'s** only to the last name:
- **John and Mary's** car (one car, shared by both)
- **Mom and Dad's** bedroom (one bedroom, shared)

**Separate possession** (each person has their own): add **'s** to each name:
- **John's and Mary's** cars (John has a car, Mary has a car — two separate cars)
- **Einstein's and Newton's** theories (each has their own theories)

---

## Apostrophe Rule 4: NO Apostrophe for Simple Plurals

This is the "grocer's apostrophe" error — using an apostrophe to form a plain plural:

**WRONG:** I bought three apple's.
**RIGHT:** I bought three apples.

**WRONG:** The 1990's were a great decade.
**RIGHT:** The 1990s were a great decade. (SAT prefers no apostrophe for decades)

**WRONG:** DVD's are on sale.
**RIGHT:** DVDs are on sale.

**Exception:** Apostrophes can be used for pluralizing lowercase letters to avoid confusion:
- Mind your **p's** and **q's**.
- Dot your **i's** and cross your **t's**.

## The #1 SAT Trap: Its vs. It's

> **🚨 SAT TRAP ALERT — MOST TESTED ITEM:** The "its" vs. "it's" distinction appears on virtually EVERY SAT. The SAT test writers know that students confuse these. Getting this right is one of the easiest ways to pick up points.

### Its = Possessive (belonging to it)
- The dog wagged **its** tail.
- The company announced **its** earnings. (company = it)
- The committee released **its** report.
- **Its** color had faded over time.

**Think:** "Its" is like "his" and "hers" — possessive pronouns never have apostrophes. His, hers, its, theirs, ours, yours — NO apostrophes.

### It's = Contraction (it is or it has)
- **It's** time to go. (it is)
- **It's** been a long day. (it has)
- **It's** not what I expected. (it is)

**Quick test:** Expand "it's" to "it is" (or "it has"). If the sentence makes sense, use "it's." If not, use "its."

## Other Homophone Traps

### Their / They're / There
- **Their** = possessive (belonging to them): **Their** house, **their** ideas
- **They're** = they are: **They're** coming over. (Expand to "they are" to test.)
- **There** = location or existence: Put it over **there**. **There** is a problem.

### Your / You're
- **Your** = possessive (belonging to you): **Your** book, **your** turn
- **You're** = you are: **You're** doing great! (Expand to "you are" to test.)

### Whose / Who's
- **Whose** = possessive (belonging to whom): **Whose** jacket is this?
- **Who's** = who is or who has: **Who's** coming? (Who is) / **Who's** been here? (Who has)

### Its / It's (review)
- **Its** = possessive: The cat licked **its** paw.
- **It's** = it is/has: **It's** raining.

> **🔍 PATTERN RECOGNITION:** ALL possessive pronouns end WITHOUT an apostrophe: my, mine, your, yours, his, her, hers, its, our, ours, their, theirs, whose. If you see an apostrophe in a pronoun (it's, you're, they're, who's), it's ALWAYS a contraction.

## Other Apostrophe Rules

### Time and Money Expressions
Use apostrophes for expressions of time and money:

- **One week's** notice
- **Two weeks'** notice (plural → apostrophe after s)
- **One dollar's** worth
- **Ten dollars'** worth
- **A day's** work
- **Three days'** journey

### Possessives for Compound Nouns
Add **'s** to the end of the compound:
- My **mother-in-law's** recipe
- The **attorney general's** decision

### Possessives for Business/Organization Names
Follow the organization's own style, but the SAT is flexible:
- **McDonald's** (the company uses apostrophe-s)
- **Starbucks** (the company doesn't use an apostrophe)

---

### Worked Example 1
**Question:** The organization announced that __________ annual conference would be held virtually this year due to budget constraints.

A) its
B) it's
C) their
D) it is

**Answer:** A — "its"

**Explanation:** The sentence requires a possessive pronoun modifying "annual conference" — the conference belonging to the organization. "Its" is the possessive form. "It's" means "it is," which doesn't make sense here. "Their" is plural but "organization" is singular. "It is" would create an ungrammatical construction.

---

### Worked Example 2
**Question:** Both Dr. Patel's and Dr. Kim's research __________ published in the same issue of the journal.

A) was
B) were
C) has been
D) is

**Answer:** B — "were"

**Explanation:** "Dr. Patel's and Dr. Kim's research" indicates SEPARATE possession — each has their own research. This means we're talking about two separate research projects, so the subject is plural. "Were published" (plural) correctly agrees. If it were joint research ("Dr. Patel and Dr. Kim's research"), it would be singular ("was published").

---

### Worked Example 3
**Question:** The puppy chased __________ tail for several minutes before collapsing in exhaustion.

A) it's
B) its
C) its'
D) his

**Answer:** B — "its"

**Explanation:** The sentence needs a possessive pronoun modifying "tail" — the tail belonging to the puppy. "Its" is the correct possessive form. "It's" is a contraction meaning "it is." "Its'" doesn't exist — there is no such word. "His" would specify a male puppy, but the sentence doesn't indicate gender, and "its" is appropriate for an animal.

## Practice Problems

### Problem 1
The study's findings challenged __________ long-held assumptions about the relationship between diet and cognitive function.

A) researchers
B) researchers'
C) researcher's
D) researchers's

### Problem 2
__________ clear that the two candidates have very different visions for the future of the organization.

A) Its
B) It's
C) Its'
D) Their

### Problem 3
All of the __________ performances were evaluated by a panel of professional musicians.

A) participant's
B) participants
C) participants'
D) participants's

### Problem 4
The museum displayed __________ collection of ancient artifacts, some dating back more than three thousand years.

A) it's
B) its
C) his
D) their

### Problem 5
__________ book is this? I found it on the desk in the library.

A) Who's
B) Whose
C) Whos
D) Whom

### Problem 6
The two __________ research projects received funding from the same government grant, though their methodologies differed significantly.

A) scientist's
B) scientists
C) scientists'
D) scientists's

---

## Answers

### Problem 1 — Answer: B ("researchers'")

**Why B is correct:** The sentence says the findings challenged assumptions — assumptions belonging to researchers (plural). "Researchers'" is the plural possessive form (apostrophe after the s).

**Why others are wrong:**
- A ("researchers"): This is a simple plural, not possessive. "Challenged researchers assumptions" is grammatically incorrect — it needs a possessive.
- C ("researcher's"): Singular possessive — would mean the assumptions of one researcher, but context suggests multiple researchers.
- D ("researchers's"): Incorrect form. Plural nouns ending in -s take only an apostrophe, not 's.

---

### Problem 2 — Answer: B ("It's")

**Why B is correct:** The sentence needs the contraction "it is" at the beginning: "It is clear that the two candidates..." Expand to test: "It is clear..." — makes perfect sense. So "It's" is correct.

**Why others are wrong:**
- A ("Its"): This is possessive. "Its clear" doesn't make sense — what does the clear belong to?
- C ("Its'"): This form doesn't exist in English.
- D ("Their"): "Their clear" doesn't make grammatical sense.

---

### Problem 3 — Answer: C ("participants'")

**Why C is correct:** "All of the" indicates multiple participants. Their performances (plural) were evaluated. "Participants'" is the correct plural possessive: the performances belonging to multiple participants.

**Why others are wrong:**
- A ("participant's"): Singular possessive — would mean the performances of one participant, contradicting "all."
- B ("participants"): Simple plural, not possessive. "Participants performances" is grammatically missing the possessive.
- D ("participants's"): Incorrect form — plural nouns ending in -s only need an apostrophe.

---

### Problem 4 — Answer: B ("its")

**Why B is correct:** The museum's collection — the collection belonging to it (the museum). "Its" is the possessive pronoun. No apostrophe needed for possessive "its."

**Why others are wrong:**
- A ("it's"): Contraction for "it is" — "The museum displayed it is collection" makes no sense.
- C ("his"): "His" would imply the museum is a male person, which is incorrect.
- D ("their"): "Their" is plural, but "museum" is singular.

---

### Problem 5 — Answer: B ("Whose")

**Why B is correct:** The sentence asks about possession — which person does the book belong to? "Whose" is the possessive form of "who." "Whose book is this?" = "This book belongs to whom?"

**Why others are wrong:**
- A ("Who's"): Contraction for "who is" — "Who is book is this?" makes no sense.
- C ("Whos"): Not a word in English.
- D ("Whom"): Object form of "who," not possessive. "Whom book is this?" is incorrect.

---

### Problem 6 — Answer: C ("scientists'")

**Why C is correct:** "Two scientists" indicates plural. Their research projects (separate possession — each has their own). "Scientists'" is the plural possessive: the research projects of two (or more) scientists.

**Why others are wrong:**
- A ("scientist's"): Singular possessive — "two" contradicts a singular possessive.
- B ("scientists"): Simple plural, not possessive. Missing the apostrophe.
- D ("scientists's"): Incorrect form for plural nouns ending in -s.
