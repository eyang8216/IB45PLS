Lesson 39: Adding, Deleting & Revising Information

What You'll Learn

On the SAT, you'll be asked whether to add or delete sentences, and how to revise sentences for conciseness and precision. This lesson gives you the decision framework to get these questions right every time.

SAT Context

These questions appear in the Expression of Ideas section — about 3-5 questions per module. They test your ability to judge whether information supports a paragraph's main idea and whether the language is as tight and precise as possible. The SAT rewards clarity and purpose; every word must earn its place.

The Add/Delete Decision Framework

When the SAT asks "Should the writer make this addition?" or "Should the sentence be deleted?", you need a simple test:

THE ADD TEST: Ask two questions:

  • Does the sentence directly support the main idea of the paragraph?
  • Does it provide NEW (not redundant) information?

    • If the answer to BOTH is yes, keep it. If either is no, delete it.

    THE DELETE TEST: Ask one question:

    • Is the sentence off-topic, redundant, or contradictory to the paragraph's main idea?

    If yes, delete it. If it supports and advances the main idea, keep it.

    > 🔍 PATTERN RECOGNITION: SAT add/delete questions almost always follow the same structure: they present a sentence that sounds interesting or factually true, but is OFF-TOPIC. The correct answer is almost always "No, it should be deleted because it does not support the paragraph's main idea."

    Revising for Conciseness

    The SAT hates wordiness. When you're asked to revise a sentence or choose the best version, the shortest grammatically correct option that preserves all essential meaning is usually correct.

    Common types of wordiness:

  • Redundancy: "The annual event happens each year" → "The event happens each year"
  • Empty phrases: "Due to the fact that" → "Because"
  • Unnecessary modifiers: "completely finished" → "finished"
  • Nominalizations: "make a decision" → "decide"
  • Circular expressions: "the reason is because" → "the reason is"

    • More examples of wordiness → concise:

    | Wordy | Concise |

    |-------|---------|

    | At this point in time | Now |

    | In spite of the fact that | Although |

    | Has the ability to | Can |

    | In the event that | If |

    | A large number of | Many |

    | Is reflective of | Reflects |

    | In order to | To |

    > 🚨 SAT TRAP ALERT: Students often pick the longest, most formal-sounding revision because it "feels academic." The SAT actually rewards the briefest version that doesn't lose meaning. When you see multiple grammatically correct options, the shortest is probably correct.

    Revising for Precision

    Sometimes the SAT wants you to choose MORE specific language, not less. This typically happens when:

    • A sentence uses vague words like "things," "stuff," "it," or "they" without clear antecedents
    • A description could be made more vivid or accurate with precise terminology
    • A verb like "do" or "make" could be replaced with a more exact verb

    The Precision Hierarchy:

  • Does the revision use the most specific, accurate terms?
  • Does it avoid vagueness without becoming wordy?
  • Does it maintain the exact intended meaning?

    • Example:

    • Vague: "The scientist did an experiment on the stuff."
    • Precise: "The scientist conducted an experiment on the bacterial culture."

    Example:

    • Vague: "The team had a meeting about the problem."
    • Precise: "The engineering team convened to address the structural deficiency."

    > 🔍 PATTERN RECOGNITION: When precision and conciseness compete, precision wins. The SAT will sometimes have you choose a slightly longer option if it's meaningfully more specific. But most of the time, the concise option IS the precise option.

    Worked Example 1: Adding a Sentence

    Question:

    [Passage excerpt about how bees navigate using the sun's position]

    The writer is considering adding the following sentence:

    "Bees are also known for producing honey, which humans have harvested for thousands of years."

    Should the writer make this addition?

    A) Yes, because it provides an additional fact about bees.

    B) Yes, because it explains why bees are important to humans.

    C) No, because it does not support the paragraph's focus on bee navigation.

    D) No, because it contradicts the idea that bees use the sun.

    Answer: C

    Explanation: The paragraph is about how bees navigate. The proposed sentence, while factually true, is about honey production — a different topic entirely. The addition should be rejected because it doesn't support the paragraph's main idea. Options A and B are classic SAT traps: they note something positive about the sentence ("it's true!" / "it shows importance!") without addressing whether it's ON-TOPIC.

    Worked Example 2: Deleting a Sentence

    Question:

    [Passage excerpt about the invention of the printing press and its impact on literacy]

    The writer is considering deleting the following sentence:

    "Johannes Gutenberg was born in Mainz, Germany, around 1400."

    Should the writer delete this sentence?

    A) Yes, because it provides irrelevant biographical detail in a paragraph about impact on literacy.

    B) Yes, because the information is factually incorrect.

    C) No, because it provides useful context about the inventor.

    D) No, because it helps establish the time period of the invention.

    Answer: A

    Explanation: If the paragraph's focus is on the impact of the printing press on literacy, Gutenberg's birthplace and birth year don't support that focus. Biographical trivia, even if interesting, doesn't advance the argument about literacy. Option D is a common trap — the time period might feel relevant, but the paragraph's argument isn't about dating the invention; it's about the invention's effects.

    Worked Example 3: Revising for Precision

    Question:

    Which choice most effectively replaces the underlined portion?

    "The researcher's findings had an impact on the way scientists think about the disease."

    A) made scientists reconsider their understanding of

    B) had a big effect on how scientists view

    C) impacted scientists' thoughts regarding

    D) changed scientific thinking concerning

    Answer: A

    Explanation: "Made scientists reconsider their understanding of" is the most precise revision. It specifies HOW the impact occurred (by causing reconsideration) and what changed (understanding). Option B is still vague ("big effect"). Option C uses the awkward nominalization "impacted...thoughts." Option D is acceptable but less precise than A — "changed scientific thinking" is broader and doesn't capture the specific notion of reconsideration that "reconsider their understanding of" provides.

    The Decision Flowchart

    When you face an add/delete question, follow this flowchart in your head:

  • Read the paragraph's topic sentence. What is the main idea?
  • Read the sentence in question.
  • Ask: Does this sentence directly support the main idea?
    • YES → continue to step 4
    • NO → select "delete" and choose the reason that matches
  • Ask: Does this sentence add NEW information?
    • YES → select "keep/add" with the reason "supports the main idea"
    • NO (redundant) → select "delete" with the reason "redundant"

    > 🚨 SAT TRAP ALERT: The SAT will often frame a "keep" option with a reason like "it provides an interesting detail" or "it adds to the reader's understanding." Don't fall for it. The ONLY valid reason to keep a sentence is that it supports the paragraph's main idea.

    Problem 1

    [1] The Great Pacific Garbage Patch is a massive accumulation of plastic debris in the Pacific Ocean. [2] Ocean currents concentrate waste from across the Pacific into this region. [3] The patch is roughly twice the size of Texas. [4] Hawaii became a U.S. state in 1959 after decades of territorial status.

    The writer wants to delete sentence 4. Should it be deleted?

    A) Yes, because it is unrelated to the topic of ocean pollution.

    B) Yes, because Hawaii is not mentioned elsewhere in the paragraph.

    C) No, because it provides geographical context for the Pacific Ocean.

    D) No, because it adds historical interest to the passage.

    Problem 2

    [1] The Great Pacific Garbage Patch is a massive accumulation of plastic debris in the Pacific Ocean. [2] Ocean currents concentrate waste from across the Pacific into this region. [3] The patch is roughly twice the size of Texas. [4] Hawaii became a U.S. state in 1959 after decades of territorial status.

    The writer wants to delete sentence 4. Should it be deleted?

    A) Yes, because it is unrelated to the topic of ocean pollution.

    B) Yes, because Hawaii is not mentioned elsewhere in the paragraph.

    C) No, because it provides geographical context for the Pacific Ocean.

    D) No, because it adds historical interest to the passage.

    Problem 2

    [1] Researchers have found that listening to music while studying can affect concentration. [2] Music with lyrics tends to distract from reading tasks, as the brain processes both linguistic inputs simultaneously. [3] Instrumental music, however, can mask disruptive background noise without competing for verbal processing resources. [4] Many famous composers, including Mozart and Beethoven, created instrumental works.

    The writer is considering deleting sentence 4. Should it be deleted?

    A) Yes, because it shifts focus from the research findings to unrelated biographical trivia.

    B) Yes, because Mozart and Beethoven are not scientists.

    C) No, because it provides examples of instrumental music composers.

    D) No, because it strengthens the argument that instrumental music is beneficial.

    Problem 3

    [1] Vertical farming — growing crops in stacked layers indoors — offers several advantages over traditional agriculture. [2] It uses significantly less water, as hydroponic systems recirculate water rather than losing it to soil absorption and runoff. [3] It also eliminates the need for chemical pesticides, since the controlled indoor environment keeps pests out. [4] The world's population is expected to reach nearly 10 billion by 2050, creating unprecedented demand for food.

    The writer is considering adding the following sentence after sentence 3:

    "Additionally, vertical farms can operate year-round, unaffected by seasonal weather patterns."

    Should this addition be made?

    A) Yes, because it provides another advantage of vertical farming that supports the paragraph's main idea.

    B) Yes, because it explains how vertical farming saves water.

    C) No, because the paragraph already mentions enough advantages.

    D) No, because year-round operation is not relevant to agriculture.

    Problem 4

    Which revision most effectively reduces wordiness while preserving meaning?

    Original: "In light of the fact that the experiment produced results that were not consistent with the original hypothesis, the research team came to the conclusion that a new approach would be necessary to take."

    A) Because the experiment produced inconsistent results, the team concluded a new approach was necessary.

    B) Due to the fact that the experiment's results were inconsistent with the hypothesis, the research team reached a conclusion about needing a new approach.

    C) The experiment produced results not consistent with the original hypothesis, so the research team came to the conclusion that a new approach was necessary.

    D) In light of the experiment's inconsistent results, the team determined that necessity required a new approach.

    Problem 5

    [1] The reintroduction of wolves to Yellowstone National Park in 1995 triggered a remarkable ecological transformation known as a trophic cascade. [2] Wolves reduced elk populations, which allowed willow and aspen trees to regrow along stream banks. [3] The returning trees stabilized the banks, reducing erosion and improving water quality. [4] This, in turn, benefited beaver populations, which depend on healthy riparian habitats. [5] Wolves are highly social animals that live and hunt in packs, typically consisting of a breeding pair and their offspring.

    The writer wants to delete sentence 5. Should it be deleted?

    A) Yes, because it breaks the logical chain of ecological effects with unrelated information about wolf social behavior.

    B) Yes, because the passage already implies that wolves hunt.

    C) No, because it provides essential information about wolf behavior.

    D) No, because it explains why wolves were able to reduce elk populations.

    Problem 6

    Which choice provides the most precise wording?

    Original: "The company's new policy had a big effect on how employees felt about their jobs."

    A) The company's new policy significantly influenced employee job satisfaction.

    B) The company's new policy had a large influence on employee feelings regarding their work.

    C) The company's new policy made employees feel differently about their positions.

    D) The company's new policy changed the way employees experienced their employment situation.

    Answers

    Problem 1

    Answer: A

    Explanation: Sentence 4 is about Hawaii's statehood, which is completely unrelated to the topic of the Great Pacific Garbage Patch and ocean pollution. It should be deleted because it's off-topic. Option B is a weak reason — a sentence isn't necessarily wrong just because its subject isn't mentioned elsewhere. Option C is wrong because Hawaii's statehood doesn't provide geographical context for understanding the garbage patch. Option D is wrong because "historical interest" isn't a valid reason to keep off-topic information.

    Problem 2

    Answer: A

    Explanation: The paragraph is about research findings on how music affects concentration. Sentence 4 shifts to mentioning famous composers who wrote instrumental music, which doesn't advance the research-based argument. While Mozart and Beethoven did compose instrumental music, that fact doesn't contribute to the paragraph's main point about cognitive effects of music. Option C is wrong because naming composers doesn't function as a useful example — the paragraph isn't discussing which instrumental music to listen to. Option D is wrong because naming composers doesn't strengthen the scientific argument.

    Problem 3

    Answer: A

    Explanation: The paragraph discusses advantages of vertical farming. The proposed sentence adds another advantage (year-round operation) that directly supports the paragraph's main idea and provides non-redundant information. It fits perfectly after the existing list of advantages. Option C is wrong because there's no rule against having multiple advantages if they're all on-topic. Option D is wrong because year-round operation IS directly relevant to agriculture and farming.

    Problem 4

    Answer: A

    Explanation: Option A preserves all the meaning of the original in the fewest words. It replaces "In light of the fact that" with "Because," "produced results that were not consistent" with "produced inconsistent results," and "came to the conclusion that" with "concluded." Option B retains many wordy phrases. Option C is shorter but still contains "came to the conclusion that." Option D is awkward ("necessity required") and less natural than A. The SAT rewards natural, concise phrasing.

    Problem 5

    Answer: A

    Explanation: Sentences 1-4 form a tight logical chain showing the trophic cascade: wolves → fewer elk → more trees → stabilized banks → better water → more beavers. Sentence 5 about wolf social behavior (pack hunting) breaks this chain. It's factually true but does not advance the cause-and-effect narrative. Option D is a classic trap — pack behavior might explain hunting, but the paragraph isn't explaining HOW wolves hunt; it's tracing the effects of wolves being present.

    Problem 6

    Answer: A

    Explanation: "Significantly influenced employee job satisfaction" is both more precise and more concise than the original. "Big effect" becomes the precise "significantly influenced," and "how employees felt about their jobs" becomes the standard term "employee job satisfaction." Option B is wordy ("large influence on employee feelings regarding their work"). Option C is vague ("differently" — how?). Option D is unnecessarily wordy without adding precision.

    Quick Recap:

    • Add only if the sentence supports the main idea AND is non-redundant
    • Delete if off-topic, redundant, or contradictory
    • For conciseness: the shortest grammatically-correct option that preserves meaning wins
    • For precision: replace vague words with specific ones
    • Don't be seduced by interesting but off-topic facts
