---

## 1. What Public Goods Are and Why They Matter

Some goods cannot be provided by private markets at all — or at least cannot be provided in sufficient quantity — even though society values them enormously. These are **public goods**, and understanding them is essential because they represent the clearest case of market failure in the entire economics syllabus. When a market fails to produce something that everyone wants and that would cost less to provide than the total benefit it would generate, government intervention is not just helpful — it is the only way the good gets produced at all.

The defining feature of a public good is not that it is provided by the government. Many things governments provide — schooling, healthcare — are not public goods in the technical sense. The defining feature is also not that the good is "good for the public." A public good has two very specific characteristics: it is non-rivalrous and non-excludable. Both conditions must hold for a good to be a pure public good.

---

## 2. The Two Defining Characteristics

### Non-Rivalry

A good is **non-rivalrous** when one person's consumption of it does not reduce the amount available for anyone else. The marginal cost of providing the good to one additional user is zero.

Street lighting is non-rivalrous. If you walk under a streetlight, the light still illuminates the pavement for the next person who comes along. Your consumption does not use up any of the light. Adding a hundred pedestrians under the same streetlight costs nothing extra — the bulb was already on, and it burns the same electricity whether one person walks beneath it or fifty do.

A sandwich, by contrast, is rivalrous. If you eat it, nobody else can. Your consumption reduces the quantity available to others by exactly one sandwich. Most goods in the economy — food, clothing, cars, housing — are rivalrous.

### Non-Excludability

A good is **non-excludable** when it is impossible, or prohibitively expensive, to prevent someone from consuming it once it has been provided — even if that person has not paid for it.

National defence is non-excludable. If a country's army protects its borders, every person inside those borders is protected. There is no practical way to say, "This neighbourhood paid its defence tax, so missiles will be intercepted over this area, but that neighbourhood did not pay, so missiles can land there." Defence covers everyone within the territory, payer and non-payer alike.

A cinema, by contrast, is excludable. The cinema owner can station an employee at the door to check tickets and turn away anyone who has not paid. Most goods in the economy are excludable — you can be prevented from consuming them if you do not pay.

### Pure Public Goods

A **pure public good** is one that is both non-rivalrous AND non-excludable. Both characteristics must be present. The combination is what makes private provision impossible.

---

## 3. The Free-Rider Problem

### What the Free-Rider Problem Is

The **free-rider problem** is the central reason markets fail to provide public goods. It works like this: because the good is non-excludable, no one can be forced to pay for it. Because no one can be forced to pay, each individual has an incentive to let others pay while they enjoy the benefit for free. And because everyone reasons the same way, nobody pays — so the good is never provided.

### A Numerical Illustration

Suppose a street of 100 houses is very dark at night, and residents worry about safety. A streetlight would cost £8,000 to install and would benefit every household. Each household values the streetlight at £100 — they would be willing to pay up to £100 to have it installed.

The total benefit is 100 households × £100 = £10,000. The cost is £8,000. The net social benefit is £2,000 — the streetlight ought to be installed. The benefit-cost ratio is 1.25 to 1.

Now suppose one resident goes door to door asking each household to contribute £80 — an equal share of the cost. Each household faces the following logic:

"If all 99 other households contribute their £80, the streetlight will be installed, and I will benefit from it whether I pay or not — because once it is up, the light shines on my doorstep just as brightly as on everyone else's. If most other households do not contribute, my £80 will not be enough to reach the £8,000 target anyway. In either case, my individual contribution does not change the outcome, but keeping my £80 makes me better off. I should not pay."

If all 100 households follow this reasoning, nobody contributes. The streetlight is not installed. A project that would generate £10,000 of benefit for £8,000 of cost — a clear net gain for society — fails because of the free-rider problem. This is market failure in its purest form: the market produces zero of a good that society values at £10,000.

A common mistake students make is thinking the free-rider problem means some people are selfish and others are generous. The logic of free-riding does not depend on selfishness. A perfectly altruistic person who wants the streetlight for everyone would still reason that their individual contribution will not determine whether it gets built, and the money might be better spent on something else. The problem is structural, not moral.

### Why Private Firms Cannot Provide Public Goods

A private firm considering whether to install streetlights would face an even more fundamental problem than the resident going door to door. The firm cannot charge for the light. Once the light is shining, anyone can walk under it. There is no mechanism — no ticket booth, no turnstile, no password — that restricts the benefit to paying customers.

Even if the firm could somehow charge, non-rivalry creates a second problem. The efficient price for a non-rivalrous good is zero, because the marginal cost of an additional user is zero. Charging any positive price would exclude some people who would benefit at zero social cost — which is allocatively inefficient. But if the price is zero, the firm earns no revenue and cannot cover its costs.

The result is that the free market produces zero public goods. Not too few — zero. The market failure is complete.

## 4. Classifying Goods: The 2×2 Matrix

Economists classify all goods along the two dimensions of rivalry and excludability. The matrix helps you see where public goods fit in the broader landscape.

|  | **Excludable** | **Non-Excludable** |
|---|---|---|
| **Rivalrous** | Private goods | Common pool resources |
| **Non-Rivalrous** | Club goods (quasi-public) | Pure public goods |

### Private Goods

Private goods are both rivalrous and excludable. Your lunch is a private good: if you eat it, nobody else can (rivalrous), and the café can refuse to give it to you until you pay (excludable). Markets provide private goods efficiently because firms can charge for them and consumers who do not pay can be excluded.

### Common Pool Resources

Common pool resources are rivalrous but non-excludable. Fish in the open ocean are the classic example. If one fishing boat catches a tuna, that tuna is gone — nobody else can catch it (rivalrous). But the open ocean is vast, and no single authority can prevent boats from entering and fishing (non-excludable). The result is overfishing — the tragedy of the commons.

### Club Goods (Quasi-Public Goods)

Club goods — also called quasi-public goods — are non-rivalrous but excludable. A cinema is a club good: up to the capacity of the screen, one more viewer does not diminish anyone else's experience (non-rivalrous), but the cinema can exclude non-ticket-holders at the door (excludable). A toll road with no congestion is also a club good: one more car does not slow anyone down, but the toll barrier excludes non-payers.

Markets can provide club goods, though they may not provide them at the socially optimal quantity. A cinema charges a price above zero, which excludes some people who would value the film at more than the zero marginal cost of letting them in — a minor inefficiency, but not the complete failure we see with pure public goods.

### Pure Public Goods

Pure public goods occupy the bottom-right cell: non-rivalrous and non-excludable. National defence, street lighting, lighthouses, clean air, and basic scientific knowledge are all pure public goods. Markets fail to provide them entirely.

---

## 5. Examples of Public Goods

### National Defence

National defence is the clearest example of a pure public good. An army, navy, and air force protect the entire territory of a country. Protecting one additional citizen costs nothing extra (non-rivalrous). There is no way to protect some citizens within the borders but not others (non-excludable). No private firm could profitably sell "defence" to individual households, because any household that did not pay would still be protected. National defence is therefore provided by the government and funded through compulsory taxation in every country in the world.

### Street Lighting

A streetlight illuminates the pavement for everyone who walks beneath it. One pedestrian's use does not dim the light for the next (non-rivalrous). Once the light is on, anyone can walk under it, and there is no practical way to charge each pedestrian a toll (non-excludable). Local governments install streetlights and fund them through council tax or equivalent local levies.

### Lighthouses

A lighthouse warns ships of a rocky coastline. The light is visible to all ships within range, and guiding one additional ship past the rocks costs nothing (non-rivalrous). The lighthouse operator cannot selectively block the light for ships that have not paid (non-excludable). The economist Ronald Coase famously pointed out that some English lighthouses in the 18th and 19th centuries were privately built and funded through harbour dues collected by the government on behalf of private operators — but this worked only because the government compelled payment at the port. Without that compulsory element, the free-rider problem would have prevented private provision.

### Clean Air

Clean air is non-rivalrous — one person breathing it does not reduce the supply for others. It is non-excludable — you cannot fence off a volume of clean air and charge people to breathe from it. Clean air is a pure public good, which is why pollution — which degrades this public good — is such a difficult policy challenge. No private firm has an incentive to provide clean air, because it cannot charge for it. The government must regulate pollution to protect the quality of this public good.

### Basic Scientific Knowledge

When Isaac Newton discovered the laws of motion, he did not patent them. The knowledge became available to everyone, and one person's use of Newton's laws does not prevent anyone else from using them (non-rivalrous). Once published, scientific knowledge cannot be restricted to paying customers — anyone can read the paper and apply the ideas (non-excludable). This is why governments fund basic research: private firms underinvest in research with public-good characteristics because they cannot capture the full social return. A pharmaceutical company will invest billions in a drug it can patent (a club good, because patents make knowledge temporarily excludable), but no private company would fund pure mathematics.

## 6. Government Provision of Public Goods

### How Government Provision Works

The standard solution to the public goods problem is government provision. The government decides which public goods to provide and in what quantity. It funds them through compulsory taxation, which solves the free-rider problem by making payment mandatory. It then makes the goods available free at the point of use, which is efficient because the marginal cost of an additional user is zero.

For national defence, the government allocates a budget — the United Kingdom spent approximately £54 billion on defence in 2024, or about 2.3% of GDP — and uses it to maintain armed forces that protect the entire country. Every taxpayer contributes, and every citizen is protected.

### Advantages of Government Provision

Government provision directly solves the free-rider problem. Compulsory taxation means that everyone who benefits also contributes, at least in proportion to their tax liability.

Government provision can capture economies of scale. Having one army is cheaper and more effective than having fifty competing private militias, each covering a different postal code. The fixed costs of public goods — the lighthouse tower, the streetlight pole, the military base — are spread over the entire population.

Government provision is equitable. Everyone receives the benefit regardless of income. National defence protects the homeless person and the billionaire equally. Streetlights shine on council estates and gated communities alike.

### Disadvantages and the Risk of Government Failure

Government provision is not costless or guaranteed to be efficient. Several problems can arise.

The first problem is determining the optimal quantity. For a private good like sandwiches, the market price tells producers how much consumers value the marginal sandwich and how much it costs to produce. For national defence, there is no market price. How much defence is "enough"? The government must rely on cost-benefit analysis, strategic assessments, and political processes — none of which guarantees that the chosen level of defence spending equals the level that would maximise net social benefit. The government might spend too little (leaving the country vulnerable) or too much (wasting resources that could have funded healthcare or education).

The second problem is productive inefficiency. Government agencies do not face competition, so they have weaker incentives to minimise costs. A government department that builds roads may have little reason to find the cheapest asphalt supplier, because its budget is determined by political negotiation rather than market discipline. The result can be higher costs than necessary for a given quantity of public good.

The third problem is political distortion. Spending on public goods is allocated through political processes, not market signals. A member of parliament may fight for a flood defence project in their constituency not because it has the highest benefit-cost ratio among all possible flood defence projects, but because it secures votes. Military contractors may lobby for larger defence budgets than are strategically justified. The allocation of public goods can become detached from the actual pattern of social benefits.

The fourth problem is the opportunity cost of taxation. Every pound the government raises in taxes to fund public goods is a pound that households and firms cannot spend or invest as they choose. Taxes also distort incentives: an income tax may reduce the incentive to work; a corporate tax may reduce the incentive to invest. The deadweight loss of taxation means that the social cost of raising £1 of government revenue is greater than £1.

A common mistake students make is to treat government provision as a perfect solution. The IB rewards students who acknowledge that government provision solves the free-rider problem but may introduce new inefficiencies. The relevant comparison is not between an imperfect government and a perfect market — it is between an imperfect government and an imperfect market. For pure public goods, the market's imperfection is catastrophic (zero provision), so government provision, with all its flaws, is the better option.

## 7. Worked Example: Benefit-Cost Analysis of a Public Good

A city of 50,000 households is considering building a flood barrier. The barrier would cost £30 million to construct and would last for 30 years with negligible maintenance. Engineers estimate that the barrier would prevent flood damage averaging £2 million per year across the city. The government's discount rate for public projects is 3%.

**Problem:** Should the government build the flood barrier?

**Approach:** We need to compare the total benefit of the barrier over its lifetime with the total cost. Since benefits occur over 30 years, we calculate the present value of the stream of annual benefits and compare it to the upfront cost.

**Step 1: Calculate the present value of benefits.** The barrier prevents £2 million in damage each year for 30 years. Using the present value formula for an annuity:

PV of benefits = £2 million × [(1 − (1.03)^(−30)) / 0.03]

PV of benefits = £2 million × [(1 − 0.4120) / 0.03]
PV of benefits = £2 million × [0.5880 / 0.03]
PV of benefits = £2 million × 19.60
PV of benefits = £39.2 million

**Step 2: Compare benefits to costs.** The cost is £30 million (all upfront). The present value of benefits is £39.2 million.

Net present value = £39.2 million − £30 million = £9.2 million.

The benefit-cost ratio = £39.2 / £30 = 1.31.

**Step 3: Consider the public goods aspect.** The flood barrier is non-rivalrous (protecting one more household costs nothing) and non-excludable (once built, it protects all 50,000 households — you cannot selectively protect some homes from flooding and not others). It is a pure public good. The private market would not build it, because no firm could charge individual households for flood protection. The free-rider problem would prevent voluntary collective action. Government provision is the only viable mechanism.

**Step 4: Conclusion.** The flood barrier has a positive net present value of £9.2 million. The government should build it, funding the £30 million cost through taxation (a one-off levy of £600 per household, or about £20 per household per year if spread over 30 years through borrowing).

**Why this makes sense:** The barrier generates benefits worth £39.2 million for a cost of £30 million. Each household pays £600 but receives, on average, £784 in flood protection benefits (in present value terms). The fact that the market would fail to provide this good — despite it being worth providing by a wide margin — is why government intervention in public goods is essential.

## 8. Quasi-Public Goods and the Shifting Boundary

Not all goods fit neatly into the four cells of the matrix. Some goods change their classification depending on circumstances.

### Roads

An uncongested rural road in the Scottish Highlands is non-rivalrous (one more car does not slow anyone down) and non-excludable (there is no toll barrier). It is a pure public good — or very close to one.

A congested urban motorway at 8:30 AM on a Tuesday is rivalrous (each additional car increases travel time for everyone else) but still non-excludable if there is no toll. It has become a common pool resource.

That same motorway with electronic congestion charging becomes a club good: the toll barrier makes it excludable, and if the toll is set to keep traffic flowing freely, it is non-rivalrous at the margin.

A single physical road can shift between three different classifications depending on the time of day and the institutional arrangements. The IB rewards students who recognise that goods are not permanently assigned to one cell of the matrix.

### Broadcast Television

Free-to-air television (BBC One, ITV) is a pure public good. One more viewer does not affect the signal quality for anyone else (non-rivalrous), and anyone with a television set can tune in without paying at the point of use (non-excludable).

Subscription television (Netflix, Sky) is a club good. It is still non-rivalrous — one more subscriber streaming a show does not affect the experience of other subscribers — but it is excludable because the provider can require a password and payment.

The same content — a television programme — can be delivered as a pure public good or a club good, depending on the business model.

## IB Exam Tip

When answering a question about public goods, always state both defining characteristics — non-rivalry and non-excludability — and explain what each means. Do not assume the examiner knows you know. Write: "A pure public good is both non-rivalrous, meaning that one person's consumption does not reduce the amount available for others, and non-excludable, meaning that it is impossible to prevent non-payers from consuming the good."

Then explain the free-rider problem by linking the characteristics to the outcome: "Because the good is non-excludable, no firm can charge for it. Because no firm can charge for it, no firm can earn revenue. Because no firm can earn revenue, the market produces zero of the good. This is a complete market failure."

Finally, for evaluation questions, acknowledge that government provision has its own problems — difficulty determining optimal quantity, productive inefficiency, and the opportunity cost of taxation — but conclude that these problems are less severe than the market's failure to provide the good at all.

The 2×2 matrix is a favourite IB diagram. Practise drawing it from memory with all four cells labelled and at least one example in each cell.

## Answers

**Answer 1.** (a) The airline seat is a private good. It is rivalrous: if you occupy the seat, nobody else can occupy it on that flight. It is excludable: the airline will not let you board without a ticket. Markets provide airline seats efficiently because the airline can charge for each seat and exclude non-payers. (b) The coral reef in international waters is a common pool resource. It is rivalrous: if one diver breaks off a piece of coral as a souvenir, that piece is gone for everyone else; if too many divers visit, the reef degrades. It is non-excludable: international waters are not owned by any country, so no authority can prevent access. The result is overuse — a tragedy of the commons. (c) The password-protected online newspaper is a club good. It is non-rivalrous: one more reader accessing the website does not reduce the availability of articles for other readers. It is excludable: the paywall and password system prevent non-subscribers from accessing the content. The market can provide this good because the newspaper can charge subscription fees. (d) The public radio broadcast is a pure public good. It is non-rivalrous: one more listener tuning in does not degrade the signal for anyone else. It is non-excludable: anyone with a radio receiver can pick up the broadcast, and the station cannot selectively block individual receivers. Markets would not provide this good (at least not through direct user charges), which is why public radio is funded through government grants or voluntary donations. (e) The fish in a privately owned lake is a private good. It is rivalrous: if you catch a fish, nobody else can catch that fish. It is excludable: the owner charges for fishing permits and can prevent non-permit-holders from fishing. The private owner has an incentive to manage the fish stock sustainably because the lake's value depends on there being fish to catch in future years — unlike the open ocean, where no single owner has that incentive. (f) Knowledge of the laws of thermodynamics is a pure public good. It is non-rivalrous: one person using the laws to design an engine does not prevent anyone else from using the same laws. It is non-excludable: once the knowledge is discovered and published, anyone can learn it and apply it. This is why basic scientific research is underprovided by the private market — firms cannot capture enough of the benefit to justify the investment — and why governments fund it.

**Answer 2.** (a) Total social benefit = 2,000 residents × £100 = £200,000. Net social benefit = £200,000 − £120,000 = £80,000. The benefit-cost ratio is 1.67, meaning the system generates £1.67 in benefit for every £1 of cost. The system ought to be installed on economic grounds. (b) A private firm cannot provide the flood warning system because it is non-excludable. Once the system is installed and operational, it will warn all 2,000 residents of an impending flood. There is no practical way to deliver the warning only to residents who have paid a subscription fee and withhold it from those who have not. Because the firm cannot exclude non-payers from the benefit, it cannot charge for the service. With no revenue, the firm cannot cover its £120,000 cost and will not enter the market. The free-rider problem is the mechanism: each resident hopes others will pay, and the collective result is that nobody pays and the system is not built. This is a complete market failure — zero provision of a good society values at £200,000. (c) Even with a compulsory levy of £60 per household (raising £120,000), the outcome is not guaranteed because residents vote on the levy itself. A resident who values the system at £100 would, in purely self-interested terms, vote for the levy because they gain £100 of benefit at a cost of £60. But a resident who values the system at only £40 — perhaps because their house is on higher ground — would vote against, because they would pay £60 for only £40 of benefit. If enough residents value the system below £60, the vote fails, even though the total benefit (£200,000) exceeds the total cost (£120,000). This illustrates a democratic variant of the public goods problem: majority voting does not guarantee that all projects with positive net social benefit will be approved, because the distribution of benefits and costs matters.

**Answer 3.** A pure public good is both non-rivalrous and non-excludable. A quasi-public good (also called a club good) is non-rivalrous but excludable. National defence is a pure public good. It is non-rivalrous: protecting one additional citizen within the country's borders costs nothing extra. It is non-excludable: there is no way to protect some citizens from an invading army while leaving others unprotected. Because it is non-excludable, a private firm cannot charge for defence — every household would free-ride — and the market provides none. A subscription streaming service is a quasi-public good. It is non-rivalrous: one more subscriber watching a film does not degrade the experience of other subscribers, up to the capacity of the provider's servers. It is excludable: the provider can require a username, a password, and a monthly payment. Because it is excludable, the market can provide the streaming service. The provider charges £10 per month, subscribers pay, and non-subscribers are locked out. The service is privately profitable. The difference between the two cases turns entirely on excludability. Both goods are non-rivalrous, but national defence cannot be fenced off and sold, while streaming content can be. That single difference explains why one is provided by the government and the other by the market.

**Answer 4.** The statement is an oversimplification. Whether a public beach is a public good depends on two factors: the number of visitors and whether access is restricted. At low usage — a cold Tuesday in November — the beach is non-rivalrous. One more person spreading a towel on the sand does not diminish anyone else's enjoyment. If access is also unrestricted — there is no fence, no ticket booth, no admission charge — then the beach is non-excludable as well. Under these conditions, the beach is a pure public good. But this classification is fragile. On a hot August bank holiday, with 5,000 people on the same stretch of sand, the beach becomes rivalrous. Each additional visitor reduces the space available for everyone else. The beach has shifted from a pure public good to a common pool resource — rivalrous but still non-excludable (if there is no entry restriction). If the local council erects a fence and charges a £2 admission fee, the beach becomes excludable. On a quiet day (non-rivalrous plus excludable), it is a club good. On a crowded day (rivalrous plus excludable), it is a private good. The same physical beach can occupy three different cells of the matrix depending on the season and the institutional arrangement. The IB rewards students who recognise that the classification of a good is not intrinsic to the good itself but depends on context, technology, and policy choices.

**Answer 5.** (a) A public good is a good that is both non-rivalrous and non-excludable. Non-rivalrous means that one person's consumption of the good does not reduce the amount available for others to consume. Non-excludable means that once the good is provided, it is impossible or prohibitively expensive to prevent anyone from benefiting from it, even if they have not paid. A pure public good satisfies both conditions simultaneously. (2 marks — 1 mark for defining both characteristics, 1 mark for clarity and precision.)

(b) The knowledge generated by the battery research is a public good. It is non-rivalrous: one company using the knowledge to build a better battery does not prevent another company from using the same knowledge. It is non-excludable: once the research findings are published, any firm can read them and apply them without paying the original researcher. Because the knowledge is non-excludable, a private firm that spends $500 million on the research cannot prevent its competitors from using the results. The competitors will adopt the new knowledge, improve their own batteries, and compete away the profits the original firm hoped to earn. The private firm therefore cannot capture enough of the total social benefit to justify the $500 million investment. The free-rider problem operates at the firm level: each firm prefers to let others do the research and then copy the results. Because all firms reason this way, nobody invests. The market produces zero basic battery research, even though the total benefit to society — cheaper batteries, faster adoption of electric vehicles, reduced carbon emissions — could be worth billions of dollars. (2 marks — 1 mark for explaining that non-excludability prevents the firm from capturing the returns, 1 mark for linking this to the free-rider problem and zero private provision.)

**Answer 6.** (a) Free nationwide broadband is non-rivalrous up to network capacity — one more household streaming video does not slow down other households' connections, provided the network has sufficient bandwidth — but it is excludable because the government could require a login and restrict access to non-payers. By the 2×2 matrix, it is a club good (non-rivalrous, excludable). It differs from national defence, which is non-excludable and therefore a pure public good. (b) One argument for government provision: broadband has positive externalities. Households with internet access can access online education, search for jobs, and participate in digital government services. Firms in rural areas that private providers would not serve (because the population density is too low to justify the infrastructure cost) would gain access under a government programme. The government can cross-subsidise — using revenue from urban areas to fund rural connections — in a way that a profit-maximising private firm would not. One argument against government provision: because broadband is excludable, the market can provide it. Private internet service providers already exist and compete on price and speed. Government provision would crowd out private investment, potentially reducing innovation. The government would also need to decide what speed of broadband to provide, how much to invest in upgrading the network, and how to price the service — decisions that markets make through competition and price signals. Government failure (inefficiency, political allocation of upgrades to swing constituencies rather than areas of greatest need) is a genuine risk. (c) The cases are similar in that both national defence and broadband generate benefits that extend beyond the individual consumer. A defended country benefits everyone; a connected country benefits everyone through higher productivity and social inclusion. The cases differ crucially in excludability. National defence cannot be fenced off and sold by private firms; broadband can. The case for government provision of national defence is absolute — the market cannot provide it at all. The case for government provision of broadband is contingent — the market can provide it, but it may not provide it everywhere, at a price everyone can afford, or at the speed that maximises social benefit. The optimal policy for national defence is full government provision. The optimal policy for broadband might be a public-private partnership, a subsidy for rural connections, or a regulated private market with universal service obligations — a more nuanced set of interventions than the all-or-nothing logic of pure public goods.

## Evaluate

**Question:** "Public goods must always be provided by the government." Evaluate this statement.

**Model Answer:**

The statement captures the standard economic logic: pure public goods are non-excludable, so private firms cannot charge for them, so the market provides none, so the government must step in. This logic is compelling, and for the core public goods that define the minimal state — national defence, law and order, the enforcement of contracts — it is essentially correct. But the word "always" invites challenge, and the challenges reveal important nuances about how public goods can be, and have been, provided through mechanisms other than direct government production.

The strongest case for the statement is national defence. No country in the world relies on a private market for its military defence. The free-rider problem is insurmountable: a private defence firm would need to charge households for protection, but any household that refused to pay would still be protected by virtue of living within the defended territory. The firm would go bankrupt. Defence is therefore a government function everywhere, funded by compulsory taxation, and there is no serious alternative.

But even for goods that appear to be pure public goods, history and institutional creativity have sometimes found alternatives. The most famous counterexample is the lighthouse. Economists from John Stuart Mill to Paul Samuelson used the lighthouse as the textbook example of a public good that only government could provide — non-rivalrous and non-excludable. Then Ronald Coase, in a 1974 paper, examined the historical record and found that many English lighthouses in the 18th and 19th centuries were built and operated by private entrepreneurs. How? The government granted the entrepreneur the right to collect a toll from ships docking at nearby ports. The lighthouse itself was non-excludable, but the complementary service — access to the port — was excludable. By tying the lighthouse payment to the port toll, the entrepreneur solved the free-rider problem. The lesson is not that government provision is unnecessary, but that the boundary between excludable and non-excludable can be shifted by institutional design.

A second challenge to "always" comes from technology. Non-excludability is not a law of nature; it is a function of the cost of exclusion. When exclusion becomes cheaper, a public good can become a club good. Roads were non-excludable for most of human history because building toll booths on every road was impractical. Electronic toll collection — number plate recognition cameras, RFID tags — has made roads excludable at much lower cost. The M6 Toll road in the West Midlands and the London Congestion Charge zone are roads where exclusion is routine. A good that was once a public good has become a club good, and the market (or a regulated private operator) can now provide it.

A third challenge comes from philanthropy and voluntary collective action. Open-source software — Linux, the Apache web server, the Python programming language — is a public good in the digital realm. It is non-rivalrous (one more download costs nothing) and non-excludable (anyone can download it for free). Yet it is provided not by government but by a global community of volunteer programmers, coordinated by norms and reputation rather than by prices or commands. Wikipedia is another example: a vast public good of knowledge, built and maintained by volunteers. The mechanism is not the profit motive but intrinsic motivation — the satisfaction of contributing to something useful and being recognised for it. This model does not scale to all public goods — no volunteer army of programmers will provide national defence — but it demonstrates that government is not the only institution capable of overcoming the free-rider problem.

The strongest argument against the statement, however, is not that alternatives exist but that government provision itself can fail. Governments may provide the wrong quantity of a public good — too much defence spending because of lobbying by military contractors, too little basic research because its benefits are diffuse and its beneficiaries are not organised. Governments may provide public goods inefficiently, with higher costs than necessary because of the absence of competitive pressure. And the taxation that funds public goods imposes its own costs — deadweight losses from distorted incentives, and the opportunity cost of public funds that could have been used for private consumption or investment.

The correct conclusion is nuanced. For pure public goods that are essential to the functioning of the state — defence, law and order, the legal framework of property and contract — government provision is the only viable option, and the statement is correct. For public goods where technological change or institutional design can create excludability (roads, broadcasting, some forms of research), a mixed approach — government funding combined with private provision, or regulated private provision — may outperform pure government provision. For public goods that can be provided through voluntary collective action (open-source software, community-maintained public spaces), government may not need to be involved at all.

The IB rewards students who can hold both ideas in their heads simultaneously: that the free-rider problem is a genuine and severe market failure, AND that government provision is an imperfect solution that must be evaluated against real alternatives rather than against an imaginary ideal. The statement "public goods must always be provided by the government" is too strong. A better formulation is: "For pure public goods where exclusion is genuinely impossible and the stakes are high, government provision is the least bad option — but institutional innovation, technological change, and voluntary action can sometimes expand the range of alternatives."
