Lesson 38: Fiscal Policy

What You'll Learn

  • The structure of the government budget: revenue and expenditure
  • The distinction between budget deficit and national debt
  • How expansionary and contractionary fiscal policy work
  • What automatic stabilisers are and how they function
  • How to diagram fiscal policy on AD/AS models
  • The Keynesian vs monetarist debate on fiscal policy
  • How to evaluate the effectiveness of fiscal policy

    • 1. The Government Budget

    The government budget is an annual statement of the government's planned revenue and expenditure. It is the primary instrument of fiscal policy.

    Revenue (mainly taxes)

    • Direct taxes: Levied on income and wealth — personal income tax, corporate profits tax, capital gains tax, inheritance tax.
    • Indirect taxes: Levied on spending — VAT (Value Added Tax), excise duties (on alcohol, tobacco, fuel), tariffs.
    • Other revenue: Profits from state-owned enterprises, fees for government services, sale of public assets (privatisation).

      • Expenditure (G)

      • Current expenditure: Ongoing spending on goods and services — public sector wages, healthcare, education, defence, interest payments on national debt.
      • Capital expenditure: Investment in infrastructure — roads, bridges, hospitals, schools, broadband networks.
      • Transfer payments: Payments to individuals without exchanging goods/services — unemployment benefits, state pensions, child benefits, housing support. Note: transfer payments are NOT part of G in GDP calculations, but they affect aggregate demand indirectly through disposable income.

        • 2. Budget Balances

        Budget Surplus: Tax revenue > Government expenditure (T > G)

        The government is taking in more than it is spending. This is contractionary — it withdraws net spending from the economy.

        Budget Deficit: Government expenditure > Tax revenue (G > T)

        The government is spending more than it takes in. This is expansionary — it injects net spending into the economy. The deficit must be financed by borrowing (issuing government bonds).

        Balanced Budget: Tax revenue = Government expenditure (T = G)

        The government's revenue exactly matches its spending. This is neutral — no net injection or withdrawal.

        Crucial Distinction: Deficit vs Debt

        • Budget deficit (or surplus): A flow concept — the difference between revenue and expenditure in a single year. E.g., "The UK budget deficit in 2023 was £120 billion."
        • National debt: A stock concept — the total accumulated borrowing over time. It is the sum of all past deficits minus past surpluses. E.g., "UK national debt is £2.5 trillion."
        • Analogy: The deficit is like water flowing into a bathtub each year; the national debt is the total water in the tub. If the deficit is zero, the debt stops growing (but doesn't shrink). To reduce the debt, you need a surplus.

          • 3. Expansionary Fiscal Policy

          Purpose: Close a recessionary (deflationary) gap — Y < Yf, unemployment is high.

          Tools:

          1. Increase government expenditure (G↑): Spend more on infrastructure, public services, transfer payments.
          2. Decrease taxes (T↓): Lower income taxes increase disposable income; lower corporate taxes increase after-tax profits and investment incentives.
          3. Combination: Both G↑ and T↓ simultaneously.

          Transmission:

          • G↑ → direct increase in AD (government spending is a component of AD: AD = C + I + G + (X−M))
          • T↓ → households have more disposable income → C↑ AND firms retain more profits → I↑
          • AD shifts right from AD₁ to AD₂
          • Real GDP rises from Y₁ toward Yf; unemployment falls
          • Price level rises modestly (if starting from a recessionary gap)

            The Multiplier Effect: An initial increase in G of £1 billion may increase GDP by MORE than £1 billion. Why? The initial spending becomes someone's income → they spend part of it → that becomes someone else's income → etc. This concept is covered more fully in HL but is relevant to understanding fiscal policy's power.

            4. Contractionary Fiscal Policy

            Purpose: Close an inflationary gap — Y > Yf, demand-pull inflation is a concern.

            Tools:

            1. Decrease government expenditure (G↓): Cut spending on public projects, reduce transfer payments.
            2. Increase taxes (T↑): Raise income taxes to reduce disposable income; raise corporate taxes.
            3. Combination: Both G↓ and T↑.

            Transmission:

            • G↓ → direct decrease in AD
            • T↑ → households have less disposable income → C↓ AND firms retain less profit → I↓
            • AD shifts left from AD₁ to AD₂
            • Price level falls from P₁ toward P₂; real GDP falls back toward Yf

              Political pain: Unlike monetary policy, contractionary fiscal policy is politically very difficult. Cutting spending or raising taxes directly affects voters and is often avoided until absolutely necessary. This "political asymmetry" — easy to expand, hard to contract — can lead to persistent deficits.

              5. Automatic Stabilisers

              Automatic stabilisers are features of the tax and transfer system that automatically cushion economic fluctuations — without any deliberate policy change by the government.

              How They Work

              Progressive income taxes:

              • In a boom: Incomes rise → people move into higher tax brackets → tax revenue automatically increases → disposable income grows less than GDP → dampens consumption growth → moderates the boom.
              • In a recession: Incomes fall → people move into lower tax brackets → tax revenue automatically falls → disposable income falls less than GDP → cushions consumption → moderates the recession.

              Unemployment benefits:

              • In a recession: Unemployment rises → more people claim benefits → government spending automatically increases → injects income into the economy → cushions the fall in AD.
              • In a boom: Unemployment falls → fewer people claim benefits → government spending automatically decreases → withdraws some spending → moderates the boom.

              Key point: No politician has to vote for these stabilisers to work — they are built into the system. This avoids the recognition and implementation lags that plague discretionary fiscal policy.

              Example: During the COVID-19 recession in 2020, automatic stabilisers kicked in globally — tax revenues fell sharply as incomes collapsed, while spending on unemployment benefits rose. In the US, automatic stabilisers were estimated to offset roughly 15–20% of the shock to GDP.

              6. Evaluation of Fiscal Policy

              Strengths

              • Direct impact: Government spending directly adds to AD — unlike monetary policy, which relies on the indirect channel of private sector borrowing.
              • Targeting: Fiscal policy can be targeted at specific sectors, regions, or income groups (e.g., infrastructure in a depressed region, tax credits for low-income families).
              • Works at the zero lower bound: When monetary policy is impotent (rates near zero), fiscal policy can still stimulate the economy. This is a key Keynesian argument.
              • Automatic stabilisers: Built-in mechanisms work quickly without political deliberation.

                • Weaknesses

                • Time lags — very significant:
                • Recognition lag: Time to identify that the economy needs intervention.
                • Administrative/implementation lag: Designing tax changes or spending programmes, passing legislation, and getting money into the economy can take months or even years.
                • Impact lag: Time for the spending or tax cut to affect the real economy.
                • Crowding out: Government borrowing to finance a deficit may push up interest rates, "crowding out" private investment. If the government issues bonds, it competes with firms for loanable funds → interest rates rise → private I falls → partially offsets the expansionary effect. The extent of crowding out is debated (minor in recession with slack; potentially significant near full employment).
                • Political constraints: Politicians face electoral incentives. Expansionary fiscal policy (tax cuts, spending) is popular; contractionary (tax hikes, spending cuts) is unpopular. This creates a bias toward persistent deficits — "deficit bias."
                • Debt sustainability: Persistent deficits increase the national debt. High debt levels may:
                • Raise borrowing costs (investors demand higher yields to compensate for risk).
                • Crowd out future government spending (more tax revenue goes to interest payments).
                • Risk a sovereign debt crisis (e.g., Greece 2010).
                • Tax disincentives: High taxes may reduce incentives to work, save, and invest — a supply-side concern.
                • Ricardian equivalence: Some economists argue that if the government cuts taxes and borrows, rational households will save the tax cut (not spend it) because they anticipate future tax increases to repay the debt. This would render fiscal policy ineffective.

                  • Keynesian vs Monetarist Views

                  • Keynesians: Fiscal policy is powerful, especially in deep recessions when private demand is weak and monetary policy is at the ZLB. Multiplier effects are significant.
                  • Monetarists: Fiscal policy is largely ineffective due to crowding out. Monetary policy is the preferred tool. They advocate for balanced budgets over the business cycle.

                  IB Exam Tip

                  When answering a fiscal policy question:

                  1. Be specific about the instrument — don't just say "fiscal policy." Say "the government increases spending on infrastructure" or "the government cuts personal income tax rates."
                  2. Show the AD/AS diagram clearly — label the gap, the shift, and new equilibrium.
                  3. Distinguish between discretionary fiscal policy and automatic stabilisers.
                  4. For evaluation, always mention crowding out and time lags as key limitations. Refer to the CLASPP framework.
                  5. Mention the difference between deficit and debt if budget balances are discussed.

(a) Calculate each country's budget balance. (b) Classify each as surplus, deficit, or balanced. (c) Explain how each balance contributes to the national debt.

                  Answers

                  Answer 1: Budget Balances

                  (a) Country A: Revenue ($450 bn) − Expenditure ($500 bn) = −$50 billion (deficit). Country B: Revenue ($320 bn) − Expenditure ($300 bn) = +$20 billion (surplus).

                  (b) Country A has a budget deficit; Country B has a budget surplus.

                  (c) Country A's deficit of $50 billion adds to its national debt — the government must borrow $50 billion to cover the shortfall, increasing the stock of accumulated debt by $50 billion. Country B's surplus of $20 billion reduces its national debt by $20 billion — the government can use the surplus to repay existing debt. However, even if the deficit narrowed (e.g., from $80 bn to $50 bn), the debt still grows — it just grows more slowly. Only a surplus reduces the stock of debt.

                  Answer 2: Expansionary Fiscal Policy

                  • Gap: The recessionary gap is $100 billion ($1,000 bn − $900 bn).
                  • Policy options: (1) Increase G — spend more on public infrastructure projects; (2) Cut T — reduce income tax rates to boost disposable income; (3) Both.
                  • Transmission: G↑ → direct addition to AD. T↓ → disposable income rises → C↑. Firms' after-tax profits rise → I↑. Multiplier effects amplify the initial injection.
                  • Diagram: LRAS at $1,000 bn. AD₁ intersects SRAS at $900 bn (recessionary gap). After fiscal expansion, AD₂ shifts right. New equilibrium closer to $1,000 bn. Price level rises modestly.
                  • Consideration: The exact size of the shift depends on the multiplier. If the multiplier is 1.5, a G increase of $67 bn would close the gap. Political will and time lags are constraints.

                    • Answer 3: Automatic Stabilisers

                    Progressive income taxes:

                    • Assume a progressive tax system: 0% on first $10,000, 20% on next $30,000, 40% above $40,000.
                    • In a boom: Worker's income rises from $40,000 to $50,000. Tax rises from $6,000 (15% average) to $10,000 (20% average). Disposable income rises by only $6,000 (not $10,000) → consumption growth is moderated.
                    • In a recession: Worker's income falls from $40,000 to $30,000. Tax falls from $6,000 to $4,000. Disposable income falls by only $8,000 (not $10,000) → consumption decline is cushioned.

                      • Unemployment benefits:

                      • In a recession: 500,000 additional workers lose jobs and claim benefits of $300/week each → government spending automatically rises by $300 × 500,000 × 52 weeks ≈ $7.8 billion. This injected income supports consumption and prevents AD from falling further.
                      • In a boom: As these workers regain employment, benefits automatically stop → government spending naturally falls.

                      Key: These happen without any legislative action — no time spent on recognition, decision, or implementation.

                      Answer 4: Crowding Out

                      Definition: Crowding out occurs when expansionary fiscal policy (financed by borrowing) leads to higher interest rates, which reduce private sector investment (I), offsetting some — or all — of the initial fiscal stimulus.

                      Mechanism:

                      1. Government runs a deficit → issues bonds to borrow.
                      2. Increased demand in the loanable funds market → interest rates rise.
                      3. At higher interest rates, firms postpone or cancel investment projects → I falls.
                      4. Net effect on AD: ΔG + ΔI (negative). If I falls by the same amount G rose, there is complete crowding out — AD doesn't shift.

                      When is crowding out most significant?

                      • Economy near full employment: Resources are scarce; government borrowing competes with private borrowing for limited loanable funds.
                      • High interest elasticity of investment: Small rate rises cause large reductions in I.

                      When is crowding out least significant?

                      • Deep recession with high unemployment: Resources are idle; government spending puts them to use without competing with private demand.
                      • Liquidity trap / ZLB: Interest rates are already near zero and cannot rise significantly.
                      • Central bank accommodates: If the central bank keeps rates low, crowding out is minimal.

                        • Answer 5: Deficit vs Debt

                        The statement is incorrect as written, unless the deficit has become a surplus.

                        • A budget deficit is a flow (annual). National debt is a stock (accumulated).
                        • If Country X reduced its deficit from, say, $100 billion to $50 billion, it is still running a deficit. The debt is still growing — just by $50 billion per year instead of $100 billion.
                        • Only if Country X achieves a budget surplus (T > G) does the debt actually fall, as the surplus can be used to repay existing debt.
                        • Example: Greece reduced its deficit from 15% of GDP in 2009 to near balance by 2017, but its national debt rose from 130% to 180% of GDP during that period — because deficits, though shrinking, still added to the debt, and GDP itself was shrinking.
                        • The correct statement would be: "Country X has reduced its budget deficit, so its national debt is growing more slowly." Or: "Country X has achieved a budget surplus, so its national debt is falling."

                          • Answer 6: IB-Style Data Response

                          (a) Macroeconomic situation:

                          • GDP growth is −2.1% (well below the 2.5% trend) → economy is in recession.
                          • Unemployment at 11.3% is more than double the NAIRU of 5.0% → significant cyclical unemployment.
                          • Inflation at 0.7% is below the 2% target → no inflationary pressure; risk of deflation.
                          • Budget deficit is very large (8.2% vs 3% limit) and national debt is very high (98% vs 60% limit).
                          • Policy rate is only 0.25% → monetary policy is near the zero lower bound.
                          • Overall: A deep recession with high unemployment, low inflation, and severely constrained fiscal space.

                            • (b) Recommended fiscal policy package:

                            • Recommend expansionary fiscal policy: Increase spending on infrastructure (targeted, high-multiplier projects) and/or cut taxes for low-income households (who have high MPC).
                            • AD/AS diagram: Economy is far inside the production possibility frontier. Fiscal expansion shifts AD right from AD₁ to AD₂. Real GDP rises, unemployment falls. Given high unemployment and low inflation, the risk of demand-pull inflation is minimal in the short run.
                            • Why fiscal over monetary: The policy rate is already 0.25%. Further cuts are nearly impossible (ZLB). Fiscal policy is the only viable demand-side option.

                              • (c) Two constraints:

                              1. Debt sustainability: The deficit (8.2% of GDP) and debt (98% of GDP) are already very high — far exceeding typical EU limits. Further borrowing risks: (i) higher bond yields if investors demand a risk premium; (ii) credit rating downgrade; (iii) potential sovereign debt crisis. Markets may punish aggressive fiscal expansion. This is a severe constraint — as seen in the Eurozone crisis.
                              2. Crowding out risk: While the economy has slack now, if the fiscal expansion is large and sustained, it could eventually push up interest rates (if the central bank does not accommodate) and crowd out private investment, reducing the net impact on growth. However, with the policy rate at 0.25%, the central bank is likely to accommodate, making crowding out less of a concern in the short term.

                              Evaluate: CLASPP Model Answer (150–200 words)

                              "Fiscal policy is too slow and politically constrained to be useful for macroeconomic management." Evaluate.

                              Conclusion: This statement captures genuine weaknesses of discretionary fiscal policy, but it overstates the case. While fiscal policy does suffer from significant time lags and political distortions, it remains an essential tool — particularly when monetary policy is exhausted.

                              Limitations: The time lags are real. Recognition lags, lengthy legislative processes, and implementation delays mean that fiscal stimulus often arrives after the recession has bottomed out — potentially becoming pro-cyclical (stimulating an already-recovering economy). The notorious "shovel-ready" infrastructure promise illustrates how spending is rarely as quick as hoped.

                              Assumptions: The statement assumes that all fiscal policy is discretionary. However, automatic stabilisers — progressive taxes and unemployment benefits — work instantly without political deliberation. These cushioned roughly 15–20% of GDP shocks in the COVID-19 crisis and are an integral part of fiscal policy.

                              Stakeholders: Political constraints are asymmetric: expansion is easy (tax cuts win votes), contraction is hard. This creates "deficit bias." Yet this does not make fiscal policy useless — it makes it imperfect and requiring institutional safeguards (e.g., independent fiscal councils).

                              Pros/Cons: Fiscal policy works when monetary policy cannot (zero lower bound). It can be precisely targeted. The 2008 and 2020 crises demonstrated that fiscal stimulus, while imperfectly timed, prevented far deeper recessions.

                              Priorities: The optimal framework combines automatic stabilisers (fast, no politics) with well-designed discretionary policy for deep crises, supported by independent fiscal oversight to mitigate political distortions.
