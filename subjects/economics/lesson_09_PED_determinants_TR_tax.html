1. Prior Knowledge Check

Q1: In Lesson 8, you calculated that when latte prices rose from £3 to £4, PED was 1.4 (elastic). What does an elastic PED tell you about how consumers respond to price changes?

Show

An elastic PED (>1) means consumers are highly responsive — the percentage change in quantity demanded is larger than the percentage change in price. When the price of lattes rose 28.6%, quantity demanded fell by 40%.

2. Determinants of PED — SPLAT

Five factors determine whether demand for a good is elastic or inelastic:

S — Substitutes (number and closeness). More close substitutes → more elastic demand. If the price of one brand of bottled water rises, consumers easily switch to another brand. If the price of insulin rises, diabetics cannot easily switch to a different treatment.

P — Proportion of income. Goods that take a large share of income → more elastic. A 10% rise in the price of a house (huge purchase) makes buyers much more sensitive than a 10% rise in the price of a box of matches (tiny purchase).

L — Luxury vs. necessity. Necessities (food, water, basic medicine) → inelastic. Luxuries (holidays, designer clothes) → elastic. You cut back on holidays before you cut back on food.

A — Addiction / habit. Addictive goods (cigarettes, alcohol, caffeine) → inelastic. The addiction means consumers will pay higher prices rather than reduce consumption.

T — Time period. Demand becomes more elastic as consumers have more time to adjust. In the short run after a petrol price rise, people still need to drive to work — demand is inelastic. Over several years, they can buy a more fuel-efficient car, move closer to work, or use public transport — demand becomes more elastic.

Check-in: Why is the demand for a specific farmer's wheat at a market almost perfectly elastic, while demand for wheat as a whole (all farmers) is inelastic?

Show

A specific farmer's wheat has perfect substitutes — every other farmer's wheat. If one farmer tries to charge more, buyers simply switch to the next stall. Demand for that farmer's wheat is perfectly elastic. But wheat as a whole (all wheat) has no close substitute — bread makers need wheat. Demand for wheat overall is inelastic.

3. PED and Tax Incidence

When a government imposes an indirect tax (e.g., £1 per unit), the tax burden is shared between consumers and producers. Who bears more of the tax depends on relative elasticities.

Tax incidence depends on PED. When demand is inelastic (left), consumers bear more. When demand is elastic (right), producers bear more.

When demand is inelastic (PED<1): Consumers cannot easily reduce consumption when the price rises. They bear most of the tax burden. Producers pass the tax on to consumers through higher prices. Example: cigarette taxes — smokers pay most of the tax.

When demand is elastic (PED>1): Consumers respond strongly to price increases. If producers try to pass the tax on as higher prices, consumers buy much less. Producers must absorb most of the tax themselves. Example: a tax on a specific brand of soft drink — consumers switch to other brands.

4. Worked Examples

Example 1: Government Tax Strategy

Problem: A government wants to (a) raise revenue and (b) reduce consumption of unhealthy products. Should it tax goods with elastic or inelastic demand? Explain the trade-off.

Step 1: For revenue: tax inelastic goods. Consumers keep buying despite higher prices → steady tax revenue. Cigarettes, alcohol, petrol — classic examples.

Step 2: For reducing consumption: tax elastic goods. The higher price causes a large fall in consumption. But revenue will fall as consumption drops.

Step 3: Trade-off: a single tax cannot easily achieve both goals. Taxing inelastic goods raises revenue but does little to change behaviour. Taxing elastic goods changes behaviour but generates less revenue. This is why governments often tax goods with moderately inelastic demand — some behaviour change, some revenue.

⚠️ Common Wrong: Assuming any tax achieves both goals equally well. The IB rewards recognising the trade-off.

Example 2: Tax Incidence Calculation

Problem: A tax of £2 per unit is imposed on a good. The price consumers pay rises from £10 to £11.50. The price producers receive (after tax) falls from £10 to £9.50. Calculate the consumer and producer burden.

Consumer burden = £11.50 − £10 = £1.50 per unit (75% of tax). Producer burden = £10 − £9.50 = £0.50 per unit (25%). Consumers bear more → demand is relatively inelastic compared to supply.

⚠️ Common Mistakes

  1. Mistake: Thinking "inelastic goods always have low PED regardless of time." Correct: Time is a determinant. Petrol has inelastic demand in the short run but more elastic in the long run.
  2. Mistake: Assuming the producer always bears the tax. Correct: Tax incidence depends on relative elasticities. The less elastic side of the market bears more of the tax.
  3. Mistake: Confusing who legally pays the tax with who bears the economic burden. Correct: Even if the tax is collected from producers, consumers bear part (or most) through higher prices if demand is inelastic.

5. Practice

Q1. [MC] Goods with many close substitutes tend to have:

A. Inelastic demand B. Elastic demand C. Unit elastic demand D. Perfectly inelastic demand

Q2. [MC] The government wants to raise maximum revenue from a tax. It should tax goods where demand is:

A. Elastic B. Inelastic C. Unit elastic D. Perfectly elastic

Q3. [Paper 2] Explain two determinants of PED and use a different real-world good to illustrate each. [4 marks]

Q4. [Paper 2, Exam Style] "Governments should tax goods with inelastic demand because they raise more revenue." Evaluate this statement, considering both the revenue objective and other possible government objectives. [6 marks]

A1: B. Many close substitutes → consumers easily switch → demand is elastic. A2: B. Inelastic demand means consumers keep buying as price rises → steady tax revenue. A3: SPLAT: (1) Substitutes — insulin has no close substitutes → inelastic. (2) Proportion of income — matches are a tiny share → inelastic. [4 marks for two explained determinants with examples.] A4: [6-mark CLASPP:] Taxing inelastic goods generates stable revenue (smokers keep buying cigarettes after tax rises) — the statement is correct on revenue. However, governments also tax to reduce consumption (e.g., sugar taxes), which requires elastic demand. Inelastic goods may be necessities (food, fuel), and taxing them is regressive — hurting the poor disproportionately. There is also a paternalistic argument: taxing addictive inelastic goods (cigarettes) is justified even if it's regressive because it reduces harm. The "best" tax policy depends on whether the government prioritises revenue, behaviour change, or equity.

6. Key Terms

TermDefinition
Tax incidenceThe division of a tax burden between consumers (higher price) and producers (lower revenue per unit)
Consumer burdenThe portion of a tax paid by consumers through higher prices
Producer burdenThe portion of a tax borne by producers through lower net revenue
