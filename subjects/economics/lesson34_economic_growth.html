Lesson 34: Economic Growth

What You'll Learn

  • The distinction between actual growth and potential growth
  • How to measure economic growth using real GDP
  • The causes of growth: factor quantity, factor quality, institutions
  • Positive and negative consequences of growth
  • The concept of sustainable growth
  • How to represent growth on PPC and AD/AS diagrams

1. Defining Economic Growth

Economic growth has TWO distinct meanings in IB Economics - and confusing them costs marks.

Actual Economic Growth

  • An increase in real GDP (actual output produced and sold)
  • Represents using spare capacity more fully
  • On PPC: movement from point inside the curve toward frontier
  • On AD/AS: AD shifts right, moving along fixed LRAS/SRAS - higher equilibrium Y

Potential Economic Growth

  • An increase in the economy's productive capacity - what it CAN produce
  • Represents expanding the frontier
  • On PPC: entire curve shifts RIGHT (outward)
  • On AD/AS: LRAS shifts RIGHT
Worked Example: Distinguishing the Two
ScenarioTypeWhy
GDP rises $500B to $530B after stimulusActualUsing previously idle resources
New port built, increasing cargo capacity 20%PotentialCapacity expanded
GDP rises 4%, output gap closedActualEliminating negative output gap
AI automation increases labor productivity 15%PotentialMore output possible per worker

2. Measuring Economic Growth

$$\text{Economic Growth Rate}_t = \frac{\text{Real GDP}_t - \text{Real GDP}_{t-1}}{\text{Real GDP}_{t-1}} \times 100\%$$

Crucial: Must use REAL GDP (adjusted for inflation), not nominal.

Worked Example: Country X: Nominal GDP 2022 = $1.2T, deflator = 110. Nominal GDP 2023 = $1.35T, deflator = 115.5.

Real GDP 2022 = ($1.2T/110) x 100 = $1.091T
Real GDP 2023 = ($1.35T/115.5) x 100 = $1.169T
Growth = ($1.169T - $1.091T)/$1.091T x 100% = 7.15%

Per Capita Growth

$$\text{Real GDP per capita growth} \approx \text{Real GDP growth} - \text{Population growth}$$

If GDP grows 3% but population grows 2%, per capita growth is only ~1%. Better measure of whether living standards are rising.

3. Causes of Economic Growth

A. Quantity of Factors of Production

  • Labor Quantity: Population growth, immigration, higher participation - more workers - LRAS/PPC shifts right
  • Capital Quantity: Investment in physical capital - factories, machines, infrastructure
  • Land/Natural Resources: Discovery of new resources, land reclamation

B. Quality of Factors - Productivity

  • Labor Productivity: Education and training (human capital), better health, technology
  • Capital Productivity: Technological progress in machines, better management, R&D
  • Total Factor Productivity (TFP): The "technology multiplier" - portion of output not explained by input quantities

Key insight: Institutions explain why countries with similar resources have vastly different outcomes (South Korea vs North Korea).

The Easterlin Paradox

Beyond ~$20,000 per capita, self-reported happiness does NOT increase significantly with further GDP growth. Challenges assumption that growth always improves well-being.

5. Sustainable Growth

Sustainable economic growth: meets present needs without compromising future generations.

Three Pillars

  1. Economic sustainability: No unsustainable debt or asset bubbles
  2. Environmental sustainability: Within planetary boundaries
  3. Social sustainability: Inclusive, reducing inequality

ALWAYS specify "actual" or "potential" growth. Never just say "economic growth" without clarifying. PPC and AD/AS diagrams both acceptable - use the one that fits context. For evaluation: "sustainable growth" is a powerful evaluation point. For data-response: if given nominal GDP and deflator, they want you to calculate real.

Practice Problems

Problem 1 (Easy)
Distinguish between actual economic growth and potential economic growth. Use a PPC diagram to illustrate.
Problem 2 (Easy)
Country A's nominal GDP in 2022 was $800B with deflator 125. In 2023, nominal GDP $880B with deflator 132. Calculate real GDP for both years and the real growth rate.
Problem 3 (Medium)
Identify and explain THREE causes of potential economic growth. For each, state whether it affects quantity or quality of factors.
Problem 4 (Medium)
Country B's real GDP grows 5% annually but population grows 3%. How long for real GDP per capita to double? (Use Rule of 70.)
Problem 5 (Challenging)
An economy experiences 6% real GDP growth for five consecutive years. However, Gini rises from 0.35 to 0.48, carbon emissions increase 40%, and life satisfaction unchanged. Evaluate whether this growth has been beneficial.
Problem 6 (IB-Style 15-mark)
"Economic growth always leads to improvements in living standards." Discuss this statement.

Answers

Answer 1: Actual growth: Increase in real GDP - using existing capacity. On PPC: movement from inside toward frontier. Potential growth: Increase in productive capacity. On PPC: entire curve shifts outward. Diagram: PPC0 and PPC1, point A inside PPC0 to point B on PPC0 (actual growth), PPC0 to PPC1 shift (potential growth).
Answer 2: Real GDP 2022 = (800/125)x100 = $640B. Real GDP 2023 = (880/132)x100 = $666.67B. Growth = (666.67-640)/640 x 100% = 4.17%. Check: nominal growth 10%, inflation ~5.6%, roughly 10%-5.6% = 4.4% (close).
Answer 3: 1. Investment in physical capital (quantity): More factories, machines, infrastructure - LRAS shifts right. Example: China's infrastructure since 1990s. 2. Education and training (quality - human capital): Better-educated workers more productive. Example: South Korea's universal education driving tech transformation. 3. Technological progress (quality - TFP): R&D creates new products and efficient processes. Example: IT revolution increased productivity across all sectors.
Answer 4: Per capita growth = 5% - 3% = 2% (approximate). Precisely: (1.05/1.03)-1 = 1.94%. Doubling time = 70/1.94 = ~36 years. At 1.94% per capita, reaching $4,000 from $2,000 takes 36 years - very slow catch-up. Compare: at 5% per capita (China peak), doubling in 14 years. Country is improving but may never converge to developed nations.
Answer 5: Cumulative growth = (1.06)^5-1 = 33.8% more output. Positives: more goods, higher tax revenue. Negatives: (1) Gini 0.35 to 0.48 = large inequality increase - benefits concentrated among wealthy, poor may be absolutely worse off. (2) Carbon +40% = environmentally unsustainable, costs falling on future generations. (3) Unchanged life satisfaction = Easterlin Paradox - growth without welfare improvement. Overall: narrowly beneficial (more output) but broadly questionable - "growth without development."
Answer 6 (Full IB-Style):
Introduction: The absolutist "always" claim requires scrutiny of what "living standards" mean and under what conditions growth translates to welfare.
Arguments For: (1) Higher material consumption - more goods/services. (2) Employment and income - jobs created, poverty reduced. (3) Public services - larger tax base enables better healthcare, education. (4) Longevity - richer countries have longer life expectancy, lower infant mortality.
Arguments Against: (1) Distribution matters - if growth benefits only top 10%, median person stagnates. (2) Environmental costs - pollution, climate change reduce living standards. (3) Non-material standards - leisure, community, mental health can erode. (4) Easterlin Paradox - beyond ~$20K/capita, happiness weakly correlates with income. (5) Composition of growth - arms vs education has different welfare implications.
What Makes Growth Improve Living Standards? Inclusive (broadly shared), sustainable (environmentally sound), directed toward genuine welfare-enhancing outputs, preserving leisure and community.
Conclusion: Economic growth does NOT "always" improve living standards. It does so only when inclusive, sustainable, and welfare-directed. Unqualified GDP growth can coexist with rising inequality, environmental destruction, and stagnant well-being. The statement is too absolute and must be rejected.

Evaluate

"Economic growth always leads to improvements in living standards."

CLASPP Analysis (184 words)

Conclusion: The statement is false in its absolute form. Economic growth improves living standards only under specific conditions - when it is inclusive, sustainable, and directed toward welfare-enhancing outputs. Growth can and does occur alongside stagnant or declining living standards for significant portions of the population.

Long-term vs Short-term: In the short run, growth visibly raises employment and incomes. In the long run, however, unsustainable resource depletion and environmental degradation may reduce living standards for future generations, while inequality may compound.

Assumptions: Assumes direct, frictionless translation of GDP growth to welfare. Ignores distribution (who gets the growth?), composition (growth of what?), and externalities (what costs accompany growth?). Implicitly treats GDP as a welfare metric, which it is not.

Stakeholders: Capital owners and high-skilled workers benefit most. Low-skilled workers may be displaced. Future generations bear environmental costs. Rural communities may lose traditional livelihoods.

Priorities: If priority is aggregate output, growth succeeds. If priority is broad-based welfare, growth is a means not an end - must be evaluated against distribution, sustainability, and well-being metrics.

Pros/Cons: Pro - growth creates resources to solve problems. Con - growth can create or exacerbate the very problems it's supposed to solve.

Overall: Growth is a powerful but conditional tool for improving living standards.
