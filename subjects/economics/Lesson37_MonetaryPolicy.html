Lesson 37: Monetary Policy

What You'll Learn

  • The role of central banks in managing the macroeconomy
  • The main tools of monetary policy
  • The transmission mechanism of interest rate changes
  • How expansionary and contractionary monetary policy work
  • How to diagram monetary policy on AD/AS models
  • How to evaluate the effectiveness of monetary policy

    • 1. The Role of the Central Bank

    The central bank is the government's bank and the banker's bank. It is responsible for maintaining stability in the financial system and managing the country's monetary policy. Key functions include:

    • Controlling interest rates: The central bank sets a policy interest rate (called the base rate, bank rate, or federal funds rate depending on the country). This rate influences all other interest rates in the economy.
    • Controlling the money supply: Through various tools, the central bank influences how much money circulates in the economy.
    • Lender of last resort: If commercial banks face liquidity crises, the central bank can lend to them to prevent bank runs and maintain confidence in the banking system.
    • Managing the exchange rate: Some central banks intervene in foreign exchange markets to influence the value of the domestic currency.
    • Regulating and supervising commercial banks: Ensuring the stability and soundness of the banking system.

      • 2. Tools of Monetary Policy

      a) The Policy Interest Rate (Base Rate)

      This is the single most important tool in modern monetary policy. The central bank announces a target for a short-term interest rate. For example:

      • Bank of England: Bank Rate (currently set by the Monetary Policy Committee)
      • US Federal Reserve: Federal Funds Rate
      • European Central Bank (ECB): Main refinancing operations rate

        • Commercial banks use this rate as a reference. When the central bank changes the policy rate, commercial banks adjust their own lending and deposit rates accordingly.

        Worked Example: Suppose the Bank of England lowers the Bank Rate from 5% to 4.5%. Commercial banks can now borrow more cheaply from the central bank. They pass this on: mortgage rates fall from 6% to 5.5%, business loan rates fall from 7% to 6.5%. Borrowers respond by taking out more loans.

        b) Open Market Operations (OMO)

        The central bank buys or sells government bonds in the open market to influence the money supply and interest rates.

        • Expansionary OMO: The central bank buys government bonds from commercial banks. This injects money into the banking system, increases bank reserves, and puts downward pressure on interest rates. This is also called quantitative easing (QE) when done on a very large scale.
        • Contractionary OMO: The central bank sells government bonds to commercial banks. This withdraws money from the banking system, reduces bank reserves, and puts upward pressure on interest rates.

        c) Reserve Requirements

        The central bank may require commercial banks to hold a certain percentage of their deposits as reserves. While less commonly used actively in developed economies today:

        • Lowering reserve requirements: Banks can lend more → money supply expands → interest rates tend to fall.
        • Raising reserve requirements: Banks must hold more reserves → lending capacity shrinks → interest rates tend to rise.

        In practice, the policy interest rate is the primary tool. The People's Bank of China, however, actively uses reserve requirement ratios.

        3. The Transmission Mechanism

        The transmission mechanism describes how a change in the policy interest rate works through the economy to affect aggregate demand (AD).

        
Change in policy rate
    ↓
Commercial banks adjust lending/deposit rates
    ↓
Cost of borrowing changes (mortgages, business loans, credit cards)
    ↓
Consumption (C) and Investment (I) change
    ↓
Aggregate Demand shifts
    ↓
Real GDP (Y) and price level (P) change

        Key channels:

        1. Consumption channel: Lower rates → cheaper mortgages and consumer credit → households borrow and spend more → C rises.
        2. Investment channel: Lower rates → firms' cost of financing capital goods falls → more projects become profitable → I rises.
        3. Exchange rate channel: Lower domestic rates → financial capital flows out seeking higher returns elsewhere → domestic currency depreciates → exports (X) rise, imports (M) fall → net exports (X−M) rises.
        4. Wealth/asset price channel: Lower rates → bond and equity prices tend to rise → households feel wealthier → C rises.

        4. Expansionary Monetary Policy (Loose/Easy Money)

        Purpose: Close a recessionary (deflationary) gap — when actual GDP is below potential GDP (Y < Yf).

        Action: The central bank lowers the policy interest rate.

        Transmission:

        • Lower interest rates → cheaper credit → consumption rises (C↑) and investment rises (I↑)
        • AD shifts right from AD₁ to AD₂
        • Real GDP rises from Y₁ toward Yf
        • Unemployment falls
        • Price level may rise slightly (or stop falling)

          • Diagram — Expansionary Monetary Policy on AD/AS:

          
Price Level
    ↑
    |     LRAS
    |     /   SRAS
    |    /   /
P2  |   /   /   AD₂ (after rate cut)
    |  /   /
P1  | /   /   AD₁ (before rate cut)
    |/   /
    +---+-----------→ Real GDP
       Y₁   Yf

          The economy moves from Y₁ (recessionary gap) toward Yf.

          Risks: If the economy is already at or near Yf, expansionary policy may cause demand-pull inflation — AD shifts right beyond Yf, pulling up the price level without a lasting increase in output.

          5. Contractionary Monetary Policy (Tight Money)

          Purpose: Close an inflationary gap — when actual GDP is above potential GDP (Y > Yf), creating demand-pull inflationary pressure.

          Action: The central bank raises the policy interest rate.

          Transmission:

          • Higher interest rates → more expensive credit → consumption falls (C↓) and investment falls (I↓)
          • AD shifts left from AD₁ to AD₂
          • Price level falls from P₁ toward P₂
          • Real GDP falls back toward Yf

            • Diagram — Contractionary Monetary Policy on AD/AS:

            
Price Level
    ↑
    |     LRAS
    |     /   SRAS
P1  |   /   /
    |  /   /   AD₁ (before rate hike)
P2  | /   /   AD₂ (after rate hike)
    |/   /
    +---+-----------→ Real GDP
       Yf   Y₁

            The economy moves from Y₁ (inflationary gap) toward Yf.

            Risks: If the economy is already at or below Yf, contractionary policy may cause rising unemployment and a recession.

            6. Evaluation of Monetary Policy (CLASPP Elements)

            Strengths

            • Speed: Central banks (e.g., MPC at Bank of England) meet regularly (8 times/year) and can adjust rates quickly without lengthy legislative processes.
            • Independence: Most central banks are politically independent, insulating decisions from electoral cycles.
            • Flexibility: Rates can be adjusted incrementally (e.g., 25 basis points at a time) and reversed if conditions change.
            • No direct fiscal cost: Unlike government spending, rate changes don't directly add to the deficit.

              • Weaknesses

              • Time lags: Recognition lag (time to identify the problem), implementation lag (though short), and impact lag (estimated at 12–24 months for full effect). The long and variable lags make fine-tuning difficult.
              • Liquidity trap / zero lower bound: When interest rates are already near zero, conventional monetary policy becomes ineffective. Japan in the 1990s–2000s is a classic example. Central banks may resort to unconventional policy (QE), but its effectiveness is debated.
              • Confidence: If consumer and business confidence is very low, even very low interest rates may not stimulate borrowing (Keynes's "pushing on a string").
              • Differential effects: Rate changes affect sectors unevenly — housing and construction are highly sensitive; export sectors are also affected via the exchange rate channel.
              • Asset bubbles: Prolonged low rates may inflate asset prices (housing, equities), creating financial stability risks.
              • Effectiveness depends on sensitivity: If C and I are interest-inelastic (insensitive to rate changes), monetary policy is less effective.

                • Real-World Examples

                • Bank of England (2008–2009, 2020): Cut Bank Rate to near zero and implemented QE during the Global Financial Crisis and COVID-19.
                • Federal Reserve (2022–2023): Raised rates aggressively from near zero to over 5% to fight inflation.
                • ECB (2014–2019): Used negative interest rates to combat deflationary pressure in the Eurozone.

                  • IB Exam Tip

                  When answering a monetary policy question, always:

                  1. State the tool (policy interest rate) and the direction of change.
                  2. Trace the transmission mechanism step by step — don't just say "rates fall, AD shifts right."
                  3. Draw and explain the AD/AS diagram — label Yf, the initial and final positions, and the gap type.
                  4. For evaluation, use CLASPP: Conclusions, Limitations, Assumptions, Stakeholders, Pros/Cons, Priorities. Mention at least two limitations (e.g., time lags, liquidity trap).

                  Practice Problems

                  Problem 1: Transmission Mechanism

                  The central bank lowers the policy interest rate from 3% to 2%. Explain, step by step, how this affects the level of consumption and investment in the economy.

                  Problem 2: Diagram Analysis

                  Problem 3: Contractionary Policy

                  Problem 4: The Zero Lower Bound

                  Explain why monetary policy may become ineffective when interest rates are already very close to zero. Provide a real-world example.

                  Problem 5: Transmission Channels

                  List and explain three different channels through which a change in the policy rate affects aggregate demand. For each channel, provide one specific example.

                  Answers

                  Answer 1: Transmission Mechanism

                  When the central bank lowers the policy rate from 3% to 2%:

                  1. Commercial banks adjust their rates: Commercial banks can now borrow from the central bank at 2% instead of 3%. They reduce their own lending rates. Mortgage rates may fall from, say, 5% to 4%; business loan rates from 6% to 5%.
                  2. Cost of borrowing falls: For households, the cost of servicing a mortgage or car loan decreases. Monthly repayments fall, increasing disposable income, and new borrowers find loans more affordable.
                  3. Consumption (C) rises: Lower mortgage costs free up income for spending. Cheaper consumer credit encourages purchases of durable goods (cars, furniture, appliances). C↑.
                  4. Investment (I) rises: For firms, lower interest rates reduce the cost of financing capital projects. The net present value (NPV) of investment projects increases. Projects that were unprofitable at 6% may become profitable at 5%. Firms invest in machinery, technology, and expansion. I↑.
                  5. AD shifts right: C↑ and I↑ together shift aggregate demand to the right.

                  Answer 2: Diagram Analysis

                  The AD/AS diagram for expansionary monetary policy:

                  • The LRAS curve is vertical at Yf (potential GDP).
                  • Initial AD₁ intersects SRAS at Y₁ < Yf — this is the recessionary gap (shaded).
                  • After the central bank lowers the policy rate, C and I increase, shifting AD right from AD₁ to AD₂.
                  • New equilibrium: AD₂ intersects SRAS at a point closer to Yf.
                  • Real GDP rises from Y₁ toward Yf. The price level rises modestly from P₁ to P₂.
                  • The recessionary gap shrinks.

                    • The diagram should show: Price Level on the vertical axis, Real GDP on the horizontal axis; LRAS vertical at Yf; SRAS upward-sloping; AD₁ initially left of Yf; AD₂ shifted right; labels showing the gap shrinking.

                    Answer 3: Contractionary Policy

                    • Initial condition: actual GDP ($1,100 bn) > potential GDP ($1,000 bn) → inflationary gap of $100 bn.
                    • Central bank raises the policy rate.
                    • Transmission: Higher rates → more expensive borrowing → C↓ and I↓ → AD shifts LEFT from AD₁ to AD₂.
                    • Intended outcome: GDP falls toward $1,000 bn (Yf). The price level falls (or inflation slows).
                    • Diagram: LRAS vertical at $1,000 bn. AD₁ intersects SRAS at $1,100 bn (inflationary gap). AD₂ shifts left. New equilibrium closer to Yf, lower price level.
                    • Risk: If the policy is too aggressive, it could overshoot and create a recessionary gap with rising unemployment.

                      • Answer 4: The Zero Lower Bound

                      When the policy interest rate is already near zero (e.g., 0.1–0.5%), central banks cannot lower it much further. This is the zero lower bound (ZLB) problem — also called a liquidity trap when it coincides with very low confidence.

                      • Why ineffective: With rates near zero, further cuts have negligible effect. Borrowers already face very low rates; if they are not borrowing, it's not because rates are too high but because of low confidence, high debt, or weak income expectations.
                      • Pushing on a string: Keynes's metaphor — the central bank can make credit cheap (push), but it cannot force people to borrow if they don't want to.
                      • Example — Japan (1990s–2000s): After its asset bubble burst, the Bank of Japan cut rates to near zero. Despite this, the economy experienced "the lost decade(s)" of stagnation and deflation because consumers and firms were paying down debt rather than borrowing and spending. The BOJ pioneered quantitative easing but with limited success.
                      • Another example: After 2008, the Fed, ECB, and Bank of England all hit near-zero rates and turned to QE.

                        • Answer 5: Three Transmission Channels

                        1. Consumption channel: Lower rates → cheaper mortgages and car loans → households increase spending on durable goods. Example: A family refinances their mortgage at 3% instead of 5%, saving £200 per month, which they spend on home improvements and dining out.
                        2. Investment channel: Lower rates → firms' cost of capital falls → more investment projects become profitable. Example: A manufacturing firm calculates that buying a new £500,000 machine is only worthwhile if the interest rate is below 4%. When the central bank cuts the policy rate, the firm's bank offers a loan at 3.5%, so the firm invests.
                        3. Exchange rate channel: Lower domestic rates → investors seek higher returns abroad → capital outflow → domestic currency depreciates. Example: When the Bank of England cuts rates relative to the Fed, the British pound depreciates against the US dollar. UK exports become cheaper for US buyers, and US imports become more expensive for UK consumers. Net exports (X−M) rise.

                        Answer 6: IB-Style Data Response

                        (a) The central bank used expansionary monetary policy from 2020–2024. The policy rate was cut from 4.5% to 3.0% — a cumulative reduction of 1.5 percentage points. This is consistent with an attempt to stimulate aggregate demand during a period of low/negative growth and high unemployment. [2 marks]

                        (b) Diagram and explanation:

                        • Initial situation (2020-2021): GDP growth was negative (−3.2%, −1.5%), unemployment high (8.7–9.2%), and inflation very low (0.5–0.8%). This indicates a recessionary gap — AD was far left of Yf.
                        • The rate cuts from 4.5% to 3.5% by 2023 lowered borrowing costs, stimulating C and I.
                        • AD shifted right. By 2023, GDP growth turned positive (1.8%) and unemployment fell to 8.1%.
                        • The diagram should show LRAS at Yf, initial AD₁ far left, AD₂ shifted right by 2023.
                        • [4 marks for correct diagram with labels PLUS step-by-step explanation of transmission]

                          • (c) Evaluation:

                          • Effective aspects: The policy helped turn GDP growth from negative to positive (from −3.2% in 2020 to +1.8% in 2023). Unemployment fell from 9.2% to 8.1%. This suggests the transmission mechanism worked — lower rates stimulated demand.
                          • Concern — inflation: In 2024, as growth accelerated to 3.5%, inflation jumped to 3.8%. This may indicate the economy is approaching or exceeding Yf — the policy may now be too loose. The central bank may have kept rates too low for too long.
                          • Time lags: The improvement was gradual over 3–4 years, consistent with the long impact lags of monetary policy.
                          • Other factors: Some recovery may be due to automatic stabilisers or global economic recovery (external factors), not solely monetary policy. Attribution is difficult.
                          • Conclusion: The policy was partially effective — it supported recovery but may have set the stage for inflationary pressure in 2024. The central bank should now consider tightening. [4 marks]

                            • Evaluate: CLASPP Model Answer (150–200 words)

                            "Monetary policy is more effective than fiscal policy at managing the macroeconomy." Evaluate.

                            Conclusion: While monetary policy has important advantages over fiscal policy — particularly its speed, flexibility, and political independence — the claim that it is categorically "more effective" is too sweeping. The relative effectiveness of each depends critically on the economic context.

                            Limitations: Monetary policy faces the zero lower bound — when rates are near zero, conventional policy becomes impotent, as Japan's experience demonstrates. Fiscal policy, by contrast, can always inject spending directly. Monetary policy also has long and variable impact lags (12–24 months), and its effectiveness depends on the interest-elasticity of consumption and investment — if confidence is low, even zero rates may not stimulate borrowing ("pushing on a string").

                            Assumptions: The claim assumes that central bank independence always produces better outcomes. Yet unelected central bankers making politically consequential decisions (e.g., raising unemployment to fight inflation) raises democratic accountability concerns.

                            Stakeholders: Monetary policy disproportionately affects interest-sensitive sectors (housing, construction) and debtors. Fiscal policy can be targeted at specific groups (e.g., the unemployed) through transfers, making it potentially fairer.

                            Pros/Cons: Monetary policy is faster to implement and politically insulated; fiscal policy can be more precisely targeted and works when rates are at the ZLB.

                            Priorities: The best approach is policy coordination — using both tools together. During the 2008 and 2020 crises, governments worldwide deployed both monetary easing and fiscal stimulus, recognising that neither alone was sufficient.
