Lesson 19: Positive Externalities — Production and Consumption

What You Will Learn

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to: - Explain what a positive externality is and distinguish between positive externalities in production and in consumption - Draw and interpret diagrams showing positive production and positive consumption externalities - Explain why positive externalities cause markets to produce or consume less than the socially optimal quantity - Define merit goods and explain why free markets underprovide them - Evaluate four government policy responses: subsidies, direct provision, regulation, and information campaigns

1. What a Positive Externality Is and Why It Matters

Every time you buy a sandwich, you pay the price and you eat the sandwich. The transaction is between you and the shop. Nobody else gains or loses — or so it seems. But many transactions spill beyond the buyer and seller. A positive externality occurs when the production or consumption of a good creates benefits for people who are not involved in the transaction, and those people receive those benefits without paying for them. Consider a family that installs solar panels on their roof. The family pays the installer and receives cheaper electricity. That is the private transaction. But the solar panels also reduce carbon emissions, which slows climate change — and that benefit is shared by everyone on the planet, whether they paid for the panels or not. The family did not install the panels in order to help strangers in distant countries. The benefit to those strangers is an externality — a spillover effect that the market price does not capture. Why does this matter? Because when private decision-makers ignore the positive effects of their choices on others, they produce or consume too little from society's point of view. The market price is too high to generate the quantity that would be best for everyone. Understanding positive externalities is the first step toward understanding why governments subsidise education, fund medical research, and run public health campaigns.

2. Positive Production Externalities

What They Are

A positive production externality arises when the act of producing a good generates benefits for third parties — people or firms not involved in making or buying the good — and those third parties do not pay for the benefits they receive. Because production creates spillover benefits, the true cost to society of producing each additional unit is lower than the private cost faced by the producer. In other words, the marginal social cost (MSC) is less than the marginal private cost (MPC). The producer looks at MPC and decides how much to make. Society would be better off if the producer looked at MSC and made more.

A Concrete Example: Beekeeping

A beekeeper in Kent keeps 200 hives to produce honey. The beekeeper's private costs include the wooden hives, the protective clothing, the sugar syrup to feed the bees in winter, and the beekeeper's own labour. The beekeeper sells honey at £8 per jar and decides how many hives to maintain based on the price of honey and these private costs. But the bees do something the beekeeper does not charge for. They fly up to 5 kilometres in every direction, pollinating apple orchards, oilseed rape fields, and garden flowers. A single hive can pollinate crops worth £400 per year to nearby farmers. With 200 hives, the pollination benefit to surrounding farms is roughly £80,000 per year. The beekeeper receives none of that £80,000. It is a spillover benefit — a positive production externality. The beekeeper's MPC (the cost the beekeeper actually pays) includes the hives, the sugar syrup, and the labour. The MSC is lower because each hive also delivers free pollination. If the beekeeper had to be paid for pollination, the beekeeper would keep more hives. As things stand, the beekeeper keeps fewer hives than would be best for the surrounding farmers — and for society as a whole.

The Diagram: Positive Production Externality

To model this, we draw a diagram with four curves — even though two of them overlap. The MPC curve slopes upward. Each additional unit of honey costs more to produce, because the beekeeper eventually runs out of convenient places to put hives and must pay more for equipment maintenance. The MSC curve lies below the MPC curve. The vertical distance between MPC and MSC at any quantity is the value of the external benefit — the pollination — per unit of honey produced. Since this is a production externality and not a consumption externality, there is no divergence on the benefit side. The MPB curve and the MSB curve are the same line — they both represent the demand for honey by consumers. The free market reaches equilibrium where MPC equals MPB. Call this quantity Q_fm. At Q_fm, the beekeeper produces the amount of honey that maximises private profit. But the socially optimal quantity is where MSC equals MSB. Call this Q_opt. Because MSC is below MPC, Q_opt lies to the right of Q_fm. The market produces too little. The triangle between Q_fm and Q_opt, above the MSC curve and below the MSB curve, represents welfare loss. For every unit between Q_fm and Q_opt, the benefit to society (the MSB, which is the price consumers would pay for honey plus the value of pollination) exceeds the true cost to society (the MSC). Those units should be produced — but they are not, because the beekeeper does not capture the pollination benefit. A common mistake students make is thinking that the MSC curve lies above the MPC curve for all externalities. That is true for negative externalities, where production imposes costs on others. For positive production externalities, production creates benefits for others, so the social cost is lower. MSC is below MPC. Keep them straight by asking: does the production help others or harm others?

Another Example: Worker Training

A manufacturing firm in Manchester spends £2,000 per worker on a welding certification programme. The workers become more productive, which benefits the firm through higher output. That is the private benefit. But after two years, some of those workers leave and take jobs at other manufacturers. Those other firms now employ certified welders without having paid for the training. The original firm's training expenditure created a positive production externality — it raised the skill level of the labour force, benefiting firms that did not invest a single pound in training. Because firms know that trained workers may leave, they train fewer workers than would be optimal for the whole economy. The MSC of training (the cost of the programme minus the value of skills that spill over to other firms) is lower than the MPC (the full £2,000 per worker). The result is underinvestment in skills.

3. Positive Consumption Externalities

What They Are

A positive consumption externality arises when a person's consumption of a good creates benefits for other people, and the consumer does not receive any payment for providing those benefits. Because consumption creates spillover benefits, the true benefit to society of each unit consumed is higher than the private benefit the consumer enjoys. The marginal social benefit (MSB) is greater than the marginal private benefit (MPB).

A Concrete Example: Vaccination

When a parent in Birmingham takes their child to a GP surgery to receive the measles, mumps, and rubella (MMR) vaccine, the parent's private benefit is straightforward: the child is protected from three serious diseases. The NHS pays the vaccine's cost, and the parent does not pay at the point of use — but even if the parent had to pay, say, £30, they might well decide that protecting their child is worth £30. But the vaccinated child also cannot transmit measles, mumps, or rubella to other children. Every vaccinated child reduces the number of carriers in the population, making it harder for the diseases to spread. When enough children are vaccinated — roughly 95% for measles — the disease cannot circulate at all. This is called herd immunity. Even children who cannot be vaccinated for medical reasons (such as those undergoing chemotherapy) are protected because the disease has nowhere to spread. The parent who decides to vaccinate their child probably does not think about protecting their neighbour's immunocompromised child. The parent thinks about their own child's health. That is the private benefit. The external benefit — the reduced risk of transmission to others — is a positive consumption externality. The MSB of the vaccination is higher than the MPB. Because parents consider only their own child's benefit, some parents decide not to vaccinate. The free market, left to itself, would produce a vaccination rate below 95%. The result is outbreaks of preventable diseases — and a welfare loss, because the MSB of additional vaccinations exceeds the MSC up to the herd immunity threshold.

The Diagram: Positive Consumption Externality

The MPB curve slopes downward. Consumers get less additional benefit from each successive vaccination as the most vulnerable children are vaccinated first. The MSB curve lies above the MPB curve. The vertical distance between MPB and MSB is the external benefit per unit consumed — the value of reduced disease transmission to others. Since this is a consumption externality, there is no divergence on the cost side. The MPC curve and the MSC curve are the same line. The free market reaches equilibrium where MPB equals MPC. Call this quantity Q_fm. The socially optimal quantity is where MSB equals MSC. Because MSB is above MPB, Q_opt lies to the right of Q_fm. The market consumes too little. The welfare loss triangle sits between Q_fm and Q_opt, above the MSC curve and below the MSB curve. Each unit in that range would deliver more benefit to society than it costs to provide — but the market does not deliver those units because individual consumers do not capture the external benefit. A common mistake students make is thinking that a positive consumption externality diagram looks the same as a subsidy diagram. They are different. The externality diagram shows MSB above MPB — that is a fact about the world, not a policy. A subsidy shifts MPC downward — that is a policy response. In an exam, draw the externality first, then show the subsidy as a separate shift if the question asks for it.

Another Example: Education

When an 18-year-old in Leeds decides whether to go to university, she weighs the costs (three years of tuition fees at £9,250 per year plus living costs, and three years of forgone earnings) against the private benefits (higher lifetime earnings — roughly £250,000 more over a career for a UK graduate compared to a non-graduate, on average). What she probably does not consider is that her degree will make her a more productive worker, which increases the output of whichever firm hires her. She is unlikely to think about the fact that graduates are statistically less likely to commit crimes, more likely to vote, more likely to volunteer in their communities, and more likely to raise children who themselves value education. These are all external benefits — positive consumption externalities — of her decision to attend university. If every 18-year-old weighed only their private costs and private benefits, fewer would choose university than the number that would be best for society. The MSB of a university education exceeds the MPB. The free market underproduces graduates.

4. Merit Goods

What Merit Goods Are

A merit good is a good that society believes everyone should have access to, regardless of their ability to pay, because consuming it generates large positive externalities or because individuals tend to undervalue its private benefits. The term was introduced by the economist Richard Musgrave in 1957. Merit goods have two defining features. First, they generate positive consumption externalities — when one person consumes education or healthcare, others benefit. Second, there is often an information failure: consumers do not fully appreciate how much they themselves would benefit. A 14-year-old does not have the perspective to weigh the lifetime returns to staying in school against the immediate gratification of leaving. A healthy 30-year-old does not lie awake thinking about the preventive healthcare that might save their life at 60. Because of these two features — external benefits plus information failure — merit goods are underconsumed in a free market. The quantity consumed is below Q_opt. Governments therefore intervene to increase consumption.

Examples of Merit Goods

Education is the classic merit good. A government that leaves schooling to parental choice alone will find that some parents keep their children at home to work — not because those parents are bad, but because they cannot afford the short-term loss of the child's income, even though staying in school would make the child far better off in the long run. Healthcare is a second major merit good. Preventive care — vaccinations, cancer screenings, prenatal check-ups — delivers enormous long-term benefits, but people procrastinate. They skip the screening. They put off the check-up. The information failure is not that people do not know that screenings are good; it is that they discount the future too heavily relative to the present. Museums, public libraries, and public broadcasting are also merit goods. A person might undervalue the enrichment they would get from visiting a museum, and their visit also helps preserve cultural institutions for future generations.

Why Merit Goods Are Not the Same as Public Goods

Students sometimes confuse merit goods with public goods. They are different concepts. A public good is defined by its characteristics: it is non-rivalrous (one person's consumption does not reduce availability for others) and non-excludable (you cannot stop non-payers from consuming it). National defence is a public good. A merit good is defined by a normative judgment: society believes it should be provided, regardless of whether the market would provide it. Education is a merit good, but it is not a public good — you can exclude non-payers (private schools charge fees), and at some level of crowding it is rivalrous (a lecture hall has limited seats). Merit goods are underprovided by the market because of externalities and information failure, not because of non-excludability and non-rivalry.

5. Government Policy Responses

When a positive externality causes a market to underproduce or underconsume, the government has four main tools. Each has strengths and weaknesses, and the best choice depends on the specific good and the context.

5.1 Subsidies

A subsidy is a payment from the government to producers or consumers that reduces the cost of producing or consuming a good. The goal is to increase the quantity toward Q_opt. Suppose a government wants to increase university attendance. It could give each university £5,000 per student per year, allowing the university to charge lower tuition fees. Alternatively, it could give each student a £5,000 grant, reducing the net price the student pays. A per-unit subsidy to producers shifts the MPC (supply) curve downward by the amount of the subsidy. The market price falls, and the quantity consumed rises. If the government sets the subsidy exactly equal to the external benefit at Q_opt — that is, the vertical distance between MSB and MPB at the optimal quantity — then the new market equilibrium will be at Q_opt. The welfare loss is eliminated. Subsidies have important advantages. They work through the market mechanism rather than replacing it, which means consumers still choose how much to consume. They can be targeted precisely — for example, a government can subsidise university places in engineering but not in subjects with lower external benefits. But subsidies also have significant drawbacks. A subsidy of £5,000 per student, multiplied by 500,000 university students, costs the government £2.5 billion per year. That money must come from taxation, which creates its own distortions. The government also has to estimate the external benefit, and getting it wrong means either undercorrecting (the subsidy is too small, and underconsumption persists) or overcorrecting (the subsidy is too large, wasting taxpayer money). A further problem is that universities may raise their fees to capture the subsidy, a phenomenon economists call incidence — the legal recipient of a subsidy is not always the economic beneficiary.

5.2 Direct Provision

Direct provision means the government produces the good itself and makes it available free at the point of use, funded by general taxation. State schools are the clearest example. The UK government does not subsidise parents to buy education from private providers. It builds schools, hires teachers, and provides education directly to every child aged 5 to 18 without charging a fee at the door. Direct provision has a powerful equity advantage. Every child receives an education regardless of their parents' income. The information failure — parents might undervalue education — is overridden because schooling is compulsory and free. But direct provision also has problems. Without competition, state schools may have weak incentives to improve. The government must decide what "good education" looks like — a centralised curriculum may not suit every child. And the absence of a market price means the government cannot easily tell whether it is providing the right quantity. How many school places is the right number? The government estimates, but it does not have a market signal to guide it.

5.3 Regulation

Regulation means the government passes a law requiring people to consume the good. Compulsory schooling laws are the most important example. In England, a young person must stay in education or training until age 18. The law does not ask whether the 17-year-old wants to stay. It requires it. Regulation has the virtue of certainty. The government knows that 100% of children up to age 16 will receive an education (minus a small number who evade the system). It does not need to guess how consumers will respond to a price change. But regulation restricts individual freedom. Some parents object to compulsory schooling on philosophical or religious grounds. Regulation also imposes enforcement costs — truancy officers, legal proceedings, fines for non-compliance. And regulation is a blunt instrument: it treats everyone the same even though the external benefit of one more year of schooling may be high for some students and low for others.

5.4 Information Campaigns

An information campaign is a government effort to provide information that corrects consumers' underestimation of a good's benefits. Public health campaigns are the most visible example. "Catch It, Bin It, Kill It" encouraged people to practise respiratory hygiene during flu season. Anti-smoking campaigns on television and cigarette packaging have shifted attitudes toward smoking over decades. Nutritional labelling on food packages gives consumers information about calories, sugar, and saturated fat. Information campaigns are relatively cheap compared to subsidies or direct provision. A television advertisement campaign might cost £10 million — a fraction of the £2.5 billion annual cost of subsidising university tuition. Information campaigns also preserve consumer choice: people are informed but not coerced. The limitation is that information alone is often insufficient. People know that smoking causes cancer. The information failure is not that they lack facts; it is that nicotine is addictive and people discount future health consequences. An information campaign will not close a large gap between Q_fm and Q_opt on its own. Information campaigns work best as a complement to other policies — providing the rationale for a tax, or reinforcing the habit that a subsidy encourages.

6. Worked Example: Finding the Optimal Subsidy

A government is considering a subsidy for a vaccination programme. The market for vaccinations is described by the following data: - MPB: P = 120 − 2Q - MSB: P = 180 − 2Q - MPC = MSC: P = 30 + Q Prices are in dollars and Q is in millions of vaccinations per year. Problem: Find the free-market quantity, the socially optimal quantity, and the per-unit subsidy needed to reach Q_opt. Approach: To find the free-market equilibrium, set MPB equal to MPC and solve for Q. To find the social optimum, set MSB equal to MSC and solve for Q. The required subsidy equals the external benefit per unit at Q_opt — which is MSB minus MPB at that quantity. Step 1: Free-market equilibrium. Set MPB = MPC. 120 − 2Q = 30 + Q 120 − 30 = 2Q + Q 90 = 3Q Q_fm = 30 million vaccinations The free-market price: P = 120 − 2(30) = 120 − 60 = $60 per vaccination. Step 2: Socially optimal quantity. Set MSB = MSC. 180 − 2Q = 30 + Q 180 − 30 = 2Q + Q 150 = 3Q Q_opt = 50 million vaccinations Step 3: Per-unit subsidy needed. At Q_opt = 50, the external benefit per unit is the vertical distance between MSB and MPB: External benefit = MSB − MPB = (180 − 2Q) − (120 − 2Q) = 60 At Q_opt = 50: external benefit = 60 dollars per vaccination. (You can also see this directly: both curves have the same slope, so the vertical distance is constant at 60.) The government needs to provide a subsidy of $60 per vaccination. Step 4: Total subsidy cost. At Q_opt = 50 million vaccinations with a $60 per-unit subsidy: Total cost = 50,000,000 × \(60 = \)3 billion per year. Why this makes sense: The free market delivers 30 million vaccinations because consumers only value their own protection. Society as a whole values each vaccination at \(60 more than the individual consumer does, because of herd immunity. The \)60 subsidy bridges the gap between private and social benefit, and the market expands to 50 million vaccinations — the quantity where the full social benefit equals the cost of provision.

IB Exam Tip

When drawing externality diagrams, label everything. Examiners look for: both axes labelled (Price/Cost and Quantity); all four curves clearly marked (MPC, MSC, MPB, MSB); Q_fm and Q_opt clearly indicated; the welfare loss triangle shaded. If two curves coincide — for example, MPB = MSB in a production externality — write "MPB = MSB" on the curve, not just "D." That label tells the examiner you understand which curves coincide and why. For written answers, state the relationship between the curves before describing the outcome. "Because MSB exceeds MPB, the free market equilibrium at Q_fm is to the left of the socially optimal quantity at Q_opt, creating a welfare loss." The examiner rewards the causal chain: curve relationship → equilibrium location → welfare consequence.

Practice Problems

Problem 1. A company operating in the solar panel industry installs a new manufacturing plant. During production, the plant's research and development team discovers a more efficient way to manufacture photovoltaic cells. Within two years, other solar panel manufacturers have adopted similar techniques without paying the original company. (a) Identify whether this is a positive production externality or a positive consumption externality. (b) Explain why the free-market quantity of solar panels produced by this company will be lower than the socially optimal quantity. Problem 2. A city has a vaccination rate of 68% against a contagious disease. The herd immunity threshold is 93%. The marginal private benefit of vaccination is given by MPB = 100 − Q, and the marginal social benefit is MSB = 160 − Q. The marginal private cost and marginal social cost are both MPC = MSC = 10 + 0.5Q, where Q is in thousands of vaccinations. (a) Calculate the free-market equilibrium quantity of vaccinations. (b) Calculate the socially optimal quantity. (c) Calculate the welfare loss at the free-market equilibrium. Problem 3. Your diagram must show the MPB, MSB, MPC, and MSC curves; label Q_fm and Q_opt; and shade the welfare loss. A government is choosing between two policies to increase museum attendance, which generates positive consumption externalities through cultural preservation and educational benefits. Policy A is a £5 subsidy for every museum ticket. Policy B is a £2 million information campaign promoting the benefits of visiting museums. Evaluate the relative strengths and weaknesses of these two approaches. Consider effectiveness, cost, and equity in your answer. Problem 5. *(IB-style, 4 marks)* A firm that manufactures electric buses invests in a worker training programme. Each trained worker increases the firm's output, but trained workers sometimes leave and take jobs at other bus manufacturers. (a) Define the term "positive production externality." (2 marks) (b) Using the concept of marginal social cost and marginal private cost, explain why the firm will train fewer workers than is socially optimal. (2 marks) Problem 6. A government is considering whether to respond to low university attendance among students from low-income families by: (i) providing a means-tested grant of £7,000 per year directly to students, (ii) making university attendance compulsory for all students who achieve certain A-level grades, or (iii) funding a nationwide advertising campaign about the lifetime earnings premium of a university degree. (a) For each option, explain how it addresses the underconsumption of university education. (b) Identify one significant disadvantage of each option. (c) Recommend a combination of policies and justify your recommendation.

Answers

Answer 1. (a) This is a positive production externality. The company's manufacturing and research activity — part of its production process — generates knowledge that spills over to other firms in the solar panel industry. Those other firms benefit from the innovation without paying for the research that produced it. The externality originates in the production process, not in the consumption of solar panels. (b) The company decides how much to produce based on its marginal private cost — the cost of employing researchers, running the manufacturing plant, and purchasing raw materials. The company does not capture the value of the knowledge spillovers that benefit its competitors. Because the marginal social cost of the company's production is lower than the marginal private cost — the social cost subtracts the value of the knowledge that benefits other firms — the quantity the company produces at its private equilibrium (MPC = MPB) is less than the quantity that would be optimal for society (MSC = MSB). The market underproduces solar panels relative to the social optimum because the company cannot charge other firms for the knowledge its R&D creates. Answer 2. (a) Free-market equilibrium: set MPB = MPC. 100 − Q = 10 + 0.5Q 100 − 10 = Q + 0.5Q 90 = 1.5Q Q_fm = 60 thousand vaccinations The free-market quantity is 60,000 vaccinations. This is below the herd immunity threshold of 93%. (b) Socially optimal quantity: set MSB = MSC. 160 − Q = 10 + 0.5Q 160 − 10 = Q + 0.5Q 150 = 1.5Q Q_opt = 100 thousand vaccinations But note: the socially optimal quantity of 100,000 exceeds the herd immunity threshold of 93,000. So the social optimum achieves herd immunity. (c) The welfare loss is the area of the triangle between Q_fm = 60 and Q_opt = 100, bounded above by MSB and below by MSC. At Q = 60: MSB = 160 − 60 = 100; MSC = 10 + 0.5(60) = 40. The gap is 100 − 40 = 60. At Q = 100: MSB = 160 − 100 = 60; MSC = 10 + 0.5(100) = 60. The gap is zero. Welfare loss = 0.5 × base × height = 0.5 × (100 − 60) × 60 = 0.5 × 40 × 60 = 1,200. The welfare loss is 1,200 (in whatever units price is measured in — if price is in dollars, the welfare loss is $1,200). A common error is to use the wrong base or height. The base is the quantity gap (Q_opt − Q_fm). The height is the gap between MSB and MSC at Q_fm — the point where the welfare loss triangle is widest. Answer 3. First diagram: The MSB curve lies above the MPB curve at every quantity, because consuming university education generates external benefits — higher productivity, lower crime, more informed voting — that individual students do not capture. The MPC and MSC curves are a single line, labelled "MPC = MSC." The free-market equilibrium Q_fm is where MPB intersects MPC. The social optimum Q_opt is where MSB intersects MSC, to the right of Q_fm. The welfare loss is the triangle between Q_fm and Q_opt, above the MPC = MSC curve and below the MSB curve. Second diagram: A per-unit subsidy to students shifts the supply curve (MPC) downward. The new equilibrium occurs where the shifted MPC (MPC minus subsidy) intersects MPB. If the subsidy per unit equals the vertical distance between MSB and MPB at Q_opt, the new equilibrium quantity will be exactly Q_opt. The size of the subsidy required is the external benefit per unit at the socially optimal quantity. The government must estimate the value of the external benefits — higher tax revenue from graduates' higher earnings, reduced crime costs, increased civic participation — and set the subsidy equal to that value at the margin. If the estimate is too low, the subsidy will be too small and underconsumption will persist. If the estimate is too high, the government will overspend and consumption will exceed Q_opt, creating a different kind of inefficiency. Answer 4. Policy A, the £5 per-ticket subsidy, works by lowering the effective price to consumers. If the current market price of a museum ticket is £12, a £5 subsidy reduces the net price to £7. At a lower price, more people visit museums. The subsidy directly addresses the price barrier that may be preventing some people from attending. The strength of this approach is that it works through the market: people who value museums most will respond most to the price reduction. The weaknesses are that the subsidy costs the government £5 times the number of tickets sold — if 500,000 tickets are sold annually, the cost is £2.5 million per year — and that the subsidy benefits people who would have visited the museum anyway, a problem known as deadweight loss. People who already buy tickets at £12 receive £5 of government money without changing their behaviour. Policy B, the £2 million information campaign, works by correcting the information failure that may cause people to undervalue museum visits. If people do not know what is in the museum or do not appreciate the educational benefits, an advertising campaign can change their perception. The strength of this approach is its low cost — £2 million is a one-off expenditure — and the fact that it preserves consumer sovereignty. The weakness is that information alone may have a small effect. Knowing that a museum has a wonderful collection of Roman artefacts does not help a family that cannot afford the £12 ticket price. Information campaigns work best when the barrier is ignorance, not poverty. In terms of cost, the information campaign is cheaper in absolute terms and does not create an ongoing fiscal commitment. In terms of equity, the subsidy is more progressive because it reduces the price for low-income visitors — though a flat £5 subsidy also benefits high-income visitors. A means-tested subsidy, such as free admission for people receiving income support, would be more equitable but more expensive to administer. Answer 5. The key elements examiners look for are: (i) the externality originates in the production process, not in consumption; (ii) it benefits third parties; and (iii) those third parties do not pay for the benefit. Answer 6. (a) The means-tested grant reduces the net price of university for low-income students. By giving £7,000 directly to the student, the government lowers the financial barrier that is most binding for students from poor families. The grant increases the MPB perceived by the student because the cost has fallen, and consumption moves closer to Q_opt. Compulsory attendance overrides the individual's decision entirely. The government mandates university for all students who achieve certain grades, ensuring that every academically qualified student attends regardless of whether they personally value it. This directly achieves the quantity the government believes is optimal. The advertising campaign addresses the information failure. Students from low-income families may not know how much more graduates earn over a lifetime. By providing that information — for example, advertising that graduates earn on average £250,000 more over a career — the campaign raises students' perception of the private benefit, shifting the MPB curve toward MSB. (b) The grant is expensive. At £7,000 per student per year for a three-year degree, the government spends £21,000 per student. If 100,000 students receive the grant, the annual cost is £2.1 billion. There is also a deadweight loss: some students who receive the grant would have attended university anyway. Compulsory attendance is coercive. It removes individual choice and may force students into university who would be better served by apprenticeships, vocational training, or immediate employment. It also treats all students identically, even though the external benefit of university varies by field of study and by individual aptitude. The advertising campaign may have limited impact. Knowing the average earnings premium does not overcome a family's immediate need for the income an 18-year-old could earn if they entered the workforce immediately. Credit constraints are a real barrier that information alone cannot solve. (c) A combination of the means-tested grant and the advertising campaign is likely to be most effective. The grant addresses the financial barrier, which is the most binding constraint for low-income students. The advertising campaign addresses the information failure, which may cause students to undervalue even a subsidised education. The two policies reinforce each other: the campaign makes students aware of the benefits, and the grant makes those benefits accessible. Compulsory attendance is unnecessarily heavy-handed for this particular market failure, because the financial and information barriers can be addressed with less coercive tools. A score of 7 in IB Economics requires recognising that policy combinations often work better than single tools, because different barriers require different solutions.

Evaluate

Question: "The government should provide all merit goods free at the point of consumption." Evaluate this statement. Model Answer: The claim that all merit goods should be free at the point of use is appealing in its simplicity and its commitment to equity. If education and healthcare are so beneficial that society has an interest in everyone consuming them, why charge any price at all? But the claim collapses under scrutiny of what different merit goods actually require and what "free" provision costs in practice. Start with the strongest case for free provision. Basic education is compulsory and free in virtually every developed country, and for good reason. A child who does not learn to read cannot participate fully in the economy or in democratic life. The external benefits — a literate workforce, an informed electorate, lower crime — are enormous. Charging for primary schooling would exclude children whose parents cannot pay, perpetuating an inequality that harms everyone. For basic education, free provision passes every test: it corrects the information failure (parents cannot undervalue schooling if it is compulsory and free), it captures maximum external benefits, and it meets a widely shared standard of equity. But now consider museums. Museums are merit goods — they generate positive consumption externalities through cultural preservation, education, and tourism. Should every museum be free? The British Museum in London is free, and its attendance is enormous — over 5 million visitors per year. The Tate Modern is free and similarly popular. But free admission is not costless. The government must fund these museums through taxation, and the tax revenue has an opportunity cost. The £50 million that funds free museum admission could fund 1,000 additional nurses in the NHS. Is free museum admission worth more than those nurses? The question is not whether museums are good — they are — but whether the marginal pound of government spending delivers more social benefit when allocated to free museums than when allocated to the next-best alternative. There is a second problem with universal free provision: it removes the price signal. When a good has a zero price, consumers have no reason to economise. If all healthcare is free, patients may visit the GP for minor ailments that would resolve on their own, clogging the system and increasing waiting times for patients with serious conditions. A small co-payment — £10 for a GP visit, with exemptions for low-income patients — can discourage frivolous use while preserving access for those who genuinely need care. Price is not just a way of raising revenue; it is a rationing mechanism, and removing it entirely can create congestion and waste. The statement also assumes that government provision is equally efficient for all merit goods. The evidence is mixed. State schools in some countries perform excellently; in others, they are plagued by absenteeism, outdated curricula, and crumbling facilities. Direct government provision does not automatically mean high-quality provision. A system of publicly funded but independently operated schools — charter schools in the United States, academies in England — may combine the equity of public funding with the efficiency incentives of competition. A further difficulty is defining the boundary of "all merit goods." If education is a merit good at primary and secondary level, is it also a merit good at PhD level? The external benefits of a PhD in art history are smaller and harder to demonstrate than the external benefits of teaching a child to read. A universal rule — "all merit goods free" — cannot distinguish between these cases. The strongest counterargument is comparative: the alternative to free provision is not zero provision. It is a system in which the government subsidises merit goods at a rate calibrated to their external benefits, combined with targeted support for those who genuinely cannot afford the residual price. A university education might be subsidised at 70% rather than 100%, with means-tested grants for low-income students. Museum tickets might be £5 instead of £0, with free admission for school groups and pensioners. This approach preserves the price signal, reduces the fiscal burden, and still captures most of the external benefit. From a stakeholder perspective, free universal provision benefits low-income households most, because they would otherwise be priced out. But it imposes costs on taxpayers, including low-income taxpayers who may not use the services as intensively as middle-class households. University students from wealthy families receive the same free tuition as students from poor families — a transfer from the general taxpayer to the relatively affluent that is hard to justify on equity grounds. In conclusion, the statement is too sweeping. Free provision is justified for core merit goods — basic education, essential healthcare, vaccinations — where the external benefits are largest, the equity case is strongest, and the information failure is most severe. For other merit goods — higher education beyond a basic level, museums, the arts, elective healthcare — a partial subsidy combined with means-tested support is more efficient and more equitable. The responsible policy stance is not "free everything" or "market everything" but a careful calibration of intervention to the specific characteristics of each good. A score of 7 requires recognising that the optimal policy varies across merit goods and that the government's budget constraint is itself a form of scarcity that demands opportunity cost reasoning.
