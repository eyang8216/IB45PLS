1. Prior Knowledge Check

Question 1: In Lesson 1, you learned the difference between positive and normative statements. Which type is testable against evidence?

Show Answer

Answer: A positive statement is testable — it can be shown to be true or false by checking against data. A normative statement ("should," "ought," "fair") involves a value judgment that cannot be settled by evidence alone.

Question 2: The PPC you studied in Lesson 2 is a very simplified representation of an economy. What was simplified — that is, what did the model leave out that exists in the real world?

Show Answer

Answer: The PPC simplified by assuming only two goods, full employment, fixed technology, and no international trade. In reality, economies produce millions of different goods, always have some unemployment, constantly develop new technology, and trade across borders. These simplifications do not make the PPC useless — they make it manageable. We can now understand the core idea (trade-offs, opportunity cost) and add complexity later.

2. What Economic Models Are and Why We Use Them

An economic model is a simplified representation of reality. It strips away the complexity of the real world — the millions of consumers, thousands of products, constant changes in technology and taste — and focuses on a few key relationships. The PPC you studied in Lesson 2 is a model. The supply-and-demand diagram you will study in Lesson 4 is a model. Every diagram in this course is part of some model.

Why do economists simplify? Because the real economy is too complex to understand all at once. A map that showed every pebble on every road would be useless for navigation. A model that tried to include every detail of every transaction in every market would be useless for understanding. Models are to economics what maps are to geography: deliberate simplifications that help us see the essential features.

The test of a model is not whether it is realistic in every detail — no model is. The test is whether it helps us explain and predict. The supply-and-demand model predicts that when a frost destroys part of the coffee harvest, coffee prices will rise. This prediction is correct, even though the model simplifies away the fact that some coffee drinkers are loyal to specific brands and some growers have long-term contracts. The model captures the essential force — scarcity — and that is enough.

Check-in: A weather forecasting model simplifies the atmosphere into grid cells because simulating every air molecule is impossible. An economic model simplifies the economy. What is the equivalent in economics of the air molecules — the details that models leave out?

Show Answer

Answer: Economic models leave out individual consumer preferences, firm-specific production techniques, personal relationships between buyers and sellers, psychological factors, cultural influences, and countless other micro-level details. They focus instead on aggregate patterns: overall demand, overall supply, and the relationship between price and quantity.

3. The Ceteris Paribus Assumption

Ceteris paribus is a Latin phrase meaning "all other things being equal" or "holding other things constant." It is the most important assumption in economic analysis, and you will use it in nearly every answer you write for the IB.

Here is the problem ceteris paribus solves. In the real world, many things change at the same time. The price of coffee might rise because of a frost in Brazil, but simultaneously, a new health study might report that coffee prevents disease (increasing demand), a recession might reduce consumers' incomes (decreasing demand), and a new harvesting technology might lower production costs (increasing supply). If we tried to analyse all these changes at once, we would never be able to isolate the effect of any single factor.

Ceteris paribus allows us to hold all other factors constant and examine one change at a time. We say: "Assuming everything else stays the same, a frost that destroys part of the coffee harvest will cause the price of coffee to rise." This is not a claim that nothing else ever changes. It is a claim about cause and effect: this specific change, by itself, has this specific effect.

Check-in: A student says: "The ceteris paribus assumption is unrealistic because in the real world, many things change at once. Therefore, economic models are useless." How would you respond?

Show Answer

Answer: The student is confusing a simplification with an error. Ceteris paribus is not a claim about how the world is — it is a tool for understanding cause and effect. When a doctor tests whether a drug lowers blood pressure, she holds other factors constant (same dose, same diet, same exercise) precisely because many things affect blood pressure. If she changed everything at once, she would learn nothing. The same logic applies in economics. Ceteris paribus allows us to isolate relationships. Once we understand each relationship individually, we can build more complex models that incorporate multiple simultaneous changes.

4. The Scientific Method in Economics

Economics is a social science. It uses the scientific method — formulating hypotheses, testing them against evidence, and refining theories based on what the data shows — but it faces challenges that natural sciences like physics or chemistry do not.

The key challenge is that economists cannot usually conduct controlled experiments. A physicist can hold temperature constant while varying pressure to isolate the effect of pressure on a gas. An economist cannot hold consumer confidence constant while varying the interest rate to isolate the effect of monetary policy. The economy does not have a laboratory with a control group. When the central bank changes interest rates, consumer confidence, global demand, technology, and political conditions all change simultaneously. Separating the effect of the interest rate change from everything else is extremely difficult.

Because of this limitation, economics relies heavily on natural experiments (situations where something changes for one group but not another, mimicking a controlled experiment), statistical methods that try to control for confounding variables, and theoretical models that make predictions which can then be tested against data.

Check-in: Is the claim "economics is not a real science because it cannot conduct controlled experiments" a fair criticism?

Show Answer

Answer: Not entirely fair. Astronomy cannot conduct controlled experiments on stars, and evolutionary biology cannot re-run the history of life on Earth, yet both are sciences. The defining feature of science is not controlled experiments — it is the systematic effort to test claims against evidence. Economics does this, even if the evidence is messier and the conclusions less certain than in physics. However, the criticism correctly identifies that economic knowledge is inherently more uncertain than knowledge in many natural sciences.

5. Why Economists Disagree

Economics has a reputation for disagreement — "if you ask three economists, you will get four opinions." This reputation is partly deserved and partly a misunderstanding of what the disagreement is about.

Economists disagree for two fundamentally different reasons:

1. Disagreement about positive facts. Two economists might disagree about what the data shows. Does raising the minimum wage reduce employment? Some studies find a small negative effect; others find no effect. The economists disagree because the evidence is genuinely ambiguous — the effect may depend on the level of the minimum wage, the state of the economy, the industry, and the methodology used to measure it. This kind of disagreement can, in principle, be resolved by better data and better methods.

2. Disagreement about normative values. Two economists might agree about what the data shows — raising the minimum wage reduces employment among teenagers by 2% but increases incomes for low-wage workers by 8% — and still disagree about whether the policy should be implemented. The first economist might prioritise minimising unemployment and oppose the increase. The second might prioritise raising incomes for the working poor and support it. Both are being intellectually honest. They simply weigh the competing goals differently.

Check-in: Two economists debate a carbon tax. Both agree the tax will reduce emissions by 15% and increase energy costs for low-income households by 3%. One supports the tax; the other opposes it. Is this a positive or normative disagreement?

Show Answer

Answer: It is a normative disagreement. Both economists agree on the positive facts (what the tax will do — reduce emissions by 15%, increase costs by 3%). They disagree about which outcome matters more: reducing emissions or protecting low-income households from higher costs. This disagreement cannot be resolved by collecting more data. It is a genuine value conflict.

6. A Brief History of Economic Thought

Understanding where economic ideas come from helps you understand why they matter. Here are the three most important schools of thought for IB Economics SL.

Classical economics (Adam Smith, late 1700s): Smith argued that individuals pursuing their own self-interest in competitive markets produce outcomes that benefit society as a whole — as if guided by an invisible hand. The classical view emphasises the self-regulating nature of markets and a limited role for government. Classical economists believed that supply creates its own demand (Say's Law) and that persistent unemployment is impossible because wages will adjust downward until all workers who want jobs are employed.

Keynesian economics (John Maynard Keynes, 1930s): Writing during the Great Depression, when unemployment in the United States reached 25%, Keynes argued that classical theory could not explain what he was seeing. An economy could get stuck in a recession with high unemployment, and there was no automatic mechanism pushing it back to full employment. Wages did not fall easily (workers resist pay cuts), and even if they did, falling wages would reduce workers' incomes and their ability to spend, potentially making the recession worse. Keynes argued that government must actively manage aggregate demand — increasing spending and cutting taxes during recessions to compensate for the shortfall in private spending.

Monetarism (Milton Friedman, 1960s–70s): Monetarists argued that Keynesians had overestimated the government's ability to manage the economy and underestimated the economy's ability to self-correct. The key macroeconomic variable, monetarists argued, is the money supply. Inflation is "always and everywhere a monetary phenomenon." The central bank should focus on keeping the money supply growing at a steady, predictable rate, and otherwise leave the economy alone. Attempts to fine-tune the economy through fiscal policy are likely to make things worse, not better, because of the long and variable time lags involved.

7. Correlation and Causation

This is one of the most important concepts in all of economics — and one of the most common exam traps.

Correlation means that two variables move together. When ice cream sales are high, drowning deaths are also high. These two variables are correlated. Causation means that one variable causes the other to change. Does eating ice cream cause drowning? No. A third factor — hot weather — causes both: people eat more ice cream when it is hot, and more people go swimming when it is hot, leading to more drowning deaths. The correlation between ice cream and drowning is real, but there is no causal relationship between them.

In economics, correlation is easy to find. Causation is hard to prove. Just because two economic variables move together does not mean one causes the other. A government might cut taxes and then observe GDP rising. Does this prove that tax cuts cause growth? Not necessarily — perhaps the global economy was already recovering, or perhaps a technological breakthrough boosted productivity at the same time. Disentangling correlation from causation requires careful statistical work, and even then, economists often disagree.

Check-in: A study finds that countries with higher chocolate consumption per person also have more Nobel Prize winners per person. Does eating chocolate cause people to win Nobel Prizes? What might explain the correlation?

Show Answer

Answer: Almost certainly not. The correlation is likely driven by a third factor: national wealth. Wealthy countries have citizens who can afford more chocolate, and wealthy countries also have better-funded universities and research institutions, producing more Nobel Prize winners. The chocolate consumption and the Nobel Prizes are both caused by wealth, not by each other. This is a classic example of correlation without causation — sometimes called a spurious correlation.

8. Worked Examples

Worked Example 1: Applying Ceteris Paribus

Problem: An economist observes that when the central bank lowered interest rates last year, GDP growth increased from 1.2% to 2.8%. A critic says: "But at the same time, the government also increased infrastructure spending, and a new trade deal boosted exports. You cannot claim the interest rate cut caused the growth." How should the economist respond, using the concept of ceteris paribus?

Step 1: The critic is correct that multiple changes occurred simultaneously. The raw observation (rates down, growth up) does not prove causation.

Step 2: The economist's claim is a ceteris paribus claim: "Holding infrastructure spending, the trade deal, and all other factors constant, a cut in interest rates will increase GDP growth." This is a theoretical claim about a mechanism — lower rates reduce borrowing costs, which increases investment and consumption, which raises GDP.

Step 3: To test this claim, the economist would need to use statistical methods that control for the effects of infrastructure spending and the trade deal, isolating the effect of the interest rate change alone. This is how economists use ceteris paribus in practice: not by pretending other things do not change, but by using statistical tools to hold them constant mathematically.

⚠️ Common Wrong Approach: Claiming "the interest rate cut caused the growth because growth increased when rates were cut" without acknowledging the other changes. This is the post hoc ergo propter hoc fallacy — "after this, therefore because of this." In an IB exam, always distinguish between observing a correlation and establishing causation.

Worked Example 2: Positive vs Normative Disagreement

Problem: Two economists are asked about a proposal to increase the tax on petrol by £0.50 per litre. Economist A says: "This tax will reduce petrol consumption by 8% and increase government revenue by £4 billion per year. It is an efficient way to reduce carbon emissions." Economist B says: "This tax will reduce petrol consumption by 8% and increase government revenue by £4 billion per year. It will disproportionately harm rural residents who have no alternative to driving." Identify where they agree (positive) and where they disagree (normative).

Step 1: Both economists make the same positive predictions: 8% reduction in consumption, £4 billion in revenue. They agree on the facts.

Step 2: Economist A's normative position: reducing carbon emissions is the priority, and the tax is "efficient" (a value-laden term implying this is a good outcome).

Step 3: Economist B's normative position: the distributional impact on rural residents is the priority, and this cost makes the tax undesirable.

Step 4: Both economists can be right about the facts and simply disagree about values. This is why economic debates often cannot be resolved by "more evidence" — the disagreement is not about evidence.

⚠️ Common Wrong Approach: Assuming that one economist must be "wrong." In normative disagreements, neither is wrong — they simply have different priorities. The IB rewards students who recognise this distinction.

Worked Example 3: Correlation vs Causation (Exam Trap)

Problem: A student looks at data and finds that in years when the winning football team's home city is in the north of the country, GDP growth averages 2.1%. In years when the winning team is from the south, GDP growth averages 3.4%. The student concludes that southern football teams winning causes higher economic growth. Identify the fallacy and explain what might actually be happening. (This targets the exam trap of mistaking correlation for causation.)

Step 1: The student has found a correlation: southern wins and higher growth occur together. But correlation is not causation.

Step 2: Plausible alternative explanations: the south might have a larger share of GDP, so the southern economy simply grows faster on average regardless of football outcomes. The correlation could be coincidental. Or a third factor (e.g., higher population and economic activity in the south means both more GDP growth and more successful football teams due to larger talent pools).

Step 3: The causation could even run the other way: higher economic growth in the south (for unrelated reasons) might increase spending on football infrastructure, making southern teams more competitive.

Step 4: Without a mechanism explaining how football wins could cause GDP growth, the claim is absurd. This is a deliberately obvious example, but the same logical error — inferring causation from correlation — appears regularly in economic policy debates.

⚠️ Common Wrong Approach (The Exam Trap): Presenting a correlation as if it were proof of causation without considering alternative explanations. In IB Economics, whenever you see a claim that "X caused Y" based on observing X and Y moving together, ask: (1) Could Y have caused X instead? (2) Could a third factor Z have caused both? (3) Is there a plausible mechanism linking X to Y? These questions are the mark scheme for any analysis of causal claims.

⚠️ Common Mistakes to Avoid

  1. Mistake: Thinking models are "wrong" because they are unrealistic. Why it's wrong: All models are unrealistic — that is the point. A model is judged by its usefulness, not its realism. Correct approach: Ask: "Does this model help us understand and predict?" not "Does this model capture every detail?"
  2. Mistake: Using ceteris paribus as an excuse to ignore obvious confounding factors. Why it's wrong: Ceteris paribus is a starting point for analysis, not a licence to pretend other changes do not matter. Correct approach: First analyse the effect of one change in isolation (ceteris paribus). Then consider how other simultaneous changes might modify the outcome. This two-step approach is what the IB expects.
  3. Mistake: Assuming that because economists disagree, economics is useless. Why it's wrong: Economists agree on far more than they disagree on. The areas of disagreement tend to be normative and tend to attract more attention. Correct approach: Distinguish between positive disagreements (which can be resolved by better evidence) and normative disagreements (which reflect genuine value differences).

Question 4. [Paper 2] A politician says: "The data clearly shows that whenever we cut taxes, the economy grows. Tax cuts cause growth." Using the concepts of correlation, causation, and ceteris paribus, evaluate the politician's claim. [5 marks]

Question 5. [Paper 2, Exam Style] "Economic models are useless because they are based on unrealistic assumptions." Evaluate this statement with reference to at least one specific economic model you have studied. [6 marks]

Answer 1: B. Ceteris paribus holds other variables constant so the effect of one variable can be isolated.

Answer 2: B. More McDonald's restaurants and higher life expectancy are both correlated with national wealth. Wealthy countries have more fast-food chains and better healthcare, so the correlation is real but McDonald's does not cause longevity.

Answer 3: Economists may agree on the predicted effects (positive analysis) but disagree on policy (normative analysis) because they weigh competing goals differently. Example: Two economists agree a £15 minimum wage will raise incomes for 3 million workers but reduce employment by 100,000. One prioritises higher incomes and supports it; the other prioritises minimising unemployment and opposes it. The disagreement is about values, not facts. [✓ 4 marks]

Answer 4: The politician has observed a correlation between tax cuts and growth. This does not prove causation. Other factors (global economic conditions, technological progress, simultaneous government spending) could explain the growth. The direction of causation could even be reversed: strong growth might create political conditions for tax cuts. To evaluate the claim properly, one would need to apply ceteris paribus analysis — holding other factors constant to isolate the effect of tax cuts alone. [✓ 5 marks]

Answer 5: [6-mark CLASPP evaluation:] The statement misunderstands the purpose of models. All models simplify reality — that is what makes them useful. The PPC model assumes only two goods and fixed technology, yet it powerfully illustrates scarcity, choice, and opportunity cost. However, the statement has some validity: models based on false assumptions can lead to bad policy if their limitations are ignored. The 2008 financial crisis was partly caused by financial models that assumed housing prices could never fall nationwide — an assumption that proved catastrophically wrong. The key is not whether a model is realistic but whether its simplifications are appropriate for the question being asked. [✓ 6 marks for balanced evaluation with specific examples and acknowledgment of limitations.]

11. Exam Tips

Paper 1: When answering an "explain" question, use ceteris paribus explicitly. Say: "Assuming all other factors remain constant, a fall in the price of a good will..." This signals to the examiner that you understand the methodological framework of economics.
Paper 2: Data response questions often present correlations. The examiner wants you to question whether the correlation represents causation. Look for: omitted variables, reverse causation, and whether a plausible mechanism connects the two variables. Simply stating "correlation does not equal causation" is worth a mark — explaining why, with reference to the specific data, is worth more.
