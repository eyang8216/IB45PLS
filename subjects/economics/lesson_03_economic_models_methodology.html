Lesson 3: Economic Models and Methodology

What You'll Learn

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to:

  • Explain what economic models are and why economists use them
  • Understand the critical role of the ceteris paribus assumption
  • Define equilibrium as a modelling concept
  • Compare the scientific method in economics vs natural sciences
  • Analyse reasons why economists disagree
  • Trace key developments in the history of economic thought
  • Distinguish correlation from causation

1. Models as Simplified Representations of Reality

What Is an Economic Model?

An economic model is a simplified, abstract representation of reality used to understand, explain, and predict economic phenomena. Models are deliberately stripped of extraneous detail to focus on the essential relationships between key variables.

Why simplify? The real economy has millions of interacting agents (households, firms, governments), thousands of goods, and complex feedback loops. Trying to analyse everything simultaneously is impossible. Models isolate a few key relationships, holding everything else constant, to derive clear, testable predictions.

Characteristics of a Good Model

  1. Parsimony — simple rather than complex; uses the minimum number of assumptions needed
  2. Tractability — can be analysed mathematically or logically
  3. Predictive power — generates testable hypotheses about the real world
  4. Generality — applies to a range of situations, not just one specific case

Models Are Not Reality

Milton Friedman argued that models should be judged by their predictive accuracy, not by the "realism" of their assumptions. A model with unrealistic assumptions (e.g., "all consumers are perfectly rational") is useful if it makes accurate predictions about behaviour.

Example: A road map is a model of a road network. It omits trees, buildings, and potholes — but these omissions make it more useful for navigation, not less. Similarly, the demand-and-supply model omits advertising, brand loyalty, and imperfect information — but still predicts price movements remarkably well.

Common IB Models

  • Demand and Supply — predicts equilibrium prices and quantities
  • Production Possibilities Curve (PPC) — illustrates scarcity, choice, and opportunity cost
  • Circular Flow Model — shows flows of goods, services, and money between sectors
  • AD/AS Model — analyses macroeconomic equilibrium, inflation, and unemployment
  • Market Structure Models — perfect competition, monopoly, oligopoly, monopolistic competition

2. The Ceteris Paribus Assumption

Definition

Ceteris paribus (Latin: "other things being equal") is the assumption that all other relevant variables are held constant when analysing the relationship between two variables.

Why Is It Essential?

In the real world, many things change simultaneously. If the price of coffee rises AND incomes fall AND a coffee health scare occurs all at once, it's hard to isolate the effect of the price change alone. Ceteris paribus allows economists to say: "If the price rises, holding everything else constant, quantity demanded falls."

Without ceteris paribus, we observe only correlations (things that happen together), not causal relationships (one thing causing another).

Example

Suppose we observe: when ice cream sales rise, drowning deaths also rise. Does ice cream cause drowning? No — both are caused by a third factor: hot weather. The ceteris paribus approach would hold weather constant, then test whether ice cream sales and drowning are still correlated. They would not be.

Limitations

Ceteris paribus is a thought experiment — in reality, "other things" are rarely equal. Critics argue that economic relationships are interdependent and complex, so isolating one variable at a time can be misleading. However, without this assumption, systematic economic analysis is nearly impossible.

3. Equilibrium as a Modelling Concept

Definition

Equilibrium in economics is a state of balance where there is no inherent tendency for change — all forces (e.g., demand and supply) are equalised.

Types of Equilibrium

TypeDescriptionExample
Stable equilibriumIf disturbed, the system returns to equilibriumMarket equilibrium: if price is above Pe, surplus pushes it back down
Unstable equilibriumIf disturbed, the system moves away from equilibriumA pencil balanced on its tip
Static equilibriumA snapshot — no tendency to change at a single point in timeMarket clearing at Pe, Qe
Dynamic equilibriumA balanced growth path — variables change but in a stable relationshipSteady-state growth in the Solow model

Equilibrium ≠ Optimal

Equilibrium does not mean the outcome is desirable, fair, or efficient. It simply means the forces are balanced:

  • Market equilibrium: Qd = Qs — but this could involve exploitation, pollution, or inequality
  • Nash equilibrium: No player can improve by changing strategy unilaterally — but all could be better off if they cooperated (prisoner's dilemma)

4. The Scientific Method in Economics

Steps of the Scientific Method

  1. Observation — notice a pattern or puzzle in the real world
  2. Hypothesis — propose a tentative explanation or relationship
  3. Model building — formalise the hypothesis mathematically or graphically
  4. Prediction — deduce what the model implies should happen
  5. Empirical testing — compare predictions with real-world data
  6. Refinement or rejection — if predictions match, tentatively accept; if not, revise or discard

Economics vs Natural Sciences

DimensionNatural Sciences (Physics, Chemistry)Economics
Controlled experimentsCommon — in laboratories, all variables can be controlledRare — economies cannot be put in a lab; natural experiments are imperfect
ReplicabilityHigh — experiments can be repeated identicallyLow — economic conditions are never exactly the same twice
Human behaviourNot relevant — particles don't change their mindCentral — people have expectations, emotions, and free will
Value judgmentsLargely absentOften embedded — what to measure, how to interpret, what policies to recommend
PrecisionVery precise (laws of motion, chemical reactions)Approximate — magnitudes and even directions of effects are debated

Social Science Status

Economics is a social science — it uses scientific methods (mathematical models, statistical testing) but studies human behaviour, which is inherently less predictable than physical phenomena. This does not make economics "unscientific" — it makes it a different kind of science.

5. Why Economists Disagree

Economists frequently offer contradictory policy advice. Disagreements arise from:

1. Different Value Judgments (Normative Disagreement)

Even when economists agree on positive facts, they may differ on what should be done. One economist may prioritise efficiency; another may prioritise equity.

Example: Both agree that rent control reduces the quantity and quality of rental housing (positive). But one concludes it should be abolished (normative: efficiency trumps), while the other concludes it should be retained with reforms (normative: protecting tenants trumps efficiency losses).

2. Different Assumptions and Models

Economists may use different models, make different simplifying assumptions, or disagree about which model best captures reality.

Example: A Keynesian economist assumes that prices and wages are "sticky" (slow to adjust) and prescribes fiscal stimulus during a recession. A monetarist assumes markets clear quickly and warns that stimulus will only cause inflation.

3. Different Time Perspectives

Short-run effects often differ from long-run effects. Economists disagreeing about policy may agree on the effects but differ on which time horizon matters more.

Example: Tariffs protect domestic jobs in the short run but reduce efficiency and consumer welfare in the long run. An economist advising a politician facing re-election may prioritise the short run; a development economist may focus on the long run.

4. Different Interpretations of Data

Economic data is noisy, incomplete, and subject to revision. Two economists can look at the same employment data and reach different conclusions about whether a minimum-wage increase helped or hurt workers.

Implications

Disagreement does not mean economics is "useless." The areas of consensus are large (e.g., trade creates gains, inflation is caused by money supply growth, incentives matter). Disagreements highlight the complexity of the subject and the need for humility in policy advice.

6. Brief History of Economic Thought

Adam Smith (1723–1790) — Classical Economics

  • Key work: The Wealth of Nations (1776)
  • Key ideas: The "invisible hand" — individuals pursuing self-interest unintentionally promote social good through market exchange. Specialisation and division of labour increase productivity. Free trade benefits all nations.
  • Policy implication: Laissez-faire — minimal government intervention.

Classical Economics (late 18th–19th century)

  • Key figures: David Ricardo (comparative advantage), Thomas Malthus (population), John Stuart Mill
  • Key ideas: Markets are self-regulating. Supply creates its own demand (Say's Law). The economy tends toward full employment.
  • Weakness: Could not explain persistent unemployment like the Great Depression.

Keynesian Economics (1930s–1970s)

  • Key figure: John Maynard Keynes — The General Theory (1936)
  • Key ideas: Aggregate demand, not just supply, determines output and employment. Economies can settle at equilibrium with high unemployment (liquidity trap). Government should use fiscal policy (spending, tax cuts) to stimulate demand during recessions.
  • Policy implication: Active government intervention to stabilise the economy.

Monetarism (1960s–1980s)

  • Key figure: Milton Friedman
  • Key ideas: Inflation is "always and everywhere a monetary phenomenon." Central banks should target steady, low growth in the money supply rather than actively managing demand. Markets are generally stable if governments don't destabilise them.
  • Policy implication: Rules-based monetary policy; scepticism of fiscal fine-tuning.

Modern Mainstream

Today, most economists draw on insights from multiple schools. There is broad consensus on some issues (central bank independence, free trade benefits) and active debate on others (optimal tax rates, stimulus effectiveness).

7. Correlation vs Causation

Definitions

  • Correlation: Two variables move together in a systematic pattern. Positive correlation: both rise together. Negative correlation: one rises as the other falls.
  • Causation: A change in one variable causes a change in another.

Why This Distinction Is Critical

Correlation does not imply causation. This is one of the most important principles in empirical economics.

Reasons for Spurious Correlation

  1. Reverse causation: A causes B, or B causes A? Example: Do more police officers reduce crime, or do high-crime areas hire more police?
  1. Omitted variable bias: A third factor C causes both A and B. Example: Ice cream sales and drowning deaths are correlated, but both are caused by summer weather.
  1. Coincidence: With enough data, some variables will be correlated purely by chance. Example: The divorce rate in Maine correlates with per capita margarine consumption — clearly spurious.

Establishing Causation

Economists use several methods to move from correlation toward causation:

  • Natural experiments: Exploiting random-like variation (e.g., a policy change affecting one region but not another)
  • Randomised controlled trials (RCTs): Gold standard, but rare in economics (expensive, ethical constraints)
  • Instrumental variables: Using a variable that affects the cause but not the outcome directly
  • Difference-in-differences: Comparing changes over time between treated and untreated groups
  • Regression discontinuity: Comparing outcomes just above and below an arbitrary threshold

IB Exam Tip

In Paper 1 (essay) and Paper 2 (data response), you may be asked to evaluate a model or discuss its limitations. Always mention:

  1. The ceteris paribus assumption — what is held constant and what might change in reality
  2. The model's simplifying assumptions — are they reasonable for the context?
  3. Short-run vs long-run predictions — models may work well in one time frame but not the other

Also, learn the names of Adam Smith, Keynes, and Friedman — IB examiners expect you to reference key thinkers.

Practice Problems

Problem 1: Model Simplification

The PPC model assumes only two goods, fixed resources, fixed technology, and full employment. For each assumption:

(a) Why is it made?

(b) What real-world complexity does it ignore?

(c) Does this weaken the model's usefulness? Explain.

Problem 2: Ceteris Paribus

A study finds that countries with more doctors per capita have higher mortality rates. Does this mean doctors cause death? Explain using the ceteris paribus concept and identify a likely omitted variable.

Problem 3: Equilibrium

A market is in equilibrium at P = £10, Q = 500 units. Then a new technology reduces production costs. Describe:

(a) The immediate disequilibrium.

(b) The process by which a new equilibrium is established.

(c) Whether the new equilibrium is stable.

Problem 4: Economist Disagreement

Two economists examine a proposal to increase the minimum wage by 20%.

  • Economist A says: "This will cause significant job losses among low-skilled workers and should not be implemented."
  • Economist B says: "Any job losses will be small and the benefit to low-income workers justifies the policy."

Identify at least two possible sources of their disagreement.

Problem 5: Correlation vs Causation

For each pair, state whether the relationship is likely causal, reverse causal, or spurious (due to an omitted variable). Explain briefly.

(a) Higher education → Higher lifetime earnings

(b) More fire trucks at a fire → Greater fire damage

(c) Higher ice cream sales → More drownings

(d) Economic growth → Increased CO₂ emissions

Problem 6: IB-Style (Challenging)

"The ceteris paribus assumption is the greatest strength and simultaneously the greatest weakness of economic analysis." Critically discuss this statement with reference to at least two economic models you have studied.

Answers

Answer 1: Model Simplification

(a) Two goods assumption: Made to allow graphical representation in two dimensions. We cannot draw a 100-good PPC on paper.

(b) What it ignores: Real economies produce millions of goods, and trade-offs exist among all of them simultaneously.

(c) Does this weaken usefulness? Not substantially. The two-good model captures the essential trade-off — allocating scarce resources — that applies to an n-good economy. Adding more goods would not change the fundamental insight about opportunity cost. The model is useful because it simplifies.

(a) Fixed resources: Necessary to isolate the effect of resource allocation from the effect of changing resource endowments.

(b) Ignores: In reality, resources change constantly — population grows, capital depreciates and is replaced, new resources are discovered.

(c) Usefulness: The model is useful for understanding a snapshot. For analysing growth over time, we extend the model to allow shifts of the PPC — so the simplification is a starting point, not a limitation.

Answer 2: Ceteris Paribus and Omitted Variables

Does correlation mean doctors cause death? No. This is a classic example of omitted variable bias.

The omitted variable is likely the general health/disease burden of the population. Countries with worse health outcomes (higher disease, poverty, poor sanitation) both: (a) have higher mortality rates, and (b) need and employ more doctors. So both high doctors and high mortality are caused by poor underlying health.

Under ceteris paribus — holding the underlying health of the population constant — more doctors would almost certainly reduce mortality. The raw correlation is spurious because "other things" (population health) are not equal across countries.

Answer 3: Equilibrium Process

(a) Immediate disequilibrium: At the original equilibrium price of £10, the new technology means firms can produce each unit at lower cost. This increases supply (the supply curve shifts right). At £10, quantity supplied now exceeds quantity demanded → excess supply (surplus).

(b) Process to new equilibrium: The surplus puts downward pressure on price. As price falls, quantity demanded increases (law of demand) and quantity supplied decreases along the new supply curve. Price continues falling until Qd = Qs at the new, lower equilibrium price and higher equilibrium quantity.

(c) Stability: The new equilibrium is stable. If price is temporarily above the new Pe, surplus pushes it down; if below, shortage pushes it up. The market converges to equilibrium.

Answer 4: Economist Disagreement Sources

Possible source 1 — Different assumptions/models: Economist A may assume labour demand is highly elastic (responsive to wage changes), so a wage increase causes large job losses. Economist B may assume labour demand is inelastic (employers cannot easily replace workers), so job losses are minimal. Both economists might use different empirical studies that yield different elasticity estimates.

Possible source 2 — Different value judgments: Both economists might agree that the minimum wage increase would cause, say, 2% job losses among low-skilled workers. But Economist A values efficiency and believes any job destruction is unacceptable. Economist B values equity and judges that the wage gains for 98% of workers who keep their jobs outweigh harm to the 2% who lose them. This is a normative disagreement.

Possible source 3 — Different time perspectives: Economist A may focus on short-run effects (immediate layoffs). Economist B may focus on long-run effects (firms adjust through reduced turnover, higher productivity, and small price increases rather than layoffs).

Answer 5: Correlation vs Causation

(a) Education → Earnings: Likely causal — education increases human capital (skills, knowledge) which raises productivity and thus wages. However, some correlation may be due to "ability bias" (more able people both get more education and would have earned more anyway), so the causal effect may be overstated.

(b) Fire trucks → Fire damage: Reverse causation and spurious — larger fires cause more fire trucks to be dispatched, not vice versa. The true causal direction is fire size → (more trucks + more damage). Controlling for fire size, more trucks likely reduce damage.

(c) Ice cream → Drownings: Spurious — omitted variable is summer/heat. Hot weather causes both more ice cream consumption and more swimming (hence more drowning). No causal link between ice cream and drowning.

(d) Growth → CO₂: Likely causal — economic growth typically increases energy consumption and industrial activity, which raises CO₂ emissions. However, the relationship may weaken at higher income levels (Environmental Kuznets Curve) if countries adopt cleaner technologies.

Answer 6: IB-Style Solution

The ceteris paribus assumption — strength and weakness:

Strength: Ceteris paribus enables analytical clarity. Without it, we could not isolate cause-and-effect relationships because in the real world, many variables change simultaneously. The demand-and-supply model depends entirely on ceteris paribus: "If price rises, ceteris paribus, quantity demanded falls." This yields a clean, testable prediction. If we simultaneously allowed incomes, tastes, and related-goods prices to change, we could not say whether a decline in quantity demanded was due to the price increase or something else.

Similarly, the PPC model holds technology, resources, and efficiency constant to isolate the pure trade-off between two goods. Removing ceteris paribus would turn the PPC from a clear analytical tool into a constantly shifting, uninterpretable blur.

Weakness: Ceteris paribus is unrealistic. In reality, "other things" are almost never equal. When the price of oil rises, it doesn't happen in isolation — it's often driven by geopolitical events that simultaneously affect incomes, expectations, and substitute-good prices. The model's prediction ("quantity demanded falls") may be swamped by other forces.

Moreover, the assumption can produce misleading policy advice. A model that predicts "minimum wage increases reduce employment, ceteris paribus" ignores that firms may respond by raising prices, improving productivity, or accepting lower margins — all of which break the ceteris paribus conditions. Policymakers relying solely on ceteris paribus models risk serious errors.

Resolution: The ceteris paribus assumption is a necessary starting point, not an end point. Good economic analysis uses ceteris paribus to derive preliminary predictions, then progressively relaxes the assumption to incorporate real-world complexity. Models are not judged by whether their assumptions are "realistic" but by whether they generate useful, approximately correct predictions. Ceteris paribus is a tool, not a truth claim — and like all tools, its value depends on how it is used.

Evaluate

Question

"Economics can never be a true science because it cannot conduct controlled experiments." Evaluate this claim.

Model Answer

Conclusion: The claim is flawed. While economics cannot conduct laboratory experiments on entire economies, it is a genuine science — a social science — that employs rigorous methodology: mathematical modelling, statistical hypothesis testing, and increasingly, experimental methods. The inability to run controlled macroeconomic experiments limits but does not disqualify economics as a science.

First, the claim misrepresents what science is. Science is defined by its method, not by the ability to run laboratory experiments: systematic observation, hypothesis formation, empirical testing, and willingness to revise theories in light of evidence. Economics does all of these. Astronomers cannot run experiments on stars; evolutionary biologists cannot re-run evolution in a lab — yet both are sciences. Economics faces a similar constraint, not a unique disqualification.

Second, the claim ignores the rise of experimental economics. Randomised controlled trials (RCTs) are now widely used in development economics (e.g., testing the impact of bed nets, microcredit, or school vouchers on outcomes). Lab experiments test game-theoretic predictions about bargaining, cooperation, and market behaviour. While these are micro-level, they are genuine controlled experiments.

Long-term vs short-term: In the short term, the absence of macro-level experiments means macroeconomic knowledge accumulates more slowly and with greater uncertainty than in physics. In the long term, repeated natural experiments (financial crises, policy changes across countries) provide a rich evidentiary base — albeit one requiring careful statistical methods to extract causal inference.

Assumptions: The claim assumes a narrow, physics-centric definition of "true science." This definition excludes not only economics but much of biology, geology, climate science, and epidemiology — all recognised sciences.

Stakeholders: The "economics is not a science" claim, if widely accepted, could undermine public trust in economic advice and justify ignoring evidence-based policy. This harms citizens who benefit from well-designed policies. Conversely, economists who overstate the precision of their "scientific" findings risk discrediting the discipline when predictions fail.

Priorities: Economics should embrace its identity as a social science — adopting the best available methods while communicating uncertainty honestly. Policymakers should treat economic evidence as informative but not infallible.

Pros/Cons: Economics shares many features with natural sciences: mathematical models (pro), empirical testing (pro), predictive ambition (pro). It differs in: no controlled macro experiments (con), human free will introducing unpredictability (con), and value judgments influencing research agendas (con). These differences make economics different from but not less than a science.

Final judgment: Economics is a science in the sense that matters — it uses systematic, evidence-based methods to understand reality and makes testable predictions. The controlled-experiment standard is an arbitrarily narrow definition of science that would exclude many disciplines. The real question is not whether economics is "scientific enough" but whether it is sufficiently humble about the limits of its knowledge — and on that front, there is always room for improvement.
