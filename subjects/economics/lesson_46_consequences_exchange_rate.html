Lesson 46: Consequences of Exchange Rate Changes

What You'll Learn

  • The multiple effects of a currency depreciation on the current account, inflation, GDP, employment, and foreign debt.
  • The Marshall-Lerner condition: the condition under which depreciation improves the current account.
  • The J-curve effect: why the current account often worsens before it improves after a depreciation.
  • The effects of currency appreciation — the symmetrical opposite of depreciation.
  • How to analyse exchange rate changes using AD-AS diagrams alongside forex diagrams.

    • 1. Effects of a Currency Depreciation

    A depreciation (fall in the value of a currency under a floating regime) has effects across multiple macroeconomic variables. The direction and magnitude depend on conditions like the Marshall-Lerner condition and the economy's starting position.

    1.1 Effect on the Current Account

    Direct mechanism: Depreciation makes:

    • Exports cheaper for foreigners (in their currency) → quantity of exports demanded rises (X↑).
    • Imports more expensive for domestic residents (in domestic currency) → quantity of imports demanded falls (M↓).

    If both export and import quantities adjust sufficiently, net exports (X − M) improve → the current account improves.

    BUT — the Marshall-Lerner condition must hold:

    Marshall-Lerner Condition: A depreciation (or devaluation) will improve the current account balance if and only if the sum of the absolute price elasticities of demand for exports and imports exceeds 1:

    $$|PED_x| + |PED_m| > 1$$

    Why? The current account in domestic currency is:

    $$CA = P_x \cdot Q_x - E \cdot P_m^* \cdot Q_m$$

    Where E is the exchange rate (domestic currency per unit of foreign currency). A depreciation increases E. The home-currency price of imports rises mechanically. Whether the value of (X − M) rises depends on whether quantity changes offset the price changes.

    Worked Example: Suppose a country's currency depreciates by 10%.

    Exports (X)0.8+8%0% (priced in domestic currency)+8%
    Imports (M)0.5−5%+10% (depreciation)Value = new Q × new P ≈ +4.5%
    PEDx+PEDm

    Export value rises 8%. Import value rises approximately 4.5%. Net exports improve by approximately 3.5% of the initial trade balance.

    Worked Example — Condition NOT Met: Same depreciation but lower elasticities: |PEDx| = 0.3, |PEDm| = 0.3. Sum = 0.6 < 1.

    Exports0.3+3%+3%
    Imports0.3−3%+10% (price) − 3% (quantity) ≈ +6.7%

    Import value rises by more than export value → current account worsens despite depreciation.

    Empirical note: In the short run (first 6–12 months), trade elasticities are typically low because:

    • Contracts are fixed in advance.
    • Buyers take time to find alternative suppliers.
    • Habits and brand loyalty slow switching.
    • Production and delivery lags.

      • In the long run (2–5 years), elasticities are higher and the Marshall-Lerner condition is generally met for most countries.

      1.2 The J-Curve Effect

      The J-curve describes the time path of the current account after a depreciation:

      Phase 1 (Short run, 0–6 months): The current account worsens. Import prices rise immediately (the depreciation means each unit of imports costs more in domestic currency), but quantities have not yet adjusted. Export quantities have also not yet increased. The trade balance deteriorates.

      Phase 2 (Medium run, 6–18 months): Quantities begin to adjust. Export orders increase, and importers find domestic alternatives or reduce purchases. The trade balance starts improving.

      Phase 3 (Long run, 18+ months): Full quantity adjustment. If the Marshall-Lerner condition holds, the trade balance improves beyond its pre-depreciation level.

      Real-world example: The UK's depreciation after the Brexit referendum (June 2016) saw the pound fall ~15% against the dollar. The UK current account initially widened (worsened) in 2016 Q3–Q4, before gradual improvement in 2017–2018 as export volumes rose and import volumes moderated.

      1.3 Effect on Inflation

      Depreciation is inflationary through multiple channels:

      1. Direct import price effect: Imported consumer goods (clothing, electronics, food) become more expensive → directly raises CPI.
      1. Imported intermediate goods: Imported raw materials, components, and energy become more expensive → raises domestic firms' production costs → cost-push inflation (SRAS shifts left).
      1. Second-round effects: Higher prices lead workers to demand higher wages to maintain real incomes → wage-price spiral if accommodated.
      1. Aggregate demand effect: Higher net exports → AD shifts right → demand-pull inflation if near full employment.

      1.4 Effect on GDP and Employment

      The net effect on GDP depends on which force dominates:

      • Positive: Higher net exports (X − M) → AD shifts right → higher Y and employment.
      • Negative: Higher import costs → SRAS shifts left → higher prices, potentially lower real output (stagflation risk).
      • Negative: Higher inflation may prompt the central bank to raise interest rates → investment and consumption fall.

        • If the economy is below full employment: The AD increase from net exports may dominate → GDP rises, employment rises.

        If the economy is at full employment: The SRAS shift (cost-push) dominates → stagflation: higher prices AND potentially lower output.

        1.5 Effect on Foreign Debt

        For countries with foreign-currency-denominated debt:

        • Depreciation increases the domestic-currency value of the debt → the debt burden rises.
        • Example: A country owes $100 billion. At $1 = 50 pesos, debt = 5 trillion pesos. After depreciation to $1 = 75 pesos, debt = 7.5 trillion pesos — a 50% increase in debt burden without any new borrowing.
        • This is particularly severe for developing countries that borrow in dollars or euros.

          • 2. Effects of a Currency Appreciation

          Appreciation is the symmetric opposite of depreciation. All effects reverse direction:

          VariableEffect of Appreciation
          Current accountWorsens: exports more expensive (X↓), imports cheaper (M↑)
          InflationReduces inflation pressure: cheaper imports reduce CPI and production costs
          GDP and employmentLower net exports → AD shifts left → lower Y and employment (unless offset by other factors)
          Foreign debtEasier to service: foreign-currency debt costs less in domestic currency

          Worked Example: Appreciation of the Swiss Franc

          The Swiss franc (CHF) appreciated sharply in January 2015 when the Swiss National Bank abandoned its cap against the euro (EUR/CHF moved from 1.20 to ~1.00).

          Effects on Switzerland:

          • Exports: Swiss watches, pharmaceuticals, and machinery became ~17% more expensive for Eurozone buyers → export volumes fell.
          • Imports: Goods from the Eurozone became cheaper for Swiss consumers → "shopping tourism" to neighbouring France, Germany, and Italy.
          • Inflation: Switzerland experienced mild deflation (−0.5% to −1% inflation in 2015–2016) as import prices fell.
          • GDP growth: Slowed as export sector contracted, though Switzerland's diversified economy and status as a safe haven cushioned the impact.

            • IB Exam Tip

            The J-curve and Marshall-Lerner condition are frequently tested on Paper 1 (essay) and Paper 2 (data response). When presented with a depreciation scenario, always ask: (1) Is the Marshall-Lerner condition satisfied? (2) What time horizon are we considering? (3) What is the state of the economy (full employment or recession)? These three questions structure your entire answer.

            Problem 6 — IB-Style Essay (Challenging)

            "A depreciation of the currency is always beneficial for an economy." Evaluate this statement. [15 marks]

            In your answer, consider:

            • The conditions under which depreciation improves the current account.
            • The inflationary consequences.
            • The effects on foreign debt.
            • The J-curve dynamics.
            • Use the CLASPP framework and real-world examples.

              • Answers

              Answer 1 — Marshall-Lerner Calculation

              (a) |PEDx| + |PEDm| = 0.9 + 0.4 = 1.3 > 1. The Marshall-Lerner condition holds.

              (b) Since the condition holds, the depreciation should improve the current account. The 15% depreciation makes exports 15% cheaper for foreigners → export quantity rises by ~13.5% (0.9 × 15%), and makes imports 15% more expensive domestically → import quantity falls by ~6% (0.4 × 15%). The export value rises and import value does not rise proportionally (because quantity falls). Net exports improve.

              (c) In the long run: |PEDx| + |PEDm| = 1.2 + 0.8 = 2.0 > 1. The condition holds with an even larger margin, so the improvement in the current account would be larger and more robust. The long-run elasticities imply greater quantity responsiveness.

              Answer 2 — J-Curve Analysis

              (a) Plotting trade balance against time:

              • Pre: −20
              • Q1: −37 (worsens significantly — the bottom of the "J")
              • Q2: −31 (beginning to recover)
              • Q3: −14 (approaching pre-depreciation level)
              • Q4: +6 (improved beyond pre-depreciation level — moved into surplus)

                • The path traces a "J" shape: initial dip then steady improvement.

                (b) The trade balance worsens in Q1 because:

                • Import prices rise immediately (the 20% depreciation means each unit of imports costs 20% more in domestic currency).
                • Import quantities have not yet adjusted (contracts are fixed, habits and supply chains are slow to change).
                • Export quantities have not yet increased (foreign buyers need time to switch suppliers, place new orders).
                • Hence, the import bill rises sharply while export revenue barely changes → trade balance worsens.

                  • (c) For the trade balance to improve by Q4, the long-run elasticities must satisfy the Marshall-Lerner condition (|PEDx| + |PEDm| > 1). The data show that by Q4, export quantity has risen 25% (from 100 to 125) and import quantity has fallen slightly (from 120 to 119, but the price has risen, so the quantity drop must be substantial in percentage terms). The elasticities must be sufficiently large in absolute value.

                  Answer 3 — Depreciation and Inflation

                  (a) AD channel: Depreciation → exports cheaper, imports more expensive → net exports (X − M) rise → AD shifts right → demand-pull inflationary pressure. At full employment, this directly translates into higher prices, not higher output.

                  AS channel: Depreciation → imported raw materials, energy, and intermediate goods cost more → production costs rise → SRAS shifts left → cost-push inflation. At the same time, output falls.

                  Combined: AD right, SRAS left → prices rise significantly from both sides. Output may rise or fall depending on which shift dominates, but with the economy at full employment, the SRAS shift likely dominates → stagflation: higher prices, lower output.

                  (b) AD shifts right (AD₁ → AD₂); SRAS shifts left (SRAS₁ → SRAS₂). Price level rises from P₁ to P₂ (significant increase). Output may fall from Yf to Y₂.

                  (c) If the central bank does nothing, the economy experiences a period of stagflation. Eventually, if the economy is flexible, wages and prices adjust: the higher unemployment puts downward pressure on wages → SRAS gradually shifts back right. But this process is slow and painful. Most central banks would raise interest rates to combat inflation, which would further reduce output in the short run but bring inflation under control.

                  Answer 4 — Foreign Debt Burden

                  (a) Before depreciation: $50B × 20 pesos/$ = 1,000 billion pesos. After depreciation: $50B × 32 pesos/$ = 1,600 billion pesos. Increase: 600 billion pesos (+60% in domestic currency value).

                  (b) Debt-to-GDP before: 1,000 ÷ 300 = 333%. Debt-to-GDP after: 1,600 ÷ 300 = 533%. The ratio rises by 200 percentage points purely from the exchange rate change.

                  (c) Effects on Country D's fiscal position:

                  • The government must allocate more domestic revenue to debt service (interest and principal).
                  • This may crowd out spending on health, education, and infrastructure.
                  • Credit rating agencies will likely downgrade Country D's sovereign debt, seeing the increased debt burden as raising default risk.
                  • Higher credit risk → higher borrowing costs (higher interest rates on new debt) → further fiscal strain.
                  • This creates a vicious cycle: depreciation → higher debt burden → credit downgrade → capital outflows → further depreciation.

                    • Answer 5 — Appreciation Scenario

                    (a) Effects of 30% appreciation: (i) Manufacturing exports: Become 30% more expensive for foreign buyers → export volumes decline → manufacturing sector contracts, jobs lost. This is the "deindustrialisation" effect. (ii) Inflation: Imported goods become 30% cheaper → downward pressure on CPI. Also, export demand falls → AD may shift left → disinflationary pressure. (iii) Non-oil current account: Worsens — exports of non-oil goods fall (more expensive), imports rise (cheaper). The non-oil trade deficit widens.

                    (b) This phenomenon is known as "Dutch disease" — named after the Netherlands' experience after discovering North Sea gas in the 1960s. A commodity boom leads to:

                    • Resource sector expansion.
                    • Currency appreciation (due to large export revenues).
                    • Contraction of the non-resource tradable sector (manufacturing, agriculture).
                    • The economy becomes over-dependent on the commodity sector.

                      • (c) Policy options:

                      1. Sovereign wealth fund: Save the commodity revenue abroad (e.g., Norway's Government Pension Fund Global) to prevent the currency from appreciating and to save wealth for future generations.
                      2. Sterilised intervention: The central bank buys foreign currency to prevent appreciation, offsetting the monetary impact through open market operations.
                      3. Productivity-enhancing policies: Invest in education, infrastructure, and R&D to raise productivity in the non-commodity tradable sector, helping it compete despite a strong currency.
                      4. Capital controls: In extreme cases, limit capital inflows to reduce appreciation pressure (though this has costs).

                      Answer 6 — IB-Style Essay (Model Answer Outline)

                      Introduction: The claim that depreciation is "always" beneficial reflects a simplistic view. While depreciation can stimulate net exports and growth under certain conditions, its effects depend critically on the state of the economy, trade elasticities, the inflation environment, and the currency composition of external debt.

                      When depreciation IS beneficial:

                      • Economy is in recession with spare capacity → higher net exports boost AD without inflation.
                      • Marshall-Lerner condition holds → current account improves.
                      • Low initial inflation → limited pass-through.
                      • Low foreign-currency debt → no debt-servicing crisis.
                      • Example: UK after 1992 ERM exit — depreciation boosted growth and exports without igniting inflation.

                        • When depreciation is HARMFUL:

                        • Economy at full employment → inflation without real growth (stagflation risk).
                        • Marshall-Lerner condition fails (short run, J-curve) → current account initially worsens.
                        • High foreign-currency debt → debt crisis risk.
                        • High import dependency (food, energy, intermediate goods) → severe cost-push inflation.
                        • Example: Turkey (2018–2023) — lira depreciation caused inflation exceeding 80%, eroded real incomes, and worsened foreign debt burden.

                          • Evaluation (CLASPP):

                          • Conclusion: Depreciation is not "always" beneficial; it is a double-edged sword whose net effect is context-dependent.
                          • Limitations: The J-curve means even "successful" depreciations impose short-term costs. The timing of benefits versus costs matters politically.
                          • Assumptions: The "always beneficial" claim assumes elasticities are high, the economy has spare capacity, foreign debt is in domestic currency, and the central bank accommodates. These conditions are rarely all met simultaneously.
                          • Stakeholders: Exporters gain; import-competing firms gain; consumers lose (higher prices); foreign-currency debtors lose; tourists and foreign students gain; domestic savers lose purchasing power.
                          • Priorities: If the priority is short-run growth in a recession, depreciation helps. If the priority is inflation control or debt sustainability, depreciation harms.
                          • Policy alternatives: Supply-side policies to improve export competitiveness without depreciation. Structural reforms to reduce import dependency. Inflation targeting to anchor expectations.

                            • Final conclusion: The statement is false. Depreciation is a tool of adjustment, not a policy objective. Whether it is beneficial depends on the economic context. An economy with flexible markets, low foreign-currency debt, and spare capacity can benefit. An economy with rigid structures, high foreign debt, and full employment risks stagflation and debt crisis. Policymakers should not pursue depreciation as a goal but should focus on the underlying competitiveness and stability of the economy.

                            Evaluate

                            "A depreciation of the currency is always beneficial for an economy." Evaluate.

                            The claim that depreciation is "always" beneficial reflects a mercantilist oversimplification that equates a weaker currency with stronger exports and ignores the complex transmission channels through which exchange rate changes affect the macroeconomy. In reality, depreciation is a double-edged sword whose net welfare effect is entirely context-dependent.

                            Conclusion: Depreciation is not universally beneficial. It can stimulate growth under specific conditions but can also inflict severe damage through inflation, debt burden escalation, and real income erosion.

                            Limitations of the claim: The J-curve effect demonstrates that even when depreciation eventually improves the current account, the short-run impact is perverse — the trade balance worsens before it improves. This is not a theoretical curiosity; it is an empirically robust finding. UK trade data after the 2016 Brexit depreciation showed initial deterioration before improvement. The claim also ignores the exchange rate pass-through to inflation, which can be as high as 0.3–0.5 in developing economies (meaning a 10% depreciation adds 3–5 percentage points to inflation).

                            Assumptions challenged: The "always beneficial" view assumes the Marshall-Lerner condition is immediately satisfied and that the economy operates with spare capacity. For developing countries reliant on imported food and fuel, demand for imports is price-inelastic in the short run, violating the condition. Furthermore, if the economy is at full employment, the AD stimulus from net exports translates into inflation rather than real growth.

                            Stakeholder analysis: Depreciation redistributes income. Exporters and import-competing producers gain; consumers (especially the poor, who spend larger income shares on tradeable goods) lose purchasing power. Holders of foreign-currency debt — including governments — see debt burdens explode. In Turkey (2018–2023), lira depreciation pushed inflation above 80%, decimated real wages, and forced the central bank into contradictory policies. These distributional effects are real welfare costs.

                            Priorities and trade-offs: A country must weigh the employment and output gains in the export sector against the inflation and debt-service costs. For a country with high foreign-currency debt (as many developing countries have), the fiscal damage from depreciation may far outweigh any trade benefit. For a country with low foreign debt and spare capacity (e.g., the UK in 1992), depreciation may be a net positive.

                            Policy alternatives: Rather than relying on depreciation — a blunt and unpredictable instrument — countries should pursue structural reforms that improve productivity and non-price competitiveness. Supply-side policies (education, infrastructure, R&D) enhance the quality of exports, reducing the need for price-based competition. A stable, predictable exchange rate achieved through sound macroeconomic management is generally preferable to the volatility and uncertainty of repeated depreciations. (198 words)
