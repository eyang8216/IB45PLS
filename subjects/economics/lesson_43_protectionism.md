# Lesson 43: Protectionism — Arguments For and Against

## What You'll Learn
- **Arguments FOR protectionism**: infant industry, anti-dumping, employment, national security, government revenue, strategic trade policy, and current account correction.
- **Arguments AGAINST protectionism**: higher consumer prices, less choice, domestic inefficiency, retaliation, corruption, and WTO constraints.
- **How to evaluate** each argument critically, distinguishing between theoretical logic and real-world evidence.

---

## 1. What Is Protectionism?

**Protectionism** refers to government policies that restrict international trade to shield domestic industries from foreign competition. Common instruments include:

- **Tariffs** — taxes on imported goods (ad valorem or specific).
- **Quotas** — quantitative limits on imports.
- **Subsidies** — payments to domestic producers to lower their costs.
- **Administrative barriers** — regulations, standards, and red tape that impede imports.

While free trade is the textbook prescription for maximising global welfare, nearly every country practises some form of protectionism. The debate is not whether protectionism exists but whether any particular instance of it is justified.

---

## 2. Arguments FOR Protectionism

### 2.1 Infant Industry Protection

The **infant industry argument** holds that new domestic industries may initially be unable to compete with established foreign firms that already enjoy economies of scale and lower average costs. Temporary protection (tariffs or subsidies) gives infant industries time to grow, achieve scale, and become internationally competitive.

**Worked Example**: Suppose a new solar panel industry in Country X has an average cost of $180 per panel, while Chinese imports sell at $140. A 30% tariff raises the import price to $182, making the domestic product viable. Over five years, the domestic firm's average cost falls to $130 through learning-by-doing. The tariff can then be removed.

**Critical evaluation**: 
- How do we identify which industries are truly "infants" with potential versus permanently uncompetitive ones?
- Protection, once granted, is politically difficult to remove — "temporary" often becomes permanent.
- South Korea's steel and automobile industries (POSCO, Hyundai) are often cited as infant-industry success stories.

### 2.2 Anti-Dumping Protection

**Dumping** occurs when foreign firms sell goods in a domestic market at prices below their cost of production or below prices charged in their home market. The intent may be predatory — to drive domestic firms out of business, then raise prices.

**Anti-dumping duties** are tariffs imposed to offset the dumping margin (difference between export price and normal value).

**Worked Example**: A steel producer in Country B sells steel domestically at $600/tonne but exports to Country C at $420/tonne. The dumping margin is $180/tonne. Country C imposes a countervailing duty of $180/tonne, bringing the import price back to $600.

**Critical evaluation**:
- Genuine predatory dumping is rare and difficult to prove.
- Anti-dumping laws are often abused as disguised protectionism — domestic firms lobby for anti-dumping duties against efficient foreign competitors.
- Price differences may simply reflect different market conditions, not predation.

### 2.3 Protecting Domestic Employment

Free trade causes structural unemployment in import-competing industries. Protection can preserve jobs in these sectors.

**Example**: The US steel industry lost approximately 400,000 jobs between 1950 and 2010 as imports rose. Tariffs on steel (e.g., Trump administration 2018, 25% tariffs) were justified in part as protecting American steelworker jobs.

**Critical evaluation**:
- Protection saves visible jobs in one industry but destroys hidden jobs elsewhere:
  - Higher input costs for steel-using industries (automobiles, construction) → reduced output and employment there.
  - Retaliation by trading partners → job losses in export industries.
  - Higher consumer prices → reduced real income and spending on other goods.
- A 2002 US steel tariff study found that while it saved ~5,000 steel jobs, it cost ~26,000 jobs in steel-using industries.

### 2.4 National Security

Certain industries are deemed essential for national defence or strategic autonomy. Examples include:
- Defence equipment manufacturing (weapons, aircraft, ships).
- Food security (agriculture, fertilisers).
- Energy independence (oil refining).
- Critical technology (semiconductors, AI, rare earth minerals).

**Example**: The US CHIPS and Science Act (2022) subsidises domestic semiconductor manufacturing because reliance on foreign chips (particularly from Taiwan) is seen as a national security vulnerability.

**Critical evaluation**:
- "National security" is a broad and slippery concept — every industry claims strategic importance.
- Countries can maintain strategic stockpiles rather than inefficient domestic production.
- Overuse of the argument undermines its legitimacy.

### 2.5 Government Revenue

For many developing countries, tariffs are a significant source of government revenue because:
- Customs are easier to administer than income or sales taxes.
- Large informal sectors make income taxation difficult.
- Low administrative capacity exists for complex tax systems.

**Worked Example**: In some Sub-Saharan African countries, trade taxes account for 20–30% of total government revenue. A 10% tariff on all imports in a country importing $5 billion worth of goods generates $500 million annually.

**Critical evaluation**:
- Reliance on trade taxes distorts the economy and creates a fiscal incentive for protectionism.
- As part of trade liberalisation, countries should develop alternative revenue sources (e.g., VAT).

### 2.6 Strategic Trade Policy

The **strategic trade policy** argument (associated with Paul Krugman) says that in industries with:
- **High fixed costs and economies of scale** (e.g., aerospace, semiconductors),
- **Large R&D spillovers** (innovations benefit the whole economy),
- **Oligopolistic market structure** (few firms globally, above-normal profits),

…government support can help domestic firms capture a larger share of global oligopoly profits, which more than compensates for the cost of protection/subsidies.

**Example**: The Boeing-Airbus rivalry. Both firms received substantial government support. The argument: whoever wins dominance in a duopoly captures enormous profits that justify the initial subsidy.

**Critical evaluation**:
- Requires the government to "pick winners" — a task governments historically do poorly.
- Retaliation: if both sides subsidise, the net effect is wasteful subsidy races.
- WTO rules increasingly restrict such policies.

### 2.7 Correcting Current Account Deficits

Restricting imports (via tariffs/quotas) reduces the import bill, potentially improving the current account balance.

**Example**: If Country D has a current account deficit of $20 billion and imposes tariffs that reduce imports by $8 billion, the deficit narrows (ceteris paribus).

**Critical evaluation**:
- Trading partners may retaliate, reducing exports and nullifying the improvement.
- Import restrictions may raise costs for domestic exporters who rely on imported inputs.
- The underlying cause (low savings, lack of competitiveness) is not addressed.
- Exchange rate adjustment is typically a more efficient mechanism.

## 3. Arguments AGAINST Protectionism

### 3.1 Higher Prices for Domestic Consumers

Protection raises the price of both imported goods and import-competing domestic goods. This reduces consumers' real income and consumer surplus.

**Worked Example — Tariff Welfare Analysis**:
| Scenario | Domestic Price | Consumer Surplus | Producer Surplus | Govt Revenue | Deadweight Loss |
|----------|---------------|-----------------|-----------------|-------------|-----------------|
| Free Trade | $100 | $2,500 | $400 | $0 | $0 |
| $20 Tariff | $120 | $1,800 | $900 | $300 | $700 |

The net welfare loss (deadweight loss) of $700 is the efficiency cost of protection — consumers lose more than producers and government gain.

### 3.2 Less Choice and Variety

Protection reduces the variety of goods available to consumers. Import quotas may eliminate certain foreign brands entirely, reducing consumer welfare beyond just higher prices.

### 3.3 Domestic Inefficiency

Without the discipline of foreign competition, protected domestic firms have little incentive to innovate, control costs, or improve quality. This is the **X-inefficiency** argument: protected firms operate above their minimum cost curve.

**Historical example**: India's pre-1991 protectionist "licence raj" led to notoriously inefficient domestic manufacturing. After liberalisation, Indian firms exposed to competition dramatically improved productivity.

### 3.4 Risk of Retaliation (Trade Wars)

One country's protection invites retaliation. The **Smoot-Hawley Tariff Act (1930)** in the United States raised tariffs on over 20,000 imported goods. Trading partners retaliated, and global trade collapsed by ~65% between 1929 and 1934, deepening the Great Depression.

**Modern example**: US-China trade war (2018–). US tariffs on Chinese goods → Chinese retaliatory tariffs on US agricultural exports → American farmers lost market share (partially offset by government bailouts).

### 3.5 Corruption and Rent-Seeking

Protectionist measures create economic **rents** — above-normal profits arising from government-created scarcity. This incentivises:

- **Rent-seeking**: firms spend resources lobbying for protection rather than improving products.
- **Corruption**: officials may sell import licences or quota allocations.
- **Smuggling**: quotas create black markets.

Under a quota system, the quota rent (difference between domestic and world price × quota quantity) accrues to whoever holds the import licence — a recipe for corruption.

### 3.6 WTO Rules Limit Protectionist Options

The World Trade Organization (WTO) framework:
- Requires members to bind tariffs at negotiated ceilings.
- Prohibits quotas (with limited exceptions).
- Requires non-discrimination (Most-Favoured-Nation principle).
- Has a binding dispute settlement mechanism.

Countries that violate WTO commitments face authorised retaliation. This raises the cost of protectionism.

## Answers

### Answer 1 — Welfare Analysis
(a) **Before tariff**: Imports = Consumption − Production = 80,000 − 20,000 = **60,000 tonnes**.
**After tariff**: Imports = 65,000 − 35,000 = **30,000 tonnes**.
Imports fall by 30,000 tonnes (50% reduction).

(b) **Tariff revenue** = Tariff × Post-tariff imports = $100 × 30,000 = **$3,000,000**.

(c) **Deadweight loss** consists of two triangles:
- **Production distortion**: (½) × (35,000 − 20,000) × $100 = ½ × 15,000 × $100 = **$750,000**.
- **Consumption distortion**: (½) × (80,000 − 65,000) × $100 = ½ × 15,000 × $100 = **$750,000**.
- **Total DWL** = $750,000 + $750,000 = **$1,500,000**.

(d) **Gainers**: Domestic steel producers (higher price, more output), government (tariff revenue). **Losers**: Domestic consumers (higher price, less consumption), foreign exporters (sell less).

### Answer 2 — Infant Industry
(a) The price difference = $150 − $110 = $40. As a percentage of import price: $40 ÷ $110 × 100 = **36.4%**.
A 36.4% ad valorem tariff raises the import price to $150.04, making domestic batteries competitive.

(b) Annual extra cost to consumers = tariff × import price × demand = 36.4% × $110 × 10 million = $400 million/year.
Over 3 years: $400M × 3 = **$1.2 billion** total protection cost.

(c) Conditions for validity:
- The learning curve must be credible (verifiable cost-reduction trajectory).
- Protection must be time-limited with a clear phase-out schedule.
- The industry must have genuine comparative advantage potential, not just temporary cost gaps.
- The welfare gain after maturation (consumer surplus from cheaper domestic batteries) must exceed the protection cost.

### Answer 3 — Anti-Dumping
(a) Dumping margin = Normal value (home market price) − Export price = $120 − $85 = **$35 per unit**.

(b) Post-duty price = $85 + $35 = **$120 per unit** — equal to the home market price.

(c) Two reasons why this may not be genuine predatory dumping:
1. **Price discrimination**: The Chinese firm may simply charge different prices in different markets based on demand elasticities (more elastic demand in Country T). This is normal business practice — airlines charge different prices for the same seat.
2. **Cost differences**: The panels sold in China may include additional distribution, retail, and after-sales service costs not incurred in bulk export transactions. The export price may genuinely reflect lower costs of serving foreign markets.

### Answer 4 — Employment Trade-off
The 15% price increase in clothing reduces quantity demanded. With labour demand elasticity of −0.5:
- A 15% price increase → output reduction of approximately 7.5% (for unit elasticity of product demand; the precise calculation depends on product demand elasticity, but as an approximation using labour demand elasticity directly): %Δ employment = −0.5 × 15% = **−7.5%**.

Job loss in clothing = 7.5% × 200,000 = **15,000 jobs**.

**Net effect**: +10,000 (textiles) − 15,000 (clothing) = **−5,000 jobs**. The tariff is **net job-destroying**. This illustrates the "seen versus unseen" problem — the textile jobs saved are visible; the clothing jobs lost are diffuse and harder to attribute.

### Answer 5 — Strategic Trade Policy
(a) Airbus profit gain: Without subsidy: 50% × $20B = $10B. With subsidy: 70% × $20B = $14B.
Increase = $14B − $10B = **$4 billion**.

(b) EU net benefit = Profit gain − Subsidy = $4B − $4B = **$0**. The EU breaks even; Airbus gains $4B in profit but it cost taxpayers $4B. There is a transfer from taxpayers to Airbus shareholders, but no net national gain (in this simplified calculation ignoring spillovers).

(c) If the US retaliates, market shares return to 50-50. Both sides have spent $4B each with no gain in market share. Net effect: **−$4B for each side**. This illustrates the **prisoner's dilemma** of strategic trade policy — individually rational to subsidise, but collectively self-defeating.

### Answer 6 — IB-Style Essay (Model Answer Outline)
**Introduction**: Protectionism — trade barriers to shield domestic industries — is controversial. While free trade maximises static welfare, real-world complexities mean some protectionist arguments have merit. Whether protectionism is "never justified" depends on context, timing, and implementation.

**Arguments for (that carry some weight)**:
- **Infant industries**: South Korea's chaebols behind tariff walls in the 1960s–1980s became world-class competitors. But "temporary" frequently becomes permanent; picking winners is hard.
- **National security**: Semiconductors (TSMC, US CHIPS Act) and food security justify some protection, but the concept is easily abused.
- **Anti-dumping**: Theoretically sound but practically overused as disguised protectionism.
- **Government revenue**: For least-developed countries, tariffs may be the only administratively feasible tax. But they should be accompanied by tax system development.

**Arguments against**:
- **Consumer harm**: Higher prices disproportionately affect the poor.
- **Retaliation and trade wars**: Smoot-Hawley (1930) and US-China (2018) demonstrate the destructive potential.
- **Domestic inefficiency**: Protection breeds complacency (X-inefficiency). India's pre-1991 licence raj is Exhibit A.
- **WTO constraints**: Rules-based system limits options.

**Evaluation (CLASPP)**:
- **Conclusion**: Not "never," but protectionism is rarely the first-best policy.
- **Limitations**: Infant industry argument assumes governments can identify viable firms — information problem.
- **Assumptions**: Anti-dumping theory assumes predation; evidence suggests most cases are not predatory.
- **Stakeholders**: Consumers lose; producers gain; government gains revenue; trading partners lose.
- **Priorities**: Short-term employment protection vs. long-term productivity growth — second-best reasoning applies.
- **Policy alternatives**: Subsidies (more targeted, WTO-compatible), adjustment assistance (retraining), exchange rate policy — all preferable to trade barriers.

**Final conclusion**: Protectionism can occasionally be justified on narrow grounds (genuine infant industries, clear national security risks) but the conditions for valid protection are stringent and rarely met. The presumption should always be for free trade.

## Evaluate

### "Protectionism can never be justified in a modern global economy." Evaluate.

The claim that protectionism can *never* be justified is too absolute and ignores legitimate market failures and strategic considerations that temper the free trade prescription. While the general case for free trade — based on comparative advantage and consumer welfare maximisation — is powerful, there are identifiable circumstances where targeted, time-limited protection may serve the national interest.

**Conclusion**: Protectionism is *rarely* the optimal policy, but it is not *never* justifiable. The burden of proof lies heavily on those proposing protection.

**Limitations of the anti-protectionist argument**: The standard trade model assumes perfectly competitive markets, full employment, and no dynamic learning effects. In reality, oligopolistic industries (strategic trade policy) and learning-by-doing (infant industries) create situations where temporary protection could, in theory, raise welfare — though practical implementation is fraught.

**Assumptions challenged**: The claim assumes all dumping is predatory and all infant industries eventually mature. Evidence shows most anti-dumping actions protect inefficient incumbents, and most infant industries never grow up. However, counterexamples like South Korea's steel industry show that under disciplined governance, protection *can* work.

**Stakeholder analysis**: Protection creates concentrated benefits (producers, their workers) and diffuse costs (consumers, downstream industries). The political economy systematically favours protection because beneficiaries are organised and vocal while losers are dispersed. This explains why protection persists even when economists nearly unanimously oppose it.

**Priorities and trade-offs**: In developing countries, tariff revenue may be fiscally indispensable (20–30% of government revenue). The priority of fiscal stability may temporarily outweigh the efficiency cost of trade distortion, provided a transition plan exists. Similarly, genuine national security concerns (semiconductors, rare earths) may override pure efficiency considerations.

**Policy alternatives**: Even where a genuine problem exists (e.g., declining industry), first-best policies exist: adjustment assistance and retraining for displaced workers, R&D subsidies rather than import barriers, multilateral negotiations rather than unilateral tariffs. Protectionism is almost always a second-best (or third-best) policy response. Thus, while protectionism cannot be said to be "never" justified, it should be the policy of last resort — and when used, it must be temporary, transparent, and tied to measurable performance criteria. (198 words), 4. Contractionary Fiscal Policy: Raising taxes or reducing government spending to lower aggregate demand and thus imports. This is politically painful — it reduces domestic incomes and increases unemployment. For a country already in recession with a current account deficit, this is a dangerous prescription (procyclical).

3. Contractionary Monetary Policy: Higher interest rates attract capital inflows (financial account) and reduce domestic spending and imports. But higher rates also discourage investment and may appreciate the currency, hurting export competitiveness.

3. Supply-Side Policies: Improve domestic productivity and competitiveness — education, infrastructure, labour market reforms, R&D incentives. These address the root cause of a deficit (lack of competitiveness) but work slowly. This is the only sustainable long-term solution.

4. Expenditure-Switching Policies: Policies that redirect domestic and foreign spending toward domestic goods:
   - **Tariffs and quotas**: Reduce imports directly but violate WTO rules and invite retaliation.
   - **Depreciation/Devaluation**: Makes exports cheaper and imports more expensive, switching expenditure toward domestic goods. Requires a floating (or adjustable peg) exchange rate.

### Answer 2 — Interpreting a Current Account Deficit
(a) Delta's deficit may **not** be a problem because:
- It is financing productive investment (FDI in manufacturing and technology), which increases future productive capacity and export capability.
- GDP growth is strong (4.5%), suggesting the investment is generating returns.
- FDI is stable, long-term financing — less prone to sudden capital flight than portfolio flows.

(b) It could become a problem if:

- The domestic economy becomes overdependent on foreign capital.

(c) Country **Epsilon's deficit is more concerning** because:
- Short-term portfolio flows are "hot money" — they can reverse quickly in a crisis.
- Financing consumption rather than investment means no future productive capacity is being built to service the debt.

### Answer 3 — Policy Analysis
(a) Three problems with tariff-based correction:
1. **WTO violation**: Unilateral tariff increases breach WTO bound commitments, inviting dispute settlement and authorised retaliation.
2. **Retaliation**: Trading partners may impose counter-tariffs on Zeta's exports, reducing exports and potentially worsening the current account.
3. **Domestic costs**: Tariffs raise input costs for domestic manufacturers that use imported components, reducing their competitiveness. They also raise consumer prices, which is regressive.
4. **Doesn't address root cause**: The deficit may reflect low domestic savings or weak competitiveness. Tariffs are a symptom treatment.

(b) Two alternative policies:
1. **Allow currency depreciation**: With a floating rate, a persistent deficit creates excess supply of the domestic currency, which should depreciate. This automatically makes exports cheaper and imports more expensive, switching expenditure toward domestic goods.
2. **Supply-side policies**: Invest in education, infrastructure, and R&D to improve productivity and the quality of exports. This is the only sustainable long-term solution.

(c) A depreciation makes exports cheaper in foreign currency (quantity demanded rises) and imports more expensive in domestic currency (quantity demanded falls). This improves the trade balance **if the Marshall-Lerner condition holds**: the sum of the absolute values of the price elasticities of demand for exports and imports must be greater than 1 (|PEDx| + |PEDm| > 1).

### Answer 4 — The "Twin Deficits"
(a) **National income identity**: Y = C + I + G + (X − M).
The current account balance (X − M) is part of aggregate demand. If government spending (G) increases without a tax increase, ceteris paribus, Y rises and imports (M) rise → (X − M) falls.

(b) **Saving-investment identity**: rearranging Y = C + I + G + (X − M) and defining national saving S = Y − C − T (private saving) and T − G (public saving):
Y − C − T = S_private
(X − M) = (S_private − I) + (T − G)
So: Current account = (Private saving − Investment) + (Government budget balance).

**Introduction**: A current account deficit — where a country's outflows from trade, income, and transfers exceed inflows — is not inherently a sign of weakness. Whether a deficit is worrying depends on its size, persistence, financing, and what the borrowed funds are used for.

**When a deficit IS a sign of weakness**:
- **Structural, persistent deficits**: Reflect fundamental lack of competitiveness — chronic deficits suggest the country cannot produce goods the world wants at competitive prices.
- **Financing consumption**: If capital inflows fund a consumption boom, no future productive capacity is built. Greece (pre-2009) ran large deficits financing consumption and an unsustainable government spending binge; the result was a sovereign debt crisis.
- **Short-term financing**: Reliance on "hot money" (portfolio flows) creates vulnerability. Mexico (1994) and the Asian Financial Crisis (1997) saw sudden capital flow reversals devastate economies with large current account deficits.
- **Unsustainable trajectory**: If foreign debt grows faster than GDP, interest payments (primary income debits) increasingly worsen the deficit — a vicious cycle.

**When a deficit is NOT a sign of weakness**:
- **Financing productive investment**: If FDI finances factories, technology, and infrastructure that boost future export capacity, the deficit is like a company borrowing to invest — sound business. The US in the late 1990s ran deficits while experiencing an investment boom and strong growth.
- **Catching-up growth**: Developing countries may run deficits as they import capital goods to industrialise. South Korea in the 1970s–1980s consistently ran current account deficits while transforming from a poor agrarian economy to an industrial powerhouse.
- **Temporary/cyclical**: A recession in trading partners temporarily depresses exports, or a commodity price cycle temporarily raises imports. These reverse.
- **"Exorbitant privilege"**: The US dollar is the world's primary reserve currency — global demand for safe dollar assets creates persistent demand for US financial assets, enabling sustained deficits that other countries could not sustain.

**Evaluation (CLASPP)**:
- **Conclusion**: A current account deficit is a symptom, not a disease. The underlying cause and financing determine whether it signals weakness or strength.
- **Limitations**: The BOP accounting identity means deficits always have a counterpart in capital inflows, but the composition of those inflows matters enormously.
- **Assumptions**: The view that deficits always signal weakness assumes imports are "bad" — but imports of capital goods raise productivity. It also ignores intertemporal trade (borrowing today to invest for tomorrow).
- **Stakeholders**: Export industries may suffer, but consumers benefit from cheaper imports. Foreign investors gain assets; domestic firms gain capital.
- **Priorities**: Sustainability (can it continue without crisis?) matters more than the existence of a deficit. A small, stable deficit may be entirely benign; a large, growing one is dangerous.
- **Policy alternatives**: Rather than targeting the deficit per se, policymakers should address the underlying cause: competitiveness (supply-side), inadequate saving (fiscal consolidation), or overvalued currency (exchange rate adjustment).

**Final conclusion**: The view that a current account deficit is "always" a sign of economic weakness is demonstrably false. The US, Australia, and (historically) South Korea are counterexamples. The key distinction is whether the deficit finances investment that generates future returns or consumption that leaves a debt overhang with no offsetting assets. Policymakers should focus on the sustainability and composition of deficits, not their mere existence.

## Evaluate

### "A current account deficit is always a sign of economic weakness." Evaluate.

The claim that a current account deficit is *always* a sign of economic weakness reflects a mercantilist fallacy that equates trade deficits with national failure. While deficits can indeed signal deep structural problems, the blanket assertion ignores the crucial distinction between deficits that finance consumption and those that finance productive investment.

**Conclusion**: A current account deficit is a symptom of underlying economic forces, not inherently a marker of weakness. Its significance depends entirely on cause, composition, and context.

**Limitations**: The mercantilist view treats the current account in isolation, ignoring the balance of payments identity. A deficit is necessarily matched by a financial account surplus — capital inflows. These inflows are not inherently harmful; if they finance investment in productive capacity, they represent the international allocation of saving to its most productive uses, exactly as economic theory prescribes.

**Assumptions challenged**: The claim assumes all imports represent "lost" domestic production. In reality, imports of capital goods and intermediate inputs raise domestic productivity. Furthermore, the claim assumes deficits are permanent; many are cyclical, self-correcting through exchange rate adjustment. The Marshall-Lerner condition and J-curve dynamics demonstrate that deficits can be transitory.

**Stakeholder analysis**: Different stakeholders experience deficits differently. Consumers benefit from cheaper and more varied imports. Export-competing industries may suffer. Foreign investors gain by acquiring domestic assets. The net welfare effect depends on whether the capital inflows are productively deployed — benefiting future consumers through higher growth — or wasted on consumption that must eventually be repaid.

**Priorities and trade-offs**: Sustainability should be the primary concern, not the deficit's mere existence. A 2% deficit that is stable and finances productive FDI is benign. A 7% deficit financed by short-term debt to fund a consumption boom is a crisis in waiting. The policy priority should be to ensure the composition of capital inflows supports productive capacity, not to eliminate the deficit.

**Policy alternatives**: Rather than targeting the deficit directly through protectionism (which reduces both imports and exports and harms consumers), policymakers should address root causes through supply-side reforms (improving productivity and competitiveness), maintaining a competitive exchange rate, and ensuring fiscal discipline to avoid twin deficits. A well-managed economy can sustain modest current account deficits indefinitely, as the United States and Australia have demonstrated for decades. (199 words)
