Lesson 26: Short-Run Aggregate Supply and Equilibrium

What You'll Learn

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to:

- Define short-run aggregate supply and explain what the "short run" means in macroeconomics

- Explain why the SRAS curve slopes upward using three distinct mechanisms

- Identify the determinants that shift the SRAS curve and predict the direction of the shift

- Determine short-run macroeconomic equilibrium as the intersection of AD and SRAS

- Analyze how changes in AD and SRAS affect the equilibrium price level and real GDP

- Distinguish between supply shocks and demand shocks in short-run analysis

1. What Short-Run Aggregate Supply Is and Why It Matters

The Supply Side of the Whole Economy

When economists talk about supply in a single market — the market for coffee, say — they mean the quantity of coffee that producers are willing to sell at each possible price of coffee. Macroeconomic supply is the same idea scaled up to the entire economy. Short-run aggregate supply (SRAS) is the total quantity of goods and services that all firms in an economy are willing and able to produce at different average price levels over a short-run time horizon, holding everything else constant.

Why does the SRAS curve matter? Because the intersection of aggregate demand and short-run aggregate supply determines the economy's actual level of output and the average price level at any moment. If AD shifts but SRAS does not, we get one set of outcomes — perhaps higher output with rising prices. If SRAS shifts but AD does not, we get a different and often more painful outcome — falling output combined with rising prices, a situation called stagflation. Understanding what moves the SRAS curve is essential for understanding why economies sometimes suffer from both recession and inflation at the same time.

What "Short Run" Means in Macroeconomics

In macroeconomics, the short run is defined as the period during which at least one key input price — most importantly, the nominal wage rate — is fixed or slow to adjust. Wages are set by contracts, norms, and regulations that do not change instantly when economic conditions change. Because wages are "sticky" in the short run, firms' production costs do not immediately reflect changes in the price level. This stickiness is the foundation of short-run aggregate supply analysis.

The short run is not a specific calendar duration. It could be months or a few years, depending on how quickly wages and other input prices adjust in a particular economy. In an economy with flexible labour markets and short wage contracts, the short run might last only a few months. In an economy with rigid labour markets, strong unions, and multi-year wage agreements, the short run could last several years.

2. Why the SRAS Curve Slopes Upward

The Shape of SRAS

The SRAS curve is drawn with the average price level on the vertical axis and real GDP on the horizontal axis. Unlike the long-run aggregate supply curve, which is vertical, the SRAS curve slopes upward — as the average price level rises, the quantity of real GDP that firms are willing to produce increases.

There are three main explanations for this upward slope. They are not competing theories; they are complementary mechanisms that operate simultaneously in real economies.

Sticky Wages (Nominal Wage Rigidity)

The most widely accepted explanation for the upward-sloping SRAS is that nominal wages — the actual dollar or euro amounts printed on workers' paychecks — are slow to change. Wages are sticky for several reasons. Labour contracts often fix wages for one to three years. Minimum wage laws set a legal floor. Many firms pay efficiency wages — wages above the market-clearing level — to motivate workers, reduce turnover, and attract higher-quality applicants. Workers themselves resist nominal wage cuts fiercely; a firm that tries to cut everyone's pay by 5 percent may face walkouts, lawsuits, and a collapse in morale, even if the price level has also fallen by 5 percent.

Here is the mechanism. Suppose the average price level rises by 10 percent but nominal wages are fixed by contracts that do not expire for another year. Because nominal wages (W) are unchanged while the price level (P) has risen, the real wage — the wage measured in terms of what it can actually buy — has fallen. Real wage equals nominal wage divided by the price level: W/P. If W stays at $20 per hour and P rises from 100 to 110, the real wage falls from $20/1.00 = $20.00 to $20/1.10 ≈ $18.18 in original purchasing power.

For firms, this is good news: labour — which typically accounts for 60 to 70 percent of production costs — has become cheaper in real terms. With lower real labour costs, producing output is more profitable at the margin. Firms respond by hiring more workers and expanding production. The result: real GDP supplied rises when the price level rises.

The mechanism works in reverse when the price level falls. If nominal wages are fixed and P falls, real wages rise, labour becomes more expensive in real terms, firms cut production, and real GDP supplied falls.

A common mistake students make is to think that sticky wages mean wages never change. That is not correct. Wages do change, but they change slowly and with a delay. The stickiness is what gives us an upward-sloping SRAS curve in the short run. In the long run, wages adjust fully, and the SRAS curve becomes vertical — but that is the subject of the next lesson.

Worked Example 1: Real Wage Change from Sticky Nominal Wages

A firm pays its workers a nominal wage of $25 per hour under a two-year contract. In the first year of the contract, the average price level rises from 100 to 112. Calculate the percentage change in the real wage.

Approach: The real wage is the nominal wage divided by the price level. We need to compute the real wage before and after the price level change, then calculate the percentage difference. Step 1: Real wage before the price increase: $25 divided by (100/100) = $25.00 per hour in base-year dollars. Step 2: Real wage after the price increase: $25 divided by (112/100) = $25 divided by 1.12 = $22.32 per hour in base-year dollars. Step 3: Percentage change: (New − Old) / Old × 100 = ($22.32 − $25.00) / $25.00 × 100 = (−$2.68 / $25.00) × 100 = −10.7 percent. Why this makes sense: The price level rose by 12 percent but the nominal wage stayed at $25. The real wage fell because each dollar of wage now buys fewer goods. The 10.7 percent fall in the real wage is roughly equal to the inflation rate, as expected when nominal wages are completely fixed.

Menu Costs

The second explanation for the upward-sloping SRAS is menu costs — the costs firms incur when they change their prices. The term comes from the literal cost of printing new menus when a restaurant changes its prices, but the concept applies much more broadly. Firms must update websites, reprint catalogues, relabel products on shelves, reprogram computer systems, and inform customers — all of which takes time and money.

Because changing prices is costly, firms do not adjust their prices continuously. When the general price level rises, some firms delay raising their own prices. During the delay, their goods are priced below the new market average — they are temporarily cheaper than competitors' products. Customers respond by buying more from these firms. To meet the increased demand, the firms increase production.

The aggregate effect: when the price level rises, the firms that have not yet adjusted their prices produce more, so total real GDP supplied increases. The SRAS curve slopes upward partly because menu costs create temporary price stickiness at the firm level.

A common misconception is that menu costs must be large to explain anything. In fact, research by Gregory Mankiw and others has shown that even small menu costs can generate large macroeconomic effects. If every firm delays repricing by only a few months, the cumulative effect across thousands of firms is a significant upward slope in SRAS.

Misperceptions (Imperfect Information)

The third explanation comes from the work of Robert Lucas and other New Classical economists. Firms may have difficulty distinguishing between a rise in the general price level (which affects all goods equally) and a rise in the relative price of their own product (which signals genuinely higher demand for what they specifically produce).

Suppose Firm X sees that the price at which it can sell its product has risen. Is this because all prices have risen due to inflation, or because demand for Firm X's product has genuinely increased? In the short run, the firm cannot be sure. If it mistakenly interprets a general price rise as a rise in demand for its own product, it will increase production. When many firms make this same misperception, aggregate output rises when the general price level rises.

This mechanism works only in the short run. Over time, firms gather more information and realize that the price rise was general, not specific to their product. Production then returns to its original level. The misperceptions theory thus explains why SRAS slopes upward while LRAS is vertical — a theme we explore in Lesson 27.

3. Determinants That Shift the SRAS Curve

Movement Along vs. Shift

A movement along the SRAS curve occurs when the price level changes and firms respond by adjusting production, as described by the three mechanisms above. The curve itself does not move.

A shift of the SRAS curve occurs when something other than the price level changes the quantity of real GDP that firms are willing to supply at every price level. If firms can produce more at any given price level, SRAS shifts right. If they can produce less, SRAS shifts left.

Changes in Input Costs

The most important category of SRAS shifters is changes in the costs of the inputs firms use — labour, raw materials, energy, and imported components.

Nominal wages are the largest input cost for most firms. If nominal wages rise — because of stronger unions, a higher minimum wage, or tight labour markets — firms' production costs rise. At any given price level, producing output is less profitable. Firms reduce the quantity they are willing to supply, and SRAS shifts left. If nominal wages fall, SRAS shifts right. Raw material prices affect every industry that uses them. A sharp rise in the price of oil, for example, increases costs for transport, plastics, chemicals, agriculture (fertilizers and fuel), and heating. At every price level, firms' costs are higher, so SRAS shifts left. The oil price shocks of 1973–1974 and 1979–1980 are classic examples: oil prices quadrupled and then doubled, SRAS shifted dramatically left, and the world experienced stagflation — falling output combined with rising prices.

Conversely, a fall in commodity prices shifts SRAS right. The collapse of oil prices in 2014–2015, from over $100 per barrel to under $30, lowered costs across many industries and shifted SRAS right in oil-importing economies.

Energy costs more broadly — electricity, natural gas, and fuel — operate the same way. An energy crisis shifts SRAS left; cheap, abundant energy shifts SRAS right. Import prices for intermediate goods matter especially for economies that rely on imported components. If the domestic currency depreciates, imported inputs become more expensive in domestic-currency terms, raising firms' costs and shifting SRAS left.

Supply Shocks

A supply shock is an unexpected event that suddenly and significantly changes the economy's productive capacity or production costs.

A negative supply shock shifts SRAS left. Examples include a natural disaster that destroys factories and disrupts supply chains, a pandemic that forces businesses to close, a war that cuts off access to key resources, or a crop failure that sends food prices soaring.

A positive supply shock shifts SRAS right. Examples include an unusually good harvest that makes food cheaper, a technological breakthrough that sharply lowers production costs, the discovery of a major new resource deposit, or a peace agreement that opens up previously inaccessible trade routes.

Government Policies Affecting Production Costs

Business taxes and subsidies change the effective cost of production. An increase in corporate taxes or payroll taxes raises costs and shifts SRAS left. A cut in these taxes shifts SRAS right. Similarly, an increase in government subsidies to firms lowers their effective costs and shifts SRAS right. Regulations impose compliance costs. Stricter environmental regulations, safety requirements, or labour protections increase the cost of doing business and shift SRAS left. Deregulation — the removal or simplification of rules — reduces compliance costs and shifts SRAS right. The key distinction is that regulation affects the cost of producing output, not the economy's ultimate productive capacity. Regulation can have benefits — cleaner air, safer workplaces — that are not captured in the SRAS diagram, so an SRAS left shift from regulation is not necessarily "bad" in a broader welfare sense.

4. Short-Run Macroeconomic Equilibrium

Where AD Meets SRAS

Short-run macroeconomic equilibrium occurs at the price level where aggregate demand equals short-run aggregate supply. At this intersection, the quantity of real GDP that all buyers want to purchase equals the quantity that all firms want to produce. There is no tendency for the price level or real GDP to change, unless some external event disturbs the equilibrium.

The equilibrium price level and real GDP are denoted Pₑ and Yₑ. At Pₑ, the goods market clears: total planned spending equals total output. The labour market may or may not be at full employment — that is a question for the long-run analysis in Lesson 27.

A common mistake students make is to think that short-run equilibrium must also be at full employment. It need not be. The whole point of the short-run AS/AD model is that the economy can settle at a level of output above or below its potential, and that adjustment back to potential takes time. If equilibrium always coincided with full employment, there would be no recessions or booms to explain.

5. Analyzing Changes in Short-Run Equilibrium

Scenario A: An Increase in Aggregate Demand

Suppose consumer confidence surges, or the government increases spending, or interest rates fall. AD shifts right. The new short-run equilibrium is at a higher price level and a higher level of real GDP.

Why does real GDP rise? At the original price level, there is now excess demand — buyers want to purchase more than firms are currently producing. Firms respond by raising prices and increasing production. Because nominal wages are sticky, the rise in prices reduces real wages, making labour cheaper in real terms and encouraging firms to hire more workers and expand output.

The outcome: higher P and higher Y. This is a demand-driven expansion.

Scenario B: A Decrease in Aggregate Demand

Suppose consumer confidence collapses, or a recession abroad reduces exports, or interest rates rise sharply. AD shifts left. The new short-run equilibrium is at a lower price level and a lower level of real GDP.

Firms face falling demand for their products. They cut prices and reduce production. Because nominal wages are sticky downward, firms cannot reduce their wage bills easily, so they lay off workers instead. Unemployment rises. The outcome: lower P and lower Y. This is a demand-driven recession.

Scenario C: A Negative Supply Shock (SRAS Shifts Left)

Suppose oil prices spike or a natural disaster disrupts production. SRAS shifts left. At every price level, firms are willing to produce less.

The new equilibrium is at a higher price level and a lower level of real GDP. This is stagflation — stagnation (falling output, rising unemployment) combined with inflation (rising prices). Stagflation is particularly painful because the usual policy tools face a dilemma: boosting AD to fight the recession worsens inflation; reducing AD to fight inflation deepens the recession.

Scenario D: A Positive Supply Shock (SRAS Shifts Right)

Suppose a technological breakthrough lowers production costs across the economy, or a commodity price collapse makes inputs cheaper. SRAS shifts right.

The new equilibrium is at a lower price level and a higher level of real GDP. This is a favourable outcome: the economy produces more while prices fall. Policymakers welcome positive supply shocks because they improve both output and inflation simultaneously.

6. The Shape of SRAS at Different Levels of Output

The SRAS curve is often drawn as a straight upward-sloping line for simplicity, but in reality, its slope changes depending on how close the economy is to its productive capacity.

At low levels of output — when the economy is in a deep recession with high unemployment and idle factories — there is substantial spare capacity. Firms can increase production by bringing idle workers and machines back into use without bidding up wages or other input prices significantly. In this range, SRAS is relatively flat: increases in AD produce large increases in output with only small increases in the price level.

At high levels of output — when the economy is near or at full capacity — resources are scarce. Firms must compete for the remaining available workers and materials, driving up wages and input prices. Bottlenecks appear: a factory may have the machines to produce more but cannot find workers with the right skills; a supplier may be unable to deliver components fast enough. In this range, SRAS is relatively steep: increases in AD produce small increases in output but large increases in the price level.

This variation in slope will become important when we compare the monetarist and Keynesian models in later lessons.

IB Exam Tip

When you are asked to analyze the effects of a shock using the AD/SRAS model, follow a four-step structure in your answer. First, identify the type of shock and which curve shifts — AD or SRAS — and in which direction. Second, state what happens to the equilibrium price level and real GDP: both may rise, both may fall, or they may move in opposite directions. Third, explain the mechanism: why did the curve shift, and how does the new equilibrium emerge? Fourth, relate the analysis to the real-world context provided in the question — for example, "this is what happened during the oil price shock of 1973."

A second tip: always draw the diagram first, even in an essay answer. The diagram helps you organize your thinking and ensures you do not accidentally reverse the direction of a shift. The IB awards marks for correctly labeled and clearly drawn diagrams.

Answers

Answer 1. The SRAS curve shows the total quantity of goods and services that all firms in an economy are willing to produce at different average price levels during the short run, when at least one input price — typically nominal wages — is fixed or slow to adjust.

The sticky-wage explanation: nominal wages are slow to change because of labour contracts, minimum wage laws, efficiency wages, and worker resistance to nominal pay cuts. When the price level rises but nominal wages do not, the real wage (the wage divided by the price level) falls. Labour becomes cheaper in real terms. Firms find it more profitable to hire additional workers and expand production, so the quantity of real GDP supplied rises. This is why the SRAS curve slopes upward.

Numerical example: The nominal wage is $30 per hour. Before the price change, when the price level is 100, the real wage is $30 / (100/100) = $30.00 per hour. After the price level rises to 115, the real wage is $30 / (115/100) = $30 / 1.15 ≈ $26.09 per hour. The real wage has fallen by approximately 13 percent. Labour is cheaper in real terms, so firms increase production.

A common error is to confuse the real wage with the nominal wage. The nominal wage is the number on the paycheck. The real wage is what that paycheck can actually buy, and it depends on the price level.

Answer 2. Menu costs are the costs firms incur when they change their prices — printing new menus, updating websites, reprogramming databases, relabeling products, and communicating new prices to customers. These costs, while often small for any single firm, cause firms to adjust prices only periodically rather than continuously.

When the general price level rises, some firms delay raising their own prices because the menu cost of doing so exceeds the short-term benefit. During the delay, their goods are priced below the average market price and therefore become temporarily cheaper relative to competitors. Customers buy more from these firms, and the firms increase production to meet the higher demand.

The reason small menu costs can have large macroeconomic effects is that price stickiness at the individual firm level aggregates across the entire economy. If thousands of firms each delay repricing by a few months, total output responds to price level changes through the mechanism described. Research by Gregory Mankiw (1985) showed that even tiny menu costs can generate substantial nominal price rigidity because firms weigh the private cost of changing prices against the private benefit, ignoring the macroeconomic benefit of flexible prices.

A common mistake is to dismiss menu costs as trivial — "how much can a new menu cost?" But in industries like retail, e-commerce, and manufacturing, the administrative costs of repricing across thousands of products, updating contracts, and informing customers can be substantial. Moreover, there is a behavioural dimension: frequent price changes may annoy customers and damage relationships.

Answer 3.

(a) SRAS shifts right. Oil is a critical input for transport, manufacturing, and agriculture. When oil prices fall, production costs decline across many industries. At every price level, firms can profitably produce more output.

(b) SRAS shifts left. A higher minimum wage raises the cost of labour for firms that employ workers at or near the minimum. With higher labour costs, firms reduce the quantity they are willing to supply at any given price level.

(c) SRAS shifts left. The earthquake destroys productive capacity — factories, ports, power lines. This is a negative supply shock. Even if prices rise, firms cannot produce as much as before because their physical capacity has been reduced.

(d) SRAS shifts right. The technological breakthrough lowers production costs in the computer chip industry and in any industry that uses computer chips as an input. This is a positive supply shock. At every price level, firms can produce more.

(e) SRAS shifts right. A reduction in payroll taxes lowers the cost of employing each worker. Firms' total labour costs fall, so they are willing to supply more output at any given price level.

(f) SRAS shifts left. Tariffs on imported steel and aluminium raise the cost of these key inputs for domestic manufacturers such as carmakers, construction firms, and appliance producers. With higher input costs, firms reduce production at every price level.

Answer 4.

(a) The initial increase in investment spending is $120 billion. With a multiplier of 2.0, the total increase in AD is $120 billion × 2.0 = $240 billion. This is the horizontal distance by which the AD curve shifts right.

(b) AD shifts right by $240 billion. Given that the economy has some spare capacity, the new short-run equilibrium will be at a higher price level and a higher level of real GDP. The exact magnitudes depend on the slope of the SRAS curve. If the economy has substantial spare capacity, most of the AD increase translates into higher output with only a modest rise in the price level. If the economy is nearer to full capacity, the price level rises more and output rises less.

(c) The actual increase in real GDP may be less than $240 billion for two reasons. First, as the price level rises, the real value of money balances falls (the wealth effect), interest rates may rise (the interest rate effect), and net exports may fall (the international trade effect) — all of which cause a movement back along the new AD curve, partially offsetting the initial shift. Second, if the economy is near full capacity, the steep portion of the SRAS curve means that higher demand pulls up prices more than output. The multiplier concept assumes that supply is perfectly elastic — that firms can produce as much as demanded without raising prices. In reality, the upward-sloping SRAS means the multiplier is smaller than the simple formula suggests.

Answer 5. *(Model answer for 10-mark IB question)*

Short-run aggregate supply is the total quantity of goods and services that all firms in an economy are willing to produce at different average price levels during the period when at least one input price — typically nominal wages — is fixed or slow to adjust.

The SRAS curve slopes upward for three interrelated reasons. In all cases, a rise in the price level leads to a larger quantity of real GDP supplied.

The SRAS curve can shift — meaning firms are willing to supply a different quantity of real GDP at every price level — when something other than the price level changes production conditions. Three categories of determinants are especially important.

First, changes in input costs shift SRAS. A rise in nominal wages, raw material prices, or energy costs increases firms' production costs and shifts SRAS left. For example, when the price of oil tripled in 1973–1974, firms across the global economy faced sharply higher transport, heating, and manufacturing input costs, and SRAS shifted left. A fall in input costs shifts SRAS right. The collapse of oil prices from over $100 per barrel in 2014 to under $30 in early 2016 lowered costs and shifted SRAS right in oil-importing economies.

Second, supply shocks — unexpected events that suddenly change productive capacity or costs — shift SRAS. A negative supply shock, such as a natural disaster that destroys factories and ports, shifts SRAS left. A positive supply shock, such as a technological breakthrough that dramatically lowers production costs, shifts SRAS right.

Third, government policies that affect production costs shift SRAS. An increase in payroll taxes or business taxes shifts SRAS left by raising costs. A reduction in these taxes shifts SRAS right. Tighter regulations raise compliance costs and shift SRAS left, while deregulation reduces costs and shifts SRAS right.

The effect of a negative supply shock on macroeconomic equilibrium: the diagram should show the AD curve, the initial SRAS₁ curve, and the new SRAS₂ curve shifted to the left of SRAS₁. The initial equilibrium is at P₁, Y₁. After the leftward shift of SRAS, the new equilibrium is at P₂ (higher than P₁) and Y₂ (lower than Y₁). The axes are labeled "Price Level" (vertical) and "Real GDP" (horizontal). The shift is labeled with the cause — for example, "oil price spike → SRAS ↓."

The economy simultaneously suffers from a higher price level (inflation) and lower output (recession, with rising unemployment). This combination is called stagflation. It creates a policy dilemma because any attempt to boost AD to restore output will worsen inflation, while any attempt to reduce AD to fight inflation will deepen the recession.

Answer 6.

(a) The rise in oil prices is a negative supply shock: SRAS shifts left. Taken alone, this raises the price level and reduces real GDP. The fall in consumer confidence is a negative demand shock: AD shifts left. Taken alone, this lowers the price level and reduces real GDP.

(b) Both shocks reduce real GDP, so the effect on output is unambiguous: Y falls. The economy enters a recession. The effect on the price level is ambiguous. The supply shock pushes P up; the demand shock pushes P down. The net effect on P depends on the relative magnitudes of the two shifts. If the supply shock is larger, P rises (stagflation). If the demand shock is larger, P falls (recession with deflation). If they are of equal size, P may be approximately unchanged.

(c) The policy dilemma is acute. The government wants to boost output to reduce unemployment and end the recession, which calls for expansionary policy — increasing G, cutting taxes, or lowering interest rates to shift AD right. But if the supply shock is large, inflation is already rising, and expansionary policy will worsen it. Conversely, if the government tightens policy to fight inflation, it deepens the recession and increases unemployment. There is no policy that simultaneously solves both problems. This is precisely the dilemma that policymakers faced during the oil shocks of the 1970s, when conventional Keynesian demand management proved unable to address stagflation.

Evaluate

Question: "The SRAS curve slopes upward only because nominal wages are sticky." Evaluate this statement. Model Answer:

The statement identifies the most quantitatively significant reason for the upward-sloping SRAS curve — sticky nominal wages — but it is incorrect to claim that sticky wages are the only reason. Two other mechanisms, menu costs and imperfect information, also contribute, and each operates through a different channel.

Sticky wages are the dominant explanation for good reason. Labour costs represent roughly 60 to 70 percent of total production costs in developed economies. When nominal wages fail to adjust to a change in the price level, the resulting change in real wages has a direct and substantial effect on firms' marginal costs and therefore on their willingness to produce. Extensive empirical evidence supports the existence of downward nominal wage rigidity. Studies of wage change distributions across many countries consistently show a sharp spike at zero — many firms freeze wages rather than cut them — and very few observations of nominal wage cuts. Truman Bewley's research, based on interviews with hundreds of managers, found that firms avoid nominal wage cuts because they devastate worker morale and productivity, even when economic conditions might justify them.

However, menu costs provide an independent channel. Menu costs cause price stickiness at the level of individual goods, not just wage stickiness in labour markets. Even if wages were perfectly flexible, firms facing menu costs would still delay price adjustments. A restaurant with fully flexible wages but a $1,500 cost to reprint menus would still keep its prices unchanged for some period after a change in the price level, and its output response during that period would contribute to an upward-sloping SRAS. The menu-cost channel is especially relevant in industries with frequent price changes — retail, e-commerce, and hospitality — where administrative repricing costs are non-trivial.

Imperfect information provides a third, conceptually distinct channel. The misperceptions theory does not rely on any cost rigidity at all. It relies on the fact that firms cannot perfectly distinguish general price changes from relative price changes. This is an informational problem rather than a cost problem. Even in a hypothetical economy with perfectly flexible wages and zero menu costs, if firms misinterpret a rise in the general price level as a rise in demand for their own product, they will increase output, producing an upward-sloping SRAS. The Lucas misperceptions model is the foundation of the New Classical approach to aggregate supply.

The three explanations are complementary, not competing. They operate simultaneously in real economies and reinforce each other. Sticky wages slow the adjustment of the largest component of firms' costs. Menu costs slow the adjustment of output prices. Misperceptions affect how firms interpret the price signals they observe. Together, they provide a comprehensive account of why SRAS slopes upward.

The statement also has an implicit policy implication that is worth examining. If sticky wages were the only reason, then the sole policy prescription for making the economy more flexible would be labour market reform — reducing union power, shortening contract lengths, and removing minimum wages. But if menu costs also matter, then policies that reduce the administrative burden of price adjustment — simplifying regulations around price displays, for example — could also be relevant. If misperceptions matter, then improving the quality and timeliness of economic information available to firms could help.

In conclusion, the statement is too narrow. Sticky nominal wages are the most important single reason for the upward-sloping SRAS curve, supported by both the weight of labour in total costs and the empirical evidence on wage rigidity. But they are not the only reason. Menu costs and imperfect information are independently valid mechanisms that complement the sticky-wage explanation. A complete understanding of short-run aggregate supply requires all three.
