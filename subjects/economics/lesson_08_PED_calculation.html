Lesson 8: Price Elasticity of Demand — Calculation and Interpretation

Syllabus reference: Unit 3.1 — Elasticities

Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to:

  • Define price elasticity of demand (PED) and state its formula
  • Calculate PED using percentage changes and the midpoint formula
  • Interpret PED values across the full range from perfectly inelastic to perfectly elastic
  • Explain the relationship between PED and total revenue
  • Explain why PED varies along a straight-line demand curve

1. Prior Knowledge Check

Q1: When the price of coffee rises, the quantity demanded falls — this is the law of demand. But how much does it fall? If the price of a life-saving medicine rises by 50%, do you think patients will buy much less? What about if the price of a specific brand of bottled water rises by 50%?

Show

For the medicine, patients will likely buy almost the same amount — they need it to survive. For the bottled water, consumers will switch to a different brand — quantity demanded will fall sharply. The difference is elasticity: how responsive quantity demanded is to a change in price.

2. What PED Is and Why It Matters

Price elasticity of demand (PED) measures the responsiveness of quantity demanded to a change in the good's own price. Formally:

$$\text{PED} = \frac{\%\text{ change in quantity demanded}}{\%\text{ change in price}}$$

PED is almost always negative (price and quantity demanded move in opposite directions due to the law of demand), but by convention, we often drop the minus sign and refer to its absolute value. When the IB asks for an interpretation, both conventions appear — the key is whether the value is greater than, equal to, or less than 1.

PED ValueClassificationMeaning
PED = 0Perfectly inelasticQuantity demanded does not respond at all to price changes
0 < PED < 1Relatively inelasticQuantity demanded changes by a smaller % than price
PED = 1Unit elasticQuantity demanded changes by the same % as price
PED > 1Relatively elasticQuantity demanded changes by a larger % than price
PED = ∞Perfectly elasticAny price rise causes quantity demanded to fall to zero

Check-in: If PED = 0.4, a 10% price increase will cause quantity demanded to fall by what percentage?

Show

4%. PED = %ΔQd ÷ %ΔP → 0.4 = %ΔQd ÷ 10 → %ΔQd = 4%. Since PED < 1, demand is inelastic — consumers are not very responsive.

3. The Midpoint Formula

When calculating PED between two points on a demand curve, you must decide which price and quantity to use as the base. Using the starting point gives a different answer than using the ending point. The midpoint formula solves this by using the average of the two:

$$\text{PED} = \frac{(Q_2 - Q_1) / ((Q_1+Q_2)/2)}{(P_2 - P_1) / ((P_1+P_2)/2)}$$

The IB requires the midpoint formula for calculations between two discrete points. The simple percentage change formula (%ΔQd ÷ %ΔP) is used when you are given percentage changes directly or when calculating PED at a specific point on a linear demand curve.

4. PED and Total Revenue

Total revenue (TR) = Price × Quantity sold. The relationship between PED and TR is one of the most practically important results in microeconomics:

  • If demand is elastic (PED > 1): Raising price reduces total revenue (the % fall in quantity outweighs the % rise in price). Lowering price increases total revenue.
  • If demand is inelastic (PED < 1): Raising price increases total revenue (the % rise in price outweighs the % fall in quantity). Lowering price reduces total revenue.
  • If demand is unit elastic (PED = 1): Total revenue is maximised and does not change with small price changes.

Check-in: A cinema currently charges £10 per ticket and sells 500 tickets (TR=£5,000). The manager knows PED=1.5 at this price. If she raises the price to £11, will total revenue rise or fall?

Show

Fall. PED=1.5 > 1 means demand is elastic. A 10% price increase causes quantity demanded to fall by 15%. The loss in quantity outweighs the gain in price, so TR falls.

5. Varying PED Along a Straight-Line Demand Curve

A common mistake is thinking PED is constant along a straight-line demand curve. It is not. At high prices (top-left of the curve), PED is elastic (>1). At the midpoint, PED=1 (unit elastic). At low prices (bottom-right), PED is inelastic (<1). Why? Because as we move down the curve, the percentage change in price (from a lower base) becomes larger relative to the percentage change in quantity (from a higher base).

6. Worked Examples

Example 1: Midpoint Calculation

Problem: When the price of lattes rises from £3 to £4, quantity demanded falls from 120 to 80 per day. Calculate PED using the midpoint formula and interpret.

Step 1: %ΔQd = (80−120)/((120+80)/2) = −40/100 = −0.40 (or −40%).

Step 2: %ΔP = (4−3)/((3+4)/2) = 1/3.5 = 0.286 (or 28.6%).

Step 3: PED = −40% ÷ 28.6% = −1.4. |PED| = 1.4 > 1 → demand is elastic. A 28.6% price rise caused a 40% fall in Qd.

⚠️ Common Wrong: Using simple % change: (80−120)/120=−33.3% and (4−3)/3=33.3% → PED=−1.0 (unit elastic). Different method, different answer. The IB expects the midpoint formula for discrete changes.

Example 2: TR Test

Problem: A firm sells 1,000 units at £20 (TR=£20,000). PED at this price is 0.6. Should the firm raise or lower the price to increase revenue?

PED=0.6 < 1 → inelastic. Raising price will increase TR. If the firm raises price to £22 (10% increase), Qd falls by 6% (0.6×10) to 940. New TR = £22×940 = £20,680 > £20,000. ✓

⚠️ Common Mistakes

  1. Mistake: Interpreting PED=−2 as inelastic because "2 is small." Correct: Take absolute value. |−2|=2 > 1 → elastic.
  2. Mistake: Using starting-point percentages instead of midpoint for discrete-point calculations. Correct: IB requires the midpoint formula: (Q₂−Q₁)/average ÷ (P₂−P₁)/average.
  3. Mistake: Thinking PED is constant along a straight demand curve. Correct: PED changes continuously — elastic at high prices, inelastic at low prices.
  4. Mistake: Confusing slope with elasticity. Correct: Slope is ΔP/ΔQ (constant for a straight line). Elasticity is %ΔQd/%ΔP (varies along the line).

7. Practice

Q1. [MC] If a 5% price increase causes a 2% fall in Qd, PED is:

A. 2.5 (elastic) B. 0.4 (inelastic) C. 0.4 (elastic) D. 2.5 (inelastic)

Q2. [MC] A firm with elastic demand should _____ price to increase total revenue:

A. Raise B. Lower C. Not change D. Either raise or lower

Q3. [Paper 2] Price rises from £5 to £7; Qd falls from 600 to 400. Calculate PED using the midpoint formula. Classify and explain what happens to TR. [4 marks]

Q4. [Paper 2, Exam Style] A government is considering raising taxes on cigarettes. The PED for cigarettes is estimated at 0.3 for adult smokers and 1.2 for teenage smokers. (a) Explain what each PED value means for the responsiveness of each group. [2 marks] (b) Using the TR test, predict the effect of a price increase on total spending by each group. [2 marks] (c) Explain why the PED for teenage smokers might be higher than for adult smokers. [2 marks]

A1: B. PED=|%ΔQd/%ΔP|=|−2/5|=0.4. Since 0.4 < 1, demand is inelastic.

A2: B. Elastic (PED>1) means % fall in Qd > % rise in P. Lowering price: the % rise in Qd > % fall in P, so TR increases.

A3: %ΔQ=(400−600)/500=−0.4. %ΔP=(7−5)/6=0.333. PED=−0.4/0.333=−1.2. |PED|=1.2>1 → elastic. Price rose → TR falls (the 40% Qd fall outweighs the 33% P rise).

A4: (a) Adults (0.3): inelastic — a 10% price rise → 3% Qd fall. Teens (1.2): elastic — a 10% price rise → 12% Qd fall. (b) Adults: TR rises (price ↑, Qd falls less than proportionally). Teens: TR falls (Qd falls more than proportionally). (c) Teens may have lower incomes (cigarettes take a larger share of budget), weaker addiction (less time to become dependent), and more substitute options (social activities, vaping).

9. Exam Tips

Paper 1: If asked to "explain" PED, you must: define it, give the formula, show the five classifications in a table, and explain the TR relationship. A diagram showing elastic vs inelastic demand curves on the same axes is essential.
Paper 2: Calculation questions: ALWAYS use the midpoint formula unless given percentages directly. Show all working. A common mark-losing error: forgetting to interpret the result after calculating — always state whether demand is elastic or inelastic and what that means for the good in question.
