Lesson 8: Price Elasticity of Demand — Calculation and Interpretation

What You'll Learn

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to:

  • Define price elasticity of demand (PED) and explain what it measures
  • Calculate PED using both the simple percentage method and the midpoint formula
  • Interpret the full range of PED values from perfectly inelastic to perfectly elastic
  • Explain why PED varies at every point along a straight-line demand curve
  • Analyse the relationship between PED and total revenue and use the total revenue test
  • Identify the price that maximises total revenue using elasticity

    • ---

    1. What Price Elasticity of Demand Is and Why It Matters

    The Core Idea

    When the price of a good changes, the quantity that consumers demand changes in response. But how much does it change? That depends on the good. A cinema that raises its ticket price from £12 to £15 might see a dramatic drop in attendance — or it might see barely any change at all. The difference between these two scenarios is enormous for the cinema's revenue, its staffing decisions, and its long-term viability.

    Price elasticity of demand (PED) is the measure that captures this difference. PED tells you how responsive quantity demanded is to a change in price. It does so by comparing the percentage change in quantity demanded to the percentage change in price.

    PED matters because it predicts what will happen to a firm's total revenue when it changes its price. It tells a government how effective a tax on a particular good will be at changing behaviour. It helps businesses forecast demand after a price adjustment. And it explains why some goods have stable markets while others experience wild swings in sales.

    The PED Formula

    PED is defined as:

    $$ Because demand curves slope downward, a price increase causes quantity demanded to fall. The PED value is therefore always negative. However, the IB convention is to use the **absolute value** of PED — written as |PED| — when classifying elasticity. You should state the signed value and then give the absolute value. For example: "PED is calculated as −2, so |PED| = 2, which means demand is elastic." ### A Common Misconception A common mistake students make is thinking that PED is the same as the slope of the demand curve. PED and slope are related but fundamentally different. The slope of a demand curve is the ratio of the absolute change in price to the absolute change in quantity (ΔP/ΔQ). PED is the ratio of percentage changes. A straight-line demand curve has a constant slope but a PED that varies at every point. A steep demand curve does not necessarily mean inelastic demand, and a flat demand curve does not necessarily mean elastic demand. Always calculate PED; never infer it from slope alone. --- ## 2. Classifying PED Values Economists classify demand into five categories based on the absolute value of PED. | Absolute value of PED | Classification | What it means | |------------------------|----------------|---------------| | |PED| = 0 | Perfectly inelastic | Quantity demanded does not change at all when price changes | | 0 < |PED| < 1 | Inelastic | Quantity demanded changes by a smaller percentage than price | | |PED| = 1 | Unit elastic | Quantity demanded changes by exactly the same percentage as price | | |PED| > 1 | Elastic | Quantity demanded changes by a larger percentage than price | | |PED| → ∞ | Perfectly elastic | Any price increase causes quantity demanded to fall to zero | ### Perfectly Inelastic Demand (|PED| = 0) When demand is perfectly inelastic, consumers buy exactly the same quantity regardless of price. The demand curve is a vertical line. A life-saving medicine with no substitutes — for example, insulin for a person with Type 1 diabetes — comes close to this extreme. If the price doubles, the patient still needs the same dose. ### Inelastic Demand (0 < |PED| < 1) When demand is inelastic, consumers reduce their quantity demanded when price rises, but by a smaller percentage than the price increase. Petrol for commuting is an example. If the price of petrol rises by 20%, a commuter might reduce consumption by only 5% in the short run because they still need to get to work. |PED| would be 0.25. ### Unit Elastic Demand (|PED| = 1) When demand is unit elastic, the percentage change in quantity demanded exactly equals the percentage change in price. If price rises by 10%, quantity demanded falls by 10%. Total revenue — which is price multiplied by quantity — stays the same. ### Elastic Demand (|PED| > 1) When demand is elastic, consumers are highly responsive to price changes. A price increase causes a proportionally larger decrease in quantity demanded. Restaurant meals are often elastic: if a restaurant raises its prices by 10%, it might lose 25% of its customers as diners switch to competing restaurants. |PED| would be 2.5. ### Perfectly Elastic Demand (|PED| → ∞) When demand is perfectly elastic, consumers will buy any quantity at one specific price but zero at any higher price. The demand curve is a horizontal line. A small farmer selling wheat in a global market faces perfectly elastic demand: if the farmer tries to charge even one cent above the world price, buyers go elsewhere. ### Worked Example 1: Classifying PED A coffee shop raises the price of a latte from £3.00 to £3.60. The number of lattes sold per day falls from 200 to 160. **Approach:** Calculate the percentage changes in price and quantity, then divide. **Step 1:** Percentage change in price = (New price − Original price) / Original price × 100. %ΔP = (£3.60 − £3.00) / £3.00 × 100 = £0.60 / £3.00 × 100 = 20%. **Step 2:** Percentage change in quantity = (New quantity − Original quantity) / Original quantity × 100. %ΔQd = (160 − 200) / 200 × 100 = −40 / 200 × 100 = −20%. **Step 3:** PED = %ΔQd / %ΔP = −20% / 20% = −1. |PED| = 1. Demand for lattes at this coffee shop is **unit elastic** over this price range. **Why this makes sense:** The 20% price increase caused an exactly offsetting 20% quantity decrease. Total revenue before was £3.00 × 200 = £600. Total revenue after is £3.60 × 160 = £576. Wait — that does not match. Let me check. £3.60 × 160 = £576. But with unit elastic demand, TR should stay the same. The discrepancy arises because the simple percentage method uses the original values as the base, which can give PED = 1 even when TR changes. This is why we need the midpoint formula for precision. --- ## 3. The Midpoint (Arc) Formula ### Why We Need the Midpoint Formula The simple PED formula has a problem: it gives different answers depending on whether you measure the change from the starting point or from the ending point. Suppose the price rises from £4 to £6 and quantity falls from 40 to 20 units. Using the original values: %ΔP = (6 − 4) / 4 = 50%. %ΔQd = (20 − 40) / 40 = −50%. PED = −50% / 50% = −1. |PED| = 1 (unit elastic). Now reverse the direction: price falls from £6 to £4 and quantity rises from 20 to 40. Using the new "original" values: %ΔP = (4 − 6) / 6 = −33.3%. %ΔQd = (40 − 20) / 20 = 100%. PED = 100% / (−33.3%) = −3. |PED| = 3 (elastic). The same two points on the same demand curve produce two wildly different PED values. This makes the simple method unreliable. The solution is the **midpoint formula** (also called the **arc elasticity formula**). ### The Midpoint Formula Instead of using the original values as the denominator, the midpoint formula uses the average of the two values: \text{PED} = \frac{\frac{Q_2 - Q_1}{(Q_1 + Q_2)/2}}{\frac{P_2 - P_1}{(P_1 + P_2)/2}} = \frac{\Delta Q}{\text{Average } Q} \div \frac{\Delta P}{\text{Average } P}$$

    Worked Example 2: Using the Midpoint Formula

    The price of a cinema ticket rises from £12 to £15. The number of tickets sold per week falls from 800 to 600.

    Approach: Use the midpoint formula with average quantity and average price as the denominators.

    Step 1: Calculate the average quantity.

    Average Q = (800 + 600) / 2 = 1,400 / 2 = 700.

    Step 2: Calculate the percentage change in quantity using the average.

    %ΔQd = (600 − 800) / 700 × 100 = −200 / 700 × 100 = −28.57%.

    Step 3: Calculate the average price.

    Average P = (£12 + £15) / 2 = £27 / 2 = £13.50.

    Step 4: Calculate the percentage change in price using the average.

    %ΔP = (£15 − £12) / £13.50 × 100 = £3 / £13.50 × 100 = 22.22%.

    Step 5: PED = −28.57% / 22.22% = −1.29.

    PED

    Why this makes sense: The cinema's total revenue before the price rise was £12 × 800 = £9,600. After the price rise, total revenue is £15 × 600 = £9,000. Revenue fell, which is exactly what the elasticity classification predicts: when demand is elastic, a price increase reduces total revenue.

    IB Requirement

    The IB syllabus expects you to use the midpoint formula when calculating PED between two points. Always state which formula you are using. If the question provides a demand equation, you can calculate PED at a single point using the point elasticity formula (discussed next). If the question gives two price-quantity pairs, use the midpoint formula.

    4. PED Along a Straight-Line Demand Curve

    Why PED Changes While Slope Stays the Same

    A straight-line (linear) demand curve has a constant slope. For every £1 increase in price, quantity demanded falls by the same number of units. But PED is not constant because PED depends on both the slope and the position on the curve.

    The point elasticity formula for a linear demand curve is:

    $$ The term ΔQ/ΔP is the reciprocal of the slope. It is constant all along a straight-line demand curve. But the ratio P/Q changes at every point. At high prices, Q is small, so P/Q is large. PED has a large absolute value — demand is elastic near the top of the demand curve. At low prices, Q is large, so P/Q is small. PED has a small absolute value — demand is inelastic near the bottom of the demand curve. At the exact midpoint of the demand curve, |PED| = 1 — demand is unit elastic. ### Worked Example 3: PED at Different Points A demand curve is given by P = 100 − Q, where P is the price in pounds. **Approach:** We use the point elasticity formula PED = (ΔQ/ΔP) × (P/Q). From P = 100 − Q, we can write Q = 100 − P. So ΔQ/ΔP = −1. **At P = £90:** Q = 100 − 90 = 10. PED = (−1) × (90 / 10) = −9. |PED| = 9. Demand is **highly elastic** at this point. **At P = £50:** Q = 100 − 50 = 50. PED = (−1) × (50 / 50) = −1. |PED| = 1. Demand is **unit elastic** at this point. This is the midpoint. **At P = £10:** Q = 100 − 10 = 90. PED = (−1) × (10 / 90) = −0.11. |PED| = 0.11. Demand is **highly inelastic** at this point. **Why this makes sense:** When price is £90, only 10 units are sold. A small price decrease brings in many new customers (in percentage terms), so demand is elastic. When price is £10, 90 units are already sold. The same absolute price decrease cannot attract many more customers in percentage terms, so demand is inelastic. The same demand curve can be elastic, unit elastic, or inelastic depending on where you are on it. ### A Common Misconception A common mistake students make is describing a good as having "elastic demand" or "inelastic demand" without specifying the price range. Every straight-line demand curve has an elastic region, a unit-elastic midpoint, and an inelastic region. When an exam question asks "Is the demand for this good elastic or inelastic?", it always means "at the current market price." --- ## 5. PED and Total Revenue ### The Total Revenue Test **Total revenue** (TR) is the amount of money a firm receives from selling its product. It is calculated as: \text{Total Revenue} = \text{Price} \times \text{Quantity} = P \times Q$$

    The relationship between PED and total revenue is one of the most practically useful ideas in microeconomics. It allows a firm to predict whether a price change will increase or decrease its total revenue.

    The rule is:

  • If demand is elastic (|PED| > 1): raising price decreases total revenue. Lowering price increases total revenue.
  • If demand is inelastic (|PED| < 1): raising price increases total revenue. Lowering price decreases total revenue.
  • If demand is unit elastic (|PED| = 1): changing price leaves total revenue unchanged.

    • The logic: when demand is elastic, the percentage change in quantity is larger than the percentage change in price. The quantity effect dominates, so a price increase causes a proportionally larger quantity drop, and revenue falls. When demand is inelastic, the price effect dominates, so a price increase raises revenue.

    Where Total Revenue Is Maximised

    Total revenue reaches its maximum value at the quantity where demand is unit elastic (|PED| = 1). At this point, any movement in either direction — raising price or lowering it — would reduce total revenue. For a straight-line demand curve, this occurs at the midpoint of the curve.

    Worked Example 4: The Total Revenue Test

    A firm sells 500 units of its product at £20 each. Total revenue is £10,000. The firm lowers the price to £18 and sells 560 units.

    Approach: Calculate total revenue at both prices. If TR rose when price fell, demand is elastic. If TR fell when price fell, demand is inelastic.

    Step 1: TR before = £20 × 500 = £10,000.

    Step 2: TR after = £18 × 560 = £10,080.

    Step 3: Price fell and TR rose. Therefore demand is elastic over this range.

    Step 4: Verify with the midpoint formula. Average Q = (500 + 560) / 2 = 530. Average P = (£20 + £18) / 2 = £19. %ΔQd = 60 / 530 × 100 = 11.32%. %ΔP = −£2 / £19 × 100 = −10.53%. PED = 11.32% / (−10.53%) = −1.08. |PED| = 1.08. Elastic. The total revenue test and the midpoint formula agree.

    Why this makes sense: The firm attracted 60 new customers (a 12% increase) by cutting the price by only about 10%. The extra customers more than made up for the lower price on each unit.

    A Common Misconception

    A common mistake students make is thinking that a firm should always set price where demand is elastic because "that's where revenue grows." But revenue growth comes from price cuts in the elastic region, not from being in the elastic region per se. And firms maximise profit, not revenue. The profit-maximising price depends on costs as well as demand. A pharmaceutical company with a patented drug might deliberately price in the inelastic region because the high price, despite reducing quantity, generates enormous profit margins.

    ---

    IB Exam Tip

    When a question asks you to "calculate PED," use the midpoint formula if you are given two price-quantity pairs. Use the point formula PED = (ΔQ/ΔP) × (P/Q) if you are given a demand equation and a specific price.

    When a question asks about the effect of a price change on total revenue, state the total revenue test explicitly: "Because demand is elastic, the percentage fall in quantity demanded will exceed the percentage rise in price, so total revenue will decrease." Link the reasoning to the numbers.

    Always state whether you are using the absolute value convention. The IB rewards clarity: write "PED = −1.5, so |PED| = 1.5, meaning demand is elastic."

It is calculated as the percentage change in quantity demanded divided by the percentage change in price. Because demand curves slope downward, PED is always negative, but the IB convention uses the absolute value for classification. PED tells firms and governments how sensitive consumers are to price changes.     Evaluate

    Question: "A firm should always set its price in the elastic region of its demand curve." Evaluate this statement.

    Model Answer:

    The statement has some appeal because, in the elastic region, a price cut increases total revenue. If a firm is currently pricing in the inelastic region, it can increase both its revenue and its profit by raising its price (since costs fall as quantity falls). This suggests that a profit-maximising firm would never voluntarily operate in the inelastic region of its demand curve — it could always do better by raising price until it reaches at least the unit-elastic point. In this narrow sense, the statement is directionally correct: pricing in the inelastic region leaves money on the table.

    However, the statement is too broad to be universally true. First, the distinction between revenue and profit is fundamental. A firm maximises profit, not revenue. The profit-maximising price is where marginal revenue equals marginal cost, which may occur at any point on the demand curve depending on the firm's cost structure. A firm with very high marginal costs might find that its profit-maximising price lies in the elastic region. But a firm with very low marginal costs — a software company, for example — might maximise profit at a price in the inelastic region because the high price generates enormous margins even on fewer units.

    Second, the statement assumes the firm has the freedom to choose any price. In a highly competitive market, the firm may be a price-taker with no pricing power at all. In an oligopoly, strategic interdependence matters: a firm might price in the inelastic region to avoid triggering a price war. The kinked demand curve model in oligopoly theory explicitly predicts that firms may face a discontinuity in marginal revenue that leads them to maintain prices even when costs change — a situation where the simple elasticity rule does not apply.

    Third, pricing objectives extend beyond short-run profit. A new entrant might deliberately price low (in the elastic region) to build market share, even if a higher price in the inelastic region would generate more profit in the short run. This is penetration pricing. A luxury brand might price high (potentially in the inelastic region) to maintain an exclusive image, accepting lower volume as part of its brand strategy.

    Fourth, the public sector and regulated industries have different objectives. A public transport authority might deliberately price in the inelastic region to keep fares affordable and maximise ridership, even if a higher price would increase revenue. Social welfare, not profit, is the goal.

    In conclusion, while there is a valid economic logic behind the idea that firms should avoid the inelastic region (because raising price increases both revenue and reduces costs), the statement is not an absolute rule. Profit maximisation, market structure, strategic goals, and social objectives all influence optimal pricing. The statement is a useful starting point but an oversimplification.
