## 1. Scarcity — The Fundamental Economic Problem

### What Scarcity Is and Why It Matters

Economics exists because of one inescapable fact: human wants are unlimited, but the resources available to satisfy those wants are limited. This is the definition of **scarcity**. It is not the same as poverty, and it is not a temporary condition. Scarcity is permanent and universal. Every society, rich or poor, faces it.

A resource is **scarce** when there is not enough of it to satisfy all the ways people want to use it. Time is scarce — you cannot simultaneously study economics, practice a sport, and sleep for twelve hours. Money is scarce — a government cannot simultaneously fund universal healthcare, build every proposed road, and cut all taxes to zero. Land is scarce — a city cannot use the same plot for both a park and an apartment building.

Scarcity forces **choice**. Because you cannot have everything, you must decide what to pursue and what to give up. This is where economics begins. Every economic decision, from a student choosing a university course to a government setting a budget, is a response to scarcity.

### A Common Misconception: Scarcity vs. Shortage

Many students confuse scarcity with a shortage. They are different ideas.

A **shortage** occurs when, at a given price, the quantity demanded of a good exceeds the quantity supplied. A shortage is temporary and can be eliminated by raising the price or producing more. For example, if a concert sells out, there is a shortage of tickets at the listed price.

**Scarcity**, by contrast, never goes away. Even if ticket prices rise and the concert moves to a larger venue, the underlying resources — the musicians' time, the venue's land, the equipment — remain scarce. There are always alternative uses for them.

| Concept | Duration | Solved by |
|---------|----------|-----------|
| Scarcity | Permanent | Nothing — it is the human condition |
| Shortage | Temporary | Raising price or increasing supply |

## 2. Choice and Opportunity Cost

### What Opportunity Cost Is

When scarcity forces you to choose, you sacrifice the next-best alternative. This sacrifice has a name: **opportunity cost**.

The opportunity cost of a choice is the value of the next-best alternative that you give up when you make that choice. It is not the sum of all alternatives you gave up — only the single best one among them. If you have three options for your evening — study economics, watch a film, or go for a run — and you choose to study, the opportunity cost is whichever of the other two you value more. It is not both.

Why does opportunity cost matter? Because rational decision-making requires comparing what you gain from a choice with what you lose. If the benefit of studying exceeds the benefit of the next-best activity, the choice is worthwhile. If it does not, you should reconsider.

### Worked Example 1: Calculating Opportunity Cost

A student has £40 and a free Saturday. She is choosing between two options: buying a new textbook for £40, or buying a concert ticket for £40. She values the textbook at £60 worth of benefit and the concert at £50 worth of benefit.

**Approach:** To find the opportunity cost, we identify the next-best alternative — the option with the highest value among those not chosen.

**Step 1:** List the two options and their benefits.
- Textbook: £60 benefit
- Concert: £50 benefit

**Step 2:** If she chooses the textbook, the concert is the forgone alternative. The opportunity cost is the benefit of the concert: £50.

**Step 3:** If she instead chooses the concert, the opportunity cost is the benefit of the textbook: £60.

**Why this makes sense:** The student faces a trade-off between £60 of benefit and £50 of benefit. Choosing the concert means giving up the larger benefit, so that choice has a higher opportunity cost. She should choose the textbook because its benefit (£60) exceeds its opportunity cost (£50).

### Free Goods and Economic Goods

A **free good** is something that is not scarce — there is enough of it to satisfy all wants at zero price. Air (in most circumstances) is a free good. You can breathe as much as you want without reducing the amount available to anyone else. Free goods have zero opportunity cost because choosing to use them does not require giving up anything else.

An **economic good** is something that is scarce. It commands a price, and using it involves an opportunity cost. Most things in an economy — food, housing, education, healthcare — are economic goods.

## 3. The Three Basic Economic Questions

Because resources are scarce, every economy must answer three fundamental questions:

**What to produce?** Given limited resources, which goods and services should be made, and in what quantities? Should a country produce more hospital beds or more school places? More consumer electronics or more grain?

**How to produce?** What combination of resources — land, labour, capital — should be used to make each good? Should electricity come from coal, wind, or nuclear power? Should farming be labour-intensive (many workers, simple tools) or capital-intensive (few workers, advanced machinery)?

**For whom to produce?** Who receives the goods and services produced? Should distribution be based on who can pay, who needs them most, or some other principle?

Different economic systems answer these questions differently. In a market economy, prices and private decisions largely determine the answers. In a planned economy, government decisions do. Most real economies mix both approaches.

## 4. Factors of Production

### What Factors of Production Are

To produce any good or service, an economy needs inputs. Economists classify these inputs into four categories called **factors of production**. Every good or service you consume is made from some combination of these four factors.

### Land

**Land** includes all natural resources: the physical ground itself, minerals beneath it, water, forests, fish stocks, oil and gas deposits, and the climate. The factor payment — the income earned by the owner of land — is **rent**.

### Labour

**Labour** is human effort, both physical and mental, used in production. A construction worker swinging a hammer provides labour. A software engineer writing code provides labour. A surgeon performing an operation provides labour. The factor payment for labour is **wages** (or salaries).

The quantity of labour in an economy depends on the size of the population, the proportion of that population of working age, and the number of those people who choose to work (the labour force participation rate). The quality of labour — called **human capital** — depends on education, training, health, and experience.

### Capital

**Capital** (or **physical capital**) is manufactured goods used to produce other goods and services. A tractor on a farm is capital. A factory building is capital. A computer used by an accountant is capital. The factor payment for capital is **interest**.

A common misconception: in everyday language, "capital" often means money. In economics, capital means physical productive assets — machines, buildings, tools, infrastructure. Money itself is not capital; it is a medium of exchange that can be used to purchase capital.

Capital is special among the factors of production because it is itself produced. A tractor does not exist in nature; someone had to build it, using other resources. This means that to increase an economy's capital stock, some current production must be devoted to making capital goods rather than consumer goods — an investment decision that involves an opportunity cost.

### Entrepreneurship

**Entrepreneurship** is the ability to combine the other three factors of production — land, labour, and capital — to produce goods and services. Entrepreneurs take risks. They identify opportunities, organise resources, innovate, and bear the financial consequences if their ventures fail. The factor payment for entrepreneurship is **profit**.

Not everyone who runs a business is an entrepreneur in the economic sense. A person who operates a small shop in exactly the same way it has been run for thirty years is providing labour (and possibly capital). An entrepreneur introduces something new — a product, a process, a market, or a way of organising production.

### Summary Table

| Factor | What It Is | Factor Payment |
|--------|-----------|----------------|
| Land | Natural resources | Rent |
| Labour | Human effort (physical and mental) | Wages |
| Capital | Manufactured goods used in production | Interest |
| Entrepreneurship | Risk-taking, innovation, organisation | Profit |

## 5. Positive and Normative Statements

### What the Distinction Is

Economists make two kinds of statements, and knowing which is which is essential for clear thinking.

A **positive statement** is a claim about what is. It can be tested against evidence and shown to be true or false. Positive statements are factual. They do not depend on the speaker's values.

Examples of positive statements:
- "The unemployment rate in Spain was 12% in 2024."
- "An increase in the minimum wage reduces employment among teenage workers."
- "The UK's inflation rate fell from 11.1% to 4.6% between October 2022 and October 2023."

Each of these claims can be checked against data. Even if a positive statement turns out to be wrong, it is still a positive statement because it makes a testable claim.

A **normative statement** is a claim about what ought to be. It involves a value judgment. Normative statements cannot be settled by facts alone because they depend on what people believe is desirable, fair, or right.

Examples of normative statements:
- "The government should reduce unemployment even if it causes inflation."
- "Income inequality is too high and must be reduced."
- "Taxes on petrol are unfair to rural residents."

Words like "should," "ought," "fair," "too high," and "unfair" signal a normative statement. Two reasonable people can look at the same data and reach different normative conclusions because they hold different values.

### Why the Distinction Matters

The IB exam rewards precision. If a question asks you to "explain" or "describe," it wants positive analysis. If it asks you to "evaluate" or "discuss," it wants positive analysis followed by a normative judgment supported by reasoning.

A common mistake is presenting a normative claim as if it were a fact. "Inflation is too high" is normative. "Inflation is 5.3%, which exceeds the central bank's 2% target" is positive. Learn to separate your values from your analysis, then bring them together deliberately in evaluation.

## 6. Microeconomics and Macroeconomics

### Two Branches of One Subject

Economics divides into two branches, distinguished by the scale of the questions they ask.

**Microeconomics** studies the behaviour of individual economic agents: households, firms, and industries. It asks questions like: why does the price of coffee rise when a frost hits Brazil? How does a firm decide how many workers to hire? What happens to rents when a city imposes rent control? Microeconomics examines individual markets.

**Macroeconomics** studies the economy as a whole. It asks questions like: what causes recessions? Why do some countries grow faster than others? What determines the overall level of prices in an economy? Macroeconomics examines aggregates — total output, total employment, the average price level — rather than individual markets.

The two branches are connected. You cannot understand a recession (macro) without understanding why firms lay off workers (micro). You cannot understand inflation (macro) without understanding how individual prices are set (micro). The distinction is useful for organising thought, but the boundaries blur in practice.

## IB Exam Tip

In Paper 1 and Paper 2, the IB frequently tests the concept of opportunity cost. When you see a question about a choice between alternatives — a government choosing between building a hospital or a road, a student choosing between two degrees, a firm choosing between two investments — the examiner expects you to identify and calculate the opportunity cost. Always state the next-best alternative explicitly. Do not list every alternative; pick the single most valuable one given up.

A second common pitfall: students confuse scarcity (universal and permanent) with a shortage (a specific market condition). If the question is about why economics exists as a subject, the answer is scarcity, not a shortage.

## Practice Problems

**Problem 1.** A farmer owns a field. She can use it to grow wheat, which would earn her £5,000, or to graze cattle, which would earn her £4,000. She cannot do both. What is the opportunity cost of growing wheat? Write your answer as a full sentence.

**Problem 2.** A government has a budget surplus of £10 billion. It is considering two uses for this money: building a new high-speed rail line, which economists estimate would generate £12 billion in economic benefits, or upgrading the country's electricity grid, which would generate £9 billion in economic benefits. What is the opportunity cost of building the rail line? If the government wants to maximise the benefit from its spending, which project should it choose and why?

**Problem 3.** Classify each of the following as a factor of production: (a) A delivery van owned by a bakery. (b) Crude oil beneath the North Sea. (c) The person who founded a technology startup and designed its first product. (d) A cashier working at a supermarket. For each, state the factor payment the owner receives.

**Problem 4.** Identify whether each statement is positive or normative. (a) "The inflation rate in Argentina exceeded 200% in 2023." (b) "The government should prioritise reducing inflation over reducing unemployment." (c) "A tax on sugary drinks reduces consumption of those drinks by 10%." (d) "Taxes on sugary drinks are an unfair restriction on personal choice."

**Problem 5.** *(IB-style, 4 marks)* A student has three options for how to spend her Saturday evening. She can attend a revision session for her economics exam (she values this at £70 of benefit), go to a concert (she values this at £50), or work a shift at a café earning £45. (a) Define the term "opportunity cost." (2 marks) (b) Using the data provided, state the opportunity cost of attending the revision session and explain your answer. (2 marks)

**Problem 6.** A government is deciding how to allocate its education budget of £50 billion. It is considering: expanding university places (estimated benefit: £60 billion), reducing primary school class sizes (estimated benefit: £55 billion), or building new vocational training centres (estimated benefit: £48 billion). It can only afford one programme. (a) State the opportunity cost of choosing to expand university places. (b) The government instead decides to split the budget equally between reducing class sizes and building vocational centres. Explain what information you would need to determine the opportunity cost of this combined choice. (c) Is the government's budget constraint an example of scarcity or a shortage? Explain the distinction in your answer.

## Answers

**Answer 1.** The opportunity cost of growing wheat is the £4,000 the farmer would have earned from grazing cattle. Opportunity cost is the value of the next-best alternative forgone. Since the farmer can only use the field for one purpose, choosing wheat means giving up the cattle income, which is the single most valuable alternative.

**Answer 2.** The opportunity cost of building the rail line is the £9 billion in benefits that would have come from upgrading the electricity grid. To maximise benefit, the government should build the rail line, because its benefit (£12 billion) exceeds its opportunity cost (£9 billion). By contrast, choosing the electricity grid would mean giving up £12 billion in benefit for only £9 billion — a worse trade-off.

**Answer 3.** (a) The delivery van is capital. The factor payment is interest. (b) Crude oil beneath the North Sea is land. The factor payment is rent. (c) The founder who designed the first product is providing entrepreneurship. The factor payment is profit. (d) The cashier is providing labour. The factor payment is wages. A common pitfall: some students might classify the van as "land" because it is a physical object. But capital is anything manufactured and used to produce other goods. The van was built in a factory; it is capital.

**Answer 4.** (a) Positive. The statement makes a factual claim about Argentina's inflation rate that can be checked against data. (b) Normative. The word "should" signals a value judgment about priorities. (c) Positive. The statement makes a testable claim about the effect of a tax on consumption. (d) Normative. The word "unfair" is a value judgment; two people could disagree about whether the restriction is fair without either being factually wrong.

**Answer 5.** (a) Opportunity cost is the value of the next-best alternative that must be given up when a choice is made. It is not the sum of all forgone alternatives, only the single most valuable one among them. (2 marks — 1 mark for "next-best alternative," 1 mark for "value of what is given up.") (b) The opportunity cost of attending the revision session is £50 — the value of the concert. This is because the concert is the next-best alternative: the student values it at £50, which is more than the £45 she values the café shift. She gives up one alternative, and the concert is the most valuable of the two she forgoes. (2 marks — 1 mark for correctly identifying £50, 1 mark for explaining why the concert rather than the café shift.)

**Answer 6.** (a) The opportunity cost of expanding university places is the £55 billion in benefit from reducing primary school class sizes. This is the single most valuable alternative among the options not chosen. (b) To determine the opportunity cost of the combined choice — half the budget on class sizes, half on vocational centres — you would need to know the benefit of the best alternative use of the remaining £25 billion that was not spent on university expansion. The government chose not to spend anything on university expansion, so the opportunity cost includes whatever benefit the university expansion would have generated from the portion of the budget now allocated elsewhere. You would also need to confirm that the partial programmes (half-funded class size reduction and half-funded vocational centres) generate benefits in proportion to their funding, which may not be the case. (c) The budget constraint is an example of scarcity. Scarcity is the universal condition that resources are limited relative to unlimited wants — the government wants to fund multiple programmes but has only £50 billion, which is a fixed limit. A shortage, by contrast, is a specific market condition where, at a given price, quantity demanded exceeds quantity supplied. The government is not facing a price-driven market shortage; it is facing the fundamental economic problem of finite resources and infinite potential uses for them.

## Evaluate

**Question:** "Scarcity will eventually be eliminated by technological progress." Evaluate this statement.

**Model Answer:**

The claim that technological progress will eliminate scarcity rests on a misunderstanding of what scarcity means in economics. Scarcity is not about a physical lack of resources in absolute terms. It is about the relationship between unlimited human wants and limited resources. Technology can dramatically expand what we can produce with given resources — a single smartphone today replaces dozens of devices that would have been unimaginable a century ago — but it cannot eliminate the fact that wants expand alongside productive capacity. This is the core of the rebuttal.

Consider what has happened throughout economic history. The Industrial Revolution multiplied output per worker by orders of magnitude. Yet scarcity did not disappear. New wants emerged: air travel, medical treatments for diseases that had no name, higher education for the masses, computing power in every pocket. Each advance in productive capacity created new possibilities, and people adapted their wants accordingly. There is no evidence that this process has a terminus. Wants appear to be insatiable.

There is a narrower sense in which the statement has limited validity. Technology can eliminate scarcity for specific goods. Clean drinking water is scarce in some regions, but desalination and filtration technology can make it abundant in those same regions. If we define scarcity as the condition where a particular good has a positive price, technology can, for some goods, push the price to zero. Digital goods — a copy of a software program, a streamed song — have near-zero marginal cost. But this does not eliminate the scarcity of the underlying resources: the programmers' time, the servers' electricity, the rare earth minerals in the devices that access the content. Scarcity shifts location; it does not vanish.

From a stakeholder perspective, the claim affects different groups differently. For people in absolute poverty, technological progress in food production, medicine, and energy has indeed reduced the most severe consequences of scarcity — starvation, preventable disease, and exposure. This is a profound achievement. But for the median person in a developed economy, the experience of scarcity persists in the form of trade-offs: more leisure time versus more income, a larger home versus a better location, saving for retirement versus spending today.

The underlying assumption of the statement is that human wants are fixed. If they were — if people only ever wanted a specific list of things, and technology could eventually produce all of them — then scarcity might, in principle, be eliminated. But all observed evidence suggests that wants are not fixed. They grow and diversify as societies become richer. This is sometimes called the "hedonic treadmill": as people satisfy one set of desires, new ones emerge.

In conclusion, while technology can alleviate the worst consequences of scarcity and can make specific goods abundant, it cannot eliminate scarcity itself. Scarcity is a relational concept — wants relative to resources — and technological progress expands both sides of that relation. The fundamental economic problem is permanent, not because resources are fixed, but because wants are unbounded. A score of 7 in IB Economics requires recognising this distinction: scarcity is a definitional feature of the human condition, not a temporary inconvenience that progress will overcome.
