## 1. Market Equilibrium — Where Buyers and Sellers Agree

### What Market Equilibrium Is and Why It Matters

A market brings together buyers and sellers, each with their own plans. Buyers plan how much to purchase at each price, as captured by the demand curve. Sellers plan how much to offer at each price, as captured by the supply curve. **Market equilibrium** is the price-and-quantity pair at which these two plans exactly coincide. At the equilibrium price, the quantity that buyers are willing and able to purchase equals the quantity that sellers are willing and able to supply.

The word "equilibrium" comes from the Latin for "equal balance." It describes a state with no internal tendency to change. At the equilibrium price, every buyer who wants to buy can find a seller, and every seller who wants to sell can find a buyer. There are no frustrated buyers left searching for the good and no frustrated sellers left with unsold stock. Economists say the market "clears" — all trades that both sides want to make at that price are made.

Why does equilibrium matter? Because understanding equilibrium lets you predict where a market is headed when it is out of balance. If the current price is above equilibrium, the model predicts the price will fall. If the current price is below equilibrium, the model predicts the price will rise. This predictive power is the central achievement of the supply-and-demand framework.

The equilibrium price is denoted **Pe** and the equilibrium quantity is denoted **Qe**. At this singular point, Qd = Qs — quantity demanded equals quantity supplied.

A common mistake students make is thinking that equilibrium means everyone is happy with the price. In fact, equilibrium only means that the quantity buyers want to buy matches the quantity sellers want to sell. Some buyers would prefer a lower price and some sellers would prefer a higher price. Equilibrium is about the alignment of quantities, not the satisfaction of every participant's price preference.

### Worked Example 1: Finding Equilibrium Algebraically

A local market for takeaway coffee has the following demand and supply schedules:

Demand: Qd = 500 − 10P
Supply: Qs = −100 + 20P

where P is the price in pence per cup and Q is the number of cups per day.

**Approach:** To find the equilibrium, we need the single price at which the quantity demanded equals the quantity supplied. We set Qd equal to Qs and solve for P.

**Step 1:** Write the equilibrium condition.
Qd = Qs

**Step 2:** Substitute the equations.
500 − 10P = −100 + 20P

**Step 3:** Collect P terms on one side by adding 10P to both sides.
500 = −100 + 30P

**Step 4:** Add 100 to both sides.
600 = 30P

**Step 5:** Divide both sides by 30.
P = 20 pence per cup
This is the equilibrium price: **Pe = 20p**.

**Step 6:** Substitute Pe = 20 into either equation to find Qe.
Qd = 500 − 10(20) = 500 − 200 = 300 cups
Check using the supply equation: Qs = −100 + 20(20) = −100 + 400 = 300 cups. Both agree.

**Why this makes sense:** At a price of 20p per cup, the café wants to supply exactly 300 cups and customers want to buy exactly 300 cups. Every cup the café makes finds a buyer, and every customer who wants a cup at 20p gets one. No cups are wasted and no customers leave empty-handed.

### Visualising Equilibrium

On a graph with price on the vertical axis and quantity on the horizontal axis, the demand curve slopes downward and the supply curve slopes upward. Equilibrium sits at the intersection of the two curves. At any price above Pe, the supply curve lies to the right of the demand curve — sellers want to supply more than buyers want to buy. At any price below Pe, the demand curve lies to the right of the supply curve — buyers want to buy more than sellers want to supply. Only at Pe do the two curves cross.

## 2. Excess Demand — When the Price Is Too Low

### What Excess Demand Is

**Excess demand** occurs when the market price sits below the equilibrium price. At this lower price, the quantity demanded exceeds the quantity supplied. Buyers want to purchase more units than sellers are willing to make available. This situation is also called a **shortage**.

Suppose the government sets a price ceiling of £2 on a good whose equilibrium price is £4. At £2, buyers demand 800 units but sellers supply only 300 units. The gap — 800 − 300 = 500 units — is the amount of excess demand. For those 500 unsatisfied buyers, the low price is meaningless because there is nothing to buy at that price.

### How the Market Corrects Excess Demand

In a free market, excess demand does not persist. Buyers who cannot find the good begin to offer higher prices to secure it. Sellers, observing that buyers are willing to pay more, raise their prices. As the price rises, two things happen simultaneously: the quantity demanded falls (buyers drop out or buy less, moving up along the demand curve) and the quantity supplied rises (sellers produce more, moving up along the supply curve). This process continues until the price reaches Pe, where Qd = Qs and the shortage vanishes.

Consider a market where the equilibrium price of a popular video game console is £400. A retailer prices it at £300. At £300, 5,000 customers want to buy the console but only 1,000 units are available. The shortage of 4,000 units creates queues outside the store. Some disappointed buyers offer £350 on resale websites. Other buyers offer £380. The retailer notices and raises the official price. As the price climbs toward £400, some buyers decide the console is not worth the higher price and leave the market, while the manufacturer ships more units to capture the higher profit margin. At £400, the 2,500 units the manufacturer supplies exactly match the 2,500 units buyers demand. The shortage has been eliminated by the price mechanism, without any government planner needing to intervene.

A common mistake students make is thinking that a shortage means no goods are available at all. A shortage means that at the current price, demand exceeds supply — goods are available, but not enough to satisfy everyone who wants to buy at that price.

## 3. Excess Supply — When the Price Is Too High

### What Excess Supply Is

**Excess supply** occurs when the market price sits above the equilibrium price. At this higher price, the quantity supplied exceeds the quantity demanded. Sellers offer more units than buyers are willing to purchase. This situation is also called a **surplus**.

Suppose a clothing retailer prices winter coats at £150, but the equilibrium price is £100. At £150, the retailer supplies 600 coats but buyers demand only 200 coats. The surplus is 600 − 200 = 400 coats. These coats sit unsold in the stockroom, tying up the retailer's money in inventory that generates no revenue.

### How the Market Corrects Excess Supply

Sellers with unsold goods face a problem: inventory costs money to store and may become worthless if fashion or season changes. To clear their stock, sellers begin to lower prices. As the price falls, two things happen: the quantity demanded rises (buyers who thought the good was too expensive at the higher price now enter the market, moving down along the demand curve) and the quantity supplied falls (sellers who cannot make a profit at the lower price stop producing, moving down along the supply curve). This process pushes the price down toward Pe until Qd = Qs.

In the clothing example, the retailer marks coats down to £120 in a January sale. At £120, demand rises to 350 coats and supply falls to 450 — the surplus shrinks to 100 units. The retailer cuts further to £100, where demand of 400 coats matches supply of 400 coats. The surplus is gone. Every coat that was produced finds a buyer at exactly the price at which buyers and sellers agree on the right quantity.

The essential insight is that in a free market, neither shortages nor surpluses are permanent. The price adjusts — rising to eliminate shortages, falling to eliminate surpluses — until the quantity demanded and quantity supplied are equal. This self-correcting property is what makes the market mechanism a powerful system for allocating scarce resources.

A common mistake students make is thinking that a surplus means nobody wants the good. A surplus means that at the current price, sellers want to sell more than buyers want to buy. The good is still wanted — just not at that price.

## 4. Double Shifts — When Both Demand and Supply Move

### Why Double Shifts Matter

Real markets rarely experience a shift in only demand or only supply. Events often affect both sides simultaneously. A heatwave might increase demand for ice cream while also raising the cost of dairy (a supply input). A recession might reduce demand for restaurant meals while cheap delivery technology increases the supply of food-delivery services.

When both curves shift, the effect on equilibrium price and quantity depends on two things: the direction of each shift and the relative size of each shift. The IB exam rewards students who can reason through both possibilities.

### Case 1: Demand Increases AND Supply Increases

If demand shifts right and supply shifts right, the equilibrium quantity definitely rises because both shifts push quantity in the same direction — more is bought and sold.

The effect on equilibrium price is ambiguous. If the demand increase is larger than the supply increase, the price rises. If the supply increase is larger than the demand increase, the price falls. If they are equal in magnitude, the price stays the same.

Consider the smartphone market. As more people want smartphones (demand shifts right) and manufacturing technology makes them cheaper to produce (supply shifts right), the quantity of smartphones sold rises sharply. The price may fall — as it has historically, with better phones becoming cheaper over time — because the supply increase (from technological progress) has likely exceeded the demand increase.

### Case 2: Demand Increases AND Supply Decreases

If demand shifts right and supply shifts left, the equilibrium price definitely rises because both shifts push the price upward.

The effect on equilibrium quantity is ambiguous. If the demand increase is larger, quantity rises. If the supply decrease is larger, quantity falls.

Consider the housing market in a growing city. Population growth shifts demand right. Simultaneously, restrictions on new construction shift supply left. The price of housing rises — this is unambiguous and matches what we observe in cities like London and San Francisco. Whether more or fewer homes are sold depends on whether the demand push or the supply constraint is stronger.

### Case 3: Demand Decreases AND Supply Increases

If demand shifts left and supply shifts right, the equilibrium price definitely falls because both shifts push the price downward.

The effect on equilibrium quantity is ambiguous. If the supply increase is larger, quantity rises. If the demand decrease is larger, quantity falls.

Consider the market for coal. Environmental regulations and a shift to renewable energy reduce demand (left shift). Simultaneously, improved mining technology increases supply (right shift). The price of coal falls steeply. Whether more or less coal is consumed depends on which force dominates.

### Case 4: Demand Decreases AND Supply Decreases

If demand shifts left and supply shifts left, the equilibrium quantity definitely falls because both shifts push quantity downward.

The effect on equilibrium price is ambiguous. If the demand decrease is larger, price falls. If the supply decrease is larger, price rises.

Consider the market for live theatre during a pandemic. Incomes fall, reducing demand for entertainment (left shift). Simultaneously, venues close, reducing supply (left shift). Fewer shows are seen — quantity falls. The price could go either way: if demand falls more dramatically than supply, ticket prices drop; if supply collapses faster than demand falls, remaining tickets become expensive.

### Decision Table for Double Shifts

| Demand | Supply | Price (Pe) | Quantity (Qe) |
|--------|--------|------------|---------------|
| ↑ Right | ↑ Right | Ambiguous | ↑ Increases |
| ↑ Right | ↓ Left | ↑ Increases | Ambiguous |
| ↓ Left | ↑ Right | ↓ Decreases | Ambiguous |
| ↓ Left | ↓ Left | Ambiguous | ↓ Decreases |

A common mistake students make is trying to memorise this table without understanding why each cell contains what it does. Ask yourself: "Does this shift push price up or down? Does the other shift push it the same way or opposite?" If both push price the same way, the result is definite. If they push in opposite directions, the result is ambiguous and depends on which shift is larger.

## 5. The Price Mechanism — Three Functions of Prices

### What the Price Mechanism Is and Why It Matters

The **price mechanism** is the process by which prices in a free market coordinate the independent decisions of millions of buyers and sellers. No central planner tells the farmer how much wheat to grow or the baker how many loaves to bake. Prices do the coordinating. Adam Smith, the founder of modern economics, called this the "invisible hand" — the idea that individuals pursuing their own self-interest, guided by price signals, produce outcomes that serve the broader society.

The price mechanism performs three distinct but interrelated functions. Every price change in a market economy can be understood through at least one of these three lenses.

### Function 1: Signalling

Prices **signal** information about relative scarcity and desirability. A rising price is a signal that says: "This good is becoming scarcer relative to demand — produce more of it and consume less of it." A falling price signals the opposite: "This good is becoming more abundant relative to demand — shift resources away from producing it."

When the price of lithium surged from £8,000 per tonne in 2020 to over £60,000 per tonne in 2022, this did not happen in a vacuum. The price increase signalled to mining companies worldwide that lithium had become far more valuable — driven by demand for electric vehicle batteries. Mining firms did not need a government directive to expand exploration; the price signal was enough. Simultaneously, battery manufacturers saw the same signal and began research into sodium-based alternatives that could bypass lithium entirely. Prices communicated information that no single person or agency could have gathered and distributed.

### Function 2: Incentive

Prices create **incentives** that change behaviour. A higher price incentivises producers to increase output because each unit sold now generates more profit. It also incentivises consumers to reduce consumption or switch to cheaper substitutes because each unit purchased now costs more of their limited income.

When the global price of coffee beans doubled in 2010, Brazilian farmers had a powerful incentive to plant more coffee trees on land that had previously grown soybeans. A coffee farmer does not need to care about global supply and demand statistics; the higher price alone provides the motivation to act. On the consumer side, British shoppers facing higher coffee prices had an incentive to switch to tea or to buy supermarket-brand instant coffee instead of premium ground beans. Prices align private incentives with the social need — in this case, the need to produce more coffee and consume less of it until the shortage is resolved.

### Function 3: Rationing

Prices **ration** scarce goods among competing uses and users. When a good is in limited supply, its price rises until the number of buyers willing to pay that price exactly matches the limited quantity available. Those unwilling or unable to pay are rationed out of the market. This may sound harsh, but it is a mechanism for allocating goods to those who value them most — as measured by their willingness to pay.

Suppose a stadium holds 20,000 seats and 50,000 people want to attend a concert. If tickets are priced at £40, excess demand is 30,000. Through dynamic pricing or resale markets, the ticket price rises. At £120, some people decide the concert is not worth that much and drop out. At £200, more drop out. The price climbs until only 20,000 people are willing to pay — these are the people for whom the concert provides at least £200 worth of enjoyment. The price has rationed the 20,000 seats among the 50,000 people who wanted them, and it has done so without a lottery, a queue, or a government allocation scheme.

### How the Three Functions Work Together

Consider a drought that destroys half the coffee harvest in Vietnam, the world's largest robusta producer:

First, the **signalling function** activates: the price of coffee rises sharply in commodity markets, telling everyone — from Vietnamese farmers to German roasters — that coffee has become scarce.

Second, the **incentive function** takes over: Brazilian and Colombian farmers, seeing the higher price, have an incentive to expand production next season and to invest in irrigation to protect against future drought. Coffee drinkers have an incentive to switch to smaller cups, cheaper blends, or alternative beverages.

Third, the **rationing function** allocates the diminished harvest: the higher price ensures that the limited beans go to buyers — roasters, cafés, consumers — who value them most, as demonstrated by their willingness to pay the higher price.

These three functions operate without any central coordination. The price does all the work.

A common mistake students make is confusing the signalling and incentive functions. Signalling is about communicating information — the price says "coffee is scarce." Incentive is about changing behaviour — the higher price makes farmers want to plant more and consumers want to drink less. Signalling describes what the price says; incentive describes what people do in response.

## 6. Efficiency of the Price Mechanism

Under a set of ideal conditions — perfect competition, perfect information, no externalities, rational consumers — the free market equilibrium is **allocatively efficient**. This means that resources cannot be rearranged to make anyone better off without making someone else worse off (a condition economists call Pareto efficiency).

At equilibrium, the price a consumer pays for the last unit reflects exactly the marginal benefit that consumer receives from that unit. At the same time, that price equals the marginal cost the producer incurred to make that unit. So at equilibrium, marginal social benefit (MSB) equals marginal social cost (MSC). Every unit for which the benefit to society is greater than the cost of producing it gets produced and consumed. Every unit for which the cost exceeds the benefit does not.

This is an elegant result, but it depends on conditions that are frequently absent in real markets. When a factory pollutes a river, the market price does not include the cost of pollution — the marginal social cost exceeds the private cost — and too much of the polluting good is produced. When a consumer lacks information about the safety of a product, the demand curve does not reflect true preferences. These "market failures" are the subject of later lessons.

For now, the key takeaway is that the price mechanism is a remarkably powerful coordination device — but it works well only under specific conditions. The craft of economic policy lies in knowing when to trust prices and when to supplement them with regulation, taxation, or public provision.

## IB Exam Tip

When analysing equilibrium changes in an IB exam, follow this six-part structure:

1. Identify the initial equilibrium — label Pe₁ and Qe₁
2. Name the shock — which curve shifts, in which direction, and which determinant (from TRIBE for demand or PINTS for supply) explains why
3. Draw the shift — show the new curve with a clear label (D₂ or S₂)
4. Locate the new equilibrium — label Pe₂ and Qe₂
5. State the outcome — "Pe rises, Qe rises" or whatever applies
6. If the shift is a double shift, state your assumption about relative magnitudes when the outcome is ambiguous

One more crucial distinction: "an increase in demand" means the entire demand curve shifts right. "An increase in quantity demanded" means movement along the same demand curve as price changes. The IB exam expects precise language — mixing these up loses marks.

**Answer 1.** (a) Set Qd = Qs: 400 − 8P = −80 + 12P → 480 = 20P → Pe = £24. Substitute into demand: Qd = 400 − 8(24) = 400 − 192 = 208. Check with supply: Qs = −80 + 12(24) = −80 + 288 = 208. So Qe = 208,000 units. (b) At P = £30: Qd = 400 − 8(30) = 400 − 240 = 160. Qs = −80 + 12(30) = −80 + 360 = 280. Qs (280) exceeds Qd (160), so there is excess supply — a surplus — of 280 − 160 = 120,000 units. (c) At P = £20: Qd = 400 − 8(20) = 400 − 160 = 240. Qs = −80 + 12(20) = −80 + 240 = 160. Qd (240) exceeds Qs (160), so there is excess demand — a shortage — of 240 − 160 = 80,000 units.

**Answer 2.** (a) The supply curve shifts left (a decrease in supply). The PINTS determinant is "S — supply shocks and input costs." The cold snap is a natural supply shock that destroys productive capacity. At every price, fewer strawberries are available. (b) At the original price of £3, the quantity demanded is still 10,000 punnets (the demand curve has not moved), but the quantity supplied has fallen — perhaps to 7,000 punnets. This creates a shortage of 3,000 punnets. (c) The shortage means frustrated buyers who cannot find strawberries at £3. Some buyers offer £3.50 to secure a punnet. Sellers, seeing that buyers are willing to pay more, raise their prices. As the price rises, quantity demanded falls (some buyers decide strawberries are not worth the higher price) and quantity supplied rises along the new, higher supply curve (though it will still be below the original 10,000 because the curve has shifted left). The price rises until it reaches the new equilibrium — say, £4 — where the reduced quantity supplied equals the reduced quantity demanded.

**Answer 3.** (a) Demand shifts right (increase — taste/preference for electric transport). Supply shifts right (increase — lower input costs from cheaper batteries). (b) Equilibrium quantity definitely rises. Both shifts push quantity in the same direction. The demand increase means consumers want to buy more at every price. The supply increase means producers want to sell more at every price. With both forces aligned, Qe unambiguously increases. (c) The effect on equilibrium price is ambiguous. The demand increase pushes price up (more buyers chasing the good). The supply increase pushes price down (cheaper to produce, so sellers accept lower prices). To determine whether Pe rises or falls, we would need to know whether the rightward shift of demand is larger or smaller than the rightward shift of supply. If demand expands more than supply, price rises. If supply expands more than demand, price falls. If they are equal, price stays the same.

**Answer 4.** (a) Demand shifts left, supply shifts right. Price: definitely falls (both push price down). Quantity: ambiguous (demand left pushes Q down, supply right pushes Q up — the net effect depends on which shift is larger). (b) Demand shifts right, supply shifts left. Price: definitely rises (both push price up). Quantity: ambiguous (demand right pushes Q up, supply left pushes Q down — depends on relative sizes). (c) Demand shifts left, supply shifts left. Quantity: definitely falls (both push Q down). Price: ambiguous (demand left pushes P down, supply left pushes P up — depends on relative sizes).

**Answer 5.** (a) The signalling function operates when the higher wheat price communicates information about relative scarcity to all market participants. At the old price, the market would show a massive shortage — more wheat is demanded than exists. The price rises, and this price increase is itself the signal: it tells farmers worldwide that wheat has become unusually scarce and valuable, and it tells bakers, food manufacturers, and consumers that wheat is now more costly relative to other goods. No government report or central planner needs to deliver this message; the price does it automatically and instantly. (3 marks) (b) The incentive function operates by changing behaviour in response to the price signal. On the producer side, farmers in regions unaffected by the drought — in Argentina, Australia, Europe — now have an incentive to plant more wheat in the next growing season, to use more fertiliser to boost yields, and to bring marginal land into cultivation. The higher price makes these actions profitable when they were not before. On the consumer side, bread manufacturers have an incentive to find cheaper flour blends, to reduce portion sizes, or to pass the cost to consumers and accept lower sales volume. Households have an incentive to reduce bread waste and to switch some consumption to rice or potatoes, which have not risen in price. Each agent acts in their own self-interest, but the collective effect moves the market toward a new balance. (4 marks) (c) The rationing function allocates the diminished harvest to those buyers who value wheat most. At the old, lower price, there was not enough wheat to satisfy all willing buyers. As the price rises, buyers drop out in order of their willingness to pay. A premium bakery that can pass costs to affluent customers stays in the market. A livestock farmer using wheat as animal feed may switch to corn and exit the wheat market. A biscuit manufacturer with thin margins may reduce production. The process continues until the quantity demanded at the new, higher price equals the quantity supplied. The wheat has been rationed not by government decree nor by queue, but by price — and it flows to the uses that generate the most value per grain. (3 marks)

## Evaluate

**Question:** "The free market price mechanism is the best way to allocate scarce resources." Evaluate this statement.

**Model Answer:**

The statement asserts that the price mechanism — the system by which prices coordinate the decisions of buyers and sellers without central direction — is superior to all other methods of resource allocation. This claim deserves careful scrutiny because it is partly true, partly false, and entirely dependent on context.

The case for the price mechanism is strong. Prices aggregate information that no central planner could gather. When the price of a good rises, it simultaneously tells producers to make more and consumers to use less, without anyone needing to understand why the good has become scarce. The mechanism is decentralised, fast, and automatic. Under ideal conditions — perfect competition, perfect information, and the absence of externalities — the equilibrium reached by a free market is allocatively efficient. Resources flow to their most valued uses, and every unit that is worth more to consumers than it costs to produce gets made. This is an elegant and genuinely powerful result. The collapse of centrally planned economies in the twentieth century is a testament to the difficulty of replacing prices with bureaucratic allocation.

However, the statement's assertion that the price mechanism is "the best way" ignores the conditions under which it fails. There are at least four major classes of failure.

First, externalities. When a producer pollutes a river or a consumer's smoking harms bystanders, the market price does not reflect the full social cost or benefit. The equilibrium quantity is wrong — too much of the polluting good, too little of the beneficial one. The price mechanism, left to itself, systematically misallocates resources in the presence of externalities.

Second, public goods. Goods that are non-excludable and non-rival — national defence, street lighting, scientific research — will not be provided in sufficient quantity by a free market because individuals can free-ride on others' contributions. The price mechanism fails entirely for this class of goods.

Third, inequality. The price mechanism allocates goods to those who can pay, not to those who need them. A market that efficiently distributes life-saving medicine to the highest bidder may be allocatively efficient in the narrow economic sense, but many people would judge it to be morally unacceptable. The statement implicitly treats efficiency as the only criterion for "best," ignoring equity.

Fourth, imperfect information and market power. When sellers know more than buyers, or when a single firm dominates a market, prices no longer reflect true marginal costs and benefits. The price mechanism produces the wrong quantity at the wrong price.

The statement also ignores the role of time. In the short run, prices can be volatile, overshooting and undershooting equilibrium and causing unnecessary hardship — farmers going bankrupt during a price dip, consumers priced out of essentials during a spike. In the long run, markets tend to self-correct, but as John Maynard Keynes observed: "In the long run we are all dead." The speed of adjustment matters.

From a stakeholder perspective, the price mechanism serves different groups differently. Well-informed consumers with high incomes thrive in a market system. Poor consumers, future generations (who cannot bid in today's markets), and workers in declining industries fare less well. A judgment about what is "best" requires asking: best for whom?

The most defensible position is a pragmatic one. The price mechanism should be the default allocation device for most private goods because it is informationally efficient and decentralised. But it must be supplemented by government intervention — Pigouvian taxes for pollution, public provision for public goods, regulation for information asymmetries, and redistribution for equity — where it demonstrably fails. The question is not whether markets or governments allocate resources, but under what conditions each does better. Every successful modern economy is a mixed economy that uses both.

In conclusion, the statement is too absolute to be correct. The price mechanism is a remarkable and often superior allocation device, but it is not universally the best. A society that relies exclusively on markets will produce too much pollution, too few lighthouses, and too much inequality. A society that relies exclusively on planning will produce too little innovation and too much inefficiency. The art of economic policy is knowing which tool to use when.
