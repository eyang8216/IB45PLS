## 1. What an Indirect Tax Is and Why Governments Use It

### What an Indirect Tax Is

An **indirect tax** is a tax levied on spending — on the purchase of goods and services. It is called "indirect" because the tax is collected by an intermediary (the seller) from the person who ultimately bears the economic burden (the consumer). This contrasts with a **direct tax**, such as income tax, which is levied directly on the person who pays it.

Every time you buy a chocolate bar, a litre of petrol, or a train ticket, a portion of the price you pay is indirect tax. The shop collects it from you and passes it to the government. The shop is the collector; you are the bearer.

### Two Types of Indirect Tax

Economists classify indirect taxes into two types based on how the tax amount is calculated.

A **specific tax** (also called a per-unit tax or excise duty) is a fixed amount of tax charged per physical unit of the good, regardless of its price. For example, the UK charges a specific tax of approximately £0.53 per litre on petrol. Whether petrol sells for £1.40 or £1.60 per litre, the tax per litre is the same. The total tax collected depends only on the number of units sold, not on their value.

An **ad valorem tax** is a tax calculated as a percentage of the selling price. Value Added Tax (VAT) is the most prominent example. In the UK, the standard VAT rate is 20%. If a restaurant meal costs £30 before tax, the VAT is £30 × 0.20 = £6, and the total price is £36. If the meal costs £50, the VAT is £10. The tax per unit rises as the price rises.

### Why Governments Impose Indirect Taxes

Governments impose indirect taxes for four main reasons.

First, indirect taxes raise government revenue. VAT alone accounts for roughly 20% of all tax revenue in the UK — approximately £160 billion per year. This revenue funds public services: schools, hospitals, roads, defence.

Second, indirect taxes can discourage consumption of goods considered harmful. High taxes on tobacco, alcohol, and sugary drinks raise their prices and reduce the quantity people buy. This is sometimes called a "sin tax."

Third, indirect taxes can correct negative externalities. When the consumption or production of a good imposes costs on third parties — such as pollution from petrol — a tax can make the private cost reflect the social cost. This type of tax is called a **Pigouvian tax**, named after the economist Arthur Pigou.

Fourth, indirect taxes can be used to redistribute income, at least in principle. Taxing luxury goods more heavily than necessities shifts the tax burden toward those with higher incomes, though the practical redistributive effect is often limited.

A common mistake students make is thinking that an indirect tax and a specific tax are the same thing. A specific tax is one type of indirect tax. An ad valorem tax is another. Both are indirect taxes. The distinction between specific and ad valorem is about how the tax is calculated — as a fixed amount per unit or as a percentage of price.

## 2. How an Indirect Tax Affects the Market

### Why the Supply Curve Shifts

When the government imposes a specific tax of £3 per unit on a good, producers face a new reality. For every unit they sell, they must send £3 to the government. This means that to be willing to supply the same quantity as before, producers now need to receive a market price that is £3 higher — enough to cover their production costs and the tax.

Equivalently, at any given market price, producers are now willing to supply fewer units because the effective price they keep (market price minus tax) is lower. This is a decrease in supply — the supply curve shifts upward (or leftward) by exactly the amount of the tax.

For a specific tax, the shift is parallel: the vertical distance between the original supply curve and the new supply curve is the same at every quantity, because the tax per unit is constant.

For an ad valorem tax, the shift is not parallel. The supply curve pivots: the gap between original and new supply grows larger as the price rises, because the tax is a percentage of the price.

### Key Prices and Quantities

Before a tax, the market settles at equilibrium (Pe, Qe) where supply equals demand.

After a tax of £t per unit is imposed:

- The new equilibrium quantity is **Qt**, which is lower than Qe because the tax raises the cost.
- The price consumers pay is **Pc**, read from the demand curve at Qt. This is higher than Pe.
- The price producers receive is **Pp**, which equals Pc minus the tax. Producers pay the tax to the government, so they keep only Pp out of the Pc consumers pay. Pp is lower than Pe.
- The tax per unit is the vertical distance Pc − Pp, which equals £t.

A common mistake students make is thinking that consumers pay the entire tax. This is almost never true. The burden of an indirect tax is shared between consumers and producers. Consumers pay more than before (Pc > Pe), but producers receive less than before (Pp < Pe). The split depends on elasticities.

## 3. Tax Incidence — Who Bears the Burden?

### What Tax Incidence Means

**Tax incidence** refers to how the burden of a tax is divided between consumers and producers. The consumer burden per unit is Pc − Pe (the amount by which the consumer price rises). The producer burden per unit is Pe − Pp (the amount by which the producer's net price falls). The two burdens sum to the tax: (Pc − Pe) + (Pe − Pp) = Pc − Pp = £t.

### The Role of Elasticity

The division of the tax burden depends entirely on the relative price elasticities of demand and supply. The side of the market that is less responsive to price changes — the more inelastic side — bears the larger share of the tax.

When demand is inelastic, consumers do not reduce their purchases much when the price rises. This means producers can pass most of the tax forward to consumers in the form of higher prices. The consumer burden is large; the producer burden is small. Cigarettes are the textbook example: addicted smokers have inelastic demand, so tobacco taxes fall overwhelmingly on consumers.

When demand is elastic, consumers reduce their purchases sharply when the price rises. Producers cannot pass much of the tax forward because doing so would cause sales to collapse. Instead, producers absorb most of the tax by accepting a lower net price. The producer burden is large; the consumer burden is small. Luxury goods with many substitutes illustrate this case.

When supply is inelastic, producers cannot easily reduce the quantity they supply. They must absorb most of the tax themselves because they cannot escape the market. Short-run housing supply is inelastic — the number of flats available for rent cannot change quickly — so a tax on rental income falls mainly on landlords.

When supply is elastic, producers can easily adjust their quantity. If a tax is imposed, they reduce supply, the price rises, and consumers bear most of the burden.

A useful rule: **the more inelastic side of the market bears the greater burden of the tax.** Whichever side finds it harder to walk away from the transaction ends up paying more.

A common mistake students make is thinking that the government decides who bears the tax by choosing whether to collect it from sellers or buyers. In economic terms, the legal liability (who sends the cheque to the government) is irrelevant to the economic incidence (who actually bears the burden). A tax on sellers shifts the supply curve; a tax on buyers would shift the demand curve. The economic outcome — how the burden is split — is the same in either case. This is a fundamental result in tax economics.

## 4. Government Revenue and Deadweight Loss

### Tax Revenue

**Government tax revenue** is the total amount the government collects from the tax. It equals the tax per unit multiplied by the quantity sold after the tax is imposed: Revenue = £t × Qt.

Graphically, tax revenue is the rectangle with height £t (the vertical distance between the new supply curve and the original supply curve, or between Pc and Pp) and width Qt (the new quantity sold). This rectangle lies between Pc and Pp, bounded by the vertical axis and Qt.

### Deadweight Loss

**Deadweight loss (DWL)** from a tax is the welfare that is destroyed — not transferred, but genuinely lost — because the tax reduces the quantity traded below the allocatively efficient level.

At the original equilibrium Qe, marginal social benefit (MSB) equals marginal social cost (MSC). Every unit for which the consumer's willingness to pay exceeds the producer's marginal cost is traded, maximising total welfare.

After the tax, only Qt units are traded. For the units between Qt and Qe, consumers value the good at more than the marginal cost of producing it — yet these units are not produced and consumed because the tax has driven a wedge between the buyer's price and the seller's price. The potential gains from these forgone transactions are lost forever. This is the DWL.

Graphically, the DWL is the triangle bounded by the demand curve (above) and the original supply curve (below), between Qt and Qe. It is not government revenue — that is the rectangle. The DWL is the area of the triangle that is captured by no one.

A common mistake students make is confusing tax revenue with deadweight loss. Tax revenue is a transfer from market participants to the government. The government can use it to provide public services. DWL is a genuine destruction of value that benefits nobody. When evaluating a tax, both the revenue gained and the welfare lost must be considered.

## 5. Real-World Examples

### UK Sugar Tax (Soft Drinks Industry Levy, 2018)

In April 2018, the UK introduced a specific tax on sugar-sweetened beverages. Drinks with more than 8g of sugar per 100ml are taxed at £0.24 per litre; those with 5-8g per 100ml are taxed at £0.18 per litre; those below 5g per 100ml are exempt. The tax was designed not just to raise revenue but to encourage reformulation — and it worked. Many manufacturers, including Coca-Cola and Pepsi, reduced the sugar content of their drinks to fall below the threshold. By 2020, the total sugar sold in soft drinks had fallen by approximately 35%, even as sales volumes remained stable. The tax raised approximately £300 million per year, which was channelled into school sports and breakfast clubs.

### Mexico Soda Tax (2014)

Mexico introduced a 1 peso per litre tax (approximately 10% of the price) on sugar-sweetened beverages in 2014. Purchases fell by an average of 6-12% in the first two years, with larger reductions among lower-income households — the group most responsive to price and also most affected by obesity-related diseases. The policy was closely studied worldwide as evidence that sugar taxes can reduce consumption.

### Tobacco Taxes

Tobacco taxes are among the most studied and most effective public health interventions. The World Health Organization considers tobacco taxation the single most cost-effective way to reduce smoking. Australia has some of the world's highest tobacco taxes — a pack of 25 cigarettes can cost over AUD 50, with taxes accounting for approximately 70% of the retail price. Australian adult daily smoking rates fell from 24% in 1991 to approximately 10.7% in 2021. The tax exploits the fact that while demand for cigarettes is inelastic in the short run, it becomes more elastic over time as people quit, and it deters uptake among young people who are more price-sensitive.

## IB Exam Tip

When drawing an indirect tax diagram in an IB exam:

- Draw the original supply (S) and demand (D) curves. Label Pe and Qe.
- Shift the supply curve upward to S + tax. For a specific tax, the shift is parallel. For an ad valorem tax, the new supply curve pivots and is steeper.
- The new equilibrium is at Qt. Read Pc from the demand curve at Qt. Read Pp from the original supply curve at Qt.
- Label consumer burden (Pc − Pe), producer burden (Pe − Pp), the tax per unit (Pc − Pp), tax revenue (rectangle between Pc and Pp), and DWL (triangle between Qt and Qe).
- If the question mentions a Pigouvian tax, explicitly state that the tax internalises the externality by making MPC shift to equal MSC.

A common error: drawing the supply curve shifting downward for a tax. A tax is an additional cost, so supply decreases — the curve shifts upward (or leftward). Subsidies shift supply downward; taxes shift it upward.

## Worked Example 1: Calculating Tax Effects

The market for a good is described by: Qd = 200 − 5P and Qs = −40 + 10P, where P is in pounds and Q is in units. The government imposes a specific tax of £6 per unit on producers.

**Problem:** (a) Find the pre-tax equilibrium. (b) Find the new supply equation after the tax. (c) Find the new equilibrium price (Pc) and quantity (Qt). (d) Calculate Pp, consumer burden per unit, producer burden per unit, government revenue, and DWL.

**Approach:** To model a specific tax on producers, we rewrite the supply equation with (P − t) in place of P, because producers only keep the price minus the tax. Then we solve the new system. For DWL, we calculate the area of the triangle between Qe and Qt.

**Step 1 — Pre-tax equilibrium:**
Set Qd = Qs: 200 − 5P = −40 + 10P
240 = 15P
P = £16
Q = 200 − 5(16) = 200 − 80 = 120 units (check: −40 + 10(16) = −40 + 160 = 120 ✓)

**Step 2 — New supply equation after a £6 tax:**
Producers receive (P − 6) per unit. Substitute into the original supply:
Qs = −40 + 10(P − 6) = −40 + 10P − 60 = −100 + 10P

**Step 3 — New equilibrium:**
Set Qd = Qs new: 200 − 5P = −100 + 10P
300 = 15P
P = £20 — this is Pc, the price consumers pay.
Qt = 200 − 5(20) = 200 − 100 = 100 units

**Step 4 — Pp and burdens:**
Pp = Pc − tax = £20 − £6 = £14 (producers receive £14 per unit after paying the £6 tax)
Consumer burden per unit = Pc − Pe = £20 − £16 = £4
Producer burden per unit = Pe − Pp = £16 − £14 = £2
Check: £4 + £2 = £6 (equals the tax ✓)
Consumers bear 2/3 of the tax; producers bear 1/3.

**Step 5 — Revenue and DWL:**
Government revenue = tax × Qt = £6 × 100 = £600
DWL = ½ × (Qe − Qt) × (tax per unit) = ½ × (120 − 100) × £6 = ½ × 20 × £6 = £60

**Why this makes sense:** Demand is Qd = 200 − 5P, supply is Qs = −40 + 10P. The slope of demand (−5) is steeper than supply (10) in absolute terms, meaning demand is relatively more inelastic than supply in this linear model. The more inelastic side — consumers — bears the larger burden (£4 vs £2). The DWL of £60 represents the lost gains from the 20 units no longer traded.

## Worked Example 2: Tax Incidence and Elasticity

Consider two markets. Market A: demand is Qd = 100 − 2P, supply is Qs = 20 + 2P. Market B: demand is Qd = 100 − 10P, supply is Qs = 20 + 2P. A specific tax of £5 is imposed in each market.

**Problem:** Calculate consumer and producer burden in each market and explain why they differ.

**Approach:** For each market, find pre-tax equilibrium, then post-tax equilibrium with the supply shifted by £5, then compute burdens.

**Market A — Pre-tax:**
100 − 2P = 20 + 2P → 80 = 4P → Pe = £20, Qe = 60.
After £5 tax: supply becomes Qs = 20 + 2(P − 5) = 20 + 2P − 10 = 10 + 2P.
100 − 2P = 10 + 2P → 90 = 4P → Pc = £22.50, Qt = 100 − 45 = 55.
Pp = £22.50 − £5 = £17.50.
Consumer burden = £22.50 − £20 = £2.50. Producer burden = £20 − £17.50 = £2.50.
Burden split 50/50.

**Market B — Pre-tax:**
100 − 10P = 20 + 2P → 80 = 12P → Pe = £6.67, Qe ≈ 33.3.
After £5 tax: supply becomes Qs = 20 + 2(P − 5) = 10 + 2P.
100 − 10P = 10 + 2P → 90 = 12P → Pc = £7.50, Qt = 100 − 75 = 25.
Pp = £7.50 − £5 = £2.50.
Consumer burden = £7.50 − £6.67 = £0.83. Producer burden = £6.67 − £2.50 = £4.17.
Burden split: consumers ~17%, producers ~83%.

**Why this makes sense:** In Market A, demand and supply have equal slope magnitudes — neither side is more elastic. The burden splits evenly. In Market B, demand is much flatter (more elastic) — consumers are very responsive to price. When the tax is imposed, producers cannot pass much of it forward because consumers would dramatically reduce purchases. Producers absorb most of the tax (£4.17 out of £5). The more elastic side of the market escapes the bulk of the burden.

## Answers

**Answer 1.** A specific tax is a fixed amount of tax charged per physical unit of the good, regardless of the selling price. For example, the UK charges approximately £0.53 in fuel duty per litre of petrol. On a diagram, a specific tax shifts the supply curve upward by a constant vertical amount — the shift is parallel. An ad valorem tax is calculated as a percentage of the selling price. For example, the UK's standard VAT rate is 20% — the tax on a £100 item is £20. On a diagram, an ad valorem tax causes the supply curve to pivot upward, becoming steeper. The vertical gap between the original and new supply curves grows larger as the price increases because the tax is a percentage.

**Answer 2.** (a) Set Qd = Qs: 500 − 10P = −100 + 20P. Then 500 + 100 = 20P + 10P, so 600 = 30P. Pe = £20. Qe = 500 − 10(20) = 500 − 200 = 300 units. (Check: −100 + 20(20) = −100 + 400 = 300. ✓)

(b) With a £9 tax, producers receive (P − 9) per unit. Substitute into supply: Qs = −100 + 20(P − 9) = −100 + 20P − 180 = −280 + 20P.

(c) Set Qd = Qs new: 500 − 10P = −280 + 20P. Then 500 + 280 = 20P + 10P, so 780 = 30P. Pc = £26. Qt = 500 − 10(26) = 500 − 260 = 240 units. Pp = Pc − tax = £26 − £9 = £17.

(d) Consumer burden per unit = Pc − Pe = £26 − £20 = £6. Producer burden per unit = Pe − Pp = £20 − £17 = £3. Check: £6 + £3 = £9 ✓. Government tax revenue = tax × Qt = £9 × 240 = £2,160. DWL = ½ × (Qe − Qt) × tax = ½ × (300 − 240) × £9 = ½ × 60 × £9 = £270.

**Answer 3.** The diagram should show: original supply (S) and demand (D) intersecting at (Pe, Qe). A new parallel supply curve (S + tax) lies above the original by exactly the tax amount. The new equilibrium is at the intersection of S + tax and D, giving Qt. Reading vertically: Pc is on the demand curve above Qt; Pp is on the original supply curve below Qt. Consumer burden is the vertical segment Pc − Pe. Producer burden is the vertical segment Pe − Pp. Tax revenue is the rectangle with height (Pc − Pp) and width Qt. DWL is the triangle bounded by D (above) and original S (below), between Qt and Qe.

DWL arises because the tax reduces the quantity traded from the allocatively efficient level Qe to Qt. For each unit between Qt and Qe, the consumer's willingness to pay (shown by the demand curve) exceeds the producer's marginal cost (shown by the original supply curve). These units would have generated net benefits for society. The tax prevents these transactions from occurring, and the value of the forgone gains is the DWL. Neither consumers, producers, nor the government capture this value — it is genuinely lost.

**Answer 4.** For cigarettes with highly inelastic demand, consumers bear most of the tax burden. Inelastic demand means that when the price rises due to a tax, smokers reduce their cigarette consumption by only a small proportion. Producers can therefore pass almost all of the tax forward to consumers in the form of higher prices. The price consumers pay rises almost by the full amount of the tax, while the price producers receive falls only slightly.

For restaurant meals with relatively elastic demand, producers bear most of the tax burden. Elastic demand means that when the price rises, diners significantly reduce the number of meals they eat out. If restaurants tried to pass the tax forward by raising prices, they would lose many customers. Instead, restaurants are forced to absorb most of the tax themselves, accepting a lower net price per meal. The price consumers pay rises by only a small amount, while the price restaurants receive falls substantially.

The underlying principle: the more inelastic side of any market bears the greater share of any tax. Inelastic demanders are "trapped" in the market and cannot escape the tax; elastic demanders can walk away, forcing suppliers to absorb it.

**Answer 5.** (a) The diagram should show original supply and demand, then supply shifted upward by the tax. (3 marks) After the tax is imposed, the supply curve shifts upward, raising the price consumers pay to Pc and reducing the quantity traded to Qt. Producers receive Pp, which is Pc minus the tax. The tax drives a wedge between the consumer and producer prices. The quantity falls because the higher consumer price reduces quantity demanded. (1 mark for correct diagram with labels; 1 mark for distinguishing Pc and Pp; 1 mark for explaining the quantity reduction.)

(b) Two reasons the tax might be less effective than expected: First, if demand is highly inelastic — as with addictive goods like cigarettes — the quantity reduction from a given tax is small. Consumers may simply pay the higher price rather than reduce consumption significantly. Second, the tax may stimulate black market activity and smuggling. If consumers can buy the good untaxed through illegal channels, the official sales data will show a reduction, but actual consumption may fall much less than the data suggest. High tobacco taxes in some countries, for example, have led to significant cross-border smuggling. (3 marks: 1 mark for first reason with explanation, 1 mark for second reason with explanation, 1 mark for using economic concepts such as elasticity or black markets.)

**Answer 6.** (a) A specific tax of £5 per unit shifts the supply curve upward by exactly £5 at every quantity. The new supply curve is parallel to the original. An ad valorem tax of 20% causes the supply curve to pivot upward. At low prices, the gap between original and new supply is small (20% of a small number). At high prices, the gap is large (20% of a large number). The new supply curve is steeper than the original — it diverges from the original as the price rises.

(b) Pc = £28, Pe = £25, Qt = 80, Qe = 100, tax = £5. Consumer burden per unit = £28 − £25 = £3. Producer burden per unit: Pp = Pc − tax = £28 − £5 = £23. Producer burden = Pe − Pp = £25 − £23 = £2. Check: £3 + £2 = £5 ✓. Tax revenue = £5 × 80 = £400.

(c) An ad valorem tax raises more revenue than a specific tax of the same initial magnitude when prices rise over time due to inflation or quality improvements. A specific tax of £5 per unit stays at £5 regardless of whether the good's price rises from £25 to £50. An ad valorem tax of 20% grows from £5 per unit to £10 per unit as the price doubles. In markets where the pre-tax price is rising — such as luxury goods, property, or goods in an inflationary economy — an ad valorem tax automatically captures more revenue. Additionally, for high-value variants of a good, an ad valorem tax raises more revenue per unit than a specific tax, which may be desirable if the goal is to make the tax progressive (higher tax on more expensive versions).

## Evaluate

**Question:** "Indirect taxes are regressive and should be replaced with direct taxes." Evaluate this statement.

**Model Answer:**

The statement makes a claim about the fairness of indirect taxes and proposes a radical reform: replacing all indirect taxes with direct taxes. While indirect taxes are indeed regressive when considered in isolation, the proposal to eliminate them entirely ignores the important advantages that indirect taxes bring to a tax system. A more balanced assessment recognises both the regressive nature of indirect taxes and the efficiency and behavioural functions they serve.

The case that indirect taxes are regressive is well-founded. A regressive tax is one that takes a larger percentage of income from lower-income households than from higher-income households. Consider VAT at 20%. When a low-income family earning £20,000 per year and a high-income family earning £100,000 per year each buy a £1,000 refrigerator, both pay £200 in VAT. The £200 represents 1% of the lower-income family's earnings but only 0.2% of the higher-income family's earnings. Because lower-income households spend a larger share of their income on consumption — and a smaller share on savings — a uniform consumption tax falls more heavily on them as a proportion of income. Direct taxes, by contrast, can be made progressive: higher-income earners pay a higher percentage of their income in income tax, which aligns with the principle of vertical equity — the idea that those with greater ability to pay should contribute a larger share.

However, the claim that indirect taxes should be replaced entirely with direct taxes overlooks several critical advantages of indirect taxation. First, indirect taxes are harder to evade. Every transaction in a formal economy generates a tax liability that is automatically collected by the seller. Direct taxes on income, by contrast, require reporting and can be evaded through under-declaration, offshore accounts, or working in the informal economy. In developing countries with large informal sectors, indirect taxes are often the only reliable source of government revenue.

Second, indirect taxes do not discourage work or savings in the way that high marginal income tax rates can. A person deciding whether to work an extra hour faces no additional indirect tax penalty — the VAT rate is the same regardless of how many hours they work. High income tax rates, by contrast, reduce the reward for additional effort and can distort labour supply decisions. Similarly, indirect taxes do not penalise savings, since they fall on consumption rather than income. This makes indirect taxes more favourable to economic growth in the long run, all else equal.

Third, indirect taxes can be targeted at specific goods to correct negative externalities. A tax on tobacco reduces smoking and the associated public health costs. A tax on carbon emissions addresses climate change. A tax on sugar helps combat obesity. Direct taxes cannot achieve this behavioural targeting — an income tax does not distinguish between money spent on cigarettes and money spent on vegetables.

Fourth, indirect taxes provide a more stable revenue base over the business cycle. Consumption is less volatile than income — people still need to eat, heat their homes, and buy essentials during a recession, even as their incomes fall. Income tax revenues, by contrast, are highly cyclical, falling sharply during downturns precisely when governments need revenue for stimulus and social safety nets.

The regressivity of indirect taxes can be mitigated without eliminating them. Many countries zero-rate or exempt basic necessities: the UK does not charge VAT on most food, children's clothing, books, or public transport. This reduces the effective VAT rate on the consumption basket of low-income households. Targeted transfers, such as means-tested benefits or tax credits, can compensate low-income households for the burden of indirect taxes. Nordic countries combine high VAT rates (25% in Denmark, Norway, and Sweden) with extensive social transfers and progressive income taxes, achieving both high revenue and relatively equitable outcomes.

Turning to stakeholders: low-income households are the most burdened by regressive indirect taxes, particularly if necessities are taxed. High-income households benefit from lower direct tax rates if the tax mix shifts toward indirect taxation. Governments value the reliable revenue, low administrative cost, and externality-correcting potential of indirect taxes. Producers of demerit goods, such as tobacco and alcohol companies, are harmed by targeted high indirect taxes. Workers in the formal economy benefit from the relative efficiency of consumption taxes compared to higher income taxes that might otherwise be levied.

In conclusion, the statement correctly identifies the regressive nature of indirect taxes but draws an excessively stark policy conclusion. The optimal tax system is a mixed one: progressive direct taxes on income and wealth provide equity; broad-based indirect taxes on consumption provide efficiency, stability, and administrative simplicity; and higher indirect taxes on demerit goods and goods with negative externalities serve behavioural objectives. The challenge is not to choose between direct and indirect taxes but to design a system that balances equity, efficiency, and revenue adequacy. Replacing all indirect taxes with direct taxes would sacrifice important advantages without necessarily achieving a fairer outcome, since the burden of the higher direct taxes required to replace the lost revenue would itself create new distortions and inequities.
