## 1. Types of Poverty

### Absolute Poverty

**Absolute poverty** refers to a condition where individuals **cannot meet their most basic needs** — food, clean water, shelter, clothing, and basic healthcare.

**World Bank Definition**: Living on less than a specified daily income threshold. As of 2022, the **international poverty line** is **$2.15 per day** (PPP-adjusted, 2017 prices). Previously $1.90/day (2011 prices) and before that $1.25/day (2005 prices).

**Characteristics**:
- Objective, measurable threshold (unlike relative poverty)
- Universal — applies the same standard everywhere
- Life-threatening — malnutrition, preventable disease, exposure
- Concentrated in low-income countries (Sub-Saharan Africa, South Asia)

**Worked Example**: A family of four in rural Ethiopia earning a combined $3.00/day lives below the $2.15/person/day threshold (combined threshold = $8.60/day). They are in absolute poverty.

### Relative Poverty

**Relative poverty** is defined in relation to the **prevailing living standards** of a particular society. It is about being unable to participate fully in normal social and economic life.

**Common Definition**: Household income below **60%** (EU) or **50%** (OECD) of the **median** equivalized household income.

**Characteristics**:
- Subjective — changes as society becomes richer
- Can never be fully eliminated (by definition, unless income is completely equal)
- Concentrated in all countries, including rich ones
- About social exclusion, not survival

**Worked Example**: Median household income in Country X is $40,000. The relative poverty threshold (60%) = $24,000. A family earning $22,000 falls below this threshold. They are in relative poverty — they have food and shelter but cannot afford vacations, extracurricular activities for children, or internet access that peers have. They are socially excluded.

### Key Distinction Table

| Feature | Absolute Poverty | Relative Poverty |
|---|---|---|
| Definition | Below a fixed threshold of basic needs | Below a % of median income |
| Universal? | Yes — same line everywhere | No — varies by country |
| Can be eliminated? | Yes (in theory) | No (by construction, if any inequality exists) |
| Primary concern in | Low-income countries | All countries, especially developed |
| Measurement | $2.15/day (World Bank) | 50–60% of median income |

## 2. Causes of Poverty

### Individual-Level Causes
- **Unemployment**: No labor income — most direct cause
- **Low wages**: Working poor — employed but still below poverty line
- **Lack of education/skills**: Low human capital → low productivity → low wages
- **Poor health/disability**: Cannot work or incur catastrophic health expenses
- **Discrimination**: Based on race, gender, ethnicity, caste → reduced opportunities
- **Family structure**: Single-parent households with dependent children

### Structural Causes
- **Economic dualism**: Coexistence of a modern, high-productivity sector and a traditional, low-productivity sector. Workers trapped in the latter.
- **Geographic isolation**: Rural areas lacking infrastructure, markets, schools
- **Lack of access to credit**: Cannot borrow to invest in education, business, or land
- **Asset inequality**: Land, capital concentrated among few → returns to assets flow to the already-rich

### Institutional Causes
- **Weak property rights**: Poor cannot secure title to land → cannot use as collateral
- **Corruption**: Resources diverted from public services; bribes required for basic services
- **Regressive tax systems**: Heavy reliance on VAT/consumption taxes that fall disproportionately on the poor
- **Inadequate public services**: Poor education and healthcare perpetuate the poverty cycle
- **Conflict and instability**: War destroys assets, disrupts education, displaces populations

### The Poverty Trap (Poverty Cycle)

Low income → poor nutrition/health → low productivity → low income (reinforcing loop)

Low income → cannot afford education → low skills → low-wage employment → low income

**Breaking the cycle requires external intervention** — the market alone cannot solve it because poverty itself prevents the investments needed to escape.

## 3. Impacts of Poverty

### Economic Impacts
| Impact | Explanation |
|---|---|
| **Reduced economic growth** | Poor cannot invest in human capital → lower aggregate productivity → slower LRAS growth |
| **Lower social mobility** | Children of poor parents remain poor → talent wasted → less innovation and dynamism |
| **Higher fiscal costs** | Government spends on emergency healthcare, crime, welfare instead of productive investment |
| **Market inefficiency** | Large segment of population cannot participate as consumers → smaller market, less competition |

### Social Impacts
| Impact | Explanation |
|---|---|
| **Crime and social unrest** | Desperation → theft, violence; inequality breeds resentment → political instability |
| **Poor health outcomes** | Malnutrition, untreated illness → lower life expectancy, higher infant mortality |
| **Reduced educational attainment** | Hungry children cannot learn; poor families pull children out of school to work |
| **Erosion of social cohesion** | Deep divisions between rich and poor → loss of trust, community breakdown |

## 4. Policies to Reduce Poverty

### A. Progressive Taxation

**How it works**: Higher-income earners pay a **larger proportion** of their income in tax. Tax revenue funds public services and transfers.

**Example of progressive structure**:

| Income Bracket | Tax Rate |
|---|---|
| $0–$10,000 | 0% (tax-free allowance) |
| $10,001–$40,000 | 15% |
| $40,001–$100,000 | 25% |
| $100,001+ | 40% |

Someone earning $25,000 pays: 0% on first $10K + 15% on $15K = $2,250 → average rate = 9%. Someone earning $150,000 pays: $0 + $4,500 + $15,000 + $20,000 = $39,500 → average rate = 26.3%.

**Advantages**: Reduces post-tax inequality directly; funds public goods that disproportionately benefit the poor.

**Disadvantages**: May reduce work incentives (workers keep less of each additional dollar earned); capital flight if rates are too high relative to other countries.

### B. Transfer Payments

**Cash transfers**: Direct payments to low-income households.
- **Means-tested**: Only those below a threshold receive (efficient but creates poverty trap — losing benefits as income rises discourages work)
- **Universal**: Everyone receives (no stigma, no poverty trap, but expensive)

**Conditional cash transfers (CCTs)**: Transfers conditional on behaviors like school attendance or health clinic visits. Example: Brazil's Bolsa Família — payments to poor families if children attend school and get vaccinated.

**In-kind transfers**: Provision of goods/services directly (food stamps, public housing, free school meals) rather than cash. Ensures spending on basics but limits recipient choice.

### C. Minimum Wage Laws

**How it works**: Government sets a legal floor on hourly wages.

**Potential benefit**: Raises income of the working poor above poverty line.

**Potential risks**:
- If set too high → firms hire fewer workers → unemployment among low-skilled
- Benefits may not reach poorest (who are unemployed)
- Compliance issues in informal sectors

**The debate**: The effect on employment depends on the level. A moderate minimum wage (40–50% of median wage) shows little employment effect in most empirical studies. A very high minimum wage can cause disemployment.

### D. Investment in Education and Healthcare

**Education**: Free, quality public education → human capital accumulation → breaks the poverty cycle across generations. Particularly important: early childhood education (highest returns) and female education (reduces fertility, improves child health).

**Healthcare**: Universal or subsidized healthcare → prevents medical bankruptcy, improves worker productivity, keeps children in school.

**Long-term nature**: These are "supply-side" anti-poverty policies — they don't provide immediate relief but address root causes permanently.

### E. Anti-Discrimination Legislation

Enforce equal opportunity in employment, education, housing. Addresses poverty concentrated among marginalized groups.

## 5. The Equity-Efficiency Trade-Off

A fundamental tension in anti-poverty policy:

- **Equity goal**: Redistribute from rich to poor → reduce poverty and inequality
- **Efficiency concern**: Redistribution may reduce incentives to work, save, invest, and innovate → smaller total output to redistribute

### The "Leaky Bucket" Metaphor (Arthur Okun)
Transferring money from rich to poor is like carrying water in a leaky bucket — some is lost in the process (administrative costs, reduced work incentives, deadweight loss of taxation). The question is: how leaky is the bucket, and how much we value the water that reaches the poor?

### Evidence
- Moderate redistribution (Nordic model: Gini ~0.25–0.30, high taxes, generous welfare) can coexist with high economic performance
- Extreme redistribution (communist central planning) demonstrably destroys incentives and produces poverty for all
- The optimal point is somewhere between laissez-faire and full equality

### Policy Design to Minimize the Trade-Off
- **Pro-work transfers**: Earned Income Tax Credit (US) — supplements low wages rather than replacing them
- **Investment over consumption**: Spend on education/healthcare (builds capacity) rather than pure consumption transfers
- **Universal Basic Services**: Provide healthcare, education, housing directly rather than cash — less distortionary

---

## 6. Evaluating Anti-Poverty Policies

| Policy | Strengths | Weaknesses | Best For |
|---|---|---|---|
| Progressive taxation | Funds redistribution, reduces post-tax inequality | Work disincentives, capital flight | All countries (with appropriate rates) |
| Cash transfers | Direct, immediate poverty relief | Poverty trap, fiscal cost | Short-term relief, vulnerable groups |
| Conditional cash transfers | Breaks poverty cycle (education + income) | Administrative complexity, paternalistic | Long-term poverty reduction |
| Minimum wage | Raises working poor income | Potential unemployment, doesn't reach non-workers | Countries with large formal sector |
| Education investment | Permanent, intergenerational benefits | Takes 10–20 years to show results | Long-term poverty elimination |
| Healthcare investment | Prevents medical poverty, improves productivity | Expensive, requires institutional capacity | All countries (as resources allow) |

## Answers

### Answer 1
**Absolute poverty**: Inability to meet basic needs (food, water, shelter). Measured by the World Bank's international poverty line of $2.15/day (PPP). It can theoretically be eliminated because it's defined against a **fixed** threshold — if everyone's income rises above this line, absolute poverty is zero.

**Relative poverty**: Income below a percentage of median income (typically 50–60%). It **cannot** be eliminated because it's defined relative to others — as long as there is ANY income inequality, some people will be below 60% of the median. If everyone's income doubles, absolute poverty may disappear, but relative poverty (by the 60% median definition) remains unchanged. Relative poverty measures social exclusion, not survival.

### Answer 2
**1. Unemployment (individual cause)**: When individuals lack jobs, they have no labor income. In countries without adequate unemployment benefits, this directly causes poverty. Structural unemployment — mismatch between skills and available jobs — is particularly pernicious because it's long-lasting.

**2. Lack of access to credit (structural cause)**: Poor individuals cannot borrow to invest in education, start a business, or buy productive assets. Without collateral, banks won't lend. This "credit constraint" traps the poor in low-productivity activities. Microfinance (e.g., Grameen Bank) attempts to address this.

**3. Weak public education (institutional cause)**: When public schools are underfunded, teacher quality is poor, and dropout rates are high, children from poor families receive inadequate education. This limits their future productivity and earnings, perpetuating intergenerational poverty. The government's failure to provide quality public goods is an institutional failure.

### Answer 3

**(a) Income = $30,000**:
- 0% on first $12,000 = $0
- 15% on $12,001–$30,000 = 0.15 × $18,000 = $2,700
- Total tax = $2,700
- Average tax rate = $2,700 / $30,000 = **9.0%**

**(b) Income = $200,000**:
- 0% on first $12,000 = $0
- 15% on $12,001–$50,000 = 0.15 × $38,000 = $5,700
- 30% on $50,001–$150,000 = 0.30 × $100,000 = $30,000
- 40% on $150,001–$200,000 = 0.40 × $50,000 = $20,000
- Total tax = $0 + $5,700 + $30,000 + $20,000 = **$55,700**
- Average tax rate = $55,700 / $200,000 = **27.85%**

**Burden comparison**: The $200,000 earner pays a much higher average rate (27.85% vs 9.0%). This is the definition of progressivity — the average rate rises with income. In absolute terms: $55,700 vs $2,700. The higher earner bears a larger burden both absolutely and proportionally.

### Answer 4

**How CCTs break the poverty cycle**:

Conditional cash transfers address poverty on two fronts simultaneously:
1. **Immediate relief**: The cash transfer raises current household income, alleviating immediate material hardship (hunger, inability to buy school supplies).
2. **Long-term investment**: The conditions (school attendance, health checkups, vaccinations) ensure children accumulate human capital, breaking the intergenerational transmission of poverty.

**Example — Brazil's Bolsa Família** (now Auxílio Brasil):
- Poor families receive monthly payments if children attend at least 85% of school days and receive regular vaccinations and health checkups
- Covers ~14 million families (about 25% of Brazil's population)
- Studies show: increased school enrollment, reduced dropout rates, improved child nutrition, reduced income inequality (Brazil's Gini fell from ~0.59 to ~0.53 during the program's peak years)

**Limitation**:
- **Administrative complexity**: Requires robust systems to verify school attendance and health visits. In countries with weak institutional capacity, monitoring and enforcement may be impossible. Fraud (ghost beneficiaries) is a risk.
- **Paternalism critique**: The government is dictating behavior to the poor — some argue unconditional cash transfers respect recipient autonomy more.
- **Supply-side constraints**: Requiring school attendance is only effective if quality schools are accessible. In remote areas without schools, the condition is meaningless or cruel.

### Answer 5

**Context**: Current minimum wage = $10, new = $15. Equilibrium wage = $11. This means the $15 minimum is **binding** — it's above the market-clearing wage.

**Poverty effects**:
- **Gainers**: Workers who keep their jobs at $15/hour see a 50% wage increase ($10→$15). If working full-time (2,000 hours/year), annual income rises from $20,000 to $30,000. Some may cross the poverty line.
- **Losers**: Workers who lose their jobs due to the higher wage. Firms reduce hiring or substitute toward capital/automation.

**Employment effects** (using elasticity = −0.3):
- Wage increase = ($15 − $11) / $11 = 36.4% (from equilibrium, not the existing minimum)
- %Δ employment ≈ −0.3 × 36.4% = **−10.9%** (approximately)
- This is a significant but not catastrophic reduction in low-skilled employment

**Overall welfare assessment**:
- Total wage bill = wage × employment. If employment falls less than wages rise, the total wage bill increases.
- Workers who remain employed are clearly better off.
- Workers who lose jobs are clearly worse off (especially without a strong safety net).
- Consumers may face slightly higher prices (firms pass through costs).
- Firms' profits fall (higher labor costs).

**Nuance**: The $10 minimum was already binding ($10 < $11 equilibrium wage), so some workers were already above the minimum. The increase to $15 affects more workers. The elasticity of −0.3 is relatively inelastic — this is plausible if firms have limited substitution options in the short run but more in the long run.

**Conclusion**: The wage increase likely reduces poverty among employed low-wage workers but may increase poverty among those who lose employment. The net effect is ambiguous without knowing the size of each group and the availability of unemployment support. Complementary policies (job training, earned income tax credits) would improve outcomes.

### Answer 6 (Full IB-Style Response)

**Introduction**: Progressive taxation (higher rates on higher incomes) combined with cash transfers (direct payments to low-income households) represents a direct fiscal approach to redistribution. The statement claims this is the "most effective" way — a strong claim requiring evaluation against alternative anti-poverty strategies.

**Arguments Supporting the Statement**:
1. **Immediate impact**: Cash transfers provide instant poverty relief. Unlike education or infrastructure investment (which take years), a transfer today means food on the table today.
2. **Targetable**: Means-tested transfers can be directed precisely at those below the poverty line, maximizing poverty reduction per dollar spent.
3. **Respects recipient autonomy**: Cash allows poor households to decide what they need most — unlike in-kind transfers (food, housing) that may not match actual needs.
4. **Proven results**: Programs like the US Earned Income Tax Credit, Brazil's Bolsa Família, and European child benefits have demonstrated significant poverty reduction in rigorous evaluations.

**Arguments Against the Statement (Alternative Approaches)**:
1. **Supply-side solutions address root causes**: Investment in education, healthcare, and infrastructure permanently increases the productive capacity of the poor, enabling self-sufficiency. Tax-and-transfer treats symptoms; education cures the disease.
2. **Poverty traps from means-testing**: As recipients earn more, they lose benefits — effectively a high marginal tax rate on earnings. This discourages work and traps people in dependency. A person earning $1 more might lose $0.70 in benefits → effective marginal rate of 70%.
3. **Political sustainability**: Highly redistributive tax systems face political opposition. High taxes may trigger capital flight, brain drain, and reduced investment — shrinking the tax base and the resources available for redistribution.
4. **Minimum wage as an alternative**: Raises the income of the working poor without fiscal cost. However, it doesn't help the unemployed poor.
5. **Inclusive growth**: Policies that make growth itself more broad-based (financial inclusion, property rights for the poor, anti-discrimination laws) may be more sustainable than ex-post redistribution.

**The Best Approach Is Typically a Mix**:
- Short-term: Cash transfers for immediate relief
- Medium-term: Progressive taxation to fund public services (education, healthcare)
- Long-term: Supply-side policies to increase the earning capacity of the poor
- Structural: Anti-discrimination, property rights, financial inclusion

**Conclusion**: Progressive taxation with cash transfers is a powerful and direct tool for reducing poverty, particularly in the short run. However, "most effective" depends on the time horizon and the nature of poverty. For immediate survival needs, cash transfers are indeed most effective. For permanently eliminating poverty across generations, supply-side investments in human capital are essential. The truly "most effective" approach combines both — using fiscal redistribution to address immediate hardship while investing in education, health, and opportunity to make redistribution eventually unnecessary.

## Evaluate

**"Progressive taxation combined with cash transfers is the most effective way to reduce poverty."**

### CLASPP Analysis (180 words)

**Conclusion**: The statement is valid in the short run but incomplete as a long-term strategy. Tax-and-transfer systems provide immediate, direct poverty relief and are among the most effective tools for addressing *current* poverty. However, permanently eliminating poverty requires complementary supply-side policies that address root causes.

**Long-term vs Short-term**: In the short term, cash transfers immediately raise incomes above poverty thresholds — no other policy works faster. In the long term, education, healthcare, and economic opportunity investments are superior because they enable self-sufficiency rather than perpetual dependence. The most effective approach spans both time horizons.

**Assumptions**: The statement assumes administrative capacity to implement progressive taxation and means-tested transfers without significant leakage or corruption. In countries with weak institutions (large informal sector, limited tax collection capacity), this assumption fails. It also assumes political sustainability of redistribution.

**Stakeholders**: The poor benefit directly from transfers. Taxpayers (particularly high-income) bear the cost. Future generations benefit if transfers are coupled with human capital investment but bear debt if funded by deficits. Workers near benefit thresholds face perverse incentives.

**Priorities**: If immediate survival is at stake (absolute poverty), cash transfers are indeed the priority. If relative poverty and social mobility are the concerns, education and opportunity creation take priority.

**Pros/Cons**: Pro — fast, targeted, respects autonomy, empirically validated. Con — treats symptoms, creates dependency risks, politically fragile, administrative complexity.

**Overall**: Essential component of anti-poverty strategy, but not sufficient alone.
