Lesson 14: Indirect Taxes

What You'll Learn

  • The difference between specific (per-unit) and ad valorem (percentage) taxes
  • How to draw indirect tax diagrams and show the supply curve shift
  • How to determine consumer burden and producer burden (tax incidence)
  • How to calculate government tax revenue and deadweight loss
  • Why governments impose indirect taxes and how to evaluate them

1. Types of Indirect Taxes

An indirect tax is a tax on spending (goods and services), collected by an intermediary (the seller) from the person who bears the tax burden (the consumer).

Specific (Per-Unit) Tax

A fixed amount per unit of the good, regardless of price.

  • Example: $2 per pack of cigarettes, $0.50 per litre of petrol.
  • The supply curve shifts upward by the exact tax amount — parallel shift.
  • The vertical distance between the old and new supply curves = tax per unit.

Ad Valorem (Percentage) Tax

A percentage of the selling price.

  • Example: 20% VAT, 10% GST.
  • The supply curve pivots upward — the gap between old and new supply grows larger as price rises.

2. Why the Supply Curve Shifts

When a specific tax of $t is imposed:

  • Producers must receive the original supply price (Ps) to be willing to supply each quantity.
  • But consumers must now pay Ps + t.
  • Therefore, at each quantity, the new supply curve lies $t above the original.

The supply curve shifts upward/leftward — representing a decrease in supply (higher cost of production).

3. The Indirect Tax Diagram

Price ↑ | S + tax | /| | / | Pc|---/--|---- Price consumers pay | / | Pe|--/----|---- Original equilibrium | / | Pp|-/-----|---- Price producers receive |/ | |-------|---------------- D | | +-------+-----------------→ Quantity Qt Qe
  • Pc = price paid by consumers (new equilibrium price)
  • Pp = price received by producers (Pc − tax)
  • Qt = new equilibrium quantity (lower than Qe)
  • Consumer burden = Pc − Pe
  • Producer burden = Pe − Pp

4. Tax Incidence — Who Bears the Burden?

ConditionConsumer BurdenProducer Burden
Demand is inelastic (PED < 1)Larger shareSmaller share
Demand is elastic (PED > 1)Smaller shareLarger share
Supply is inelastic (PES < 1)Smaller shareLarger share
Supply is elastic (PES > 1)Larger shareSmaller share
Rule: The more inelastic side of the market bears the greater burden of the tax.

5. Government Revenue and Deadweight Loss

Government revenue = tax per unit × Qt

This is the rectangle bounded by Pc, Pp, and Qt.

Deadweight Loss (DWL) is the welfare loss triangle between Qt and Qe, bounded by the original supply and demand curves. DWL arises because the tax reduces output below the allocatively efficient level (Qe, where MB = MC).

6. Why Governments Impose Indirect Taxes

  1. Revenue generation — efficient way to raise government funds.
  2. Reduce consumption of demerit goods — tobacco, alcohol, sugar.
  3. Correct negative externalities — make the polluter pay (Pigouvian tax).
  4. Redistribution — taxing luxury goods more heavily.

7. Real-World Examples

  • UK Sugar Tax (2018): Specific tax on sugar-sweetened beverages — reduced sugar content in drinks as firms reformulated.
  • Mexico Soda Tax (2014): 1 peso per litre tax — consumption fell ~6–12% in first two years.
  • Tobacco Taxes: Among the most effective demand-reduction tools. Australia's high tobacco taxes have contributed to declining smoking rates.
  • Fuel Duties: Raise revenue and aim to reduce driving-related externalities.

IB Exam Tip

When drawing an indirect tax diagram:
• Draw original S and D, label Pe and Qe.
• Shift S upward by the tax amount (parallel for specific tax).
• Label Pc and Pp clearly.
• Show consumer burden, producer burden, tax revenue rectangle, and DWL triangle.
• For ad valorem tax, the new S pivots — steeper than original.

Practice Problems

Problem 1 (Basic Identification)

Distinguish between a specific tax and an ad valorem tax, giving one example of each.

Problem 2 (Diagram)

The market for a good has equilibrium at P = $10, Q = 100. The government imposes a specific tax of $3 per unit. After the tax, Pc = $12 and Qt = 80.
(a) Find Pp (price received by producers).
(b) Calculate consumer burden per unit and producer burden per unit.
(c) Draw a fully labelled diagram.

Problem 3 (Tax Revenue and DWL)

Using data from Problem 2:
(a) Calculate government tax revenue.
(b) Identify the DWL on your diagram. Explain why it arises.

Problem 4 (Elasticity and Incidence)

Explain why a tax on cigarettes falls mainly on consumers, while a tax on luxury yachts falls mainly on producers. Use elasticity concepts.

Problem 5 (Ad Valorem Tax)

A 20% ad valorem tax is imposed on a good that sells for $50 at equilibrium.
(a) Would the new supply curve shift parallel or pivot? Explain.
(b) If after the tax, Pc = $54 and Qt = 90 (from Qe = 100), calculate the tax per unit, consumer burden, and producer burden.

Problem 6 (Challenging / IB-Style)

"Governments should use indirect taxes rather than direct taxes to raise revenue." Evaluate this statement using economic arguments.

Answers

Answer 1

  • Specific tax: A fixed amount per unit sold (e.g., $2 per pack of cigarettes). The supply curve shifts upward by a constant vertical amount.
  • Ad valorem tax: A percentage of the sale price (e.g., 20% VAT). The supply curve pivots so the gap between old and new supply increases as price increases.

Answer 2

(a) Pp = Pc − tax = $12 − $3 = $9.

(b) Consumer burden per unit = Pc − Pe = $12 − $10 = $2. Producer burden per unit = Pe − Pp = $10 − $9 = $1. Total = $2 + $1 = $3 ✓

(c) Diagram: Original S and D intersect at (Pe=$10, Qe=100). New S+tax parallel shift up by $3. New equilibrium: Pc=$12, Qt=80. Pp=$9 on original S. Tax revenue = $3 × 80 = $240 rectangle. DWL = triangle between Qt=80 and Qe=100.

Answer 3

(a) Tax revenue = tax per unit × Qt = $3 × 80 = $240.

(b) DWL arises because the tax reduces quantity from the allocatively efficient level (Qe=100) to Qt=80. The 20 units between Qt and Qe represent transactions where MB > MC — consumers valued these units more than the cost of producing them, but the tax prevented the trades.

Answer 4

  • Cigarettes: Demand is highly inelastic (addictive, few substitutes). Consumers cannot easily reduce consumption, so they absorb most of the tax.
  • Luxury yachts: Demand is highly elastic (many substitutes, luxury good). Consumers reduce purchases significantly. Producers must absorb much of the tax to maintain sales.

Answer 5

(a) The supply curve pivots (not parallel shift). The tax amount grows as price increases — the vertical gap between old and new supply increases at higher prices.

(b) Tax per unit at Qt = 20% × $54 = $10.80. Pp = $54 − $10.80 = $43.20. Consumer burden = $54 − $50 = $4. Producer burden = $50 − $43.20 = $6.80.

Answer 6

Model answer outline:

  • Arguments for indirect taxes: Easier to collect, less distortionary (people choose whether to consume), can target demerit goods/externalities, less disincentive to work/save.
  • Arguments against: Regressive (larger share of low incomes), can be inflationary, may be ineffective if demand is inelastic.
  • Conclusion: The optimal tax mix includes both — indirect taxes for efficiency and externality correction, direct taxes for equity and redistribution.

Evaluate

"Indirect taxes are regressive and should be replaced with direct taxes." Evaluate.

Model Answer (CLASPP Structure)

Conclusion: While indirect taxes are indeed regressive, replacing them entirely with direct taxes would sacrifice important efficiency and behavioural objectives. A mixed tax system is preferable.

Logic (Arguments For the Statement): Indirect taxes like VAT and sales taxes are regressive because they take a larger percentage of income from lower-income households than from higher-income ones. A poor family and a rich family pay the same VAT on a litre of milk, but that amount represents a much larger share of the poor family's budget. Direct taxes (income tax) can be made progressive — higher rates on higher incomes — achieving vertical equity. Relying on indirect taxes therefore worsens income inequality and places disproportionate burden on those least able to pay.

Logic (Arguments Against): However, indirect taxes have important advantages. First, they are harder to evade — collected at every transaction, whereas income can be hidden. Second, they do not discourage work or savings the way high income taxes can, reducing deadweight loss in labour markets. Third, indirect taxes can be used to correct negative externalities (Pigouvian taxes on tobacco, carbon, sugar) — direct taxes cannot target specific harmful consumption. Fourth, they provide a stable revenue base — consumption is less volatile than income over the business cycle.

Assumptions: The statement assumes that equity (fairness) should be the overriding tax principle. However, tax systems balance equity, efficiency, simplicity, and revenue adequacy. It also assumes the poor consume the same mix of goods — but exemptions or zero-rating of necessities (food, medicine) can make indirect taxes less regressive.

Stakeholders: Low-income households suffer most from regressive indirect taxes. High-income households benefit from lower direct tax rates if the burden shifts. Governments need reliable revenue — indirect taxes are a crucial source, especially in developing countries with large informal sectors. Producers in demerit good industries are harmed by targeted indirect taxes.

Prescribed Solutions: Rather than replacement, the optimal approach is a mixed tax system with: (1) progressive direct taxes for equity, (2) indirect taxes with exemptions for basic necessities to reduce regressivity, and (3) higher indirect taxes on demerit goods and goods with negative externalities to improve social outcomes. This balances equity and efficiency.

Precedent/Evidence: Nordic countries combine high progressive income taxes with high VAT (~25%) but provide strong social transfers that offset regressivity. The UK zero-rates food and children's clothing for VAT. These examples show that indirect taxes can be part of an equitable overall fiscal system when designed carefully.

Thus, indirect taxes are regressive in isolation, but the statement's prescription is too extreme — the real question is how to design the tax system as a whole to be both fair and efficient.
