Lesson 29: Shifts in AS and Comparing Monetarist vs Keynesian Models

What You'll Learn

  • How LRAS shifts affect an economy
  • How the same AD shock has different effects in the monetarist and Keynesian models
  • How to compare the two schools of thought across multiple dimensions
  • Why the debate between these models still matters for policy today

1. What Happens When LRAS Shifts

What LRAS Shifts Are and Why They Matter

In both the monetarist and Keynesian models, the long-run aggregate supply curve can shift to the right. When LRAS shifts right, an economy's potential output — the maximum it can produce when all resources are fully employed — increases. This is the definition of potential economic growth: an increase in the productive capacity of the economy, not just a temporary boost in spending.

A rightward shift of LRAS matters because it is the only way to achieve sustained, non-inflationary growth. When AD increases, output may rise in the short run, but in the monetarist model, the economy eventually returns to its original potential output at a higher price level. When LRAS shifts right, both output and price stability can improve simultaneously.

What causes LRAS to shift? The same factors in both models:

  • An increase in the quantity of factors of production: more workers (through population growth or immigration), more capital (through investment in machinery and buildings), more land brought into productive use.
  • An increase in the quality of factors: better education and training (human capital), technological progress, improved health of the workforce.
  • Improvements in efficiency: better institutions, stronger property rights, reduced corruption, more competitive markets.

In both models, a rightward shift of LRAS is drawn the same way: the vertical LRAS line moves to the right, from Yf₁ to Yf₂. The result is higher potential output, and if AD stays in the same position, the price level falls.

A Common Misconception

A common mistake students make is thinking that an increase in aggregate demand also shifts LRAS. It does not. AD shifts move the economy along the existing AS curves. Only changes in the productive capacity of the economy — the quantity, quality, or efficiency of resources — shift LRAS. AD changes affect actual output in the short run, not potential output in the long run.

2. How an AD Shock Plays Out in Each Model

Why the Two Models Give Different Answers

Suppose the government increases spending by £50 billion. Aggregate demand shifts to the right. What happens next depends entirely on which model you use.

The Monetarist (New Classical) Story

Short run: AD shifts right along the SRAS curve. Both real GDP (Y) and the price level (P) increase. The economy moves above full employment — actual output exceeds potential output. Unemployment falls below the natural rate. Firms compete for scarce workers, bidding up wages.

Long-run adjustment: Higher wages increase firms' production costs. SRAS shifts left. As SRAS shifts left, output falls back toward Yf and the price level rises further. This process continues until the economy returns to its original potential output, Yf, but at a permanently higher price level.

The key monetarist claim: the economy self-corrects. No government action is needed to return to Yf. The adjustment happens through flexible wages and prices. The only lasting effect of the AD increase is a higher price level — inflation.

The Keynesian Story

The effect of AD shifting right depends on where the economy starts.

If the economy is in the horizontal segment (deep recession, lots of spare capacity): AD shifts right. Output increases from a low level, but the price level stays the same. Firms can hire unemployed workers and use idle machinery without bidding up prices. Real GDP rises with zero inflation. This is the ideal scenario for expansionary policy.

If the economy is in the upward-sloping segment (approaching full employment): AD shifts right. Both output and the price level increase. Some industries hit capacity constraints while others still have room to expand. The result is higher real GDP with moderate inflation.

If the economy is at the vertical segment (at full employment): AD shifts right. Only the price level rises. Output cannot increase because all resources are already employed. Any attempt to push output beyond Yf causes pure demand-pull inflation.

The key Keynesian claim: the outcome depends on how much spare capacity exists. In a deep recession, government spending raises output without inflation. Near full employment, the same policy causes inflation. The economy does not automatically return to Yf — if it is stuck below full employment (because wages resist falling), it can stay there indefinitely without government help.

Worked Example: Comparing the Two Models

An economy has potential output of $2,000 billion. Its current equilibrium is at $1,800 billion with a price level of 100. The government increases spending by $80 billion, shifting AD to the right.

Monetarist analysis:

Approach: In the monetarist model, the short-run effect is higher output and prices. Then rising wages shift SRAS left, returning output to $2,000 billion at a higher price level.

Step 1: In the short run, AD shifts right. New equilibrium: output rises to $1,950 billion, price level rises to 105.

Step 2: Because output is still below Yf ($2,000 billion), there is slack in the economy. But as the economy moves toward Yf, wages begin to rise. SRAS shifts left gradually.

Step 3: Final long-run equilibrium: output returns to $2,000 billion (Yf), price level at 108. The only lasting effect is a higher price level.

Why this makes sense: The monetarist model says the economy always gravitates toward Yf. The AD increase provided a temporary boost, but the market adjusted wages upward, eroding the output gain.

Keynesian analysis:

Approach: In the Keynesian model, we first identify which segment of the AS curve the economy is on. At $1,800 billion out of $2,000 billion potential, the economy is in the upward-sloping segment — approaching capacity but not yet there.

Step 1: AD shifts right. Because there is still some spare capacity, output increases from $1,800 billion to $1,920 billion. The price level rises from 100 to 104.

Step 2: The economy reaches a new equilibrium. It is closer to Yf but not quite there. There is no automatic mechanism pushing it the rest of the way. If the government wants to reach exactly $2,000 billion, it needs further AD increases.

Step 3: Unlike the monetarist model, there is no prediction that SRAS will shift left on its own. Wages in the Keynesian model are sticky downward, meaning they resist falling when unemployment is high. They do not automatically rise when output is below Yf.

Why this makes sense: The Keynesian model says the economy can settle at any equilibrium where AD meets AS. It does not assume automatic return to Yf. The policy implication is clear: if the government wants full employment, it must actively manage AD.

3. Full Comparison: Monetarist vs Keynesian

A Side-by-Side Summary

DimensionMonetarist / New ClassicalKeynesian
Shape of LRASVertical at Yf — output is supply-determined in the long runThree segments — horizontal, upward-sloping, vertical
Wage and price flexibilityFully flexible, even downwardSticky downward (workers resist nominal wage cuts)
Self-correctionEconomy always returns to Yf automaticallyEconomy can remain below Yf indefinitely without intervention
Role of governmentMinimal — focus on stable money supply and low inflationActive — use fiscal and monetary policy to manage AD
UnemploymentAll unemployment is voluntary or structural; no involuntary unemployment in long runInvoluntary unemployment can persist due to deficient AD
Speed of adjustmentRapid (if wages/prices are flexible)Slow or nonexistent (wages are sticky)
Policy recommendationRules-based monetary policy (e.g., inflation targeting)Discretionary fiscal policy in recessions
View of recessionsTemporary deviations caused by shocks; self-correctingCan be prolonged and severe; require policy response

A Common Misconception

A common mistake students make is thinking that one model is "right" and the other is "wrong." Both models capture important truths about how economies behave. The monetarist model explains why economies tend to return to trend growth over long periods. The Keynesian model explains why deep recessions — like the Great Depression of the 1930s or the Global Financial Crisis of 2008 — can persist for years without aggressive government intervention. Modern macroeconomics draws on insights from both traditions. The IB expects you to understand both and to use the appropriate model depending on the context of the question.

IB Exam Tip

When a question asks about the effects of a policy, check whether it specifies "in the short run" or "in the long run," and whether it tells you which model to use. If it does not specify, you should present both the monetarist and Keynesian perspectives and then evaluate which is more applicable to the given scenario. This is what separates a good answer from a 7-level answer.

A second common pitfall: students sometimes draw the Keynesian AS curve as a straight line. It is not. It has three distinct segments. Draw each segment clearly and label the axes: Real GDP on the horizontal axis and Price Level on the vertical axis. Mark Yf at the point where the vertical segment begins.

Practice Problems

Problem 1. An economy is in long-run equilibrium in the monetarist model. Its potential output is $3,000 billion. The central bank unexpectedly increases the money supply, shifting AD to the right. Describe what happens to output, employment, and the price level in the short run. Then describe what happens in the long run. Use the terms "SRAS," "LRAS," and "Yf" in your answer.

Problem 2. An economy is operating in the horizontal segment of the Keynesian AS curve. The government increases spending on infrastructure by £30 billion. Explain the effect on real GDP and the price level. Why is the outcome different from what would happen if the economy were in the vertical segment?

Problem 3. State two factors that would cause the LRAS curve to shift to the right in both the monetarist and Keynesian models. Explain the mechanism by which each factor increases potential output.

Problem 4. A country discovers large new oil reserves and simultaneously invests heavily in university education. Using the AD/AS framework, explain the likely effects on potential output, actual output, and the price level. Assume the economy begins at full employment.

Problem 5. (IB-style, 10 marks) Using separate diagrams for the monetarist/new classical model and the Keynesian model, explain how an increase in aggregate demand affects real GDP and the price level. (a) Monetarist model: show both short-run and long-run effects. (4 marks) (b) Keynesian model: show effects when the economy is in the horizontal segment and when it is in the upward-sloping segment. (4 marks) (c) Explain one reason why the outcomes differ between the two models. (2 marks)

Problem 6. "In the monetarist model, the government can never increase output in the long run. In the Keynesian model, it can." Evaluate the extent to which this statement is true. Identify one scenario where the Keynesian model would predict no long-run gain from demand-side policy, and one scenario where the monetarist model would predict a long-run gain from supply-side policy.

Answers

Answer 1. In the short run, the increase in the money supply shifts AD to the right. The new equilibrium is where the new AD curve meets the existing SRAS curve. At this new intersection, real GDP rises above Yf ($3,000 billion) — perhaps to $3,200 billion — and the price level increases. Unemployment falls because firms hire more workers to produce the higher output. In the long run, the monetarist model predicts that low unemployment drives wages up. Higher wages increase firms' costs, causing SRAS to shift left. As SRAS shifts left, real GDP falls back toward $3,000 billion and the price level rises further. The economy returns to Yf but at a permanently higher price level. The only lasting effect of the monetary expansion is inflation.

Answer 2. In the horizontal segment, the economy has substantial spare capacity — unemployed workers and idle capital. When government spending shifts AD to the right, firms can increase production by hiring from this pool of unemployed resources without needing to pay higher wages or prices. Real GDP increases. The price level remains unchanged because there is no upward pressure on costs. If the economy were instead in the vertical segment (at full employment), the same AD shift would cause only the price level to rise. Real GDP could not increase because all resources are already fully employed. Any attempt to produce more would simply bid up prices as firms compete for scarce inputs. This is the key difference between the segments.

Answer 3. Factor one: an increase in the quantity of capital, such as new factories, machinery, or infrastructure. More capital means workers can produce more output per hour, raising the economy's productive capacity. LRAS shifts right. Factor two: technological progress, such as the development of more efficient production methods or new products. Technology allows the same quantity of inputs to produce more output, increasing potential output. LRAS shifts right. Both factors operate in both models because both models agree that LRAS is determined by the productive capacity of the economy, not by the level of aggregate demand.

Answer 4. The discovery of oil reserves increases the quantity of the land factor of production. Investment in university education increases the quality of labour — human capital. Both changes increase the economy's productive capacity. In the AD/AS framework, LRAS shifts to the right — potential output increases, perhaps from $2,000 billion to $2,300 billion. If AD remains unchanged, the price level falls because the same amount of spending is now chasing a larger output capacity. Actual output increases to match the new, higher potential output. The economy experiences non-inflationary growth: higher real GDP and a lower price level simultaneously.

Answer 5. (a) Monetarist diagram: Draw AD₁, SRAS₁, and vertical LRAS at Yf. AD shifts to AD₂. New short-run equilibrium: higher Y and higher P. Label this point. Then draw SRAS shifting left to SRAS₂ (due to rising wages). New long-run equilibrium: back at Yf but at a higher P than before. Label all three equilibria clearly. (b) Keynesian diagram: Draw the three-segment AS curve. First, show AD₁ intersecting the horizontal segment. AD shifts to AD₂. Output increases, P unchanged. Then show a separate diagram with AD₁ intersecting the upward-sloping segment. AD shifts to AD₂. Both Y and P increase. Label both scenarios. (c) The outcomes differ because the monetarist model assumes wages and prices are fully flexible, so the economy always returns to Yf after an AD shock. The Keynesian model assumes wages are sticky, so the economy can settle at an equilibrium below Yf if AD is insufficient, and the effect of an AD increase depends entirely on how much spare capacity exists.

Answer 6. The statement is partly true but needs qualification. In the monetarist model, demand-side policy (increasing AD) cannot raise output above Yf in the long run because rising costs shift SRAS left and return the economy to Yf. However, the monetarist model does allow supply-side policy — anything that shifts LRAS right — to increase output permanently. So the statement is true for demand-side policy but false for supply-side policy in the monetarist framework. In the Keynesian model, demand-side policy can increase output permanently if the economy is stuck below Yf in the horizontal or upward-sloping segments. But if the economy is at the vertical segment (full employment), Keynesian theory also predicts that demand-side policy causes only inflation, with no output gain. So the statement is only conditionally true for the Keynesian model. A scenario where Keynesians would predict no long-run gain: the economy is already at Yf on the vertical segment. A scenario where monetarists would predict a long-run gain: the government invests in education or infrastructure, shifting LRAS right.

Evaluate

Question: "The debate between Keynesians and monetarists is no longer relevant to modern economic policy-making." Evaluate this statement.

Model Answer:

The claim that the Keynesian-monetarist debate has become irrelevant is only partially true. On the surface, modern central banks and governments draw from both traditions without declaring allegiance to either. Central banks target inflation — a monetarist idea — but they do so using interest rate adjustments that affect aggregate demand through a Keynesian-style transmission mechanism. Governments run deficits during recessions — a Keynesian prescription — but they also worry about debt sustainability and inflationary consequences, concerns that monetarists emphasised. The synthesis is so complete that policymakers rarely label their actions as belonging to one school or the other.

However, the debate remains alive in two important respects. First, during severe economic crises, the old divisions resurface with force. The response to the 2008 Global Financial Crisis and the 2020 COVID-19 pandemic involved massive fiscal stimulus, quantitative easing, and direct government intervention — policies that a strict monetarist would have opposed. The debate about whether these interventions prevented depression or stored up future inflation is precisely the Keynesian-monetarist debate in modern form. The post-2020 inflation surge, and the disagreement about whether it was caused by excessive stimulus (a monetarist diagnosis) or supply-chain disruptions (a more Keynesian structural explanation), shows that the underlying theoretical dispute is far from settled.

Second, the debate persists because it reflects a deeper philosophical divide about the nature of markets. Monetarists and their new classical successors believe markets are fundamentally stable and self-correcting; government intervention is usually unnecessary and often harmful. Keynesians believe markets can fail in ways that do not self-correct, creating a role for active government management. This divide is not merely academic. It shapes real policy choices: how aggressively should a central bank raise interest rates when inflation appears? Should governments prioritise deficit reduction during a weak recovery? The answers depend on which model's assumptions you find more convincing.

The assumptions underlying each model also have different real-world validity in different contexts. In economies with flexible labour markets and strong institutional frameworks — such as the United States or United Kingdom — the monetarist assumption of rapid self-correction may be more plausible, and the case for minimal intervention stronger. In economies with rigid labour markets, powerful unions, or underdeveloped financial systems, wages and prices may adjust slowly, if at all, making the Keynesian model more applicable. The relevance of the debate is therefore context-dependent: it may be dormant in normal times and in flexible economies, but it becomes urgent during crises and in economies with structural rigidities.

In conclusion, the Keynesian-monetarist debate is not irrelevant. It has been absorbed into a policy synthesis during calm periods, but it re-emerges whenever the economy faces a major shock or when policymakers must choose between competing priorities. The questions the debate raised — about the speed of market adjustment, the effectiveness of government intervention, and the causes of unemployment and inflation — remain at the heart of macroeconomic policy. A student aiming for a 7 in IB Economics should understand that these are not historical footnotes; they are live disagreements that shape the decisions affecting millions of people.
