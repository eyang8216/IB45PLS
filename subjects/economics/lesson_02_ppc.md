# Lesson 2: The Production Possibilities Curve (PPC)

## What You'll Learn

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to:
- Define the Production Possibilities Curve and explain why economists use it
- Construct a PPC from a table of output combinations
- Explain why the PPC is typically concave (bowed outward) due to increasing opportunity cost
- Interpret points on, inside, and outside the PPC
- Distinguish between actual growth and potential growth
- Identify and explain causes of outward shifts in the PPC
- Differentiate between economic growth and economic development

---

## 1. The PPC — What It Is and Why It Matters

### The Core Question the PPC Answers

Every economy faces a fundamental problem: resources are scarce, but the things people want are unlimited. A government cannot simultaneously build every hospital citizens demand, fund every school at ideal staffing levels, and invest in every proposed transport project. A farmer cannot use the same field to grow wheat for bread and corn for animal feed and soybeans for export. These are not temporary inconveniences. They are permanent constraints.

The **Production Possibilities Curve (PPC)** — also called the Production Possibilities Frontier (PPF) — is a model that lets economists visualise this constraint. It is a graph that shows the maximum combinations of two goods that an economy can produce in a given time period, using all its resources fully and efficiently, with a fixed state of technology.

The word "maximum" is essential. The PPC is not a menu of comfortable options. It is a boundary. Every point on the curve represents a situation where the economy is operating at full capacity. There is no slack, no idle factory, no unemployed worker who could be put to work without sacrificing something else.

### Why Study the PPC?

The PPC is one of the first models taught in economics because it captures several of the subject's deepest ideas in a single diagram: scarcity, choice, opportunity cost, efficiency, unemployment, and economic growth. If you understand the PPC thoroughly, you understand why economics exists as a discipline. Every subsequent model in the IB syllabus — demand and supply, market structures, the AD/AS model — builds on the logic first encountered here.

### The Five Assumptions Behind the PPC

Every economic model rests on assumptions that simplify reality so we can think clearly. The PPC makes five:

1. **Only two goods are produced.** This allows a two-dimensional graph. In reality, economies produce millions of goods. The model treats one axis as a stand-in for all possible outputs in one category.
2. **Resources are fixed in quantity and quality.** The number of workers, the hectares of land, the stock of machines — all are held constant for the duration of the analysis.
3. **Technology is fixed.** The methods available for turning inputs into outputs do not change.
4. **All resources are fully and efficiently employed.** There is no unemployment, no idle machinery, no waste.
5. **The time period is specified.** Usually one year. This matters because in the very short run, even fewer things can change.

These assumptions are unrealistic by design. A road map that included every tree and lamppost would be useless for navigation. The PPC omits detail so that the essential relationships become visible.

## 2. Constructing a PPC

### From Table to Graph

Consider an economy that produces only two goods: **capital goods** (machines, factory buildings, tools used to produce other goods) and **consumer goods** (food, clothing, entertainment consumed directly by households). The table below shows what the economy can produce in one year if it uses all resources fully and efficiently.

| Combination | Capital Goods (thousands of units) | Consumer Goods (thousands of units) |
|---|---|---|
| A | 0 | 100 |
| B | 10 | 95 |
| C | 20 | 80 |
| D | 30 | 55 |
| E | 40 | 20 |
| F | 45 | 0 |

To construct the PPC, plot each combination as a point on a graph. Place capital goods on the horizontal axis and consumer goods on the vertical axis. Connect the points with a smooth curve. The resulting line is the PPC.

Combination A shows that if the economy devotes all resources to consumer goods, it produces 100 thousand units of consumer goods and zero capital goods. Combination F shows the opposite extreme: 45 thousand units of capital goods and zero consumer goods. Combinations B through E show various mixes in between.

A common mistake students make is plotting quantity on the wrong axis. Price never appears on a PPC diagram. The axes show quantities of two goods, not price and quantity. If you find yourself labelling an axis with a pound or dollar sign, you have confused the PPC with a demand or supply diagram.

## 3. Why the PPC Is Concave — Increasing Opportunity Cost

### Observing the Pattern

Look at what happens when the economy moves from one combination to the next, shifting resources toward capital goods:

- Moving from A to B: the economy gains 10 thousand capital goods but sacrifices 5 thousand consumer goods. The **opportunity cost** — the value of what is given up — is 5 thousand consumer goods, which works out to **0.5 consumer goods per unit of capital goods gained**.

- Moving from B to C: gaining another 10 thousand capital goods costs 15 thousand consumer goods. The opportunity cost rises to **1.5 consumer goods per unit of capital goods**.

- Moving from C to D: gaining 10 thousand capital goods costs 25 thousand consumer goods. Opportunity cost: **2.5 consumer goods per unit**.

- Moving from D to E: gaining 10 thousand capital goods costs 35 thousand consumer goods. Opportunity cost: **3.5 consumer goods per unit**.

- Moving from E to F: gaining the final 5 thousand capital goods costs the remaining 20 thousand consumer goods. Opportunity cost: **4.0 consumer goods per unit**.

The pattern is unmistakable. Each time the economy tries to produce more capital goods, the sacrifice — measured in consumer goods given up — grows larger. This is the **law of increasing opportunity cost**, and it explains why the PPC is concave (bowed outward from the origin).

### Why Does Opportunity Cost Increase?

Resources are not interchangeable. They are heterogeneous — different resources are suited to different tasks.

Consider land. Some fields have deep, fertile soil ideal for growing wheat (a consumer good). Other plots are rocky and better suited for factory foundations (a capital good). When an economy first begins shifting toward capital goods, it converts the rocky plots — the ones poorly suited to farming anyway. The loss in consumer goods is small because the land being converted was not very productive at farming in the first place.

As the shift continues, the economy eventually must convert the deep, fertile wheat fields into factory sites. Now the sacrifice is enormous. Land that was producing 100 bushels of wheat per hectare is now producing no wheat at all. The opportunity cost has risen dramatically.

The same logic applies to labour. Some workers are gifted bakers and tailors. Others are skilled machinists and engineers. When the economy reallocates workers from consumer goods to capital goods, it naturally starts with the machinists and engineers — workers whose comparative advantage lies in capital goods production. Only later, when those workers are exhausted, does the economy pull bakers and tailors off their work and retrain them — inefficiently — for factory work.

### A Straight-Line PPC: The Special Case

If every resource were equally productive in both uses — if a hectare of land, a worker, or a machine produced exactly the same output regardless of whether it was assigned to capital goods or consumer goods — then opportunity cost would be constant. Each additional unit of capital goods would cost the same amount of consumer goods, and the PPC would be a straight line.

This situation is theoretically possible but vanishingly rare in reality. The IB exam expects you to draw a concave PPC unless the question explicitly states that opportunity cost is constant.

## 4. Interpreting Points on the PPC

### Three Kinds of Points

Every point on the PPC diagram falls into one of three categories, and each tells a different story about the economy.

**Points on the curve** represent combinations where all resources are fully and efficiently employed. The economy is operating at its productive capacity. There is no way to produce more of one good without producing less of the other. This is called **productive efficiency** — the economy is getting the maximum possible output from its available resources.

**Points inside the curve** represent combinations where resources are unemployed, underemployed, or used inefficiently. An economy producing at a point inside its PPC is producing less than it could. This situation might arise from any of the following: workers without jobs (unemployment), skilled workers doing unskilled tasks (underemployment), factories running below capacity, wasteful production methods, or a recession that reduces economic activity across the board. A point inside the PPC is attainable — the economy can physically produce that combination — but it is undesirable because the economy is leaving output on the table.

**Points outside the curve** represent combinations that are unattainable given current resources and technology. An economy cannot produce beyond its PPC in the short run. This is the definition of scarcity made visible: the PPC is a hard boundary, and every point beyond it is a wish, not a possibility.

A common mistake students make is thinking that a point outside the PPC is "inefficient" because it is far away. In fact, a point outside the PPC is not inefficient — it is impossible. Inefficiency is represented by points inside the curve.

## 5. Shifts of the PPC — Actual Growth vs. Potential Growth

### Two Different Meanings of "Growth"

The word "growth" appears in two distinct senses in PPC analysis, and confusing them is one of the most frequent errors in IB exams.

**Actual growth** occurs when an economy moves from a point inside the PPC to a point on the PPC (or closer to it). The PPC itself does not move. What changes is that the economy stops wasting resources and starts using them. This might happen because unemployed workers find jobs, idle factories resume production, or the economy recovers from a recession. The economy's measured output — real GDP — increases, but its underlying productive capacity has not changed.

**Potential growth** occurs when the entire PPC shifts outward. The economy's productive capacity — the maximum it can produce with full employment — increases. This is a deeper kind of growth. Even if the economy were already operating on its PPC, potential growth would allow it to reach combinations that were previously unattainable.

The distinction matters for policy. A government that reduces unemployment achieves actual growth — a one-time gain as the economy moves toward its existing frontier. A government that invests in education and infrastructure achieves potential growth — it pushes the frontier itself outward, making sustained increases in living standards possible.

---

## 6. Causes of Outward PPC Shifts

### What Pushes the Frontier Outward?

The PPC shifts outward when the quantity or quality of resources increases, or when technology improves. Each cause operates through a specific mechanism.

**An increase in the quantity of resources.** If a country discovers new mineral deposits, reclaims land from the sea, experiences population growth through immigration, or builds new factories and roads (capital accumulation), its productive capacity expands. More inputs mean more output is possible.

**An increase in the quality of resources.** Quality improvements make existing resources more productive. Better education and training raise the skill level of the workforce — this is an increase in **human capital**. Irrigation and fertiliser improve land quality. Newer, more precise machines improve the quality of physical capital.

**Technological progress.** A new production method that extracts more output from the same inputs shifts the PPC outward. This includes not only dramatic inventions — the steam engine, the microprocessor — but also incremental improvements in management, logistics, and organisation.

**Institutional improvements.** A legal system that enforces contracts reliably, protects property rights, and reduces corruption does not directly produce goods, but it enables the other drivers of growth to work. A country with strong institutions will see its PPC shift outward faster than an otherwise identical country with weak ones.

### The Shape of the Shift

An outward shift is not always parallel. If the improvement affects both sectors equally — a general increase in labour productivity, for example — the PPC shifts outward in roughly parallel fashion. If the improvement is specific to one sector — a new crop variety that doubles wheat yields, but does nothing for manufacturing — the PPC shifts outward only on the axis representing that good, producing a skewed shift. The maximum output of the unaffected good remains unchanged.

### The Growth Trade-Off: Today vs. Tomorrow

An economy that produces more capital goods today is investing in its future productive capacity. Capital goods — factories, machines, transport networks — are themselves used to produce other goods. An economy at a point heavy on capital goods (say, 40 thousand capital goods and only 20 thousand consumer goods) will see its PPC shift outward faster than an economy that prioritises consumption. The cost is lower living standards today. The benefit is much higher living standards in the future.

This is not a hypothetical. South Korea in the 1960s and 1970s deliberately suppressed consumption and channelled resources into capital accumulation and export industries. The result was one of the fastest sustained growth episodes in economic history. The trade-off was real: an entire generation accepted lower consumption so that subsequent generations could be far richer.

## 7. Economic Growth vs. Economic Development

### Not the Same Thing

Students often use "growth" and "development" as synonyms. In IB Economics, they are distinct concepts with different meanings, measurements, and policy implications.

**Economic growth** is a quantitative increase in the output of goods and services, measured by the percentage change in real GDP or real GDP per capita. It is a number. On the PPC diagram, economic growth corresponds to an outward shift of the curve (potential growth) or a movement toward the curve (actual growth).

**Economic development** is a broader, qualitative improvement in living standards, well-being, and human capabilities. It encompasses not just income but health (life expectancy, infant mortality), education (literacy rates, years of schooling), and freedom from poverty. Development is measured by composite indices like the Human Development Index (HDI), not by a single number.

A country can experience economic growth without economic development. Imagine an oil-rich nation where a state-owned company extracts petroleum using foreign engineers and imported machinery. GDP rises, and the PPC shifts outward. But if the oil revenue is captured by a small elite, if schools remain underfunded, if life expectancy stagnates, the country has grown without developing.

Conversely, a country might achieve development gains — better nutrition, higher literacy, falling child mortality — even if measured GDP growth is modest, provided resources are directed toward health and education rather than physical capital.

The distinction will return throughout the IB syllabus, particularly in the macroeconomics and development economics sections. Plant it firmly in your mind now.

## IB Exam Tip

When drawing a PPC for an IB exam, follow these rules:

1. Label both axes clearly with the two goods — use specific names, not just "Good X" and "Good Y."
2. Draw the curve concave to the origin unless the question specifies constant opportunity cost.
3. Mark initial and new positions clearly: use Pe₁/Qe₁ style notation or label points with letters.
4. Use arrows to show the direction of shifts and explain the cause in your annotation.
5. Always state that the concave shape reflects the law of increasing opportunity cost, caused by resource heterogeneity.

A second common pitfall: students draw a PPC shift when the question describes a movement toward the curve. If the scenario says "unemployment falls," the economy moves from inside the PPC toward the curve. The PPC itself does not shift. Reserve shifts for changes in resource quantity, resource quality, or technology.

## Worked Example 1: Calculating Opportunity Cost from a PPC Table

An economy produces only wheat and steel. Its PPC is given by the following table:

| Combination | Wheat (thousands of tonnes) | Steel (thousands of tonnes) |
|---|---|---|
| P | 0 | 500 |
| Q | 100 | 450 |
| R | 200 | 350 |
| S | 300 | 200 |
| T | 350 | 0 |

Calculate the opportunity cost per tonne of wheat when the economy moves from combination R to combination S.

**Approach:** To find the opportunity cost, we identify how much steel is sacrificed and how much wheat is gained. We then divide the steel sacrificed by the wheat gained to express the cost per unit of wheat.

**Step 1:** Identify the change in each good.
- Wheat at R: 200 thousand tonnes. Wheat at S: 300 thousand tonnes.
- Gain in wheat = 300 − 200 = 100 thousand tonnes.

- Steel at R: 350 thousand tonnes. Steel at S: 200 thousand tonnes.
- Sacrifice in steel = 350 − 200 = 150 thousand tonnes.

**Step 2:** Calculate the opportunity cost per unit.
- Opportunity cost = sacrifice in steel ÷ gain in wheat
- Opportunity cost = 150 ÷ 100 = 1.5 tonnes of steel per tonne of wheat.

**Why this makes sense:** The economy gains 100 thousand tonnes of wheat but loses 150 thousand tonnes of steel. Each additional tonne of wheat costs 1.5 tonnes of steel. If we compare this with the move from Q to R (where the cost was 1.0 tonnes of steel per tonne of wheat), we can see the opportunity cost rising, which is consistent with the concave PPC shape.

## Worked Example 2: Determining Whether a Point Is Attainable

An economy's PPC shows that it can produce a maximum of 80 million units of healthcare per year or a maximum of 200 million units of education per year. The PPC is concave. A government minister proposes producing 50 million units of healthcare and 160 million units of education. Is this combination attainable?

**Approach:** A point is attainable if it lies on or inside the PPC. Since we do not have the full PPC schedule, we check whether the proposed combination exceeds either maximum. We also reason about whether it plausibly lies within the concave boundary.

**Step 1:** Check against the axis intercepts.
- Proposed healthcare: 50 million. Maximum healthcare: 80 million. 50 < 80, so healthcare alone is within the limit.
- Proposed education: 160 million. Maximum education: 200 million. 160 < 200, so education alone is within the limit.

**Step 2:** Consider the trade-off. The PPC is concave, which means that producing a mix of both goods requires sacrificing some of each. The economy cannot simultaneously produce 80 million healthcare and 200 million education — those are extremes achievable only when the other good is zero.

**Step 3:** A concave PPC always lies below a straight line connecting the two intercepts. The straight-line equation would be: Healthcare = 80 − (80/200) × Education = 80 − 0.4 × Education. At Education = 160: Healthcare on the straight line = 80 − 0.4(160) = 80 − 64 = 16 million. The proposed healthcare of 50 million is far above 16 million.

Because the concave PPC lies below this straight line, the actual healthcare possible at 160 million education would be even less than 16 million. The proposed combination of 50 million healthcare and 160 million education lies well outside the concave PPC.

**Step 4:** Conclusion. The combination is **not attainable** with current resources and technology. To reach it, the economy would need potential growth — an outward shift of the PPC.

**Why this makes sense:** The minister is essentially asking for a combination that sits far to the northeast of where the concave curve would pass. The further a point is from both axes, the more likely it is to lie outside the PPC unless it is very close to a straight line connecting the intercepts.

## Practice Problems

**Problem 1.** An economy produces food and machines. Its PPC shows Point J at (8 million tonnes of food, 25 thousand machines) lying inside the curve, and Point K at (15 million tonnes of food, 50 thousand machines) lying outside the curve. Describe what Point J tells you about the current state of the economy. Describe what must happen for the economy to eventually reach Point K. Write your answers as full paragraphs.

**Problem 2.** A country's PPC for healthcare (H) and education (E) is given by the table below. All values are in thousands of units per year.

| Combination | Healthcare (H) | Education (E) |
|---|---|---|
| V | 120 | 0 |
| W | 110 | 30 |
| X | 90 | 60 |
| Y | 60 | 80 |
| Z | 0 | 95 |

Calculate the opportunity cost of increasing education from 30 to 60 thousand units (moving from W to X). Calculate the opportunity cost of increasing education from 60 to 80 thousand units (moving from X to Y). Explain why the two opportunity costs are different, using the concept of resource heterogeneity.

**Problem 3.** For each of the following events, state whether the effect on the PPC is an outward shift, an inward shift, a movement toward the curve (actual growth), or no change. If the PPC shifts, state whether the shift is likely to be parallel or skewed, and explain your reasoning.

(a) A major earthquake destroys several factories and a section of the national highway network.
(b) The government launches a nationwide scholarship programme that doubles the number of university graduates within five years.
(c) A new oil field containing an estimated 2 billion barrels is discovered off the coast.
(d) The unemployment rate falls from 11% to 5% over an eighteen-month recovery.
(e) A research team develops a genetically modified wheat variety that produces 40% higher yields per hectare with no additional inputs.

**Problem 4.** Two countries, Alphaland and Betaland, begin with identical PPCs. Alphaland chooses to produce at a point corresponding to 65% capital goods and 35% consumer goods. Betaland chooses to produce at a point corresponding to 25% capital goods and 75% consumer goods. Both points lie on their respective PPCs. Which country will enjoy higher consumption per person today? Which country will likely experience a faster outward shift of its PPC over the next twenty years? Explain the trade-off involved, and discuss one reason why Betaland's choice might still be reasonable despite slower potential growth.

**Problem 5.** *(IB-style, 10 marks)* The table below shows the production possibilities for an economy that produces only agricultural goods and manufactured goods. All values are in millions of units per year.

| Combination | Agricultural goods | Manufactured goods |
|---|---|---|
| A | 0 | 60 |
| B | 20 | 55 |
| C | 40 | 45 |
| D | 60 | 30 |
| E | 75 | 0 |

(a) Define the term "production possibilities curve." (2 marks)
(b) Using the data above, calculate the opportunity cost of increasing agricultural output from 20 million to 40 million units. (2 marks)
(c) Explain why the opportunity cost of producing agricultural goods increases as more agricultural goods are produced. (2 marks)
(d) The economy is currently producing at combination C. A technological improvement in manufacturing raises the maximum possible manufactured-goods output by 25% at every level of agricultural production. Calculate the new maximum for manufactured goods when agricultural output is zero. Construct the new combination that corresponds to what was previously combination B, and explain one implication of this technological change for the economy's choice between current consumption and future growth. (4 marks)

## Answers

**Answer 1.** Point J, which lies inside the PPC, tells us that the economy is producing below its full capacity. Resources are either unemployed, underemployed, or being used inefficiently. This might be because some workers are without jobs, factories are running below their designed output, or the economy is in a recession. The economy is capable of producing more food, more machines, or more of both, without any increase in resources or technology — it simply is not doing so. Point K lies outside the current PPC, which means it is unattainable with existing resources and technology. To reach Point K, the economy must experience potential growth: an outward shift of the PPC. This could be achieved through an increase in the quantity of resources (more workers through immigration, more capital through investment), an improvement in the quality of resources (better education, better technology), or technological progress that allows more output from the same inputs. Simply reducing unemployment would move the economy toward the existing PPC but would not reach a point as far out as K.

**Answer 2.** For the move from W to X: the gain in education is 60 − 30 = 30 thousand units. The sacrifice in healthcare is 110 − 90 = 20 thousand units. The opportunity cost is 20 ÷ 30 ≈ 0.67 units of healthcare per unit of education. For the move from X to Y: the gain in education is 80 − 60 = 20 thousand units. The sacrifice in healthcare is 90 − 60 = 30 thousand units. The opportunity cost is 30 ÷ 20 = 1.5 units of healthcare per unit of education. The opportunity cost rises from approximately 0.67 to 1.5 because resources are heterogeneous — they are not equally suited to producing both goods. When the economy first expands education (moving from W to X), it can reallocate resources that are relatively poor at producing healthcare (perhaps administrators and buildings better suited to schools than hospitals). The sacrifice in healthcare is modest. As education expands further (from X to Y), the economy must reallocate resources that are highly productive in healthcare — skilled doctors, specialised hospital equipment — and repurpose them for education, where they are far less productive. The result is a much larger sacrifice of healthcare for each additional unit of education.

**Answer 3.** (a) Inward shift, likely skewed. The earthquake destroys physical capital — factories and highways — which reduces the economy's productive capacity. Since the damage affects manufacturing more directly than agriculture (assuming factories are hit harder than farmland), the shift is probably skewed, with the manufactured-goods axis contracting more than the agricultural axis. (b) Outward shift, likely parallel over time. The scholarship programme increases human capital — the quality of labour improves as more workers have university-level skills. A better-educated workforce tends to raise productivity across all sectors, so the shift is likely to be broadly parallel, though the full effect takes years to materialise as graduates enter the workforce. (c) Outward shift, skewed. The oil discovery increases the quantity of the land resource. The effect is concentrated in oil-related production and industries that use oil as an input, so the PPC expands more on axes involving those goods than on others. (d) Movement toward the curve (actual growth). Falling unemployment means previously idle workers are now producing output. The economy was inside its PPC and is now moving closer to it. The PPC itself does not shift because the underlying resources and technology have not changed. This is actual growth, not potential growth. (e) Outward shift, skewed. The GM wheat variety is a technological improvement specific to agriculture. The maximum output of wheat (and possibly other crops) increases, but the maximum output of non-agricultural goods is unaffected. The PPC shifts outward only on the agricultural axis.

**Answer 4.** Betaland, with 75% of its output in consumer goods, will enjoy higher consumption per person today. Its citizens have more food, clothing, entertainment, and other goods for immediate enjoyment. Alphaland, with 65% of its output in capital goods, will likely experience a faster outward shift of its PPC over the next twenty years. Capital goods — factories, machines, transport infrastructure — are used to produce other goods, including more capital goods. By investing heavily now, Alphaland expands its productive capacity, which means it can produce more of everything — including consumer goods — in the future. This is the classic growth trade-off: sacrifice consumption today for higher consumption tomorrow. Betaland's choice might still be reasonable if its citizens are currently very poor and cannot afford to reduce consumption further without suffering malnutrition or hardship. A country at subsistence level may rationally prioritise immediate survival over long-term growth. Additionally, Betaland might argue that healthy, well-fed workers are more productive, so some minimum level of consumption is itself an investment in human capital. The optimal balance depends on a society's starting point and its values.

**Answer 5.** (a) A production possibilities curve is a graphical representation of the maximum combinations of two goods that an economy can produce in a given time period, using all available resources fully and efficiently, with a fixed state of technology. It illustrates the fundamental economic concepts of scarcity, choice, and opportunity cost by showing the trade-off between the two goods. (2 marks — 1 for "maximum combinations," 1 for reference to full employment of resources or fixed technology.) (b) Moving from B (20 agricultural, 55 manufactured) to C (40 agricultural, 45 manufactured): gain in agricultural goods = 40 − 20 = 20 million units. Sacrifice in manufactured goods = 55 − 45 = 10 million units. Opportunity cost = 10 ÷ 20 = 0.5 units of manufactured goods per unit of agricultural goods. (2 marks — 1 for correct calculation, 1 for correct units.) (c) The opportunity cost increases because resources are not equally suited to producing both goods. As the economy shifts resources from manufacturing to agriculture, it first uses resources that are relatively unproductive in manufacturing — perhaps rocky land unsuited for factories, or workers with farming skills rather than factory skills. The loss in manufactured output is small. As agricultural production expands further, the economy must eventually convert resources that are highly productive in manufacturing — fertile land near cities that would be ideal for factories, or skilled engineers — and use them for agriculture, where they are much less productive. The sacrifice of manufactured goods per additional unit of agricultural goods grows larger. This resource heterogeneity produces the concave shape of the PPC. (2 marks — 1 for recognising heterogeneity of resources, 1 for explaining why this causes rising cost.) (d) The original maximum for manufactured goods (at combination A, with zero agricultural goods) was 60 million units. A 25% increase raises this to 60 × 1.25 = 75 million units. For the new combination corresponding to the old B: at the old B, agricultural goods were 20 million and manufactured goods were 55 million. The technological improvement increases manufactured output by 25% at every level of agricultural production, so the new manufactured output at 20 million agricultural goods is 55 × 1.25 = 68.75 million units. One implication: the technological improvement expands the economy's productive capacity asymmetrically. Because the improvement is in manufacturing rather than agriculture, the economy can now produce more capital goods (if manufactured goods include machinery and equipment) without sacrificing as much current consumption. This shifts the growth trade-off favourably: the economy can invest more in future productive capacity while still maintaining a given level of consumer goods. Alternatively, if the economy keeps capital-goods production constant, it can enjoy more consumer goods immediately — the trade-off between today and tomorrow has been softened. (4 marks — 1 for correct new maximum, 1 for correct new combination B, 2 for well-explained implication.)

## Evaluate

**Question:** "A country should always maximise its production of capital goods to achieve the highest possible rate of economic growth." Evaluate this statement.

**Model Answer:**

The statement contains an important insight — capital accumulation does drive growth — but "always maximise" pushes the argument too far. A sensible growth strategy balances capital investment against current consumption, human capital, and institutional quality.

The PPC framework reveals why the insight is powerful. An economy at a point weighted toward capital goods — say, 70% capital goods and 30% consumer goods — is building factories, machines, roads, and ports. These assets make workers more productive in the future. The PPC shifts outward faster than it would if the economy consumed most of its output today. South Korea's transformation from one of the world's poorest countries in the 1950s to a high-income industrial economy by the 1990s was built on exactly this strategy: suppressed consumption, high savings, and aggressive capital investment.

But "always maximise" ignores several realities. First, there is a biological floor on consumption. Workers who are malnourished, exhausted, or lack basic healthcare cannot be productive, no matter how many machines surround them. An economy that produces zero consumer goods would quickly find its labour force collapsing. Second, human capital — the skills, knowledge, and health of workers — is at least as important as physical capital for long-run growth. Education and healthcare are typically classified as consumer goods or services in PPC diagrams, but they are also investments in future productivity. A country that maximises factory construction while neglecting schools may find its growth stalling once the easy gains from capital accumulation are exhausted.

Third, institutions matter enormously for whether capital investment translates into growth. A country with weak property rights, endemic corruption, or an unreliable legal system may accumulate physical capital that is wasted — bridges to nowhere, factories that never open, machines that rust in ports. The Soviet Union invested massively in capital goods for decades yet experienced slowing growth because central planning could not allocate that capital efficiently. The statement assumes that all capital investment is productive, which is an assumption, not a guarantee.

There is also a distributional dimension. The costs of suppressed consumption fall disproportionately on the poor, who spend a larger share of their income on basic necessities. The benefits of future growth may accrue to different people — often those who own the capital being accumulated. A society might reasonably judge that a slightly lower growth rate is acceptable if it means less severe hardship for the most vulnerable citizens today.

Finally, the statement assumes that growth is the only objective. Economic development — improvements in health, education, and human capability — may require resources directed toward consumption-like activities (schools, clinics, nutrition programmes) that the PPC classifies as consumer goods. A country that maximises capital goods production might achieve high GDP growth but mediocre development outcomes.

In conclusion, the statement is half right. Capital accumulation is essential for growth, and a high rate of investment is desirable for countries that can afford it. But "always maximise" is an extreme prescription that ignores the complementarity between physical capital and human capital, the institutional prerequisites for productive investment, and the ethical claim of current citizens to a decent standard of living. The optimal point on the PPC is not at the extreme of either axis. It lies somewhere in the middle, determined by a society's starting income, its institutional quality, and the weight it places on the welfare of current versus future generations.
