Lesson 32: Inflation — Measurement and Causes

What You'll Learn

  • How inflation is defined and measured using CPI
  • How to calculate the inflation rate from CPI data
  • The two main types of inflation: demand-pull and cost-push
  • The critical distinction between deflation and disinflation
  • Limitations of CPI as a measure

1. Definition and the CPI

What Is Inflation?

Inflation is a sustained increase in the general price level of goods and services in an economy over a period of time. This does NOT mean all prices rise equally — individual prices may rise or fall, but the average level rises.

One-off price increases (e.g., due to a tax change) = NOT inflation. Inflation requires a continuous, self-perpetuating rise over multiple periods.

The Consumer Price Index (CPI)

The CPI tracks the cost of a representative basket of goods and services purchased by a typical household.

Construction Steps:

  1. Select a base year — the reference point
  2. Define the basket — survey households
  3. Assign weights — each item's share of total expenditure
  4. Collect prices monthly from retail outlets
  5. Compute the index:
$$\text{CPI}_t = \frac{\text{Cost of basket in year }t}{\text{Cost of basket in base year}} \times 100$$

2. Calculating the Inflation Rate

$$\text{Inflation Rate}_t = \frac{\text{CPI}_t - \text{CPI}_{t-1}}{\text{CPI}_{t-1}} \times 100\%$$

Using our example:
Inflation 2021 = (105.01 − 100.00) / 100.00 × 100% = 5.01%
Inflation 2022 = (109.82 − 105.01) / 105.01 × 100% = 4.58%

CPI rising but at a decreasing rate = disinflation.

3. Limitations of the CPI

a) Substitution Bias

CPI uses a fixed basket with fixed weights. When beef prices surge, consumers substitute toward chicken. The fixed basket overstates the inflation consumers actually experience.

b) Quality Changes

A smartphone today costs more than a phone in 2005, but it's vastly superior. CPI records only the price increase, missing quality improvement. Result: CPI overstates inflation when quality improves.

c) New Goods Bias

New products (e.g., streaming services) take time to enter the basket. These often fall rapidly in price after introduction. CPI misses early price declines.

d) Different Household Patterns

CPI reflects an average household. Elderly, students, rural, and urban households have very different spending patterns.

e) Geographic Variation

Prices differ across regions; national CPI averages mask regional differences.

4. Demand-Pull Inflation

Demand-pull inflation occurs when AD increases while aggregate supply cannot keep up: "Too much money chasing too few goods."

AD/AS Diagram

  • Start: AD₀, SRAS₀, LRAS at Yf, P₀
  • AD shifts RIGHT to AD₁ (due to ↑C, ↑I, ↑G, or ↑(X−M))
  • New equilibrium: higher P AND higher Y (Y₁ > Yf)
AD ComponentCauses
Consumption (C)Tax cuts, consumer confidence ↑, wealth effect
Investment (I)Interest rate cuts, business confidence ↑
Government (G)Fiscal stimulus, infrastructure spending
Net Exports (X−M)Currency depreciation, foreign income growth

5. Cost-Push Inflation

Cost-push inflation occurs when costs of production rise, reducing SRAS.

AD/AS Diagram

  • Start: AD₀, SRAS₀, LRAS at Yf, P₀
  • SRAS shifts LEFT to SRAS₁ (due to rising input costs)
  • New equilibrium: higher P AND LOWER Y (Y₁ < Yf) — STAGFLATION
CauseMechanism
Rising commodity pricesOil, food, metals price shocks (OPEC 1973)
Rising wagesStrong unions, minimum wage exceeding productivity
Supply chain disruptionsNatural disasters, pandemics, wars
Currency depreciationImported inputs become more expensive

Policy Dilemma

  • Contractionary reduces inflation but worsens recession (Y already < Yf)
  • Expansionary boosts Y but worsens inflation
  • Preferred: Supply-side policies to shift SRAS back right

Why deflation is dangerous: Consumers delay purchases → C↓ → AD↓ → further deflation → deflationary spiral. Real debt burden rises → bankruptcies. Japan's "lost decades" is the classic case study.

Practice Problems

Problem 1 (Easy)
The CPI in Country A was 120 in 2022 and 126 in 2023. Calculate the inflation rate for 2023. Is this inflation, deflation, or disinflation if 2022's inflation rate was 9%?
Problem 2 (Easy)
Explain TWO limitations of the CPI as a measure of the cost of living. Use specific examples.
Problem 3 (Medium)
Using an AD/AS diagram, explain the difference between demand-pull and cost-push inflation in terms of their effects on real GDP.
Problem 4 (Medium)
A country's CPI values are: 2020 = 100, 2021 = 105, 2022 = 108.15, 2023 = 109.23. Calculate the inflation rate for each year and identify any periods of disinflation or deflation.
Problem 5 (Challenging)
Country X's CPI basket is weighted as: Food 30%, Housing 35%, Transport 15%, Healthcare 10%, Entertainment 10%. In 2023, food prices rose 8%, housing rose 3%, transport fell 2%, healthcare rose 12%, and entertainment rose 1%. Calculate the overall inflation rate. If the central bank targets 2–4%, should it intervene?
Problem 6 (IB-Style 15-mark)
"Cost-push inflation is more difficult to control than demand-pull inflation." Discuss this statement.

Answers

Answer 1: Inflation rate = (126 − 120) / 120 × 100% = 6/120 × 100 = 5.00%. 2022 inflation was 9%, so inflation fell from 9% to 5%. Prices are still rising, just more slowly. This is disinflation (not deflation — CPI is not falling, only the rate of increase is falling).
Answer 2: 1. Substitution Bias: CPI uses a fixed basket. If beef prices surge, consumers switch to chicken. CPI continues to assume heavy beef consumption, overstating true cost-of-living increase. 2. Quality Changes: A laptop today costs $1,200 vs. $1,000 five years ago. Raw price comparison suggests 20% inflation, but today's laptop has 4× RAM, SSD, Retina display. CPI may not fully adjust for quality, overstating inflation.
Answer 3: Demand-pull: AD shifts RIGHT → P↑ AND Y↑ (economy overheats above Yf). Policy: Contractionary — no dilemma. Cost-push: SRAS shifts LEFT → P↑ AND Y↓ (stagflation). Policy dilemma: contractionary fixes inflation but worsens recession; expansionary fixes recession but worsens inflation. The crucial distinction: demand-pull shows higher Y; cost-push shows lower Y.
Answer 4:
2021: (105−100)/100 = 5.00%
2022: (108.15−105)/105 = 3.00%
2023: (109.23−108.15)/108.15 = 1.00%
2021→2022: Inflation fell 5%→3% = disinflation. 2022→2023: Inflation fell 3%→1% = disinflation. No deflation: CPI never fell below previous year.
Answer 5: Weighted inflation = Σ(weight × price change):
Food: 0.30×8% = 2.40% | Housing: 0.35×3% = 1.05% | Transport: 0.15×(−2%) = −0.30% | Healthcare: 0.10×12% = 1.20% | Entertainment: 0.10×1% = 0.10%
Overall = 4.45%

4.45% exceeds the 2–4% target band. BUT: Food (8%) and healthcare (12%) surges may be supply shocks (cost-push), which contractionary policy would worsen. Transport is falling (−2%), suggesting demand weakness. Recommendation: Investigate whether food/healthcare are supply-side shocks before acting. If demand-pull, raise rates. If cost-push, consider supply-side measures or tolerate a temporary overshoot.
Answer 6 (Full IB-Style):
Introduction: Cost-push inflation arises from rising production costs shifting SRAS left. The claim that it is harder to control rests in the policy dilemma of stagflation.

The Policy Dilemma: To reduce inflation → contractionary (AD left) → Y falls further below Yf. To restore output → expansionary (AD right) → inflation worsens. No policy can simultaneously fix both problems using demand-side tools alone. The appropriate remedy — shifting SRAS back right — is slow, uncertain, and often outside policymakers' direct control (e.g., global oil prices).

Why Demand-Pull Is Easier: Contractionary policy (raise rates) shifts AD left. No dilemma: both P and Y return toward equilibrium in the same direction. Central banks can act quickly and independently.

BUT — Cost-Push May Self-Correct: If caused by a temporary supply shock, SRAS shifts back as shock fades. No policy response may be best.

AND — Demand-Pull Has Its Own Complexities: Identifying the output gap precisely is hard; time lags (6–18 months); political pressure to avoid rate hikes.

Supply-Side Solutions for Cost-Push: Alternative energy investment, productivity improvements, trade liberalization → shift LRAS right, addressing root causes permanently.

Conclusion: Cost-push is more difficult in the short run because of the stagflation dilemma. However, the ideal approach is prevention — supply-side policies that make the economy resilient to cost shocks — combined with central bank credibility that anchors inflation expectations.

Evaluate

"Cost-push inflation is more difficult to control than demand-pull inflation."

CLASPP Analysis (166 words)

Conclusion: The statement is largely valid in the short run due to the stagflation policy dilemma. Cost-push inflation presents a trade-off between price stability and output that demand-pull does not, making it genuinely harder to manage with conventional tools.

Long-term vs Short-term: In the short run, cost-push creates the dilemma described above. In the long run, supply shocks often reverse naturally, and supply-side policies can shift SRAS/LRAS right, resolving cost-push permanently. Demand-pull may be harder to control in the long run if driven by structural excess demand.

Assumptions: Assumes cost-push is a domestic problem controllable by domestic policy. In reality, cost-push often originates from global commodity prices outside domestic control. Also assumes policymakers value both price stability and output equally.

Stakeholders: Workers suffer most from cost-push (rising prices, potential layoffs). Central banks face credibility crises. Governments lose popularity.

Priorities: If inflation expectations become unanchored (wage-price spiral), controlling inflation must be the priority regardless of output costs. If the shock is clearly temporary, preserving output takes priority.

Pros/Cons: Pro — accurately identifies the unique policy dilemma. Con — oversimplifies; demand-pull control has timing/identification challenges, and cost-push solutions exist albeit slower.

Overall: Cost-push is harder short-run, but both require thoughtful policy mixes.
