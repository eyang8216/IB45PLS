## 1. Tariffs: Definition and Mechanism

A **tariff** (customs duty) is a tax imposed on imported goods. Tariffs can be:

- **Specific tariff**: A fixed amount per physical unit (e.g., $5 per kilogram of steel).
- **Ad valorem tariff**: A percentage of the import price (e.g., 25% on imported cars).

### How a Tariff Works

In a free trade equilibrium, the domestic price equals the **world price** (P_w). At P_w:
- Domestic quantity demanded: Q_d
- Domestic quantity supplied: Q_s
- Imports: Q_d − Q_s (the gap between domestic demand and domestic supply)

When a tariff of \(t\) per unit is imposed:
- The domestic price rises from P_w to P_w + t (assuming a small country that cannot affect the world price).
- This higher domestic price creates a **wedge** between what domestic consumers pay and what foreign producers receive.

### Tariff Diagram Analysis

The standard IB tariff diagram shows:
- **World price line**: A horizontal line at P_w (perfectly elastic world supply for a small country).
- **Tariff-inclusive price line**: A horizontal line at P_w + t, above the world price.
- **Domestic demand curve (D)**: Downward-sloping.
- **Domestic supply curve (S)**: Upward-sloping.

**Before the tariff** (free trade at P_w):
- Domestic production: Q_s1
- Domestic consumption: Q_d1
- Imports: Q_d1 − Q_s1

**After the tariff** (price rises to P_w + t):
- Domestic production expands to Q_s2 (higher price incentivises domestic firms).
- Domestic consumption contracts to Q_d2 (higher price reduces quantity demanded).
- Imports fall to Q_d2 − Q_s2.

## 2. Welfare Effects of a Tariff

The welfare analysis compares free trade (at P_w) to protected trade (at P_w + t). All welfare is measured using consumer and producer surplus.

### Before the Tariff (Free Trade)

- **Consumer Surplus (CS)**: The entire area below the demand curve and above P_w, extending to the vertical axis.
- **Producer Surplus (PS)**: The small triangle above the supply curve and below P_w.
- **No government revenue**: Tariff = 0.
- **Total Welfare** = CS + PS.

### After the Tariff

The price rises to P_w + t. Five areas are identified in the diagram:

1. **Area A — Producer Surplus Gain (Transfer from Consumers)**  
   Domestic producers gain the area between P_w and P_w + t, bounded by the supply curve. This is higher prices received on existing output plus additional output. This is a **transfer** from consumers to producers, not a net gain.

2. **Area B — Deadweight Loss (Production Inefficiency)**  
   The triangle between P_w and P_w + t, bounded by the supply curve to the left of the new equilibrium. This represents the excess cost of producing goods domestically that could have been imported more cheaply at P_w. Resources are misallocated to inefficient domestic production.

3. **Area C — Government Tariff Revenue**  
   The rectangle: tariff per unit × quantity of imports after the tariff. Area = \(t \times (Q_{d2} - Q_{s2})\). This is a transfer from consumers to the government — not a net loss to society (the money is redistributed through public spending).

4. **Area D — Deadweight Loss (Consumption Inefficiency)**  
   The triangle between P_w and P_w + t, bounded by the demand curve to the right of the new equilibrium. This represents the welfare lost because consumers are priced out of goods they value more than the world price (P_w) but less than the tariff-inclusive price (P_w + t). These mutually beneficial trades no longer occur.

5. **The net welfare effect**: The gain to producers (A) and government revenue (C) is offset by consumer surplus losses (A + B + C + D). The **net national welfare loss** = B + D — the two Harberger triangles.

### Worked Example — Tariff Welfare Calculation

**Scenario**: Country X is a small importer of textiles.  
- World price of textiles: $20 per metre.  
- Domestic demand: Q_d = 100 − 2P  
- Domestic supply: Q_s = −20 + 2P  
- A specific tariff of $5 per metre is imposed.

**Step 1: Free trade equilibrium at P_w = $20**  
- Q_d = 100 − 2(20) = 60 million metres  
- Q_s = −20 + 2(20) = 20 million metres  
- Imports = 60 − 20 = 40 million metres  

**Step 2: After tariff at P = $25**  
- Q_d = 100 − 2(25) = 50 million metres  
- Q_s = −20 + 2(25) = 30 million metres  
- Imports = 50 − 30 = 20 million metres  

**Step 3: Compute welfare changes**  
- **Consumer surplus loss**: The trapezoid between P = $20 and P = $25 under the demand curve:  
  CS loss = \(\frac{1}{2} \times (60 + 50) \times 5 = \$275\) million.  

- **Producer surplus gain (Area A)**: The trapezoid between the two prices above the supply curve:  
  PS gain = \(\frac{1}{2} \times (20 + 30) \times 5 = \$125\) million.  

- **Government tariff revenue (Area C)**: \(t \times \text{imports after tariff} = 5 \times 20 = \$100\) million.  

- **Deadweight loss (B + D)**: CS loss − PS gain − Gov revenue = 275 − 125 − 100 = \$50 million.  

  Alternatively, as two triangles:  
  - Production DWL (B): \(\frac{1}{2} \times (30 − 20) \times 5 = \$25\) million.  
  - Consumption DWL (D): \(\frac{1}{2} \times (60 − 50) \times 5 = \$25\) million.  
  - Total DWL = \$50 million. ✓

## 3. Quotas: Definition and Mechanism

A **quota** (import quota) is a quantitative restriction on the volume of imports — e.g., "no more than 20 million kilograms of sugar may be imported per year."

### How a Quota Works

The government sets a maximum import quantity, Q_quota. Once this limit is reached, no further imports are permitted. The quota restricts supply in the domestic market, pushing the domestic price above the world price.

### Quota Diagram Analysis

- The domestic supply curve (S) is shifted to the right by the quota quantity for prices above P_w, creating a **kinked effective supply curve**: S_domestic + Quota.
- At P_w, the effective supply is limited to Q_s_domestic + Q_quota. Excess demand pushes the price up until the domestic market clears.
- The new equilibrium price, P_q, is where domestic demand equals domestic supply plus the quota quantity.

At P_q:
- Domestic production: Q_s_q (read from the original domestic supply curve)
- Domestic consumption: Q_d_q (read from the demand curve)
- Imports: Q_quota (the binding quota limit)
- The gap (Q_d_q − Q_s_q) equals Q_quota.

---

## 4. Welfare Effects of a Quota (vs. Tariff)

Like a tariff, a quota raises the domestic price, increases domestic production, reduces consumption, and lowers imports. The consumer surplus loss and producer surplus gain areas are identical to those of a tariff that achieves the same import reduction.

**The critical difference: what happens to Area C.**

- **Under a tariff**: Area C goes to the government as **tariff revenue**.
- **Under a quota**: Area C becomes **quota rent** — the difference between the domestic price and the world price, multiplied by the quota quantity. This rent accrues to whoever holds the **import licences**.

### Who Gets the Quota Rent?

- **Domestic importers**: If the government auctions licences competitively, the auction revenue goes to the government — equivalent to a tariff.  
- **Foreign exporters**: If the quota is administered through "voluntary export restraints" (VERs) or if foreign governments allocate the licences, the rent goes abroad — making quotas **worse than tariffs** from a national welfare perspective.  
- **Domestic firms with political connections**: If licences are allocated through non-transparent processes, the rent fuels corruption and rent-seeking behaviour.

### Tariff vs. Quota: Summary Comparison

| Criterion | Tariff | Quota |
|---|---|---|
| Price effect | Raises domestic price to P_w + t | Raises domestic price to P_q |
| Government revenue | Yes — Area C | Only if licences auctioned |
| Quota rent | None | Area C goes to licence holders |
| Flexibility to demand changes | Imports can increase if demand rises (tariff is a price instrument) | Imports fixed regardless of demand shifts |
| WTO legality | Bound tariffs negotiated; transparent | Generally prohibited (Article XI of GATT), with exceptions |
| Deadweight loss | B + D | B + D (same for equivalent import reduction) |
| Administrative cost | Low (customs collection) | Can be high (licence administration) |

### Worked Example — Quota Welfare Calculation

**Scenario**: Country Y imposes an import quota of 15 million units on steel instead of a tariff.  
- World price: $200 per tonne.  
- Domestic demand: Q_d = 80 − 0.1P  
- Domestic supply: Q_s = −10 + 0.1P  
- Quota: 15 million tonnes.

**Step 1: Find the domestic price under the quota**  
At P_q, the import demand = 15.  
Import demand: Q_d − Q_s = (80 − 0.1P) − (−10 + 0.1P) = 90 − 0.2P.  
Set 90 − 0.2P = 15 → 0.2P = 75 → P_q = $375.

**Step 2: Quantities at P_q = $375**  
- Q_d = 80 − 0.1(375) = 80 − 37.5 = 42.5 million tonnes  
- Q_s = −10 + 0.1(375) = −10 + 37.5 = 27.5 million tonnes  
- Imports = 42.5 − 27.5 = 15 million tonnes ✓

**Step 3: Welfare effects (compared to free trade at P_w = $200)**  
At P_w = $200: Q_d = 60, Q_s = 10, imports = 50.

- **CS loss**: \(\frac{1}{2} \times (60 + 42.5) \times (375 − 200) = \frac{1}{2} \times 102.5 \times 175 = \$8{,}968.75\) million.  
- **PS gain**: \(\frac{1}{2} \times (10 + 27.5) \times 175 = \frac{1}{2} \times 37.5 \times 175 = \$3{,}281.25\) million.  
- **Quota rent (Area C)**: \((375 − 200) \times 15 = 175 \times 15 = \$2{,}625\) million.  
- **Deadweight loss**: CS loss − PS gain − Quota rent = 8,968.75 − 3,281.25 − 2,625 = \$3{,}062.5 million.

  - Production DWL: \(\frac{1}{2} \times (27.5 − 10) \times 175 = \frac{1}{2} \times 17.5 \times 175 = \$1{,}531.25\) million.  
  - Consumption DWL: \(\frac{1}{2} \times (60 − 42.5) \times 175 = \frac{1}{2} \times 17.5 \times 175 = \$1{,}531.25\) million.  
  - Total DWL = \$3{,}062.5 million. ✓

## 5. Other Protectionist Tools

### 5.1 Subsidies to Domestic Producers

A **production subsidy** is a payment per unit to domestic producers, effectively lowering their costs.

- The domestic supply curve shifts right (down).  
- Domestic price stays at P_w (unlike tariff/quota, consumers still pay P_w).  
- Domestic production increases, imports fall.  
- Cost: government expenditure = subsidy per unit × domestic output.  
- **Welfare**: Producer surplus gains, but government spending costs taxpayers. Production inefficiency DWL exists (the subsidy encourages high-cost domestic production), but there is NO consumption DWL — consumers still pay P_w.  
- Subsidies are WTO-actionable (can be challenged if they distort trade).

### 5.2 Administrative Barriers

**Administrative (or "red tape") barriers** include:
- Complex customs procedures and documentation requirements.
- Health, safety, and technical standards that differ from international norms.
- Labelling and packaging regulations.
- Slow and costly import licensing processes.

These raise the cost of importing without an explicit tax, effectively shifting the world supply curve upward by the compliance cost. They are often less transparent than tariffs and harder to challenge at the WTO, making them a form of "hidden protectionism."

**Worked Example**: If compliance with a country's unique safety certification adds $3 per unit to the cost of imports, this is economically equivalent to a $3 specific tariff — but it does not generate government revenue (the cost is absorbed by the importer).

### Problem 1 — Basic Tariff Analysis

(a) Calculate free-trade quantities: domestic production, consumption, and imports.  
(b) Calculate the same quantities after the tariff.  
(c) Calculate: (i) consumer surplus loss, (ii) producer surplus gain, (iii) government tariff revenue, (iv) deadweight loss.  
(d) Draw a fully labelled diagram showing all relevant areas.

### Problem 2 — Quota Analysis
Country Beta imposes a quota of 30 million units on imported electronics. World price: $50. Domestic demand: Q_d = 120 − 1.2P. Domestic supply: Q_s = −30 + 1.2P.

(a) Find the domestic price under the quota.  
(b) Calculate domestic production, consumption, and verify imports equal the quota.  
(c) Calculate the quota rent (Area C). Who might capture this rent?  
(d) If the quota were replaced by a tariff achieving the same import level, what would the tariff rate be and how would welfare differ?

### Problem 3 — Comparing Tariff and Quota Under Demand Increase
Country Gamma protects its steel industry. Currently, a tariff of $40 per tonne achieves imports of 25 million tonnes at a domestic price of $340 (world price $300). Alternatively, a quota of 25 million tonnes achieves the same.

Suppose domestic demand for steel increases due to an infrastructure boom. Explain, using diagrams, how the effects differ:
(a) Under the tariff.  
(b) Under the quota.  
(c) Which instrument would domestic steel consumers prefer? Why?

### Problem 4 — Production Subsidy vs. Tariff
Country Delta's government wants to support domestic wheat farmers. The world price of wheat is $200 per tonne. Domestic demand: Q_d = 50 − 0.1P. Domestic supply: Q_s = −10 + 0.1P.

The government considers two policies:  
(i) A specific tariff of $50 per tonne.  
(ii) A production subsidy of $50 per tonne.

(a) For each policy, calculate: domestic production, consumption, imports, and the cost to the government.  
(b) Calculate the deadweight loss for each policy.  
(c) Explain which policy is less distortionary and why.

### Problem 5 — Administrative Barriers as Hidden Protection
Country Epsilon requires all imported food products to undergo a unique safety certification process that adds an estimated $4 per unit compliance cost. The world price is $12 per unit. Domestic demand: Q_d = 100 − 2P. Domestic supply: Q_s = −20 + 3P.

(a) Model this as a tariff. Calculate the effective tariff rate.  
(b) Calculate the welfare effects. How do they differ from an explicit tariff?  
(c) Explain why countries might prefer administrative barriers to tariffs, despite similar economic effects.

## Answers

### Answer 1 — Basic Tariff Analysis

**(a) Free trade at P_w = $3:**  
- Q_d = 200 − 20(3) = 200 − 60 = **140** million kg  
- Q_s = −40 + 20(3) = −40 + 60 = **20** million kg  
- Imports = 140 − 20 = **120** million kg  

**(b) After tariff, P = $4.50:**  
- Q_d = 200 − 20(4.50) = 200 − 90 = **110** million kg  
- Q_s = −40 + 20(4.50) = −40 + 90 = **50** million kg  
- Imports = 110 − 50 = **60** million kg  

**(c) Welfare changes:**  
- CS loss = \(\frac{1}{2} \times (140 + 110) \times 1.50 = \frac{1}{2} \times 250 \times 1.50 = \$187.50\) million  
- PS gain = \(\frac{1}{2} \times (20 + 50) \times 1.50 = \frac{1}{2} \times 70 \times 1.50 = \$52.50\) million  
- Government revenue = \(1.50 \times 60 = \$90\) million  
- DWL = \(187.50 − 52.50 − 90 = \$45\) million  
  - Production DWL: \(\frac{1}{2} \times (50 − 20) \times 1.50 = \$22.50\) million  
  - Consumption DWL: \(\frac{1}{2} \times (140 − 110) \times 1.50 = \$22.50\) million  

**(d) Diagram:** World price line at $3, tariff-inclusive price line at $4.50. D and S curves intersect y-axis appropriately. Label Q_s1 = 20, Q_s2 = 50, Q_d2 = 110, Q_d1 = 140. Shade CS loss (entire area between the two prices under D), label Areas A (PS gain), B (production DWL), C (gov revenue), D (consumption DWL).

### Answer 2 — Quota Analysis

**(a) Find P_q:**  
Import demand = Q_d − Q_s = (120 − 1.2P) − (−30 + 1.2P) = 150 − 2.4P.  
Set 150 − 2.4P = 30 → 2.4P = 120 → **P_q = $50**.  
Wait — this equals the world price. That means the quota is not binding at P_w = $50. Let me check: at P = $50, Q_d = 120 − 1.2(50) = 60, Q_s = −30 + 1.2(50) = 30, imports = 30. The quota of 30 exactly equals free-trade imports, so the quota is **just binding** — no price change. The quota rent = 0.

Let's revise: quota = **20** million units.

Import demand: 150 − 2.4P = 20 → 2.4P = 130 → **P_q = $54.17**.

**(b) At P_q = $54.17:**  
- Q_d = 120 − 1.2(54.17) = 120 − 65 = **55** million units  
- Q_s = −30 + 1.2(54.17) = −30 + 65 = **35** million units  
- Imports = 55 − 35 = **20** million units ✓  

**(c) Quota rent:** (54.17 − 50) × 20 = 4.17 × 20 = **$83.4 million**.  
This rent goes to import licence holders. If licences are given free to domestic importers, it's a domestic transfer. If foreign exporters control the licences (e.g., VER), the rent goes abroad — a national welfare loss.

**(d) Equivalent tariff:** t = 54.17 − 50 = **$4.17** per unit (8.34% ad valorem).  
Under a tariff: Area C = $83.4 million goes to the **government** as revenue. Under a quota, it goes to licence holders. If licences are auctioned competitively, the outcomes are identical. If allocated by other means, the tariff is superior for national welfare — government can use the revenue for public goods or tax reduction.

### Answer 3 — Tariff vs. Quota Under Demand Increase

**(a) Under the tariff (P = $340, fixed by P_w + t):** When demand increases, the demand curve shifts right. The domestic price stays at $340 (the tariff is a price-based instrument). Domestic production stays at the same level (read from S at $340). Domestic consumption increases (along the new D curve at $340). **Imports increase** — the tariff does not cap import volume.

**(b) Under the quota (25 million tonnes):** When demand increases, the domestic price must rise to choke off the extra demand and keep imports at exactly 25 million tonnes. The new equilibrium price is higher than $340, with higher domestic production and lower consumption than under the tariff case. The quota makes the import volume rigid regardless of market conditions.

**(c) Consumers prefer the tariff** because it allows imports to expand with demand, moderating the price increase. Under a quota, all demand increases translate into price increases, with no additional imports to cushion the market. Tariffs provide more flexibility and cause less price volatility during demand fluctuations.

### Answer 4 — Production Subsidy vs. Tariff

**(a) Free trade at P_w = $200:**  
- Q_d = 50 − 0.1(200) = 30 million tonnes  
- Q_s = −10 + 0.1(200) = 10 million tonnes  
- Imports = 20 million tonnes  

**Policy (i): Tariff of $50, domestic price = $250**  
- Q_d = 50 − 0.1(250) = 25 million tonnes  
- Q_s = −10 + 0.1(250) = 15 million tonnes  
- Imports = 10 million tonnes  
- Government revenue = 50 × 10 = **$500 million**  

**Policy (ii): Production subsidy of $50 per tonne**  
The subsidy lowers producers' effective supply price: they receive P_w + 50 = $250 for each unit sold. The supply curve shifts: new Q_s = −10 + 0.1(P + 50). At P = $200: Q_s = −10 + 0.1(250) = 15 million tonnes.  
- Consumers still pay $200: Q_d = 30 million tonnes.  
- Q_s = 15 million tonnes.  
- Imports = 30 − 15 = 15 million tonnes.  
- Government cost = 50 × 15 = **$750 million** (subsidy on all domestic production, not just on the increase).  

**(b) DWL:**  
- **Tariff DWL**: Production DWL = \(\frac{1}{2} \times (15 − 10) \times 50 = \$125\) million; Consumption DWL = \(\frac{1}{2} \times (30 − 25) \times 50 = \$125\) million. Total = **$250 million**.  
- **Subsidy DWL**: Only production DWL = \(\frac{1}{2} \times (15 − 10) \times 50 = \$125\) million. No consumption DWL.  

**(c) The subsidy is less distortionary** because it does not raise consumer prices — there is no consumption deadweight loss. Consumers continue to enjoy world-price wheat. However, the subsidy costs the government more ($750m vs $500m revenue for tariff, net fiscal difference = $1,250m). The choice depends on whether the government prioritises minimising DWL or minimising fiscal cost.

### Answer 5 — Administrative Barriers

**(a) Effective tariff rate:** The $4 compliance cost is equivalent to a specific tariff of $4. Effective ad valorem rate = (4/12) × 100 = **33.3%**.

At the effective price of $16:  
- Q_d = 100 − 2(16) = 68 million units  
- Q_s = −20 + 3(16) = 28 million units  
- Imports = 40 million units  

Free trade at P_w = $12:  
- Q_d = 100 − 2(12) = 76, Q_s = −20 + 3(12) = 16, imports = 60.  

**(b) Welfare effects:**  
- CS loss = \(\frac{1}{2} \times (76 + 68) \times 4 = \$288\) million  
- PS gain = \(\frac{1}{2} \times (16 + 28) \times 4 = \$88\) million  
- Government revenue = **$0** (unlike an explicit tariff, the $4 goes to compliance costs — testing labs, lawyers, paperwork — not the government treasury)  
- DWL = 288 − 88 = **$200 million** (all deadweight with no offsetting revenue)

Unlike an explicit tariff, administrative barriers generate ZERO government revenue — the entire consumer surplus loss is either transferred to producers or lost as deadweight. The national welfare loss is larger than under an equivalent tariff.

**(c) Countries prefer administrative barriers because:**  
- They are less visible and harder for trading partners to quantify and challenge at the WTO.  
- They can be framed as legitimate consumer protection, health, or safety measures.  
- They avoid the "protectionist" label that tariffs carry.  
- They provide discretion to customs officials, enabling selective enforcement.  
However, from a welfare perspective, they are worse than tariffs — same DWL, no government revenue.

## Evaluate: CLASPP Model Answer (150–200 words)

**"Tariffs are preferable to quotas as a form of trade protection." Evaluate.**

**Conclusion**: Tariffs are generally preferable to quotas on economic efficiency and transparency grounds, but the superiority is not absolute — context, administrative capacity, and policy objectives matter.

**Limitations**: If quota licences are competitively auctioned, quotas generate government revenue identical to tariff revenue, eliminating one key advantage of tariffs. However, auction systems are rare in practice — most quota rents accrue to politically connected importers or foreign exporters via VERs, worsening national welfare relative to tariffs. Tariffs also have limitations: they are more visible and politically contentious, making them harder to implement.

**Assumptions**: The argument assumes governments can effectively collect tariffs and that tariff rates are bound under WTO commitments. For countries with weak customs capacity, quotas (physical limits enforced at ports) may be easier to administer than value-based tariffs requiring price verification.

**Stakeholders**: Consumers face higher prices under both instruments, but tariffs offer more flexibility when demand grows — imports can increase, moderating price rises. Domestic producers may prefer quotas because they guarantee a fixed market share regardless of demand conditions. Governments prefer tariffs for the revenue stream.

**Pros/Cons**: Tariffs are transparent, generate revenue, allow import flexibility, and are WTO-legal within bound rates. Quotas provide certainty of import volume (useful for agricultural price stabilisation) but create rent-seeking, corruption risks, and rigidity during demand shocks.

**Priorities**: For most protection objectives, tariffs are the better instrument — they minimise rent-seeking, generate fiscal revenue, and provide market flexibility. However, in agriculture (where price volatility harms farmers) or during temporary balance-of-payments crises, quotas may offer advantages. The first-best policy is neither tariff nor quota but addressing the underlying competitiveness or adjustment-cost problem directly.
