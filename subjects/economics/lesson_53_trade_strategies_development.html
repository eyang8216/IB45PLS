Lesson 53: Trade Strategies for Development (Ch 20.1)

What You'll Learn

  • The rationale, mechanics, and outcomes of import substitution industrialisation (ISI)
  • The export promotion (export-led growth) strategy and its successes
  • Trade liberalisation as a development strategy
  • Fair trade schemes and their role in development

    • 1. Import Substitution Industrialisation (ISI)

    1.1 What Is ISI?

    ISI is a development strategy where a country replaces imported manufactured goods with domestically produced substitutes by protecting domestic industries from foreign competition.

    Policy tools used in ISI:

    • Tariffs: taxes on imported goods to make them more expensive than domestic alternatives
    • Quotas: quantitative limits on imports
    • Subsidies: government financial support for domestic industries
    • Overvalued exchange rates: making imports of capital goods cheaper (but hurting export competitiveness)
    • State-owned enterprises: government directly establishes industries in strategic sectors

      • 1.2 Rationale for ISI

      ISI was popular in the 1950s–1970s, particularly in Latin America, based on:

      • Infant industry argument: new domestic industries need temporary protection to grow large enough to achieve economies of scale and become competitive
      • Dependence theory: developing countries were locked into exporting cheap raw materials and importing expensive manufactures — ISI could break this colonial-era pattern
      • Declining terms of trade: the Prebisch–Singer thesis argued commodity prices fall relative to manufactures over time, so specialising in commodities is a losing strategy
      • Balance of payments concerns: reducing imports would save scarce foreign exchange

        • 1.3 Advantages of ISI

        • Protects infant industries, giving them time to develop
        • Reduces import dependence, saving foreign exchange
        • Creates domestic employment in manufacturing
        • Develops domestic industrial capacity and technological skills
        • Diversifies the economy away from primary commodities

          • 1.4 Disadvantages of ISI

          • Inefficient industries: behind high protection, firms face no competitive pressure to improve productivity or quality — they become permanently high-cost producers
          • Limited domestic market: many developing countries have small populations and low incomes, so domestic demand is insufficient to achieve economies of scale
          • Capital-intensive production: ISI often favours capital-intensive techniques (imported machinery), creating relatively few jobs compared to labour-intensive alternatives
          • Anti-export bias: protection makes inputs expensive, and overvalued exchange rates hurt all exporters, not just competing imports
          • Retaliation: trading partners may retaliate with their own tariffs, limiting export opportunities
          • Rent-seeking and corruption: protection creates opportunities for firms to lobby for continued privileges rather than improve efficiency

            • 1.5 Evidence on ISI

            Latin American countries that pursued ISI (Argentina, Brazil, Mexico) initially saw industrial growth but later experienced stagnation, inefficiency, and debt crises. By the 1980s, most had abandoned ISI in favour of more open trade policies. In contrast, East Asian countries that pursued export-oriented strategies achieved much stronger long-term growth.

            2. Export Promotion (Export-Led Growth)

            2.1 What Is Export Promotion?

            Export promotion is a strategy focused on producing goods for export markets, integrating into the global economy rather than retreating from it.

            Policy tools:

            • Subsidies and tax incentives for export industries
            • Competitive (not overvalued) exchange rates
            • Investment in infrastructure (ports, roads, power)
            • Investment in education to build a skilled workforce
            • Free trade zones and special economic zones
            • Gradual trade liberalisation

              • 2.2 The East Asian Tigers

              South Korea, Taiwan, Singapore, and Hong Kong are the classic success stories. From the 1960s onward, they:

              • Started with labour-intensive manufacturing (textiles, toys, electronics assembly)
              • Used export earnings to invest in education and technology
              • Moved up the value chain to more sophisticated products (steel, shipbuilding, semiconductors)
              • Achieved sustained GDP growth rates of 7–10% for decades

                • Key features: strong state guidance (industrial policy), competitive exchange rates, high savings rates, and massive investment in education.

                2.3 Advantages of Export Promotion

                • Competition forces efficiency: firms exposed to global markets must continuously improve productivity and quality
                • Larger markets allow economies of scale: the global market is vastly larger than any domestic market, enabling firms to reach efficient scale
                • Foreign exchange earnings: exports earn the foreign currency needed to import capital goods and technology
                • Technology transfer: exposure to foreign buyers and competitors accelerates learning and adoption of new technologies
                • Employment creation: export-oriented manufacturing is often labour-intensive, creating jobs for unskilled workers moving out of agriculture

                  • 2.4 Disadvantages of Export Promotion

                  • Dependence on foreign demand: if major export markets enter recession (e.g., 2008–09 global financial crisis), export-dependent economies suffer severely
                  • Vulnerability to external shocks: volatile commodity prices, exchange rate fluctuations, and trade policy changes in partner countries create instability
                  • Labour exploitation risk: pressure to keep costs low may lead to poor working conditions and low wages
                  • Uneven benefits: export sectors may become enclaves with limited linkages to the rest of the economy
                  • Environmental costs: rapid industrialisation can degrade the environment if regulations are weak

                    • 3. Trade Liberalisation

                    3.1 What Is Trade Liberalisation?

                    Trade liberalisation is the reduction or removal of barriers to international trade — tariffs, quotas, subsidies, and regulatory obstacles — often as part of structural adjustment programmes or WTO commitments.

                    3.2 Advantages

                    • Access to cheaper imports: consumers and firms benefit from lower prices, raising real incomes and reducing production costs
                    • Competition drives efficiency: domestic firms must improve or exit, reallocating resources to more productive uses
                    • Attracts FDI: open economies are more attractive to foreign investors seeking production platforms
                    • Technology and knowledge spillovers: imports embody foreign technology; trade exposes firms to new ideas

                      • 3.3 Disadvantages

                      • Domestic industries may be destroyed: sudden exposure to competition can wipe out firms before they have time to adjust, causing unemployment
                      • Short-term adjustment costs: workers in import-competing industries lose jobs and may not easily find new ones
                      • Revenue loss: tariffs are an important source of government revenue in many developing countries
                      • May increase inequality: if liberalisation benefits skilled workers and capital owners more than unskilled labour

                        • 4. Fair Trade Schemes

                        Fair trade is an alternative approach that aims to provide better prices, decent working conditions, and sustainable production for producers in developing countries.

                        Key features:

                        • Minimum price guarantees: producers receive a floor price that covers sustainable production costs
                        • Fair trade premium: additional payment for community development projects (schools, clinics, infrastructure)
                        • Long-term trading relationships: stability and predictability for producers
                        • Environmental standards: encourage sustainable farming practices
                        • Democratic organisation: producers participate in cooperative decision-making

                          • Limitations:

                          • Small scale: fair trade accounts for a tiny fraction of world trade
                          • Higher consumer prices limit market size
                          • Certification costs can be burdensome
                          • May not reach the very poorest producers
                          • Some economists argue it distorts market signals, encouraging overproduction

                            • IB Exam Tip

                            When comparing ISI and export promotion, use the East Asian Tigers vs. Latin America as your core case study. Be specific: South Korea's GDP per capita was similar to Ghana's in 1960; by 2000 it was comparable to developed European countries. The contrast with Argentina's stagnation under ISI is powerful evidence.

                            Practice Problems

                            Problem 1

                            Explain the infant industry argument as a justification for import substitution industrialisation (ISI). (4 marks)

                            Problem 2

                            Identify and explain two disadvantages of ISI as a development strategy. (4 marks)

                            Problem 3

                            Explain how export promotion can lead to increased productivity in domestic industries. (4 marks)

                            Problem 4

                            Using examples, compare the development outcomes of ISI (in a Latin American country) and export promotion (in an East Asian country). (6 marks)

                            Problem 5

                            Explain how fair trade schemes aim to address the disadvantages of conventional trade for developing country producers. (4 marks)

                            Problem 6 (IB-Style)

                            "Export promotion is a more effective development strategy than import substitution." Evaluate this statement. (10 marks)

                            Answers

                            Answer 1

                            The infant industry argument states that new industries in developing countries cannot initially compete with established firms from developed countries, which have economies of scale, experienced workers, and established distribution networks. If these infant industries are given temporary protection (tariffs, subsidies), they can grow, achieve lower costs through learning and scale, and eventually become internationally competitive without protection. The argument assumes that the long-run benefits of a competitive domestic industry outweigh the short-run costs of protection. However, a key risk is that protected industries may never "grow up" — they may use protection to remain inefficient indefinitely rather than become competitive.

                            Answer 2

                            Disadvantage 1 — Perpetual inefficiency: Protection removes competitive pressure. Domestic firms face no incentive to reduce costs, improve quality, or innovate. They may remain high-cost, low-quality producers indefinitely because consumers have no alternatives. The infant industry never matures; it becomes a permanent dependent on state protection.

                            Disadvantage 2 — Limited market size: Many developing countries have small populations and low incomes, so the domestic market is too small to support efficient-scale production. A car factory, for instance, may need to produce hundreds of thousands of units annually to minimise unit costs, but a small developing country might only demand a few thousand. Without export markets, high unit costs are inevitable, making the industry permanently uncompetitive.

                            Answer 3

                            Export promotion increases productivity through several channels:

                            1. Competitive pressure: Firms selling in global markets must match or exceed the price, quality, and innovation of international competitors. This forces continuous improvement in efficiency and product quality — unlike protected domestic markets where inefficiency is tolerated.
                            1. Economies of scale: Access to the global market allows firms to produce at much larger volumes, spreading fixed costs over more units and lowering average costs. A factory producing for 8 billion global consumers can reach efficient scale that is impossible when producing only for a domestic market of a few million.
                            1. Learning and technology transfer: Exposure to sophisticated foreign buyers and competitors accelerates the adoption of better production techniques, management practices, and technologies.

                            Answer 4

                            ISI — Argentina: Argentina pursued ISI from the 1940s through the 1970s, protecting domestic manufacturing with high tariffs. Initially, industrial output grew. But over time, protected industries became inefficient, producing high-cost, low-quality goods. The domestic market was too small for scale economies. Export agriculture (Argentina's comparative advantage) was penalised by overvalued exchange rates and taxes. The result was chronic inflation, repeated balance of payments crises, and stagnation. By the 1980s, Argentina was in severe debt crisis.

                            Export promotion — South Korea: South Korea from the 1960s pursued export-led growth, initially in labour-intensive manufacturing (textiles, wigs, plywood). Competitive exchange rates and state support for exporters drove rapid export growth. Firms were exposed to global competition, forcing efficiency. Export earnings funded education and technology investment, enabling movement up the value chain to steel, shipbuilding, electronics, and semiconductors. South Korea's GDP per capita rose from among the world's poorest to developed-country levels in two generations.

                            Contrast: The key difference is that ISI insulated firms from competition, breeding inefficiency, while export promotion forced efficiency through market discipline.

                            Answer 5

                            Fair trade schemes address the disadvantages of conventional trade for developing country producers in the following ways:

                            1. Price volatility and low prices: Commodity producers face highly volatile world prices and may receive prices below production costs when markets are oversupplied. Fair trade guarantees a minimum price that covers sustainable production costs, providing income stability.
                            1. Unequal bargaining power: Small producers dealing with large multinational buyers have little negotiating power. Fair trade promotes democratic producer cooperatives and long-term relationships, giving producers more voice and predictability.
                            1. Lack of investment in community development: The fair trade premium provides additional funds designated for community projects (schools, clinics, clean water) that conventional trade profits do not fund.
                            1. Environmental and labour standards: Fair trade certification requires adherence to environmental sustainability and decent working conditions, addressing the "race to the bottom" in conventional supply chains.

                            Answer 6

                            Introduction: The historical record strongly favours export promotion over ISI, but a nuanced evaluation must consider context, timing, and how each strategy is implemented.

                            Arguments that export promotion is more effective:

                            • Evidence: The East Asian Tigers achieved sustained, rapid development through export-led growth, while ISI countries in Latin America and Africa stagnated.
                            • Competition breeds efficiency: Export-oriented firms face market discipline that ISI firms avoid, driving continuous productivity improvement.
                            • Scale economies: Access to global markets allows efficient production scale impossible in small domestic markets.
                            • Foreign exchange: Export earnings provide the hard currency needed to import capital goods and technology.

                              • Arguments for ISI in some contexts / limitations of export promotion:

                              • Timing and sequencing: Some argue East Asian success involved both strategies — early protection for selected industries combined with export discipline (South Korea protected some industries while pushing them to export).
                              • Global demand required: Export-led growth requires growing global markets. A world recession (2008) or protectionism in major markets can devastate export-dependent economies.
                              • ISI may be partly justified for very large countries: India and China, with enormous domestic markets, could achieve scale economies behind moderate protection.
                              • Export promotion depends on capable state: The East Asian model required competent, relatively non-corrupt government to guide industrial policy — not replicable everywhere.

                                • Conclusion: On balance, the evidence strongly favours export promotion as a development strategy, but the most successful cases combined export orientation with strategic government intervention. Pure ISI has largely failed. However, the effectiveness of export promotion depends on external market conditions and domestic institutional capacity — it is not a universal blueprint.

                                Evaluate

                                Question: "Export promotion is a more effective development strategy than import substitution."

                                CLASPP Model Answer (195 words):

                                Conclusion: Export promotion has proven substantially more effective than ISI across most historical cases, though the most successful development experiences combined elements of both approaches strategically.

                                Logic and assumptions: The statement assumes that openness and competition drive efficiency while protection breeds stagnation. The comparative experience of East Asia vs. Latin America provides strong empirical support for this logic.

                                Advantages of export promotion: Competition forces continuous productivity improvement; global markets enable economies of scale; export earnings finance capital goods imports; technology transfer accelerates. South Korea transformed from aid-dependent to high-income in one generation through export-led growth.

                                Disadvantages / Limitations: Export-led growth is vulnerable to external demand shocks and protectionism in partner countries. It may exploit low-wage labour and degrade the environment. The East Asian model relied on unusually capable state institutions that may not be replicable. Some successful exporters (South Korea, China) used selective protection alongside export promotion — not pure free trade.

                                Stakeholders: Workers in export industries may gain employment but face poor conditions. Consumers worldwide benefit from lower prices. Firms in developed countries face increased competition, potentially displacing workers.

                                Preconditions: Export promotion requires infrastructure, a reasonably educated workforce, political stability, and access to foreign markets — conditions not equally available to all developing countries.

                                Priorities: While export promotion is generally preferable, a pragmatic approach that uses targeted, temporary protection to develop export capacity — "infant industry protection with an export mandate" — may be the most effective strategy for the poorest countries.

                                Summary

                                The debate between ISI and export promotion is one of the most important in development economics, and the historical evidence provides a clear answer. ISI has largely failed because protection breeds inefficiency, domestic markets are too small for scale economies, and agriculture (where many countries have comparative advantage) is penalised. Export promotion, as practised by the East Asian Tigers, has been far more successful — competition drives efficiency, global markets allow scale, and export earnings finance investment. However, the most successful cases used a pragmatic mix: strategic, temporary protection combined with export discipline. Trade liberalisation and fair trade schemes offer additional pathways, each with strengths and limitations. The key lesson is not openness vs. protection as absolutes, but finding the right strategy for each country's circumstances — and avoiding the trap of permanent, unconditional protection.
