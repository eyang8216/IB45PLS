**Answer: A**

### Solution 2 — Supply: Determinants (PINTS)

**Answer: A**

**Reasoning:**

- **Drought destroys 30% of crop** → this is a natural condition affecting production (PINTS: **N** — Nature/weather). The supply of coffee decreases → **leftward shift** of the supply curve.
- **Removal of 15% export tax** → this reduces the cost of production/export (PINTS: **T** — Taxes and subsidies, or more broadly **I** — Indirect taxes). Removing a tax makes production more profitable at each price → **rightward shift** of the supply curve.
- The two shifts are in opposite directions. Without data on their relative magnitudes, the **net effect on equilibrium price and quantity is indeterminate** (the price could rise, fall, or stay the same; quantity could rise, fall, or stay the same).

**Mark scheme:** 1 mark for A. ✓

**Common mistake:** Selecting D — assuming events cancel out. Shifts in opposite directions do not necessarily cancel; the magnitudes are almost certainly different, and even if they did cancel by coincidence, the correct characterisation is "net effect indeterminate," not "no shift."

### Solution 9 — Indirect Taxes: Deadweight Loss

**Answer: B**

**Reasoning:**

Deadweight loss from a tax is the welfare loss triangle:
- **DWL = ½ × tax per unit × (Q_pre-tax − Q_post-tax)**

### Solution 11 — Demand: TRIBE, Normal/Inferior, Substitutes/Complements

**(a)** State the law of demand and distinguish between movement and shift. **[2 marks]**

**Answer:**

The **law of demand** states that, *ceteris paribus*, there is an inverse relationship between the price of a good and the quantity demanded — as price rises, quantity demanded falls, and vice versa. ✓ (1 mark)

A **movement along** the demand curve occurs when the **price of the good itself** changes, causing a change in quantity demanded. ✓ A **shift** of the demand curve occurs when a **non-price determinant** (one of the TRIBE factors) changes, causing the entire curve to move left (decrease in demand) or right (increase in demand) — at every given price, consumers now demand a different quantity. ✓ (1 mark)

---

**(b)** Identify and explain **two** determinants of demand (TRIBE) affecting instant noodles when incomes rise. **[2 marks]**

**Answer:**

1. **Income (TRIBE: I):** Instant noodles are likely an **inferior good** for most consumers in this economy. As household income rises from 35,000 to 49,000 rupees (+40%), consumers tend to buy fewer inferior goods, switching to higher-quality alternatives (e.g., fresh noodles, restaurant meals). → Demand for instant noodles **shifts left**. ✓

2. **Related goods — substitutes (TRIBE: R):** Fresh noodles are a close substitute for instant noodles. With higher incomes, consumers can afford more fresh noodles (a normal good with a higher price of 60 rupees). The increased consumption of the substitute reduces demand for instant noodles. → Demand for instant noodles **shifts left**. ✓

*Also acceptable:* Expectations (TRIBE: E) — consumers anticipating permanently higher income may begin shifting consumption patterns immediately. Number of buyers is also acceptable if linked to demographic changes coinciding with income growth.

---

**(c)** Effect of a fall in the price of fresh noodles on demand for instant noodles. **[2 marks]**

**Answer:**

Fresh noodles are a **substitute** for instant noodles. ✓ When the price of fresh noodles falls from 60 to 45 rupees, consumers find the substitute relatively cheaper → they switch consumption **away from** instant noodles toward fresh noodles. This causes the **demand curve for instant noodles to shift left** (decrease in demand) — at every given price of instant noodles, consumers now demand fewer packs. ✓

*(Note: This is a change in the price of a related good, not instant noodles' own price, so it is a **shift** of demand, not a movement along.)*

**Total: 6 marks**

**(a)** Calculate PED using the midpoint (arc) formula. **[2 marks]**

**Answer:**

### Solution 15 — Subsidies: Stakeholder Effects

**(a)** Explain why the new supply curve is P = 10 + 0.4Q. **[1 mark]**

**Answer:**

The original supply curve P = 100 + 0.4Q shows the price producers must receive to supply Q units. A subsidy of £90 per unit paid to producers means they now receive the market price **plus** £90 for every unit: (P + 90) = 100 + 0.4Q. ✓ Rearranging: **P = 10 + 0.4Q**. The subsidy effectively lowers the minimum price producers require, shifting the supply curve **vertically downward** by the full £90 subsidy amount.

---

**(b)** Calculate the new equilibrium quantity and consumer price. **[2 marks]**

**Answer:**

New equilibrium: Demand = New supply
- 1,000 − 0.5Q = 10 + 0.4Q

**Price consumers pay:**
- Pc = 1,000 − 0.5(1,100) = 1,000 − 550 = **£450** ✓ (½ mark)

**Price producers receive:**
- Pp = Pc + subsidy = 450 + 90 = **£540** ✓ (½ mark)

Check using supply: Pp = 100 + 0.4(1,100) = 100 + 440 = £540 ✓

**(c)** One advantage and one disadvantage from a stakeholder perspective. **[1 mark]**

**Answer:**

**Advantage:** Consumers benefit from a lower price (£450 vs. the pre-subsidy equilibrium — solving original: 1,000 − 0.5Q = 100 + 0.4Q → Q = 1,000, P = £500). The £50 price reduction makes solar panels more affordable, encouraging adoption and generating positive environmental externalities (reduced carbon emissions). ✓

**Disadvantage:** The government (and ultimately taxpayers) bears a cost of £90 × 1,100 = **£99,000**. This represents an opportunity cost — the funds could have been used for alternative public goods or have distortionary effects if funded through taxation. ✓

### Solution 17 — Positive Externalities of Consumption

**(a)** Distinguish between MPB and MSB. **[1 mark]**

**Answer:**

**Marginal Private Benefit (MPB)** is the additional benefit received by the **individual consumer** from consuming one more unit of a good. ✓ **Marginal Social Benefit (MSB)** is the total additional benefit to **society** — it equals MPB **plus** any external benefits to third parties (MSB = MPB + external benefit). ✓ When positive consumption externalities exist, MSB > MPB at every quantity.

---

**(b)** Explain underconsumption and state its size. **[1 mark]**

**Answer:**

In a free market, consumers make decisions based only on their **private benefit (MPB)**, ignoring the external benefits (e.g., herd immunity, reduced NHS burden). At P = £15, the MPB curve intersects at Q = 40,000 vaccinations — this is the free-market outcome. ✓ However, the MSB curve (MPB + £8) intersects MSC (= £15) at Q = 65,000 — the social optimum. The resulting **underconsumption = 65,000 − 40,000 = 25,000 vaccinations**. ✓

---

**(c)** Suggest one government policy to correct this failure. **[1 mark]**

**Answer:**

A **Pigouvian subsidy** of £8 per vaccination would shift the effective MPB curve upward by £8 (or equivalently, reduce the price consumers pay by £8, making the effective consumer price £7). ✓ This would align private incentives with social benefits, shifting consumption from 40,000 toward the socially optimal 65,000 vaccinations. (Alternatively: government provision of free vaccinations funded through taxation would achieve the same outcome.) ✓

**Total: 3 marks**

### Solution 18 — Public Goods and the Free-Rider Problem

**(a)** Define a public good and explain the two characteristics of the sea wall. **[2 marks]**

**Answer:**

A **public good** is a good that is **non-rivalrous** and **non-excludable**. ✓ (1 mark for definition)

**Sea wall characteristics:**

1. **Non-rivalrous:** One resident's "consumption" of flood protection does **not reduce** the protection available to any other resident. All 5,000 residents benefit simultaneously without depleting the good. ✓ (½ mark)

2. **Non-excludable:** Once the sea wall is built, it is **impossible to exclude** any resident from its protection — even those who did not contribute to its funding. The wall protects the entire coastal area indiscriminately. ✓ (½ mark)

---

**(b)** Explain the free-rider problem using the data. **[2 marks]**

**Answer:**

The **free-rider problem** arises because individuals have an incentive to **understate their true willingness to pay**, hoping others will fund the good while they still benefit. ✓

**Applied to the data:**
- Total social benefit = 5,000 residents × £600 = **£3,000,000**
- Cost of sea wall = **£2,000,000**
- Since total benefit (£3M) > cost (£2M), the wall **should** be built from a social perspective. ✓

However, each resident reasons: "If others contribute enough, the wall gets built and I receive £600 of protection for free. If they don't contribute enough, my contribution is wasted." With voluntary contributions, many residents will attempt to **free-ride**, leading to **under-provision** — insufficient funds raised, and the socially beneficial sea wall may not be constructed despite the net social benefit of £1,000,000. ✓

**Total: 4 marks**

### Solution 21 — XED Calculation

**(a)** Calculate XED (butter → margarine) using midpoint formula. **[2 marks]**

**Answer:**

- ΔQ(margarine) = 59,000 − 50,000 = +9,000
- Midpoint Q = (50,000 + 59,000) / 2 = 54,500
- %ΔQ(margarine) = +9,000 / 54,500 = **+0.16514** ✓ (½ mark)

- ΔP(butter) = 2.34 − 1.80 = +0.54
- Midpoint P = (1.80 + 2.34) / 2 = 2.07
- %ΔP(butter) = +0.54 / 2.07 = **+0.26087** ✓ (½ mark)

- **XED = +0.16514 / +0.26087 = +0.63** (to 2 d.p.) ✓ (1 mark for correct answer)

---

**(b)** Interpret: substitutes or complements? **[1 mark]**

**Answer:**

XED = +0.63 is **positive** → butter and margarine are **substitutes**. ✓ |XED| = 0.63 < 1 → they are **weak substitutes** — the quantity of margarine demanded increases less than proportionately to the butter price increase (+16.5% vs +26.1%). Consumers switch, but not completely; brand loyalty, taste preferences, or baking requirements may limit substitution. ✓

---

**(c)** Calculate XED (butter → bread) and interpret. **[1 mark]**

**Answer:**

- ΔQ(bread) = 36,000 − 40,000 = −4,000
- Midpoint Q = (40,000 + 36,000) / 2 = 38,000
- %ΔQ(bread) = −4,000 / 38,000 = **−0.10526**
- %ΔP(butter) = **+0.26087** (from part a)

- **XED = −0.10526 / +0.26087 = −0.40** ✓

Interpretation: XED is **negative** → butter and bread are **complements**. |XED| = 0.40 < 1 → they are **weak complements** — when butter becomes more expensive, bread consumption falls, but less than proportionately. Consumers reduce joint consumption of the two goods, but bread is also consumed independently (with other spreads, or plain), moderating the complementarity effect. ✓

**Total: 4 marks**

### Solution 22 — Tax Incidence and Surplus Calculation

**(a)** Pre-tax equilibrium. **[1 mark]**

**Answer:**

Set demand = supply:
- 80 − Q = 20 + 2Q
- 60 = 3Q
- **Q = 20 million units** ✓ (½ mark)
- **P = 80 − 20 = £60** ✓ (½ mark)

Check: P = 20 + 2(20) = £60 ✓

---

**(b)** Post-tax equilibrium. **[2 marks]**

**Answer:**

New supply: P = 35 + 2Q (shifted up by £15, the amount of the specific tax)

Set new supply = demand:
- 35 + 2Q = 80 − Q
- 3Q = 45
- **Q = 15 million units** ✓ (1 mark)

**Price consumers pay (Pc):**
- Pc = 80 − 15 = **£65** ✓ (½ mark)

**Price producers receive (Pp):**
- Pp = Pc − tax = 65 − 15 = **£50** ✓ (½ mark)

Check using old supply: Pp = 20 + 2(15) = 20 + 30 = £50 ✓

---

**(c)** Tax revenue and burden shares. **[2 marks]**

**(i) Total tax revenue:**
- Tax revenue = tax per unit × post-tax quantity
- = £15 × 15 million = **£225 million** ✓ (½ mark)

**(ii) Burden shares:**
- **Consumer burden per unit** = Pc − P_pre-tax = £65 − £60 = **£5** ✓ (½ mark)
- Consumer burden as % = £5 / £15 × 100 = **33.3%** ✓ (¼ mark)

- **Producer burden per unit** = P_pre-tax − Pp = £60 − £50 = **£10** ✓ (½ mark)
- Producer burden as % = £10 / £15 × 100 = **66.7%** ✓ (¼ mark)

**Interpretation:** Producers bear a larger share (⅔) of the tax because supply is relatively **less elastic** than demand in this market. Demand: slope = −1 (flatter = more elastic). Supply: slope = +2 (steeper = more inelastic). The more inelastic side bears the larger tax burden.

**Total: 5 marks**

### Solution 23 — Rent Ceilings — Evaluation

**(a)** Two likely consequences of the rent ceiling. **[4 marks]**

**Answer:**

**1. Persistent housing shortage:** At the ceiling price of £600, Qd = 65,000 but Qs = 38,000, creating a **shortage of 27,000 units**. ✓ Unlike a market-clearing price, the ceiling prevents the price mechanism from eliminating the shortage — it becomes a permanent feature of the market. This means 27,000 households willing and able to pay £600 cannot find housing. ✓

**2. Deterioration in rental housing quality and emergence of black markets:** With rents capped below equilibrium, landlords have **reduced incentive to maintain properties** — maintenance costs eat into diminished returns. Over time, the quality of the rental stock declines. ✓ Additionally, tenants may pay **"key money"** or under-the-table payments to secure scarce units, creating an informal black market where the effective price exceeds the legal ceiling. ✓

**(Each consequence: 1 mark for identification + 1 mark for explanation = 2 marks × 2 = 4 marks)**

---

**(b)** Two advantages and two disadvantages. **[4 marks]**

**Answer:**

**Advantages:**

1. **Immediate affordability for existing tenants:** Tenants in rent-controlled units pay £600 instead of £750, saving £150/month (£1,800/year). For low-income households, this represents significant disposable income that can be spent on necessities. ✓

2. **Prevents displacement and gentrification:** Without rent control, rising market rents (driven by the growing city) could force long-term, lower-income residents out of their neighbourhoods. Rent ceilings provide **stability and security of tenure**. ✓

**Disadvantages:**

1. **Chronic shortage and misallocation:** The 27,000-unit shortage means many families cannot find housing at all. Those who secure rent-controlled units may stay in apartments that are too large or poorly located relative to their needs ("locking-in" effect), while new arrivals — often the most vulnerable — are shut out. ✓

2. **Reduced supply in the long run:** Developers face lower expected returns → fewer new rental units are built. Existing landlords may convert rental properties to owner-occupied housing or commercial use, **contracting supply further** and worsening the shortage over time. ✓

**(1 mark per point — must have two advantages AND two disadvantages)**

---

**(c)** CLASPP evaluation: Rent ceilings vs housing subsidies. **[12 marks]**

**Model answer (186 words):**

Rent ceilings provide **immediate relief** for existing tenants but generate significant long-run costs. In the short run, the benefit is concentrated — 38,000 tenant households gain — but the 27,000-household shortage reveals a fundamental trade-off: protecting some renters excludes others entirely.

The policy's effectiveness rests on a critical **assumption** — that supply is relatively inelastic. If landlords can easily exit the market (convert to condominiums, sell to owner-occupiers), the long-run supply contraction may be severe, eroding the policy's initial gains. The **stakeholder** analysis is stark: incumbent tenants gain, prospective tenants lose heavily, landlords lose income and reduce investment, and the government faces political pressure as the housing crisis deepens.

On **balance**, housing subsidies to low-income renters represent a superior alternative. Unlike rent ceilings, which distort the entire market, subsidies are **targeted** — they assist those in genuine need without suppressing the price signal that incentivises new construction. This preserves allocative efficiency while addressing equity concerns. However, subsidies impose a **fiscal cost** and may themselves be capitalised into higher rents if supply is constrained.

**Prioritisation** favours a hybrid approach: means-tested housing vouchers combined with supply-side reforms (zoning liberalisation, social housing construction). This addresses both the symptom (unaffordability) and the cause (insufficient supply), whereas rent ceilings alone treat only the symptom while exacerbating the underlying disease.

**Mark scheme:**
- **C** (Conclusion): Clear overall judgement — 2 marks
- **L** (Long-run vs short-run): Distinguishes temporal effects — 2 marks
- **A** (Assumptions): Identifies key assumptions — 2 marks
- **S** (Stakeholders): Analyses effects on different groups — 2 marks
- **P** (Pros & Cons): Balanced discussion — 2 marks
- **P** (Prioritisation): Ranks or recommends policies with justification — 2 marks

**Total: 12 marks (part c) | Overall Problem 23: 20 marks**

**Common mistake:** Treating evaluation as a simple "list of pros and cons" without synthesising a judgement. The CLASPP framework requires a **reasoned conclusion** that weighs evidence and prioritises among alternatives. Avoid one-sided answers — IB examiners expect nuance.

### Solution 24 — Indirect Tax vs Other Methods — Evaluation

**(a)** Why the tax may reduce but not fully correct the externality. **[4 marks]**

**Answer:**

The specific tax of £3 per 100 bags **equals** the estimated external cost of £3 per 100 bags. In theory, this is a Pigouvian tax — it internalises the externality, making producers/consumers face the true social cost. ✓

After the tax, quantity fell from 800 million to 600 million bags — a **25% reduction**. This suggests the tax is partially effective. ✓

However, the tax may not fully correct the market failure because:

1. **The external cost estimate may be inaccurate:** £3 per 100 bags (£0.03 per bag) may underestimate the true social cost if it excludes long-term environmental damage, ecosystem effects, or the cost of microplastic pollution. If the true external cost is higher, the tax is **too low** — MSC still exceeds MSB at the post-tax equilibrium, and some deadweight loss remains. ✓

2. **Demand may be relatively inelastic:** The quantity only fell from 800M to 600M (PED calculation: %ΔQ ≈ −28.6% using midpoint, %ΔP for consumers = (7−5)/6 = +33.3%, PED ≈ −0.86). With inelastic demand, the tax achieves only a modest quantity reduction. The remaining 600M bags still generate external costs, and if the social optimum is significantly below 600M, the market failure persists. ✓

**(2 marks for explaining the tax mechanism + 2 marks for explaining limitations)**

---

**(b)** One strength and one limitation of indirect tax vs tradable permits. **[4 marks]**

**Answer:**

**Strength of indirect tax over tradable permits:** Indirect taxes **generate government revenue** (£3 × 600M = £1,800 million = £1.8 billion). ✓ This revenue can be hypothecated (earmarked) for environmental clean-up, recycling infrastructure, or reducing other distortionary taxes — generating a "double dividend." Tradable permits, if initially allocated for free (grandfathered), generate no public revenue — the scarcity rent accrues to permit holders. ✓

**Limitation of indirect tax vs tradable permits:** The government **does not know the exact quantity reduction** an indirect tax will achieve — it depends on the (imperfectly known) price elasticities of demand and supply. ✓ With tradable permits, the government sets a **binding quantity cap** (e.g., 400 million bags), providing **certainty** about the environmental outcome. The market then discovers the price. For environmental goals where exceeding a threshold is catastrophic (e.g., fishery collapse), quantity certainty is crucial. ✓

**(2 marks for strength + 2 marks for limitation)**

---

**(c)** CLASPP evaluation: Indirect tax, tradable permits, or regulation? **[12 marks]**

**Model answer (198 words):**

Each policy instrument operates through a fundamentally different mechanism, and their effectiveness depends heavily on context. A **ban (regulation)** provides the strongest environmental guarantee — plastic bag consumption falls to (near) zero. However, it is the bluntest instrument: it eliminates consumer choice, may create a black market, and generates no revenue. For single-use plastic bags — where close substitutes exist (reusable bags) — a ban's welfare cost may be modest.

An **indirect tax** preserves consumer choice while internalising the externality. Its key advantage is the **double dividend**: the £1.8 billion in revenue can fund environmental remediation. However, the tax's effectiveness hinges on the **accuracy of the external cost estimate** and the **price elasticity of demand**. The PED of ≈ −0.86 (implied by the data) suggests demand is relatively inelastic — the quantity reduction may be insufficient.

**Tradable permits** offer **quantity certainty** while harnessing market mechanisms. Firms with low abatement costs reduce usage and sell permits; high-cost firms buy. This achieves the target at minimum total cost. However, the administrative complexity and need for robust monitoring make permits less suitable for diffuse sources like millions of individual consumers.

**Prioritisation:** For single-use plastic bags, a phased approach is optimal: a modest tax to begin shifting behaviour (generating revenue for transition), transitioning to a ban once reusable alternatives achieve widespread adoption. This combines the Pigouvian signal of the tax with the certainty of eventual elimination. Command-and-control (a ban) is ultimately superior here because the substitutes are readily available, and the transaction costs of permits outweigh their efficiency gains for this specific market failure.

**Mark scheme:**
- **C** (Conclusion): Clear prioritised judgement — 2 marks
- **L** (Long-run vs short-run): Temporal dimension addressed — 2 marks
- **A** (Assumptions): Identifies critical assumptions (PED, external cost accuracy) — 2 marks
- **S** (Stakeholders): Consumer, producer, government, environmental perspectives — 2 marks
- **P** (Pros & Cons): Balanced comparison of all three policies — 2 marks
- **P** (Prioritisation): Specific, justified recommendation (phased approach) — 2 marks

**Total: 12 marks (part c) | Overall Problem 24: 20 marks**

### Solution 25 — Government Responses: Comprehensive Evaluation

**(a)** One policy for each of the three market failures. **[6 marks]**

**Answer:**

**1. Coal power (negative production externality):** A **Pigouvian tax** of £25 per MWh on coal-fired electricity. ✓ This would shift the MPC curve upward by £25, aligning it with MSC. Producers would face the true social cost of production, reducing output from 200 million MWh toward the social optimum. Alternatively: a **tradable permit scheme** (cap-and-trade) setting a binding emissions cap. ✓ (2 marks — identification + brief explanation)

**2. Public transport (positive consumption externality):** A **subsidy** of £1.50 per passenger journey. ✓ This would lower the effective price to consumers, shifting the MPB curve upward by £1.50 to align with MSB. Ridership would increase from 300 million toward the social optimum of 450 million. Alternatively: direct **government provision** of free or heavily subsidised public transport. ✓ (2 marks)

**3. Overfishing (common pool resource):** **Individual Transferable Quotas (ITQs)** — a system of tradable fishing rights that caps the total catch at the maximum sustainable yield (350,000 tonnes). ✓ Each fishing vessel receives or purchases quotas, which can be traded. This creates property rights over the fishery, eliminating the "race to fish" and aligning private incentives with long-term sustainability. The total allowable catch (TAC) is set at the MSY of 350,000 tonnes. ✓ (2 marks)

**(2 marks per policy: 1 for identifying appropriate policy + 1 for explaining how it addresses the specific failure)**

---

**(b)** Two reasons government intervention may fail. **[4 marks]**

**Answer:**

**1. Information failure / imperfect knowledge:** Governments may lack the data to set policies correctly. ✓ For the coal tax, the true external cost of air pollution may be higher or lower than £25/MWh. If the tax is set too low, overproduction persists. If set too high, it creates deadweight loss from underproduction. For the fishery, the MSY of 350,000 tonnes is an estimate — if the true sustainable yield is lower, ITQs at 350,000 tonnes still lead to stock depletion. ✓

**2. Political pressures and regulatory capture:** Policy design is influenced by vested interests. ✓ Coal companies may lobby for lower taxes or exemptions; fishing industry groups may pressure for higher quotas. Politicians facing re-election may prioritise short-term employment over long-term sustainability. This results in policies that are **weaker than optimal** — a £10/MWh tax instead of £25, or TAC of 420,000 tonnes instead of 350,000 — undermining the correction of market failure. ✓

**(2 marks per reason: 1 for identification + 1 for application to the scenario)**

**(c)** CLASPP evaluation: Market-based vs command-and-control policies. **[12 marks]**

**Model answer (197 words):**

**Market-based policies** (taxes, tradable permits, subsidies) and **command-and-control** (bans, quotas, regulation) represent fundamentally different philosophies of government intervention. Neither is universally superior; their relative effectiveness depends on the **nature of the market failure** and the **institutional context**.

For coal power, a tradable permit scheme (market-based) is likely superior to mandated technology standards (command-and-control). Permits achieve the emissions target at **minimum total cost** by allowing firms with different abatement costs to trade — the market discovers the cheapest path to compliance. However, this assumes a functioning permit market with low transaction costs and robust monitoring — an **assumption** that may fail in countries with weak regulatory capacity.

For overfishing, ITQs (market-based) have proven effective in New Zealand and Iceland, but their **long-run** success depends on accurate TAC setting and enforcement against illegal fishing. A simple ban on fishing in certain zones (command-and-control) may be easier to enforce where monitoring capacity is limited.

For public transport, a **subsidy** (market-based) outperforms mandating ridership (command-and-control, which is impractical). The subsidy preserves consumer choice while correcting the price signal.

**Prioritisation** favours market-based instruments where the externality can be quantified and markets can function — they harness self-interest rather than fighting it. However, command-and-control remains essential as a **backstop**: where transaction costs are high, time is critical (e.g., species extinction), or political feasibility demands visible action. The optimal approach is a **hybrid**: market-based instruments as the primary tool, with regulatory floors as safeguards. In all three cases here, market-based policies are conditionally superior, but their effectiveness is contingent on institutional quality.

- **S** (Stakeholders): Firms, consumers, fishers, government, environment — 2 marks
- **P** (Pros & Cons): Balanced comparison across all three market failures — 2 marks
- **P** (Prioritisation): Hybrid recommendation with conditions — 2 marks

**Total: 12 marks (part c) | Overall Problem 25: 22 marks**
