### Problem 1 — Multiple Choice (2 marks)

**Answer: B** — Resources are limited while human wants are unlimited.

**Mark scheme:**
- 2 marks for selecting B.
- 0 marks for any other selection.

**Explanation:** Scarcity is the fundamental economic problem arising from finite resources and infinite human wants. It affects all societies regardless of wealth. Option A is incorrect because scarcity is not confined to the poor — even billionaires face scarcity of time. Option C describes government failure, and Option D confuses scarcity with inflation.

### Problem 2 — Calculation (3 marks)

**(a)** Opportunity cost is the value of the next best alternative forgone when a choice is made. ✓ (1 mark)

**(b)** The opportunity cost is $145,000 — the salary she gives up by leaving her job. ✓ (1 mark)

**(c)** Economic profit = Total revenue − (Explicit costs + Implicit costs)
= $210,000 − ($62,000 + $145,000)
= $210,000 − $207,000
= **$3,000** ✓ (1 mark)

**Mark scheme:**
- (a) 1 mark for a precise definition referencing "next best alternative forgone."
- (b) 1 mark for $145,000. Accept "her forgone salary" if the figure is stated.
- (c) 1 mark for correct calculation and answer of $3,000. Award the mark for correct method even if a minor arithmetic error is made, provided the method is clearly shown.

> **Common error:** In part (b), students often state the opportunity cost as $62,000 (explicit costs only) or $207,000 (the sum of all costs). The opportunity cost is specifically the value of the next best alternative — her forgone salary. In part (c), students confuse economic profit with accounting profit ($210,000 − $62,000 = $148,000). Economic profit accounts for both explicit and implicit costs.

### Problem 5 — Short Answer (3 marks)

**(a)** The law of increasing opportunity cost states that as the production of one good increases, the opportunity cost of producing additional units of that good — measured in terms of the other good forgone — rises. ✓ (1 mark)

**(b)** A concave PPC illustrates this law because resources are not equally suited to all types of production. ✓ (1 mark) When an economy shifts resources from manufacturing to agriculture, it first transfers those resources that are least suited to manufacturing and relatively better at agriculture — so the opportunity cost is initially low. As more resources are shifted, increasingly unsuitable resources must be used, causing the opportunity cost (the amount of manufactured goods sacrificed per additional unit of agricultural output) to rise. ✓ (1 mark)

**Mark scheme:**
- (a) 1 mark for a definition that captures "as production increases, opportunity cost per additional unit rises."
- (b) 1 mark for identifying resource heterogeneity as the cause. 1 mark for explaining the logic of transferring increasingly unsuitable resources. Accept references to "imperfect factor substitutability."

> **Common error:** Students sometimes state that the PPC is concave "because of scarcity." Scarcity explains why the PPC exists at all, not its shape. A straight-line PPC is also consistent with scarcity. Concavity specifically follows from increasing opportunity cost, which arises from resources not being equally efficient in all uses.

This shifts the entire PPC outward in the long run. Option A describes a movement from inefficiency to efficiency (actual growth, not potential growth). Option B describes a reallocation along an existing PPC. ### Problem 9 — Short Answer (2 marks)

**(a)** The ceteris paribus assumption means "all other things being equal" — it is the assumption that all variables other than those under immediate consideration are held constant. ✓ (1 mark)

**(b)** When modelling demand, economists want to isolate the relationship between price and quantity demanded. Without ceteris paribus, it would be impossible to determine whether a change in quantity demanded was caused by a price change or by some other factor such as a change in income, tastes, or the price of a substitute good. ✓ (1 mark)

**Mark scheme:**
- (a) 1 mark for "all other things being equal" or equivalent phrasing.
- (b) 1 mark for explaining the need to isolate the variable of interest.

> **Common error:** Students sometimes describe ceteris paribus as "everything stays the same," which would mean the variable being studied also cannot change — a contradiction. Ceteris paribus means *other* variables stay the same, allowing one variable to change so its effect can be observed.

### Problem 11 — Multiple Choice (2 marks)

**Answer: B** — Air available for breathing at sea level in a rural area.

**Mark scheme:** 2 marks for B.

**Explanation:** A free good is one that is abundant in supply relative to demand at zero price — its opportunity cost is zero. Clean air in a rural area satisfies this condition. Option A is an economic good provided at zero price to the consumer (but it has a positive opportunity cost to produce). Option C is a scholarship — a service with positive opportunity cost — provided without charge to the recipient. Option D is an economic good sold below market price through a subsidy.

> **Common error:** Students often select A or C, equating "zero price to the consumer" with "free good." A free good is defined by zero opportunity cost to society, not zero price to the individual consumer.

### Problem 12 — Calculation (3 marks)

**(a)** Increasing X from 50 to 100: Y falls from 750 to 650.
Opportunity cost = 750 − 650 = **100 units of Product Y**. ✓ (1 mark)

**(b)** Increasing X from 150 to 200: Y falls from 450 to 0.
Opportunity cost = 450 − 0 = **450 units of Product Y**. ✓ (1 mark)

**(c)** The opportunity cost rises from 100 Y (per 50 X) in part (a) to 450 Y (per 50 X) in part (b). This demonstrates **increasing opportunity cost**, meaning the PPC is **concave** (bowed outward from the origin). ✓ (1 mark)

**Mark scheme:**
- (a) 1 mark for 100 units of Y (or equivalent). Accept "750 − 650 = 100 Y."
- (b) 1 mark for 450 units of Y (or equivalent).
- (c) 1 mark for identifying concavity/increasing opportunity cost, explicitly linked to the calculated figures.

> **Common error:** Students calculate the per-unit opportunity cost (2 Y per X in part (a); 9 Y per X in part (b)) rather than the total opportunity cost of the specified interval. Both are acceptable provided the interval is clearly stated. In part (c), students sometimes state "the PPC is downward sloping" — this is true of all PPCs and does not answer the question about shape.

### Problem 15 — Multiple Choice (2 marks)

**Answer: C** — Both actual and potential growth.

**Mark scheme:** 2 marks for C.

**Explanation:** The outward shift of the PPC represents potential growth — the economy's productive capacity has increased. The movement to point Y on the new PPC (assuming Y represents a higher output level than X) also represents actual growth — more is being produced. Since the description states the economy moves from X on the old PPC to Y on the new, outward-shifted PPC, both types of growth are occurring.

### Problem 16 — Short Answer (3 marks)

**(a)** Any two of the following (1 mark each):

1. **Risk-bearing:** The entrepreneur invests personal capital and bears the financial risk of business failure — if the venture fails, the entrepreneur loses their investment while workers can seek employment elsewhere.
2. **Innovation:** The entrepreneur identifies market opportunities and develops new products, processes, or business models — e.g., introducing a new technology or combining existing resources in a novel way.
3. **Organisation/coordination:** The entrepreneur brings together land, labour, and capital, organising them into a productive enterprise.
4. **Decision-making:** The entrepreneur makes strategic decisions about what to produce, how to produce it, and at what scale.

✓✓ (2 marks)

**(b)** While both entrepreneurship and labour involve human effort, entrepreneurship is distinct because it involves **risk-bearing and the organisation of other factors**, whereas labour involves providing effort under the direction of others in return for a contractually fixed wage or salary. The entrepreneur receives **profit** (a residual and uncertain reward), while labour receives **wages** (a predetermined and contractually guaranteed payment). ✓ (1 mark)

**Mark scheme:**
- (a) 2 marks for two distinct, well-described functions. Award 1 mark if only one function is described or if two are listed with insufficient explanation.
- (b) 1 mark for identifying risk-bearing/profit vs. wages as the key distinction.

> **Common error:** Students list "hard work" or "making decisions" as entrepreneurial functions without explaining why these differ from what a senior manager (labour) does. The distinguishing feature is the nature of the reward — residual profit vs. contractual wages — and the bearing of non-insurable risk.

### Problem 17 — Multiple Choice (2 marks)

**Answer: B** — Confusing scarcity with a lack of usefulness.

**Mark scheme:** 2 marks for B.

**Explanation:** The student's argument reflects the paradox of value (diamond-water paradox). Scarcity in economics is about the relationship between availability and desire, not about physical necessity. Water is essential but typically abundant (low marginal utility, low price), while diamonds are non-essential but scarce (high marginal utility at the margin, high price). The student confuses the concept of scarcity (limited relative to wants) with usefulness or importance.

> **Common error:** Students select A (confusing total and marginal utility). While the diamond-water paradox is indeed explained by marginal vs. total utility, the question asks specifically about the **misconception** in the student's reasoning. The student's error is assuming that "essential = scarce," which is a confusion about the definition of scarcity itself — not (yet) a confusion about utility theory, which is a Unit 2 topic.

### Problem 18 — Short Answer (2 marks)

**(a)** Equilibrium is a state in an economic model where the quantity demanded equals the quantity supplied, and there is no tendency for change — all opposing forces are in balance. At the equilibrium price of $380,000, the number of homes buyers wish to purchase exactly equals the number sellers wish to sell. ✓ (1 mark)

**(b)** Any one valid reason (1 mark):

- Building material costs may fluctuate, shifting supply.
- Government intervention — such as a housing subsidy, tax change, or zoning regulation — may alter the market outcome.
- The model may omit relevant variables (e.g., speculative demand, interest rate changes, population growth).
- Consumer confidence or expectations about future prices may shift demand in ways not captured by the model.

**Mark scheme:**
- (a) 1 mark for identifying equilibrium as a state of balance with no tendency to change, referencing demand and supply.
- (b) 1 mark for a specific, plausible reason linked to the model's assumptions.

> **Common error:** Students define equilibrium as "the price where demand equals supply" without noting that this is a state of rest. The phrase "no tendency for change" is important — it distinguishes equilibrium from a mere intersection of curves in a temporary sense.

### Problem 19 — Evaluation (5 marks)

**Model answer — CLASPP structure (179 words):**

**Conclusion:** The statement is overly simplistic. While capital goods enable future growth, an economy cannot maximise welfare by indefinitely sacrificing consumer goods — a balance must be struck based on a society's stage of development and preferences.

**Limitations:** The argument ignores diminishing returns to capital investment. Building factories today yields growth, but continuous prioritisation may lead to overcapacity and wasted resources. It also overlooks that a malnourished or uneducated workforce (resulting from insufficient consumer goods like food and healthcare) undermines long-term productivity.

**Assumptions:** The statement assumes all capital goods investment translates efficiently into growth, that consumer goods provide only short-term satisfaction, and that the economy starts from a position where more capital goods are always beneficial. These are contestable: in a capital-intensive economy, additional capital may yield minimal returns.

**Stakeholders:** Current consumers lose welfare immediately from reduced access to goods. Future generations gain from expanded productive capacity. Workers may face short-term job losses in consumer-good industries but gain long-term employment opportunities.

**Prioritisation/Proof:** The optimal point on the PPC depends on societal time preference. Empirical evidence (e.g., South Korea's balanced investment-consumption growth path vs. the Soviet Union's overemphasis on heavy industry) suggests that extreme prioritisation of either good type is suboptimal. A mixed approach, periodically reassessed, best serves intertemporal welfare.

**Mark scheme:**
| Band | Descriptor | Marks |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Vague, descriptive response with no structure. One-sided argument. | 1 |
| 2 | Some structure, mentions stakeholders or trade-offs but lacks depth. | 2–3 |
| 3 | Structured response addressing multiple CLASPP elements. Recognises both sides. Uses specific examples/reasoning. | 4 |
| 4 | Fully structured CLASPP response (at least 5 of 6 elements addressed). Balanced, nuanced, with concrete reasoning. 150–200 words. | 5 |

> **Common error:** Students write a purely theoretical answer — "it depends on the PPC and opportunity cost" — without grounding their analysis in specific stakeholders, real-world examples, or explicit evaluation of assumptions. A strong IB answer must move beyond the diagram into applied reasoning. Weaker responses also tend to agree or disagree entirely with the statement rather than evaluating its conditions and limitations.
