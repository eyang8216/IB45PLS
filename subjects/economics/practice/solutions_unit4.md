### Question 1 — Comparative Advantage

**Answer: C**

**Step-by-step reasoning:**

Calculate opportunity costs for each country:

| Country | Wheat (kg) | Cloth (m) | OC of 1 kg wheat | OC of 1 m cloth |
|---------|------------|-----------|-------------------|-----------------|
| X       | 60         | 30        | 30/60 = **0.5 m** | 60/30 = **2 kg** |
| Y       | 40         | 10        | 10/40 = **0.25 m**| 40/10 = **4 kg** |

- **Comparative advantage in wheat**: Country Y (OC = 0.25 m) < Country X (OC = 0.5 m) → Y gives up less cloth per kg of wheat.
- **Comparative advantage in cloth**: Country X (OC = 2 kg) < Country Y (OC = 4 kg) → X gives up less wheat per metre of cloth.

Thus: Country X has comparative advantage in **cloth**, and Country Y has comparative advantage in **wheat**.

> **✓ 1 mark** for correct identification of option C.

> **Common mistake:** Students often confuse absolute advantage (higher output numbers) with comparative advantage (lower opportunity cost). Country X has absolute advantage in both goods, but comparative advantage only in cloth.

### Question 6 — Trade Creation vs Trade Diversion

**Answer: B**

**Reasoning:**

- **Trade creation**: joining a customs union shifts consumption from a high-cost domestic producer to a lower-cost partner → welfare gain.
- **Trade diversion**: joining a customs union shifts consumption from a low-cost non-member to a higher-cost member because the common external tariff (CET) makes the non-member uncompetitive → **welfare loss**.

Country M switches from low-cost Country N (non-member) to higher-cost Country P (member). This is trade diversion.

> **✓ 1 mark** for option B.

> **Common mistake:** Choosing A (trade creation). The defining feature of trade diversion is the switch from a *more efficient* non-member to a *less efficient* member — exactly what the question describes.

### Question 7 — J-Curve

**Answer: B**

**Reasoning:**

The J-curve describes the short-run vs. long-run effect of depreciation on the current account:

- **Short run**: Import contracts are fixed in quantity terms (inelastic demand). When the domestic currency depreciates, import prices in domestic-currency terms rise immediately. Export volumes have not yet adjusted. → **Current account worsens**.
- **Long run**: Export firms expand capacity, foreign buyers switch to cheaper domestic goods, importers find substitutes. If the Marshall-Lerner condition holds, the current account improves.

The question asks about "immediately following" depreciation → B is correct.

> **✓ 1 mark** for option B.

> **Common mistake:** Choosing A — it sounds intuitive that cheaper exports improve the current account, but this ignores the short-run rigidity of contracts and the immediate rise in import costs.

### Question 9 — Absolute and Comparative Advantage *(4 marks)*

**(a) Define absolute advantage. (1 mark)**

Absolute advantage exists when a country can produce **more output** of a good using the **same quantity of resources** (or the same output using fewer resources) compared to another country.

> **✓ 1 mark** for clear reference to greater output per unit of input / greater productivity.

Comparative advantage exists when a country can produce a good at a **lower opportunity cost** (in terms of the next-best alternative forgone) compared to another country.

### Question 12 — Determinants of Exchange Rates *(3 marks)*

Three factors that could cause an appreciation of the JPY against the USD:

1. **Higher interest rates in Japan**: If the Bank of Japan raises interest rates relative to the US Federal Reserve, Japanese financial assets become more attractive. Foreign investors demand JPY to purchase Japanese bonds → increased demand for JPY → appreciation.

3. **Foreign direct investment (FDI) inflows into Japan**: If foreign firms invest in Japanese factories or acquire Japanese companies, they must convert their currency into JPY → increased demand for JPY → appreciation.

**(b) One component of the financial account (1 mark):**

Any one from:

**(c) Why the BoP always sums to zero (1 mark):**

The balance of payments is constructed using **double-entry bookkeeping**. Every international transaction involves both a credit entry (inflow of funds) and an equal debit entry (outflow of funds). For example, when a country imports goods (debit in current account), it must pay for them — either by drawing down reserves, borrowing, or selling assets (credit in financial account). The two entries offset exactly, so the overall balance is always zero in an accounting sense.

> **✓ 1 mark** for reference to double-entry bookkeeping / every credit has an offsetting debit.

### Question 14 — Marshall-Lerner Condition *(3 marks)*

**(a) State the Marshall-Lerner condition (1 mark):**

The Marshall-Lerner condition states that a depreciation (or devaluation) of a country's currency will improve its current account balance **if the sum of the absolute price elasticities of demand for exports and imports is greater than 1**:

$$|PED_{exports}| + |PED_{imports}| > 1$$

> **✓ 1 mark** for correct statement including the "greater than 1" threshold.

---

**(b) Why depreciation may fail if ML is not satisfied (2 marks):**

If $|PED_{exports}| + |PED_{imports}| < 1$, then:

- The **volume** of exports rises by a smaller percentage than the **price** of exports falls (in foreign-currency terms) → export revenue in domestic currency may actually fall.
- The **volume** of imports falls by a smaller percentage than the **price** of imports rises (in domestic-currency terms) → import expenditure in domestic currency rises.

The net effect: export revenue rises insufficiently (or falls) while import expenditure rises → **current account worsens** rather than improves.

> **✓ 1 mark** for explaining that export volume response is insufficient to offset lower prices.  
> **✓ 1 mark** for explaining that import expenditure rises because quantity does not fall enough.

> **Common mistake:** Assuming depreciation always improves the current account. The ML condition shows this depends on elasticities.

### Question 17 — Quota Diagram and Quota Rent *(4 marks)*

**(a) Quota diagram (2 marks):**

The correct diagram should show:

- **Axes**: Price (vertical), Quantity (horizontal).
- **Demand curve (D)**: downward-sloping.
- **Supply curve (S_domestic)**: upward-sloping.
- **World price (P_w)**: horizontal line below domestic equilibrium.
- **Quota limit**: a vertical line to the right of Q₁ (domestic supply at P_w) that is shorter than free-trade imports (Q₄ − Q₁). Total supply becomes S_domestic + quota.
- The quota creates a new effective supply curve: S_domestic up to Q₁, then horizontal for the quota amount, then S_domestic above that.
- **New domestic price (P_q)**: where the new supply curve intersects demand — higher than P_w.
- **Quota rent area**: the rectangle between P_q and P_w over the quota volume (imports under quota). This is the difference between the domestic selling price and the world purchase price, multiplied by the quota quantity.

> **✓ 1 mark** for correctly drawn D, S, P_w, and quota-constrained supply.  
> **✓ 1 mark** for correctly labelling and shading the quota rent rectangle.

---

**(b) Quota rent explained (2 marks):**

**Quota rent** is the economic rent earned by the holders of import licences (the right to import under the quota). It equals $(P_q - P_w) \times \text{quota volume}$ — the premium importers can charge domestic buyers above the world price.

**Who captures it depends on the allocation system:**
- **Government auction of licences**: the government captures the rent (auction revenue = quota rent) — equivalent to tariff revenue.
- **First-come-first-served or historical allocation**: domestic importers capture the rent as windfall profits.
- **Voluntary export restraint (VER)**: the foreign exporting country administers the quota — foreign exporters capture the rent (one reason importing countries' welfare loss is larger under VERs).

> **✓ 1 mark** for correctly defining quota rent as (P_q − P_w) × quota quantity.  
> **✓ 1 mark** for explaining who captures rent under different systems.

> **Common mistake:** Confusing quota rent with government revenue. Under a tariff, government collects revenue; under a quota, the rent may go to private actors (or the government, if licences are auctioned).

### Question 19 — Tariff Welfare Analysis *(8 marks)*

Given:
- $Q_d = 200 - 2P$
- $Q_s = -40 + 2P$
- $P_w = €30$
- $t = €10$

---

**(a) Free-trade quantities and imports (2 marks):**

At $P_w = 30$:

$$Q_d = 200 - 2(30) = 200 - 60 = \mathbf{140} \text{ (thousand units)}$$

$$Q_s = -40 + 2(30) = -40 + 60 = \mathbf{20} \text{ (thousand units)}$$

$$\text{Imports} = Q_d - Q_s = 140 - 20 = \mathbf{120} \text{ (thousand units)}$$

> **✓ 1 mark** for correct Qd and Qs.  
> **✓ 1 mark** for correct import volume.

---

**(b) Post-tariff quantities and imports (2 marks):**

At $P_w + t = 30 + 10 = 40$:

$$Q_d = 200 - 2(40) = 200 - 80 = \mathbf{120} \text{ (thousand units)}$$

$$Q_s = -40 + 2(40) = -40 + 80 = \mathbf{40} \text{ (thousand units)}$$

$$\text{Imports} = Q_d - Q_s = 120 - 40 = \mathbf{80} \text{ (thousand units)}$$

> **✓ 1 mark** for correct Qd and Qs at P = 40.  
> **✓ 1 mark** for correct new import volume.

---

**(c) Welfare calculations (4 marks):**

**Preliminary — find intercepts:**
- Demand choke price: $Q_d = 0 \Rightarrow 0 = 200 - 2P \Rightarrow P = 100$
- Supply minimum price: $Q_s = 0 \Rightarrow 0 = -40 + 2P \Rightarrow P = 20$

---

**(i) Loss of consumer surplus (1 mark):**

Consumer surplus is the area below the demand curve and above the price line.

**Method — area of trapezoid** (or difference of two triangles):

CS at P = 30: $\frac{1}{2} \times (100 - 30) \times 140 = \frac{1}{2} \times 70 \times 140 = 4,900$

CS at P = 40: $\frac{1}{2} \times (100 - 40) \times 120 = \frac{1}{2} \times 60 \times 120 = 3,600$

**Loss of CS** = $4,900 - 3,600 = \mathbf{€1,300 \text{ thousand}}$

Alternatively: trapezoid = $\frac{(140 + 120)}{2} \times (40 - 30) = 130 \times 10 = 1,300$.

> **✓ 1 mark** for correct loss of consumer surplus (€1,300k).

---

**(ii) Gain in producer surplus (1 mark):**

PS at P = 30: $\frac{1}{2} \times (30 - 20) \times 20 = \frac{1}{2} \times 10 \times 20 = 100$

PS at P = 40: $\frac{1}{2} \times (40 - 20) \times 40 = \frac{1}{2} \times 20 \times 40 = 400$

**Gain in PS** = $400 - 100 = \mathbf{€300 \text{ thousand}}$

Alternatively: trapezoid = $\frac{(20 + 40)}{2} \times (40 - 30) = 30 \times 10 = 300$.

> **✓ 1 mark** for correct gain in producer surplus (€300k).

---

**(iii) Government tariff revenue (1 mark):**

$$\text{Revenue} = t \times \text{imports after tariff} = 10 \times 80 = \mathbf{€800 \text{ thousand}}$$

> **✓ 1 mark** for correct tariff revenue (€800k).

---

**(iv) Deadweight loss (1 mark):**

**Method 1 — residual**: Loss of CS − Gain in PS − Government revenue = $1,300 - 300 - 800 = \mathbf{€200 \text{ thousand}}$.

**Method 2 — sum of two triangles**:
- Production DWL: $\frac{1}{2} \times (40 - 20) \times (40 - 30) = \frac{1}{2} \times 20 \times 10 = 100$
- Consumption DWL: $\frac{1}{2} \times (140 - 120) \times (40 - 30) = \frac{1}{2} \times 20 \times 10 = 100$
- Total DWL: $100 + 100 = \mathbf{€200 \text{ thousand}}$

> **✓ 1 mark** for correct deadweight loss (€200k).

---

**Summary table:**

| Item | Value (€ thousands) |
|------|---------------------|
| Loss of consumer surplus | 1,300 |
| Gain in producer surplus | 300 |
| Government tariff revenue | 800 |
| Deadweight loss | **200** |

### Question 22 — Evaluate the use of tariffs as a tool of trade protection. *(10 marks)*

**Model Answer (187 words):**

Tariffs — taxes on imported goods — remain a widely used tool of trade protection, but their effectiveness is highly contested.

**Pros:** Tariffs protect domestic infant industries from foreign competition, allowing them to achieve economies of scale. They generate government revenue, particularly important for developing countries with weak tax systems. The 2018 US steel tariffs (25%) protected domestic producers and preserved manufacturing jobs in the short term.

**Cons:** Tariffs create deadweight loss through production and consumption inefficiencies. Domestic consumers pay higher prices, and downstream industries (e.g., US car manufacturers using imported steel) face higher input costs, reducing their international competitiveness. Retaliation is likely — the EU imposed counter-tariffs on US goods, harming other sectors.

**Stakeholders:** Domestic producers gain; consumers and exporting industries lose; government gains revenue. The net welfare effect is negative for a small country (deadweight loss triangle).

**Limitations and assumptions:** The analysis assumes perfectly competitive markets and ignores dynamic benefits of protection (learning-by-doing) or strategic trade policy arguments.

**Conclusion:** Tariffs are a blunt instrument that almost always reduce aggregate welfare. While they may be justified temporarily for infant industries or national security, alternative policies such as production subsidies achieve protection at lower welfare cost. The priority should be gradual liberalisation with adjustment assistance for affected workers.

### Question 23 — Evaluate the view that developing countries should adopt a fixed exchange rate regime rather than a floating exchange rate regime. *(10 marks)*

**Model Answer (192 words):**

The choice between fixed and floating exchange rates involves fundamental trade-offs, especially for developing countries.

**Arguments for fixed rates:** A fixed peg (e.g., to USD or EUR) provides exchange rate certainty, encouraging trade and foreign investment by eliminating currency risk. It imposes monetary discipline — governments cannot finance deficits through inflation, as this would deplete reserves. China's de facto dollar peg (1994–2005) supported its export-led growth strategy. The CFA franc zone in West Africa has delivered low inflation through its euro peg.

**Arguments against fixed rates:** Maintaining a peg requires large foreign exchange reserves, which have high opportunity costs. Fixed rates eliminate independent monetary policy — the central bank cannot lower interest rates during a recession. Speculative attacks can destroy a peg (e.g., the 1997 Asian Financial Crisis, where Thailand exhausted reserves defending the baht before being forced to float). Floating rates allow automatic current account adjustment.

**Stakeholders:** Exporters and foreign investors benefit from fixed-rate certainty; domestic policymakers lose monetary autonomy; speculators may profit from attacking overvalued pegs.

**Limitations:** This is not a binary choice — managed floats and crawling pegs offer intermediate options.

**Conclusion:** For most developing countries, a managed float or crawling peg is preferable to either extreme. A pure fixed peg is only viable with strong institutions, ample reserves, and capital controls. The priority should be building institutional credibility regardless of regime choice.

### Question 24 — Evaluate the role of FDI and MNCs in promoting economic development in low-income countries. *(10 marks)*

**Model Answer (198 words):**

FDI and MNCs are often promoted as engines of development, but their impact is complex and conditional.

**Benefits:** MNCs bring capital, technology transfer, managerial expertise, and access to global markets. Workers acquire skills through training, raising human capital (positive externality). FDI creates formal-sector employment and contributes to tax revenue. Vietnam's Samsung factories employ over 100,000 workers and have catalysed a local electronics supply chain, contributing significantly to Vietnam's rapid HDI improvement.

**Drawbacks:** Profits are often repatriated, limiting the multiplier effect within the host economy. MNCs may exploit weak labour and environmental regulations. They can crowd out local firms through superior technology and economies of scale. "Race to the bottom" dynamics may suppress wages and corporate tax rates (tax competition). Resource-extractive FDI (e.g., mining in the DRC) may cause environmental damage and "resource curse" effects without broad-based development.

**Stakeholders:** Host-country workers may gain employment but face precarious conditions; local entrepreneurs may be displaced; the government gains tax revenue but may lose policy autonomy.

**Assumptions and limitations:** The benefits of FDI depend on absorptive capacity — countries need adequate infrastructure, education, and institutions to capture spillovers. Linkage effects are not automatic.

**Conclusion:** FDI can promote development when host countries have strong regulatory frameworks, policies to encourage joint ventures and local sourcing, and reinvestment of tax revenues into public goods. FDI should complement, not replace, domestic investment strategies.

### Question 25 — Evaluate the relative merits of ISI versus export promotion as trade strategies for economic development. *(10 marks)*

**Model Answer (199 words):**

Import substitution industrialisation (ISI) and export promotion represent contrasting development paths, and historical experience strongly favours one.

**ISI:** Popular in Latin America (1950s–1970s), ISI protects domestic infant industries through high tariffs, quotas, and state ownership. Brazil and Argentina developed manufacturing capacity behind trade barriers. **Pros:** Builds domestic industrial base; reduces reliance on volatile commodity exports. **Cons:** Protected industries lack incentive to become efficient; consumers pay higher prices; overvalued exchange rates hurt agricultural exports; chronic balance-of-payments problems emerge as capital goods imports rise. ISI ultimately failed in most of Latin America, contributing to the 1980s "lost decade."

**Export promotion:** The East Asian "Tiger" economies (South Korea, Taiwan, Singapore) pursued outward-oriented strategies: competitive exchange rates, investment in education, selective government intervention, and integration into global value chains. **Pros:** Firms face global competition, driving efficiency; export earnings finance imports; technology transfer occurs through trade. **Cons:** Vulnerability to external demand shocks; may increase inequality initially.

**Stakeholders:** Under ISI, protected industrialists and urban workers gain; agricultural exporters and consumers lose. Under export promotion, skilled workers and export sectors gain; import-competing sectors lose.

**Conclusion:** The historical record strongly favours export promotion. However, the East Asian model involved significant state intervention (not pure free markets), and developing countries today face a more protectionist global environment. A pragmatic, context-specific hybrid strategy may be optimal.
