### Problem 1 · Circular Flow — Leakages

**Correct Answer: C**

**Reasoning (step-by-step):**

1. In the four-sector circular flow model (households, firms, government, financial sector, foreign sector), **injections** are flows that add spending into the income-expenditure stream *without arising from household income*. The three injections are:
   - **Government spending (G)**
   - **Investment (I)** by firms
   - **Exports (X)** — spending by foreigners

2. **Leakages** (withdrawals) are flows that remove income from the circular flow before it returns as spending. The three leakages are:
   - **Saving (S)** — income held in financial institutions
   - **Taxation (T)** — income paid to government
   - **Imports (M)** — spending on foreign goods

3. Checking each option:
   - **A**: Government spending on infrastructure → an **injection** ✓
   - **B**: Exports → an **injection** ✓
   - **C**: Household saving → a **leakage**, not an injection ✗ ← **this is the answer**
   - **D**: Investment spending → an **injection** ✓

> ✓ **1 mark** for correctly identifying C as the only leakage among the options.

> **Common mistake:** Students confuse saving as "money entering banks" with an injection into the circular flow. Saving is money *withdrawn* from the spending stream; it only becomes an injection if banks lend it out as investment. The distinction is that saving itself is the leakage — subsequent lending for investment is a separate injection.

### Problem 5 · Aggregate Demand — Slope

**Correct Answer: C**

**Reasoning (step-by-step):**

1. The AD curve slopes downward for three reasons (the three effects):

   - **Wealth effect (Pigou effect)**: As the price level falls, the real value (purchasing power) of households' nominal wealth (cash, savings, bonds) increases → consumption (C) rises.
   - **Interest rate effect**: A lower price level reduces the transactions demand for money → lower interest rates → investment (I) and consumption of durables rise.
   - **Exchange rate effect (net exports effect)**: A lower domestic price level makes exports relatively cheaper and imports relatively more expensive → net exports (X − M) rise.

2. Checking each option:

   - **B**: Incorrect — the central bank does not *always* raise rates; the interest rate effect works through money demand, not central bank policy
   - **C**: Correctly describes the wealth effect ← **correct**
   - **D**: Incorrect — when the price level rises, exports become *more* expensive, so net exports *decrease*, not increase

> ✓ **1 mark** for correctly linking the downward-sloping AD to the wealth effect.

> **Common mistake:** Confusing AD slope with AS slope. Option A describes why the SRAS curve slopes upward (profit motive), not the AD curve. Also, option D correctly notes the direction of net exports but reverses the logic.

**Correct Answer: B**

### Problem 9 · Phillips Curve

**Correct Answer: B**

**Reasoning (step-by-step):**

1. The **short-run Phillips curve (SRPC)** shows an inverse relationship between the **unemployment rate** and the **inflation rate**.

2. As unemployment falls (the economy moves leftward along the SRPC), the labour market tightens:
   - Fewer workers available → firms bid up wages to attract scarce labour
   - Higher wages → higher production costs → firms raise prices
   - Result: **higher inflation**

3. A movement from 6% to 4% unemployment is a **decrease in unemployment**:
   - This is a movement *upward and leftward* along the SRPC
   - Therefore, inflation **increases**

4. Checking options:
   - **A**: Incorrect — lower unemployment is associated with higher (not lower) inflation along the SRPC
   - **B**: Correct — an increase in the inflation rate ← **correct**
   - **C**: Incorrect — the long-run Phillips curve (LRPC) is vertical at the natural rate of unemployment; an SRPC movement does not shift the LRPC
   - **D**: Incorrect — the inverse relationship means inflation *does* change

> ✓ **1 mark** for correctly applying the inverse SRPC relationship.

> **Common mistake:** Thinking lower unemployment → lower inflation (intuitively, "a better economy means lower prices"). The Phillips curve shows the opposite: a booming economy with low unemployment puts upward pressure on wages and prices.

### Problem 10 · Fiscal Policy — Automatic Stabilisers

**Correct Answer: C**

**Reasoning (step-by-step):**

1. **Automatic stabilisers** are fiscal mechanisms that **automatically** counteract fluctuations in the business cycle **without discretionary government action**:
   - They are built into the tax and transfer system.
   - In a recession: tax revenues fall (lower incomes → lower tax bills), and transfer payments rise (more unemployment benefits, welfare payments).
   - In a boom: tax revenues rise, transfer payments fall.

2. Checking each option:
   - **A**: Infrastructure spending bill → **discretionary** fiscal policy (requires an act of Parliament)
   - **B**: Central bank lowering rates → **monetary policy**, not fiscal policy
   - **C**: Tax revenues declining and unemployment benefits rising automatically → **automatic stabiliser** ← **correct**
   - **D**: Tariff increase → **trade policy** (protectionism), discretionary

3. The key distinction: automatic stabilisers operate **without new legislation**. The tax code and welfare system are already in place; they respond automatically to changes in economic conditions.

> ✓ **1 mark** for correctly distinguishing automatic (built-in) from discretionary fiscal policy.

> **Common mistake:** Confusing any government action during a recession as automatic. If Parliament must vote on it, it's discretionary. Also, confusing monetary policy (option B) with fiscal policy — only options A, C, and D are fiscal.

### Problem 11 · Circular Flow — Diagram

#### (a) Four-sector circular flow diagram **[4 marks]**

**Correct diagram description:**

Draw a diagram with the following labelled elements:

- **Five sectors** shown as boxes or nodes:
  - **Households** (top or centre-left)
  - **Firms** (bottom or centre-right)
  - **Government** (one side)
  - **Financial sector** (banks, one side)
  - **Foreign sector** (one side)

- **Inner flow** (real flows):
  - Households provide **factors of production** (land, labour, capital, entrepreneurship) to firms

  - **Government spending (G)** — from government to firms
  - **Investment (I)** — from financial sector to firms (via lending)
  - **Exports (X)** — from foreign sector to firms

- **Three leakages** (withdrawn from the inner flow):
  - **Taxation (T)** — from households/firms to government
  - **Saving (S)** — from households to financial sector
  - **Imports (M)** — from households/firms to foreign sector

- **Important**: The diagram must show that injections = leakages in equilibrium (S + T + M = I + G + X).

> ✓ **1 mark** for showing the basic inner/outer flow between households and firms.
> ✓ **1 mark** for correctly adding and labelling the government, financial, and foreign sectors.
> ✓ **1 mark** for correctly identifying and labelling the three injections (I + G + X).
> ✓ **1 mark** for correctly identifying and labelling the three leakages (S + T + M).

#### (b) Effect of increased MPS on the circular flow **[3 marks]**

**Answer:**

1. The marginal propensity to save (MPS) rising from 0.2 to 0.35 means households save 35% (instead of 20%) of each additional dollar of income.

2. **Effect on circular flow**: Saving is a **leakage**. A higher MPS increases the leakage from the circular flow — more income is diverted to the financial sector rather than being spent on consumption (C). This means: **leakages (S + T + M) > injections (I + G + X)**.

3. **Why the economy may contract**: Reduced consumption (C) is the largest component of aggregate demand (AD). With lower consumption, AD shifts left. Firms respond by reducing output and laying off workers → incomes fall further → consumption falls again (negative multiplier effect). The economy enters a **contractionary spiral**. The fall in AD may create a **deflationary (recessionary) gap** where actual GDP < potential GDP (Yf).

> ✓ **1 mark** for identifying saving as a leakage.
> ✓ **1 mark** for explaining that higher MPS reduces consumption spending.
> ✓ **1 mark** for linking reduced consumption to falling AD, lower output, and economic contraction (the multiplier effect in reverse).

#### (c) Government spending offset with MPW = 0.4 **[3 marks]**

**Answer:**

1. The **marginal propensity to withdraw (MPW)** = MPS + MPT + MPM = 0.4 (given).

2. The **Keynesian multiplier (k)**:

   $$k = \frac{1}{\text{MPW}} = \frac{1}{0.4} = 2.5$$

3. Total impact on GDP:

   $$\Delta \text{GDP} = k \times \Delta G = 2.5 \times \$40\text{ billion} = \textbf{\$100 billion}$$

4. The \$40 billion increase in government spending (an injection) generates a \$100 billion total increase in GDP through successive rounds of spending and re-spending. This offsets the initial contraction by more than the original fall in consumption, helping restore the circular flow to equilibrium where injections = leakages.

> ✓ **1 mark** for correctly stating the multiplier formula: k = 1/MPW.
> ✓ **1 mark** for calculating k = 2.5.
> ✓ **1 mark** for calculating ΔGDP = \$100 billion and explaining the offsetting mechanism.

> **Common mistake:** Forgetting to calculate the multiplier and simply stating that \$40 bn of government spending offsets \$40 bn of lost consumption. The multiplier means the impact is amplified beyond the initial injection.

### Problem 12 · GDP — Expenditure Approach Components

#### (a) Define GDP using the expenditure approach **[3 marks]**

**Answer:**

Gross Domestic Product (GDP) is the total market value of all **final goods and services** produced within a country's borders in a given time period (usually one year).

Using the expenditure approach:

$$\text{GDP} = C + I + G + (X - M)$$

Where:
- **C = Consumption expenditure**: Spending by households on durable goods, non-durable goods, and services
- **I = Investment**: Spending by firms on capital goods (machinery, equipment, buildings), inventory changes, and new residential construction
- **G = Government spending**: Government consumption expenditure and gross investment (excludes transfer payments)
- **X = Exports**: Spending by foreigners on domestically produced goods and services
- **M = Imports**: Spending by domestic residents on foreign-produced goods and services (subtracted because included in C, I, and G)

> ✓ **1 mark** for the full equation.
> ✓ **1 mark** for naming all four main components (C, I, G, and net exports).
> ✓ **1 mark** for correctly explaining that (X − M) represents net exports.

#### (c) Why GDP may overstate economic activity with environmental degradation **[3 marks]**

**Answer:**

GDP may overstate the true level of economic activity for three reasons:

1. **GDP treats environmental damage as output**: The \$80 bn from mining counts as positive economic output in GDP. However, the associated environmental degradation — polluted water, destroyed ecosystems, health problems — is **not subtracted** from GDP.

2. **GDP counts "defensive" expenditures as growth**: If the government or firms later spend money to clean up the pollution or treat related illnesses, this spending *adds* to GDP again, creating a perverse double-counting of the environmental damage as "economic activity."

3. **GDP ignores depletion of natural capital**: Mining extracts non-renewable resources. GDP treats this as income generation but does not account for the reduction in the country's natural resource wealth — it confuses **drawing down assets** with **creating income**.

In short, GDP measures *market transactions*, not *well-being* or *sustainability*.

> ✓ **1 mark** for noting that environmental costs are not subtracted from GDP.
> ✓ **1 mark** for explaining that cleanup/healthcare spending from degradation further inflates GDP.
> ✓ **1 mark** for recognising that GDP ignores natural capital depletion.

#### (d) Distinguish between GDP and GNI **[2 marks]**

**Answer:**

| Criterion | GDP | GNI (Gross National Income) |
|-----------|-----|-----------------------------|
| **Definition** | Value of output produced **within a country's borders** | Total income earned by a country's **residents** (wherever the production occurs) |
| **Formula** | C + I + G + (X − M) | GDP + Net factor income from abroad |
| **Key difference** | Geographic basis | Ownership/residence basis |

**Example**: If a Japanese-owned factory produces cars in Country Z, that output counts in Country Z's **GDP** (production within borders) but the profits repatriated to Japan are excluded from Country Z's **GNI**.

> ✓ **1 mark** for correctly defining GDP (geographic) vs GNI (residence/ownership).
> ✓ **1 mark** for stating the formula link: GNI = GDP + net factor income from abroad.

> **Common mistake:** Confusing GNI with GDP. For countries with many multinational corporations repatriating profits (e.g., Ireland, Singapore), GNI can be significantly lower than GDP.

### Problem 13 · Output Gaps — AD/AS Diagram

#### (a) Define recessionary and inflationary gaps **[4 marks]**

**Answer:**

A **recessionary gap** (also called a deflationary gap) exists when the equilibrium level of real GDP is **below** the full-employment level of output (Yf). The economy is operating with unemployed resources — there is a shortfall of aggregate demand relative to the economy's productive capacity.

An **inflationary gap** exists when the equilibrium level of real GDP is **above** the full-employment level of output (Yf). The economy is operating beyond its sustainable capacity — aggregate demand exceeds what the economy can produce at full employment, creating upward pressure on the price level.

| Feature | Recessionary Gap | Inflationary Gap |
|---------|-----------------|------------------|
| Equilibrium vs Yf | Y < Yf | Y > Yf |
| Unemployment | High (cyclical) | Very low (below natural rate) |
| Price pressure | Downward (disinflation/deflation) | Upward (demand-pull inflation) |

> ✓ **2 marks** for correctly defining the recessionary gap (Y < Yf, unemployment).
> ✓ **2 marks** for correctly defining the inflationary gap (Y > Yf, inflationary pressure).

#### (c) Self-correction to Yf according to monetarist model **[2 marks]**

**Answer:**

According to the monetarist/neoclassical model, the economy **self-corrects** automatically:

1. At Y₁ (< Yf), there is high **unemployment** — more workers are seeking jobs than firms need. This puts **downward pressure on wages**.

2. As wages fall, firms' **production costs decrease**. Lower costs shift the **SRAS curve to the right** (from SRAS₁ to SRAS₂).

3. The new equilibrium occurs where AD now intersects the new SRAS at Yf, but at a **lower price level** (P₂ < P₁). The economy returns to full employment **without government intervention**.

The key monetarist assumption: **wages and prices are flexible** in the long run, enabling this automatic self-correction mechanism.

> ✓ **1 mark** for explaining that high unemployment → falling wages → lower production costs.
> ✓ **1 mark** for describing the rightward shift of SRAS restoring equilibrium at Yf.

> **Common mistake:** Using Keynesian logic (AD must shift right via government intervention) to explain monetarist self-correction. Monetarists believe SRAS shifts, not AD.

#### (b) Why Country B has higher HDI despite slightly lower GDP per capita **[4 marks]**

#### (c) One limitation of HDI as a measure of well-being **[3 marks]**

**Answer:**

**Limitation: HDI does not capture inequality within each dimension.** The HDI uses **national averages** for life expectancy, education, and income. It therefore masks significant disparities between:
- Different regions within a country (urban vs rural)
- Different ethnic or socioeconomic groups
- Men and women (gender inequality)

A country could have a high average HDI while a large portion of the population — e.g., an ethnic minority or rural poor — experiences far worse outcomes.

**Other acceptable limitations** (one fully explained is sufficient):
- **Environmental quality**: HDI does not account for pollution, resource depletion, or climate change impacts on well-being.
- **Political freedom and human rights**: A country may score well on HDI while restricting civil liberties (e.g., some high-HDI authoritarian states).
- **Subjective well-being**: HDI does not measure happiness, life satisfaction, or mental health.

> ✓ **1 mark** for identifying the limitation.
> ✓ **2 marks** for a clear explanation of how this undermines HDI as a well-being measure.

### Problem 15 · Aggregate Demand — Determinants

#### (a) Define aggregate demand (AD) **[2 marks]**

**Answer:**

Aggregate demand (AD) is the **total amount of spending** on domestically produced goods and services at each **average price level** in an economy over a given time period (usually one year).

$$\text{AD} = C + I + G + (X - M)$$

The AD curve is downward-sloping, showing an inverse relationship between the average price level and the quantity of real GDP demanded (due to the wealth effect, interest rate effect, and exchange rate effect).

> ✓ **1 mark** for the definition (total spending on domestic output at each price level).
> ✓ **1 mark** for stating the components: C + I + G + (X − M).

---

#### (b) AD component and direction for each event **[8 marks]**

**(i) The central bank cuts interest rates from 5.0% to 3.5%**

- **AD component affected**: **Investment (I)** — and also consumption of durable goods (part of C)
- **Direction**: **AD increases**
- **Reasoning**: Lower interest rates reduce the cost of borrowing for firms (making investment projects more profitable) and for households (making mortgages, car loans, and credit cheaper). This stimulates spending on capital goods and consumer durables. The AD curve shifts **right**.

> ✓ **1 mark** for correctly identifying I (or I and C).
> ✓ **1 mark** for stating AD increases.

**(ii) Households expect a recession and reduce spending on durable goods**

- **AD component affected**: **Consumption (C)**
- **Direction**: **AD decreases**
- **Reasoning**: Consumer confidence falls — households become pessimistic about future income and employment. They postpone purchases of durable goods (cars, appliances, furniture) and increase precautionary saving. This reduces the consumption component of AD. The AD curve shifts **left**.

> ✓ **1 mark** for correctly identifying C.
> ✓ **1 mark** for stating AD decreases.

**(iii) The exchange rate appreciates by 12% against major trading partners**

- **AD component affected**: **Net exports (X − M)**
- **Direction**: **AD decreases**
- **Reasoning**: An appreciation makes the domestic currency stronger → domestic exports become **more expensive** for foreigners (X falls) and imports become **cheaper** for domestic residents (M rises). Both effects reduce net exports (X − M). The AD curve shifts **left**.

- **AD component affected**: **Government spending (G)**
- **Direction**: **AD increases**
- **Reasoning**: Government expenditure on infrastructure (roads, bridges, energy projects) is a direct component of G in the AD equation. This is an injection into the circular flow. The AD curve shifts **right** — and the total impact may be amplified by the multiplier effect.

> ✓ **1 mark** for stating AD increases.

> **Common mistake (across all parts):** Confusing a *movement along* the AD curve with a *shift of* the AD curve. A movement along AD is caused by a change in the **price level**; a shift of AD is caused by changes in any of the components (C, I, G, X, M) *independent of* the price level. ## Problem 16 · Short-Run Aggregate Supply — Shifts

---

### (a) Two reasons why the SRAS curve is upward sloping **[4 marks]**

**Reason 1 — Sticky nominal wages:**
In the short run, nominal wages are fixed by contracts, minimum-wage laws, or worker resistance to pay cuts. When the general price level rises, real wages (W/P) fall, so labour becomes cheaper in real terms. Firms respond by hiring more workers and expanding output. ✓ 1 mark for identifying sticky wages; ✓ 1 mark for linking lower real wages → higher output.

**Reason 2 — Sticky input prices / menu costs:**
Some firms face fixed input prices in the short run (e.g. energy contracts, rents). When the price level rises but input costs remain unchanged, profit margins widen, incentivising firms to increase production. Alternatively, firms may misinterpret a general price increase as a rise in the relative price of their own product (misperception theory), leading them to supply more. ✓ 1 mark for identifying sticky input prices or misperception; ✓ 1 mark for linking to profit incentive → increased output.

### (b) SRAS shifts — three scenarios **[6 marks]**

**(b)(i) Sharp rise in global oil prices increases firms' energy costs by 25%** **[2 marks]**

- **Direction:** SRAS shifts **left** (decreases).
- **Reason:** Higher energy costs raise firms' production costs at every output level. At any given price level, firms now supply less because profitability is squeezed. This is a negative supply shock.
- ✓ 1 mark for "shifts left / decreases"; ✓ 1 mark for linking higher energy costs to reduced supply.

**(b)(ii) New technology reduces average production costs in manufacturing by 15%** **[2 marks]**

- **Direction:** SRAS shifts **right** (increases).
- **Reason:** Technological improvement raises productivity — the same inputs produce more output, so unit costs fall. Firms can profitably supply more at every price level. This is a positive supply shock.

### (c) Why the economy can remain stuck below Yf — sticky wages **[4 marks]**

According to Keynes, the economy can remain at a below-full-employment equilibrium indefinitely because there is **no automatic mechanism** that restores Yf — unlike the monetarist model where flexible wages and prices self-correct the economy.

- Minimum-wage legislation that sets a legal wage floor.

## Problem 20 · Monetary Policy — Transmission Mechanism

---

### (a) Define monetary policy and the responsible institution **[3 marks]**

**Monetary policy** is the use of interest rates, money supply, and credit controls by the central bank to influence aggregate demand and achieve macroeconomic objectives such as price stability, full employment, and economic growth.
- ✓ 1 mark for definition referencing interest rates / money supply.
- ✓ 1 mark for mentioning influence on AD / macroeconomic objectives.

The institution typically responsible is the **central bank** (e.g. the US Federal Reserve, the European Central Bank, the Bank of England). Central banks are often independent of government to insulate monetary policy from political pressure.
- ✓ 1 mark for naming the central bank (with or without an example).

---

### (b) Transmission mechanism — from 4.0% to 2.5% policy rate **[5 marks]**

The transmission mechanism traces how a change in the central bank's policy rate affects real GDP through multiple channels:

**Link 1 — Lower policy rate → Lower market interest rates:**
The central bank cuts its policy rate (e.g. the federal funds rate) from 4.0% to 2.5%. Commercial banks, which borrow from and lend to the central bank, pass this on by lowering the interest rates they charge on loans (mortgages, business loans, credit cards).
- ✓ 1 mark for link: policy rate → market rates.

**Link 2 — Cheaper credit → Increased consumption (C) and investment (I):**
Lower interest rates reduce the cost of borrowing for households (cheaper mortgages, car loans) and firms (cheaper loans for capital expansion). This incentivises higher consumption of durable goods and higher business investment. Additionally, lower rates reduce the return on saving, encouraging spending.
- ✓ 1 mark for link: lower rates → higher C and I.

**Link 3 — Asset prices and wealth effect:**
Lower interest rates make bonds less attractive relative to equities, driving up share prices and housing prices. Households feel wealthier (wealth effect) and increase consumption.
- ✓ 1 mark for link: lower rates → higher asset prices → wealth effect → higher C.

**Link 4 — Exchange rate depreciation → Higher net exports (X − M):**
Lower domestic interest rates make holding the domestic currency less attractive for foreign investors, causing the exchange rate to depreciate. A weaker currency makes exports cheaper and imports more expensive, improving net exports.
- ✓ 1 mark for link: lower rates → depreciation → higher (X − M).

**Link 5 — Increased AD → Higher real GDP:**
The combined increase in C, I, and (X − M) shifts the AD curve to the right. Assuming the economy is not at full capacity, real GDP rises (and unemployment falls). The overall effect: policy rate 4.0% → 2.5% → higher AD → higher real GDP.
- ✓ 1 mark for final link: higher AD → higher real GDP.

*Award marks for any three well-explained links plus the final AD → GDP link. The complete chain is 5 marks.*

---

### (c) One limitation of monetary policy during a deep recession **[2 marks]**

**Limitation: The liquidity trap.**

In a deep recession, interest rates may already be near zero (the zero lower bound), so the central bank cannot cut rates further to stimulate borrowing. Moreover, even if rates are low, pessimistic firms may not want to invest (low business confidence) and risk-averse households may prefer to save rather than spend — a situation Keynes called the "liquidity trap." Monetary policy becomes ineffective because the transmission mechanism breaks down: low rates fail to boost C and I.

- ✓ 1 mark for identifying the liquidity trap / zero lower bound.
- ✓ 1 mark for explaining why it renders monetary policy ineffective.

**Alternative acceptable answer:** banks may be unwilling to lend even with low policy rates (credit crunch); households and firms may be too indebted to borrow more (balance-sheet recession); time lags mean policy effects arrive too late.

### (a) Calculate the marginal propensity to withdraw (MPW) **[3 marks]**

The MPW is the fraction of each additional dollar of income that leaks out of the circular flow through saving, taxation, and imports.

**MPW = MPS + MRT + MPM**

- **MPS** (marginal propensity to save) = 1 − MPC = 1 − 0.75 = **0.25**
- **MRT** (marginal rate of taxation) = **0.20**
- **MPM** (marginal propensity to import) = **0.10**

### (c) Total impact on real GDP of \$18 billion spending increase **[3 marks]**

**ΔGDP = k × ΔG**
= 1.8181… × \$18 billion
= **\$32.73 billion**

- ✓ 1 mark for correct formula (ΔGDP = k × ΔG).
- ✓ 1 mark for correct substitution.
- ✓ 1 mark for correct answer: \$32.73 bn (accept \$32.7 bn).

**Working:** The initial injection of \$18 bn generates rounds of re-spending. With MPW = 0.55, in each round 55% leaks out and 45% (MPC net of tax and imports) is re-spent domestically. Over all rounds, the total increase in GDP converges to \$32.73 billion.

---

### (d) Why the multiplier is smaller in an open economy with taxes **[2 marks]**

In a **closed economy with no taxes**, the only leakage is saving: MPW = MPS = 0.25, so **k = 1/0.25 = 4**. Each \$1 of injection generates \$4 of GDP.

In the **open economy with taxes**, there are **three leakages** — saving, taxation, and imports — so MPW = 0.55 and k ≈ 1.82. The additional leakages mean that in each spending round, less income is re-spent domestically because a portion goes to the government (taxes) and to foreign producers (imports). This reduces the multiplier effect substantially — from 4 to approximately 1.82.

- ✓ 1 mark for comparing the two MPW values and multiplier values.
- ✓ 1 mark for explaining that taxes and imports are additional leakages that reduce re-spending in each round.

**Key insight:** The more open an economy is (higher MPM) and the higher the tax rate (MRT), the smaller the multiplier and the less effective fiscal policy will be.

- **Interventionist:** Government investment in education, infrastructure, and R&D to improve human capital and productivity.

### (b) CLASPP Evaluation — "Supply-side policies are always preferable" **[6 marks · 150–200 words]**

**Conclusions:** Supply-side policies are not *always* preferable to monetary policy. Each has distinct strengths, and the optimal choice depends on the nature of the economic problem. Supply-side policies excel at long-run growth, while monetary policy is better suited to short-run demand management.

**Priorities:** In a deep recession with high unemployment, monetary policy takes priority (demand-side crisis). For long-run prosperity and competitiveness, supply-side reform is essential. **Absolute poverty** is a condition where individuals lack the minimum resources to meet basic human needs — food, clean water, shelter, and healthcare. ## Problem 30 · Inflation — Policy Trade-Offs (Evaluation)

---

### (a) Demand-pull vs cost-push inflation **[3 marks]**

**Demand-pull inflation** occurs when aggregate demand exceeds aggregate supply at the full-employment level of output — "too much money chasing too few goods." The AD curve shifts right when the economy is at or near Yf, bidding up the price level. **Cause:** Excessive growth in the money supply, expansionary fiscal policy, or a consumption/investment boom.
- ✓ 1 mark for definition: AD > AS at/near Yf.
- ✓ 0.5 mark for cause.

**Cost-push inflation** occurs when production costs rise, shifting the SRAS curve left. Firms pass higher input costs (wages, energy, raw materials) on to consumers through higher prices, reducing output while raising the price level — **stagflation**. **Cause:** Rising oil prices, wage-push from strong unions, or supply-chain disruptions.
- ✓ 1 mark for definition: rising costs shift SRAS left.
- ✓ 0.5 mark for cause.

**Key difference:** Demand-pull is driven by the **demand side** (AD shift right, output can rise with prices); cost-push is driven by the **supply side** (SRAS shift left, output falls while prices rise).

---

### (b) Contractionary monetary policy — diagram and trade-off **[3 marks]**

- Contractionary monetary policy raises interest rates → reduces C and I → AD shifts left from AD₁ to AD₂.
- New equilibrium: AD₂ intersects SRAS at Yf with lower price level P₂. Inflation is reduced.

**The trade-off:** In the short run, reducing demand-pull inflation by shifting AD left also reduces real GDP (from Y₁ to Yf). If the central bank overshoots and shifts AD too far left, output falls *below* Yf, creating a recessionary gap and raising unemployment. This is the **short-run Phillips curve trade-off**: lower inflation may come at the cost of higher unemployment and lost output.

### (c) CLASPP Evaluation — "Central banks should prioritise low inflation above all else" **[4 marks · 150–200 words]**

**Conclusions:** While low and stable inflation is a crucial macroeconomic objective, prioritising it *above all else* is misguided. Central banks must balance inflation control with other objectives — particularly employment and growth — because an exclusive focus on inflation can cause severe short-run damage.

**Long-term vs Short-term:** In the long run, low inflation supports investment, preserves the value of savings, and maintains international competitiveness. However, in the short run, aggressive disinflation (sharply raising interest rates) can trigger a deep recession, mass unemployment, and business bankruptcies — as seen in the early 1980s under the Volcker Fed.

**Assumptions:** The position assumes inflation is always the greatest threat. But when an economy faces a severe recession or deflationary pressures, overly tight monetary policy worsens the downturn. The zero lower bound also limits how much deflation can be fought, making moderate inflation a useful buffer.

**Stakeholders:** Savers and those on fixed incomes benefit from low inflation (preserved purchasing power). Borrowers, workers, and businesses suffer if tight policy causes unemployment to rise. Homeowners with mortgages may face foreclosure if rates spike.

**Pros & Cons:** Pro-inflation-targeting — anchors expectations, provides policy credibility, and fosters long-run stability. Con — rigid targets ignore supply shocks (cost-push inflation) where raising rates worsens stagflation. The Eurozone crisis illustrated the dangers of an inflation-only mandate.

**Priorities:** A **dual mandate** (price stability *and* maximum employment, as practiced by the Fed) or a flexible inflation-targeting framework is more appropriate. Central banks should prioritise low inflation in normal times but retain flexibility to support the economy during severe downturns or supply shocks.
