Lesson 40: Macroeconomic Policy Evaluation and Conflicts

What You'll Learn

  • All five macroeconomic objectives and their interrelationships
  • Key policy conflicts between different objectives
  • The Phillips Curve trade-off between unemployment and inflation
  • How to coordinate monetary, fiscal, and supply-side policies
  • A systematic framework for evaluating any macroeconomic policy
  • How to analyse complex macro scenarios and recommend policy packages

    • 1. The Five Macroeconomic Objectives

    Governments and policymakers pursue multiple objectives simultaneously:

    ObjectiveTypical MeasureDesirable Direction
    1. Economic GrowthReal GDP growth rateHigh, stable, sustainable
    2. Low UnemploymentUnemployment rateAs low as possible (near NAIRU)
    3. Low and Stable InflationCPI inflation rate~2% in most developed economies
    4. External BalanceCurrent account balanceSustainable — not persistently large deficits
    5. Equitable Income DistributionGini coefficientLow (more equal); fairness

    The fundamental challenge: Policies that help achieve one objective may hinder another. There is no single policy lever that simultaneously moves all indicators in the desired direction. Policymakers must prioritise and accept trade-offs.

    2. Policy Conflicts

    Conflict 1: Growth vs Inflation

    • Nature of conflict: Expansionary demand-side policies (lower rates, higher G, lower T) increase AD → higher real GDP — but also higher price level. If the economy is near Yf, the inflation cost is significant.
    • Example: A government cuts taxes and increases spending to boost GDP from 1% to 3% growth. But if Y is already close to Yf, demand-pull inflation may rise from 2% to 5%.
    • Mitigation: Use supply-side policies to increase Yf alongside demand expansion — this allows growth with less inflation. Target fiscal expansion at sectors with spare capacity.

      • Conflict 2: Unemployment vs Inflation — The Phillips Curve

      • Short-Run Phillips Curve (SRPC) : Shows an inverse relationship between inflation and unemployment.
      • Lower unemployment → tighter labour market → workers demand higher wages → firms pass on costs → inflation rises.
      • Higher unemployment → slack labour market → wage growth slows → inflation falls.
      • Diagram: SRPC slopes downward. On the vertical axis: Inflation rate. On the horizontal axis: Unemployment rate.
      • Policy implication: Policymakers face a trade-off — they can reduce unemployment at the cost of higher inflation, or reduce inflation at the cost of higher unemployment.
      • Long-Run Phillips Curve (LRPC) : Vertical at the NAIRU (Non-Accelerating Inflation Rate of Unemployment). In the long run, there is no trade-off — unemployment returns to the NAIRU regardless of the inflation rate. The LRPC is the "monetarist" or "new classical" view.

        • Conflict 3: Growth vs External Balance

        • Nature: Higher domestic income (from growth) → households buy more goods, including imports (M↑). If export growth does not keep pace, the current account deficit widens.
        • Example: The US economy has often experienced this — during strong economic expansions, the trade deficit widens because American consumers buy more imported goods. During recessions, the deficit tends to narrow.
        • Mitigation: Supply-side policies that improve export competitiveness (productivity, innovation) can allow growth without worsening external balance. Exchange rate depreciation can also help.

          • Conflict 4: Growth vs Equity

          • Nature: Economic growth may disproportionately benefit certain groups — capital owners, high-skilled workers, urban residents — while leaving others behind. The "rising tide" does not necessarily lift all boats equally.
          • Evidence: In many developed countries (US, UK), GDP has grown substantially since the 1980s, but income inequality (Gini coefficient) has also risen. The top 1% have captured a disproportionate share of gains.
          • Mitigation: Progressive taxation and targeted transfers can redistribute some gains from growth. Investment in education and training gives more people the skills to participate in growth.

            • Conflict 5: Unemployment vs Equity

            • Nature: Reducing unemployment may require labour market deregulation (supply-side) that creates more low-wage, precarious jobs → the newly employed are still poor → inequality may not improve, or may worsen.
            • "Working poor": In the US and UK, many people are employed but still live below the poverty line. Low minimum wages and weak union power mean that "having a job" does not guarantee an adequate standard of living.

            3. Policy Coordination

            Given these conflicts, effective macroeconomic management requires coordinating multiple policies:

            Monetary + Fiscal Policy Together

            • Both expansionary: In a deep recession (like 2008 or 2020), both monetary easing (lower rates, QE) AND fiscal expansion (higher G, lower T) work together. Fiscal policy directly boosts AD; monetary policy ensures low borrowing costs support private spending AND accommodates fiscal expansion (reducing crowding out risks).
            • Opposite directions: Rare but possible. In 2022–2023, many central banks tightened monetary policy (raised rates to fight inflation) while governments maintained expansionary fiscal stances (energy subsidies). This created a "pull-push" tension: fiscal policy was adding to AD while monetary policy was trying to restrain it.

            Demand-Side + Supply-Side Together

            • Ideal policy mix: Use supply-side policies (education, infrastructure, deregulation) to shift LRAS right for long-run growth. Use demand-side policies (monetary/fiscal) to manage the business cycle and keep actual output near potential.
            • Example: Germany's "Agenda 2010" reforms (2003–2005) combined labour market liberalisation (supply-side) with short-run demand management. The reforms are credited with Germany's subsequent strong export performance and low unemployment.

            4. A Framework for Evaluating Any Macro Policy

            Use this systematic approach for IB evaluation questions:

            1. Effectiveness

            • Does the policy actually achieve its stated objective?
            • What is the evidence (data, historical examples)?
            • How large is the effect relative to the problem?

              • 2. Time Lags

              • Recognition lag, implementation lag, impact lag
              • Is the policy likely to arrive at the right time, or could it be pro-cyclical?

              3. Side Effects

              • Does the policy worsen other macroeconomic objectives?
              • Unintended consequences (e.g., asset bubbles from low rates, inequality from deregulation)

              4. Stakeholder Impacts

              • Who benefits? Who loses?
              • Consumers, firms, workers, savers, borrowers, government, foreign sector

              5. Political Constraints

              • Is the policy politically feasible?
              • Electoral incentives, special interest groups, constitutional/legal constraints

              6. Context Dependence

              • Effectiveness depends on the state of the economy (recession vs boom, open vs closed)
              • Effectiveness depends on institutional quality (independent central bank, efficient bureaucracy)
              • No one-size-fits-all policy

                IB Exam Tip

                When asked to recommend policy for a macro scenario:

                1. Identify the primary problem from the data (recessionary gap? inflationary gap? stagflation? long-run stagnation?).
                2. Recommend a specific policy package — not just "use fiscal and monetary policy," but "lower the policy rate by 50bp AND increase infrastructure spending."
                3. Acknowledge conflicts: "This may worsen inflation / the budget deficit / the current account."
                4. Use diagrams: AD/AS, LRAS shifts, Phillips Curve.
                5. Evaluate: Why might it not work? What are the risks? Under what conditions would you change the recommendation?
                6. Conclude: Give a balanced final judgment with conditions.

                Answers

                Answer 1: Identifying Conflicts

                (a) Unemployment vs inflation — the Phillips Curve trade-off:

                • Reducing unemployment below the NAIRU requires expansionary demand-side policy → AD shifts right → firms need more workers → wages bid up → costs rise → inflation accelerates.
                • Real-world example: In the late 1960s, the US attempted to reduce unemployment below ~4%. Expansionary fiscal (Vietnam War spending + Great Society programmes) and monetary policy led to accelerating inflation — reaching double digits by the mid-1970s. This experience contributed to the development of the expectations-augmented Phillips Curve.

                (b) Growth vs external balance:

                • Higher domestic income → consumers buy more imports → trade deficit widens unless exports rise equally. The marginal propensity to import determines the severity.
                • Example: During India's rapid growth phase (2004–2008, GDP growing at ~8–9% annually), the current account deficit widened significantly as imports of oil, gold, and capital goods surged. The deficit reached over 4% of GDP by 2012, raising concerns about external vulnerability.

                (c) Unemployment vs equity:

                • Policies that reduce unemployment may do so by creating (or tolerating) low-quality jobs → the employed may still be poor → the Gini coefficient may not improve.
                • Example: Post-labour-market-reform UK (2000s): unemployment fell to historic lows (~5%) but in-work poverty rose. Many jobs were part-time, zero-hours, or minimum wage. The Gini coefficient remained high. Reducing unemployment did not automatically improve equity.

                Answer 2: Phillips Curve

                Short-Run Phillips Curve (SRPC) :

                • Downward-sloping curve showing inverse relationship between the rate of inflation (or wage inflation) and the unemployment rate.
                • Vertical axis: Inflation rate. Horizontal axis: Unemployment rate.
                • At point A: high unemployment (e.g., 8%), low inflation (e.g., 1%).
                • At point B: low unemployment (e.g., 3%), high inflation (e.g., 5%).
                • Movement along the SRPC: Expansionary policy reduces unemployment but raises inflation.

                  • Long-Run Phillips Curve (LRPC) :

                  • According to monetarist/neoclassical economists (Friedman, Phelps), the LRPC is vertical at the NAIRU — the Natural Rate of Unemployment (or Non-Accelerating Inflation Rate of Unemployment).
                  • Why the trade-off disappears: In the long run, workers and firms adjust their inflation expectations. If the government tries to keep unemployment below the NAIRU, actual inflation exceeds expected inflation → workers demand higher nominal wages to compensate → firms' costs rise further → inflation keeps accelerating. Eventually, unemployment returns to the NAIRU but at a higher inflation rate. The LRPC is vertical at the NAIRU — there is no permanent trade-off.
                  • Policy implication: Expansionary demand policy can only reduce unemployment temporarily. In the long run, only supply-side policies (improving labour market efficiency, education) can reduce the NAIRU.

                    • Answer 3: Policy Coordination

                    Situation analysis: Country is in a severe recession (0.5% growth, 9% unemployment). Inflation is very low (1.5%) — risk of deflation. The policy rate (0.1%) is at the zero lower bound — conventional monetary policy is exhausted. Budget deficit (10%) is high — fiscal space appears limited. However, with deflation risk and ZLB, fiscal policy is the only viable demand-side tool.

                    Recommended coordinated response:

                    1. Expansionary fiscal policy (primary tool): Increase government spending on infrastructure — "shovel-ready" projects that create jobs quickly. Targeted tax cuts for low-income households (high MPC). Accept that the deficit will rise temporarily. With interest rates near zero, crowding out is minimal.
                    1. Unconventional monetary policy: Quantitative easing (QE) — the central bank buys government bonds to keep long-term interest rates low and inject liquidity. This supports fiscal expansion by keeping government borrowing costs low.
                    1. Supply-side policies (complementary, for medium-term): Fund retraining programmes for the unemployed to reduce structural unemployment. Improve job-matching services.

                    How they complement: Fiscal policy directly boosts AD. QE ensures low rates support private borrowing and keeps government bond yields low (enabling fiscal expansion). Supply-side measures address the risk that some unemployment is structural, not cyclical — reducing the chance that expansionary policy creates inflation before full employment is restored. This is a "three-pronged" approach: demand, monetary support, and supply.

                    Answer 4: Evaluation Framework — Tax Cuts

                    C — Conclusion: Tax cuts can stimulate growth in the short run and potentially increase productive capacity in the long run, but the net benefit depends heavily on the type of tax cut, how it is financed, and the state of the economy.

                    L — Limitations: (1) If tax cuts are financed by borrowing, they may raise interest rates and crowd out private investment. (2) Ricardian equivalence: if households expect future tax increases to repay debt, they may save rather than spend the tax cut. (3) Supply-side effects (increased work effort) are uncertain in magnitude.

                    A — Assumptions: The growth effect assumes (1) households spend a significant portion of the tax cut (high MPC) and (2) lower marginal rates significantly increase work effort and investment. Both are disputed.

                    S — Stakeholders: Tax cuts disproportionately benefit higher-income earners (who pay more tax in absolute terms), potentially worsening income inequality. Lower-income groups benefit less in absolute terms. Future generations bear the cost if tax cuts are debt-financed.

                    P — Pros/Cons: Pros: Quick implementation (relative to spending programmes), increases disposable income, may improve work incentives. Cons: May increase budget deficit and national debt, may worsen inequality, may not stimulate demand if households save, may fuel inflation if economy is near full capacity.

                    P — Priorities: If the economy is in recession with low inflation and the ZLB is binding, tax cuts for low-income households (high MPC) make sense as part of a broader stimulus package. If the economy is near full employment with high debt, tax cuts are inappropriate — they would be inflationary and fiscally irresponsible.

                    Answer 5: Conflicting Policies

                    The situation: In 2022–2023, Europe (and other regions) faced an energy price shock from the Russia-Ukraine war. Governments responded with large-scale energy subsidies, price caps, and cash transfers to shield households and firms — this is expansionary fiscal policy (increasing G/transfer payments). Simultaneously, central banks (ECB, Bank of England, Fed) raised interest rates aggressively to combat high inflation — this is contractionary monetary policy.

                    The tension:

                    • Fiscal policy was adding to AD: Energy subsidies meant households had more disposable income → they maintained spending. Government deficits widened.
                    • Monetary policy was trying to reduce AD: Higher interest rates were meant to cool spending, reduce borrowing, and bring down inflation.
                    • These policies work in opposite directions. The fiscal expansion partially offset the monetary contraction. Central banks had to raise rates MORE than they otherwise would have, increasing the cost in terms of forgone growth and potential unemployment.

                      • Why the conflict arose: Governments prioritised protecting households from immediate energy poverty (political/social imperative), while central banks prioritised the inflation target (statutory mandate). Different institutions pursued different objectives, creating a macro policy tug-of-war.

                      Resolution: Ideally, fiscal support should have been more targeted (only to the most vulnerable), reducing the offsetting effect on monetary policy. The IMF and OECD recommended "targeted and temporary" fiscal measures, not broad-based subsidies.

                      Answer 6: IB-Style Data Response

                      (a) Two most pressing problems:

                      1. Rising inflation: Inflation accelerated from 3.5% in 2022 to a forecast 6.4% in 2024 — well above typical ~2% targets. This erodes purchasing power, creates uncertainty, and may become entrenched. Combined with very low unemployment (2.8%), this suggests the economy is operating above capacity (positive output gap).
                      2. Deteriorating external balance: The current account deficit is widening rapidly — from −2.1% to a forecast −5.8% of GDP. This indicates growing external vulnerability and may be unsustainable. It likely reflects strong domestic demand sucking in imports.

                      (b) Recommended policy package:

                      • Contractionary monetary policy: Raise the policy interest rate. This increases borrowing costs, reduces consumption (C↓) and investment (I↓). AD shifts LEFT → reduces inflationary pressure and lowers import demand (improving the current account).
                      • Fiscal consolidation: Reduce government spending and/or increase taxes to reduce the budget deficit (also worsening). This further cools AD.
                      • AD/AS diagram: Show AD₁ (overheating, to the right of Yf) shifting left to AD₂ after policy tightening. The price level falls (inflation declines) and GDP returns toward Yf. The current account improves as lower domestic income reduces imports.
                      • Phillips Curve diagram (optional): Show movement along the SRPC — higher interest rates move the economy northwest along the curve (lower inflation, higher unemployment).
                      • Note: Given the data, supply-side policies are less urgent — the problem is cyclical overheating, not inadequate capacity. [4 marks]

                        • (c) Two policy conflicts:

                        1. Growth/Unemployment vs Inflation: Contractionary policies will likely slow GDP growth from ~5% toward a more sustainable rate. Unemployment, currently very low at 2.8%, will likely rise as demand cools. The government must accept a trade-off — sacrificing some growth and employment to control inflation. Politically, this is difficult: voters accustomed to rapid growth and plentiful jobs may punish the government.
                        2. Fiscal consolidation vs Equity: If the government cuts spending (e.g., on social programmes) or raises taxes (e.g., VAT), this may disproportionately affect lower-income groups. The Gini coefficient is already rising (0.38 to 0.43) — austerity measures could worsen inequality. The government faces a tension between macroeconomic stability (reducing the deficit and inflation) and equity. This is a classic "austerity vs fairness" dilemma, as seen in Eurozone countries post-2010. [4 marks]

                        Evaluate: CLASPP Model Answer (150–200 words)

                        "No single macroeconomic policy can achieve all macroeconomic objectives simultaneously." Evaluate.

                        Conclusion: This statement is fundamentally correct. The five macroeconomic objectives — growth, low unemployment, low inflation, external balance, and equity — involve inherent trade-offs that no single policy instrument can reconcile. However, well-designed policy packages can mitigate these conflicts.

                        Limitations: The Phillips Curve illustrates the foundational trade-off: expansionary policies that reduce unemployment tend to raise inflation in the short run. Similarly, growth driven by domestic demand often worsens the current account. Supply-side policies that increase potential output can ease some trade-offs (non-inflationary growth), but they cannot eliminate them entirely — and they introduce their own conflicts, particularly with equity.

                        Assumptions: The statement correctly assumes that policymakers face multiple binding constraints. However, it may understate the role of complementary policies. While no single policy suffices, a coordinated package — combining monetary, fiscal, and supply-side measures — can make progress on multiple fronts.

                        Stakeholders: Different objectives matter differently to different stakeholders. Savers prioritise low inflation; workers prioritise employment; exporters care about external balance. Policymakers must weigh these competing interests, and there is no purely "technical" solution — value judgments are unavoidable.

                        Pros/Cons: The advantage of acknowledging this truth is that it encourages realistic expectations and balanced policymaking. The risk is that it may induce policy paralysis — "if we can't achieve everything, why try?"

                        Priorities: The optimal approach is to prioritise based on the most urgent problem, coordinate policies to minimise conflicts, and use supply-side reforms to expand the set of achievable outcomes over the long term.
