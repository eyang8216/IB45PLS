Lesson 4: Demand — Law, Curve, and Determinants

What You'll Learn

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to:

  • Define demand and state the law of demand
  • Construct and interpret demand schedules and curves
  • Distinguish between individual and market demand
  • Explain three reasons why demand curves slope downward
  • Differentiate between a movement along the curve and a shift of the curve
  • Identify and apply the non-price determinants of demand (TRIBE)
  • Distinguish normal goods from inferior goods, and substitutes from complements

1. Definition of Demand and the Law of Demand

What Is Demand?

Demand in economics is not just wanting something — it is the quantity of a good or service that consumers are willing and able to purchase at each possible price, over a given time period, ceteris paribus.

The phrase "willing and able" is crucial:

  • Willing: The consumer desires the good
  • Able: The consumer has the purchasing power (income) to buy it

A billionaire may want a private island but not be willing to pay the price, or a student may be willing but not able. Neither counts as demand.

The Law of Demand

As the price of a good rises, the quantity demanded falls; as the price falls, the quantity demanded rises — ceteris paribus.

There is an inverse (negative) relationship between price and quantity demanded. This is one of the most robust empirical regularities in all of economics.

2. Demand Schedule and Demand Curve

Demand Schedule

A demand schedule is a table showing the quantity demanded at each price. Example: weekly demand for coffee in a university café:

Demand Curve

The demand curve is the graphical representation of the demand schedule. Price (P) is on the vertical axis; quantity demanded (Q) is on the horizontal axis. The curve slopes downward from left to right.

Individual Demand vs Market Demand

  • Individual demand: The demand of a single consumer for a good
  • Market demand: The horizontal (quantity) summation of all individual demand curves in a market
Example: If Alice demands 3 cups at £2 and Bob demands 5 cups at £2, market demand at £2 is 3 + 5 = 8 cups.

3. Why Do Demand Curves Slope Downward?

Three effects explain the inverse relationship between price and quantity demanded:

1. The Income Effect

When the price of a good falls, the consumer's real income (purchasing power) increases. They can buy more of all normal goods with the same money income, including the good whose price fell.

Example: If the price of bread halves, a consumer with the same money income can now afford twice as much bread and has money left over for other goods. Some of that increased purchasing power is spent on buying more bread.

2. The Substitution Effect

When the price of a good falls, it becomes relatively cheaper compared to its substitutes. Consumers switch (substitute) away from now-relatively-expensive alternatives toward the cheaper good.

Example: If beef prices fall while chicken prices stay the same, consumers substitute beef for chicken. Quantity demanded of beef rises.

3. Diminishing Marginal Utility

As a consumer consumes more units of a good, the additional satisfaction (marginal utility) from each extra unit falls. A consumer will only buy an additional unit if the price is lower — they value the first cup of coffee highly (willing to pay £4) but the fifth cup far less (willing to pay only £1). Lower prices are needed to induce purchases of additional units.

4. Movement Along vs Shift of the Demand Curve

This distinction is critical for IB exams. Confusing the two is one of the most common mistakes.

Movement Along the Demand Curve

A change in the good's own price causes a movement along the existing demand curve.

  • Price rises → movement up and left along the curve (lower Qd) — called a contraction of demand
  • Price falls → movement down and right along the curve (higher Qd) — called an expansion of demand

The demand curve itself does NOT shift. The relationship between price and quantity demanded is unchanged.

Shift of the Demand Curve

A change in any non-price determinant causes the entire demand curve to shift.

  • Increase in demand → curve shifts RIGHT (more demanded at every price)
  • Decrease in demand → curve shifts LEFT (less demanded at every price)

5. Non-Price Determinants of Demand — TRIBE

The factors that shift the demand curve can be remembered with the acronym TRIBE:

T — Tastes and Preferences

Changes in what consumers like, want, or find fashionable. Tastes can shift due to:

  • Advertising and marketing
  • Social trends and peer influence
  • Health information (e.g., studies linking red meat to cancer reduce demand for beef)
  • Seasonal factors
Example: A celebrity endorsement of a sneaker brand shifts demand right.

R — Related Goods Prices (Substitutes and Complements)

Substitutes: Goods that can replace each other in consumption. If the price of good B rises, demand for good A increases (shift right) as consumers switch to A.

  • Examples: Tea and coffee; butter and margarine; bus travel and train travel
  • Cross-price elasticity is positive for substitutes.

Complements: Goods used together. If the price of good B rises, demand for good A falls (shift left) because fewer people buy B and therefore need less A.

  • Examples: Printers and ink cartridges; cars and petrol; smartphones and apps
  • Cross-price elasticity is negative for complements.

I — Income (Normal vs Inferior Goods)

Normal goods: Demand rises when income rises (and falls when income falls). Most goods are normal.

  • Necessities: Normal goods where demand rises less than proportionally with income (e.g., basic food, water, electricity)
  • Luxuries: Normal goods where demand rises more than proportionally with income (e.g., luxury cars, international travel, fine dining)

Inferior goods: Demand falls when income rises (and rises when income falls). Consumers buy these only because they cannot afford better alternatives.

  • Examples: Instant noodles, second-hand clothing, bus travel (vs taxi/private car), supermarket own-brand basics
Key distinction: A good is "inferior" economically, not in quality. As incomes rise, consumers switch away from inferior goods to superior substitutes.

B — Buyers (Number of Consumers)

More buyers in a market → market demand increases (shifts right). Fewer buyers → market demand decreases (shifts left).

  • Population growth increases demand for housing, food, energy
  • Ageing population increases demand for healthcare, decreases demand for education
  • Immigration expands the number of consumers in a country

E — Expectations

What consumers expect about future prices, income, or availability affects current demand.

  • Future price expected to rise → current demand increases (buy now before it gets expensive)
  • Future price expected to fall → current demand decreases (wait for the lower price)
  • Future income expected to fall (recession fears) → current demand for durables and luxuries may decrease (precautionary saving)
Example: If consumers expect a housing market crash, demand for houses today falls as buyers wait for lower prices.

IB Exam Tip

When asked to analyse a change in a market, follow this sequence:

  1. Identify whether it's a change in price (→ movement along) or a non-price determinant (→ shift)
  2. Name the specific determinant (use the correct terminology: "tastes," "income," "number of buyers" — not vague phrases)
  3. State direction: "increase in demand" (right) or "decrease in demand" (left)
  4. Explain the mechanism: why does the shift occur?
  5. Diagram: Draw clearly with labelled axes, initial and new curves (D₁ → D₂), and arrows

Never say "demand increases" when you mean "quantity demanded increases" — the IB rewards precise terminology.

Practice Problems

Problem 1: Movement vs Shift

(a) The price of cinema tickets falls.

(b) A new study shows that watching films at the cinema improves mental health.

(c) Netflix raises its subscription price.

(d) Consumer incomes rise and cinema visits are a normal good.

Problem 6: IB-Style (Challenging)

The market for coffee has an initial demand given by: Qd = 500 − 20P (where P is in £ and Qd is in thousands of cups per day).

(a) Calculate Qd at P = £3, £5, £7, and £10. Plot the demand curve (sketch).

(b) Consumer incomes rise by 10%, and coffee is a normal good. The new demand is Qd = 600 − 20P. Calculate the increase in Qd at P = £5 and explain how this illustrates a shift vs a movement.

(c) The price of tea (a substitute) rises from £2 to £4. The demand for coffee becomes Qd = 580 − 20P. Compare the effects of the income change (part b) and the tea price change (part c) at P = £5. Which has the larger effect on coffee demand?

(d) If the price of coffee itself rises from £5 to £7, use the ORIGINAL demand function to calculate the change in Qd. Is this a movement or a shift? Explain why the distinction matters for a café owner making pricing decisions.

Answers

Answer 1: Movement vs Shift

(a) Movement along — price of cinema tickets (own price) falls → expansion of demand (movement down and right along the curve).

(b) Shift right — tastes/preferences change favourably (T in TRIBE) → more cinema visits demanded at every price.

(c) Shift — Netflix is a substitute for cinema. Netflix price rises → substitutes become more expensive → demand for cinema shifts right (R in TRIBE).

(d) Shift right or left — depends on whether cinema is a normal or inferior good. For most consumers, cinema is normal → income rises → demand shifts right (I in TRIBE).

(e) Shift left — number of buyers decreases (B in TRIBE) → market demand shifts left at every price.

Answer 2: Normal vs Inferior Goods

(a) Used 10-year-old car: Typically inferior. As income rises, consumers switch to newer or new cars.

(b) Fresh organic vegetables: Normal (likely a luxury). As income rises, consumers buy more and upgrade from conventional to organic.

(c) Cheap fast food: Inferior. As income rises, consumers switch to higher-quality restaurants or home-cooked meals with better ingredients.

(d) Private healthcare: Normal (luxury). As income rises, consumers may supplement public healthcare or upgrade from public to private.

(e) Public bus transport: Inferior for many. As income rises, consumers switch to taxis, ride-sharing, or private cars. (Note: in cities with excellent bus systems, this may not hold — context matters.)

Answer 3: Substitutes vs Complements

(a) Butter and margarine: Substitutes. Price of margarine (B) rises → demand for butter (A) shifts right (increases).

(b) Petrol and cars: Complements. Price of cars (B) rises → fewer cars bought → less petrol needed → demand for petrol (A) shifts left (decreases).

(c) Train tickets and domestic flights: Substitutes. Price of flights (B) rises → more people take trains → demand for train tickets (A) shifts right (increases).

(d) Tennis rackets and tennis balls: Complements. Price of rackets (B) rises → fewer rackets sold → fewer people playing tennis → demand for balls (A) shifts left (decreases).

Answer 4: Demand Curve Drawing

(a) Inverse relationship: As price falls, Qd rises. This conforms to the law of demand.

(b) Demand has changed (shift right). The subsidy is NOT a change in the own price of EVs. It effectively increases consumers' willingness and ability to buy at every price. More EVs are demanded at each price → the entire demand curve shifts right. This is an increase in demand.

(c) Quantity demanded has changed (movement along). The fall from $50,000 to $40,000 is a change in the EV's own price. We move along the existing demand curve from Qd = 200 to Qd = 350. The demand curve itself hasn't shifted.

Answer 5: Applying TRIBE

(a) T — Tastes/preferences. Celebrity endorsement makes plant-based burgers more desirable. Demand shifts right.

(b) R — Related goods (complements). Printers and ink cartridges are complements. Price of printers falls → more printers bought → more cartridges needed. Demand for cartridges shifts right.

(c) I — Income. Recession → incomes fall. Luxury handbags are normal goods (luxuries). Demand shifts left.

(d) B — Buyers (number of). More students → more potential renters. Demand for apartments shifts right.

(e) E — Expectations. Expected future price increase → buy now to avoid higher prices later. Current petrol demand shifts right.

Answer 6: IB-Style Solution

(a) Qd at each price:

  • P = £3: Qd = 500 − 20(3) = 500 − 60 = 440 thousand cups
  • P = £5: Qd = 500 − 20(5) = 500 − 100 = 400 thousand cups
  • P = £7: Qd = 500 − 20(7) = 500 − 140 = 360 thousand cups
  • P = £10: Qd = 500 − 20(10) = 500 − 200 = 300 thousand cups

The demand curve is a downward-sloping straight line (linear demand).

(b) At P = £5:

  • Original Qd = 400
  • New Qd = 600 − 20(5) = 600 − 100 = 500
  • Increase = 100 thousand cups

This is a shift (right) of the entire demand curve, caused by a change in a non-price determinant (income, I in TRIBE). At every single price, more coffee is demanded. Compare this to a movement along: if the price had fallen from £5 to £3, Qd would rise from 400 to 440 — but that's moving along the same curve. The café owner sees 500 cups demanded at £5 after the income change vs 400 before — the demand relationship itself has changed.

(c) At P = £5:

  • Income effect: Qd = 500
  • Tea price effect: Qd = 580 − 20(5) = 580 − 100 = 480
  • Income change increases Qd by 100; tea price change increases Qd by 80.
  • Income change has a larger effect (100 vs 80).

This comparison shows that different non-price determinants can have different magnitudes of impact. The café owner should pay attention to both consumer incomes and competitor (tea) pricing.

(d) Price rise from £5 to £7 using ORIGINAL demand:

  • At P = £5: Qd = 400
  • At P = £7: Qd = 360
  • Change = −40 thousand cups

This is a movement along the demand curve (contraction of demand). The distinction matters enormously for the café owner:

  • If the change is a movement (own price change), the owner directly controls this and can try different price points along the same demand curve.
  • If the change is a shift (income or tea prices), the owner has no control over these external factors and must adapt strategy accordingly — perhaps by changing product offerings rather than prices.

Evaluate

Question

"The law of demand always holds true." Evaluate this statement, considering Veblen goods and Giffen goods.

Model Answer

Conclusion: The law of demand is one of the most robust empirical regularities in economics and holds true for the vast majority of goods in the vast majority of circumstances. However, there are theoretically and empirically documented exceptions — Veblen goods and Giffen goods — that challenge the claim that it always holds.

The law of demand states that as price rises, quantity demanded falls, ceteris paribus. This is driven by the income effect, substitution effect, and diminishing marginal utility. For normal and most inferior goods, these effects work in the same direction: a price rise makes the good relatively more expensive (substitution effect → less demanded) AND reduces real income (income effect → less demanded for normal goods; for inferior goods, the income effect works opposite but is typically smaller than the substitution effect).

Veblen goods are luxury goods for which higher prices increase demand because the high price itself signals status and exclusivity. Named after economist Thorstein Veblen, who wrote about "conspicuous consumption." Examples: Rolex watches, Hermès Birkin bags, luxury sports cars. If a Birkin bag's price fell dramatically, its exclusivity would evaporate and demand from status-seeking consumers might actually fall. The Veblen effect violates the law of demand but applies only to a narrow class of positional/luxury goods consumed for their signaling value.

Giffen goods are a rare type of inferior good where the income effect is so strong (and perverse) that it outweighs the substitution effect. For a Giffen good, a price rise makes consumers so much poorer in real terms that they buy more of the inferior good because they can no longer afford superior substitutes. The classic example (Sir Robert Giffen, 19th century) was potatoes during the Irish famine: when potato prices rose, poor families could afford even less meat, so they bought more potatoes to survive. Note: Giffen goods require (1) the good to be a very large share of the consumer's budget, (2) the good to be strongly inferior, and (3) no close substitutes — explaining why documented real-world examples are extremely rare.

Long-term vs short-term: In the short term, Giffen behaviour might emerge during extreme scarcity or poverty. In the long term, as consumers adjust and markets evolve, the substitution effect tends to dominate, and the law of demand reasserts itself.

Assumptions: The law of demand assumes rational consumers, perfect information, and ceteris paribus. Veblen and Giffen goods violate these assumptions — Veblen consumers value price qua price (irrational from a conventional utility standpoint), and Giffen goods arise under extreme income constraints that break the assumption that the substitution effect dominates.

Stakeholders: For most firms and most products, the law of demand is a reliable guide for pricing strategy. Luxury goods marketers, however, must carefully consider that price cuts might damage brand equity and reduce demand. Policymakers designing taxes on staple foods in very poor regions should be aware of the theoretical possibility of Giffen behaviour, though it is unlikely to manifest outside extreme conditions.

Priorities: The existence of exceptions does not invalidate the law of demand as a general principle. Physics has exceptions to Newton's laws (relativity, quantum mechanics) yet Newtonian physics remains enormously useful. Similarly, economics students should learn the law of demand as the central tendency while being aware of its theoretical limits.

Pros/Cons: The law of demand is supported by overwhelming empirical evidence across thousands of goods and markets (pro). The exceptions are theoretically interesting but empirically negligible — there are perhaps a handful of documented Giffen-good cases in economic history (con to the "always" claim). The Veblen effect is better understood as a different preference structure (consumers value exclusivity) rather than a violation of rational choice per se.

Final judgment: The statement "the law of demand always holds true" is too absolute. A more precise formulation: the law of demand is a powerful and near-universal empirical regularity that holds for virtually all goods in virtually all market conditions, with rare and narrowly circumscribed exceptions (Veblen goods, Giffen goods) that do not undermine its practical utility or theoretical centrality.
