Lesson 4: Demand — Law, Curve, and Determinants

Syllabus reference: Unit 2.1–2.2 — Demand in competitive markets

Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to:

  • Define demand and state the law of demand
  • Distinguish between a movement along the demand curve and a shift of the demand curve
  • Identify the non-price determinants of demand using the TRIBE framework
  • Explain the difference between normal goods and inferior goods
  • Explain the difference between substitute goods and complementary goods
  • Draw and interpret demand curves and shifts in demand

1. Prior Knowledge Check

Question 1: In Lesson 3, we discussed the ceteris paribus assumption. What does this Latin phrase mean, and why is it important?

Show Answer

Answer: Ceteris paribus means "all other things being equal." It is the assumption that we hold all other variables constant while examining the effect of one variable. It is essential because in the real world many things change at once; ceteris paribus allows us to isolate cause-and-effect relationships.

Question 2: Think about a product you buy regularly — perhaps coffee, or bus tickets, or streaming subscriptions. If the price of that product doubled, would you buy the same amount? Less? Why?

Show Answer

Answer: Most people would buy less when the price rises. At a higher price, the product takes up more of your budget, so you might cut back. You might also switch to a cheaper alternative. This intuitive response — buy less when price rises — is the law of demand.

2. What Demand Means in Economics

Demand is the quantity of a good or service that consumers are willing and able to buy at each possible price over a given period of time, ceteris paribus. Every word in this definition matters:

  • "Willing and able" — wanting something is not enough. You must also have the money to pay for it. A student might want a Ferrari, but that desire is not "demand" in the economic sense unless the student has the £200,000 to buy one.
  • "At each possible price" — demand is not a single number. It is a relationship between price and quantity across a range of prices.
  • "Over a given period of time" — demand is a flow. It makes no sense to say "demand for coffee is 500 cups" without specifying whether that is per day, per week, or per year.
  • "Ceteris paribus" — the relationship between price and quantity demanded assumes that all other factors affecting demand (income, tastes, prices of other goods) are held constant.

The law of demand states that, ceteris paribus, as the price of a good rises, the quantity demanded of that good falls. As the price falls, the quantity demanded rises. Price and quantity demanded are inversely related.

Check-in: If a cinema lowers ticket prices from £12 to £8 and the number of tickets sold rises from 200 to 350 per day, has demand increased? Use precise economic language.

Show Answer

Answer: No — demand has not increased. The quantity demanded has increased (from 200 to 350) in response to a fall in price. This is a movement along the existing demand curve, not an increase in demand (which would require the entire curve to shift right). This distinction between "demand" and "quantity demanded" is one of the most mark-sensitive points in IB Economics.

3. The Demand Curve

A demand schedule is a table showing the quantity demanded at each price. A demand curve is the graphical representation of the demand schedule, with price on the vertical axis and quantity demanded on the horizontal axis.

The demand curve slopes downward for three reasons:

  1. The income effect: When the price of a good falls, the purchasing power of your income increases — you can buy more of the good (and more of other goods) with the same money.
  2. The substitution effect: When the price of a good falls, it becomes relatively cheaper compared to substitute goods. Consumers switch from the now-relatively-expensive substitutes toward the cheaper good.
  3. Diminishing marginal utility: As you consume more units of a good, each additional unit provides less additional satisfaction. You are only willing to buy more units if the price is lower to compensate for the lower satisfaction per unit.

4. Movement Along vs. Shift of the Demand Curve

This distinction is fundamental. Getting it wrong is one of the most common errors on IB exams.

A movement along the demand curve occurs when the price of the good itself changes and the quantity demanded adjusts in response. The demand curve does not move; we simply slide along it to a new point. If price falls, we move down and to the right (higher quantity demanded). If price rises, we move up and to the left (lower quantity demanded).

A shift of the demand curve occurs when a non-price determinant of demand changes. The entire curve moves — to the right (increase in demand — at every price, consumers want to buy more) or to the left (decrease in demand — at every price, consumers want to buy less).

Check-in: A new health study reports that eating blueberries significantly reduces the risk of heart disease. The price of blueberries does not change. Does this cause a movement along the demand curve or a shift? In which direction?

Show Answer

Answer: A shift of the demand curve to the right — an increase in demand. The health study changes consumers' tastes and preferences (a non-price determinant). At every given price, consumers now want to buy more blueberries than before. The price has not changed; what has changed is the desire to buy at each price.

5. Non-Price Determinants of Demand — TRIBE

Five categories of factors can shift the entire demand curve. We use the mnemonic TRIBE:

T — Tastes and preferences. If a product becomes more popular (through advertising, trends, health recommendations), demand increases (shifts right). If it falls out of favour, demand decreases (shifts left).

R — Related goods' prices. This has two sub-categories:

  • Substitute goods: goods that can be used in place of each other. Tea and coffee are substitutes. If the price of coffee rises, some coffee drinkers switch to tea, increasing the demand for tea (tea demand shifts right). The price of one good and the demand for its substitute move in the same direction.
  • Complementary goods: goods that are used together. Printers and ink cartridges are complements. If the price of printers falls, more people buy printers, and they also need ink cartridges — the demand for ink cartridges increases (shifts right). The price of one good and the demand for its complement move in opposite directions.

I — Income. Changes in consumers' incomes shift demand, but the direction depends on the type of good:

  • Normal goods: demand increases when income rises (most goods — restaurant meals, clothing, electronics). Demand shifts right when incomes rise.
  • Inferior goods: demand decreases when income rises, because consumers switch to higher-quality alternatives. Examples: supermarket own-brand beans, bus travel (when people can afford cars), instant noodles. Demand shifts left when incomes rise.

B — Buyers (number of). More consumers in a market means more demand at every price. Population growth, immigration, or a new export market opening up all increase the number of buyers and shift demand right.

E — Expectations. If consumers expect prices to rise in the future, they may buy more now, shifting current demand right. If they expect the economy to enter a recession and their incomes to fall, they may reduce spending now, shifting demand left.

Check-in: The price of petrol rises sharply. What happens to the demand for large fuel-inefficient cars? What happens to the demand for electric cars? Classify each relationship (substitute or complement) and state the direction of the demand shift.

Show Answer

Answer: Petrol and large fuel-inefficient cars are complements — they are used together. When petrol becomes more expensive, driving a fuel-inefficient car becomes more costly, so demand for these cars decreases (shifts left). Petrol prices and demand for electric cars: electric cars are substitutes for petrol-powered cars. When petrol becomes more expensive, some consumers switch to electric cars, so demand for electric cars increases (shifts right).

6. Worked Examples

Worked Example 1: Movement vs Shift

Problem: The price of cinema tickets falls from £12 to £8. At the same time, a highly anticipated film is released, attracting audiences who had not been going to the cinema. Analyse the effects on the market for cinema tickets, distinguishing between movements along the demand curve and shifts.

Step 1: The fall in price from £12 to £8 causes a movement along the demand curve — the quantity demanded increases as we slide down the existing curve.

Step 2: The release of the anticipated film changes tastes and preferences (T in TRIBE). This causes the demand curve to shift to the right — at every price, more people want cinema tickets.

Step 3: The combined effect: we observe a large increase in ticket sales. Part of this increase is a movement along the old demand curve (due to the price fall) and part is due to the curve shifting right (due to the film release).

⚠️ Common Wrong Approach: Attributing the entire change in quantity to either the price change alone or the taste change alone. In the real world, both can happen simultaneously. The IB examiner wants you to distinguish between the two mechanisms even when they occur together.

Worked Example 2: Normal vs Inferior Goods

Problem: During an economic boom, average household incomes rise by 15%. Sales of supermarket own-brand baked beans fall by 8%, while sales of branded organic beans rise by 22%. Classify each type of bean and explain the demand shifts.

Step 1: Own-brand beans: When incomes rose, demand fell. This means own-brand beans are an inferior good. As households become richer, they switch away from the cheapest option. The demand curve for own-brand beans shifted left.

Step 2: Organic beans: When incomes rose, demand rose sharply. Organic beans are a normal good (in fact, with a large positive response to income, they may be a luxury good — a normal good where the percentage increase in demand exceeds the percentage increase in income). The demand curve for organic beans shifted right.

⚠️ Common Wrong Approach: Assuming that all goods are normal goods and that rising incomes increase demand for everything. The concept of inferior goods — goods you buy less of as you get richer — is a standard exam trap.

Worked Example 3: The Expectation Trap

Problem: The government announces that from next month, a new tax will raise the price of cigarettes by £2 per pack. Before the tax takes effect, what happens to the current demand for cigarettes? Explain using the TRIBE framework. (Exam trap: students confuse expectations with the actual price change.)

Step 1: The announcement changes consumers' expectations (E in TRIBE). Consumers expect the price of cigarettes to rise in the near future.

Step 2: In response to the expected future price increase, some smokers stock up now — they buy more cigarettes before the price goes up. Current demand shifts right.

Step 3: After the tax takes effect and the price actually rises, there will be a movement along the new demand curve (quantity demanded falls because price is higher). But the demand curve itself may also shift left over time as some smokers quit in response to the permanently higher price.

⚠️ Common Wrong Approach (The Exam Trap): Students often say "the price is going up, so demand will fall" — correctly predicting the long-run outcome but missing the short-run spike in demand caused by expectations. The announcement of a future price increase can temporarily increase current demand, even though the eventual effect of the higher price is a lower quantity demanded.

⚠️ Common Mistakes to Avoid

  1. Mistake: Using "demand" and "quantity demanded" interchangeably. Why it's wrong: "Demand" refers to the entire demand curve — the relationship between price and quantity. "Quantity demanded" refers to a specific point on that curve at a specific price. Correct approach: If price changes, say "quantity demanded changes." If a non-price determinant changes, say "demand changes" (the curve shifts). IB mark schemes penalise this confusion.
  2. Mistake: Thinking that all goods are normal goods. Why it's wrong: Inferior goods exist, and the IB tests them regularly. Bus travel, cheap groceries, and second-hand goods are often inferior. Correct approach: When analysing the effect of an income change on demand, always ask: "Is this good normal or inferior?"
  3. Mistake: Confusing substitutes and complements when analysing cross-price effects. Why it's wrong: The direction of the demand shift is opposite. If the price of coffee rises, tea demand goes UP (substitute) but cream demand goes DOWN (complement). Correct approach: Ask: "Do consumers use these goods together (complements) or instead of each other (substitutes)?"
  4. Mistake: Treating a shift and a movement as the same thing when both happen. Why it's wrong: In complex real-world scenarios, a price change (movement) and a preference change (shift) can occur simultaneously. Correct approach: Analyse each effect separately, then combine them. The IB rewards this layered analysis.

9. Key Terms

TermDefinition
DemandThe quantity of a good that consumers are willing and able to buy at each possible price over a given time period, ceteris paribus
Law of demandAs the price of a good rises, the quantity demanded falls; as price falls, quantity demanded rises — ceteris paribus
Quantity demandedThe specific amount consumers are willing and able to buy at a particular price
Demand curveA graphical representation of the demand schedule, showing the relationship between price and quantity demanded
Movement along the demand curveA change in quantity demanded caused by a change in the good's own price
Shift of the demand curveA change in demand caused by a change in a non-price determinant; the entire curve moves left or right
Normal goodA good for which demand increases when consumer income rises
Inferior goodA good for which demand decreases when consumer income rises
Substitute goodsGoods that can be used in place of each other; a rise in the price of one increases demand for the other
Complementary goodsGoods that are used together; a rise in the price of one decreases demand for the other

10. Exam Tips

Paper 1: When asked to "explain the law of demand," you must include the phrase "ceteris paribus" in your definition. Then explain the income effect, substitution effect, and diminishing marginal utility. A diagram is essential — draw a downward-sloping curve with price on the vertical axis and quantity on the horizontal, and label at least two points.
Paper 2: Data response questions often present a scenario with multiple changes affecting demand. Your job is to separate them: which changes cause movements along the curve (own-price changes) and which cause shifts (non-price determinants)? List each change, classify it, and state the direction of the effect. Then combine them to give the net result.
