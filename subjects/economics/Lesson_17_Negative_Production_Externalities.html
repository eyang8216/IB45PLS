Lesson 17: Negative Production Externalities

What You'll Learn

  • The difference between private cost, external cost, and social cost
  • How to draw and interpret a negative production externality diagram
  • Why negative production externalities cause market failure (overproduction)
  • How to calculate and show the welfare loss (DWL)
  • Government solutions: Pigouvian taxes, regulation, and tradable permits
  • How to evaluate which solution is most effective

1. Private Cost, External Cost, and Social Cost

  • Marginal Private Cost (MPC) — the cost borne by the producer for producing one more unit. This is the firm's supply curve.
  • Marginal External Cost (MEC) — the cost imposed on third parties from producing one more unit. Example: pollution from a factory.
  • Marginal Social Cost (MSC) — the total cost to society of producing one more unit.
MSC = MPC + MEC

2. The Negative Production Externality Diagram

Price/Cost ↑ | MSC (= MPC + MEC) | /| | / | | / | MPC (= S) | / | | / | |-/-----|---------------- D (= MSB = MPB) |/ | +-------+-----------------→ Quantity Qopt Qfm
  • MPC is the firm's private supply curve.
  • MSC lies above MPC by the external cost at each quantity.
  • Qfm: where MPC = MPB — the free-market quantity.
  • Qopt: where MSC = MSB — the socially optimal quantity.
  • Result: Qfm > Qopt — overproduction.

3. The Welfare Loss (Deadweight Loss)

At Qfm, for units between Qopt and Qfm: MSC > MSB. These units cost society more to produce than the benefit they provide. The welfare loss (DWL) is the triangle bounded by MSC and MSB between Qopt and Qfm.

4. Why the Market Fails

  1. Producers only consider MPC (their private costs).
  2. They ignore external costs — pollution, health damage — because they don't pay for them.
  3. The market price (Pfm) is too low — it does not reflect the true social cost.
  4. Because price is too low, quantity is too high.

5. Government Solutions

A. Indirect Tax (Pigouvian Tax)

  • Tax per unit = MEC at Qopt. Shifts MPC upward to align with MSC.
  • Advantages: Market-based, incentivises cleaner tech, generates revenue.
  • Disadvantages: Difficult to measure exact external cost; if demand inelastic, may not reduce Q much.

B. Regulation (Command and Control)

  • Government sets emission limits or requires specific technology.
  • Advantages: Simple, direct, guarantees outcome if enforced.
  • Disadvantages: Inflexible, no incentive to go beyond minimum, costly monitoring.

C. Tradable Permits (Cap and Trade)

  • Set total emission cap, issue permits, allow trading.
  • Advantages: Cost-efficient (reductions happen where cheapest), certainty on total cap, incentivises innovation.
  • Disadvantages: Complex setup, initial allocation contentious, requires monitoring.

6. Real-World Examples

  • Factory Pollution: Chemical factories dumping waste — MSC exceeds MPC.
  • Carbon Emissions: Coal power plants emitting CO2 — global external costs.
  • EU Emissions Trading System (ETS): World's largest cap-and-trade, covers ~40% of EU emissions. Has faced permit overallocation and price volatility but reduced emissions.

IB Exam Tip

When drawing a negative production externality diagram:
• Draw MPC and MSC clearly, with MSC above MPC.
• Label vertical gap as "external cost" or MEC.
• Mark Qfm (MPC = MPB) and Qopt (MSC = MSB).
• Shade DWL triangle between Qopt and Qfm.

Answers

Answer 1

  • MPC: cost borne by producer of one more unit (supply curve).
  • MEC: cost imposed on third parties from one more unit produced.
  • MSC: total cost to society = MPC + MEC.

Answer 2

(Diagram — MPC upward-sloping, MSC above MPC, D=MSB downward-sloping. Qfm where MPC = MSB. Qopt where MSC = MSB. DWL shaded between Qopt and Qfm.)

Answer 3

(a) Firms base decisions on MPC only. They maximise profit where MPC = MPB, ignoring external costs.

(b) At Qfm, for units between Qopt and Qfm, MSC > MSB — cost to society exceeds benefit. DWL captures cumulative loss.

(c) A Pigouvian tax equal to MEC at Qopt shifts MPC up to coincide with MSC. New equilibrium at Qopt where MSC = MSB, eliminating DWL. The tax "internalises the externality."

(a) MSC = MPC + MEC = (10 + 2Q) + 6 = 16 + 2Q.

(b) Qfm: 10 + 2Q = 40 − Q → 3Q = 30 → Qfm = 10. Qopt: 16 + 2Q = 40 − Q → 3Q = 24 → Qopt = 8.

Answer 6

Model answer outline: Arguments for: market-based, static & dynamic efficiency, double dividend, price certainty. Arguments against: hard to set right rate, politically unpopular, cap-and-trade guarantees emission levels, regulation better for point-source pollution. Conclusion: carbon tax has strong theoretical advantages but real-world success depends on political design and complementary policies.

Evaluate

"A carbon tax is the most efficient way to reduce carbon emissions." Evaluate.

Model Answer (CLASPP Structure)

Conclusion: A carbon tax has strong theoretical efficiency advantages but may not always be "the most efficient" in practice, depending on political feasibility, measurement challenges, and the specific emission context. A well-designed carbon tax is among the best tools, but a combination with other policies may be more effective.

Logic (Arguments For): A carbon tax is a classic Pigouvian tax — it addresses the root cause of the externality by making emitters pay the social cost of carbon. It achieves static efficiency because firms with the lowest abatement costs reduce emissions most, minimising the total cost of achieving a given reduction. It provides dynamic efficiency by creating an ongoing incentive to innovate — every unit of carbon avoided saves the tax. The "double dividend" hypothesis suggests carbon tax revenue can replace distortionary taxes (e.g., on labour), improving overall economic efficiency. Unlike regulation, a carbon tax gives firms flexibility in how they reduce emissions. Unlike cap-and-trade, it provides price certainty, helping businesses plan long-term investments.

Logic (Arguments Against): However, the efficiency of a carbon tax depends on setting it at the correct rate — equal to the MEC of carbon at Qopt. This is extraordinarily difficult, involving uncertain climate projections and contested valuations of future damages. If set too low, emissions barely fall; too high, the economy is unnecessarily burdened. Political resistance is severe — carbon taxes are visible and unpopular (e.g., France's gilets jaunes protests). For some emissions sources, regulation may be more efficient: requiring specific technology (e.g., catalytic converters) can be simpler than taxing thousands of diffuse emitters.

Assumptions: The claim assumes that market-based instruments always outperform regulation and that governments can measure and set the optimal tax rate — both questionable. It also assumes competitive markets where firms respond rationally to price signals.

Stakeholders: Low-income households are disproportionately affected if carbon taxes raise energy prices — regressive without compensating transfers. Energy-intensive industries may lose competitiveness, leading to "carbon leakage." The environment and future generations are intended beneficiaries. Governments gain a new revenue stream.

Prescribed Solutions: The most effective climate policy likely combines: (1) a carbon price (tax or cap-and-trade) as the central mechanism, (2) targeted regulations for sectors where pricing is impractical, (3) subsidies for green R&D to accelerate innovation, and (4) revenue recycling to address regressivity and political feasibility. Sweden's carbon tax (~$130/tonne CO2) combined with broader policy has successfully reduced emissions while maintaining economic growth.

Precedent/Evidence: Sweden, with the world's highest carbon tax, has cut emissions ~27% since 1990 while GDP grew ~80%. British Columbia's revenue-neutral carbon tax reduced emissions and was initially popular. Conversely, Australia's carbon tax was repealed after political backlash. The EU ETS (cap-and-trade) has reduced emissions but suffered from price volatility.

A carbon tax is arguably the most theoretically efficient single instrument but may not be the most effective in isolation — real-world success depends on political design, complementary policies, and managing distributional impacts.
