## 1. What a Minimum Price Is and Why Governments Use It

### What a Price Floor Is

A **minimum price**, also called a **price floor**, is a legally established lowest price at which a good, service, or factor of production may be sold. For the floor to have any effect on the market, it must be set above the free-market equilibrium price. Sellers cannot legally sell below this price.

The **free-market equilibrium price** is the price at which the quantity that buyers want to purchase exactly equals the quantity that sellers want to supply. It is where the demand curve and the supply curve intersect. At this price, there is no surplus and no shortage — the market clears.

If a price floor is set below the equilibrium price, it has no effect. The market naturally settles at a higher price, and the floor is never reached. Economists call this a **non-binding price floor**. Only a floor set above equilibrium — a **binding price floor** — changes market outcomes.

### Why Governments Impose Minimum Prices

Governments impose price floors for three main reasons.

First, governments use price floors to protect the incomes of producers. Agricultural markets are volatile. A bumper harvest can cause prices to collapse, leaving farmers unable to cover their costs. A minimum price guarantees farmers a stable income regardless of market fluctuations. The European Union operated such a system for decades through its Common Agricultural Policy.

Second, governments use price floors to protect the incomes of workers. The **minimum wage** is the most widespread example of a price floor. It sets a legal minimum hourly wage that employers must pay their workers. As of 2024, the UK National Living Wage is £11.44 per hour for workers aged 21 and over.

Third, governments use minimum prices to discourage consumption of goods considered harmful. Scotland introduced a minimum unit price for alcohol in 2018, making it illegal to sell alcohol below 50 pence per unit of alcohol. The policy targeted cheap, high-strength drinks — the kind that can cause severe harm at a very low price.

A common mistake students make is thinking that a minimum price is always binding. A price floor set below the equilibrium price does nothing — the market ignores it entirely. For the floor to change outcomes, the regulated price must be above where supply and demand naturally meet. Always check whether the floor is above or below equilibrium before analysing its effects.

## 2. The Minimum Price Diagram — Goods Market

### What Happens When a Binding Floor Is Imposed

Consider a concrete example: the market for wheat. In an unregulated market, the equilibrium price is £200 per tonne, and at that price, farmers supply exactly 50,000 tonnes and millers demand exactly 50,000 tonnes. The market clears.

The government, worried about volatile farm incomes, sets a minimum price of £260 per tonne. Sellers cannot legally sell wheat below £260. This price is above the equilibrium of £200, so the floor is binding.

At £260 per tonne, two things happen simultaneously. On the demand side, the law of demand operates: millers face a higher price, so they reduce their purchases. Suppose quantity demanded falls to 30,000 tonnes. On the supply side, the law of supply operates: farmers see a guaranteed high price, so they expand production. Suppose quantity supplied rises to 80,000 tonnes.

The result is a **persistent surplus**: quantity supplied (80,000 tonnes) minus quantity demanded (30,000 tonnes) equals 50,000 tonnes of wheat that nobody wants to buy at the floor price.

### Why the Surplus Does Not Self-Correct

In an unregulated market, a surplus pushes the price down until equilibrium is restored. Sellers, unable to sell at the current price, cut prices to attract buyers. The price falls, quantity demanded rises, quantity supplied falls, and the surplus disappears.

A price floor blocks this mechanism. By law, the price cannot fall below £260 per tonne. The surplus persists because the price cannot adjust downward.

### The Government's Problem: What to Do with the Surplus

For agricultural price supports, governments typically solve this problem by buying the surplus themselves. The government becomes the purchaser of last resort, buying everything farmers produce at the guaranteed price. This has a direct cost to taxpayers: the floor price multiplied by the surplus quantity.

Using the numbers above: £260 per tonne × 50,000 tonnes = £13,000,000. That is £13 million that taxpayers must provide to purchase wheat that nobody in the market wants at that price.

This is not the only cost. The government must store the surplus wheat, which costs money for warehousing, climate control, and pest management. Wheat can spoil over time. If the government dumps the surplus on world markets, it depresses world prices and harms farmers in developing countries who depend on those prices.

The European Union's Common Agricultural Policy (CAP) generated famous examples of this problem. By the 1980s, the EU had accumulated vast stockpiles nicknamed "butter mountains" and "wine lakes" — surplus dairy products and wine bought at guaranteed minimum prices and stored in refrigerated warehouses at enormous taxpayer expense.

A common mistake students make is thinking that a price floor reduces the price consumers pay. The opposite is true: consumers pay the higher floor price. The floor protects sellers, not buyers. Consumer surplus falls because consumers pay more and buy less.

## 3. Welfare Analysis of a Price Floor

### What Welfare Analysis Measures

Welfare analysis examines how economic surplus — the total benefit that market participants derive from trade — changes when a policy is introduced. In a free market at equilibrium, total welfare (consumer surplus plus producer surplus) is maximised. A price floor reduces total welfare.

### Consumer Surplus

**Consumer surplus** is the difference between the maximum price consumers are willing to pay (shown by the demand curve) and the price they actually pay, summed over all units purchased. Before the floor, consumer surplus is the triangular area below the demand curve and above the equilibrium price, from zero to Qe. After the floor, consumers pay a higher price (Pf instead of Pe) and buy fewer units (Qd instead of Qe). Consumer surplus shrinks to the area below the demand curve and above Pf, from zero to Qd. Consumers are unambiguously worse off.

### Producer Surplus

**Producer surplus** is the difference between the price producers receive and the minimum price they would accept (shown by the supply curve), summed over all units sold. The effect of a price floor on producer surplus is ambiguous. Producers receive a higher price on the units they do sell, which increases surplus. However, they sell fewer units to private buyers (Qd instead of Qe), which decreases surplus. If the government buys the entire surplus at the floor price, producer surplus increases substantially — producers effectively sell everything they produce at the high floor price, either to private buyers or to the government.

### Government Expenditure

**Government expenditure** is the cost to taxpayers of buying the surplus. It equals the floor price multiplied by the surplus quantity: Pf × (Qs − Qd). This money has an opportunity cost — it could have funded schools, hospitals, or infrastructure instead of purchasing surplus agricultural output.

### Deadweight Loss

**Deadweight loss (DWL)** is welfare that is lost to society and not captured by anyone — not consumers, not producers, and not the government. A price floor creates DWL because it prevents mutually beneficial transactions from occurring.

Between Qd and Qe, there are consumers who value wheat more than the marginal cost of producing it. At the free-market equilibrium, these consumers would have bought wheat and both sides would have gained. Under the price floor, these transactions cannot legally happen because the floor price is above what these consumers are willing to pay and above what producers would need to receive. The lost gains from these prevented trades constitute the DWL. Graphically, the DWL is the triangle bounded by the demand curve and the supply curve between Qd and Qe.

A common mistake students make is confusing the DWL with the government expenditure rectangle. Government expenditure is a transfer from taxpayers to producers — it is not welfare lost to society, just redistributed. The DWL is genuinely destroyed welfare that no one receives.

## 4. The Minimum Wage — A Price Floor in the Labour Market

### What the Minimum Wage Is

A **minimum wage** is a price floor applied specifically to the labour market. It sets a legal minimum hourly wage that employers must pay their workers. In most countries, the minimum wage applies to all or most workers, though there are often exceptions for younger workers, apprentices, or certain sectors.

When the minimum wage is set above the equilibrium wage rate, it is binding. The analysis follows the same logic as a price floor in a goods market, but the labels and stakeholders differ.

### Labour Market Diagram

In the labour market diagram, the horizontal axis measures the **quantity of labour** — this can be the number of workers or the number of hours worked. The vertical axis measures the **wage rate**, which is the price of labour. The demand curve for labour comes from firms, who hire workers up to the point where the wage equals the marginal revenue product of labour. The supply curve of labour comes from workers, who offer their time up to the point where the wage compensates them for the opportunity cost of leisure.

When a minimum wage (Wm) is set above the equilibrium wage (We), two effects occur simultaneously. Firms, facing a higher cost per worker, reduce the number of workers they hire. The quantity of labour demanded falls from Le to Ld. Workers, seeing a higher wage on offer, increase the quantity of labour they are willing to supply. The quantity of labour supplied rises from Le to Ls.

The result is a surplus of labour. Ls minus Ld represents workers who want to work at the minimum wage but cannot find a job. This surplus is **unemployment** — specifically, it is the unemployment created by the minimum wage, distinct from unemployment caused by other factors such as recessions or structural changes in the economy.

### Key Effects on the Labour Market

First, there is a potential unemployment effect. Some workers — particularly young, low-skilled workers whose productivity is below the minimum wage — may lose their jobs or fail to find one. A 17-year-old with no experience whose labour is worth £8 per hour to an employer cannot legally be hired at that wage if the minimum is £11.44.

Second, workers who keep their jobs earn more. Their incomes rise, which can reduce poverty among the working poor. The wage increase is most significant for those at the very bottom of the wage distribution.

Third, firms may substitute capital for labour over time. If labour becomes more expensive relative to machinery, firms have an incentive to automate. Supermarkets install more self-checkout machines. Restaurants adopt ordering kiosks. Factories invest in robots.

Fourth, the magnitude of the unemployment effect depends critically on the **elasticity of labour demand**. Elasticity of labour demand measures how responsive firms' hiring is to changes in the wage rate. If firms find it difficult to replace workers with machines — as in care work, where human contact is essential — demand is inelastic, and the employment reduction is small. If firms can easily substitute capital for labour — as in routine manufacturing — demand is elastic, and the employment reduction is larger.

A common mistake students make is thinking that the minimum wage always causes massive unemployment. Empirical evidence tells a more nuanced story. David Card and Alan Krueger's influential 1994 study of fast-food restaurants in New Jersey found no evidence of employment reduction after a minimum wage increase. The actual effect depends on how high the minimum wage is set relative to the median wage and on the structure of the labour market. In a **monopsony** — a labour market with only one or a few employers — a moderate minimum wage can actually increase employment by countering the employer's wage-setting power.

## 5. Real-World Examples

### The European Union's Common Agricultural Policy (CAP)

For decades after its inception, the CAP set minimum prices for agricultural products including wheat, dairy, beef, and wine. The policy succeeded in stabilising farm incomes and ensuring food security after the shortages of the post-war period. However, by the 1980s, the CAP was consuming over 60% of the entire EU budget. Vast surplus stockpiles — butter mountains containing over a million tonnes of butter, and wine lakes of surplus wine distilled into industrial alcohol — became symbols of wasteful intervention. Reforms beginning in the 1990s shifted the CAP away from price supports toward direct income payments to farmers, decoupling support from production levels. This reduced surplus production while still protecting farm incomes.

### Minimum Wage in the United Kingdom

The UK introduced a National Minimum Wage in 1999 and later a higher National Living Wage (NLW) for workers aged 23 and over (reduced to 21 and over in 2024). As of April 2024, the NLW is £11.44 per hour. When the NLW was introduced and subsequently increased, many economists predicted job losses in low-wage sectors such as hospitality, retail, and social care. In practice, employment in these sectors continued to grow. The Low Pay Commission, the independent body that recommends the rate, aims for the NLW to reach 66% of median hourly earnings — a level that evidence suggests raises pay without causing significant employment damage.

### Scotland's Minimum Unit Pricing for Alcohol

In May 2018, Scotland became the first country in the world to introduce a minimum unit price (MUP) for alcohol, initially set at 50 pence per unit of alcohol (10ml of pure alcohol). The policy did not target all alcohol equally. It targeted the cheapest, strongest drinks — typically high-strength white cider sold in large bottles for very low prices, associated with chronic harmful drinking. At 50 pence per unit, a 3-litre bottle of 7.5% ABV cider containing 22.5 units must sell for at least £11.25, compared to the £3-4 it might have cost before. Early evaluations by Public Health Scotland found a 3% reduction in total alcohol sales and a larger reduction among the heaviest drinkers. The policy was raised to 65 pence per unit in September 2024. The policy remains debated, with critics arguing it is regressive and supporters pointing to reduced alcohol-related hospital admissions.

## IB Exam Tip

When drawing a minimum price diagram in an IB exam, label every element precisely. Axes must say "Price" and "Quantity" (or "Wage Rate" and "Quantity of Labour" for minimum wage). Draw and label the supply and demand curves, marking the equilibrium price and quantity. Draw a horizontal line at the floor price above equilibrium and label it "Price Floor" or "Minimum Price." Drop vertical dashed lines to the axes to mark Qd and Qs. Clearly indicate the surplus as the horizontal gap between Qs and Qd.

If the question asks about welfare, shade consumer surplus, producer surplus, the government expenditure rectangle, and the deadweight loss triangle. Use different patterns or labelling to distinguish them.

For minimum wage questions, the axes must say "Wage Rate" and "Quantity of Labour." Using "Price" on the vertical axis for a labour market diagram is incorrect and will lose marks. State explicitly that the floor must be set above equilibrium to be binding — this single sentence demonstrates understanding and earns marks on nearly every price floor question.

## Worked Example 1: Calculating the Effects of a Price Floor

A government sets a minimum price of £15 per kilogram for coffee beans. The demand for coffee beans is given by Qd = 1,000 − 40P, and the supply is given by Qs = −200 + 60P, where P is the price in pounds per kilogram and Q is in kilograms.

**Problem:** (a) Find the free-market equilibrium price and quantity. (b) Calculate quantity demanded and quantity supplied at the floor price. (c) Determine the size of the surplus. (d) Calculate the government's expenditure if it buys the entire surplus.

**Approach:** To find the free-market equilibrium, set quantity demanded equal to quantity supplied and solve for P. Then substitute this P into either equation to find Q. For the floor, evaluate both equations at P = 15. The surplus is Qs minus Qd. Government expenditure is the floor price multiplied by the surplus.

**Step 1 — Find equilibrium:**
Set Qd = Qs:
1,000 − 40P = −200 + 60P
1,000 + 200 = 60P + 40P
1,200 = 100P
P = £12 per kilogram

Substitute P = 12 into the demand equation:
Qd = 1,000 − 40(12) = 1,000 − 480 = 520 kilograms
At equilibrium, Qs also equals 520 kilograms. (Check: −200 + 60(12) = −200 + 720 = 520. ✓)

**Step 2 — Evaluate at the floor price of P = £15:**
Qd at floor = 1,000 − 40(15) = 1,000 − 600 = 400 kilograms
Qs at floor = −200 + 60(15) = −200 + 900 = 700 kilograms

**Step 3 — Calculate the surplus:**
Surplus = Qs − Qd = 700 − 400 = 300 kilograms

**Step 4 — Calculate government expenditure:**
Government expenditure = Pf × surplus = £15 × 300 = £4,500

**Why this makes sense:** At the higher floor price (£15 versus £12 equilibrium), consumers want less coffee (400 kg instead of 520 kg) and producers want to supply more (700 kg instead of 520 kg). The gap of 300 kg is the surplus. The government must spend £4,500 to buy this surplus and maintain the floor. This £4,500 is a cost to taxpayers that did not exist in the free market.

## Worked Example 2: Minimum Wage in a Local Labour Market

A local labour market for restaurant workers has demand for labour given by Ld = 500 − 10W and supply given by Ls = −100 + 20W, where W is the hourly wage in pounds and L is the number of workers.

**Problem:** (a) Find the equilibrium wage and employment level. (b) If the government imposes a minimum wage of £25 per hour, calculate the number of workers employed, the number seeking work, and the unemployment created. (c) Compare the total wage bill before and after the minimum wage.

**Approach:** To find equilibrium, set Ld = Ls and solve for W. Then evaluate Ld and Ls at the minimum wage. The total wage bill is the wage rate multiplied by the number of employed workers (Ld at the relevant wage), not the number seeking work.

**Step 1 — Find equilibrium:**
Set Ld = Ls:
500 − 10W = −100 + 20W
500 + 100 = 20W + 10W
600 = 30W
W = £20 per hour

Employment at equilibrium: L = 500 − 10(20) = 500 − 200 = 300 workers
(Check: Ls = −100 + 20(20) = −100 + 400 = 300. ✓)

**Step 2 — Evaluate at Wm = £25 per hour:**
Workers employed (Ld at W = 25) = 500 − 10(25) = 500 − 250 = 250 workers
Workers seeking work (Ls at W = 25) = −100 + 20(25) = −100 + 500 = 400 workers
Unemployment = Ls − Ld = 400 − 250 = 150 workers

**Step 3 — Compare wage bills:**
Wage bill at equilibrium = 300 workers × £20 = £6,000 per hour
Wage bill after minimum wage = 250 workers × £25 = £6,250 per hour
Change in wage bill = £6,250 − £6,000 = +£250 per hour

**Why this makes sense:** Total earnings rise slightly despite the fall in employment because the wage increase (25%, from £20 to £25) is proportionally larger than the employment reduction (16.7%, from 300 to 250). However, this aggregate figure hides the distribution: 250 workers each gain £5 per hour, 50 workers lose their entire £20 per hour wage, and 100 additional workers who would like to work at £25 cannot find jobs. The net effect on the wage bill alone does not capture the welfare loss from the unemployment created.

## Answers

**Answer 1.** A minimum price, also called a price floor, is a legally established lowest price at which a good, service, or factor of production may be sold. A binding price floor is set above the free-market equilibrium price. For example, if the equilibrium price for milk is £2 per litre, a floor set at £3 per litre is binding — sellers cannot sell below £3, so the market price is forced above equilibrium. This creates a surplus because quantity supplied exceeds quantity demanded at the higher price. A non-binding price floor is set below the free-market equilibrium price. For example, if equilibrium is £2 per litre, a floor set at £1 per litre has no effect because the market naturally settles at £2, well above the floor. No seller would voluntarily sell at £1 when they can get £2 in the free market. Only binding floors change market outcomes.

**Answer 2.** (a) The free-market equilibrium occurs where quantity demanded equals quantity supplied. At £2 per litre, Qd is 700 litres and Qs is 500 litres — quantity demanded exceeds quantity supplied, so there is a shortage. At £3 per litre, Qd is 500 litres and Qs is 700 litres — quantity supplied exceeds quantity demanded, so there is a surplus. The equilibrium must lie between £2 and £3 where the two quantities are equal. Since the data are given at discrete price intervals, the exact equilibrium price is approximately £2.50 per litre and the equilibrium quantity is approximately 600 litres — the midpoint where the demand and supply cross.

(b) At the floor price of £3 per litre, Qd is 500 litres and Qs is 700 litres. The surplus is Qs minus Qd, which equals 700 − 500 = 200 litres.

(c) The government buys the surplus at the floor price. Direct cost to taxpayers = Pf × surplus = £3 × 200 = £600.

A common pitfall: students sometimes calculate the surplus as Qs − Qe rather than Qs − Qd at the floor price. The surplus is the gap between what producers want to sell and what consumers want to buy at the regulated price, not at the original equilibrium. At £3, consumers only want 500 litres, so 200 of the 700 litres produced remain unsold.

**Answer 3.** The diagram should show a downward-sloping demand curve (D) and an upward-sloping supply curve (S) intersecting at equilibrium (Pe, Qe). The price floor (Pf) is a horizontal line drawn above Pe. Where Pf meets the demand curve, a vertical dashed line drops to mark Qd on the horizontal axis. Where Pf meets the supply curve, a vertical dashed line drops to mark Qs. The horizontal distance between Qs and Qd is labelled "Surplus." Government expenditure is the rectangle with height Pf (from the horizontal axis to the floor line) and width equal to the surplus (Qs − Qd), extending from Qd to Qs. Deadweight loss is the triangle bounded by the demand curve above and the supply curve below, between Qd and Qe. Consumer surplus after the floor is the triangle below D and above Pf, from zero to Qd.

**Answer 4.** (a) Set Ld = Ls: 600 − 20W = −100 + 30W. Then 600 + 100 = 30W + 20W, so 700 = 50W, and W = £14 per hour. Substitute W = 14 into the demand equation: L = 600 − 20(14) = 600 − 280 = 320 workers. At equilibrium, 320 workers are employed at a wage of £14 per hour.

(b) At Wm = £18 per hour:
Workers employed = Ld at W = 18: 600 − 20(18) = 600 − 360 = 240 workers.
Workers seeking work = Ls at W = 18: −100 + 30(18) = −100 + 540 = 440 workers.
Unemployed = Ls − Ld = 440 − 240 = 200 workers.

(c) Total wage bill at equilibrium: 320 workers × £14 = £4,480 per hour.
Total wage bill after minimum wage: 240 workers × £18 = £4,320 per hour.
The total wage bill falls by £160 per hour. Although the wage per worker rose by £4 (a 29% increase), the number of employed workers fell by 80 (a 25% decrease). The employment reduction more than offsets the wage gain. This outcome occurs because labour demand in this market is relatively elastic — firms reduce hiring substantially when the wage rises.

**Answer 5.** (a) The diagram should show a supply and demand graph with the floor price drawn above equilibrium. At the floor price, the quantity supplied exceeds the quantity demanded. The gap between Qs and Qd is the surplus. The price mechanism cannot clear this surplus because the law prevents the price from falling. The surplus persists, and the government must either purchase it, store it, or allow it to go to waste. (3 marks: 1 for correct diagram with labels, 1 for identifying that Qs > Qd at the floor, 1 for explaining why the surplus persists.)

(b) One advantage for producers: farmers receive a guaranteed price above the market equilibrium, which stabilises their incomes and protects them from price volatility. This enables them to plan production, invest in their farms, and avoid the risk of prices falling below production costs during bumper harvests. One disadvantage for consumers: they pay a higher price for food, which reduces their purchasing power. This effect is regressive — lower-income households spend a larger share of their income on food than higher-income households. Taxpayers also lose because they must fund the government purchases of surplus production, money that could have been spent on schools, healthcare, or public infrastructure. (3 marks: 1 for producer advantage with explanation, 1 for consumer or taxpayer disadvantage with explanation, 1 for explicitly identifying the stakeholders.)

**Answer 6.** (a) A minimum price sets a legal floor above the free-market equilibrium. The government typically commits to buying whatever surplus is produced. The market price rises above equilibrium, consumers buy less, and producers supply more. The cost is borne by taxpayers who fund the surplus purchases, and by consumers who pay higher prices. A per-unit subsidy is a payment from the government to producers for each unit produced. The subsidy shifts the supply curve downward by the subsidy amount. The market price falls, consumers buy more, and producers receive the market price plus the subsidy. The cost is borne entirely by taxpayers through government spending.

(b) The minimum price is more likely to create deadweight loss from physical waste. If the government buys the surplus, it must store it, and perishable goods like milk or fresh produce may spoil. Even if stored, the resources used to produce the surplus — land, labour, fertiliser, water — were used to create goods that nobody values at the floor price. This is a genuine waste of resources. Under a subsidy, the market clears at the lower equilibrium price — there is no physical surplus to store or dispose of. The DWL from a subsidy comes from overproduction (units produced beyond the efficient quantity where MC > MB), but there is no additional waste from storage or spoilage.

(c) A subsidy is preferable when the government's objective includes keeping consumer prices low. If the aim is to make food affordable while still supporting farmers, a subsidy achieves both — farmers receive more (market price plus subsidy) while consumers pay less. A minimum price raises consumer prices, which may be politically unpopular and regressive. A subsidy is also preferable when the government wants to avoid the administrative complexity and waste of storing and disposing of surplus production. However, if the primary objective is simply to guarantee farmers a minimum income regardless of market conditions, a minimum price with government purchase provides more direct income certainty than a subsidy, where the final price farmers receive still depends on the market clearing level.

## Evaluate

**Question:** "A minimum wage always increases unemployment among low-skilled workers." Evaluate this statement.

**Model Answer:**

The statement asserts a universal, inevitable connection between minimum wages and unemployment. The basic supply-and-demand model of a competitive labour market does predict that a price floor set above equilibrium creates a surplus of labour — unemployment. However, the real-world evidence is considerably more nuanced. The statement is too absolute to be accepted without significant qualification.

The theoretical case for the statement begins with the competitive labour market model. When a minimum wage Wm is set above the equilibrium wage We, the quantity of labour demanded falls from Le to Ld while the quantity supplied rises from Le to Ls. The resulting surplus — Ls minus Ld — represents workers who would like to work at Wm but cannot find employment. Low-skilled workers, whose productivity (and therefore their equilibrium wage) is closest to the minimum wage, are most at risk of being priced out of the market. Over time, firms may respond by substituting capital for labour — installing self-checkout kiosks to replace cashiers, or using automated phone systems instead of receptionists.

However, this analysis rests on the assumption that labour markets are perfectly competitive. Many labour markets are not. A **monopsony** is a market with a single or dominant employer who has wage-setting power. A monopsonist hires fewer workers and pays a lower wage than a competitive market would produce because the firm faces an upward-sloping labour supply curve and must raise wages for all workers to hire an additional one. In a monopsonistic labour market, a moderate minimum wage can actually increase both wages and employment. By preventing the firm from suppressing wages, the floor forces the monopsonist toward the competitive outcome. Monopsony power is not rare: many local labour markets, particularly in rural areas, for specialised healthcare workers, or in company towns, exhibit monopsonistic features.

The empirical evidence is instructive. David Card and Alan Krueger's landmark 1994 study examined employment in fast-food restaurants in New Jersey after the state raised its minimum wage, using neighbouring Pennsylvania (which did not raise its wage) as a control. They found no evidence of employment reduction; in some specifications, employment in New Jersey actually rose relative to Pennsylvania. Subsequent meta-analyses covering hundreds of studies across many countries have generally found that moderate minimum wages — set at roughly 45% to 55% of the median wage — produce small or statistically insignificant employment effects. Large increases, particularly those pushing the minimum wage above 60% of the median, do tend to show measurable job losses, especially among teenagers and in low-wage sectors.

The elasticity of labour demand also determines the magnitude of any employment effect. In industries where labour is a small share of total costs and cannot easily be substituted — care work, for example, where human interaction is the product itself — labour demand is inelastic. The employment reduction from a higher minimum wage is small. In industries where labour is a large share of costs and automation is feasible — routine manufacturing, data entry — labour demand is more elastic, and employment effects are larger.

The statement also ignores dynamic effects that can offset the static employment reduction. Higher wages may reduce costly employee turnover, lowering firms' recruitment and training expenses. Better-paid workers may be more productive and motivated. Higher wages increase workers' purchasing power, boosting demand for goods and services, which in turn increases the demand for labour. These effects do not necessarily cancel the employment reduction entirely, but they can reduce its size.

Considering stakeholders: low-skilled workers who retain their jobs unambiguously gain — they earn more per hour. Those who lose jobs or cannot find them bear significant losses. Firms face higher wage bills but may benefit from lower turnover and higher productivity. Consumers may face slightly higher prices if firms pass on increased labour costs. The government may spend less on in-work benefits (such as tax credits or housing benefit) if higher wages reduce the need for income supplementation, partially offsetting the fiscal cost.

The most defensible policy stance is not to abolish minimum wages entirely, nor to raise them without limit, but to calibrate them carefully. Setting the minimum wage with reference to a benchmark — such as 60% of median hourly earnings, as the UK Low Pay Commission does — provides a data-driven anchor. Differentiated rates for younger workers, apprentices, or regions with different living costs can preserve the protective function of the floor while minimising employment damage. Complementary policies such as earned income tax credits and publicly funded training can support the incomes and productivity of low-skilled workers without placing the entire burden on employers.

In conclusion, the statement "a minimum wage always increases unemployment among low-skilled workers" is an oversimplification that does not survive scrutiny. A minimum wage set at a moderate level in a labour market with some monopsony power and inelastic demand may produce little to no unemployment and may even increase employment. A minimum wage set too high, in a highly competitive labour market with elastic demand, will indeed cause job losses. The effect is contingent on the level at which the floor is set, the competitive structure of the labour market, the elasticity of labour demand, and the broader policy environment. The theoretical prediction of the simple competitive model is a starting point for analysis, not the final word.
