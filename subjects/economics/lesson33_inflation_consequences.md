## 1. Redistribution Effects: Winners and Losers

Unexpected inflation transfers real resources between groups. It does NOT make society poorer overall — it **redistributes** wealth.

### Losers from Unexpected Inflation

| Group | Why They Lose |
|---|---|
| **Savers** | Real value of savings erodes. $10,000 saved at 2% interest with 5% inflation = real return of −3%. Purchasing power shrinks. |
| **Fixed-income earners** | Pensioners on fixed nominal pensions see real income fall. A $2,000/month pension with 6% inflation buys 6% less real goods. |
| **Lenders/Creditors** | They are repaid in money worth less than when they lent it. Banks, bondholders suffer unless interest rates adjust. |
| **Workers on slow-to-adjust contracts** | Nominal wages lag behind inflation → real wages fall until contracts are renegotiated. |
| **Export-competing firms** | If domestic inflation exceeds trading partners', exports lose competitiveness (detailed in Section 4). |

### Winners from Unexpected Inflation

| Group | Why They Win |
|---|---|
| **Borrowers/Debtors** | They repay loans with "cheaper" money. A $200,000 mortgage becomes easier to repay as nominal incomes rise with inflation. |
| **Government** | Inflation erodes the real value of national debt. Also, fiscal drag (bracket creep) increases tax revenue without rate increases. |
| **Asset holders** | Real assets (property, gold, equities) tend to rise with inflation, preserving real wealth. |

### Anticipated vs Unanticipated Inflation
- **Anticipated**: If inflation is correctly expected, interest rates incorporate an inflation premium, contracts are indexed, and redistribution is minimal.
- **Unanticipated**: Creates the redistributions above — the key damage is from **unexpected** inflation.

### Worked Example: The Saver's Loss
A retiree has $100,000 in a savings account earning 1.5% interest. Inflation is 4%.
- Nominal gain = $100,000 × 0.015 = $1,500
- Real value erosion = $100,000 × 0.04 = $4,000 lost in purchasing power
- Real return = 1.5% − 4% = **−2.5%**
- After one year, real purchasing power = $100,000 × (1 − 0.025) = $97,500
- The retiree effectively "paid" $2,500 (in real terms) to hold cash.

## 2. Real Costs of Inflation (Even Anticipated)

Even perfectly anticipated inflation imposes costs:

### Menu Costs
The physical and administrative costs of changing prices: printing new menus, updating price tags, reprogramming vending machines, updating websites. In hyperinflation, restaurants may change prices daily — significant waste of resources.

### Shoe Leather Costs
The costs of minimizing cash holdings (running to the bank more often). When inflation is high, holding cash is costly, so people make more frequent trips to withdraw smaller amounts — wearing out their "shoe leather." Metaphorically: the time and effort spent managing money instead of productive activities.

### Uncertainty → Reduced Investment
- When inflation is **volatile**, firms cannot predict future costs, revenues, or prices
- Long-term contracts become risky → firms delay investment
- NPV calculations become unreliable → capital formation slows
- **Result**: Lower potential growth (LRAS shifts right more slowly)

### Fiscal Drag (Bracket Creep)
- Progressive income tax brackets are typically set in **nominal** terms
- As nominal incomes rise with inflation, workers move into higher tax brackets
- Their tax rate rises even though **real** income has not
- Government collects more revenue without legislating a tax increase
- **Example**: Tax bracket at $50,000–$90,000 = 20%; above $90,000 = 30%. With 10% inflation, a worker earning $82,000 now earns $90,200 and enters the 30% bracket — but can buy exactly the same real goods.

## 3. International Competitiveness

If a country's inflation rate **exceeds that of its trading partners**:

1. Domestic goods become **relatively more expensive**
2. Exports (X) fall — foreign buyers switch to cheaper alternatives
3. Imports (M) rise — domestic consumers buy cheaper foreign goods
4. Net exports (X−M) fall → AD shifts LEFT
5. **Trade deficit** widens
6. Downward pressure on the exchange rate (in floating systems)

**The European debt crisis example**: Southern eurozone countries (Greece, Spain, Italy) had higher inflation than Germany for years. Unable to devalue (same currency), their exports became uncompetitive → persistent current account deficits.

## 5. The Short-Run Phillips Curve (SRPC)

### The Trade-Off

A.W. Phillips (1958) observed an **inverse relationship** between wage inflation and unemployment in UK data (1861–1957). This was generalized to the relationship between **price inflation and unemployment**:

$$\pi = \pi^e - \beta(u - u_n)$$

Where π = actual inflation, πᵉ = expected inflation, u = unemployment, uₙ = natural rate of unemployment, β = sensitivity parameter.

### The SRPC in Diagram Form

- **Vertical axis**: Inflation rate (π)
- **Horizontal axis**: Unemployment rate (u)
- **SRPC slopes DOWNWARD** — lower unemployment is associated with higher inflation, and vice versa

### Why the Trade-Off Exists

Link to AD/AS:
1. AD increases → economy moves along SRAS → higher Y, higher P
2. Higher Y → lower unemployment (more workers needed)
3. Higher P → higher inflation
4. **Thus**: lower unemployment ↔ higher inflation (the SRPC trade-off)

**Similarly**:
1. AD decreases → lower Y, lower P
2. Lower Y → higher unemployment
3. Lower P → lower inflation
4. **Thus**: higher unemployment ↔ lower inflation

### What Shifts the SRPC?

The SRPC **shifts** when SRAS shifts:

| SRAS shift | Cause | SRPC shift |
|---|---|---|
| SRAS LEFT (cost-push) | Oil price spike, wage surge | SRPC shifts RIGHT (worse trade-off) |
| SRAS RIGHT (supply improvement) | Productivity gains, commodity price fall | SRPC shifts LEFT (better trade-off) |

**Key insight**: A right-shifted SRPC means that any given unemployment rate now comes with **higher inflation** — stagflation visualized.

## 6. The Long-Run Phillips Curve (LRPC) — Monetarist View

### The Natural Rate Hypothesis

Milton Friedman and Edmund Phelps argued the Phillips Curve trade-off exists only in the **short run**. In the long run:

- Expected inflation adjusts to actual inflation
- Economy returns to the **natural rate of unemployment (NRU)**
- At NRU, any inflation rate is possible — the LRPC is **vertical**

### The Mechanism

1. Central bank expands money supply → AD↑ → unemployment temporarily falls below NRU
2. Inflation rises (workers' real wages fall initially)
3. Workers realize real wages have fallen → demand higher nominal wages
4. Nominal wages rise → SRAS shifts LEFT (cost-push)
5. Economy returns to NRU, but at a **permanently higher inflation rate**
6. To keep unemployment below NRU again → must accelerate inflation further

**This is the accelerationist hypothesis**: Keeping unemployment below NRU requires ever-accelerating inflation.

### The LRPC Diagram
- **Vertical line** at the NRU (u = uₙ)
- The SRPC intersects the LRPC at the point where π = πᵉ (expected inflation = actual)
- If π > πᵉ: unemployment < NRU (move along SRPC)
- As πᵉ adjusts upward: SRPC shifts RIGHT until unemployment = NRU again

### Policy Implication
- **Monetarist view**: Expansionary policy cannot permanently reduce unemployment below NRU. Attempts only produce accelerating inflation.
- **Implication**: Focus on supply-side policies to reduce the NRU itself (education, retraining, labor market reforms).

## Answers

### Answer 1
**Group 1: Savers**. A saver with $50,000 earning 1% interest when inflation is 4% suffers a real return of −3%: nominal earnings = $500, but purchasing power loss = $2,000. Real wealth shrinks by $1,500.

**Group 2: Fixed-income pensioners**. A pensioner receiving $1,800/month with 5% inflation sees real income fall to $1,800/1.05 ≈ $1,714 in base-year purchasing power each month — an annual real loss of about $1,032. Their lifestyle must contract unless pension is indexed.

### Answer 2
**Menu costs**: The costs firms incur to change prices — printing new menus, relabeling shelves, updating computer systems, informing customers. Example: During Brazil's hyperinflation in the 1990s, supermarkets employed workers whose sole job was to change price tags daily.

**Shoe leather costs**: The costs (time, effort, transaction fees) of minimizing cash holdings during inflation. When holding cash is costly, people make more frequent bank trips to withdraw smaller amounts. Example: In high-inflation Zimbabwe (2008), workers rushed to convert wages into goods or foreign currency the same day they were paid, spending hours in bank queues.

**Key distinction**: Menu costs = firms' costs of changing prices. Shoe leather costs = individuals' costs of managing money.

### Answer 3
**The SRPC**: Downward-sloping curve with inflation rate (π) on the vertical axis and unemployment rate (u) on the horizontal. Illustrates the inverse short-run trade-off: lower unemployment is associated with higher inflation.

**Link to AD/AS**:
- When AD shifts RIGHT: economy moves up along SRAS → higher real GDP (Y↑) and higher price level (P↑)
- Higher Y means firms need more workers → unemployment falls (u↓)
- Higher P means inflation rises (π↑)
- This gives us a point on the SRPC: lower u, higher π

- When AD shifts LEFT: Y↓, P↓ → unemployment rises (u↑), inflation falls (π↓) → point on SRPC: higher u, lower π

The entire SRPC is traced by shifting AD along a given SRAS curve. When SRAS itself shifts, the entire SRPC shifts.

### Answer 4
**AD/AS framework**: An oil price spike raises firms' production costs → SRAS shifts LEFT. New equilibrium: higher P (inflation ↑) AND lower Y (unemployment ↑). This is stagflation.

**Phillips Curve**: Before the shock, the economy is on SRPC₀. After the shock, at any given unemployment rate, inflation is now higher (because SRAS has shifted left, raising costs and prices). The entire SRPC shifts RIGHT to SRPC₁.

**New trade-off**: The economy now faces higher inflation at every unemployment level. To achieve the same unemployment as before, policymakers must accept more inflation. To achieve the same inflation, they must accept more unemployment.

**Diagram**: SRPC₀ → SRPC₁ (shift right). The economy moves to a point with both higher u and higher π than the original equilibrium.

### Answer 5

**Sequence (Monetarist Model)**:

**Phase 1 — AD Expansion**:
- Government/central bank expands AD (fiscal stimulus or rate cut) → AD shifts RIGHT
- Economy moves along SRAS → Y↑, P↑ → unemployment falls from 5% to 3%
- On the Phillips Curve: economy moves UP-LEFT along SRPC₀ (u = 3%, π = 4%, say)

**Phase 2 — Workers Adjust Expectations**:
- Workers realize inflation is now 4%, not the 2% they expected
- Their real wages have fallen → they demand higher nominal wage increases in the next bargaining round
- Firms pass higher wage costs to consumers → SRAS shifts LEFT

**Phase 3 — Return to NRU**:
- Economy returns to Yf, but at a higher P (and higher π)
- Unemployment returns to 5% (NRU)
- On the Phillips Curve: SRPC shifts RIGHT to SRPC₁. Economy is now at u = 5%, but π = 6%

**Phase 4 — If Government Persists**:
- To get unemployment back to 3%, government must expand AD again
- This produces temporary u = 3%, but inflation accelerates to, say, 8%
- Workers adjust again → SRPC shifts further right
- **Result**: Unemployment keeps returning to NRU, but at ever-higher inflation rates — an **accelerating inflation spiral**.

**Conclusion**: Persistently targeting unemployment below NRU produces accelerating inflation, not permanently lower unemployment. The only lasting solution is supply-side policies to reduce the NRU itself.

### Answer 6 (Full IB-Style Response)

**Introduction**: A 2% inflation target has been adopted by most major central banks (Fed, ECB, Bank of England, Bank of Japan). The statement claims this is the "ideal" target — a claim requiring examination of the costs and benefits at different inflation levels.

**Arguments for 2%**:
1. **Avoids deflation**: 2% provides a buffer against deflation. If a recession hits, inflation can fall 2–3 percentage points before crossing into deflation, giving central banks room to cut rates.
2. **Greases the wheels**: Near-zero inflation makes relative price adjustments harder. With 2%, firms can cut real wages without cutting nominal wages (nominal wage freeze = real wage cut of ~2%), facilitating labor market adjustment.
3. **Zero lower bound**: With 2% baseline inflation, nominal interest rates can be higher, giving more room to cut before hitting zero (the "zero lower bound" constraint on monetary policy).
4. **Measured inflation overstates true inflation**: CPI overstates inflation by ~0.5–1% due to substitution bias and quality changes. A 2% CPI target means true inflation is closer to 1–1.5%.

**Arguments Against 2% (or for a different target)**:
1. **Why 2% and not 0%, 3%, or 4%?** The 2% target is somewhat arbitrary — historically derived from New Zealand's pioneering inflation targeting in 1990, not from rigorous optimization.
2. **Menu costs and shoe leather costs still exist at 2%**: Prices double every ~35 years at 2%. Some argue 0% would eliminate these costs entirely.
3. **Higher target for more policy room**: After 2008, some economists (e.g., Blanchard, Krugman) argued for 4% to give central banks more room to cut rates in recessions.
4. **One-size-fits-all problem**: 2% may be too high for rapidly aging economies with low potential growth (Japan) and too low for fast-growing emerging economies where productivity-driven inflation is natural.

**The Deflation Risk Perspective**:
- Deflation is far more dangerous than moderate inflation (deflationary spiral, rising real debt)
- A target slightly above zero is insurance against this catastrophic scenario
- The asymmetry of risks (deflation >> moderate inflation) justifies a positive target

**Conclusion**: A low and stable inflation rate of around 2% is a reasonable and empirically successful target. It avoids the catastrophe of deflation while keeping inflation costs manageable. However, "ideal" is too strong — the optimal target depends on country-specific factors, and the case for 2% specifically (rather than 1% or 3%) rests more on convention and central bank credibility than on definitive economic proof. The key word is "stable" — predictability matters more than the precise number.

## Evaluate

**"A low and stable inflation rate of 2% is the ideal target."**

### CLASPP Analysis (168 words)

**Conclusion**: The statement has merit as a pragmatic central bank consensus, but "ideal" overstates the case. A 2% target provides a useful buffer against deflation while keeping inflation costs low, but the precise figure is conventional rather than scientifically optimal.

**Long-term vs Short-term**: In the short run, a 2% target anchors expectations and provides policy predictability. In the long run, even 2% erodes purchasing power significantly — prices double every ~35 years — suggesting lower might be "more ideal" if sustainable.

**Assumptions**: The statement assumes central banks can credibly target 2%, that the zero lower bound is a binding constraint, and that measured CPI inflation accurately reflects true inflation. It also assumes the costs of 2% inflation (menu costs, redistribution) are small — which is largely true but not universally.

**Stakeholders**: Savers benefit from any movement toward 0%; borrowers benefit from any movement above 0%. Central banks benefit from a clear, communicable target. Workers benefit from the "grease" effect on real wages.

**Priorities**: Avoiding deflation must be the highest priority given its catastrophic potential. A positive target serves this priority. Stability of the target matters as much as its level.

**Pros/Cons**: Pro — empirically successful, deflation insurance, expectation anchoring. Con — arbitrary number, one-size-fits-all, erodes purchasing power slowly but steadily.

**Overall**: 2% is a reasonable, not provably ideal, target.
