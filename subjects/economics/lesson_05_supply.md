## 1. What Supply Means in Economics

### The Producer's Side of the Market

If demand represents the behaviour of consumers, supply represents the behaviour of producers. In economics, **supply** is the quantity of a good or service that producers are willing **and able** to produce and sell at each possible price, over a given time period, ceteris paribus.

The phrase "willing and able" does the same work here that it does on the demand side. A firm must be **willing** to produce — meaning it finds production profitable or strategically worthwhile at the prevailing price. A firm must also be **able** to produce — meaning it has the physical capacity, the workers, the raw materials, and the technology to manufacture the good.

A small bakery might be willing to supply 5,000 loaves of bread per day at a price that would earn healthy profits. But if its ovens can only produce 800 loaves per day, its ability constrains its supply. The bakery's quantity supplied is the smaller of what it wants to sell and what it can physically produce.

Supply is not about what exists in a warehouse or what could theoretically be made. It is about what producers actually choose to bring to market at each price.

### Why Supply Matters

Supply is the other half of every market. Consumers express what they want through demand. Producers determine what is available through supply. The interaction between the two determines prices, quantities, and the allocation of resources across the economy. If you understand demand but not supply, you understand only half of any market question.

---

## 2. The Law of Supply

### The Statement

The **law of supply** states:

> As the price of a good rises, the quantity supplied rises, ceteris paribus. As the price falls, the quantity supplied falls, ceteris paribus.

There is a **positive (direct) relationship** between price and quantity supplied. When the market price of wheat rises from £200 to £260 per tonne, farmers plant more hectares, apply more fertiliser, and harvest more intensively. When the price of oil falls from $90 to $55 per barrel, extraction companies idle rigs, cancel exploration projects, and reduce output.

Higher prices give producers both the incentive and the means to expand production. The incentive is profit: at a higher price, each unit sold generates more revenue relative to its cost. The means is the ability to cover the higher marginal costs that come with expanded output.

---

## 3. Why Supply Curves Slope Upward

### Two Mechanisms Producing the Positive Relationship

**The profit motive.** When the market price rises, producing the good becomes more profitable. Existing firms respond by expanding output — running factories for additional shifts, hiring more workers, using capacity that was previously idle. New firms, observing the profit opportunity, may enter the market, adding their output to the total. Higher prices act as a signal: "Make more of this. The market rewards you for doing so."

**Increasing marginal costs.** In the short run, as a firm expands output, the cost of producing each additional unit — the **marginal cost** — tends to rise. This happens because of the **law of diminishing marginal returns**: when at least one factor of production is fixed (a factory building, a piece of land), adding more of the variable factors (workers, raw materials) eventually produces smaller and smaller increases in output. The tenth worker hired into the same factory adds less additional output than the ninth worker did, because the fixed factory space becomes crowded and workers get in each other's way. Since each additional unit costs more to produce, a firm will only produce that unit if the price it receives covers the higher cost. A higher price justifies higher marginal cost, and therefore higher output.

A profit-maximising firm produces up to the point where the price it receives equals the marginal cost of the last unit produced. At a price of £10, the firm produces every unit whose marginal cost is £10 or less. At a price of £16, it also produces the units whose marginal cost lies between £10 and £16. The supply curve, for a firm in a competitive market, is its marginal cost curve — at least above the point where the firm would choose to shut down rather than produce at all.

## 4. Supply Schedules and Supply Curves

### From Table to Graph

A **supply schedule** is a table showing the quantity of a good that producers are willing and able to supply at each possible price. Consider the weekly supply of sandwiches from a campus café:

| Price per sandwich (£) | Quantity supplied (sandwiches per week) |
|---|---|
| 1.00 | 100 |
| 2.00 | 250 |
| 3.00 | 450 |
| 4.00 | 700 |
| 5.00 | 1,000 |
| 6.00 | 1,350 |

At £1 per sandwich, the café supplies only 100 — the price barely covers the cost of ingredients plus some labour, so the café limits sandwich production and focuses on higher-margin items. At £6 per sandwich, the profit margin is substantial, and the café expands production to 1,350 sandwiches, hiring extra staff and using all available kitchen capacity.

The **supply curve** is the graphical representation of this data. Price is on the vertical axis; quantity supplied is on the horizontal axis. The curve slopes upward — as price rises, quantity supplied rises.

A common mistake in labelling: students sometimes write "Price" and "Quantity" without units. Always specify: "Price (£ per sandwich)" and "Quantity supplied (sandwiches per week)."

### Individual Supply and Market Supply

**Individual supply** is the supply of a single firm. **Market supply** is the horizontal summation of all individual firm supply curves — at each price, add up the quantities supplied by every firm in the market.

If Firm A supplies 200 sandwiches at £3 and Firm B supplies 300 sandwiches at £3, market supply at £3 is 500 sandwiches. Repeat this addition at every price to build the market supply schedule.

## 5. Movement Along the Curve vs. Shift of the Curve

### The Same Logic as Demand, Applied to Producers

The distinction between movement and shift is as important on the supply side as on the demand side, and the logic is identical.

A **movement along the supply curve** occurs when the price of the good itself changes. The supply curve does not move. The producer simply responds to the new price by moving to a different point on the existing curve.

- When price rises, there is an **expansion of supply**: quantity supplied increases as we move up and to the right along the curve.
- When price falls, there is a **contraction of supply**: quantity supplied decreases as we move down and to the left along the curve.

A **shift of the supply curve** occurs when something other than the good's own price changes — the cost of inputs, the state of technology, the number of firms in the market, government tax or subsidy policy, or a supply shock. The entire curve moves.

- When supply **increases**, the curve shifts to the right. At every price, quantity supplied is higher.
- When supply **decreases**, the curve shifts to the left. At every price, quantity supplied is lower.

The terminology matters for the same reason it matters on the demand side. "Supply increases" describes a rightward shift caused by a non-price determinant. "Quantity supplied increases" describes a movement along the curve caused by a rise in the good's own price. The IB examiner expects you to use these phrases precisely.

## 6. The Non-Price Determinants of Supply — PINTS

Five categories of factors, independent of the good's own price, can shift the entire supply curve. The acronym **PINTS** helps you remember them.

### P — Productivity

Productivity measures how much output a firm gets from a given quantity of inputs. When productivity rises, the firm can produce more output with the same workers, machines, and materials. Per-unit costs fall. Supply increases — the curve shifts right.

Productivity can rise for many reasons. Better training and education improve the quality of labour — workers produce more per hour. Improved management practices reduce waste and downtime. Better organisation of the production process — rearranging a factory floor so that materials flow more smoothly — can yield productivity gains without any new equipment.

A fall in productivity — caused perhaps by worker exhaustion, outdated methods, or disruption from a reorganisation — shifts supply left.

### I — Indirect Taxes and Subsidies

An **indirect tax** is a tax on spending, collected by firms and paid to the government. Value Added Tax (VAT) and excise duties on alcohol, tobacco, and fuel are indirect taxes. When the government imposes or increases an indirect tax on a good, the firm's costs rise. For any given market price, the firm now receives less after paying the tax. Supply decreases — the curve shifts left.

A **subsidy** is a payment from the government to firms, typically per unit of output. A subsidy reduces the firm's effective costs. For any given market price, the firm now receives the market price plus the subsidy. Supply increases — the curve shifts right.

A common mistake is to think that a tax on consumers (like VAT) shifts the demand curve. In IB diagrams, an indirect tax is typically shown as a leftward shift of the supply curve, because the tax is collected by firms and raises their costs of supplying each unit. The distinction between who legally pays the tax (the firm) and who bears the economic burden (which depends on elasticities) is covered in later lessons. For now, remember: indirect taxes shift supply left; subsidies shift supply right.

### N — Number of Firms

Market supply is the sum of all individual firm supplies. When new firms enter the market, market supply increases — the curve shifts right. When firms leave the market — through bankruptcy, merger, or voluntary exit — market supply decreases — the curve shifts left.

Entry and exit depend on the profitability of the industry. If existing firms are earning large profits, new firms have an incentive to enter, attracted by the prospect of capturing some of those profits. If firms are incurring losses, some will exit, reducing market supply. The ease of entry also matters. Industries with high barriers to entry — large capital requirements, legal restrictions, patents, control over essential resources — will see less entry even when profits are high.

### T — Technology

Technological improvement allows firms to produce more output from the same inputs, or the same output with fewer inputs. Either way, per-unit costs fall, and supply increases — the curve shifts right.

Examples of technological improvement include: automation that replaces manual labour (robotic assembly lines reducing the labour cost per car), better software that optimises logistics and inventory management, and new production techniques such as precision agriculture that uses GPS and sensors to apply exactly the right amount of water and fertiliser to each square metre of a field.

Technological regression is rare but possible — the destruction of infrastructure in war, the loss of specialised knowledge when an industry collapses, or the deterioration of equipment without replacement. It shifts supply left.

### S — Supply Shocks and Input Costs

This is a broad category covering anything that changes the cost of inputs or disrupts the production process.

**Input costs** include the prices of raw materials, energy, wages, rent, and any other resource the firm purchases. When input prices rise, production costs rise, and supply decreases — the curve shifts left. When the global price of crude oil rises, the cost of producing petrol, plastics, fertiliser, and anything transported by truck rises, shifting supply curves left across multiple industries.

**Supply shocks** are sudden, unexpected events that affect production. A negative supply shock — a drought destroying crops, a pandemic disrupting supply chains, a war cutting off energy supplies — shifts supply left. A positive supply shock — an unexpectedly good harvest, the discovery of a large and cheap energy source — shifts supply right.

## 7. Joint Supply and Competitive Supply

### Two Relationships in Production

Just as demand-side analysis distinguishes substitutes and complements, supply-side analysis distinguishes goods that are linked in production.

**Joint supply** occurs when the production of one good automatically yields another good as a by-product. The two goods are produced together. You cannot produce more of one without also producing more of the other. Examples include: beef and leather (slaughtering cattle yields both the meat and the hide), crude oil and natural gas (often extracted from the same well), and wheat and straw (the grain and the stalk). When the demand for beef rises, cattle ranchers increase production, and the supply of leather automatically increases — the supply curve for leather shifts right, even though nothing about the leather market itself has changed.

**Competitive supply** occurs when two goods compete for the same resources in production. Producing more of one good requires diverting resources away from the other, reducing its output. Examples include: wheat and corn (competing for the same farmland), a factory that can produce either chairs or tables on the same assembly line, and a dairy farmer who can use milk to produce cheese or butter. When the price of wheat rises, farmers allocate more land to wheat and less to corn. The supply of corn decreases — the supply curve for corn shifts left.

A common mistake students make is to confuse competitive supply with substitutes in consumption. Substitutes are about how consumers use goods (tea or coffee). Competitive supply is about how producers make goods (wheat or corn on the same land). The two classifications are logically independent. Two goods can be substitutes in consumption but produced with entirely different resources, so no competitive supply relationship exists. And two goods can be in competitive supply but not be substitutes in consumption — a farmer choosing between growing wheat and raising dairy cows on the same land faces competitive supply, but wheat and milk are not substitutes for consumers.

## IB Exam Tip

When analysing supply-side changes in an IB exam, follow this sequence:

1. **Identify whether the good's own price changed.** If yes, it is a movement along the supply curve. Use "expansion" for price rises and "contraction" for price falls.

2. **If the own price did not change, identify which PINTS determinant is at work.** Use the precise terminology: "an improvement in technology (T in PINTS)," "an increase in input costs (S in PINTS)," "the imposition of an indirect tax (I in PINTS)."

3. **Explain the mechanism.** How exactly does the determinant affect costs or the ability to produce? Connect the determinant to the behaviour of firms.

4. **State the direction of the shift.** "Increase in supply" means right. "Decrease in supply" means left.

5. **Draw the diagram.** Label axes with names and units. Show initial and new positions. Use arrows.

A second common pitfall: when analysing the effect of a tax or subsidy, students sometimes shift the demand curve instead of the supply curve. In the IB framework, an indirect tax or a subsidy to producers shifts supply. A direct tax on income or a subsidy to consumers shifts demand. Keep this distinction clear.

## Worked Example 1: Identifying Movement vs. Shift on the Supply Side

A firm produces wooden chairs. Its weekly supply schedule is:

| Price per chair (£) | Quantity supplied (chairs per week) |
|---|---|
| 40 | 15 |
| 60 | 30 |
| 80 | 55 |
| 100 | 90 |
| 120 | 140 |

**Scenario A:** The market price of chairs rises from £80 to £100. What happens to the quantity of chairs supplied? Is this a movement or a shift?

**Scenario B:** The price of timber, the main raw material for chairs, doubles. At every chair price, the firm now supplies 40 fewer chairs per week. What has happened to supply? Is this a movement or a shift?

**Approach:** As with demand, the test is whether the variable that changed is on one of the axes. The good's own price is on the vertical axis — changes in it cause movement. Input prices are not on the axes — changes in them cause shifts.

**Step 1, Scenario A:** The own price of chairs rises from £80 to £100. This is a movement along the existing supply curve — specifically, an expansion of supply. At the original price of £80, quantity supplied was 55 chairs. At the new price of £100, quantity supplied is 90 chairs. The supply curve itself has not moved. The firm is simply responding to the higher price by producing more, as the law of supply predicts.

**Step 2, Scenario B:** The own price of chairs has not changed. What has changed is the cost of an input — timber. This is the S in PINTS (Supply shocks and input costs). The doubling of timber prices raises the firm's production costs at every level of output. For any given chair price, producing chairs is now less profitable than before. The firm responds by reducing the quantity it is willing to supply at each price. This is a shift of the supply curve to the left — a decrease in supply. At a chair price of £80, the firm now supplies 55 − 40 = 15 chairs instead of the original 55. At every other price, the same 40-chair reduction applies.

**Why this makes sense:** The key insight is that the firm's behaviour changes for different reasons in the two scenarios. In Scenario A, nothing about the firm's costs or production capabilities has changed. The higher price simply makes it profitable to expand along the existing cost structure. In Scenario B, the firm's costs are now higher, so its willingness to supply at any given price has deteriorated. The entire relationship between price and quantity supplied has shifted.

## Worked Example 2: Analysing a Technology Improvement

A farm produces wheat. Its annual supply schedule is:

| Price per tonne (£) | Quantity supplied (thousands of tonnes) |
|---|---|
| 160 | 20 |
| 200 | 35 |
| 240 | 55 |
| 280 | 80 |
| 320 | 110 |

The farm adopts a new precision-agriculture system that uses GPS-guided tractors and soil sensors. As a result, the farm can produce 30% more wheat from the same inputs. Construct the new supply schedule and explain what has happened to supply.

**Approach:** A 30% productivity improvement means that at each price, the farm can now supply 30% more wheat. Multiply each original quantity by 1.30 and verify that the supply curve has shifted right.

**Step 1:** Calculate the new quantities.
- At P = £160: 20 × 1.30 = 26 thousand tonnes.
- At P = £200: 35 × 1.30 = 45.5 thousand tonnes.
- At P = £240: 55 × 1.30 = 71.5 thousand tonnes.
- At P = £280: 80 × 1.30 = 104 thousand tonnes.
- At P = £320: 110 × 1.30 = 143 thousand tonnes.

**Step 2:** Interpret the change. At every single price, the quantity supplied is now higher. This is a rightward shift of the supply curve — an increase in supply. The cause is an improvement in technology (T in PINTS). The GPS and sensor system allows the farm to apply water, fertiliser, and pesticide more precisely, reducing waste and increasing yield per hectare.

**Step 3:** Check profitability. Although the price of wheat has not changed, the farm's costs per tonne have fallen because it gets more output from the same spending on inputs. At any given market price, the profit margin per tonne is now wider, and the farm is willing to expand production.

**Why this makes sense:** The new technology has changed the underlying production function — the relationship between inputs and outputs. The farm can now do more with the same resources. This is exactly the kind of change that shifts the supply curve rather than causing movement along it. The price of wheat has not changed, but the farm supplies more at every price because the cost of producing each tonne has fallen.

## Evaluate

**Question:** "The law of supply is more reliable than the law of demand because producers are more rational than consumers." Evaluate this statement.

**Model Answer:**

The statement is problematic on several levels. It asserts that producers are more rational than consumers — a claim with little empirical support — and it draws from this a conclusion about the relative reliability of two economic laws that is not sustained by careful analysis.

The premise that producers are systematically more rational than consumers is unsubstantiated. Both producers and consumers generally act in their perceived self-interest, given the constraints and information available to them. Consumers may sometimes make impulsive purchases or fall prey to behavioural biases — present bias, loss aversion, overoptimism. But firms are run by human beings who exhibit the same biases. Corporate history is replete with examples of overconfident investment decisions, herd behaviour (the dot-com bubble, the 2008 housing bubble), and the sunk-cost fallacy (continuing a failing project because of money already spent). There is no compelling evidence that the people running firms are more rational than the people buying products.

The reliability of the two laws depends on the mechanisms that produce them, not on the rationality of the agents involved. The law of demand is supported by three mechanisms — the substitution effect, the income effect, and diminishing marginal utility — that are individually strong and mutually reinforcing. The law of supply is supported by the profit motive and increasing marginal costs. Both sets of mechanisms are robust. Both laws are well-supported by empirical evidence across thousands of markets and goods. Neither is "more reliable" in a way that matters for practical economic analysis.

There are conditions where each law can be disrupted. The law of demand can be violated by Veblen goods (where high price signals status) and Giffen goods (where a perverse income effect dominates). The law of supply can be disrupted in the very short run, when production capacity is fixed and a price rise cannot immediately increase output — the supply curve becomes vertical over some range. In the long run, the law of supply may be complicated by increasing returns to scale, where marginal costs fall rather than rise with output. Neither law is inviolable. Both describe central tendencies with well-understood exceptions.

The statement also confuses the purpose of the two laws. They address different sides of the market. The law of demand explains consumer behaviour. The law of supply explains producer behaviour. Ranking them for "reliability" is like asking whether the law of gravity is more reliable than the laws of thermodynamics — they apply to different domains and answer different questions. For a firm setting a price, the law of demand is more directly relevant. For a government considering a production subsidy, the law of supply is more directly relevant. The question of which is "more reliable" is ill-posed.

In conclusion, the statement fails on its empirical premise (producers are not systematically more rational), on its implied comparison (both laws are robust central tendencies with different domains), and on its framing (reliability is not the right metric for comparing laws that serve different analytical purposes). A more productive approach is to understand the conditions under which each law operates reliably and the circumstances — narrow but real — in which each might break down.
