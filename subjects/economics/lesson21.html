Lesson 21: Government Responses to Market Failure — Full Evaluation

What You Will Learn

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to: - Compare every major policy instrument for correcting market failure: taxes, subsidies, tradable permits, regulation, direct provision, property rights, information campaigns, and nudges - Apply six evaluation criteria — effectiveness, efficiency, equity, administrative cost, political feasibility, and unintended consequences — to any government policy - Explain how tradable permits (cap and trade) achieve pollution reduction at minimum total cost - Understand the Coase theorem and the role of property rights in solving externality problems - Define government failure and explain its causes - Construct a balanced evaluation that weighs market failure against government failure

1. The Policy Toolkit: What Governments Can Do

When a market fails — producing too much pollution, too little education, or none of a public good — the government has a range of instruments to intervene. This lesson brings together every tool introduced in earlier lessons on externalities and public goods and provides a framework for comparing them. Each tool has a logic, a mechanism, and a set of trade-offs. No tool is best in every situation. The skill the IB tests is your ability to select the appropriate tool for a given market failure, justify your choice, and acknowledge its limitations.

2. The Instruments

2.1 Pigouvian Taxes

A Pigouvian tax — named after the economist Arthur Pigou — is a tax per unit of output equal to the marginal external cost at the socially optimal quantity. The tax forces the producer to face the full social cost of their production, not just their private cost. Suppose a steel mill emits sulphur dioxide, which causes acid rain that damages forests 300 kilometres away. The mill's marginal private cost of producing a tonne of steel is \(400. But every tonne of steel also causes \)50 of environmental damage — the cost of dying trees, acidified lakes, and corroded buildings. The marginal social cost is $450. If the government imposes a tax of \(50 per tonne of steel, the mill's MPC rises to \)450 — exactly equal to MSC. The mill now faces the true cost of its production. It reduces output from Q_fm to Q_opt, and the welfare loss from overproduction is eliminated. The tax has two effects working in the same direction: it raises the price, which reduces the quantity demanded, and it gives the mill an incentive to find cleaner production methods that reduce the tax bill. If the mill can install scrubbers that eliminate the sulphur dioxide at a cost of \(30 per tonne, it will do so — because avoiding the \)50 tax saves more than the $30 cost of the scrubber. Pigouvian taxes work best when the external cost is measurable and constant per unit. Carbon taxes — such as Sweden's $137 per tonne of CO₂ — are the most prominent real-world example. The main disadvantage of a Pigouvian tax is that the government must know the marginal external cost to set the tax correctly. If the government sets the tax at \(30 when the true external cost is \)50, production falls but not far enough — the market still overproduces relative to Q_opt. If the government sets the tax at $80, production falls too far, and society loses the benefit of steel that would have been worth producing. A second disadvantage is political. Taxes are unpopular. Australia introduced a carbon tax in 2012 and repealed it in 2014 after a change of government. France's attempt to raise fuel taxes in 2018 triggered the "gilets jaunes" (yellow vests) protests that paralysed the country for months. A policy that is economically efficient may be politically impossible.

2.2 Tradable Permits (Cap and Trade)

A tradable permit system — also called cap and trade — is an alternative to a tax for controlling pollution. The government sets a cap on the total quantity of pollution allowed — say, 100 million tonnes of CO₂ per year — and issues permits equal to that cap. Each permit allows the holder to emit one tonne. Firms can buy and sell permits among themselves. The mechanism works because firms have different costs of reducing pollution. A factory that can switch to clean energy for \(10 per tonne of avoided emissions will do so and sell its surplus permits. A factory that would have to spend \)80 per tonne to reduce emissions will buy permits instead. The permits flow from firms with low abatement costs to firms with high abatement costs. The resulting permit price — established in the market for permits — is the price at which the quantity of permits demanded equals the fixed supply (the cap). The key advantage over a tax is certainty about the quantity of pollution. With a tax, the government sets a price and hopes the quantity of emissions falls to the target level — but it may not, if firms are less responsive to the tax than expected. With a cap, the quantity is fixed by the cap itself. The government knows exactly how much total pollution will occur. A second advantage is that tradable permits achieve the target reduction at minimum total cost. The firms that can cut emissions most cheaply do most of the cutting. A uniform regulation — requiring every firm to cut emissions by 20% — would force some firms to make very expensive reductions while others could have cut more cheaply. Permit trading eliminates that inefficiency. The main disadvantage is that permit prices can be volatile. In Phase 1 of the European Union Emissions Trading System (EU ETS), from 2005 to 2007, permits were over-allocated — the government handed out more permits than total emissions. The permit price crashed to near zero, and the system provided no incentive to reduce emissions. A cap is only as good as its enforcement and its calibration. A second disadvantage is administrative complexity. A functioning permit market requires accurate monitoring of every firm's emissions, a registry of permit holdings, a trading platform, and penalties for firms that emit without permits. Setting up this infrastructure is more demanding than adding a line to the tax code. The EU ETS is the world's largest cap-and-trade system, covering about 40% of EU greenhouse gas emissions. The US Acid Rain Program, which began in 1995, used cap and trade to cut sulphur dioxide emissions from power plants by 50% at roughly one-quarter of the predicted cost — a widely cited success. A common mistake students make is confusing a tradable permit with a subsidy. A tradable permit is a right to emit; it raises the cost of polluting. A subsidy for clean energy lowers the cost of alternatives. Both can reduce emissions, but they work through opposite mechanisms. A tradable permit makes pollution more expensive; a subsidy makes clean production cheaper.

2.3 Subsidies

A subsidy is a payment from the government to producers or consumers per unit of a good, designed to increase the quantity produced or consumed toward Q_opt when positive externalities cause underprovision. The logic is the mirror image of a Pigouvian tax. For a positive externality, the private benefit or private cost does not reflect the full social value of the good. A subsidy bridges the gap. The government pays $60 for every vaccination, the effective price to consumers falls, and consumption rises from 30 million to 50 million vaccinations per year. Subsidies work through the market rather than replacing it. Consumers still choose how much to consume. The government sets the subsidy and lets the price mechanism do the rest. Subsidies have three main drawbacks. First, they cost money. If the government subsidises 50 million vaccinations at \(60 each, the annual cost is \)3 billion. That money must come from taxation, which has its own efficiency costs. Second, the government has to estimate the external benefit, and getting it wrong means either undercorrecting or overcorrecting. Third, subsidies can be captured by producers. If the government offers universities £5,000 per student, universities may raise their fees by £5,000, absorbing the subsidy without increasing the number of places. The economic incidence of a subsidy — who actually benefits — is not necessarily the legal recipient.

2.4 Direct Provision

Direct provision means the government produces the good or service itself, making it available free at the point of use, funded through general taxation. State education is the most important example. The government builds schools, hires teachers, designs a curriculum, and provides education to every child at no direct charge to the family. Direct provision is the appropriate response when the market failure is complete — the good is a public good that private markets cannot provide at all — or when equity concerns are paramount. National defence cannot be provided any other way. Basic education could be provided through vouchers and private schools, but most societies have concluded that a universal public system is more equitable. The disadvantages of direct provision mirror the disadvantages of government monopoly in any sector. Without competition, there may be weak pressure to improve quality or reduce costs. The government must guess the optimal quantity — how many school places, how many hospital beds — without the guidance of market prices. And the "one-size-fits-all" nature of public provision may not serve families with diverse preferences or needs.

2.5 Regulation (Command and Control)

Regulation — also called command and control — means the government passes a law requiring or prohibiting certain behaviour, backed by penalties for non-compliance. Regulation takes several forms. A technology standard requires firms to use a specific technology: every car must have a catalytic converter. A performance standard sets a maximum emission level: no power plant may emit more than 0.5 kilograms of sulphur dioxide per megawatt-hour. A ban prohibits a product or activity entirely: leaded petrol was banned in the UK in 2000; chlorofluorocarbons (CFCs), which destroy the ozone layer, were banned globally by the Montreal Protocol of 1987. Regulation has the advantage of certainty. When a substance is so toxic that the socially optimal quantity is zero, a ban is the only appropriate response. A tradable permit system for asbestos fibres, allowing firms to buy and sell the right to release a known carcinogen into the air, would be absurd. Some things should simply be prohibited. Regulation is also straightforward to communicate and enforce relative to market-based instruments. "You may not dump this chemical into the river" is easier for firms to understand and for inspectors to verify than "you must pay a tax of $X per kilogram, which has been calibrated to reflect the estimated marginal external cost." The disadvantage of regulation is that it is typically more expensive than market-based instruments for achieving a given environmental outcome. If the government requires every factory to cut emissions by 30%, it ignores the fact that some factories can cut emissions cheaply (by switching fuels) while others can only cut emissions at great expense (by replacing entire production lines). The same total reduction could be achieved at lower cost by letting the cheap reducers cut more and the expensive reducers cut less — which is exactly what a tax or a tradable permit system does. A second disadvantage is that regulation provides no ongoing incentive for improvement. Once a factory meets the emission standard, it has no reason to reduce emissions further. A tax, by contrast, rewards every additional tonne of reduction.

2.6 Information Campaigns and Nudges

An information campaign is a government effort to correct consumers' inaccurate beliefs about a product's costs or benefits. Anti-smoking advertisements, nutritional labelling on food, and public health campaigns during flu season are all information campaigns. A nudge, a term popularised by the behavioural economists Richard Thaler and Cass Sunstein, is a small change in the "choice architecture" that steers people toward better decisions without restricting their options. Making organ donation opt-out rather than opt-in — so that people are donors by default but can choose not to be — is a nudge. Placing fruit at eye level in a school cafeteria and desserts on a lower shelf is a nudge. Information campaigns and nudges are attractive because they are cheap. An advertising campaign costs a few million pounds; a subsidy programme costs billions. They also preserve freedom of choice — unlike a ban or a mandate, a nudge does not force anyone to do anything. The limitation is that information and nudges alone are rarely sufficient to correct a large market failure. A smoker who has seen a thousand anti-smoking advertisements and knows the health risks in detail may still be unable to quit because nicotine is addictive. Information corrects ignorance; it does not correct addiction, impulsiveness, or the inability to afford healthier alternatives. Nudges and information campaigns work best as complements to stronger policies — reinforcing the logic of a tax, or making a subsidy programme more salient to the people it is intended to help.

2.7 Property Rights and the Coase Theorem

The Coase theorem, proposed by Ronald Coase in 1960, states that if property rights are clearly defined and transaction costs are low, private parties can negotiate a solution to an externality problem without government intervention. The outcome will be efficient regardless of who initially holds the property rights. Consider a factory that pollutes a river, harming a downstream fishery that earns \(200,000 per year from catching and selling trout. The factory could install pollution control equipment at a cost of \)150,000 per year. If the fishery has the legal right to clean water, the factory must compensate the fishery for any pollution. The factory will compare the cost of polluting (compensating the fishery for lost earnings) with the cost of installing the control equipment. Since \(150,000 is less than \)200,000, the factory will install the equipment. If instead the factory has the right to pollute, the fishery can offer to pay the factory to install the equipment. The fishery will pay up to \(200,000 (the value of clean water to the fishery), and the factory will accept any payment above \)150,000 (the cost of the equipment). Since \(200,000 exceeds \)150,000, a deal is possible — for example, the fishery pays the factory $175,000 to install the equipment. In both cases, the outcome is the same: the equipment is installed, pollution stops, and the fishery survives. The only difference is who pays whom. The initial assignment of property rights affects the distribution of wealth but not the efficiency of the outcome. The Coase theorem is elegant, but its practical application is limited by two words in its statement: "transaction costs are low." In the real world, transaction costs are often high. The downstream fishery might involve 50 separate fishing businesses. The factory might pollute a river that flows past 200 farms, 3 towns, and a nature reserve. Coordinating negotiations among hundreds of affected parties, measuring each party's damages, and preventing individual parties from holding out for a larger share — all of these are costly. When transaction costs are high, private bargaining will not produce an efficient outcome, and government intervention — a tax, a regulation, a tradable permit — becomes necessary. The Coase theorem is best understood not as a policy prescription but as a benchmark. It tells us what would happen in an ideal world of zero transaction costs. The size of the gap between that ideal and reality determines how much intervention is needed.

3. The Six Criteria for Evaluating Any Government Intervention

When the IB asks you to "evaluate" a policy, it expects you to go beyond describing how the policy works. You must assess its strengths and weaknesses systematically. These six criteria provide a structure for that assessment.

3.1 Effectiveness

Does the policy achieve its stated goal? If the goal is to reduce carbon emissions by 40% by 2030, does the policy put the country on track to meet that target? Effectiveness is not binary. A carbon tax that reduces emissions by 35% when the target was 40% is partly effective. A regulation that is widely evaded — firms dump waste at night to avoid inspectors — is largely ineffective. Effectiveness depends on the design of the policy (is the tax high enough? is the cap tight enough?), the capacity to enforce it, and the responsiveness of the targeted behaviour to the policy instrument.

3.2 Efficiency

Efficiency has two dimensions. Allocative efficiency asks whether the policy moves the market to Q_opt — the quantity where marginal social benefit equals marginal social cost. A subsidy that is too small leaves the market short of Q_opt; a tax that is too large overshoots it. Productive efficiency asks whether the policy achieves its goal at minimum cost. Tradable permits are generally more productively efficient than uniform regulation, because they allow firms with low abatement costs to do more of the cutting. Direct government provision is often less productively efficient than private provision, because government agencies face weaker cost-minimisation incentives. A policy can be effective but inefficient — a ban on all cars would certainly reduce urban pollution, but the cost in lost mobility and economic activity would be enormous relative to the environmental gain.

3.3 Equity

Who gains and who loses from the policy? Is the distribution of those gains and losses fair? A carbon tax on heating fuel is regressive: low-income households spend a larger fraction of their income on heating than high-income households, so the tax takes a larger percentage of a poor household's budget. A subsidy for electric vehicles is regressive in a different way: it goes disproportionately to wealthy households who can afford to buy a new car in the first place. Policymakers do not ignore equity. A carbon tax can be paired with a "carbon dividend" — a lump-sum rebate to every household, funded by the tax revenue — that makes the overall package progressive. The British Columbia carbon tax, introduced in 2008, returns revenue to households through reductions in other taxes, making it approximately distribution-neutral.

3.4 Administrative Cost

How expensive is the policy to implement, monitor, and enforce? A simple per-unit tax that piggybacks on an existing tax collection system — adding a carbon charge to the existing fuel duty, for example — has low administrative costs. A tradable permit system that requires emissions monitoring at thousands of industrial facilities, a permit registry, a trading platform, and an enforcement agency has much higher administrative costs. Nudges and information campaigns are cheap to implement. Direct provision — building and staffing schools, hospitals, and military bases — is expensive not just in the direct expenditure but in the ongoing administrative cost of managing large public-sector organisations.

3.5 Political Feasibility

Can the policy actually be enacted and sustained through the political process? A policy that is economically optimal — a high, uniform carbon tax — may be politically impossible if voters perceive it as a new tax that raises their cost of living. The policy that is actually implemented may be second-best economically but first-best politically: a renewable energy mandate that achieves some emissions reduction without the word "tax" appearing anywhere. Political feasibility is not a trivial consideration. A perfect policy that cannot pass into law is worth less than a good policy that can.

3.6 Unintended Consequences

What effects might the policy have beyond its stated goal? Do those effects undermine or reinforce the policy's purpose? The US ethanol mandate required petrol to contain a minimum percentage of ethanol, produced from corn. The goal was to reduce carbon emissions and dependence on foreign oil. The unintended consequences included: higher corn prices, which raised food costs globally; conversion of grassland and forest to corn cultivation, which released stored carbon and may have increased net emissions; and increased fertiliser runoff into rivers, contributing to a dead zone in the Gulf of Mexico. A well-intentioned environmental policy may have made the environment worse. Every policy carries the risk of unintended consequences. The evaluator's job is to identify the most likely ones and assess whether they are severe enough to undermine the policy's case.

4. Government Failure

What Government Failure Is

Government failure occurs when a government intervention intended to correct a market failure instead makes the situation worse — either by failing to correct the original problem or by creating new problems whose costs exceed the benefits of correction. The concept is essential because it prevents the lazy assumption that every market failure justifies government action. The relevant question is not "does the market fail?" but "is government intervention likely to produce a better outcome than the imperfect market?"

Causes of Government Failure

Imperfect information. To set a Pigouvian tax, the government must know the marginal external cost. To set a subsidy, it must know the marginal external benefit. In reality, these numbers are estimates, and the estimates can be wrong. A tax set too low leaves the externality partly uncorrected. A tax set too high creates a new inefficiency. Political incentives. Politicians are accountable to voters, not to an abstract social welfare function. They may favour policies with visible, short-term benefits (a new hospital building with a plaque bearing their name) over policies with diffuse, long-term benefits (preventive healthcare that avoids illness years in the future). They may be captured by well-organised interest groups. The sugar industry lobbying against a sugar tax, the oil industry lobbying against carbon pricing, the defence industry lobbying for larger military budgets — all are examples of regulatory capture, where the regulated industry influences the regulator to serve the industry's interests rather than the public interest. Bureaucratic inefficiency. Government agencies are rarely subject to the competitive pressure that drives private firms to minimise costs. A government department that administers an education subsidy may have little incentive to streamline its application process, because its budget is determined by a political negotiation rather than by customers who can take their business elsewhere. The law of unintended consequences. As discussed in section 3.6, policies can produce effects that their designers did not foresee and that work against the policy's purpose. Moral hazard. If the government insures against a risk, people may take more of that risk. Government flood insurance, intended to protect households, may encourage building on flood plains — increasing the total damage when floods occur.

How to Use Government Failure in an IB Answer

The strongest evaluation answers acknowledge that both market failure and government failure are real possibilities. The conclusion should compare them: "For this specific problem, under these specific conditions, the risk of government failure is small relative to the severity of the market failure, so intervention is justified" — or the reverse. Blanket statements ("government always makes things worse" or "the market always gets it wrong") score poorly.

5. Worked Example: Choosing Between a Tax and a Permit System

A country has two power plants. Plant A can reduce its carbon emissions at a cost of \(20 per tonne. Plant B can reduce its carbon emissions at a cost of \)60 per tonne. Both currently emit 100 tonnes per year. The government wants to reduce total emissions from 200 tonnes to 100 tonnes. Problem: Compare the total cost of achieving this reduction under (a) a uniform regulation requiring each plant to cut emissions by 50 tonnes, and (b) a tradable permit system with 100 permits (each allowing 1 tonne of emissions), allocated equally (50 permits to each plant). Assume zero transaction costs. Approach: Under uniform regulation, each plant cuts 50 tonnes, regardless of cost. Under tradable permits, the plants trade permits until the marginal cost of abatement is equalised — Plant A cuts more because it is cheaper, and Plant B buys permits from Plant A. Step 1: Cost under uniform regulation. Plant A must cut 50 tonnes at \(20/tonne: 50 × \)20 = \(1,000. Plant B must cut 50 tonnes at \)60/tonne: 50 × \(60 = \)3,000. Total cost = $4,000. Step 2: Cost under tradable permits. Each plant receives 50 permits initially and currently emits 100 tonnes, so each needs to reduce emissions by 50 tonnes — unless they trade. Plant A's cost of abatement is \(20/tonne. It can cut more cheaply than Plant B. Plant B's cost of abatement is \)60/tonne. It would rather buy permits. The two plants will trade until neither can gain from further trade. The permit price will settle between \(20 and \)60. Let us suppose it settles at $40 per permit. At a permit price of \(40: Plant A compares cutting one more tonne (cost \)20) with buying a permit (cost \(40). Cutting is cheaper, so Plant A reduces its emissions by more than 50 tonnes. Plant A cuts from 100 tonnes to zero — a reduction of 100 tonnes. Plant A now has 50 unused permits (it received 50 and emits zero). It sells all 50 to Plant B at \)40 each. Revenue = 50 × \(40 = \)2,000. Plant B compares cutting one more tonne (cost \(60) with buying a permit (cost \)40). Buying is cheaper, so Plant B buys permits instead of cutting. Plant B buys 50 permits from Plant A and continues to emit 100 tonnes. Cost = 50 × \(40 = \)2,000. Total abatement cost: Plant A spends 100 × \(20 = \)2,000 to cut 100 tonnes. Plant B spends \(0 on abatement (it buys permits instead). Total abatement cost = \)2,000. Total cost under tradable permits = $2,000. Total cost under uniform regulation = $4,000. Saving from permit trading = $2,000 — a 50% reduction in the total cost of achieving the same environmental outcome. Why this makes sense: The tradable permit system lets the low-cost reducer (Plant A) do most of the cutting, while the high-cost reducer (Plant B) pays for permits instead. The environmental outcome — total emissions fall from 200 to 100 tonnes — is identical to the regulation. But the cost of achieving it is halved, because the cheapest reductions are made first. This is the central economic argument for market-based instruments over command-and-control regulation.

IB Exam Tip

For Paper 1 evaluation questions, the IB expects you to compare at least two policies. The best answers use the six criteria explicitly: "A carbon tax scores well on efficiency because it equalises marginal abatement costs across firms, but it scores poorly on political feasibility because voters resist new taxes. A renewable energy subsidy scores well on political feasibility because it is perceived as a reward rather than a punishment, but it scores poorly on efficiency because the government must choose which technologies to subsidise and may pick the wrong ones." Never present a policy as perfect. Every policy has trade-offs. The top band of the mark scheme rewards students who "make a reasoned judgment" — which means weighing the criteria against each other and concluding which policy is best for the specific context, not in the abstract. The Coase theorem and government failure are both powerful evaluation tools. Coase lets you say: "In principle, the parties could negotiate a solution, but transaction costs in this case are high because..." Government failure lets you say: "The government may intend to correct the externality, but imperfect information about the external cost may lead to..."

Practice Problems

Problem 1. A factory emits pollution that causes $40 of external damage per unit of output. The factory's MPC is given by MPC = 20 + 2Q, and the market demand (MPB = MSB) is given by P = 200 − Q, where Q is in thousands of units. (a) Calculate the free-market equilibrium quantity and price. (b) Calculate the socially optimal quantity. (c) Calculate the Pigouvian tax per unit needed to achieve Q_opt. (d) Calculate the total tax revenue the government would collect at Q_opt. Problem 2. A government is choosing between a carbon tax and a tradable permit system to reduce CO₂ emissions. For each instrument, explain one significant advantage and one significant disadvantage. In your judgment, which instrument is preferable when the government has a specific emissions target that must be met and why? Problem 3. A railway line runs past 30 houses. The trains create noise that reduces each household's well-being by an estimated £500 per year. The railway company could install noise barriers along the track at a cost of £10,000 per year. Assume transaction costs are zero. (a) If the households have the right to quiet enjoyment of their property, what outcome does the Coase theorem predict? (b) If the railway company has the right to make noise, what outcome does the Coase theorem predict? (c) If there are 300 affected households rather than 30, explain why the Coase theorem is less likely to apply. Problem 4. Evaluate the following policy using at least four of the six evaluation criteria: A city introduces a congestion charge of £15 per day for vehicles entering the city centre between 7 AM and 7 PM on weekdays. The charge is enforced by automatic number plate recognition cameras. Revenue from the charge is used to fund improvements to public transport. Problem 5. *(IB-style, 4 marks)* A government is concerned about the overconsumption of soft drinks containing high levels of sugar. (a) Explain how a tax on sugary drinks could correct this market failure. (2 marks) (b) Explain one reason why an information campaign might be less effective than a tax in reducing consumption of sugary drinks. (2 marks) Problem 6. "When a market fails, the government should always intervene." Critically evaluate this statement. In your answer, refer to at least two different types of market failure and consider the concept of government failure. Use real-world examples where possible.

Answers

Answer 1. (a) Free-market equilibrium: set MPB = MPC. 200 − Q = 20 + 2Q 200 − 20 = Q + 2Q 180 = 3Q Q_fm = 60 thousand units Price: P = 200 − 60 = $140 per unit. (b) The marginal external cost is $40 per unit. MSC = MPC + MEC. MSC = (20 + 2Q) + 40 = 60 + 2Q. Socially optimal quantity: set MSB = MSC. 200 − Q = 60 + 2Q 200 − 60 = Q + 2Q 140 = 3Q Q_opt = 46.67 thousand units. (c) The Pigouvian tax should equal the marginal external cost at Q_opt. Since the MEC is a constant \(40 per unit, the tax is \)40 per unit regardless of the quantity. At Q_opt = 46.67, MEC is still \(40. The tax per unit is \)40. (d) Total tax revenue at Q_opt = \(40 × 46,670 units = \)1,866,800. A common error is to calculate the tax as the difference between MSC and MPC at Q_fm rather than at Q_opt. The correct Pigouvian tax equals the MEC at the optimal quantity, not at the free-market quantity. In this problem, the MEC is constant, so the distinction does not matter — but if the MEC increased with output, it would matter a great deal. Answer 2. A carbon tax has the advantage of price certainty. Firms know exactly what each tonne of emissions will cost, which allows them to plan investments in clean technology with confidence. The tax also generates government revenue, which can be used to reduce other distortionary taxes (the "double dividend" — correcting an externality while reducing the deadweight loss of income taxation). The disadvantage of a carbon tax is quantity uncertainty. The government does not know in advance how much emissions will fall. If firms are less responsive to the tax than expected, the emissions reduction may fall short of the target. A tradable permit system has the advantage of quantity certainty. The cap fixes the total level of emissions. If the government needs to meet a specific target — for example, a 40% reduction in CO₂ by 2030, as required by an international treaty — a cap guarantees that the target is met (assuming enforcement). The disadvantage is price volatility. Permit prices can fluctuate, creating uncertainty for firms that need to plan investments over decades. When the government has a specific emissions target that must be met — perhaps to comply with an international agreement with binding targets and penalties for non-compliance — a tradable permit system is preferable because it guarantees the quantity outcome. The price uncertainty is a cost, but it is a cost the government accepts in exchange for certainty about the environmental outcome. If the consequence of missing the target is severe (international sanctions, irreversible environmental damage), quantity certainty trumps price certainty. Answer 3. (a) If the 30 households have the right to quiet, the railway company must compensate them for the noise. Total damages are 30 × £500 = £15,000 per year. The company compares this with the cost of noise barriers: £10,000 per year. Since £10,000 is less than £15,000, the company installs the barriers. The efficient outcome is achieved: the barriers are installed, and the total cost to society is £10,000 rather than the £15,000 of noise damage. (b) If the railway company has the right to make noise, the 30 households can collectively offer to pay the company to install the barriers. The households are willing to pay up to £15,000 (their total damages). The company will accept any payment above £10,000 (the cost of the barriers). Since the maximum the households will pay exceeds the minimum the company will accept, a deal is possible. For example, the households pay £12,500 — they gain £15,000 in noise reduction for £12,500, a net gain of £2,500. The company receives £12,500 for spending £10,000, a net gain of £2,500. The barriers are installed. The Coase theorem predicts the same efficient outcome regardless of the initial assignment of rights. (c) With 300 households, transaction costs become prohibitive. Organising 300 households — identifying all of them, measuring each household's individual noise damage, negotiating a collective payment, and preventing individual households from free-riding by refusing to contribute while still benefiting from the barriers — is extremely difficult. Legal fees, the cost of everyone's time, and the strategic behaviour of holdouts can easily consume the potential gains from trade. When transaction costs are high, private bargaining fails, and government intervention — a noise regulation, a tax on railway noise — becomes the appropriate response. The Coase theorem's assumption of zero transaction costs is violated, and its prediction of an efficient outcome no longer holds. Answer 4. Effectiveness: The London congestion charge, introduced in 2003, reduced traffic in the charging zone by approximately 15% and congestion by 30% in its first year. The £15 daily charge gives drivers a price signal: if the journey is worth more than £15 to them, they pay and drive; if not, they use public transport, cycle, travel at a different time, or forgo the trip. The charge has been effective at reducing congestion. Efficiency: The congestion charge is an example of a Pigouvian tax: it prices the external cost that each driver imposes on others (increased travel time). By charging for access to a congested road, the charge ensures that only drivers who value the journey at more than the social cost actually make it. This is allocatively efficient. The use of automatic number plate recognition keeps administrative costs relatively low compared to manual toll collection. The charge is more efficient than a ban on cars or an odd-even licence plate rationing system, both of which would prevent some high-value journeys. Equity: The congestion charge is regressive in the sense that a £15 daily charge is a larger fraction of a low-income driver's budget than a high-income driver's. However, the charge only applies to people who drive into the city centre — and in many cities, car commuters are disproportionately higher-income. Low-income residents are more likely to use public transport, which benefits from the revenue. The equity impact depends on the specific city and the alternatives available. Political feasibility: The London congestion charge was highly controversial before its introduction. The mayor who introduced it, Ken Livingstone, faced significant opposition from businesses and motorists. However, once implemented and shown to work, public support increased. The charge has survived multiple mayoral elections and changes of administration — a testament to its political durability once established. New congestion charges (such as New York's, introduced in 2025) face similar political headwinds. Unintended consequences: One unintended consequence in London was an increase in cycling, which was positive. Another was that some businesses on the boundary of the charging zone reported reduced custom, as shoppers shifted to locations outside the zone. A further consequence was that the charge may have contributed to rising property prices inside the zone, as the area became more pleasant to live in — a benefit to property owners but a cost to renters. Overall, the London congestion charge is widely regarded as a policy success. It achieved its primary goal (reducing congestion) at reasonable administrative cost, with manageable equity concerns and positive knock-on effects for public transport and cycling. Its political survival for over two decades suggests that the initial opposition was surmountable. Answer 5. (a) A tax on sugary drinks increases the price paid by consumers. Because the demand curve for sugary drinks slopes downward, the higher price reduces the quantity demanded. The tax works by internalising the negative consumption externality: each sugary drink consumed increases the risk of obesity, type 2 diabetes, and dental decay, which impose costs on the public healthcare system. By raising the price, the tax makes consumers face a cost closer to the full social cost of their consumption, moving the market quantity from Q_fm toward Q_opt. The UK Soft Drinks Industry Levy, introduced in 2018, led to a 35% reduction in the sugar content of soft drinks, primarily through manufacturers reformulating their products to fall below the tax threshold. (2 marks — 1 mark for explaining the price mechanism (tax raises price → quantity demanded falls), 1 mark for linking to the externality (internalising the external cost on the healthcare system).) (b) An information campaign — such as labelling sugary drinks with their sugar content or running public health advertisements — works by changing consumers' perceptions of the private costs and benefits. The logic is that consumers may not know how much sugar is in a soft drink or may not appreciate the long-term health consequences of regular consumption. However, an information campaign may be less effective than a tax for several reasons. First, many consumers already know that sugary drinks are unhealthy — the information failure may be small — and their consumption is driven by taste, habit, or addiction rather than ignorance. Second, information does not change the price. A low-income consumer who knows that a £1 sugary drink is unhealthy may still buy it because the healthier alternative costs £2. A tax changes relative prices; an information campaign does not. Third, the effects of information campaigns tend to fade over time, while a tax provides a permanent price signal. The statement is an oversimplification that ignores the possibility, frequency, and severity of government failure. A more defensible position is that market failure creates a case for considering government intervention, but intervention is only justified if the expected improvement from intervention exceeds the expected costs — including the risk that the government makes the problem worse. Consider the market failure of pollution externalities. A steel mill that dumps sulphur dioxide into the atmosphere imposes costs on downwind communities in the form of respiratory illness, acidified lakes, and damaged crops. The market, left alone, will produce too much steel and too much pollution. The case for intervention is strong: the externality is well understood, the harms are measurable, and policy instruments (taxes, tradable permits) have a track record of success. The US Acid Rain Program cut sulphur dioxide emissions by 50% at a fraction of the predicted cost. The UK Clean Air Act of 1956 ended the "killer fogs" that had caused thousands of deaths. In this case, government intervention clearly improved on the market outcome. Now consider the market failure of monopoly. A single firm controls the supply of an essential good and charges a price well above marginal cost. In theory, the government could regulate the price or break up the monopoly. In practice, government attempts to manage prices in complex industries have a mixed record. Price controls can create shortages (as with rent controls in many cities) or discourage investment (as with pharmaceutical price controls that reduce drug companies' incentive to develop new medicines). The government failure — distorted incentives, regulatory capture, bureaucratic inefficiency — may be worse than the monopoly itself, especially if the monopoly is contestable (the threat of new entrants disciplines the incumbent's pricing even without actual entry). The general point is that government intervention is a choice between two imperfect alternatives: an imperfect market and an imperfect government. The relevant question is comparative: which imperfection is smaller? The answer depends on context. For large, well-understood externalities (carbon emissions, sulphur dioxide), the balance favours intervention. For small, uncertain externalities where the costs of intervention (administrative, political, distortionary) exceed the likely gains, the balance favours leaving the market alone. A further complication is that the existence of a market failure does not tell us which intervention is appropriate. The wrong intervention can make things worse than no intervention at all. A price ceiling on rental housing, intended to help tenants, can reduce the supply of rental housing and increase homelessness. A subsidy for biofuels, intended to reduce carbon emissions, can drive up food prices and increase deforestation. The design of the intervention matters as much as the decision to intervene. In conclusion, "the government should always intervene when a market fails" is a recipe for hubris. The correct principle is: "When a market fails, the government should consider whether a well-designed intervention can improve the outcome, weighing the expected benefits against the risks of government failure and choosing the least costly instrument capable of achieving the goal." This is less satisfying as a sound bite, but it is more likely to produce good policy. A score of 7 in IB Economics requires demonstrating that you understand both the rationale for intervention and the reasons it can go wrong.

Question: "Market-based solutions — taxes and tradable permits — are always superior to command-and-control regulation for addressing environmental problems." Evaluate this statement. Model Answer: The statement captures an important economic insight: market-based instruments harness the price mechanism to achieve environmental goals at lower total cost than uniform regulation. But the word "always" overreaches, and the situations where regulation is the better choice are instructive about the limits of market logic. The economic case for market-based instruments rests on the heterogeneity of abatement costs. In any real economy, different firms face different costs of reducing pollution. A factory that burns coal can switch to natural gas relatively cheaply. A factory that requires intense heat for an industrial process — a cement kiln, a steel blast furnace — may have no cheap alternative to coal in the short run. A uniform regulation that orders every firm to cut emissions by 30% forces the cement plant into expensive reductions while leaving cheap reductions at the coal-fired power plant unexploited. A carbon tax, by contrast, makes every firm face the same price per tonne of emissions. The coal plant, facing a \(50 per tonne tax, reduces its emissions significantly — it is cheaper to switch fuels than to pay the tax. The cement plant, also facing \)50 per tonne, reduces emissions less — paying the tax is cheaper than rebuilding the kiln. The total reduction is achieved, but the reductions come disproportionately from firms that can cut cheaply. The total cost to the economy is minimised. This is the static efficiency argument for market-based instruments. There is also a dynamic efficiency argument. A firm that meets a regulatory standard has no incentive to go further. A firm facing a carbon tax always has an incentive to develop cheaper abatement technology, because every tonne of emissions it eliminates saves the tax. The tax creates a permanent incentive for innovation. These arguments are powerful, and they explain why most economists prefer carbon taxes or cap-and-trade systems to command-and-control regulation for large-scale pollution problems. But the case for regulation becomes stronger as we move from large, diffuse externality problems toward small, acute, or toxic ones. When the optimal quantity of a pollutant is zero — or very close to zero — a ban is the appropriate instrument. Asbestos fibres cause mesothelioma, a fatal cancer. There is no safe level of exposure, and there is no reason to allow firms to trade the right to release asbestos into the air. The optimal policy is a ban, enforced by criminal penalties. Market-based instruments assume that some positive quantity of pollution is acceptable because the marginal damage is finite. For some substances, that assumption is wrong. Regulation is also appropriate when the source of the externality is concentrated and easy to monitor. If a single factory is dumping toxic waste into a river, sending an inspector to the factory and ordering it to stop is simpler and cheaper than designing a tradable permit system for toxic waste — which would require defining the pollutant, measuring emissions, creating a trading platform, and policing compliance for what may be a problem caused by one actor. The administrative cost argument cuts in favour of regulation when the problem is small-scale and localised. Regulation may also be more politically feasible in some contexts. The United States has not enacted a federal carbon tax, but it has enacted fuel economy standards for vehicles (Corporate Average Fuel Economy, or CAFE, standards) that have reduced emissions by forcing automakers to improve efficiency. A regulation that hides its cost in the price of a product — consumers pay more for a car with better fuel economy technology but do not see a line item on the receipt — may face less political resistance than a visible tax, even if the regulation is more expensive per tonne of carbon avoided. A second-best policy that exists is better than a first-best policy that does not. The statement also assumes that governments have the administrative capacity to run market-based systems effectively. A developing country with weak tax administration and limited technical capacity to monitor emissions may find that a simple regulation — "no factory may emit smoke darker than Shade 2 on the Ringelmann scale" — enforced by visual inspection, is more workable than a carbon tax or a permit trading system that requires metering, reporting, and verification. The choice of instrument depends on institutional capacity as well as economic logic. In conclusion, market-based instruments have strong theoretical and empirical advantages for large-scale, well-understood pollution problems where the goal is to reduce — but not eliminate — emissions and where administrative capacity is adequate. For toxic substances requiring zero emissions, for small-scale and localised problems, and for contexts where administrative capacity is limited, regulation is the more appropriate tool. The optimal environmental policy framework uses market-based instruments as the default for broad externality problems, supplemented by regulation for toxic substances, emergency situations, and localised harms. "Always" is too strong; the question is not which instrument is best in theory, but which instrument is best for this specific problem in this specific context.
