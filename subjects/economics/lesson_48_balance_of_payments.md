# Lesson 48: The Balance of Payments

## What You'll Learn
- The definition and structure of the **Balance of Payments (BOP)** — a record of all economic transactions between a country and the rest of the world
- The four components of the **Current Account**: trade in goods, trade in services, primary income, and secondary income
- The meaning and implications of a **current account deficit vs. surplus**
- The **Financial/Capital Account**: FDI, portfolio investment, loans, and reserve assets
- The fundamental BOP identity: Current Account + Financial Account + Capital Account + Balancing Item = 0
- When a current account deficit IS a problem (structural, consumption-financing) vs. when it is NOT (productive investment, cyclical)
- Policies to correct a current account deficit: expenditure-switching, expenditure-reducing, and supply-side

---

## 1. What Is the Balance of Payments?

The **Balance of Payments (BOP)** is a systematic record of all economic transactions between the residents of a country and the rest of the world over a specific period (usually a year or quarter). It is constructed using double-entry bookkeeping — every transaction is recorded as both a credit (+) and a debit (−), so in principle, the BOP must sum to zero.

The BOP is divided into three main accounts:
1. **Current Account** — flows of goods, services, income, and transfers.
2. **Financial Account** — flows of financial assets (investment, loans, reserves).
3. **Capital Account** — capital transfers and acquisition/disposal of non-produced, non-financial assets (typically very small; often merged with the financial account in IB).

A **balancing item** (net errors and omissions) is added to ensure the accounts sum to zero, reflecting measurement errors and unrecorded transactions.

### The Fundamental Identity

<div class="box">$$\text{Current Account} + \text{Financial Account} + \text{Capital Account} + \text{Balancing Item} = 0$$</div>

This means that a current account deficit must be **financed** by a financial account surplus (net capital inflow) — the country is borrowing from or selling assets to the rest of the world.

---

## 2. The Current Account — Four Components

### 2.1 Trade in Goods (Visible Trade)

Exports and imports of physical goods — raw materials, manufactured products, agricultural produce, energy. This is often called the **balance of trade** or **visible balance**.

- **Trade surplus in goods**: Exports of goods > Imports of goods.
- **Trade deficit in goods**: Imports of goods > Exports of goods.

**Worked Example**: If Germany exports €1,200 billion of goods and imports €950 billion, the balance of trade in goods = +€250 billion (surplus).

### 2.2 Trade in Services (Invisible Trade)

Exports and imports of services — tourism, transport, financial services, insurance, consulting, education, and digital services.

- Many developed countries run a surplus in services to offset a deficit in goods (e.g., the UK runs a services surplus driven by financial and business services, partially offsetting its goods deficit).

### 2.3 Primary Income (Investment Income)

Income earned from foreign investments minus income paid to foreign investors. This includes:
- **Compensation of employees**: Wages earned by residents working abroad minus wages paid to non-residents working domestically.
- **Investment income**: Profits, dividends, and interest from foreign assets (FDI, portfolio investments) minus similar payments to foreign investors.

Countries with large net foreign asset positions (e.g., Japan) earn significant positive primary income. Countries that are net debtors (e.g., the US, though complex) pay more primary income abroad.

### 2.4 Secondary Income (Current Transfers)

Unrequited transfers — payments made without receiving a good or service in return. Includes:
- **Remittances**: Money sent by migrant workers to families in their home country (a major inflow for many developing countries — e.g., remittances to the Philippines exceed 10% of GDP).
- **Foreign aid** (current, not capital, transfers).
- Contributions to international organisations.

### Current Account Balance Formula

<div class="box">$$\text{Current Account} = (\text{Trade in Goods}) + (\text{Trade in Services}) + (\text{Primary Income}) + (\text{Secondary Income})$$</div>

### Worked Example — Calculating the Current Account

Country Zeta (all figures in $ billions):
- Exports of goods: 500
- Imports of goods: 580
- Exports of services: 120
- Imports of services: 90
- Income received from abroad: 45
- Income paid abroad: 70
- Secondary income received: 20
- Secondary income paid: 15

**Calculation**:
- Trade balance in goods = 500 − 580 = **−80**
- Trade balance in services = 120 − 90 = **+30**
- Balance of trade in goods and services = −80 + 30 = **−50**
- Primary income balance = 45 − 70 = **−25**
- Secondary income balance = 20 − 15 = **+5**
- **Current account balance** = −50 + (−25) + 5 = **−$70 billion (deficit)**

## 3. The Financial Account

The **Financial Account** records transactions in financial assets and liabilities between residents and non-residents. It is often (somewhat inaccurately) combined with the Capital Account in IB syllabi. Key components:

### 3.1 Foreign Direct Investment (FDI)

Investment involving a **lasting interest** and significant influence (typically ≥10% ownership) in an enterprise in another country. FDI includes equity capital, reinvested earnings, and intra-company loans.

- **Inward FDI**: Foreign firms building factories, buying domestic companies. This is a **credit** (capital inflow) on the financial account.
- **Outward FDI**: Domestic firms investing abroad. This is a **debit** (capital outflow).

### 3.2 Portfolio Investment

Investment in foreign stocks and bonds **without** significant control (less than 10% ownership). Portfolio flows are often more volatile than FDI — "hot money" that can reverse quickly.

- Foreigners buying domestic government bonds → capital inflow (credit).
- Domestic residents buying foreign stocks → capital outflow (debit).

### 3.3 Other Investment

Loans, currency and deposits, trade credits — financial flows not classified as FDI or portfolio investment.

### 3.4 Reserve Assets

Foreign exchange reserves held by the central bank. An **increase** in reserve assets is recorded as a **debit** (the central bank buys foreign currency — a capital outflow). A **decrease** in reserves is a credit (financing a deficit).

### The Relationship: Current Account + Financial Account = 0 (approximately)

A current account deficit means the country spends more on imports, income payments, and transfers than it earns from exports and receipts. This deficit must be financed by:
- Selling assets to foreigners (FDI or portfolio inflows) — **financial account surplus**.
- Borrowing from abroad (loans) — **financial account surplus**.
- Running down foreign reserves — **financial account surplus** (reserve assets decrease).

The current account and the financial account are mirror images: **CA deficit ≈ FA surplus** (net capital inflow).

### Worked Example — Matching the Accounts

Continuing Country Zeta from above: Current account = −$70 billion.

The financial account shows:
- FDI inflow: +85
- FDI outflow: −40
- Portfolio inflow: +35
- Portfolio outflow: −20
- Other investment (net): −5
- Reserve assets (net increase): −25

Net financial account = 85 − 40 + 35 − 20 − 5 − 25 = **+30**.

The balancing item (net errors and omissions) must be: −(−70) − 30 = **+40**. (The accounts do not perfectly balance because of measurement errors; a $40 billion balancing item is added to make the identity hold.)

*Note: In IB exams, the data will typically balance more closely or the balancing item will be given.*

## 4. Is a Current Account Deficit a Problem?

This is one of the most important evaluative questions in IB Economics. A deficit is **not inherently bad** — its significance depends on **why** it exists, **how** it is financed, and **for how long** it persists.

### When a Deficit IS a Problem

1. **Structural deficit**: The deficit reflects a fundamental lack of international competitiveness — low productivity, high costs, poor quality. Without structural reform, the deficit will persist indefinitely, requiring ever-increasing borrowing.

2. **Financing consumption**: If capital inflows are used to finance consumption (importing consumer goods) rather than productive investment, the country accumulates debt without building capacity to repay it. This is unsustainable.

3. **Financed by short-term ("hot money") flows**: Portfolio inflows can reverse suddenly — a "sudden stop" — triggering a currency crisis. East Asian financial crisis (1997) and the European periphery crisis are examples.

4. **Large relative to GDP**: A persistent deficit above 4–5% of GDP signals imbalance. IMF guidelines consider deficits above this threshold as potentially problematic.

5. **Depleting reserves**: If the deficit is financed by running down central bank reserves, the country's ability to manage future external shocks diminishes.

### When a Deficit Is NOT a Problem

1. **Financing productive investment**: If capital inflows fund investment in productive capacity (factories, technology, infrastructure), the future returns from that investment will generate the foreign exchange needed to service and repay the debt. A developing country importing capital equipment to build export industries runs a "good" deficit.

2. **Cyclical/temporary**: A deficit caused by strong domestic demand during an economic boom is part of the normal business cycle. As growth moderates, imports fall and the deficit narrows. This is self-correcting.

3. **Offset by strong income flows**: A country may run a trade deficit but earn enough from foreign investments (primary income surplus) to keep the current account balanced or in surplus. The UK often runs a trade deficit but a smaller current account deficit due to services and investment income.

4. **Counterpart to high domestic investment**: The identity \((X - M) = (S - I) + (T - G)\) shows that a current account deficit can reflect high domestic investment (I > S) rather than a competitiveness problem. If investment is productive, the deficit is benign.

### Worked Example — Good vs. Bad Deficit

**Country A**: Current account deficit of 6% of GDP for three years. FDI inflows are 7% of GDP, concentrated in export-oriented manufacturing. GDP growth: 6% per year. **Assessment**: Likely benign — FDI is building productive capacity that will generate future exports.

**Country B**: Current account deficit of 4% of GDP. Financed by short-term portfolio inflows. Economy is experiencing a consumption boom (imported consumer goods). Domestic investment is low. **Assessment**: Concerning — deficit finances consumption, not investment. Vulnerable to sudden capital flow reversal.

## 5. Policies to Correct a Current Account Deficit

Policies fall into three broad categories:

### 5.1 Expenditure-Switching Policies

These aim to redirect spending from foreign goods to domestic goods — switching **what** is spent, not how much.

- **Depreciation/Devaluation**: Makes exports cheaper and imports more expensive. Under a floating regime, depreciation occurs automatically; under a fixed regime, devaluation may be necessary. Requires the Marshall-Lerner condition to hold for the trade balance to improve.

- **Tariffs and import restrictions**: Raise the price of imports, encouraging consumers to switch to domestic substitutes. However, tariffs violate WTO rules, invite retaliation, and create deadweight loss (see Lesson 42). They treat the symptom, not the cause.

- **Export subsidies**: Lower costs for exporters, making exports more competitive. But this is WTO-actionable and costly to the budget.

### 5.2 Expenditure-Reducing Policies

These aim to reduce overall spending in the economy, which reduces imports (since imports are a function of national income: M = mY, where m is the marginal propensity to import).

- **Contractionary fiscal policy**: Raise taxes and/or cut government spending → lower disposable income → lower consumption, including of imports. Politically painful — reduces living standards and may increase unemployment.

- **Contractionary monetary policy**: Raise interest rates → reduce borrowing and spending → lower imports. Also attracts capital inflows (improving the financial account), but higher rates discourage domestic investment and may appreciate the currency, paradoxically worsening export competitiveness.

These policies are **blunt instruments** — they reduce imports but also reduce domestic output and employment. For an economy already in recession with a current account deficit, expenditure-reducing policies are procyclical and dangerous.

### 5.3 Supply-Side Policies

These address the **root cause** of a persistent deficit: lack of international competitiveness.

- Investment in **education and training** → higher labour productivity → lower unit labour costs → more competitive exports and import-competing industries.
- Investment in **infrastructure** (transport, digital, energy) → reduces business costs.
- **R&D incentives** → innovation and higher value-added production.
- **Labour market reforms** → flexibility to respond to changing global demand patterns.
- **Competition policy** → domestic firms become leaner and more efficient.

Supply-side policies are the only **sustainable long-term solution**, but they work slowly — years or decades. In the short term, a country may need a mix of expenditure-switching policies (to buy time) and supply-side reforms (to build lasting competitiveness).

## Answers

### Answer 1 — Structure of the Balance of Payments

**(a) Balances:**
- (i) Trade balance in goods = 320 − 380 = **−$60 billion**
- (ii) Trade balance in services = 95 − 70 = **+$25 billion**
- (iii) Primary income balance = 40 − 65 = **−$25 billion**
- (iv) Secondary income balance = 15 − 25 = **−$10 billion**

**(b) Current account balance** = −60 + 25 + (−25) + (−10) = **−$70 billion** (deficit).

**(c) Financial account (excluding reserves):**
- FDI (net) = 80 − 45 = +35
- Portfolio (net) = 30 − 60 = −30
- Financial account (excl. reserves) = 35 + (−30) = **+$5 billion**

**(d) Overall balance:**
- Current account: −70
- Financial account (excl. reserves): +5
- Reserve assets (net increase, recorded as debit): −25
- Total recorded = −70 + 5 − 25 = −90
- This does **not** sum to zero, so a **balancing item** of **+$90 billion** must be added. In practice, the balancing item reflects unrecorded flows, measurement errors, and timing differences. The identity: −70 + 5 − 25 + 90 = 0 ✓.

---

### Answer 2 — Interpreting a Current Account Deficit

**(a) Why it may NOT be a problem:**
- The deficit is financed by **stable, long-term FDI** (not volatile portfolio flows), reducing the risk of a sudden capital flow reversal.
- FDI is concentrated in **manufacturing and technology** — productive sectors that should generate future export revenue to service and repay external liabilities.
- GDP growth is **strong** (4.5%), meaning the economy is expanding and the ratio of external debt to GDP may be stable or declining.
- The deficit is effectively importing capital goods for investment — building future productive capacity (a "good deficit").

**(b) Scenario where it COULD become a problem:**
- If foreign investors lose confidence and FDI inflows suddenly stop or reverse. Even long-term FDI can be withdrawn (factories sold, projects cancelled) if political stability deteriorates, the investment climate worsens, or global conditions change.
- If the sectors receiving FDI fail to become internationally competitive (e.g., protected domestic market, no export orientation), the deficit's underlying cause remains unaddressed.
- If global interest rates rise sharply, the cost of servicing any external debt accumulated alongside FDI rises.

**(c) Comparison:**
Country Epsilon's 3% deficit is **more concerning** despite being smaller as a share of GDP. The financing is **short-term portfolio flows** — "hot money" that can reverse instantly if investor sentiment shifts, triggering a currency and/or financial crisis. Moreover, the inflows finance **consumption**, not investment — the country is borrowing to consume, leaving no productive assets to generate future repayment capacity. Country Delta's 7% deficit, financed by productive FDI, is structurally healthier than Epsilon's 3% consumption-financed deficit. **What matters is not the size of the deficit but its composition and financing.**

### Answer 3 — Policy Analysis

**(a) Problems with tariff increases:**
1. **WTO violation**: Unilateral tariff increases violate bound tariff commitments, inviting retaliation from trading partners that could harm Zeta's exports — potentially worsening the current account.
2. **Deadweight loss and inefficiency**: Tariffs create production and consumption inefficiency (Harberger triangles), reducing overall national welfare (see Lesson 42).
3. **Treats symptom, not cause**: If the deficit stems from low productivity or lack of competitiveness, tariffs protect inefficient domestic industries without improving their competitiveness. The deficit resumes as soon as tariffs are removed.
4. **Higher consumer prices**: Tariffs raise costs for domestic consumers and firms that use imported inputs, potentially fuelling inflation (currently at target — upsetting this balance).
5. **Does not address the full current account**: Tariffs only affect the goods trade balance, not services, primary income, or secondary income deficits.

**(b) Alternative policies:**
1. **Supply-side policies**: Invest in education, infrastructure, and R&D to improve labour productivity and international competitiveness. This addresses the **root cause** of the deficit. Though slow, it is the only sustainable solution.
2. **Allow currency depreciation**: With a freely floating currency, the central bank could signal tolerance for depreciation (or even encourage it through monetary easing). A weaker currency makes exports cheaper and imports more expensive — an automatic expenditure-switching mechanism — provided the Marshall-Lerner condition holds.

**(c) Depreciation mechanism:**
A depreciation of Zeta's currency means foreigners pay less of their currency for Zeta's exports → export demand increases. Simultaneously, Zeta's residents pay more domestic currency for imports → import demand decreases. The trade balance improves. **The Marshall-Lerner condition must hold**: the sum of the price elasticities of demand for exports and imports must be greater than 1 (|PED_X| + |PED_M| > 1). Otherwise, the price effects dominate quantity effects, and the trade balance worsens (J-curve: worsens before it improves).

### Answer 4 — The "Twin Deficits"

**(a) National income identity:**
\(Y = C + I + G + (X - M)\). Rearranging: \((X - M) = Y - C - I - G\). Since national saving \(S = Y - C - T\) (where T is taxes net of transfers), we have: \(Y - C = S + T\). Substituting: \(X - M = (S + T) - I - G = (S - I) + (T - G)\).

**(b) Saving-investment identity:**
\((X - M) = (S - I) + (T - G)\). The current account balance equals the sum of the private sector's net saving (S − I) and the government's budget balance (T − G). If the government runs a budget deficit (T − G < 0), and private saving and investment are unchanged, the current account must worsen. This is the theoretical link: a government budget deficit "absorbs" domestic saving, requiring foreign saving (capital inflow) to finance investment — which manifests as a current account deficit.

**(c) Why twin deficits might NOT hold:**
- **Ricardian equivalence**: If households anticipate that government borrowing today means higher taxes tomorrow, they increase private saving to offset the budget deficit. S rises, offsetting the fall in (T − G), leaving (X − M) unchanged.
- **Private saving response**: Private saving may rise automatically if households perceive increased uncertainty or reduce consumption. Empirically, the twin deficits relationship is strongest in the US (1980s, 2000s) but weak or absent in many other countries.
- **Investment changes**: If I falls when G rises (crowding out), (S − I) rises, offsetting the government deficit.
- **Small open economy with capital mobility**: The link may be weak if capital flows are driven by global factors rather than domestic fiscal positions.

### Answer 5 — Remittances and the Current Account

**(a) Current account balance:**
Trade balance (goods + services) = −$12 billion.
Primary income = −$3 billion.
Secondary income = +$18 billion.
Current account = −12 + (−3) + 18 = **+$3 billion (surplus).**

**(b) Risks of reliance on remittances:**
- **External dependence**: Remittances depend on economic conditions in **other countries** — beyond Theta's control. If host countries suffer a recession, migrant workers lose jobs and remittances fall sharply.
- **Exchange rate risk**: Remittance flows may be sensitive to exchange rate movements. A depreciation of the host-country currency reduces the domestic-currency value of remittances.
- **Political risk**: Host countries may tighten immigration policies, deporting workers and cutting remittance flows.
- **Structural neglect**: A steady remittance inflow may reduce pressure on the government to address the underlying trade deficit through competitiveness reforms — creating a form of "Dutch disease" where the trade sector atrophies.
- **Migration dependency**: The economy becomes structurally dependent on exporting labour rather than goods — a fragile development model.

**(c) Global recession effect:**
A global recession in host countries would reduce demand for migrant labour. Theta's diaspora workers would face job losses and reduced incomes, causing remittances to fall sharply. Simultaneously, Theta's exports of goods and services might also decline (reduced global demand). The current account could swing from a $3 billion surplus to a significant deficit, potentially triggering a balance of payments crisis if foreign reserves are inadequate.

## Evaluate: CLASPP Model Answer (150–200 words)

**"A current account deficit is always a sign of economic weakness." Evaluate.**

**Conclusion**: A current account deficit is not always a sign of economic weakness — its significance depends fundamentally on its cause, financing, duration, and the broader economic context. Blanket characterisation of all deficits as "weakness" is analytically unsound.

**Limitations**: The claim ignores the distinction between deficits financing productive investment and those financing consumption. A developing country importing capital goods to build export-oriented industries runs a deficit that builds future strength. The US has run persistent current account deficits for decades while remaining the world's largest economy — hardly a picture of "weakness."

**Assumptions**: The claim assumes that a deficit necessarily reflects competitiveness problems. But the saving-investment identity \((X - M) = (S - I) + (T - G)\) shows that a deficit can also reflect high domestic investment (productive) or a government budget deficit (potentially problematic but distinct from "economic weakness"). It also assumes deficits are always unsustainable, ignoring that FDI-financed deficits can persist indefinitely without crisis.

**Stakeholders**: Export-competing firms may interpret any deficit as weakness, but foreign investors may view the same deficit as an attractive investment opportunity. Workers in import-competing industries face job losses, while workers in FDI-receiving sectors gain employment.

**Pros/Cons**: Deficits can signal dynamism (high investment, strong growth attracting capital) or vulnerability (low competitiveness, excessive consumption). The East Asian crisis (1997) showed that even rapidly growing economies could face crisis if deficits were financed by short-term flows.

**Priorities**: The key policy-relevant question is not "is there a deficit?" but "is this deficit sustainable?" Sustainability depends on: (1) composition of financing (FDI vs. short-term flows), (2) use of foreign savings (investment vs. consumption), (3) size relative to GDP and trajectory, (4) exchange rate regime and reserve adequacy, and (5) underlying competitiveness. A deficit that fails these sustainability checks is indeed a sign of weakness — but many deficits pass them comfortably.</p>
