1. Prior Knowledge Check

Q1: State the law of demand and the law of supply.

Show

Law of demand: as price rises, quantity demanded falls (ceteris paribus). Law of supply: as price rises, quantity supplied rises (ceteris paribus).

Q2: What is the difference between a movement along a curve and a shift of the curve?

Show

A movement along is caused by a change in the good's own price. A shift is caused by a change in a non-price determinant (TRIBE for demand, PINTS for supply).

2. Market Equilibrium

Market equilibrium occurs at the price where the quantity demanded equals the quantity supplied. At this price, every unit that producers want to sell is bought by consumers, and every unit that consumers want to buy is supplied by producers. There is no tendency for the price to change вЂ” the market is in balance.

The equilibrium price (Pe) and equilibrium quantity (Qe) are found at the intersection of the demand and supply curves.

3. Excess Demand and Excess Supply

If the price is above equilibrium, quantity supplied exceeds quantity demanded вЂ” there is excess supply (a surplus). Producers cannot sell all they want. They cut prices to clear unsold stock. As the price falls, quantity demanded rises and quantity supplied falls until equilibrium is restored.

If the price is below equilibrium, quantity demanded exceeds quantity supplied вЂ” there is excess demand (a shortage). Consumers compete for limited supply, bidding the price up. As the price rises, quantity demanded falls and quantity supplied rises until equilibrium is restored.

5. The Price Mechanism вЂ” Three Functions

Prices in a free market perform three essential functions:

1. Signalling function: Prices convey information. A rising price signals to producers that the good is becoming more scarce or more desired вЂ” "make more of this." A falling price signals that the good is becoming more abundant or less desired вЂ” "make less of this." Consumers also respond to price signals: a higher price signals "consume less; find alternatives."

2. Incentive function: Prices motivate behaviour. A higher price gives producers an incentive to increase output (higher profits). It gives consumers an incentive to reduce consumption (to save money) and to seek substitutes. Prices align the self-interest of individual agents with the needs of the market.

3. Rationing function: When a good is scarce, the price rises until the quantity demanded equals the limited quantity supplied. The good is rationed to those willing and able to pay the higher price. This is how markets allocate scarce resources without anyone needing to decide who "deserves" the good.

Check-in: A frost in Brazil destroys 30% of the coffee harvest. Explain how the price mechanism responds, identifying the signalling, incentive, and rationing functions.

Show

Signalling: The higher price signals scarcity вЂ” "coffee is harder to find." Incentive: Higher prices encourage farmers to plant more coffee trees (increasing future supply) and encourage consumers to switch to tea or reduce consumption. Rationing: The limited coffee harvest is allocated to those willing to pay the higher price.
