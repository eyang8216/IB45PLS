Lesson 47: Government Intervention in Exchange Markets

What You'll Learn

  • The definition of a fixed exchange rate system and how governments maintain a currency peg
  • How central banks use foreign exchange reserves to intervene in currency markets
  • The difference between devaluation/revaluation (fixed systems) and depreciation/appreciation (floating systems)
  • The concept of a managed float (dirty float) — partial intervention alongside market forces
  • The advantages of fixed exchange rates: certainty, anti-inflation anchor, speculation deterrence
  • The disadvantages: large reserve requirements, conflict with domestic monetary policy, speculative attack vulnerability
  • Real-world applications: China's managed float and the UK's 1992 ERM crisis

1. Fixed Exchange Rate Systems

A fixed exchange rate (pegged exchange rate) is a system in which the government or central bank sets the value of its currency at a specific rate relative to another currency or a basket of currencies. The central bank commits to maintaining this rate through market intervention.

How a Fixed Rate Is Maintained

The government announces a fixed (par) value — say, 1 peso = $0.50 USD. If market forces push the exchange rate away from this target, the central bank must intervene by buying or selling its own currency (using foreign exchange reserves).

Case 1: Upward pressure (currency facing appreciation)

If the peso is under upward pressure (excess demand at the fixed rate), the market-clearing rate would be above the fixed rate. To keep the rate fixed, the central bank must sell pesos and buy foreign currency — increasing the supply of pesos on the foreign exchange market. This shifts the supply curve of pesos to the right, bringing the equilibrium rate back down to the peg. The central bank accumulates foreign reserves in the process.

Case 2: Downward pressure (currency facing depreciation)

If the peso is under downward pressure (excess supply at the fixed rate), the market-clearing rate would be below the fixed rate. To defend the peg, the central bank must buy pesos and sell foreign reserves — increasing the demand for pesos. This shifts the demand curve for pesos to the right, pushing the rate back up to the peg. The central bank's foreign reserves decrease.

The Fixed Exchange Rate Diagram

The diagram shows:

  • The D (demand for pesos) and S (supply of pesos) curves.
  • The fixed rate line — a horizontal line at the pegged value.
  • If the fixed rate is above equilibrium: there is excess supply of pesos (downward pressure). The central bank buys pesos (increases D).
  • If the fixed rate is below equilibrium: there is excess demand for pesos (upward pressure). The central bank sells pesos (increases S).
  • Intervention arrows: show the shift of D or S caused by central bank intervention, bringing the market rate back to the peg.

Worked Example — Defending a Fixed Rate

Country A pegs its currency (the dollar-A) at 1 A$ = US$0.40. The free-market equilibrium (without intervention) is 1 A$ = US$0.35.

Analysis: The peg at US$0.40 is above the market-clearing rate — the dollar-A is overvalued. At US$0.40, there is excess supply of A$: more people want to sell A$ (to buy imports, invest abroad) than want to buy A$ (to buy exports, invest in Country A).

Intervention required: The central bank must buy the excess supply of A$ — using its US dollar reserves. It demands A$ (shifting the D curve right) until the rate returns to US$0.40. This depletes foreign reserves.

If reserves run low and the market believes the peg is unsustainable, speculators will sell A$ heavily (a speculative attack), forcing either a devaluation or abandonment of the peg.

2. Terminology: Devaluation/Revaluation vs. Depreciation/Appreciation

IB examiners are strict about terminology. Use the correct term depending on the exchange rate regime:

TermRegimeMeaning
DepreciationFloatingA fall in the currency's value due to market forces (S and D)
AppreciationFloatingA rise in the currency's value due to market forces
DevaluationFixedA deliberate government decision to lower the pegged value of the currency
RevaluationFixedA deliberate government decision to raise the pegged value of the currency

Key distinction: Depreciation/appreciation are market-driven; devaluation/revaluation are policy decisions. Never say "the government depreciated the currency" — that is incorrect. The correct phrasing is "the government devalued the currency."

Worked Example: In 1994, Mexico abandoned its crawling peg and allowed the peso to float. The peso fell from 3.4 to 7.2 pesos per dollar. This was initially a devaluation (policy decision to change the peg) followed by further depreciation (market forces after the float). IB exam answers should note both phases.

3. Managed Float (Dirty Float)

A managed float (also called a dirty float) is a hybrid system in which:

  • The exchange rate is primarily determined by market forces (supply and demand).
  • However, the central bank occasionally intervenes to smooth excessive volatility, prevent "disorderly market conditions," or influence the trend direction.

How It Works

Unlike a fixed rate, there is no announced peg. The central bank steps in only when it judges the exchange rate to be "out of line" with fundamentals or moving too rapidly. Intervention is typically not intended to permanently change the rate but to reduce short-run volatility.

Tools used:

  • Direct intervention (buying/selling currency).
  • Indirect intervention (changing interest rates to attract/deter capital flows).
  • Verbal intervention ("jawboning" — officials signal desired exchange rate direction).

China's Managed Float

China provides the most prominent real-world example. From 1994 to 2005, China maintained a fixed peg at approximately 8.28 yuan per US dollar. In 2005, China shifted to a managed float — the yuan was allowed to fluctuate within a narrow daily band (initially ±0.3%, later widened). The People's Bank of China (PBOC) intervenes heavily, buying US dollars to prevent excessive yuan appreciation, accumulating over $3 trillion in foreign reserves at its peak.

The IMF classifies China's regime as a "crawling-like arrangement" or managed float — not a pure float. The PBOC's intervention prevents full market determination of the yuan's value, a persistent source of trade tension with the US.

4. Advantages of Fixed Exchange Rates

4.1 Certainty and Reduced Exchange Rate Risk

When exchange rates are fixed, importers and exporters know exactly what exchange rate will apply to future transactions. This eliminates exchange rate risk, encouraging international trade and cross-border investment. Firms can sign long-term contracts without fear of currency movements wiping out profit margins.

4.2 Anti-Inflation Anchor

A country with a history of high inflation can "import" the monetary policy credibility of the country to which it pegs. By fixing to a low-inflation currency (e.g., the US dollar or euro), the country commits to maintaining the peg — which requires disciplined monetary policy. If the domestic central bank prints too much money, the currency comes under downward pressure, forcing it to use reserves to defend the peg. This constraint disciplines policy.

Example: Argentina's 1991 "Convertibility Plan" pegged the peso 1:1 to the US dollar, breaking hyperinflation. Inflation fell from over 2,300% (1990) to single digits within three years.

4.3 Prevention of Harmful Speculation (in Theory)

If a peg is credible, speculators have no incentive to bet against the currency because they expect the rate to remain fixed. However, this applies only when the peg is perceived as sustainable.

4.4 Promotes Trade and Investment

Exchange rate stability reduces transaction costs and uncertainty, potentially increasing the volume of trade and foreign direct investment. This is one reason the European countries moved toward fixed rates (the ERM) and eventually a single currency (the euro).

5. Disadvantages of Fixed Exchange Rates

5.1 Requires Large Foreign Exchange Reserves

Defending a peg requires the central bank to hold substantial reserves of foreign currency. If the market persistently pushes against the peg, reserves may be depleted. A country with limited reserves cannot sustain a fixed rate indefinitely.

5.2 Conflict with Domestic Monetary Policy

A fixed exchange rate means the central bank cannot use monetary policy independently for domestic goals (the impossible trinity or "trilemma"). If the economy is in recession and needs lower interest rates, lowering rates would cause capital outflows, putting downward pressure on the currency — conflicting with the peg. The country must either sacrifice domestic policy autonomy or abandon the peg.

5.3 Vulnerability to Speculative Attacks

If markets believe a peg is unsustainable — perhaps because reserves are declining, the current account deficit is large, or domestic economic conditions diverge from the anchor country — speculators will attack the currency. They sell the domestic currency short, forcing the central bank to spend reserves defending the peg. Eventually, the central bank may run out of reserves and be forced to devalue or float.

5.4 Real-World: UK ERM Crisis (Black Wednesday, 1992)

The UK joined the European Exchange Rate Mechanism (ERM) in 1990, pegging the pound to the Deutsche Mark within a ±6% band. The UK's economic conditions (high inflation, recession) required lower interest rates, but maintaining the peg required rates to stay high to attract capital inflows (to match Germany's post-reunification high rates).

By September 1992, speculators — most famously George Soros's Quantum Fund — bet heavily against the pound, believing the peg was unsustainable. The Bank of England spent billions in reserves and raised interest rates from 10% to 12% to 15% in a single day. These efforts failed. On 16 September 1992 ("Black Wednesday"), the UK withdrew from the ERM and allowed the pound to float. The pound depreciated sharply, but the UK economy subsequently recovered, freed from the interest-rate straitjacket. Soros reportedly made over $1 billion.

Lesson: A fixed exchange rate that conflicts with domestic economic needs invites speculative attack and can inflict severe costs before failing.

6. Evaluating Exchange Rate Regimes

There is no universally superior regime. The choice depends on a country's economic structure, trade openness, credibility of institutions, and vulnerability to external shocks.

Practice Problems

Problem 1 — Fixed Rate Diagram and Intervention

Country Beta pegs its currency (the bata) at 1 bata = US$0.25. The free-market equilibrium rate (without intervention) is 1 bata = US$0.30.

(a) Is the bata overvalued or undervalued at the pegged rate? Explain.
(b) Draw the foreign exchange market diagram for the bata. Show the fixed rate, the market equilibrium, and identify whether there is excess demand or excess supply at the peg.
(c) What intervention must Beta's central bank undertake to maintain the peg? Show this on your diagram.
(d) How does this intervention affect Beta's foreign exchange reserves?

Problem 2 — Devaluation and Its Effects

Country Gamma maintains a fixed exchange rate of 5 gammas = US$1. Due to persistent current account deficits and declining reserves, Gamma decides to devalue its currency to 7 gammas = US$1.

(a) Explain the difference between devaluation and depreciation.
(b) What must Gamma's central bank do during the transition from 5 to 7 gammas per dollar?
(c) Analyse the likely effects of this devaluation on Gamma's: (i) exports, (ii) imports, (iii) current account balance, (iv) inflation rate.
(d) Why might the J-curve effect mean the current account does not improve immediately?

Problem 3 — The Impossible Trinity

The "impossible trinity" (trilemma) states that a country cannot simultaneously have: (i) a fixed exchange rate, (ii) free capital mobility, and (iii) an independent monetary policy. Only two of the three are possible.

(a) Explain why a country with a fixed exchange rate and free capital mobility loses monetary policy autonomy. Use an example: if the country lowers interest rates to fight a recession, what happens?
(b) How does China partially resolve this trilemma? What does China sacrifice?
(c) How does the US (a floating-rate country) resolve this trilemma? What does it gain?

Problem 4 — Managed Float Analysis

Country Delta operates a managed float. Its currency has been appreciating rapidly due to large capital inflows from a commodity boom. The central bank is concerned that an overvalued currency will harm export competitiveness.

(a) Describe two interventions the central bank could use to slow the appreciation.
(b) What are the risks of persistent intervention to prevent appreciation?
(c) How might sterilised intervention differ from unsterilised intervention in its macroeconomic effects?

Problem 5 — Speculative Attack and the UK ERM

On Black Wednesday (16 September 1992), the UK was forced to withdraw the pound from the ERM.

(a) Explain the economic conditions that made the pound's ERM peg unsustainable.
(b) Using a foreign exchange market diagram, show how speculative selling of the pound created downward pressure that the Bank of England could not resist.
(c) What happened to the UK economy after the pound was allowed to float? Explain why some economists view the ERM exit as a policy success despite the humiliation.

Problem 6 — IB-Style Essay Question (Challenging)

"Evaluate the view that fixed exchange rates provide more benefits than costs for developing economies." [15 marks]

Answers

Answer 1 — Fixed Rate Diagram and Intervention

(a) The bata is undervalued at the peg (US$0.25). The market-clearing rate is US$0.30, meaning the market values the bata more highly than the peg. At US$0.25, there is excess demand for the bata — buyers want more batas than sellers are willing to supply at that artificially low price.

(b) Diagram: D and S curves intersect at quantity Q* where P = US$0.30. The fixed rate line is horizontal at US$0.25 (below equilibrium). At US$0.25: Q_d > Q_s — excess demand. The gap between Q_d and Q_s at the pegged rate represents the shortage of batas.

(c) Intervention: The central bank must sell batas (increase the supply of batas) to meet the excess demand. This shifts the S curve to the right until it intersects D at the fixed rate US$0.25. The central bank supplies the difference between Q_d and Q_s.

(d) Effect on reserves: Selling batas means the central bank buys foreign currency (US dollars). Beta's foreign exchange reserves increase as the central bank accumulates dollars. This is a relatively comfortable position — building reserves is easier than depleting them.

Answer 2 — Devaluation and Its Effects

(a) Devaluation is a deliberate government decision to lower the fixed value of the currency (changing the peg). Depreciation is a market-driven fall in a floating currency's value due to changes in supply and demand — no government decision is involved.

(b) During the transition, the central bank must stop defending the old peg (stop buying gammas at 5/US$1). It may also intervene to guide the rate to the new target of 7 gammas/US$1, selling gammas if the rate overshoots. Essentially, reserves that were being spent to keep the rate at 5 are no longer needed — the central bank can step back.

(c) Effects:

  • (i) Exports: Gamma's exports become cheaper to foreigners (a US buyer needs fewer dollars to buy gamma goods). Export quantity should increase, and export revenue (in foreign currency) may rise if demand is price-elastic (Marshall-Lerner condition).
  • (ii) Imports: Imports become more expensive in gamma terms. Import quantity should fall, but import expenditure (in domestic currency) may rise initially.
  • (iii) Current account: Should improve in the medium term if the Marshall-Lerner condition holds (|PED_exports| + |PED_imports| > 1).
  • (iv) Inflation: Imported inflation rises — raw materials, energy, and consumer goods priced in foreign currency cost more in gammas. This may trigger a wage-price spiral.

(d) J-curve effect: In the very short run, trade contracts are fixed — quantities cannot adjust immediately. The higher import prices (in domestic currency) worsen the trade balance before quantities respond. Only after contracts are renegotiated and consumers adjust do export quantities rise and import quantities fall, improving the current account. The time path resembles a "J" — initial worsening followed by improvement.

Answer 3 — The Impossible Trinity

(a) If a country has a fixed exchange rate and free capital mobility, lowering domestic interest rates causes capital to flow out (investors seek higher returns abroad). The capital outflow increases the supply of domestic currency on forex markets, creating downward pressure on the exchange rate. To maintain the peg, the central bank must buy domestic currency (using reserves) — which reduces the domestic money supply, reversing the interest rate cut. The attempt at expansionary monetary policy is self-defeating — the interest rate is anchored to the anchor country's rate.

(b) China maintains a managed exchange rate and retains partial monetary policy autonomy by restricting capital mobility. China imposes capital controls — limits on how much money can flow in/out, restrictions on foreign investment, and tight regulation of financial flows. China sacrifices free capital mobility, gaining both exchange rate stability and domestic monetary policy control.

(c) The US has a floating exchange rate and free capital mobility. By letting the exchange rate adjust to market forces, the US retains full monetary policy autonomy — the Fed can set interest rates purely for domestic objectives (inflation, employment) without worrying about the exchange rate. The US sacrifices exchange rate stability, gaining both free capital flows and independent monetary policy.

Answer 4 — Managed Float Analysis

(a) Two interventions:

  1. Direct intervention: The central bank sells domestic currency and buys foreign currency on forex markets. This increases the supply of domestic currency, putting downward pressure on the exchange rate and slowing appreciation.
  2. Interest rate intervention: Lower the domestic interest rate to reduce the attractiveness of domestic assets to foreign investors, reducing capital inflows and thus demand for the domestic currency.

(b) Risks of persistent intervention:

  • Reserve accumulation: The central bank accumulates large foreign reserves, which may lose value if the foreign currency depreciates (capital loss risk).
  • Inflation: Selling domestic currency increases the domestic money supply, which can fuel inflation if not sterilised.
  • Misallocation: Propping up an undervalued currency distorts resource allocation — it subsidises exporters and penalises consumers of imports.
  • Trade tensions: Persistent intervention to maintain an undervalued currency is seen as currency manipulation, inviting retaliation from trading partners (as the US has accused China).

(c) Sterilised vs. unsterilised:

  • Unsterilised intervention: The central bank sells domestic currency → domestic money supply increases → lower interest rates → potential inflation. This has macroeconomic effects beyond the exchange rate.
  • Sterilised intervention: The central bank simultaneously sells domestic bonds (open market operation) to absorb the extra money supply. The money supply is unchanged, so domestic interest rates and inflation are unaffected. However, sterilisation changes the composition of assets held by the public (more foreign bonds, fewer domestic bonds), which may have subtle portfolio balance effects.

Answer 5 — UK ERM Speculative Attack

(a) Unsustainable conditions: The UK joined the ERM at a central rate of DM 2.95 per pound. But German reunification (1990) forced the Bundesbank to raise interest rates to combat inflationary pressures. The UK, in recession, needed low interest rates. To maintain the ERM peg, the UK had to match Germany's high rates — devastating for the UK's housing market and economy. The fundamental conflict: Germany's monetary policy was suited to German conditions, not UK conditions. Markets recognised this incompatibility — the pound was overvalued at DM 2.95 given UK economic weakness.

(b) Diagram: The demand for pounds (D) and supply of pounds (S) intersect at a rate below DM 2.95 — the equilibrium was below the central ERM rate. Speculators massively increased the supply of pounds (selling short), shifting S sharply right. The Bank of England bought pounds (shifting D right) to counter, but speculators' resources (the entire global forex market) vastly exceeded the UK's reserves. D could not shift far enough — the peg broke.

(c) After floating: The pound depreciated by approximately 15%. UK interest rates were cut. Exports became more competitive. The UK economy exited recession and began a sustained period of growth. Inflation remained contained. Many economists view the ERM exit as a blessing in disguise — it freed monetary policy to serve domestic objectives. The "humiliation" was political; economically, it was a necessary correction. This experience reinforced British euroscepticism and is a major reason the UK never adopted the euro.

Answer 6 — Fixed Rates for Developing Economies

See the Evaluate section below for a full CLASPP model answer.

Evaluate: CLASPP Model Answer (150–200 words)

"Fixed exchange rates provide more benefits than costs for developing economies." Evaluate.

Conclusion: Fixed exchange rates can serve as a powerful stabilisation tool for developing economies with weak monetary institutions, but the costs — particularly loss of policy autonomy and speculative vulnerability — often outweigh the benefits once the economy matures or faces asymmetric shocks.

Limitations: The anti-inflation benefit is real but depends entirely on the anchor country maintaining low inflation. If the anchor country (e.g., the US) raises interest rates sharply, the developing economy must follow — regardless of its own economic conditions — as Argentina painfully discovered when US rates rose in the late 1990s while Argentina was in recession.

Assumptions: The argument assumes the developing economy can accumulate and maintain sufficient reserves to defend the peg. Many developing economies have limited reserve buffers — making pegs fragile. It also assumes the country's trade structure aligns with the anchor currency zone; if most trade is with countries outside the anchor zone, the peg provides little certainty benefit.

Stakeholders: Exporters and foreign investors benefit from exchange rate certainty — encouraging trade and FDI. However, domestic manufacturers competing with imports lose the buffer of a depreciating currency, and workers may face unemployment when competitiveness erodes.

Pros/Cons: Fixed rates impose monetary discipline (pro), but this discipline becomes a straitjacket when domestic conditions diverge from the anchor (con). The certainty benefit is substantial for trade-dependent small economies; the loss of monetary autonomy is crippling for economies facing unique shocks.

Priorities: For developing economies emerging from hyperinflation or lacking credible central banks, a temporary fixed peg (or currency board) can provide essential stability — Argentina (1991) and Bulgaria (1997) demonstrate this. However, the long-term goal should be building credible domestic institutions that allow a transition to managed floating. Fixed rates are a crutch, not a permanent solution — as the eurozone periphery crisis also demonstrated. The balance tips toward fixed rates only in the short-to-medium term and only when backed by adequate reserves and fiscal discipline.
