Lesson 28: Keynesian Aggregate Supply and Equilibrium

What You'll Learn

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to:

  • Draw and explain the Keynesian AS curve with its three distinct segments
  • Define spare capacity, bottlenecks, and full employment output in the Keynesian framework
  • Explain why Keynesian equilibrium can occur below full employment and why this matters
  • Understand sticky downward wages as the core reason the economy fails to self-correct
  • Articulate the policy implication that government must intervene to close a recessionary gap
  • Compare the Keynesian and monetarist models, identifying where students commonly confuse them
  • Apply the Keynesian model to real-world recessionary situations

1. The Keynesian Aggregate Supply Curve — Three Segments

Why Keynes Rejected the Classical View

John Maynard Keynes wrote The General Theory of Employment, Interest and Money in 1936, in the depths of the Great Depression. The classical economists of his day argued that economies self-correct: if unemployment is high, wages fall, firms hire more, and the economy returns to full employment. Keynes looked at the world around him — factories idle, millions unemployed for years — and concluded that this self-correction story was wrong, or at least so slow as to be irrelevant for policy.

Keynes developed an alternative model of aggregate supply. Unlike the monetarist model, where LRAS is vertical at full employment and all unemployment is voluntary or transitional, the Keynesian AS curve has three distinct segments, each representing a different state of the economy.

Segment 1: The Horizontal (Keynesian) Range

At low levels of real GDP — when the economy is in a deep recession or depression — the AS curve is perfectly horizontal. In this range, the economy has massive spare capacity: factories are idle, machinery sits unused, and unemployment is high. Firms can increase production simply by calling back laid-off workers and restarting idle equipment. They do not need to bid up wages or input prices to do so, because there is an abundance of unemployed resources.

Because firms can expand output without facing rising costs, an increase in aggregate demand in this segment produces a large increase in real GDP with no increase in the price level. The economy is so far below its productive capacity that demand can grow substantially before any inflationary pressure appears.

This is the segment Keynes considered most relevant during depressions. The Great Depression was, in his view, an economy stuck in the horizontal range, where millions were willing to work but no one would hire them because there was insufficient demand for goods.

Spare capacity is the extent to which an economy's current output falls short of what it could produce with its existing resources if they were fully employed. An economy with 10 percent unemployment and factories running at 60 percent of capacity has substantial spare capacity. In the horizontal segment, spare capacity is abundant.

Segment 2: The Upward-Sloping (Intermediate) Range

As the economy recovers and output expands, it eventually enters the upward-sloping segment. In this range, spare capacity is diminishing. Firms start to encounter bottlenecks — specific shortages that constrain further expansion even though other resources remain available.

A manufacturing firm might have the machines to produce more but cannot find enough skilled welders. A construction company might have the workers but faces delays in obtaining steel or lumber. A technology firm might have the orders and the engineers but lacks the specialised semiconductor chips it needs. These bottlenecks are localised constraints — one sector or one input hitting capacity limits before the economy as a whole has reached full employment.

Because bottlenecks create rising costs in specific areas before the whole economy is at capacity, the AS curve begins to slope upward. Further increases in AD now produce both higher real GDP and a rising price level. The steeper the curve gets, the more of each additional dollar of spending translates into inflation rather than output.

Bottlenecks are supply constraints in specific sectors or for specific inputs that arise before the overall economy reaches full employment. They explain why inflation can emerge even when aggregate unemployment is still above the natural rate.

Segment 3: The Vertical (Classical) Range

Eventually, the economy reaches full employment output (Yf). At this point, all resources — labour, capital, land — are fully employed at normal rates. The AS curve becomes perfectly vertical, exactly as in the monetarist model. Beyond Yf, no increase in aggregate demand can raise real output. Any further increase in AD produces only a higher price level — pure inflation.

Full employment output in the Keynesian model means the same thing as in the monetarist model: real GDP when the only unemployment is the natural rate (frictional plus structural), with no cyclical unemployment. The difference is that the Keynesian model sees this as a limit the economy may remain far below for a long time, whereas the monetarist model sees it as the economy's default position to which it always returns.

The Full Keynesian AS Diagram

The complete Keynesian AS curve looks like a reversed L rotated 90 degrees — horizontal at low output, then curving upward, then vertical at Yf. When drawing it for IB exams:

  • Label the horizontal axis "Real GDP" and the vertical axis "Price Level"
  • Mark the three segments clearly
  • Label Yf on the horizontal axis where the vertical segment begins
  • The horizontal segment extends from the origin to some level of output well below Yf
  • The upward-sloping segment connects the horizontal and vertical ranges
  • The vertical segment rises from Yf

A common mistake: students sometimes draw the Keynesian AS as three disconnected lines. The three segments form one continuous curve — it bends, but it does not break.

2. Keynesian Equilibrium Below Full Employment

The Key Insight

In the monetarist model, the economy is always at or returning to Yf. Short-run equilibrium may deviate from Yf, but forces are always pulling it back. In the Keynesian model, this is not true. The economy can settle into an equilibrium that is below full employment and stay there indefinitely. There is no automatic mechanism that guarantees a return to Yf.

Why? Because the Keynesian AS curve is horizontal in the range where spare capacity exists. In this range, a fall in AD reduces real GDP but does not cause the price level to fall. Without falling prices, the mechanisms that monetarists rely on for self-correction — falling wages, increased real money balances, lower interest rates — do not operate, or operate only very weakly.

Sticky Downward Wages: The Core Mechanism

The key assumption in the Keynesian model is that nominal wages are sticky downward. Workers and unions fiercely resist nominal wage cuts. A firm that tries to cut wages by 5 percent during a recession may face strikes, lawsuits, collapsed morale, and the loss of its best workers to other employers. Even unemployed workers may refuse job offers at wages substantially below what they previously earned, holding out for better offers. Minimum wage laws set a legal floor.

If nominal wages cannot fall, then the self-correction mechanism of the monetarist model breaks down. The sequence in the monetarist model is: recession → unemployment → falling wages → lower costs → SRAS shifts right → back to Yf. If the "falling wages" step is blocked, the chain is broken. The economy stays stuck.

This does not mean wages never adjust. Over very long periods, wages may gradually decline relative to what they would have been with growth. But the adjustment is so slow that waiting for it means accepting years or decades of unnecessarily high unemployment and lost output.

Worked Example 1: Equilibrium Below Full Employment

An economy has potential output Yf = $2,000 billion. The Keynesian AS curve is horizontal at a price level of 100 for all output levels up to $1,600 billion, then slopes upward until Yf = $2,000 billion, after which it is vertical. Aggregate demand is currently $1,200 billion at the given price level. Determine the equilibrium and identify whether there is a gap.

Approach: We locate where AD intersects the AS curve and compare equilibrium output to Yf.

Step 1: At P = 100, AD is $1,200 billion. The AS curve is horizontal at P = 100 for output up to $1,600 billion, so firms are willing to supply $1,200 billion at P = 100. The short-run equilibrium is at Y = $1,200 billion, P = 100.

Step 2: Yf = $2,000 billion. Equilibrium Y = $1,200 billion is well below Yf. The output gap is $800 billion, or 40 percent of potential output.

Step 3: Because the equilibrium is in the horizontal segment, there is no downward pressure on the price level, and therefore no automatic force pushing the economy back toward Yf. The economy can remain in this state indefinitely unless AD increases.

Why this makes sense: This is the Keynesian explanation for the Great Depression. The economy settled into equilibrium with massive unemployment, and left to itself, it showed no sign of recovering. Only the massive fiscal expansion of World War II — a huge rightward shift of AD — finally pulled the economy back to full employment.

3. Policy Implication: Government Must Intervene

The Case for Demand Management

The Keynesian model leads to a clear and powerful policy conclusion: when the economy is in a recessionary gap, the government must intervene to shift aggregate demand to the right. Waiting for the economy to self-correct is not just slow — it may never happen at all, or at least not within any politically or humanly acceptable timeframe.

The government has two tools for shifting AD right:

Fiscal policy: Increase government spending (G) directly, or cut taxes to increase households' disposable income and boost consumption (C). Either way, AD shifts right.

Monetary policy: The central bank can lower interest rates to encourage investment (I) and consumption of durable goods, shifting AD right.

In the Keynesian horizontal range, the entire increase in AD translates into higher real GDP with zero inflation. This is a free lunch — output and employment rise, and prices do not. In the upward-sloping range, the benefits are mixed: output rises but so does the price level. Policymakers must weigh the gain in employment against the cost of inflation. In the vertical range, any attempt to boost AD produces only inflation with no gain in output — policy is impotent.

Why This Was Revolutionary

Before Keynes, the prevailing view among economists and policymakers was that government budgets should be balanced and that the economy, if left alone, would take care of itself. Depressions were seen as unfortunate but unavoidable — like storms, you just had to wait them out. Keynes argued that this passivity was not only unnecessary but cruel. If the private sector would not spend enough to maintain full employment, the government should spend on its behalf. The government's budget, far from needing to be balanced every year, should run deficits during recessions to fill the spending gap left by the private sector.

This idea — counter-cyclical fiscal policy — transformed economic policy worldwide and remains the foundation of how most governments respond to recessions today.

4. Comparing Keynesian and Monetarist Models

A Common Mistake Students Make

A common mistake students make is thinking the Keynesian and monetarist models are just two ways of saying the same thing — that one is a "short-run" model and the other is a "long-run" model of the same economy. They are not. They are built on fundamentally different assumptions about how the economy works, especially about the flexibility of wages and prices and the stability of the private sector.

The monetarist (or New Classical) model assumes that wages and prices are flexible, that markets clear, and that the economy tends naturally toward full employment. The Keynesian model assumes that wages are sticky downward, that markets can fail to clear for long periods, and that the economy can get stuck in an underemployment equilibrium.

These different assumptions lead to radically different policy conclusions. Monetarists advocate for minimal government intervention and a focus on supply-side policies to shift LRAS. Keynesians advocate for active demand management — using fiscal and monetary policy to steer the economy toward Yf.

Worked Example 2: Same Shock, Different Predictions

Suppose consumer confidence collapses, reducing consumption spending at every price level. What happens in each model?

Monetarist prediction: AD shifts left. In the short run, Y falls below Yf and P falls. Unemployment rises above the natural rate. Over time, the labour surplus pushes nominal wages down. Lower wages reduce firms' costs, SRAS shifts right, and the economy returns to Yf at a permanently lower price level. No government action needed; the economy self-corrects.

Keynesian prediction: AD shifts left. If the economy was in the horizontal segment, Y falls but P does not fall (or falls only slightly). Because P does not fall, real wages do not rise (the nominal wage floor prevents adjustment), and there is no mechanism to push the economy back. The economy settles at the new, lower equilibrium and stays there. Government must intervene — increase G, cut taxes, or lower interest rates — to shift AD back right.

The same initial shock produces the same initial movement but fundamentally different predictions about what happens next.

5. Diagram Practice for IB Exams

The IB frequently tests the Keynesian AS model in Paper 1. The essential diagram shows:

  1. The three-segment AS curve, labeled "Keynesian AS" or simply "AS"
  2. A downward-sloping AD curve
  3. Equilibrium in the horizontal segment (for recession) or upward-sloping segment (for recovery)

When showing a recessionary gap in the Keynesian model:

  • AD intersects AS in the horizontal range
  • The vertical distance to Yf is clearly marked
  • The output gap is labeled
  • No shift of AS occurs — the point is that AS does not shift on its own

When showing government intervention:

  • AD shifts right (from AD₁ to AD₂)
  • The new equilibrium is at or closer to Yf
  • If in the horizontal segment, P is unchanged
  • If in the upward-sloping segment, P rises somewhat
IB Exam Tip

In a Paper 1 part (a) question asking you to explain the Keynesian AS model, structure your answer around the three segments. For each segment, describe the state of the economy, explain why the curve takes that shape, and state the relationship between AD changes and output/price level changes in that segment. Use a fully labeled diagram. This is typically a 10-mark question, so depth on each segment is expected.

For part (b) evaluation questions, the comparison with the monetarist model is a rich source of analysis. Consider: which model better describes different real-world situations? When is each model more useful? What are the weaknesses of the Keynesian assumption of completely sticky wages? What are the weaknesses of the monetarist assumption of rapid self-correction?

Practice Problems

Problem 1. Draw the Keynesian AS curve and label its three segments. For each segment, explain: (a) the state of the economy in terms of spare capacity and unemployment, (b) the shape of the curve in that segment, and (c) what happens to real GDP and the price level if AD increases while the economy is in that segment.

Problem 2. Define the following terms in the context of the Keynesian model: (a) spare capacity, (b) bottlenecks, (c) full employment output. For each, provide a concrete example from a real economy.

Problem 3. An economy is described by the following Keynesian AS: horizontal at P = 105 for Y from 0 to $800 billion, upward-sloping for Y from $800 billion to $1,200 billion, and vertical at Y = $1,200 billion (Yf). Current AD intersects AS at Y = $600 billion.

(a) Identify the segment in which the economy is operating. Is there a recessionary gap? If so, calculate its size.

(b) Suppose the government increases infrastructure spending, shifting AD right by $300 billion. What are the new equilibrium Y and P? Has the gap been fully closed?

(c) Instead of $300 billion, suppose the government increases spending by $700 billion. What are the new equilibrium Y and P? Explain why the outcome is different from part (b).

Problem 4. Explain why sticky downward wages are the central assumption of the Keynesian model. In your answer, explain: (a) what is meant by "sticky downward," (b) three reasons why nominal wages might not fall during a recession, and (c) why sticky wages prevent the economy from self-correcting.

Problem 5. (IB-style, 10 marks) Explain the Keynesian view of aggregate supply and the determination of equilibrium output. In your answer, you should describe the three segments of the Keynesian AS curve, explain why each has the shape it does, and use a diagram to show how equilibrium can occur below full employment. (10 marks)

Problem 6. An economy has Yf = $2,500 billion. The Keynesian AS curve is horizontal for output up to $1,800 billion, then slopes upward until Yf. Currently, AD = $1,500 billion and the economy is in the horizontal segment at a price level of 110.

(a) Calculate the output gap as a percentage of Yf.

(b) The government proposes a stimulus package that would increase AD by $600 billion. Calculate the new equilibrium Y and state whether the stimulus moves the economy into a different segment of the AS curve.

(c) A monetarist critic argues that the stimulus is unnecessary because the economy will self-correct. Explain the monetarist self-correction mechanism and evaluate whether it is likely to work in this scenario. Consider the time frame, the output gap size, and the assumption of wage flexibility.

Answers

Answer 1. The Keynesian AS curve has three segments. Segment 1 (horizontal): The economy is in deep recession with massive spare capacity — high unemployment, idle factories, unused machinery. The curve is horizontal because firms can increase output by bringing idle resources back into production without bidding up wages or input prices. An increase in AD in this segment raises real GDP without any increase in the price level. Segment 2 (upward-sloping): The economy is recovering. Spare capacity is diminishing, and bottlenecks appear — specific skills or inputs become scarce even while other resources are available. The curve slopes upward because further increases in output now require bidding up wages and input prices. An increase in AD raises both real GDP and the price level. Segment 3 (vertical): The economy is at full employment output (Yf). All resources are fully employed at normal rates. The curve is vertical because no further increase in output is possible — any increase in AD produces only a higher price level, with no increase in real GDP.

A common mistake is to describe the horizontal segment as representing "no scarcity." Scarcity still exists — the resources are there but are idle. The segment represents the failure of the market to employ available resources, not the absence of scarcity.

Answer 2. (a) Spare capacity is the gap between an economy's current output and its potential output — the amount of extra output that could be produced if all idle resources were brought into use. Example: during the 2008–2009 recession, US manufacturing capacity utilisation fell from about 80 percent to under 65 percent. Factories that could produce $100 million in goods were producing only $65 million. The $35 million difference per factory is spare capacity.

(b) Bottlenecks are specific supply constraints that limit production even when spare capacity exists elsewhere in the economy. Example: in 2021, the global semiconductor chip shortage created bottlenecks for car manufacturers. Automakers had factories, workers, and demand for vehicles, but could not produce cars because they could not obtain enough microchips — a bottleneck in one input constrained output across an entire industry.

(c) Full employment output (Yf) is the level of real GDP when the only unemployment is the natural rate — frictional plus structural — with no cyclical unemployment. It represents the maximum sustainable output of the economy. Example: if a country's natural rate of unemployment is 4 percent and its labour force is 50 million workers, full employment means approximately 48 million workers are employed. Output at that level of employment is Yf.

Answer 3. (a) The economy is in the horizontal segment because Y = $600 billion is below $800 billion, the point at which the curve begins to slope upward. There is a recessionary gap: Yf = $1,200 billion, current Y = $600 billion, so the gap is $600 billion.

(b) Adding $300 billion to AD shifts AD right by $300 billion horizontally. The new AD intersects AS at Y = $600 billion + $300 billion = $900 billion. Since $900 billion is in the upward-sloping segment, the price level rises above 105. The exact price increase depends on the slope of the upward-sloping segment — without more information, we can say P rises above 105 but we cannot specify by exactly how much. The gap is now $1,200 billion − $900 billion = $300 billion, so it has been partially but not fully closed. Half the gap remains.

(c) A $700 billion increase would shift AD right by $700 billion. If the price level were unchanged, Y would rise to $600 billion + $700 billion = $1,300 billion. But Y cannot exceed Yf = $1,200 billion in real terms. The economy hits the vertical segment at Yf. The portion of the AD increase that pushes Y beyond $1,200 billion — the final $100 billion of the $700 billion — translates entirely into a higher price level with no additional real output. The new equilibrium is at Y = $1,200 billion (the gap is fully closed) and P is above the level in part (b) because excess demand pressure has driven prices higher. This illustrates a key Keynesian insight: stimulus beyond what is needed to reach Yf is wasted on inflation.

Answer 4. (a) "Sticky downward" means that nominal wages resist falling even when labour market conditions — high unemployment — would, in a flexible-price model, push them down. Wages may stay flat (zero change) or rise slowly, but they do not fall in nominal terms.

(b) Three reasons wages may not fall: (1) Labour contracts fix wages for one to three years, and renegotiation takes time. (2) Efficiency wages — firms pay above the market-clearing level to maintain productivity, morale, and low turnover; they will not cut wages even when unemployment is high because doing so would harm worker quality and effort. (3) Worker resistance and morale — workers perceive nominal wage cuts as deeply unfair, even if the price level has fallen. A firm that cuts wages may face strikes, sabotage, or the departure of its best workers. Truman Bewley's research interviewed hundreds of managers and found that almost none would impose nominal wage cuts during recessions for precisely these morale reasons.

(c) Sticky wages break the monetarist self-correction chain. In the monetarist model: recession → high unemployment → falling wages → lower costs → SRAS shifts right → back to Yf. If wages do not fall, costs do not fall, SRAS does not shift, and the economy stays stuck below Yf. The price level does not fall either (because costs have not fallen), so none of the automatic stabilisers — wealth effect, interest rate effect, international trade effect — are triggered. The economy remains in a recessionary equilibrium indefinitely.

Answer 5. (Model answer for 10-mark IB question)

The Keynesian aggregate supply curve describes the relationship between the total quantity of goods and services firms are willing to produce and the average price level, under the assumption that nominal wages are sticky downward and that the economy can operate at less than full employment for extended periods.

Unlike the monetarist AS, which is either upward-sloping (SRAS) or vertical (LRAS), the Keynesian AS has three distinct segments, each corresponding to a different state of the economy.

The first segment is perfectly horizontal. This represents an economy in deep recession with substantial spare capacity — high unemployment, idle factories, and unused resources. In this range, firms can expand output without facing rising costs because they can hire unemployed workers at the going wage and restart idle equipment. An increase in aggregate demand in this segment increases real GDP without any increase in the price level. Keynes argued that the Great Depression represented an economy stuck in this horizontal range.

The second segment is upward-sloping. As output expands, spare capacity diminishes and bottlenecks begin to appear — specific skills or inputs become scarce even as other resources remain abundant. Firms must bid up wages for workers with the right skills or pay premiums for scarce materials. Costs begin to rise, so the price level rises along with output. An increase in AD in this segment raises both real GDP and the price level, with the split depending on how close the economy is to full capacity.

The third segment is perfectly vertical at Yf, the full employment level of output. At this point, all resources are fully employed at normal rates, and no further increase in real output is possible. Any increase in AD produces only a higher price level — pure demand-pull inflation. This segment corresponds to the monetarist LRAS.

The determination of equilibrium output: aggregate demand intersects the AS curve at whichever segment the economy is currently in. Crucially, equilibrium can occur in the horizontal segment, with Y substantially below Yf. This is a recessionary gap, and unlike in the monetarist model, there is no automatic mechanism that closes it. Because nominal wages are sticky downward, the price level does not fall, and the economy can remain in this underemployment equilibrium indefinitely. Only an increase in AD — through government spending, tax cuts, or monetary expansion — can move the economy back toward Yf.

The diagram should show the three-segment AS curve with AD intersecting it in the horizontal range, with Yf marked to the right of the current equilibrium, and the recessionary gap clearly labeled. Axes are "Price Level" (vertical) and "Real GDP" (horizontal).

A common pitfall in answering this question is to treat the three segments as three separate curves rather than one continuous curve. Another is to claim that the horizontal segment implies scarcity does not exist — scarcity always exists; the horizontal segment reflects resources that exist but are not being used because of insufficient demand.

Answer 6. (a) The output gap is Yf − Y = $2,500 billion − $1,500 billion = $1,000 billion. As a percentage of Yf: ($1,000 billion / $2,500 billion) × 100 = 40 percent. This is a severe recessionary gap.

(b) The AD increase of $600 billion shifts AD right. Since the economy is in the horizontal segment, the entire $600 billion translates into higher real GDP with no price increase. New Y = $1,500 billion + $600 billion = $2,100 billion. Since $2,100 billion is between $1,800 billion (end of horizontal segment) and $2,500 billion (Yf), the economy is now in the upward-sloping segment. The price level rises above 110, but we cannot specify the exact level without more information about the slope.

(c) The monetarist self-correction mechanism: high unemployment → downward pressure on nominal wages → lower labour costs → SRAS shifts right → Y returns to Yf, P falls. However, in this scenario, the gap is enormous — 40 percent of Yf. Even if wages could fall, the required wage decline would be catastrophically large. A 40 percent fall in nominal wages is politically and socially impossible — it would trigger mass defaults on debts, bank failures, and social unrest. The Keynesian argument is precisely that self-correction at this scale is either impossible or would take decades, during which the human cost in lost output and unemployment would be unacceptable. Moreover, the monetarist model assumes competitive labour markets with flexible wages — an assumption that does not hold in most real economies where contracts, minimum wages, efficiency wages, and worker morale constrain nominal wage cuts. In this scenario, the Keynesian case for government stimulus is strong: the economy is deeply depressed, spare capacity is abundant, and the cost of waiting is enormous.

Evaluate

Question: "The Keynesian model is more realistic than the monetarist model in describing how actual economies behave." Evaluate this statement.

Model Answer:

The statement raises a fundamental debate in macroeconomics that has persisted since the 1930s. Both models capture important truths about real economies, and the question of which is "more realistic" depends on the time horizon, the economic context, and the specific question being asked.

The Keynesian model's core realism lies in its recognition that nominal wages are, in fact, sticky downward. The empirical evidence for downward nominal wage rigidity is overwhelming. Studies of wage-change distributions across dozens of countries consistently find a sharp spike at zero and very few observations of nominal wage cuts. Truman Bewley's research, based on extensive interviews with managers, confirmed that firms avoid nominal wage cuts because they devastate worker morale and productivity. The Keynesian model's implication — that economies can remain below full employment for extended periods — is confirmed by historical experience. The Great Depression saw US unemployment above 14 percent for a decade. Japan's "lost decades" after 1990 involved persistent output gaps. The Eurozone after 2010 took years to recover pre-crisis output. The Keynesian model explains these episodes; the monetarist model struggles to account for why self-correction took so long.

The Keynesian model also captures the reality that inflation can emerge before the economy reaches full employment, through bottlenecks. The upward-sloping segment of the Keynesian AS explains why we sometimes observe rising prices alongside significant unemployment — a phenomenon the simple monetarist dichotomy cannot easily accommodate.

However, the monetarist model has its own claims to realism. In the very long run, economies do tend to grow at rates determined by supply-side factors — resources, technology, institutions — rather than by the level of aggregate demand. Across decades, the correlation between money supply growth and inflation is close to one, while the correlation between money supply growth and real GDP growth is close to zero. The monetarist emphasis on long-run monetary neutrality is well supported. Moreover, in economies with very flexible labour markets — such as the United States in the 1990s or the United Kingdom after the labour market reforms of the 1980s — the self-correction mechanism does appear to operate more quickly, lending some support to the monetarist view that labour market flexibility matters.

The Keynesian model also has weaknesses. Its assumption of completely sticky downward wages is too strong. Wages are sticky, but they are not frozen. Over time, even in rigid labour markets, real wages adjust through inflation eroding the purchasing power of nominally fixed wages, or through new hires being brought in at lower wages while existing workers keep their old rates. The model also arguably underpredicts the economy's ability to recover from recessions without massive government intervention — many recessions have ended without the kind of fiscal expansion Keynes advocated.

The most realistic view is probably a synthesis: the monetarist model describes the long-run destination of the economy reasonably well, while the Keynesian model describes the short-run and medium-run dynamics better, especially during deep recessions. This is close to the "new Keynesian" synthesis that dominates modern central banking: in the long run, money is neutral and output is supply-determined; in the short run, sticky prices and wages mean that demand matters and policy can be effective.

The statement also needs qualification by context. For an economy in a mild downturn with flexible labour markets, the monetarist model's self-correction story may be broadly right, and government intervention may indeed be unnecessary or counterproductive. For an economy in a deep depression with rigid labour markets, the Keynesian model is far more realistic, and the monetarist prescription of waiting for self-correction would be catastrophic.

In conclusion, neither model has a monopoly on realism. The Keynesian model is more realistic in acknowledging sticky wages, the possibility of persistent underemployment equilibrium, and the role of bottlenecks in generating inflation. The monetarist model is more realistic in its account of long-run growth and the ultimate neutrality of money. The most defensible position is that the Keynesian model better describes short-run and crisis economics, while the monetarist model better describes long-run trend economics. The art of macroeconomic policy lies in knowing which model applies to the situation at hand.
