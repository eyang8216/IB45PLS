Lesson 41: Benefits of International Trade

What You'll Learn

  • Why countries engage in international trade
  • The concepts of absolute advantage and comparative advantage
  • How opportunity cost determines comparative advantage
  • The gains from trade: how specialisation benefits all countries
  • How to use linear PPCs to illustrate gains from trade
  • The sources of comparative advantage
  • The limitations and criticisms of comparative advantage theory

    • 1. Why Countries Trade

    International trade — the exchange of goods and services across national borders — is a fundamental feature of the global economy. Countries trade for several reasons:

    a) Access to Goods They Cannot Produce

    Some goods cannot be produced domestically due to climate, natural resource endowments, or technology. The UK cannot grow bananas; Japan has limited oil reserves. Trade provides access to these goods.

    b) Cheaper Goods

    Even if a country CAN produce a good, another country may produce it more cheaply — due to lower labour costs, more advanced technology, abundant resources, or economies of scale. Consumers benefit from lower prices.

    c) Greater Variety

    Trade gives consumers access to a wider range of products — different types of cheese, wine, cars, electronics — than domestic production alone could provide.

    d) Economies of Scale

    By selling to a global market, firms can produce at a larger scale, reducing average costs. A car manufacturer serving only the domestic market may not reach minimum efficient scale; exporting allows it to do so. Consumers everywhere benefit from lower costs.

    e) Increased Competition and Efficiency

    Trade exposes domestic firms to foreign competition, forcing them to be more efficient and innovative. This is the supply-side benefit of trade.

    2. Absolute Advantage

    Definition: A country has an absolute advantage in the production of a good if it can produce that good using fewer resources (or produce more output with the same resources) than another country.

    Worked Example:

    Consider two countries (France and Italy) and two goods (wine and cheese). Each country has 100 units of labour.

    CountryWine (litres per 100 labour units)Cheese (kg per 100 labour units)
    France1,000500
    Italy800600
    • France has an absolute advantage in wine: with the same labour, France produces 1,000 litres vs Italy's 800 litres.
    • Italy has an absolute advantage in cheese: 600 kg vs France's 500 kg.

    According to absolute advantage, France should specialise in wine and Italy in cheese. But what if one country has an absolute advantage in BOTH goods? This is where comparative advantage becomes essential.

    3. Comparative Advantage (SL Level — Conceptual Understanding)

    Definition: A country has a comparative advantage in producing a good if it can produce that good at a lower opportunity cost than another country.

    Comparative advantage is the fundamental insight of David Ricardo (1817). It shows that even if a country has an absolute disadvantage in everything, both countries can still benefit from trade.

    The key principle: Countries should specialise in producing the goods in which they have a comparative advantage (lowest opportunity cost) and trade for other goods.

    Calculating Opportunity Cost

    The opportunity cost of producing one unit of a good is the quantity of the other good that must be sacrificed.

    Worked Example 1: Two countries, two goods.

    CountryOutput per worker per day
    England105
    Portugal128

    Portugal has an absolute advantage in BOTH goods (12 > 10 cloth, 8 > 5 wine). Does this mean Portugal has no reason to trade with England? No!

    Opportunity costs in England:

    • To produce 1 metre of cloth, England gives up 5/10 = 0.5 litres of wine.
    • To produce 1 litre of wine, England gives up 10/5 = 2 metres of cloth.

    Opportunity costs in Portugal:

    • To produce 1 metre of cloth, Portugal gives up 8/12 = 0.67 litres of wine.
    • To produce 1 litre of wine, Portugal gives up 12/8 = 1.5 metres of cloth.

    Comparative advantage:

    • Cloth: England's opportunity cost (0.5 wine) < Portugal's (0.67 wine) → England has comparative advantage in cloth.
    • Wine: Portugal's opportunity cost (1.5 cloth) < England's (2 cloth) → Portugal has comparative advantage in wine.

    The counterintuitive result: Even though Portugal is more efficient at both goods, it should specialise in wine (where its relative advantage is greatest) and England should specialise in cloth (where its relative disadvantage is smallest). Then they trade — and BOTH are better off!

    4. Gains from Trade: The PPC Diagram

    Trade allows a country to consume beyond its Production Possibility Curve (PPC). Without trade, a country can only consume what it produces — constrained by its PPC. With trade, it can specialise and exchange, achieving a consumption combination outside its PPC.

    Diagram — Linear PPC with Trade:

    
Wine (litres)
    ↑
    |\
    | \  PPC (without trade)
C*  |  ●  ← consumption point WITH trade (outside PPC)
    |   \
    |    \
C   |     ● ← consumption point without trade
    |      \
    |       \
    +--------+-----→ Cloth (metres)
    P         P*

    Before trade: The country produces and consumes at point (P, C) on its PPC. After trade: The country specialises in its comparative advantage good, produces at (P, 0) — an endpoint of the PPC. It exports some of this good in exchange for the other good. The consumption point (C) lies OUTSIDE the PPC — the country consumes more of both goods than it could produce domestically.

    Key insight: The slope of the PPC represents the opportunity cost. When the world terms of trade (the exchange rate between the two goods) lies BETWEEN the two countries' opportunity costs, both gain from trade.

    5. Sources of Comparative Advantage

    Why do countries have different opportunity costs? The sources of comparative advantage include:

    a) Factor Endowments

    Countries are endowed with different quantities and qualities of factors of production:

    • Land: Australia has abundant agricultural land → comparative advantage in wheat, wool.
    • Labour: Bangladesh has abundant low-cost labour → comparative advantage in garments.
    • Capital: Germany has abundant capital → comparative advantage in high-tech manufacturing.
    • Natural resources: Saudi Arabia has oil; Chile has copper.

      • This is the Heckscher-Ohlin theory: countries export goods that intensively use their abundant factors and import goods that intensively use their scarce factors.

      b) Technology

      Differences in technological knowledge and capabilities create comparative advantage. The US has a technological lead in pharmaceuticals and software; Japan in robotics and precision engineering.

      c) Climate and Geography

      Climate determines what can be grown. Tropical countries have a comparative advantage in coffee, bananas, and cocoa. Countries with deep natural harbours have advantages in shipping and logistics (Singapore, Netherlands).

      d) Human Capital

      Countries with a highly educated and skilled workforce have an advantage in knowledge-intensive industries (e.g., Switzerland in pharmaceuticals and finance).

      e) Historical Accident and First-Mover Advantage

      Sometimes comparative advantage emerges from historical circumstances — Silicon Valley's tech cluster, London's financial centre — rather than inherent factor endowments. Path dependency matters.

      6. Limitations of Comparative Advantage Theory

      The theory, while powerful, relies on simplifying assumptions that may not hold in practice.

      Unrealistic Assumptions

      • Perfect factor mobility: Assumes resources can easily move from one industry to another. In reality, a textile worker made redundant by imports cannot instantly become a software engineer — structural unemployment results.
      • Constant returns to scale: Assumes no economies of scale. In reality, specialisation may create increasing returns — the more you produce, the lower the average cost — reinforcing existing advantages.
      • No transport costs or trade barriers: Real-world trade faces shipping costs, tariffs, quotas, and regulatory barriers that can eliminate the gains from comparative advantage.
      • Full employment: The model assumes all resources are employed. If specialisation creates unemployment in some sectors, the net welfare effect is less clear.

        • Strategic Industries

        Some industries may have strategic importance (national defence, food security) that justifies domestic production even without comparative advantage. No country wants to be entirely dependent on imports for its food or energy supply.

        Over-Specialisation Risk

        Specialising heavily in one product makes a country vulnerable to demand shocks, price volatility, and technological obsolescence. Countries dependent on a single commodity export (e.g., Zambia — copper; Venezuela — oil) experience "Dutch disease" and extreme economic volatility.

        Dynamic Comparative Advantage

        Comparative advantage is not static. Today's disadvantage may become tomorrow's advantage through investment in education, infrastructure, and technology. South Korea moved from labour-intensive manufacturing to high-tech industries by deliberately building comparative advantage. Infant industry protection may be justified.

        Distribution of Gains

        Even if total gains from trade are positive, the gains may be very unevenly distributed within a country. Workers in import-competing industries may lose their jobs and suffer long-term income losses, while consumers and export-industry workers gain. This creates political resistance to free trade.

        IB Exam Tip

        When asked about comparative advantage:

        1. Always calculate opportunity costs — show the working step by step.
        2. Identify who has the comparative advantage in each good.
        3. State the pattern of specialisation: "Country A should specialise in X, Country B in Y."
        4. Show the PPC diagram with the trade consumption point outside the PPC.
        5. For the Evaluate question, always mention at least two limitations (transport costs, factor immobility, over-specialisation risk, strategic industries, or distributional concerns).

        Answer 1: Absolute Advantage

        • Rice: Vietnam produces 8 tonnes per worker vs Cambodia's 5 tonnes → Vietnam has absolute advantage in rice.
        • Textiles: Cambodia produces 15 metres per worker vs Vietnam's 12 metres → Cambodia has absolute advantage in textiles.

        Both countries have an absolute advantage in different goods, so according to absolute advantage theory, they should each specialise in their respective advantaged good and trade. But even if one country had absolute advantage in BOTH, trade could still be beneficial (see comparative advantage).

        Answer 2: Opportunity Cost and Comparative Advantage

        (a) Opportunity cost of 1 tonne of rice:

        • Vietnam: To produce 8 tonnes of rice, Vietnam gives up 12 metres of textiles. So 1 tonne of rice costs 12/8 = 1.5 metres of textiles.
        • Cambodia: To produce 5 tonnes of rice, Cambodia gives up 15 metres of textiles. So 1 tonne of rice costs 15/5 = 3 metres of textiles.

        (b) Opportunity cost of 1 metre of textiles:

        • Vietnam: 12 metres cost 8 tonnes of rice → 1 metre costs 8/12 = 0.67 tonnes of rice.
        • Cambodia: 15 metres cost 5 tonnes of rice → 1 metre costs 5/15 = 0.33 tonnes of rice.

        (c) Comparative advantage:

        • Rice: Vietnam's opportunity cost (1.5 textiles) < Cambodia's (3 textiles) → Vietnam has comparative advantage in rice.
        • Textiles: Cambodia's opportunity cost (0.33 rice) < Vietnam's (0.67 rice) → Cambodia has comparative advantage in textiles.

        (d) Pattern of specialisation: Vietnam should specialise in rice, Cambodia should specialise in textiles. Then they trade — Vietnam exports rice and imports textiles; Cambodia does the reverse.

          Answer 5: Three Limitations

          1. Transport costs and trade barriers: The theory assumes zero transport costs and no trade barriers. In reality, shipping goods across the world incurs significant costs, and tariffs/quotas alter relative prices. A country might have a comparative advantage in cement production, but the transport cost of shipping heavy, low-value cement may exceed the production cost advantage, making trade uneconomical. Similarly, a 25% tariff can wipe out a 20% comparative cost advantage.
          1. Factor immobility and structural unemployment: The theory assumes resources can move seamlessly from one industry to another. In reality, when a country specialises and its import-competing industry shrinks, workers may not be able to easily transition to the expanding export sector. A steelworker laid off when steel imports rise cannot quickly become a software developer. The result is structural unemployment — a cost the theory ignores. The "Rust Belt" in the US illustrates this: comparative-advantage-driven deindustrialisation left communities with persistent unemployment and decline.
          1. Over-specialisation and vulnerability: The theory encourages maximum specialisation, but this creates vulnerability. A country specialising in a single commodity (e.g., Zambia with copper, which constitutes ~70% of export earnings) is exposed to global price volatility. If copper prices collapse, the entire economy suffers. Diversification provides insurance, even if it sacrifices some static efficiency gains. Similarly, a country specialising in tourism (like some Caribbean nations) is vulnerable to external shocks — a global pandemic, a hurricane — that can devastate the economy.

              • Evaluate: CLASPP Model Answer (150–200 words)

              "Free trade always benefits all countries involved." Evaluate.

              Conclusion: The theory of comparative advantage demonstrates that free trade can generate net welfare gains for participating countries by allowing specialisation and consumption beyond domestic production possibilities. However, the claim that free trade "always" benefits "all" countries is too absolute — both the theory and real-world evidence reveal important caveats.

              Limitations: The theory relies on unrealistic assumptions — zero transport costs, perfect factor mobility, constant returns to scale, and no trade barriers. In reality, workers displaced by imports cannot instantly transition to export sectors, leading to structural unemployment and concentrated losses in import-competing regions. Transport costs and tariffs can eliminate theoretical gains. The distribution of gains within countries is highly uneven — some groups may be worse off even if the country as a whole gains.

              Assumptions: The claim assumes that comparative advantage is static and that specialisation patterns determined by current endowments will always be optimal. But comparative advantage is dynamic — countries can build new advantages through investment in education and technology. Free trade may lock countries into low-value-added production.

              Stakeholders: Consumers and export-industry workers gain from cheaper goods and expanded markets. Import-competing workers lose jobs and may face long-term income decline. Developing countries with weak institutions may be exploited through unequal terms of trade.

              Pros/Cons: Free trade promotes efficiency, innovation, and consumer choice. But it also creates losers, increases vulnerability to external shocks (over-specialisation), and may threaten strategic industries.

              Priorities: The optimal policy is not unfettered free trade, but managed liberalisation with complementary policies — retraining programmes, social safety nets, and selective protection for infant industries — to ensure gains are broadly shared and transition costs are mitigated.
