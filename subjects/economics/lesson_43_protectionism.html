## 4. Contractionary Fiscal Policy: Raising taxes or reducing government spending to lower aggregate demand and thus imports. This is politically painful — it reduces domestic incomes and increases unemployment. For a country already in recession with a current account deficit, this is a dangerous prescription (procyclical). 3. Contractionary Monetary Policy: Higher interest rates attract capital inflows (financial account) and reduce domestic spending and imports. But higher rates also discourage investment and may appreciate the currency, hurting export competitiveness. 3. Supply-Side Policies: Improve domestic productivity and competitiveness — education, infrastructure, labour market reforms, R&D incentives. These address the root cause of a deficit (lack of competitiveness) but work slowly. This is the only sustainable long-term solution. 4. Expenditure-Switching Policies: Policies that redirect domestic and foreign spending toward domestic goods: - **Tariffs and quotas**: Reduce imports directly but violate WTO rules and invite retaliation. - **Depreciation/Devaluation**: Makes exports cheaper and imports more expensive, switching expenditure toward domestic goods. Requires a floating (or adjustable peg) exchange rate. --- ## IB Exam Tip On Paper 2 (data response) and Paper 1 (essay), you must be able to **calculate** current account balances from data and **interpret** whether a deficit is problematic. (ii) **Trade balance in services** = 95 − 70 = **+$25 billion** (surplus). (iii) **Primary income balance** = 40 − 65 = **−$25 billion** (deficit). (iv) **Secondary income balance** = 15 − 25 = **−$10 billion** (deficit). (b) **Current account balance** = (−60) + 25 + (−25) + (−10) = **−$70 billion** (deficit). (c) **Financial account (excluding reserves)**: Net private capital = Inflows − Outflows = (80 + 30) − (45 + 60) = 110 − 105 = **+$5 billion** surplus. (d) **Overall balance**: Current account (−$70B) + Financial account (+$5B) + Reserve assets (+$25B as a credit because acquiring reserves is a use of funds, i.e., recorded as debit of −$25B) + Errors and omissions = 0. −70 + 5 − 25 + E&O = 0 → −90 + E&O = 0 → **E&O = +$90 billion**. The accounts must balance to zero; any discrepancy is captured in "net errors and omissions." ### Answer 2 — Interpreting a Current Account Deficit (a) Delta's deficit may **not** be a problem because: - It finances productive investment (FDI in manufacturing/technology) that increases future productive capacity. - GDP growth of 4.5% suggests investment is generating returns. - FDI is stable, long-term financing — less prone to sudden reversal than portfolio flows. - Future returns from investments will generate income to service foreign obligations. (b) It could become a problem if FDI projects fail to generate expected returns (overinvestment), or if cumulative foreign liabilities grow so large that profit repatriation worsens the current account further. (c) **Epsilon's deficit is more concerning**: Short-term portfolio flows are "hot money" that can reverse quickly in a crisis. Financing consumption (not investment) means no future productive capacity. Epsilon is living beyond its means unsustainably, while Delta is borrowing to build assets. ### Answer 3 — Policy Analysis (a) Problems with tariffs: 1. **WTO violation**: Unilateral tariff increases breach bound commitments, inviting retaliation. 2. **Retaliation risk**: Trading partners may impose counter-tariffs on Zeta's exports, reducing exports. 3. **Domestic costs**: Higher input costs for domestic manufacturers using imported components and higher consumer prices. 4. **Symptom, not cause**: Tariffs don't address the root cause of the deficit. (b) Alternatives: (1) Allow currency depreciation to improve trade balance via expenditure-switching. (2) Supply-side policies (education, infrastructure, R&D) to improve long-run competitiveness. (c) Depreciation makes exports cheaper (quantity demanded rises) and imports more expensive (quantity demanded falls). Net exports improve if the **Marshall-Lerner condition** holds: |PEDx| + |PEDm| > 1. ### Answer 4 — The "Twin Deficits" (a) Y = C + I + G + (X − M). If G rises, ceteris paribus, Y rises and M rises, reducing (X − M). (b) Rearranging: (X − M) = (S_private − I) + (T − G). A fall in (T − G) — a larger budget deficit — directly reduces the current account if private saving and investment are unchanged. (c) Twin deficits may not hold: **Ricardian equivalence** (consumers save more anticipating future tax rises → S_private rises); **crowding out** (deficits raise interest rates, reducing I, which improves current account); private saving may respond to offset public dissaving. Empirically, the US had budget surpluses and current account deficits in the late 1990s. ### Answer 5 — Remittances and the Current Account (a) Current account = Trade balance (−$12B) + Primary income (−$3B) + Secondary income (+$18B) = **+$3 billion (surplus)**. (b) Risks: (1) Vulnerability to recessions in host countries reducing remittances. (2) Policy changes in host countries (immigration rules, remittance taxes). (3) Brain drain — emigration of skilled workers may cost more than remittances gained. (4) Reduces urgency of addressing trade competitiveness. (c) Global recession in host countries → lower employment/incomes for diaspora → reduced remittances → current account surplus shrinks or becomes deficit → currency pressure and reduced ability to finance imports. ### Answer 6 — IB-Style Essay (Model Answer Outline) **Introduction**: A current account deficit — where outflows exceed inflows in trade, income, and transfers — is not inherently a weakness signal. Its significance depends on size, persistence, financing, and use of borrowed funds. **When it IS weakness**: Structural/persistent deficits (lack of competitiveness); financing consumption (Greece pre-2009); reliance on hot money (Asian Financial Crisis 1997); unsustainable foreign debt trajectories. **When it is NOT weakness**: Financing productive FDI (late-1990s US); catching-up growth importing capital goods (South Korea 1970s–80s); temporary/cyclical deficits; US "exorbitant privilege" (dollar as reserve currency enables sustained deficits). **Evaluation (CLASPP)**: - **Conclusion**: Deficit is a symptom, not a disease. Cause and financing determine significance. - **Limitations**: Identity means deficits always have capital inflow counterpart; composition matters. - **Assumptions**: Not all imports are "bad" — capital goods raise productivity. Ignores intertemporal trade logic. - **Stakeholders**: Consumers gain (cheaper imports); export sectors may lose; foreign investors gain assets. - **Priorities**: Sustainability matters more than existence. Small stable deficits are benign; large growing ones dangerous. - **Policy alternatives**: Address root causes via supply-side reform, not protectionism. US and Australia demonstrate sustainable deficits are possible under the right conditions. **Final conclusion**: "Always" is demonstrably false. The key distinction is investment versus consumption financing. Policymakers should focus on sustainability and composition, not merely the existence of a deficit. --- ## Evaluate ### "A current account deficit is always a sign of economic weakness." Evaluate. The claim that a current account deficit is *always* a sign of economic weakness reflects a mercantilist fallacy that equates trade deficits with national failure. While deficits can indeed signal deep structural problems, the blanket assertion ignores the crucial distinction between deficits that finance consumption and those that finance productive investment. **Conclusion**: A current account deficit is a symptom of underlying economic forces, not inherently a marker of weakness. Its significance depends entirely on cause, composition, and context. **Limitations**: The mercantilist view treats the current account in isolation, ignoring the balance of payments identity. A deficit is necessarily matched by a financial account surplus — capital inflows. These inflows are not inherently harmful; if they finance investment in productive capacity, they represent the international allocation of saving to its most productive uses, exactly as economic theory prescribes. **Assumptions challenged**: The claim assumes all imports represent "lost" domestic production. In reality, imports of capital goods and intermediate inputs raise domestic productivity. Furthermore, the claim assumes deficits are permanent; many are cyclical, self-correcting through exchange rate adjustment. The Marshall-Lerner condition and J-curve dynamics demonstrate that deficits can be transitory. **Stakeholder analysis**: Different stakeholders experience deficits differently. Consumers benefit from cheaper and more varied imports. Export-competing industries may suffer. Foreign investors gain by acquiring domestic assets. The net welfare effect depends on whether the capital inflows are productively deployed — benefiting future consumers through higher growth — or wasted on consumption that must eventually be repaid. **Priorities and trade-offs**: Sustainability should be the primary concern, not the deficit's mere existence. A 2% deficit that is stable and finances productive FDI is benign. A 7% deficit financed by short-term debt to fund a consumption boom is a crisis in waiting. The policy priority should be to ensure the composition of capital inflows supports productive capacity, not to eliminate the deficit. **Policy alternatives**: Rather than targeting the deficit directly through protectionism (which reduces both imports and exports and harms consumers), policymakers should address root causes through supply-side reforms (improving productivity and competitiveness), maintaining a competitive exchange rate, and ensuring fiscal discipline to avoid twin deficits. A well-managed economy can sustain modest current account deficits indefinitely, as the United States and Australia have demonstrated for decades. (199 words)
