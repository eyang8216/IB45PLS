## 1. Income Elasticity of Demand (YED) — What It Is and Why It Matters

### The Core Idea

When a country's economy grows and average incomes rise, households do not simply buy more of everything in equal proportion. They dramatically increase their spending on some goods — restaurant meals, foreign holidays, new cars — while actually reducing their spending on others — supermarket own-brand baked beans, bus travel, second-hand furniture. Understanding which goods fall into which category is essential for businesses planning their future and for governments forecasting tax revenue and demand for public services.

**Income elasticity of demand** (YED) is the measure that captures this. YED tells you how responsive the quantity demanded of a good is to a change in consumer income. It is calculated as:

$$\text{YED} = \frac{\text{Percentage change in quantity demanded}}{\text{Percentage change in income}} = \frac{\%\Delta Q_d}{\%\Delta Y}$$

Where Y stands for income.

### Why YED Matters

YED matters for three main reasons. First, it allows firms to forecast how demand for their products will evolve as the economy grows or shrinks. A luxury car manufacturer expects sales to grow faster than GDP during an economic expansion — and to fall faster during a recession. Second, it helps governments plan long-term infrastructure: if healthcare has a high YED, an ageing and growing population will demand proportionally more healthcare, requiring more hospitals and staff. Third, it explains structural changes in the economy as countries develop — the shift from agriculture to manufacturing to services that occurs as incomes rise.

### Unlike PED, the Sign of YED Matters

With PED, the sign is always negative because demand curves slope downward, so economists typically use the absolute value. With YED, the sign is just as important as the magnitude. A positive YED means demand rises when income rises — the good is a **normal good**. A negative YED means demand falls when income rises — the good is an **inferior good**.

This distinction is fundamental. It tells us not just how much demand changes, but in what direction.

## 2. Classifying Goods by YED

Economists classify goods into three categories based on YED.

| YED value | Classification | What happens when income rises |
|-----------|----------------|-------------------------------|
| YED > 1 | Luxury good (a type of normal good) | Demand rises by a larger percentage than income |
| 0 < YED < 1 | Necessity (a type of normal good) | Demand rises but by a smaller percentage than income |
| YED < 0 | Inferior good | Demand falls when income rises |
| YED = 0 | Perfectly income-inelastic | Demand is unaffected by income changes |

### Normal Goods (YED > 0)

A **normal good** is one for which demand increases when consumer income increases. Most goods are normal goods. Within this broad category, economists distinguish between necessities and luxuries based on the magnitude of YED.

### Necessities (0 < YED < 1)

A **necessity** is a normal good for which demand rises less than proportionally with income. If your income rises by 10%, your spending on electricity might rise by only 3%. You use a bit more — perhaps you heat your home more comfortably or run the air conditioning more — but most of your electricity consumption is non-negotiable. YED for electricity is approximately 0.3.

Other examples of necessities include basic food items (bread, milk), water, and public transport for commuting. Engel's Law — one of the most robust empirical findings in economics — states that as income rises, the proportion of income spent on food falls. This is precisely because food has a YED between 0 and 1: absolute spending on food rises, but it rises more slowly than income, so its budget share shrinks.

### Luxuries (YED > 1)

A **luxury good** is a normal good for which demand rises more than proportionally with income. If your income rises by 10%, your spending on overseas holidays might rise by 25%. You do not just take the same holiday and pay more; you take a longer holiday, to a more distant destination, in a better hotel. YED for overseas holidays might be around 2.5.

Other examples of luxury goods include fine dining, designer clothing, premium cars, and private education. These are the goods that boom during economic expansions and slump during recessions. They are disproportionately sensitive to the business cycle.

### Inferior Goods (YED < 0)

An **inferior good** is one for which demand falls when income rises. As consumers become richer, they switch away from these goods toward higher-quality substitutes. Bus travel in cities with good metro systems is a classic example: as incomes rise, commuters switch from buses to trains, taxis, or private cars. Demand for bus travel falls even as total travel increases.

Other examples include supermarket own-brand basic ranges (consumers switch to branded or premium products), instant noodles (consumers switch to fresh pasta or restaurant meals), and second-hand clothing (consumers switch to new clothing).

The label "inferior" is an economic term of art. It does not mean the good is of poor quality in any absolute sense. It means only that consumers choose to buy less of it as they become wealthier. Bus travel can be an excellent service, but it is an inferior good if richer people choose alternatives.

### Worked Example 1: YED for Restaurant Meals

A consumer's annual income rises from £30,000 to £36,000. Her annual spending on restaurant meals rises from £2,000 to £2,600. The average price of a restaurant meal is unchanged, so the quantity of meals she buys is proportional to her spending.

**Approach:** Calculate the percentage change in income and the percentage change in quantity demanded, then divide.

**Step 1:** Percentage change in income.

%ΔY = (£36,000 − £30,000) / £30,000 × 100 = £6,000 / £30,000 × 100 = 20%.

**Step 2:** Percentage change in quantity demanded. Since prices are constant, spending change equals quantity change.

%ΔQd = (£2,600 − £2,000) / £2,000 × 100 = £600 / £2,000 × 100 = 30%.

**Step 3:** YED = 30% / 20% = +1.5.

**Why this makes sense:** YED is positive, so restaurant meals are a normal good. YED > 1, so restaurant meals are specifically a luxury good. When this consumer's income rose by 20%, her restaurant spending rose by 30%. She treated her extra income as an opportunity to upgrade her lifestyle, directing a disproportionate share of the increase toward restaurant dining.

## 3. Engel Curves and Engel's Law

### What an Engel Curve Is

An **Engel curve** is a graph that shows the relationship between a consumer's income and the quantity of a particular good that the consumer demands. It is named after the 19th-century German statistician Ernst Engel.

For a **necessity**, the Engel curve slopes upward but gets flatter as income rises. The consumer buys more of the good as income increases, but the rate of increase slows down. Spending on food follows this pattern.

For a **luxury**, the Engel curve slopes upward and gets steeper as income rises. The consumer buys disproportionately more as income grows.

For an **inferior good**, the Engel curve eventually slopes downward. At low incomes, the consumer may buy more of the good as income rises from very low levels. But beyond some income threshold, the consumer switches to superior substitutes and demand for the inferior good falls.

### Engel's Law

**Engel's Law** states that as a household's income rises, the proportion of its income spent on food falls, even though the absolute amount spent on food may rise. This is one of the most consistently observed empirical regularities in economics. It holds across countries, across time periods, and across income levels.

Engel's Law has profound implications. As countries develop and incomes grow, agriculture becomes a smaller share of the economy. Farmers must become more productive just to maintain their incomes, because the demand for their output grows more slowly than the economy as a whole. This structural transformation — from agriculture to industry to services — is driven in part by Engel's Law and the low YED of food.

## 4. Cross Elasticity of Demand (XED) — What It Is and Why It Matters

### The Core Idea

The demand for one good can depend on the price of another good. If the price of Pepsi rises, consumers buy more Coca-Cola. If the price of printers rises, consumers buy fewer printer ink cartridges. If the price of shoes changes, the demand for milk is unaffected.

**Cross elasticity of demand** (XED) measures the responsiveness of the quantity demanded of one good (Good A) to a change in the price of another good (Good B). It is calculated as:

$$\text{XED} = \frac{\%\Delta \text{ in quantity demanded of Good A}}{\%\Delta \text{ in price of Good B}}$$

### Why XED Matters

XED matters because it tells firms and competition authorities about the relationships between goods. A firm that knows the XED between its product and a competitor's product can predict how its sales will change if the competitor changes prices. A competition authority assessing a proposed merger between two firms will examine the XED between their products: if the XED is high and positive, the products are close substitutes, and the merger might significantly reduce competition. A firm that sells complementary products — razors and blades, game consoles and games — uses XED to optimise its pricing across the product range.

### Unlike PED, the Sign of XED Is Everything

With XED, the sign tells you the nature of the relationship between the two goods.

| XED value | Relationship | What it means |
|-----------|-------------|---------------|
| XED > 0 | Substitutes | A rise in the price of Good B increases demand for Good A |
| XED < 0 | Complements | A rise in the price of Good B decreases demand for Good A |
| XED ≈ 0 | Unrelated goods | Price changes in Good B have no effect on demand for Good A |

The magnitude of XED tells you the **strength** of the relationship. A large magnitude (|XED| well above 1) means the goods are closely linked — they are close substitutes or strong complements. A small magnitude (|XED| close to 0) means the relationship is weak — the goods are distant substitutes, weak complements, or effectively unrelated.

### Substitutes (XED > 0)

Two goods are **substitutes** if an increase in the price of one causes consumers to buy more of the other. Consumers switch from the now-more-expensive good to its alternative.

Consider butter and margarine. If the price of butter rises by 20%, some consumers switch to margarine. The quantity demanded of margarine might rise by 16%. The XED of margarine with respect to the price of butter would be +16% / +20% = **+0.8**.

The closer the substitutes, the larger the positive XED. The XED between Coca-Cola and Pepsi might be +2.5, reflecting the fact that they are nearly interchangeable for many consumers. The XED between cinema tickets and bowling might be +0.4 — both are entertainment, but they are distant substitutes.

### Complements (XED < 0)

Two goods are **complements** if they are used together, so an increase in the price of one reduces demand for both. When the price of a complement rises, consumers buy less of that good, and consequently they also buy less of the good that goes with it.

Consider printers and printer ink. If the price of printers rises by 25%, fewer people buy printers. Because fewer people own printers, the demand for printer ink falls. The quantity demanded of ink might fall by 10%. The XED of printer ink with respect to the price of printers would be −10% / +25% = **−0.4**.

The stronger the complementary relationship, the larger the negative XED in absolute value. The XED between hot dogs and hot dog buns might be −1.0: a 10% rise in hot dog prices reduces bun demand by 10%, because they are used in nearly fixed proportions.

### Unrelated Goods (XED ≈ 0)

If two goods are unrelated, a price change in one has no measurable effect on demand for the other. The XED between the price of socks and the demand for milk is approximately 0.

### Worked Example 2: Classifying Goods Using XED

The price of a train ticket from London to Manchester rises by 15%. The quantity demanded of coach tickets on the same route rises by 30%. The quantity demanded of hotel rooms in Manchester falls by 6%.

**Approach:** Calculate XED for each pair. The sign tells us the relationship.

**Step 1:** XED between coach tickets and train tickets.

XED = +30% / +15% = **+2.0**.

XED is positive, so coach tickets and train tickets are **substitutes**. The magnitude is large (2.0), so they are **close substitutes**. A 15% rise in the train fare causes a 30% increase in coach demand — consumers switch strongly.

**Step 2:** XED between hotel rooms in Manchester and train tickets.

XED = −6% / +15% = **−0.4**.

XED is negative, so hotel rooms and train tickets are **complements**. The magnitude is moderate (0.4). When train travel to Manchester becomes more expensive, fewer people travel, so hotel demand falls. The relationship exists but is not one-to-one: not every train traveller stays in a hotel, so the link is partial.

**Why this makes sense:** Trains and coaches serve the same purpose — intercity travel — so they are substitutes. Trains and hotels are used together — travelling somewhere and staying there — so they are complements. The same price change (train fares rising) increases demand for substitutes and decreases demand for complements.

### A Common Misconception

A common mistake students make is confusing the direction of XED. XED always measures the effect of a change in the price of Good B on the quantity demanded of Good A. The formula puts the change in A's quantity in the numerator and the change in B's price in the denominator. If you reverse the goods, you get a different XED value. The XED of Coke with respect to Pepsi's price is not the same as the XED of Pepsi with respect to Coke's price. Be precise about which good is A and which is B.

## 5. Business and Policy Applications

### Using YED for Business Strategy

A firm that knows the YED of its products can make better decisions about production capacity, marketing, and product mix.

During an economic expansion, a firm producing luxury goods (YED > 1) should invest in capacity, hire staff, and market aggressively. Its sales will grow faster than the economy. During a recession, that same firm should conserve cash, reduce inventory, and prepare for a sharp fall in demand.

A firm producing necessities (0 < YED < 1) faces more stable demand across the business cycle. Its sales grow slowly in expansions and fall only modestly in recessions. This stability is valuable — it reduces risk and allows more predictable planning.

A firm producing inferior goods (YED < 0) may actually see demand rise during a recession as consumers trade down from more expensive alternatives. Supermarket own-brand ranges and budget retailers typically perform well during economic downturns.

### Using YED for Long-Run Forecasting

As developing countries grow, their consumers shift spending toward goods with high YEDs. A firm that identifies which goods have high YED in emerging markets can position itself for decades of above-average growth. This is one reason why luxury goods companies have invested heavily in China as Chinese incomes have risen.

### Using XED for Competitive Strategy

A firm that knows its XED with respect to competitors' prices can anticipate the effects of competitors' pricing decisions. If the XED between a firm's product and a rival's product is +3.0 (very close substitutes), the firm knows it will gain or lose significant market share when the rival changes prices. This informs pricing strategy: the firm must monitor the rival's prices closely and may need to match price cuts to avoid losing customers.

### Using XED for Merger Analysis

When two firms propose to merge, competition authorities examine the XED between their products. If the XED is high and positive, the products are in the same market. A merger between the two firms would remove a close competitor and could allow the merged firm to raise prices. This is one of the key economic tests in merger review.

## IB Exam Tip

For YED, always state the sign first, then the magnitude, then the classification. For example: "YED = −0.8. The negative sign indicates that this is an inferior good. The magnitude of 0.8 means that a 10% rise in income reduces quantity demanded by 8%."

For XED, the sign tells the relationship (positive = substitutes, negative = complements) and the magnitude tells the strength. A full answer states both: "XED = +2.5, indicating that the goods are close substitutes."

When asked about business implications, link the elasticity value to a specific decision: "Because YED = +2.0, the firm should expect demand to grow at twice the rate of income growth during an economic expansion, so it should invest in additional production capacity."

## Evaluate

**Question:** "YED is more useful for a firm's long-term planning than PED." Evaluate this statement.

**Model Answer:**

The statement highlights a genuine difference in the time horizons over which the two elasticities are most informative. YED captures how demand responds to changes in consumer income, which typically evolve gradually over years and decades. For long-term decisions — whether to build a new factory, enter a new geographic market, or shift the product portfolio toward different income segments — YED provides the relevant strategic intelligence. A firm that understands that its product has a YED of +2.5 can plan for demand to double over a period when average incomes rise by 40%, or for demand to halve if a severe recession cuts incomes by 20%.

PED, by contrast, addresses shorter-term pricing decisions. It tells a firm what will happen to its revenue if it changes its price next month. For a firm's annual budgeting, PED is essential. For a firm's ten-year strategic plan, YED is arguably more important because the broader economic currents — the rate of income growth, the direction of structural change — matter more than the precise price point.

However, the claim that YED is "more useful" oversimplifies. First, the two elasticities address different questions. A firm does not choose between them; it needs both. A luxury car manufacturer needs YED to forecast how many cars it can sell as the economy grows. It needs PED to decide whether to offer a £2,000 discount this quarter. One is not more useful — they are useful for different purposes.

Second, PED can matter enormously for long-term planning in industries where technological change drives prices down. In consumer electronics, prices fall rapidly over time even as quality improves. Understanding how quantity demanded responds to these price declines (PED) is critical for planning production capacity, supply chains, and component orders years in advance. The distinction between long-run and short-run PED also blurs the line: long-run PED estimates (which are higher because consumers have time to adjust) are directly relevant to long-term planning.

Third, YED estimates are often less reliable than PED estimates. PED can be estimated from historical price and quantity data within a relatively stable demand environment. YED requires disentangling the effect of income changes from simultaneous changes in tastes, technology, demographics, and relative prices. The further out the forecast, the wider the confidence interval. A firm that makes a massive investment based on an imprecise YED estimate may find that the actual income-demand relationship is weaker than expected.

Fourth, the statement ignores the interaction between YED and XED. For many firms, the most important long-term threat is not falling incomes but competition from substitutes. XED with respect to new products or technologies can render YED analysis irrelevant — no amount of income growth will save a firm whose product is being replaced by a superior alternative. The horse-drawn carriage industry had a positive YED, but that did not help when automobiles arrived.

In conclusion, YED is indispensable for long-run strategic planning, in a way that short-run PED estimates are not. But the statement overreaches. Both elasticities matter on all time horizons, and their relative importance depends on the industry, the question being asked, and the reliability of the estimates. The best firms use both, alongside XED and other market intelligence, to build a complete picture.
