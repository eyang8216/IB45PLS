# Lesson 11: Price Elasticity of Supply (PES)

## What You'll Learn

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to:

- Define price elasticity of supply (PES) and explain what it measures

- Interpret the full range of PES values from perfectly inelastic to perfectly elastic

- Compare PES for primary commodities and manufactured goods
- Analyse how PES affects the outcome of government policies such as subsidies and price controls

## 1. What Price Elasticity of Supply Is and Why It Matters

### The Core Idea

When the price of a good rises, producers want to supply more of it. But how much more can they supply, and how quickly? The answer varies enormously across goods. A coffee shop can make more lattes within minutes. A wheat farmer must wait until the next planting season. An oil company needs years to discover and develop a new field. The same price increase generates very different supply responses depending on the good and the time horizon.

**Price elasticity of supply** (PES) measures the responsiveness of quantity supplied to a change in price. It is defined as:

$$\text{PES} = \frac{\text{Percentage change in quantity supplied}}{\text{Percentage change in price}} = \frac{\%\Delta Q_s}{\%\Delta P}$$

Unlike PED, PES is almost always positive, because supply curves slope upward: higher prices incentivise more production. There is no need to use absolute values.

### Why PES Matters

PES matters because it determines how well markets can respond to changes in demand. When demand for a good surges, the increase translates into either a large price rise (if supply is inelastic) or a large quantity increase (if supply is elastic). For policymakers, PES determines the effectiveness of supply-side interventions. A subsidy to encourage renewable energy will increase production much more in an industry with elastic supply (solar panel manufacturing, in the long run) than in one with inelastic supply (nuclear power, in the short run). For firms, PES affects how quickly they can capitalise on market opportunities.

### A Common Misconception

A common mistake students make is confusing the slope of the supply curve with PES. A steep supply curve does not necessarily mean inelastic supply, and a flat supply curve does not necessarily mean elastic supply. PES depends on both the slope and the position on the curve (the P/Q ratio), just as PED does on the demand side. Always calculate PES rather than inferring it from the visual steepness of the curve.

## 2. Classifying PES Values

Economists classify supply elasticity into five categories.

| PES value | Classification | What it means |
|-----------|----------------|---------------|
| PES = 0 | Perfectly inelastic | Quantity supplied does not change when price changes |
| 0 < PES < 1 | Inelastic | Quantity supplied changes by a smaller percentage than price |
| PES = 1 | Unit elastic | Quantity supplied changes by the same percentage as price |
| PES > 1 | Elastic | Quantity supplied changes by a larger percentage than price |
| PES → ∞ | Perfectly elastic | Producers will supply any quantity at a given price, but zero below it |

### Perfectly Inelastic Supply (PES = 0)

When supply is perfectly inelastic, the quantity available is fixed regardless of price. On a diagram, the supply curve is a vertical line.

Examples include seats at a concert in a fixed-capacity venue (once the show is about to start, no more seats can be created), original paintings by a deceased artist, and beachfront land in a fully developed city. In the momentary period — the very short run in which all inputs are fixed — all goods have perfectly inelastic supply. Fresh fish already caught and brought to the docks at 6am have perfectly inelastic supply for that day's market: the quantity cannot change regardless of price.

### Inelastic Supply (0 < PES < 1)

When supply is inelastic, producers can increase output when prices rise, but only by a smaller percentage than the price increase. This is typical in the short run, when at least one factor of production is fixed.

Consider a wheat farmer. If the price of wheat rises by 30% in March, the farmer cannot instantly produce more wheat — the crop is already in the ground. The farmer might apply extra fertiliser or irrigate more intensively, increasing the yield per hectare modestly. But the total quantity of wheat harvested six months later might rise by only 10%. PES = 10% / 30% = 0.33. Supply is inelastic.

### Unit Elastic Supply (PES = 1)

When supply is unit elastic, the percentage change in quantity supplied equals the percentage change in price. Any straight-line supply curve that passes through the origin has PES = 1 at every point on the curve. This is a useful geometric property.

### Elastic Supply (PES > 1)

When supply is elastic, producers can increase output by a larger percentage than the price increase. This is typical in the long run, when all factors of production are variable. New firms can enter the industry, existing firms can build new factories, and technology can be deployed at scale.

Consider T-shirt manufacturing. If the price of T-shirts rises by 20%, manufacturers can add production shifts, hire more workers, and within months increase output by perhaps 40%. PES = 40% / 20% = 2.0. Supply is elastic.

### Perfectly Elastic Supply (PES → ∞)

When supply is perfectly elastic, producers are willing to supply any quantity at a specific price, but zero at any price below that. The supply curve is a horizontal line.

Perfectly elastic supply occurs in industries with constant returns to scale and no capacity constraints — at least over the relevant range of output. A small firm in a global commodity market (for example, a single coffee farmer selling to the world market) faces a price it cannot influence and can sell as much or as little as it wants at that price.

### Worked Example 1: Calculating PES

The price of copper rises from $8,000 per tonne to $9,600 per tonne. The quantity of copper supplied globally rises from 20 million tonnes to 22 million tonnes.

**Approach:** Calculate the percentage changes and divide. For precision between two points, use the midpoint formula.

**Step 1:** Average Q = (20 + 22) / 2 = 21 million tonnes. Average P = ($8,000 + $9,600) / 2 = $8,800.

**Step 2:** %ΔQs = (22 − 20) / 21 × 100 = 2 / 21 × 100 = 9.52%. %ΔP = ($9,600 − $8,000) / $8,800 × 100 = $1,600 / $8,800 × 100 = 18.18%.

**Step 3:** PES = 9.52% / 18.18% = **0.52**.

Supply of copper is **inelastic** (PES < 1) over this price range.

**Why this makes sense:** Copper mining is capital-intensive and has long lead times. Opening a new mine takes years, and expanding an existing mine takes months. The 20% price increase generated only a 10% supply increase, reflecting these real-world constraints.

## 3. PES Along a Straight-Line Supply Curve

### Point Elasticity for Supply

For a linear supply curve, the point elasticity formula is:

$$\text{PES} = \frac{\Delta Q_s}{\Delta P} \times \frac{P}{Q_s}$$

Where ΔQs/ΔP is the reciprocal of the slope (dQ/dP from the supply equation).

The geometric pattern is:

- If the supply curve intersects the **price axis** (positive P-intercept), then PES > 1 at every point. Supply is elastic everywhere.
- If the supply curve passes through the **origin**, then PES = 1 at every point. Supply is unit elastic everywhere.
- If the supply curve intersects the **quantity axis** (positive Q-intercept), then PES < 1 at every point. Supply is inelastic everywhere.

This is different from PED, which varies along the demand curve. For supply, the classification (elastic, unit elastic, or inelastic) is the same at every point on a given straight-line supply curve, as long as the curve starts from the same axis intercept.

### Worked Example 2: PES from an Equation

The supply of a good is given by Qs = −50 + 5P, where P is the price in pounds.

**Approach:** Use the point elasticity formula at two different prices to confirm that PES changes.

**Step 1:** From Qs = −50 + 5P, ΔQs/ΔP = 5. The supply curve intersects the quantity axis at Q = −50 when P = 0 (which, practically, means the minimum price to induce any supply is P = 10, where Q = 0).

**Step 2:** At P = £20: Qs = −50 + 5(20) = 50. PES = 5 × (20 / 50) = 5 × 0.4 = **2.0. Elastic**.

**Step 3:** At P = £30: Qs = −50 + 5(30) = 100. PES = 5 × (30 / 100) = 5 × 0.3 = **1.5. Elastic**.

**Step 4:** At P = £100: Qs = −50 + 5(100) = 450. PES = 5 × (100 / 450) = 5 × 0.222 = **1.11. Elastic** (approaching 1 from above).

**Why this makes sense:** This supply curve intersects the quantity axis at a negative value (Q = −50 when P = 0), which means it would intersect the price axis at P = 10 (when Q = 0). A supply curve that intersects the price axis has PES > 1 everywhere. As P and Q become very large, P/Q approaches 0.2 (since Q ≈ 5P for large P), and PES approaches 5 × 0.2 = 1 from above. PES is never exactly 1, but it gets arbitrarily close.

## 4. Determinants of PES

### (1) Time Period — The Most Important Determinant

Time is the single most systematic determinant of PES because it governs how many factors of production can be varied. Economists distinguish three time horizons:

**Momentary period (very short run):** All factors of production are fixed. Firms cannot change output at all. PES = 0. The only way to meet higher demand is to draw down existing inventories.

**Short run:** At least one factor is fixed (typically capital — factories, machinery, land). Firms can increase output by using variable factors more intensively: adding overtime shifts, hiring temporary workers, using more raw materials. PES is positive but inelastic (0 < PES < 1).

**Long run:** All factors are variable. Firms can build new factories, enter the industry, adopt new technology. PES is elastic (PES > 1).

The progression from PES = 0 through PES < 1 to PES > 1 as the time horizon lengthens is universal across industries. What varies is how long each period lasts. For a coffee shop, the short run might be a week (time to hire and train a new barista) and the long run might be three months (time to lease and fit out a second location). For a nuclear power plant, the short run might be five years and the long run might be twenty.

### (2) Spare Production Capacity

Firms operating below their maximum capacity can increase output quickly when prices rise, simply by using idle resources. Their supply is more elastic. A hotel with 60% occupancy can accommodate more guests at short notice. A factory running at 95% capacity faces bottlenecks — adding more output requires investment in new equipment, which takes time.

### (3) Ability to Store Inventories

Goods that can be stored without deteriorating allow producers to smooth supply over time. When prices are high, firms can sell from storage as well as from current production, increasing the effective quantity supplied. When prices are low, firms can build inventories. Manufactured goods (electronics, clothing, canned food) are highly storable and therefore have more elastic supply. Perishable goods (fresh milk, cut flowers, fresh fish) cannot be stored and therefore have more inelastic supply — whatever is produced must be sold immediately, regardless of price.

### (4) Production Complexity and Lead Times

Goods with simple production processes and short lead times have more elastic supply. A screen-printing shop can produce custom T-shirts within days. Goods with complex production and long lead times have more inelastic supply. A commercial aircraft takes years from order to delivery. The longer it takes to produce one more unit, the less responsive supply is to price changes.

### (5) Mobility of Factors of Production

If the resources needed to produce a good — labour, capital, raw materials — can easily move from other uses into this industry, supply is more elastic. If specialised skills or equipment are required that cannot be quickly acquired, supply is less elastic. The supply of delivery drivers (low skill requirements, ubiquitous) can expand faster than the supply of neurosurgeons (fifteen years of training required).

### (6) Availability of Raw Materials

If key inputs are abundant and easily obtained, firms can expand output without running into input shortages. Supply is more elastic. If key inputs are scarce or controlled by a small number of suppliers, firms face a binding constraint on expansion. Supply is less elastic. The supply of lithium for batteries is constrained by the limited number of operating lithium mines, making supply inelastic in the short to medium run.

### A Common Misconception

A common mistake students make is assuming that all agricultural goods have inelastic supply. While many do in the short run (seasonal growing cycles, perishability), agricultural supply can become quite elastic in the long run as farmers switch between crops, adopt higher-yield varieties, and bring new land under cultivation. The time horizon is everything.

## 5. PES in Primary Commodities versus Manufactured Goods

### Primary Commodities

Primary commodities — agricultural products (wheat, coffee, cocoa), minerals (copper, iron ore), and energy (oil, natural gas) — typically have **low PES** in the short run. There are several reasons:

- Long gestation periods: coffee trees take 3 to 5 years to mature. An oil field takes 5 to 10 years from discovery to production.
- Natural constraints: weather, soil quality, geology. A drought reduces agricultural supply regardless of price.
- Perishability: fresh produce cannot be stored, so supply in any period is whatever was harvested.
- Geographical fixity: minerals exist where geology put them, not where demand is highest.

The consequence is **price volatility**. Because supply is inelastic, shifts in demand (or supply shocks such as bad harvests) cause large price swings. This is a defining feature of commodity markets.

### Manufactured Goods

Manufactured goods — electronics, clothing, automobiles, processed food — typically have **higher PES**. The reasons mirror the constraints on commodities:

- Flexible production: factories can add shifts, outsource, and use temporary workers.
- Storability: manufactured goods can be held in inventory and released when prices rise.
- Fewer natural constraints: factories are not weather-dependent (though they may depend on commodity inputs).
- Replicable technology: a successful factory design can be copied and built elsewhere.

The consequence is **price stability**. Supply responds to price signals, dampening the price effects of demand shifts.

## 6. How PES Affects Policy Outcomes

### Subsidies

A government subsidy to producers shifts the supply curve to the right. The effect on quantity depends on PES. If PES is elastic, the subsidy generates a large increase in quantity — the policy goal (more housing, more renewable energy, more electric vehicles) is achieved. If PES is inelastic, the subsidy mainly raises the price that producers receive without much increase in quantity. The policy is expensive and ineffective.

### Taxes

The deadweight loss from a tax depends on both PED and PES. The more inelastic is supply (alongside more inelastic demand), the smaller the deadweight loss, because quantity changes less. But producers bear more of the tax burden when supply is inelastic.

### Price Controls

When a price ceiling is imposed, the resulting shortage is larger when supply is inelastic. With inelastic supply, producers cannot increase output to meet the higher quantity demanded at the lower price. Rent controls in cities with inelastic housing supply (geographical constraints, planning restrictions) create larger shortages than in cities where new housing can be built quickly.

---

## IB Exam Tip

When answering questions about PES, always specify the time period. A complete answer distinguishes between the short run and the long run. The phrase "In the short run, PES is inelastic because capital is fixed, but in the long run, PES is elastic because firms can build new capacity" is one of the most useful sentences in IB Economics.

When drawing supply curves of different elasticities, draw them passing through the same point so you can illustrate that at a given price, a flatter supply curve means more elastic supply.

For "determinants" questions, lead with the time period. Then select the two or three most relevant additional determinants for the specific good. Do not list all six — quality of explanation matters more than quantity.

(b) PES < 1: supply is **inelastic**. (c) The most likely reason is the **time period** determinant. Wheat is an annual crop with a fixed growing season. Once the crop is in the ground, farmers cannot significantly increase the quantity harvested until the next planting season. Land is also a fixed factor in the short run — farmers cannot instantly acquire more fields. A 20% price increase generated only an 8% supply response because the agricultural production cycle imposes a natural time constraint.

**Answer 2.** (a) ΔQs/ΔP = 10. At P = $50: Qs = −200 + 10(50) = 300. PES = 10 × (50/300) = 10 × 0.1667 = **1.67. Elastic**. (b) At P = $80: Qs = −200 + 10(80) = 600. PES = 10 × (80/600) = 10 × 0.1333 = **1.33. Elastic**. (c) PES falls as P and Q increase, approaching 1 asymptotically from above. The supply curve has a positive P-intercept: when Q = 0, P = 20. A supply curve that intersects the price axis has PES > 1 everywhere, but PES declines as the ratio P/Q falls. Setting PES = 1: 10 × (P / (−200 + 10P)) = 1 → 10P = −200 + 10P → 0 = −200, which is impossible. Therefore PES is never exactly 1 on this curve. As P → ∞, PES → 1 from above. (d) The supply curve Qs = −200 + 10P (or P = 20 + 0.1Q) has a P-intercept of 20 and a Q-intercept of −200. The positive P-intercept means PES > 1 everywhere — supply is elastic at every point. As quantity becomes very large, P/Q approaches 0.1, and PES approaches 10 × 0.1 = 1.

**Answer 3.** (a) Canned strawberries have a higher PES than fresh strawberries. The key determinant is **ability to store inventories**. Canned strawberries can sit in a warehouse for months or years and be released when prices rise. Fresh strawberries are perishable — they must be sold within days. This means the supply of fresh strawberries at any moment is fixed by the harvest; supply of canned strawberries can be adjusted by managing inventory. (b) Mass-produced T-shirts have a higher PES than custom-tailored wedding dresses. The key determinant is **production complexity and lead times**. Mass-produced T-shirts use simple, automated processes that can be scaled quickly by adding shifts. Custom wedding dresses require skilled artisans, individual fittings, and weeks of work per dress. Capacity is constrained by the availability of specialised labour. (c) Rental housing in a suburb with available land has a higher PES than rental housing in a densely built city centre. The key determinant is **spare capacity and factor mobility**. In the suburb, new housing can be built on available land — a variable factor. In the city centre, land is fully built out, and new housing requires demolishing and rebuilding at higher density, which is slow, expensive, and often restricted by planning regulations. The city centre supply is inherently inelastic.

## Evaluate

**Question:** "The most important determinant of price elasticity of supply is the time period." Evaluate this statement.

**Model Answer:**

The statement correctly identifies time as a uniquely systematic determinant of PES. Across every industry, the progression from momentary (PES = 0) through short-run (0 < PES < 1) to long-run (PES > 1) holds. No other determinant has this universality. The time framework provides policymakers and firms with a reliable starting point for analysis: evaluate any supply-side policy over the appropriate time horizon, because its effectiveness depends on whether you are in the short run or the long run.

However, claiming time is the "most important" oversimplifies for several reasons. First, for some goods, other determinants dominate regardless of the time horizon. Perishable agricultural goods such as fresh milk have inherently inelastic supply even in the long run — you cannot store milk for years, you cannot speed up a cow's gestation period beyond biology, and land suitable for dairy farming is finite. Time helps (farmers can expand herds over years), but the biological and physical constraints place a low ceiling on PES regardless of how much time you allow. For these goods, storability and natural constraints are more important than time.

Second, goods requiring highly specialised human capital — brain surgery, nuclear engineering, professional sports — have inelastic supply over any realistic time horizon. The bottleneck is not time per se but the years of training, the innate talent required, and the limited number of people willing and able to acquire the skills. Time is a necessary condition for increasing supply, but it is not sufficient.

Third, industry structure can override the time effect. A competitive industry with low barriers to entry, such as food trucks or mobile app development, can achieve elastic supply in a relatively short period because new firms enter quickly. A natural monopoly such as a water utility or railway infrastructure has inelastic supply almost indefinitely, regardless of time, because the massive fixed costs and regulatory barriers prevent competitive entry.

Fourth, the statement's focus on time may lead to policy errors. A policymaker who assumes that "in the long run, supply will become elastic" for every good may neglect to address the specific barriers — planning restrictions, skills shortages, monopolised input markets — that keep supply inelastic even over long periods. Time alone does not guarantee elastic supply; it only creates the possibility.

In conclusion, the time period is the most universally applicable and conceptually important determinant of PES. It provides the essential organising framework. But its importance varies across goods, and for many goods, other determinants — storability, factor specificity, natural constraints, and industry structure — are equally or more important in practice. The most accurate assessment is that time *enables* the other determinants to operate but does not guarantee elastic supply. The statement is a valid broad generalisation but not an absolute truth.
