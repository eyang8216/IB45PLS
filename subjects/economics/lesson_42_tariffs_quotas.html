  • The welfare analysis of tariffs: consumer surplus loss, producer surplus gain, government revenue, and deadweight loss
  • The definition of a quota and how it limits imports through quantity restrictions

1. Tariffs: Definition and Mechanism

A tariff (customs duty) is a tax imposed on imported goods. Tariffs can be:

  • Specific tariff: A fixed amount per physical unit (e.g., $5 per kilogram of steel).
  • Ad valorem tariff: A percentage of the import price (e.g., 25% on imported cars).

How a Tariff Works

In a free trade equilibrium, the domestic price equals the world price (\(P_w\)). At \(P_w\):

  • Domestic quantity demanded: \(Q_d\)
  • Domestic quantity supplied: \(Q_s\)
  • Imports: \(Q_d - Q_s\) (the gap between domestic demand and domestic supply)

When a tariff of \(t\) per unit is imposed:

  • The domestic price rises from \(P_w\) to \(P_w + t\) (assuming a small country that cannot affect the world price).
  • This higher domestic price creates a wedge between what domestic consumers pay and what foreign producers receive.

Tariff Diagram Analysis

The standard IB tariff diagram shows:

  • World price line: A horizontal line at \(P_w\) (perfectly elastic world supply for a small country).
  • Tariff-inclusive price line: A horizontal line at \(P_w + t\), above the world price.
  • Domestic demand curve (D): Downward-sloping.
  • Domestic supply curve (S): Upward-sloping.

Before the tariff (free trade at \(P_w\)): domestic production = \(Q_{s1}\), consumption = \(Q_{d1}\), imports = \(Q_{d1} - Q_{s1}\).

After the tariff (price rises to \(P_w + t\)): domestic production expands to \(Q_{s2}\), consumption contracts to \(Q_{d2}\), imports fall to \(Q_{d2} - Q_{s2}\).

2. Welfare Effects of a Tariff

The welfare analysis compares free trade (at \(P_w\)) to protected trade (at \(P_w + t\)). All welfare is measured using consumer and producer surplus.

Before the Tariff (Free Trade)

  • Consumer Surplus (CS): The entire area below the demand curve and above \(P_w\).
  • Producer Surplus (PS): The small triangle above the supply curve and below \(P_w\).
  • No government revenue.
  • Total Welfare = CS + PS.

After the Tariff

The price rises to \(P_w + t\). Five areas are identified:

  1. Area A — Producer Surplus Gain (Transfer from Consumers): Domestic producers gain. This is a transfer from consumers to producers, not a net gain.
  2. Area B — Deadweight Loss (Production Inefficiency): The excess cost of producing goods domestically that could have been imported more cheaply at \(P_w\). Resources are misallocated to inefficient domestic production.
  3. Area C — Government Tariff Revenue: The rectangle: tariff per unit × quantity of imports after the tariff. \(\text{Area C} = t \times (Q_{d2} - Q_{s2})\). This is a transfer from consumers to the government.
  4. Area D — Deadweight Loss (Consumption Inefficiency): Welfare lost because consumers are priced out of goods they value more than \(P_w\) but less than \(P_w + t\).
  5. The net national welfare loss = B + D — the two Harberger triangles.
Worked Example — Tariff Welfare Calculation

Scenario: Country X is a small importer of textiles. World price: $20 per metre.
Domestic demand: \(Q_d = 100 - 2P\)  |  Domestic supply: \(Q_s = -20 + 2P\)
A specific tariff of $5 per metre is imposed.

Step 1: Free trade at \(P_w = \$20\)
\(Q_d = 100 - 2(20) = 60\) million metres
\(Q_s = -20 + 2(20) = 20\) million metres
Imports = 60 − 20 = 40 million metres

Step 2: After tariff at \(P = \$25\)
\(Q_d = 100 - 2(25) = 50\) million metres
\(Q_s = -20 + 2(25) = 30\) million metres
Imports = 50 − 30 = 20 million metres

Step 3: Welfare changes
CS loss = \(\frac{1}{2} \times (60 + 50) \times 5 = \$275\) million
PS gain (Area A) = \(\frac{1}{2} \times (20 + 30) \times 5 = \$125\) million
Gov revenue (Area C) = \(5 \times 20 = \$100\) million
DWL (B + D) = \(275 - 125 - 100 = \$50\) million
Production DWL (B) = \(\frac{1}{2} \times (30 - 20) \times 5 = \$25\) million
Consumption DWL (D) = \(\frac{1}{2} \times (60 - 50) \times 5 = \$25\) million ✓

3. Quotas: Definition and Mechanism

A quota (import quota) is a quantitative restriction on the volume of imports — e.g., "no more than 20 million kilograms of sugar may be imported per year."

How a Quota Works

The government sets a maximum import quantity, \(Q_{\text{quota}}\). Once this limit is reached, no further imports are permitted. The quota restricts supply in the domestic market, pushing the domestic price above the world price.

Quota Diagram Analysis

  • The domestic supply curve (S) is shifted right by the quota quantity, creating a kinked effective supply curve: S_domestic + Quota.
  • At \(P_w\), effective supply is limited to \(Q_{s,\text{domestic}} + Q_{\text{quota}}\). Excess demand pushes the price up.
  • The new equilibrium price \(P_q\) is where domestic demand equals domestic supply plus the quota quantity.
  • At \(P_q\): domestic production = \(Q_{s,q}\), consumption = \(Q_{d,q}\), imports = \(Q_{\text{quota}}\).

4. Welfare Effects of a Quota (vs. Tariff)

Like a tariff, a quota raises the domestic price, increases domestic production, reduces consumption, and lowers imports. The CS loss and PS gain areas are identical to those of a tariff achieving the same import reduction.

The critical difference: what happens to Area C.

  • Under a tariff: Area C goes to the government as tariff revenue.
  • Under a quota: Area C becomes quota rent — the difference between domestic and world price, multiplied by the quota quantity. This rent accrues to whoever holds the import licences.

Who Gets the Quota Rent?

  • Domestic importers: If government auctions licences competitively → equivalent to tariff revenue.
  • Foreign exporters: If quota administered through VERs or foreign licence allocation → rent goes abroad, making quotas worse than tariffs for national welfare.
  • Politically connected domestic firms: If licences allocated non-transparently → corruption and rent-seeking.

Worked Example — Quota Welfare Calculation

Scenario: Country Y imposes an import quota of 15 million tonnes on steel.
World price: $200/tonne. \(Q_d = 80 - 0.1P\), \(Q_s = -10 + 0.1P\). Quota: 15 million tonnes.

Step 1: Find \(P_q\)
Import demand = \(Q_d - Q_s = 90 - 0.2P\). Set \(90 - 0.2P = 15 \rightarrow P_q = \$375\).

Step 2: Quantities at \(P_q = \$375\)
\(Q_d = 80 - 0.1(375) = 42.5\)  |  \(Q_s = -10 + 0.1(375) = 27.5\)  |  Imports = 15 ✓

Step 3: Welfare effects (vs. free trade at \(P_w = \$200\))
At \(P_w\): \(Q_d = 60, Q_s = 10\), imports = 50.
CS loss = \(\frac{1}{2} \times 102.5 \times 175 = \$8{,}968.75\)M
PS gain = \(\frac{1}{2} \times 37.5 \times 175 = \$3{,}281.25\)M
Quota rent (Area C) = \(175 \times 15 = \$2{,}625\)M
DWL = \(\$3{,}062.5\)M ✓

5. Other Protectionist Tools

5.1 Subsidies to Domestic Producers

A production subsidy is a payment per unit to domestic producers, effectively lowering their costs. The supply curve shifts right (down). Domestic price stays at \(P_w\) (unlike tariff/quota). Domestic production increases, imports fall. Cost = subsidy per unit × domestic output. There is production DWL but no consumption DWL — consumers still pay \(P_w\). Subsidies are WTO-actionable.

5.2 Administrative Barriers

Administrative ("red tape") barriers include complex customs procedures, unique health/safety standards, labelling regulations, and slow licensing processes. These raise the cost of importing without an explicit tax, effectively shifting world supply upward by the compliance cost. They are less transparent than tariffs and harder to challenge at the WTO — a form of "hidden protectionism." Crucially, they generate zero government revenue; the cost is absorbed by importers as compliance expense.

Worked Example: If compliance with a country's unique safety certification adds $3/unit to import costs, this is economically equivalent to a $3 specific tariff — but generates no government revenue. The compliance cost is a pure efficiency loss.

IB Exam Tip

On Paper 1 (essay), you must be able to draw and fully label the tariff and quota diagrams — including world price line, tariff-inclusive price, all quantity points (\(Q_{s1}, Q_{s2}, Q_{d1}, Q_{d2}\)), and clearly identify areas A, B, C, D. On Paper 2 (data response), you may need to calculate welfare changes from provided data. Always state clearly: consumer surplus loss = A + B + C + D, producer surplus gain = A, government revenue/quota rent = C, and deadweight loss = B + D.

Practice Problems

Problem 1 — Basic Tariff Analysis

Country Alpha imports coffee. World price: $3/kg. \(Q_d = 200 - 20P\). \(Q_s = -40 + 20P\). Alpha imposes a specific tariff of $1.50/kg.

(a) Calculate free-trade quantities: domestic production, consumption, and imports.

(b) Calculate the same quantities after the tariff.

(c) Calculate: (i) CS loss, (ii) PS gain, (iii) government tariff revenue, (iv) deadweight loss.

(d) Draw a fully labelled diagram showing all relevant areas.

Problem 2 — Quota Analysis

Country Beta imposes a quota of 20 million units on imported electronics. World price: $50. \(Q_d = 120 - 1.2P\). \(Q_s = -30 + 1.2P\).

(a) Find the domestic price under the quota.

(b) Calculate domestic production, consumption, and verify imports equal the quota.

(c) Calculate the quota rent (Area C). Who might capture this rent?

(d) If the quota were replaced by a tariff achieving the same import level, what would the tariff rate be and how would welfare differ?

Problem 3 — Comparing Tariff and Quota Under Demand Increase

Country Gamma protects its steel industry. Currently, a tariff of $40/tonne achieves imports of 25 million tonnes at a domestic price of $340 (world price $300). Alternatively, a quota of 25 million tonnes achieves the same.

Suppose domestic demand increases due to an infrastructure boom. Explain, using diagrams, how the effects differ under (a) the tariff and (b) the quota. (c) Which instrument would domestic steel consumers prefer? Why?

Problem 4 — Production Subsidy vs. Tariff

Country Delta's government wants to support wheat farmers. World price: $200/tonne. \(Q_d = 50 - 0.1P\). \(Q_s = -10 + 0.1P\). Two policies: (i) tariff of $50/tonne, (ii) production subsidy of $50/tonne.

(a) For each, calculate: domestic production, consumption, imports, and government cost.

(b) Calculate DWL for each.

(c) Explain which is less distortionary and why.

Problem 5 — Administrative Barriers as Hidden Protection

Country Epsilon requires all imported food to undergo unique safety certification adding $4/unit compliance cost. World price: $12/unit. \(Q_d = 100 - 2P\). \(Q_s = -20 + 3P\).

(a) Model this as a tariff. Calculate the effective tariff rate.

(b) Calculate welfare effects. How do they differ from an explicit tariff?

(c) Why might countries prefer administrative barriers to tariffs?

Problem 6 — IB-Style Essay Question

"Evaluate the view that tariffs are preferable to quotas as a form of trade protection." [15 marks]

Answers

Answer 1 — Basic Tariff Analysis

(a) Free trade at \(P_w = \$3\): \(Q_d = 140\), \(Q_s = 20\) million kg. Imports = 120 million kg.

(b) After tariff, \(P = \$4.50\): \(Q_d = 110\), \(Q_s = 50\) million kg. Imports = 60 million kg.

(c) Welfare: CS loss = \(\frac{1}{2} \times (140 + 110) \times 1.50 = \$187.50\)M. PS gain = \(\frac{1}{2} \times (20 + 50) \times 1.50 = \$52.50\)M. Gov revenue = \(1.50 \times 60 = \$90\)M. DWL = \(187.50 - 52.50 - 90 = \$45\)M.

(d) Diagram: world price at $3, tariff-inclusive price at $4.50. Label \(Q_{s1}=20\), \(Q_{s2}=50\), \(Q_{d2}=110\), \(Q_{d1}=140\). Label Areas A (PS gain), B (production DWL, $22.50M), C (gov revenue, $90M), D (consumption DWL, $22.50M).

Answer 2 — Quota Analysis

(a) Import demand = \(150 - 2.4P = 20\) → \(P_q = \$54.17\).

(b) \(Q_d = 55\), \(Q_s = 35\) million. Imports = 20 ✓.

(c) Quota rent = \((54.17 - 50) \times 20 = \$83.4\)M. If licences given free to domestic importers → domestic transfer. If foreign exporters control licences (VER) → rent lost abroad.

(d) Equivalent tariff = $4.17/unit (8.34%). Under tariff, $83.4M goes to government. If licences auctioned competitively, outcomes identical. Otherwise, tariff superior — government can use revenue for public goods.

Answer 3 — Tariff vs. Quota Under Demand Increase

(a) Under tariff: Price stays at $340. Domestic production unchanged. Domestic consumption increases along new D curve. Imports increase.

(b) Under quota: Domestic price rises to choke off extra demand — imports fixed at 25M. Higher price, higher domestic production, lower consumption than tariff case.

(c) Consumers prefer the tariff. It allows imports to expand with demand, moderating the price increase. Under a quota, all demand increases translate into price increases — no additional imports cushion the market.

Answer 4 — Production Subsidy vs. Tariff

(a) Free trade: \(Q_d = 30\), \(Q_s = 10\)M tonnes. Imports = 20M.

Tariff ($50): P = $250. \(Q_d = 25\), \(Q_s = 15\). Imports = 10M. Gov revenue = $500M.

Subsidy ($50): Consumers pay $200. \(Q_d = 30\), \(Q_s = 15\). Imports = 15M. Gov cost = $750M.

(b) DWL: Tariff: production DWL = $125M + consumption DWL = $125M = $250M. Subsidy: only production DWL = $125M.

(c) Subsidy is less distortionary — no consumption DWL (consumers still pay \(P_w\)). But subsidy costs government more ($750M vs. $500M revenue from tariff). Choice depends on priority: minimise DWL or minimise fiscal cost.

Answer 5 — Administrative Barriers

(a) $4 compliance cost = specific tariff equivalent of $4. Effective ad valorem = \((4/12) \times 100 = 33.3\%\).

At effective price $16: \(Q_d = 68\), \(Q_s = 28\), imports = 40M. Free trade: \(Q_d = 76\), \(Q_s = 16\), imports = 60M.

(b) CS loss = \(\frac{1}{2} \times 144 \times 4 = \$288\)M. PS gain = \(\frac{1}{2} \times 44 \times 4 = \$88\)M. Gov revenue = $0. DWL = $200M. Unlike explicit tariff, administrative barriers generate ZERO government revenue — the entire CS loss is either transferred to producers or lost as DWL. National welfare loss is larger.

(c) Administrative barriers are less visible, harder for trading partners to quantify and challenge at WTO, can be framed as legitimate consumer protection, and provide discretion to customs officials.

Evaluate: CLASPP Model Answer (150–200 words)

"Tariffs are preferable to quotas as a form of trade protection."

Conclusion: Tariffs are generally preferable to quotas on economic efficiency and transparency grounds, but the superiority is not absolute — context, administrative capacity, and policy objectives matter.

Limitations: If quota licences are competitively auctioned, quotas generate government revenue identical to tariff revenue, eliminating one key advantage of tariffs. However, auction systems are rare in practice — most quota rents accrue to politically connected importers or foreign exporters via VERs, worsening national welfare. Tariffs are more visible and politically contentious.

Assumptions: The argument assumes effective tariff collection and WTO-bound rates. Countries with weak customs capacity may find quotas (physical limits at ports) easier to administer than value-based tariffs.

Stakeholders: Consumers face higher prices under both, but tariffs offer more flexibility when demand grows — imports can increase, moderating price rises. Domestic producers may prefer quotas for guaranteed market share. Governments prefer tariffs for revenue.

Pros/Cons: Tariffs are transparent, generate revenue, allow import flexibility, and are WTO-legal within bound rates. Quotas provide certainty of import volume but create rent-seeking, corruption risks, and rigidity during demand shocks.

Priorities: For most protection objectives, tariffs are the better instrument. However, in agriculture (price volatility) or temporary balance-of-payments crises, quotas may offer advantages. The first-best policy is neither — it is addressing the underlying competitiveness or adjustment-cost problem directly through supply-side measures.
