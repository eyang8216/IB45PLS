# Lesson 44: Economic Integration — Trading Blocs and WTO

## What You'll Learn
- The five **stages of economic integration**: preferential trade area, free trade area, customs union, common market, and monetary union.
- The difference between **trade creation** (welfare gain) and **trade diversion** (welfare loss).
- Benefits and costs of **monetary union**, including loss of independent monetary policy.
- The role of the **WTO** in trade liberalisation, dispute settlement, and the most-favoured-nation principle.

---

## 1. Stages of Economic Integration

Economic integration refers to the process by which countries reduce trade barriers and coordinate economic policies. It proceeds through progressively deeper stages:

### Stage 1: Preferential Trade Area (PTA)
Countries agree to lower trade barriers on selected goods from member countries, but barriers are not eliminated entirely. External tariffs differ by country. Example: Early stages of ASEAN cooperation.

### Stage 2: Free Trade Area (FTA)
Members **eliminate** tariffs and quotas on trade among themselves, but **each country maintains its own external tariff** on non-members. This creates a problem: **trade deflection** — goods enter the FTA through the member with the lowest external tariff and then move freely within the bloc.

**Solution**: Rules of origin — administrative requirements that goods must be substantially produced within the FTA to qualify for duty-free treatment.

**Example**: USMCA (formerly NAFTA): Canada, Mexico, and the United States eliminated tariffs on most goods traded among them, but each sets its own tariffs on goods from China, the EU, and other non-members.

### Stage 3: Customs Union
Like an FTA but members adopt a **common external tariff** (CET) on imports from non-members. Rules of origin are no longer needed because the same tariff applies regardless of entry point.

**Example**: The European Economic Community (EEC) from 1968 — the predecessor to the EU — was initially a customs union. The Southern African Customs Union (SACU) is another example.

**Key implication**: A customs union is the first stage where trade diversion becomes possible (see Section 2).

### Stage 4: Common Market
A customs union PLUS **free movement of factors of production** (labour and capital) across member borders. Workers can work in any member country; capital can be invested freely.

**Example**: The European Union from 1993 (Maastricht Treaty) established the "single market" with four freedoms: free movement of goods, services, labour, and capital.

**Additional requirements**: Harmonisation of regulations, mutual recognition of professional qualifications, coordinated competition policy.

### Stage 5: Monetary Union
A common market PLUS:
- **Common currency** (e.g., the euro)
- **Common monetary policy** (e.g., European Central Bank)
- **Coordinated fiscal policies** (though not unified)

**Example**: The Eurozone (currently 20 EU member states using the euro).

## 2. Trade Creation vs. Trade Diversion

When a trading bloc forms, two opposing effects on welfare occur:

### Trade Creation (Welfare Gain)

**Definition**: Trade creation occurs when the formation of a trading bloc causes a country to switch from a high-cost domestic producer to a lower-cost partner-country producer. This increases efficiency and welfare.

**Worked Example**:
- Before customs union: Country A produces textiles domestically at $50/unit. Country B (now a customs union partner) produces at $35/unit but previously faced a 40% tariff (import price from B = $35 × 1.40 = $49). Country C (non-member) produces at $30/unit but faced the same 40% tariff (import price = $42).
- Country A imported from Country C at $42/unit.
- After customs union: tariff on B eliminated. Price from B = $35. Price from C (with common external tariff of 30%) = $30 × 1.30 = $39.
- Country A now imports from B at $35 — cheaper than the previous $42 from C and cheaper than $39 from C now.
- **Trade creation**: A switches from the $42-after-tariff source to the genuinely cheaper $35 source (B). Global resources are allocated more efficiently.

### Trade Diversion (Welfare Loss)

**Definition**: Trade diversion occurs when the formation of a trading bloc causes a country to switch from a more efficient non-member producer to a less efficient member-country producer because of preferential tariff treatment. This reduces global welfare.

**Worked Example** (same setup, different CET):
- Same as above, but the common external tariff is set at 60%.
- Price from non-member C after CET = $30 × 1.60 = $48.
- Price from partner B = $35 (tariff-free).
- Country A switches from C ($42 under previous tariff) to B ($35 now).
- But C is the genuinely most efficient producer ($30 vs. $35).
- **Trade diversion**: trade shifts from the globally efficient source (C at $30) to a less efficient partner (B at $35). Global welfare decreases — resources are allocated less efficiently.

**Table Summary**:
| Effect | Before Bloc | After Bloc | Welfare |
|--------|------------|------------|---------|
| Trade Creation | High-cost domestic → Low-cost partner (blocked by tariffs) | Partner tariff removed; switch to partner | Gain |
| Trade Diversion | Low-cost non-member → Higher-cost member | Preferential treatment favours member | Loss |

**Key insight**: Whether a customs union raises or lowers global welfare depends on whether trade creation exceeds trade diversion. Empirical studies suggest the EU's trade creation has significantly exceeded trade diversion.

## 3. Monetary Union — Benefits and Costs

### Benefits of Monetary Union

1. **Elimination of transaction costs**: No need to exchange currencies — saves billions in fees (estimated 0.5% of GDP for smaller Eurozone economies).

2. **Elimination of exchange rate uncertainty**: Firms can trade and invest across borders without currency risk, encouraging cross-border trade and investment.

3. **Price transparency**: Consumers and firms can directly compare prices across countries, increasing competition and reducing price discrimination.

4. **Enhanced trade and investment**: The euro is estimated to have increased intra-Eurozone trade by 5–15%.

5. **Lower interest rates for some members**: Countries with historically weak currencies (e.g., Greece, Italy) benefited from the ECB's credibility, reducing their borrowing costs.

6. **International role**: A common currency can become a global reserve currency, reducing transaction costs with the rest of the world.

### Costs of Monetary Union

1. **Loss of independent monetary policy**: Member countries cannot set their own interest rates. The ECB sets rates for the entire Eurozone based on average conditions. If one country is in recession while others are booming, the "one-size-fits-all" rate may be inappropriate.

**Worked Example**: During the Eurozone crisis (2010–2012), Greece needed very low interest rates to stimulate its depressed economy, while Germany was near full employment. The ECB set rates appropriate for the average, which was too high for Greece and arguably too low for Germany.

2. **Loss of exchange rate as an adjustment mechanism**: Countries cannot devalue to restore competitiveness. If a country's costs rise relative to trading partners, it cannot depreciate — it must undergo "internal devaluation" (reducing wages and prices domestically), which is slow and politically painful.

3. **Fiscal policy constraints**: While fiscal policy remains national, it is constrained by EU rules (e.g., Stability and Growth Pact: deficit < 3% GDP, debt < 60% GDP). Countries cannot freely use fiscal expansion during recessions.

4. **Optimal Currency Area (OCA) criteria** — A monetary union works best when:
   - Labour is mobile across member countries (can move to where jobs are).
   - Economies are synchronised (similar business cycles).
   - Trade is highly integrated.
   - A fiscal transfer mechanism exists.

   The Eurozone scores well on trade integration but poorly on labour mobility and fiscal transfers. This asymmetry contributed to the Eurozone crisis.

## 4. The World Trade Organization (WTO)

The WTO (founded 1995, successor to GATT 1947) is the international organisation governing global trade rules.

### Key Principles

1. **Non-discrimination**:
   - **Most-Favoured-Nation (MFN)**: A trade advantage granted to one WTO member must be extended to all. If the US lowers tariffs on German cars, it must lower them for Japanese cars too.
   - **National Treatment**: Imported goods must be treated no less favourably than domestically produced goods.

2. **Binding tariffs**: Countries "bind" their tariffs at negotiated ceilings and cannot raise them above bound levels without compensating affected trading partners.

3. **Prohibition of quotas**: Quantitative restrictions are generally prohibited (though exceptions exist for agriculture, national security, and balance-of-payments crises).

4. **Dispute settlement**: A binding mechanism for resolving trade disputes. If a country is found to violate rules, it must correct the violation or face authorised retaliation.

### WTO Achievements
- Reduced average tariffs from ~22% (1947) to ~9% today.
- Expanded coverage from goods to services (GATS) and intellectual property (TRIPS).
- Provided a rules-based framework that prevented a repeat of 1930s-style protectionism after the 2008 financial crisis.

### WTO Criticisms
- Doha Round (launched 2001) has largely failed, leaving agricultural protectionism and developing-country concerns unresolved.
- Dispute settlement is slow and has been weakened (Appellate Body crisis since 2019).
- Free trade agreements (FTAs) and regional blocs proliferate, undermining the multilateral framework.

## Practice Problems

### Problem 1 — Trade Creation
Before joining a free trade area, Country P produces bicycles at $200, imports from Country Q (potential partner) at $160 subject to a 50% tariff, and imports from Country R (non-member) at $140 subject to the same 50% tariff.

(a) From whom does Country P import before the FTA? At what price?
(b) After the FTA with Q, the tariff on Q is removed but the tariff on R stays at 50%. From whom does P import now? At what price?
(c) Is this trade creation or trade diversion? Explain.
(d) What is the welfare gain per bicycle?

### Problem 2 — Trade Diversion
Country S forms a customs union with Country T. Before the union, S imported electronics from Country U (a low-cost global producer) at $90/unit with a 20% tariff (post-tariff price: $108). Country T produces electronics at $100/unit and also faced a 20% tariff before the union. The common external tariff is set at 30%.

(a) Calculate the post-union price from U (non-member) and from T (partner).
(b) From whom does S import after the union?
(c) Is this trade creation or trade diversion? Why?
(d) Calculate the welfare loss per unit.

### Problem 3 — Monetary Union: Costs vs. Benefits
Two countries, A and B, are considering joining a monetary union. Country A is a large exporter to B (60% of A's exports go to B). Labour mobility between A and B is very low. Business cycles between A and B are not synchronised — A is often in recession when B is booming and vice versa.

(a) Identify two benefits of monetary union that would be significant for A.
(b) Identify two costs of monetary union that would be significant for A.
(c) Based on OCA criteria, should A join? Explain.
(d) If A and B were to create a fiscal transfer mechanism (like a federal budget), how would this change your answer?

### Problem 4 — WTO Principles
Country X is a WTO member. It imposes a 5% tariff on rice imports from Country Y but a 15% tariff on rice imports from Country Z. Country Z is also a WTO member.

(a) Which WTO principle has Country X violated? Explain.
(b) What can Country Z do under the WTO framework?
(c) Identify one exception under WTO rules that might allow Country X's differential treatment.
(d) Explain how the WTO's dispute settlement mechanism works in this case.

### Problem 5 — Integration Stages
Match each scenario to the correct stage of economic integration and explain why.

(a) Members have zero tariffs on internal trade, free movement of workers, and a common external tariff of 10%.
(b) Members have zero internal tariffs, each sets its own external tariffs, and rules of origin are required.
(c) Members use the same currency, share a central bank, and have a common monetary policy.
(d) Members agree to reduce tariffs on agricultural products from fellow members by 50%, but not eliminate them.

### Problem 6 — IB-Style Essay (Challenging)

## Answers

### Answer 1 — Trade Creation
(a) Before the FTA, compare post-tariff prices:
- Domestic: $200
- From Q: $160 × 1.50 = **$240**
- From R: $140 × 1.50 = **$210**
Country P imports from **R at $210** (cheapest option).

(b) After FTA with Q (tariff on Q removed, tariff on R stays):
- From Q: $160 (no tariff)
- From R: $140 × 1.50 = $210
Country P imports from **Q at $160**.

(c) **Trade creation**: P switches from a higher-cost-after-tariff source (R at $210) to a genuinely lower-cost source (Q at $160). Q's cost ($160) < R's cost ($140) after accounting for pre-existing tariff distortions? No — R's actual cost ($140) is lower than Q's ($160). But P was importing from R at $210, not $140. The welfare gain arises because the tariff-distorted price ($210) was above Q's true cost ($160). Switching eliminates the deadweight loss.

(d) **Welfare gain per bicycle** = Previous price − New price = $210 − $160 = **$50** per bicycle. (This represents both the lower resource cost and the elimination of the tariff wedge on trade between P and Q.)

### Answer 2 — Trade Diversion
(a) Post-union prices:
- From U (non-member) with 30% CET: $90 × 1.30 = **$117**
- From T (partner): $100 (no tariff) = **$100**

(b) S imports from **T at $100** (cheaper than $117 from U).

(c) **Trade diversion**: S switches from the globally most efficient producer (U at $90 true cost) to a higher-cost partner (T at $100). Before the union, S paid $108 for U's goods (which covered the true $90 cost plus tariff). After the union, S pays $100 for T's goods (which covers T's less efficient $100 cost). The world loses because production shifts from a $90-cost producer to a $100-cost producer. The $9 tariff revenue loss ($108 − $90 = $18 previously collected; now $0 on the $100 from T) is also a factor.

(d) **Welfare loss per unit**: The additional resource cost = T's cost − U's cost = $100 − $90 = **$10 per unit**. This represents the efficiency loss from diverting production to a less efficient source.

### Answer 3 — Monetary Union: Costs vs. Benefits
(a) Two significant benefits for A:
1. **Elimination of exchange rate risk**: With 60% of exports going to B, currency uncertainty is a major cost. A common currency eliminates this, reducing hedging costs and promoting trade.
2. **Lower transaction costs**: No currency exchange fees on the large volume of trade with B, potentially saving 0.3–0.5% of transaction values.

(b) Two significant costs for A:
1. **Loss of independent monetary policy**: When A is in recession and B is booming, A needs low interest rates but the common central bank sets rates for the average, leaving A with inappropriately high rates.
2. **Cannot use exchange rate adjustment**: If A loses competitiveness, it cannot depreciate its currency against B. It must undergo painful "internal devaluation" (wage and price reductions).

(c) Based on OCA criteria, **A should probably NOT join**:
- **Labour mobility**: Very low → asymmetric shocks cannot be absorbed by workers moving from A to B.
- **Business cycle synchronisation**: Poor → common monetary policy will frequently be inappropriate for one country.
- **Trade integration**: High → this criterion supports joining.
- Overall, the failure on labour mobility and cycle synchronisation suggests that the costs of losing monetary independence outweigh the trade benefits.

(d) A **fiscal transfer mechanism** (like a federal budget automatically transferring funds from booming B to recession-hit A) would partially substitute for labour mobility and independent monetary policy. This would significantly improve the case for joining, as asymmetric shocks could be cushioned through fiscal transfers rather than requiring interest rate or exchange rate adjustment. This is how the United States — with high labour mobility AND a large federal budget — functions as a successful monetary union despite diverse regional economies.

### Answer 4 — WTO Principles
(a) Country X has violated the **Most-Favoured-Nation (MFN) principle**, which requires that any trade advantage granted to one WTO member must be extended to all other WTO members. The 5% tariff for Y vs. 15% for Z is discriminatory.

(b) Country Z can:
1. Request **consultations** with X to resolve the issue bilaterally.
2. If consultations fail, request the WTO to establish a **dispute settlement panel**.
3. If the panel rules in Z's favour and X does not comply, Z may be authorised to impose **retaliatory tariffs** on X's exports.

(c) A possible exception: if Country X and Country Y are part of a **free trade agreement or customs union** (allowed under GATT Article XXIV), they may grant each other preferential rates without extending them to all WTO members. Another exception: if Y is a **developing country** benefiting from the Generalised System of Preferences (GSP).

(d) The WTO dispute settlement process:
1. **Consultations** (60 days) — parties try to reach a negotiated solution.
2. **Panel establishment** — if consultations fail, a panel of trade experts is appointed.
3. **Panel ruling** (6–9 months) — the panel issues a report.
4. **Appeal** (if Appellate Body is functioning) — either party may appeal legal findings.
5. **Implementation** — the losing party must bring its measure into compliance within a "reasonable period."
6. **Retaliation** — if non-compliance continues, the winning party may be authorised to suspend equivalent trade concessions (retaliate).

### Answer 5 — Integration Stages
(a) **Common Market**: Zero internal tariffs + common external tariff (customs union element) + free movement of workers (factor mobility). This is the EU single market model.

(b) **Free Trade Area**: Zero internal tariffs but different external tariffs per country, requiring rules of origin to prevent trade deflection. This is the USMCA model.

(c) **Monetary Union**: Common currency, shared central bank, common monetary policy. This is the Eurozone model.

(d) **Preferential Trade Area**: Reduced but not eliminated tariffs on selected goods from members. This is the shallowest form of integration — early-stage arrangements like the ASEAN preferential trading arrangements in the 1970s.

### Answer 6 — IB-Style Essay (Model Answer Outline)
**Introduction**: The claim that monetary union benefits "always" outweigh costs is empirically false. The Eurozone's experience demonstrates that the balance depends critically on whether the participating economies meet Optimal Currency Area (OCA) criteria. For some countries, the costs have been severe; for others, benefits have dominated.

**Benefits**:
- **Transaction cost savings**: Estimates suggest 0.3–0.5% of GDP for smaller Eurozone economies.
- **Exchange rate certainty**: Boosted intra-Eurozone trade by 5–15% (Rose effect).
- **Price transparency and competition**: Harder for firms to price-discriminate across borders.
- **Credibility transfer**: Countries like Italy and Greece borrowed at lower interest rates after joining the euro — though this benefit became a cost when markets abruptly repriced sovereign risk in 2010.
- **Global role**: The euro is the second-most-important reserve currency, reducing transaction costs with non-Eurozone countries.

**Costs**:
- **One-size-fits-all monetary policy**: During 2010–2012, the ECB's rates were too high for Greece (in depression) and arguably too low for Germany (near full employment).
- **Loss of exchange rate flexibility**: Greece, Spain, and Portugal could not devalue to regain competitiveness, forcing painful "internal devaluation" — wage cuts, high unemployment, social unrest.
- **No fiscal union**: Without significant cross-border fiscal transfers, asymmetric shocks cannot be cushioned. The US, by contrast, has a large federal budget that automatically transfers ~30–40% of regional income shocks.
- **Divergent competitiveness**: Without exchange rate adjustment, productivity and inflation divergence creates persistent imbalances — Germany's persistent current account surpluses mirror deficits in Southern Europe.

**Evaluation (CLASPP)**:
- **Conclusion**: Benefits do NOT always outweigh costs. The outcome depends on OCA criteria satisfaction.
- **Limitations**: The textbook benefits (transaction costs, transparency) are small relative to the macroeconomic costs of losing adjustment mechanisms when OCA criteria are not met.
- **Assumptions**: The "always" claim assumes business cycles converge automatically (endogeneity of OCA), but Eurozone evidence shows convergence has been slow and incomplete.
- **Stakeholders**: Exporters and financial sectors gain; workers in less competitive regions bear the costs of adjustment through unemployment.
- **Priorities**: For highly integrated, synchronised economies (e.g., Germany and Austria), benefits clearly outweigh costs. For less integrated, divergent economies (e.g., Greece and Germany), costs dominate.
- **Policy alternatives**: Before joining, countries should assess OCA criteria honestly. The alternative of a managed float with inflation targeting may better serve diverging economies.

**Final conclusion**: The statement is too absolute. Monetary union can be beneficial for countries that satisfy OCA criteria, but it imposes severe costs on those that do not. The Eurozone's experience is a cautionary tale: incomplete integration without fiscal transfers creates fragility that can magnify, rather than mitigate, economic shocks.

## Evaluate

### "The benefits of joining a monetary union always outweigh the costs." Evaluate.

The assertion that monetary union benefits "always" outweigh costs is contradicted by the Eurozone's experience, which demonstrates that outcomes depend critically on structural alignment among members and the institutional framework supporting the currency.

**Conclusion**: Monetary union is neither universally beneficial nor universally harmful. The balance of costs and benefits is contingent on the degree to which participating economies satisfy Optimal Currency Area criteria and whether compensating institutional mechanisms exist.

**Limitations of the claim**: The "always" qualifier ignores the fundamental trade-off at the heart of monetary union: the gain from microeconomic efficiency (lower transaction costs, price transparency, reduced exchange rate uncertainty) versus the loss of macroeconomic policy tools (independent monetary policy, exchange rate adjustment). If macroeconomic shocks are asymmetric — one country in recession while another booms — the loss of policy autonomy can be catastrophic.

**Assumptions challenged**: Proponents assume business cycles converge after monetary union (the "endogeneity" hypothesis) and that labour mobility provides sufficient adjustment. Eurozone evidence is mixed: trade integration deepened post-euro, but labour mobility remains far below US levels due to linguistic and cultural barriers, and fiscal transfers are minimal at ~1% of Eurozone GDP versus ~30% of regional income shocks smoothed in the US.

**Stakeholder analysis**: Export-oriented firms and financial institutions benefit from currency stability and deeper capital markets. Workers in regions experiencing asymmetric negative shocks, however, bear the costs through prolonged unemployment — Spain's youth unemployment exceeded 50% during the Eurozone crisis, a direct consequence of being unable to devalue or set accommodative monetary policy.

**Priorities and trade-offs**: The priority for a country considering membership is honest self-assessment against OCA criteria: trade integration, business cycle correlation, labour mobility, and the existence of a fiscal transfer mechanism. Where these are strong (e.g., Austria within the Eurozone), benefits dominate. Where they are weak (e.g., Greece relative to the Eurozone core), the costs of lost policy autonomy are potentially devastating.

**Policy alternatives**: Countries that do not satisfy OCA criteria should consider retaining monetary sovereignty with a credible inflation-targeting framework and a managed float. The United Kingdom and Denmark negotiated opt-outs from the euro precisely because they recognised the trade-offs. Monetary union is not a destination all countries should reach; it is an arrangement suitable only when the structural preconditions are satisfied. (198 words)
