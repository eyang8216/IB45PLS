Lesson 36: Poverty - Types, Causes, and Policies

What You'll Learn

  • The distinction between absolute and relative poverty
  • The causes of poverty at individual, structural, and institutional levels
  • The economic and social impacts of poverty
  • Policy options to reduce poverty (progressive taxation, transfers, minimum wage, human capital investment)
  • The equity-efficiency trade-off
  • How to evaluate anti-poverty policies using CLASPP

1. Types of Poverty

Absolute Poverty

Individuals cannot meet their most basic needs - food, clean water, shelter, clothing, basic healthcare.

World Bank Definition: Living on less than $2.15 per day (PPP-adjusted, 2017 prices). Previously $1.90/day.

  • Objective, measurable threshold
  • Universal - same standard everywhere
  • Life-threatening - malnutrition, preventable disease
  • Concentrated in low-income countries
Worked Example: A family of four in rural Ethiopia earning $3.00/day combined lives below $2.15/person/day (threshold = $8.60/day combined). They are in absolute poverty.

Relative Poverty

Income below a percentage of the median income - unable to participate fully in normal social and economic life.

EU definition: below 60% of median equivalized household income. OECD: below 50%.

  • Subjective - changes as society becomes richer
  • Can never be fully eliminated (by definition)
  • About social exclusion, not survival

2. Causes of Poverty

Individual-Level

  • Unemployment, low wages, lack of education/skills, poor health/disability, discrimination, family structure

Structural Causes

  • Economic dualism, geographic isolation, lack of access to credit, asset inequality

Institutional Causes

  • Weak property rights, corruption, regressive tax systems, inadequate public services, conflict

The Poverty Trap (Poverty Cycle)

Low income - poor nutrition/health - low productivity - low income (reinforcing loop). Low income - cannot afford education - low skills - low-wage employment - low income. Breaking the cycle requires external intervention - market alone cannot solve it.

4. Policies to Reduce Poverty

A. Progressive Taxation

Higher-income earners pay larger proportion. Funds public services and transfers.

Example structure: 0% on $0-$10K, 15% on $10K-$40K, 25% on $40K-$100K, 40% on $100K+. Someone earning $25K pays $2,250 (9%). Someone earning $150K pays $39,500 (26.3%).

B. Transfer Payments

  • Means-tested: Only below threshold (efficient but poverty trap risk)
  • Universal: Everyone receives (no stigma but expensive)
  • Conditional cash transfers (CCTs): Brazil's Bolsa Familia - payments conditional on school attendance and vaccinations

C. Minimum Wage Laws

Raises working poor income, but if set too high - firms hire fewer workers, compliance issues in informal sectors.

D. Investment in Education and Healthcare

Supply-side anti-poverty policies - address root causes permanently. Early childhood education (highest returns), female education (reduces fertility, improves child health), universal healthcare (prevents medical bankruptcy).

E. Anti-Discrimination Legislation

Equal opportunity in employment, education, housing. Addresses poverty concentrated among marginalized groups.

5. The Equity-Efficiency Trade-Off

Equity goal: Redistribute from rich to poor - reduce poverty.
Efficiency concern: Redistribution may reduce work, save, invest incentives - smaller total output.

The "Leaky Bucket" (Arthur Okun)

Transferring money from rich to poor is like carrying water in a leaky bucket - some lost in process (admin costs, reduced incentives, deadweight loss). How leaky is acceptable?

Policy Design to Minimize the Trade-Off

  • Pro-work transfers (Earned Income Tax Credit supplements wages)
  • Investment over consumption (education/healthcare builds capacity)
  • Universal Basic Services (healthcare, education directly, less distortionary)

Always distinguish absolute vs relative poverty in definitions. When evaluating policies, discuss BOTH equity and efficiency effects. "Poverty trap" or "poverty cycle" and how to break it is excellent analysis. "Equity-efficiency trade-off" is the key concept. Case studies help: Brazil's Bolsa Familia, Nordic welfare states, US Earned Income Tax Credit.

Practice Problems

Problem 1 (Easy)
Distinguish between absolute poverty and relative poverty. State one measure for each and explain why absolute poverty can theoretically be eliminated while relative poverty cannot.
Problem 2 (Easy)
Identify and explain THREE causes of poverty. For each, state whether it is individual, structural, or institutional.
Problem 3 (Medium)
A country introduces progressive tax: 0% up to $12K; 15% on $12K-$50K; 30% on $50K-$150K; 40% above $150K. Calculate tax and average rate for (a) $30K and (b) $200K. Who bears larger burden?
Problem 4 (Medium)
Explain how conditional cash transfers (CCTs) break the poverty cycle. Use a specific CCT program example. Identify one limitation.
Problem 5 (Challenging)
A government proposes raising minimum wage from $10 to $15/hour (50% increase). Equilibrium wage for low-skilled is $11, labor demand elasticity = -0.3. Evaluate likely effects on poverty, employment, and welfare.
Problem 6 (IB-Style 15-mark)
"Progressive taxation combined with cash transfers is the most effective way to reduce poverty." Discuss this statement.

Answers

Answer 1: Absolute poverty: Inability to meet basic needs. World Bank: $2.15/day (PPP). Can theoretically be eliminated because defined against fixed threshold - if everyone's income rises above it, absolute poverty zero. Relative poverty: Below % of median income (50-60%). Cannot be eliminated because defined relative to others - as long as ANY income inequality exists, some will be below 60% of median. Relative poverty measures social exclusion, not survival.
Answer 2: 1. Unemployment (individual): No labor income, especially without unemployment benefits. 2. Lack of access to credit (structural): Poor can't borrow for education, business, or assets. Without collateral, banks won't lend - credit constraint traps poor. Microfinance attempts to address this. 3. Weak public education (institutional): Underfunded schools with poor quality and high dropout rates - children from poor families receive inadequate education, limiting future productivity, perpetuating intergenerational poverty.
Answer 3: (a) $30K: 0% on $12K = $0; 15% on $18K = $2,700. Total = $2,700. Average = 9.0%. (b) $200K: 0% on $12K = $0; 15% on $38K = $5,700; 30% on $100K = $30,000; 40% on $50K = $20,000. Total = $55,700. Average = 27.85%. The $200K earner pays much higher average rate (27.85% vs 9.0%) - definition of progressivity. Higher earner bears larger burden absolutely ($55,700 vs $2,700) and proportionally.
Answer 4: CCTs address poverty on two fronts: (1) Immediate relief - cash raises current income. (2) Long-term investment - conditions (school attendance, health checkups) ensure children accumulate human capital, breaking intergenerational poverty. Example: Brazil's Bolsa Familia - ~14 million families receive payments if children attend 85%+ school days and get vaccinations. Studies show increased enrollment, reduced dropout, improved nutrition, reduced inequality (Gini fell ~0.59 to ~0.53). Limitation: Administrative complexity - requires robust systems for monitoring. Fraud risk. Supply-side constraints - requiring school attendance only effective if quality schools accessible.
Answer 5: Current minimum $10, new $15, equilibrium $11 - $15 is binding (above market-clearing). Poverty effects: Workers keeping jobs at $15 see 50% increase ($10-$15). Full-time annual: $20K to $30K - some cross poverty line. But workers losing jobs become poorer. Employment effects: Wage increase = ($15-$11)/$11 = 36.4%. Employment change = -0.3 x 36.4% = -10.9%. Significant but not catastrophic reduction. Overall welfare: Total wage bill = wage x employment. If employment falls less than wages rise, wage bill increases. Remaining workers clearly better off. Job-losers clearly worse off without safety net. Consumers face slightly higher prices. Firms' profits fall. Conclusion: Likely reduces poverty among employed low-wage but may increase among those losing employment. Net effect ambiguous. Complementary policies (job training, EITC) would improve outcomes.
Answer 6 (Full IB-Style):
Introduction: Progressive taxation + cash transfers = direct fiscal redistribution. "Most effective" is a strong claim requiring evaluation against alternatives.
Arguments For: (1) Immediate impact - transfers provide instant relief, unlike education taking years. (2) Targetable - means-tested transfers directed at those below poverty line. (3) Respects autonomy - cash allows poor to decide what they need most. (4) Proven results - EITC, Bolsa Familia, European child benefits show significant poverty reduction.
Arguments Against: (1) Supply-side solutions address root causes - education, healthcare permanently increase earning capacity. Tax-and-transfer treats symptoms; education cures disease. (2) Poverty traps from means-testing - losing benefits as income rises discourages work. (3) Political sustainability - highly redistributive systems face opposition, capital flight, brain drain. (4) Minimum wage alternative - raises working poor income without fiscal cost (but doesn't help unemployed poor). (5) Inclusive growth - making growth broad-based may be more sustainable than ex-post redistribution.
Best Approach is a Mix: Short-term: cash transfers for immediate relief. Medium-term: progressive taxation funding public services. Long-term: supply-side policies increasing poor's earning capacity. Structural: anti-discrimination, property rights, financial inclusion.
Conclusion: Progressive taxation + cash transfers is powerful and direct, particularly short-run. For immediate survival needs, cash transfers are indeed most effective. For permanently eliminating poverty across generations, supply-side human capital investments are essential. Truly "most effective" combines both.

Evaluate

"Progressive taxation combined with cash transfers is the most effective way to reduce poverty."

CLASPP Analysis (180 words)

Conclusion: The statement is valid in the short run but incomplete as a long-term strategy. Tax-and-transfer systems provide immediate, direct poverty relief and are among the most effective tools for addressing current poverty. However, permanently eliminating poverty requires complementary supply-side policies addressing root causes.

Long-term vs Short-term: In the short term, cash transfers immediately raise incomes above poverty thresholds - no other policy works faster. In the long term, education, healthcare, and economic opportunity investments are superior because they enable self-sufficiency rather than perpetual dependence. The most effective approach spans both time horizons.

Assumptions: Assumes administrative capacity for progressive taxation and means-tested transfers without significant leakage or corruption. In countries with weak institutions (large informal sector, limited tax collection), this assumption fails. Also assumes political sustainability of redistribution.

Stakeholders: The poor benefit directly from transfers. Taxpayers (particularly high-income) bear the cost. Future generations benefit if transfers coupled with human capital investment but bear debt if funded by deficits. Workers near benefit thresholds face perverse incentives.

Priorities: If immediate survival is at stake (absolute poverty), cash transfers are indeed the priority. If relative poverty and social mobility are concerns, education and opportunity creation take priority.

Pros/Cons: Pro - fast, targeted, respects autonomy, empirically validated. Con - treats symptoms, creates dependency risks, politically fragile, administrative complexity.

Overall: Essential component of anti-poverty strategy, but not sufficient alone.
