Lesson 31: Unemployment — Policies and Evaluation

What You'll Learn

  • Policies for each type of unemployment (cyclical, structural, frictional, seasonal)
  • The concept of hysteresis and its dangers
  • How to evaluate unemployment policies using CLASPP
  • The AD/AS diagram mechanics behind demand-side policies

1. Policies for Cyclical (Demand-Deficient) Unemployment

Cyclical unemployment arises from insufficient aggregate demand (AD) in the economy — when the economy operates below full-employment output. The remedy is straightforward: shift AD to the right.

Expansionary Fiscal Policy

  • Government spending ↑ on infrastructure, public services, defense
  • Taxation ↓ (personal income tax, corporate tax) to boost disposable income and consumption
  • These increase the \(G\) and \(C\) components of AD: AD = C + I + G + (X − M)
  • Diagram effect: AD shifts right from AD₀ to AD₁, real GDP rises from Y₀ to Y₁, and unemployment falls
Worked Example: Suppose an economy's equilibrium is at Y₀ = $800 billion, but full-employment Y = $950 billion, with MPC = 0.75. What government spending increase would close the gap?

• The output gap = $950B − $800B = $150 billion
• Multiplier k = 1/(1 − MPC) = 1/(1 − 0.75) = 1/0.25 = 4
• Required ΔG = output gap ÷ k = $150B ÷ 4 = $37.5 billion ✓

Expansionary Monetary Policy

  • Interest rates ↓ → cost of borrowing falls → investment (I) and consumption (C) rise
  • Money supply ↑ (quantitative easing) → lower long-term rates, asset prices rise → wealth effect
  • Both shift AD right

Key advantage: Monetary policy can be changed relatively quickly (central bank independence). Key disadvantage: May be less effective in deep recessions ("liquidity trap" — near-zero rates already).

Limitations of Demand-Side Policies

  1. Time lags: Fiscal policy takes months to legislate; monetary policy transmission takes 6–18 months
  2. Crowding out: Government borrowing raises interest rates, reducing private I
  3. Inflation risk: If economy near full employment, AD right → demand-pull inflation
  4. Budget deficit: Expansionary fiscal increases government debt

2. Policies for Structural Unemployment

Structural unemployment is a supply-side problem — mismatch between workers' skills/location and employer needs. Demand-side policies cannot fix it.

Education and Retraining

  • Government-funded vocational training, apprenticeships, STEM programs
  • Retraining for workers displaced by technology
  • Effect: Improves occupational mobility of labor → LRAS shifts right

Subsidies for Hiring/Training

  • Firms receive tax credits or direct subsidies to hire long-term unemployed
  • Reduces marginal cost of hiring → firms willing to train new workers

Improving Geographic Mobility

  • Housing subsidies/relocation grants for workers to move to job-rich areas
  • Infrastructure investment connecting depressed regions to growth centers
Worked Example: A region has 15% structural unemployment after factory closure. Government funds a 6-month coding bootcamp at $8,000 per worker for 2,000 workers. If 70% find jobs at $45,000/year:

• Cost = 2,000 × $8,000 = $16 million
• New income stream = 1,400 × $45,000 = $63 million/year
• Payback period: $16M / ($63M × tax rate ~0.25) ≈ $16M / $15.75M ≈ 1.02 years ✓

3. Policies for Frictional Unemployment

Frictional unemployment is natural — workers between jobs searching for better matches. Policy aims to reduce search time, not eliminate it.

Improving Job Information

  • Public employment agencies, online job portals
  • Better matching algorithms linking workers to vacancies
  • Career counseling services
Important nuance: Some frictional unemployment is desirable — it signals a dynamic labor market where workers find optimal matches. Overly aggressive reduction could trap workers in bad fits.

4. Policies for Seasonal Unemployment

  • Economic diversification: Develop off-season activities (e.g., ski resort → summer hiking destination)
  • Flexible employment contracts: Year-round averaging of hours, multiple seasonal jobs coordinated

5. Hysteresis: When Cyclical Becomes Structural

Hysteresis (from Greek "hysterēsis" = shortcoming, delay) describes the phenomenon where prolonged cyclical unemployment transforms into structural unemployment.

The Mechanism

  1. Workers laid off during recession lose skills (atrophy)
  2. Stigma of long-term unemployment makes them less attractive to employers
  3. Detach from labor force entirely (discouraged workers)
  4. Even when AD recovers, these workers cannot fill vacancies → the NRU rises

Policy Implication: Act fast — short-term stimulus prevents hysteresis. Active labor market policies needed alongside demand management.

Practice Problems

Problem 1 (Easy)
Define cyclical unemployment and explain why expansionary monetary policy can reduce it.
Problem 2 (Easy)
A government increases spending by $50 billion. MPC = 0.8. Calculate the total increase in GDP and explain the multiplier process.
Problem 3 (Medium)
Distinguish between structural and frictional unemployment. For each, identify one appropriate policy and explain its mechanism.
Problem 4 (Medium)
Using an AD/AS diagram, explain how a prolonged recession might lead to hysteresis. What are the consequences for the natural rate of unemployment?
Problem 5 (Challenging)
Country X has: cyclical unemployment of 4%, structural of 3%, and frictional of 2%. The government proposes: (a) cutting interest rates by 50 basis points, (b) launching a $2B retraining program, and (c) creating a national jobs portal. Which policy addresses which type? Are there any risks?
Problem 6 (IB-Style 15-mark)
"Expansionary fiscal policy is the best tool to reduce unemployment." Discuss this statement.

Answers

Answer 1: Cyclical unemployment is unemployment caused by insufficient aggregate demand, typically during recessions when the economy operates below full-employment output. Expansionary monetary policy reduces it by lowering interest rates, which decreases the cost of borrowing for firms (boosting investment I) and households (boosting consumption C). Both increase AD. As AD shifts right, firms produce more output and hire more workers, reducing cyclical unemployment.
Answer 2: Multiplier k = 1/(1 − 0.8) = 1/0.2 = 5. Total ΔGDP = $50B × 5 = $250 billion.

Process: The initial $50B becomes income for construction workers, engineers, etc. (Round 1: +$50B). With MPC = 0.8, they spend 80% ($40B) on goods and services. That $40B becomes income for shopkeepers, manufacturers, etc. (Round 2: +$40B). They spend 80% of that ($32B), and so on. The infinite geometric series sums: 50 + 40 + 32 + 25.6 + … = 50 × 1/(1 − 0.8) = $250B.
Answer 3: Structural unemployment: Mismatch between workers' skills/location and available jobs. Policy: Retraining programs — workers learn new skills aligned with market demand, increasing occupational mobility. LRAS shifts right.

Frictional unemployment: Short-term unemployment of workers between jobs. Policy: Improved job information systems — reduces search time by making vacancies more visible and matching more efficient.

Key distinction: Structural is about mismatch in skills; frictional is about search/matching time.
Answer 4: Start with AD₀-LRAS-SRAS at Y₀ < Yf (recessionary gap). If recession persists for years: (1) Laid-off workers lose skills through disuse (human capital depreciation); (2) Long-term unemployed become stigmatized; (3) Some become discouraged and leave labor force; (4) The effective labor force shrinks → LRAS shifts LEFT.

Consequence: The NRU rises. Even when AD recovers, the economy reaches full employment at a lower Yf and higher unemployment rate. The recession leaves a permanent scar.
Answer 5: (a) Cutting interest rates → targets cyclical unemployment. Risk: If structural and frictional dominate (5%), economy may be near NRU, and further AD stimulus causes demand-pull inflation.
(b) Retraining program → targets structural unemployment. Risk: Takes years; workers may resist retraining; $2B might be insufficient.
(c) National jobs portal → targets frictional unemployment. Risk: Low risk, but effectiveness limited.

Overall: Country X's total unemployment = 9%. NRU = 5%, cyclical = 4%. Monetary policy addresses the 4% cyclical; retraining addresses the 3% structural. The mix is appropriate, but timing matters.
Answer 6 (Full IB-Style Response):
Introduction: Expansionary fiscal policy increases AD through higher G or lower taxes. It directly targets cyclical unemployment. However, "best" implies superiority over all alternatives — a claim requiring careful evaluation.

Arguments For: (1) Direct and potent — G spending is a direct injection; multiplier amplifies it. (2) Targetable — toward labor-intensive sectors or distressed regions. (3) Works in liquidity trap when monetary policy can't. (4) Speed of impact — infrastructure creates jobs quickly.

Arguments Against: (1) Only fixes cyclical unemployment — structural, frictional, and seasonal are supply-side problems. (2) Time lags — recognition, legislative, implementation. Stimulus may become pro-cyclical. (3) Crowding out — government borrowing raises rates, reducing private I; in open economies, exchange rate appreciates, reducing X−M. (4) Budget deficit and debt burden on future generations. (5) Hysteresis risk if debate creates delay.

Better Alternatives for Non-Cyclical Types: Structural → Education, retraining (shift LRAS right, non-inflationary, permanent). Frictional → Job information improvements. Seasonal → Economic diversification.

Conclusion: Expansionary fiscal is powerful for cyclical unemployment, but not "the best" in general. A comprehensive approach matches policy to unemployment type. The best strategy is a policy mix tailored to the specific composition, applied quickly to prevent hysteresis.

Evaluate

"Expansionary fiscal policy is the best tool to reduce unemployment."

CLASPP Analysis (157 words)

Conclusion: The statement is partly true but oversimplifies. Expansionary fiscal policy effectively targets cyclical unemployment but fails against structural and frictional unemployment, making it only one component of a broader policy toolkit.

Long-term vs Short-term: In the short run, fiscal expansion quickly boosts AD and cuts cyclical unemployment. In the long run, supply-side policies (education, retraining) are essential for reducing the natural rate. Short-term fiscal fixes may create long-term debt burdens.

Assumptions: The statement assumes unemployment is primarily cyclical. It also assumes a functioning multiplier, no crowding out, and timely implementation — all contestable.

Stakeholders: Cyclical-unemployed workers benefit immediately. Taxpayers bear debt costs. Future generations face higher taxes or reduced services. Workers with structural unemployment gain nothing.

Priorities: In a deep recession with high cyclical unemployment, fiscal expansion is indeed the priority. When structural unemployment dominates, retraining programs take priority.

Pros/Cons: Pro — fast-acting, targeted, multiplier-amplified. Con — only addresses demand-side, creates deficits, risks inflation and crowding out, useless against skills mismatch.

Overall: "Best" is contextual. A policy mix matching the composition of unemployment is superior to any single tool.
