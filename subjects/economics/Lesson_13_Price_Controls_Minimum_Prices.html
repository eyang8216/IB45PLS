# Lesson 13: Price Controls — Minimum Prices (Price Floors) ## What You'll Learn By the end of this lesson, you will be able to: - Define a minimum price (price floor) and explain why governments impose them - Draw and label a minimum price diagram for goods markets - Analyse the consequences: surpluses, government expenditure, and welfare effects - Apply minimum price analysis to the minimum wage in labour markets - Evaluate minimum price policies using economic reasoning and real-world evidence --- ## 1. What a Minimum Price Is and Why Governments Use It ### What a Price Floor Is A **minimum price**, also called a **price floor**, is a legally established lowest price at which a good, service, or factor of production may be sold. For the floor to have any effect on the market, it must be set above the free-market equilibrium price. Sellers cannot legally sell below this price. If a price floor is set below the equilibrium price, it has no effect — the market naturally settles at a higher price, and the floor is never reached. Economists call this a non-binding price floor. ### Why Governments Impose Minimum Prices Governments impose price floors for three main reasons. First, governments use price floors to protect the incomes of producers. Agricultural markets are volatile — a bumper harvest can cause prices to collapse, leaving farmers unable to cover their costs. A minimum price guarantees farmers a stable income regardless of market fluctuations. Second, governments use price floors to protect the incomes of workers. The minimum wage is the most widespread example: it sets a floor on the price of labour, ensuring that workers earn at least a specified amount per hour. Third, governments use minimum prices to discourage consumption of goods considered harmful. For example, Scotland introduced a minimum unit price for alcohol in 2018, making it illegal to sell alcohol below 50 pence per unit. The aim was to reduce binge drinking by raising the price of the cheapest, strongest drinks. A common mistake students make is thinking that a minimum price is always binding. In fact, a price floor set below the equilibrium price does absolutely nothing — the market ignores it. For the floor to change outcomes, the regulated price must be above where supply and demand naturally meet. --- ## 2. The Minimum Price Diagram — Goods Market ### What Happens When a Binding Floor Is Imposed Consider a market for wheat. In an unregulated market, the equilibrium price is £200 per tonne, and at that price, farmers supply exactly as much as millers want to buy — say, 50,000 tonnes. The government, worried about volatile farm incomes, sets a minimum price of £260 per tonne. Sellers cannot legally sell below £260. At £260 per tonne, two things happen simultaneously. On the demand side, the law of demand operates: millers face a higher price, so they reduce their purchases. Suppose quantity demanded falls to 30,000 tonnes. On the supply side, the law of supply operates: farmers see a guaranteed high price, so they expand production. Suppose quantity supplied rises to 80,000 tonnes. The result is a persistent surplus: quantity supplied (80,000 tonnes) minus quantity demanded (30,000 tonnes) equals 50,000 tonnes that nobody wants to buy at the floor price. ### The Government's Problem: What to Do with the Surplus In an unregulated market, a surplus would push the price down until equilibrium was restored. But the price floor prevents the price from falling. The surplus does not disappear — it sits there, unsold. For agricultural price supports, governments typically solve this problem by buying the surplus themselves. The government becomes the purchaser of last resort. This has a direct cost to taxpayers: the floor price multiplied by the surplus quantity. At £260 per tonne and a surplus of 50,000 tonnes, the government spends £260 × 50,000 = £13,000,000. This is not the only cost. The government must also store the surplus wheat, which costs money. Wheat can spoil. If the government dumps the surplus on world markets, it depresses world prices and harms farmers in other countries. The European Union's Common Agricultural Policy famously generated "butter mountains" and "wine lakes" — vast stockpiles of surplus produce bought at guaranteed minimum prices and stored at enormous taxpayer expense. A common mistake students make is thinking that a price floor reduces the price consumers pay. The opposite is true: consumers pay the higher floor price. The floor protects sellers, not buyers. Consumer surplus falls because consumers pay more and buy less. --- ## 3. Welfare Analysis of a Price Floor ### Consumer Surplus, Producer Surplus, and Deadweight Loss Welfare analysis lets us measure who gains and who loses from a price floor. **Consumer surplus** is the difference between what consumers are willing to pay and what they actually pay, summed over all units purchased. Before the floor, consumer surplus is the area below the demand curve and above the equilibrium price. After the floor, consumers pay a higher price and buy fewer units. Consumer surplus shrinks — consumers are unambiguously worse off. **Producer surplus** is the difference between the price producers receive and their marginal cost, summed over all units sold. The effect on producer surplus is ambiguous. Producers receive a higher price on the units they sell, which increases surplus. But they sell fewer units (Qd instead of Qe), which decreases surplus. If the government buys the surplus, producer surplus increases substantially — producers sell everything they produce at the high floor price. Without government purchase, the net effect depends on the elasticities of supply and demand. **Government expenditure** is the cost of buying the surplus: the floor price multiplied by the surplus quantity. This money comes from taxpayers and has an opportunity cost — it could have funded schools, hospitals, or infrastructure instead. **Deadweight loss (DWL)** is the welfare that is lost to society and not captured by anyone. It arises because the price floor prevents mutually beneficial transactions. Between Qd and Qe, there are consumers who value wheat more than the cost of producing it but cannot legally buy it at a price both they and producers would accept. The transactions that would have occurred at the free-market equilibrium do not happen. The DWL is represented by the triangle bounded by the demand and supply curves between Qd and Qe. --- ## 4. The Minimum Wage — A Price Floor in the Labour Market ### What the Minimum Wage Is A **minimum wage** is a price floor applied to the labour market. It sets a legal minimum hourly wage that employers must pay their workers. When the minimum wage is set above the equilibrium wage rate, it is binding and produces effects analogous to those in the goods market. ### How to Analyse the Minimum Wage In the labour market diagram, the horizontal axis measures quantity of labour (number of workers or hours), and the vertical axis measures the wage rate (price of labour). The demand for labour comes from firms; the supply of labour comes from workers. When a minimum wage (Wm) is set above the equilibrium wage (We), two effects occur. Firms, facing a higher cost per worker, reduce the number of workers they hire. The quantity of labour demanded falls from Le to Ld. Workers, seeing a higher wage, increase the quantity of labour they supply. The quantity of labour supplied rises from Le to Ls. The result is a surplus of labour: Ls minus Ld represents workers who want to work at the minimum wage but cannot find a job. This surplus is unemployment created by the minimum wage. ### Key Effects on the Labour Market First, there is a potential unemployment effect. Some workers — particularly young, low-skilled workers whose productivity is below the minimum wage — may lose their jobs or fail to find one. Second, workers who keep their jobs earn more. Their incomes rise, which can reduce poverty among the working poor. Third, firms may substitute capital for labour. If labour becomes more expensive, firms have an incentive to invest in automation — self-checkout machines replacing cashiers, for example. Fourth, the magnitude of the unemployment effect depends on the elasticity of labour demand. If firms find it difficult to replace workers with machines (inelastic demand), the employment reduction is small. If firms can easily automate (elastic demand), the employment reduction is large. A common mistake students make is thinking that the minimum wage always causes massive unemployment. In fact, empirical evidence is mixed. David Card and Alan Krueger's influential 1994 study of fast-food restaurants in New Jersey found no evidence of employment reduction after a minimum wage increase. The effect depends on how high the minimum wage is set relative to the prevailing wage and on the structure of the labour market. --- ## 5. Real-World Examples ### The European Union's Common Agricultural Policy (CAP) For decades, the CAP set minimum prices for agricultural products including grain, dairy, and wine. The policy succeeded in stabilising farm incomes but at enormous cost. By the 1980s, the EU was spending over 60% of its total budget on agriculture, much of it to purchase and store surplus production. The "butter mountains" and "wine lakes" became symbols of wasteful intervention. Reforms since the 1990s have shifted the CAP away from price supports and toward direct income payments to farmers, decoupling support from production levels. ### Minimum Wage in the United Kingdom The UK introduced a National Minimum Wage in 1999 and later a higher National Living Wage for workers aged 23 and over. As of 2024, the National Living Wage is £11.44 per hour. Despite predictions of job losses, employment in low-wage sectors such as hospitality and retail continued to grow in subsequent years. The Low Pay Commission, which recommends the rate, sets it with reference to the 60% of median earnings target, aiming to raise pay without causing significant employment damage. ### Scotland's Minimum Unit Pricing for Alcohol In 2018, Scotland became the first country in the world to introduce a minimum unit price for alcohol, initially set at 50 pence per unit. The policy targeted cheap, high-strength alcohol — the kind sold in large bottles for very low prices that is associated with harmful and binge drinking. Early evaluations found a reduction in alcohol purchases, particularly among the heaviest drinkers, though the policy remains debated. --- ## IB Exam Tip When drawing a minimum price diagram in an IB exam, label every element: the axes (Price and Quantity or Wage Rate and Quantity of Labour), the supply and demand curves, the equilibrium price and quantity, the floor price, the quantities demanded and supplied at the floor, and the surplus. If asked about welfare, shade the consumer surplus, producer surplus, government expenditure rectangle, and deadweight loss triangle clearly. For minimum wage questions, the axes must say "Wage Rate" and "Quantity of Labour." Do not use "Price" for the wage axis — that loses a mark. Always state explicitly that the floor must be set above equilibrium to be binding. This single sentence demonstrates understanding and earns marks. --- ## Worked Example 1: Calculating the Effects of a Price Floor A government sets a minimum price of £15 per kilogram for coffee beans. The demand for coffee beans is given by Qd = 1,000 − 40P, and the supply is given by Qs = −200 + 60P, where P is the price in pounds per kilogram and Q is in kilograms. **Problem:** (a) Find the free-market equilibrium price and quantity. (b) Calculate quantity demanded and quantity supplied at the floor price. (c) Determine the size of the surplus. (d) Calculate the government's expenditure if it buys the entire surplus. **Approach:** To find the free-market equilibrium, we set quantity demanded equal to quantity supplied and solve for P. Then we substitute this P into either equation to find Q. For the floor, we evaluate both equations at P = 15. The surplus is Qs minus Qd. Government expenditure is the floor price multiplied by the surplus. **Step 1 — Find equilibrium:** Set Qd = Qs. 1,000 − 40P = −200 + 60P 1,000 + 200 = 60P + 40P 1,200 = 100P P = £12 per kilogram Substitute P = 12 into the demand equation: Qd = 1,000 − 40(12) = 1,000 − 480 = 520 kilograms At equilibrium, Qs also equals 520 kilograms. (Check: −200 + 60(12) = −200 + 720 = 520. ✓) **Step 2 — Evaluate at the floor price of P = £15:** Qd at floor = 1,000 − 40(15) = 1,000 − 600 = 400 kilograms Qs at floor = −200 + 60(15) = −200 + 900 = 700 kilograms **Step 3 — Calculate the surplus:** Surplus = Qs − Qd = 700 − 400 = 300 kilograms **Step 4 — Calculate government expenditure:** Government expenditure = Pf × surplus = £15 × 300 = £4,500 **Why this makes sense:** At the higher floor price (£15 versus £12 equilibrium), consumers want less (400 kg instead of 520 kg) and producers want to supply more (700 kg instead of 520 kg). The gap is a surplus of 300 kg. The government must spend £4,500 to buy this surplus and maintain the floor. This £4,500 is a cost to taxpayers that did not exist in the free market. --- ## Worked Example 2: Minimum Wage in a Local Labour Market A local labour market for restaurant workers has demand for labour given by Ld = 500 − 10W and supply given by Ls = −100 + 20W, where W is the hourly wage in pounds and L is the number of workers. **Problem:** (a) Find the equilibrium wage and employment level. (b) If the government imposes a minimum wage of £25 per hour, calculate the number of workers employed, the number seeking work, and the unemployment created. (c) Compare the total wage bill before and after the minimum wage. **Approach:** To find equilibrium, set Ld = Ls. Then evaluate Ld and Ls at the minimum wage. The wage bill is the wage rate multiplied by the number of employed workers (Ld at the relevant wage). **Step 1 — Find equilibrium:** Set Ld = Ls. 500 − 10W = −100 + 20W 500 + 100 = 20W + 10W 600 = 30W W = £20 per hour Employment at equilibrium: L = 500 − 10(20) = 500 − 200 = 300 workers (Check: Ls = −100 + 20(20) = −100 + 400 = 300. ✓) **Step 2 — Evaluate at Wm = £25 per hour:** Workers employed (Ld at W = 25) = 500 − 10(25) = 500 − 250 = 250 workers Workers seeking work (Ls at W = 25) = −100 + 20(25) = −100 + 500 = 400 workers Unemployment = Ls − Ld = 400 − 250 = 150 workers **Step 3 — Compare wage bills:** Wage bill at equilibrium = 300 workers × £20 = £6,000 per hour Wage bill after minimum wage = 250 workers × £25 = £6,250 per hour Change = £6,250 − £6,000 = +£250 per hour **Why this makes sense:** The total wage bill increases slightly despite the fall in employment because the wage increase (£5 per hour, a 25% rise) is proportionally larger than the employment reduction (50 workers, a 16.7% fall). However, 150 workers are now unemployed — they earn nothing. The average wage among those who keep their jobs rises, but 50 workers lose their jobs and 100 additional workers who would like to work at £25 cannot find employment. --- ## Practice Problems **Problem 1.** Define a minimum price (price floor). A binding price floor is set above the free-market equilibrium price and has an effect on the market because it prevents the price from falling to equilibrium. A non-binding price floor is set below the free-market equilibrium price and has no effect because the market naturally settles at a higher price where the floor never comes into play. Only binding floors create surpluses and change market outcomes. **Answer 2.** (a) The free-market equilibrium occurs where quantity demanded equals quantity supplied. Looking at the data, at £2, Qd is 700 and Qs is 500 — they are not equal. At £3, Qd is 500 and Qs is 700 — also not equal. The equilibrium must lie between £2 and £3, where the two quantities cross. Since the data are given at discrete price points, the equilibrium price is approximately £2.50 per litre, with an equilibrium quantity of approximately 600 litres. (b) At the floor price of £3 per litre, Qd is 500 litres and Qs is 700 litres. The surplus is Qs minus Qd, which is 700 − 500 = 200 litres. (c) The government buys the surplus at the floor price. Direct cost = Pf × surplus = £3 × 200 = £600. A common pitfall: students sometimes calculate the surplus as Qs − Qe rather than Qs − Qd at the floor price. The surplus is the gap between what producers want to sell and what consumers want to buy at the regulated price, not at the original equilibrium. **Answer 3.** The diagram should show: a downward-sloping demand curve and an upward-sloping supply curve intersecting at the equilibrium point (Pe, Qe). A horizontal line at Pf above Pe marks the floor. Where this line meets the demand curve, drop down to find Qd. Where it meets the supply curve, drop down to find Qs. The horizontal distance between Qs and Qd is the surplus. Government expenditure is the rectangle with height equal to Pf and width equal to the surplus (Qs − Qd). The deadweight loss is the triangle bounded by the supply and demand curves between Qd and Qe. **Answer 4.** (a) Set Ld = Ls: 600 − 20W = −100 + 30W. Then 600 + 100 = 30W + 20W, so 700 = 50W, and W = £14 per hour. Substitute into demand: Ld = 600 − 20(14) = 600 − 280 = 320 workers. At equilibrium, 320 workers are employed at £14 per hour. (b) At Wm = £18 per hour: Ld = 600 − 20(18) = 600 − 360 = 240 workers employed. Ls = −100 + 30(18) = −100 + 540 = 440 workers seeking work. Unemployment = Ls − Ld = 440 − 240 = 200 workers. (c) Total wage bill before minimum wage: 320 workers × £14 per hour = £4,480. Total wage bill after minimum wage: 240 workers × £18 per hour = £4,320. Total earnings fall by £160. Although the wage rate rose by £4 per hour, the number of employed workers fell by 80, which more than offset the wage increase. This demonstrates that a minimum wage does not always increase total labour income — it depends on the elasticity of labour demand. **Answer 5.** (a) A minimum price for agricultural goods, when set above the free-market equilibrium, creates a persistent surplus. At the higher regulated price, consumers reduce the quantity they demand while producers expand the quantity they supply. The gap between quantity supplied and quantity demanded is a surplus that does not self-correct because the price cannot fall. For example, if the equilibrium price for wheat is £200 per tonne and the floor is set at £260 per tonne, farmers may supply 80,000 tonnes while millers demand only 30,000 tonnes, creating a surplus of 50,000 tonnes. (3 marks: 1 for binding floor, 1 for demand/supply responses, 1 for surplus description.) (b) One advantage is that farmers benefit from higher and more stable incomes, which protects them from volatile world prices and allows them to plan production without fear of price collapse. One disadvantage is that taxpayers bear the cost of purchasing and storing the surplus, which has an opportunity cost — the funds could have been used for education, healthcare, or infrastructure. Additionally, consumers pay higher prices for food, which is regressive since food takes a larger share of low-income household budgets. (3 marks: 1 for advantage to stakeholder, 1 for disadvantage to stakeholder, 1 for explicitly naming stakeholders.) **Answer 6.** (a) A minimum price sets a legal floor above equilibrium. At the floor, supply exceeds demand, creating a surplus. The government typically buys the surplus. In the diagram, the price is on the vertical axis, quantity on the horizontal axis, and the floor is a horizontal line above equilibrium. A subsidy is a payment per unit to producers that shifts the supply curve downward (or rightward) by the subsidy amount. The new equilibrium has a lower consumer price and a higher quantity. Producers receive the consumer price plus the subsidy. (b) Cost bearer: for the minimum price, taxpayers fund the purchase of the surplus. For the subsidy, taxpayers fund the per-unit payment on all units produced. Consumer price: under a minimum price, consumers pay a higher price. Under a subsidy, consumers pay a lower price. Surplus: the minimum price creates a physical surplus that must be stored or disposed of. The subsidy does not create a physical surplus — the market clears at the new equilibrium. (c) A subsidy may be preferable when the government wants to lower consumer prices (for example, to make food affordable) rather than raise them. A subsidy may also be preferable when the government wants to avoid the waste and administrative cost of purchasing and storing surplus production. Additionally, if the goal is to increase consumption of a good with positive externalities — such as nutritious food — a subsidy achieves this while a minimum price reduces consumption. --- ## Evaluate **Question:** "A minimum wage always increases unemployment among low-skilled workers." Evaluate this statement. **Model Answer:** The statement asserts a universal, inevitable relationship between minimum wages and unemployment. While the basic supply-and-demand model predicts that a price floor above equilibrium creates a surplus — in this case, unemployment — the real-world evidence is far more nuanced. The statement is too absolute to be accepted without qualification. The theoretical case for the statement rests on the competitive labour market model. When a minimum wage is set above the equilibrium wage, the quantity of labour demanded falls while the quantity supplied rises, producing a surplus of workers who cannot find employment. Low-skilled workers, whose productivity is closest to the minimum wage, are the most likely to be priced out of the market. Firms may substitute machinery for expensive labour — installing self-checkout kiosks rather than hiring cashiers, for example. However, this analysis assumes that labour markets are perfectly competitive, which they often are not. In a **monopsony** — a labour market dominated by a single or few employers — firms have wage-setting power. The monopsonist hires fewer workers and pays a lower wage than a competitive market would produce. In this context, a moderate minimum wage can simultaneously increase both wages and employment by forcing the monopsonist closer to the competitive outcome. This is not a theoretical curiosity: many local labour markets, especially in rural areas or for specialised skills, have monopsonistic features. The empirical evidence is instructive. David Card and Alan Krueger's landmark 1994 study examined fast-food restaurants in New Jersey and neighbouring Pennsylvania after New Jersey raised its minimum wage. They found no evidence of employment reduction; in some specifications, employment actually increased. Subsequent research across many countries has generally found that moderate minimum wages — set at roughly 45 to 55 per cent of the median wage — have small or negligible employment effects. Large increases, however, particularly those that push the minimum wage above 60 per cent of the median, do show measurable job losses. The magnitude of any employment effect also depends on the elasticity of labour demand. In industries where labour is a small share of total costs and cannot easily be replaced by machines — such as care work — demand is inelastic, and the employment effect is small. In manufacturing, where automation is feasible and labour is a larger cost share, the effect may be larger. The statement ignores the possibility that higher wages can generate offsetting benefits. Better-paid workers may be more productive, reducing turnover and training costs for firms. Higher wages increase workers' purchasing power, which feeds back into demand for goods and services. These dynamic effects can partially or fully offset the static employment reduction predicted by the simple model. Turning to stakeholders: low-skilled workers who retain their jobs unambiguously gain from a higher minimum wage. Those who lose jobs or cannot find them lose. Firms face higher wage bills but may benefit from lower turnover. Consumers may pay slightly higher prices if firms pass on costs. The government may spend less on in-work benefits if higher wages reduce the need for income top-ups. The most sensible policy approach is not to abolish minimum wages but to set them at a level that balances the income gains for employed workers against the risk of job loss for those at the margin. Differentiated minimum wages — lower rates for younger workers or for regions with lower living costs — can preserve the protective function while minimising employment damage. Complementary policies, such as earned income tax credits and investment in training, can support low-skilled workers without placing the entire burden on employers. In conclusion, the statement is an oversimplification. A minimum wage set at a moderate level in an economy with some monopsony power and inelastic labour demand may produce little to no unemployment. A minimum wage set too high in a competitive market with elastic demand will indeed cause job losses. The effect is not universal; it depends on the level of the minimum wage, the structure of the labour market, and the presence of complementary policies. --- **Next Lesson:** Lesson 14 — Indirect Taxes
