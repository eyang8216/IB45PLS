Lesson 7: Consumer & Producer Surplus, Allocative Efficiency

Syllabus reference: Unit 2.5

Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to:

  • Define consumer surplus and producer surplus and identify them on a diagram
  • Explain the concept of community (social) surplus and allocative efficiency
  • Calculate consumer and producer surplus from demand and supply data
  • Analyse how shifts in demand and supply affect welfare

1. Prior Knowledge Check

Q1: In Lesson 6, you learned that the equilibrium price is where Qd = Qs. At the equilibrium price of £6 for pizzas, 300 pizzas are traded. Why do some consumers value a pizza at more than £6, and why are some producers willing to sell at less?

Show

Consumers have different willingness to pay — some really want the product and would pay more. Producers have different costs — some can produce cheaply and would still profit at a lower price. This diversity creates surplus: the gap between what someone is willing to pay/accept and what they actually pay/receive.

2. Consumer Surplus

Consumer surplus (CS) is the difference between the maximum price a consumer is willing to pay for a good and the price they actually pay. It measures the benefit consumers receive from participating in a market — the "extra" satisfaction they get beyond what they paid for.

On a diagram, CS is the area below the demand curve and above the equilibrium price, up to the quantity traded. If the demand curve shows willingness to pay at each quantity, and the horizontal line at Pe shows what consumers actually pay, the triangle between them is CS.

3. Producer Surplus

Producer surplus (PS) is the difference between the price a producer actually receives for a good and the minimum price they would be willing to accept. It measures the benefit producers receive — the "extra" revenue above their costs.

On a diagram, PS is the area above the supply curve and below the equilibrium price, up to the quantity traded.

4. Community Surplus and Allocative Efficiency

Community surplus (also called social surplus or total welfare) = CS + PS. At the free-market equilibrium, community surplus is maximised. Any deviation from equilibrium (price controls, taxes, monopoly) reduces community surplus — this reduction is called deadweight loss (DWL).

Allocative efficiency occurs when resources are allocated to produce the combination of goods that society most values. At the market equilibrium, marginal benefit (MB) = marginal cost (MC), which is the condition for allocative efficiency. The last unit produced provides exactly as much benefit to consumers as it costs to produce.

Check-in: If a market produces 200 units but the equilibrium quantity is 300, is the market achieving allocative efficiency? What about community surplus?

Show

No — at 200 units (below Qe), MB > MC. Consumers value the next unit more than it costs to produce. Society would gain from producing more. Community surplus is not maximised; there is a deadweight loss — surplus that neither consumers nor producers capture, representing wasted potential gains from trade.

5. Worked Examples

Example 1: Calculating Surplus

Problem: The demand curve for cinema tickets is P=20−0.05Q. The supply curve is P=2+0.01Q. Find the equilibrium, then calculate CS and PS.

Step 1: 20−0.05Q = 2+0.01Q → 18=0.06Q → Qe=300. Pe=20−0.05(300)=£5.

Step 2: CS is the triangle below D (max price=20) and above Pe=5, from Q=0 to 300. CS = ½ × (20−5) × 300 = £2,250.

Step 3: PS is the triangle below Pe=5 and above S (min price=2), from Q=0 to 300. PS = ½ × (5−2) × 300 = £450. Community surplus = £2,700.

⚠️ Common Wrong: Using the equilibrium quantity for only one of the triangle dimensions. Both base and height must correspond correctly.

Example 2: Welfare After a Shift

Problem: Demand for a good increases, shifting the demand curve right. Pe rises from £10 to £14 and Qe rises from 200 to 260. Analyse the change in CS. (Exam trap: assuming CS always falls when price rises.)

Step 1: The price rise alone would reduce CS (consumers pay more). But demand increased — the demand curve itself shifted right, meaning consumers value the good more. CS can increase even at a higher price if the demand shift is large enough.

Step 2: The net effect on CS depends on the relative sizes of the demand shift and the resulting price increase. ⚠️ Common Wrong: Mechanically saying "price up → CS down" without considering that the demand curve itself moved.

⚠️ Common Mistakes

  1. Mistake: Calculating CS as the rectangle (Pmax−Pe)×Q instead of the triangle. Correct: CS is the triangular area ½×base×height.
  2. Mistake: Thinking allocative efficiency means maximising producer surplus. Correct: It means maximising community surplus (CS+PS), not one side alone.
  3. Mistake: Assuming any deviation from equilibrium is inefficient. Correct: There may be valid reasons (equity, correcting externalities) for deviating, even if it creates DWL.

7. Key Terms

TermDefinition
Consumer surplusDifference between maximum willingness to pay and actual price paid
Producer surplusDifference between price received and minimum willingness to accept
Community surplusSum of consumer and producer surplus; measure of total welfare
Deadweight lossLoss of community surplus when market is not at equilibrium
Allocative efficiencyResources allocated to maximise community surplus; MB = MC
