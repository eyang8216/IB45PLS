Lesson 35: Inequality - Lorenz Curve and Gini Coefficient

What You'll Learn

  • The difference between income and wealth, and between equality and equity
  • How to construct and interpret Lorenz curves
  • How to calculate the Gini coefficient
  • Limitations of the Gini coefficient
  • How to use these tools to compare inequality across countries and time

1. Income vs Wealth

Income

  • A flow concept - money received per period
  • Sources: wages, rent, interest, dividends, profits, transfers

Wealth

  • A stock concept - value of assets owned at a point in time
  • Components: property, shares, bonds, savings, vehicles

Why the distinction matters: Wealth inequality is typically far greater than income inequality. Wealth generates income (rent, dividends) - wealth inequality perpetuates income inequality.

3. The Lorenz Curve

Graphical representation of income (or wealth) distribution (Max O. Lorenz, 1905).

Construction

  1. Rank households from poorest to richest
  2. Group into quintiles (fifths) or deciles (tenths)
  3. Plot cumulative % income (vertical) vs cumulative % population (horizontal)
  • 45-degree line = perfect equality (each x% of population receives x% of income)
  • Lorenz curve bows below - further bow = greater inequality
  • Area A = area between 45-degree line and Lorenz curve
  • Area B = area under Lorenz curve

4. The Gini Coefficient

$$\text{Gini Coefficient} = \frac{A}{A + B}$$

Where A = area between 45-degree line and Lorenz curve, B = area under Lorenz curve. A + B = 0.5 (triangle area).

Scale Interpretation

Gini ValueMeaning
0Perfect equality
0.0-0.3Low inequality
0.3-0.5Medium inequality
0.5-0.7High inequality
0.7-1.0Very high inequality
1 (or 100)Perfect inequality (one person has ALL income)

6. Limitations of the Gini Coefficient

a) Same Gini, Different Distributions

Two countries can have identical Gini but very different underlying distributions. The Gini doesn't tell you WHERE inequality occurs.

b) No Absolute Income Information

Gini of 0.40 in Switzerland (GDP/capita $90K) vs Bangladesh ($2.5K) means entirely different things. Gini is purely relative.

c) Different Definitions of Income

Before-tax or after-tax? Including transfers? Including non-cash benefits? Comparability issues across countries.

d) Lifecycle Effects

Society where everyone has identical lifetime earnings but different earnings at different ages shows positive Gini. Not "unfair" inequality.

Problem 6 (IB-Style 15-mark)
"The Gini coefficient is the single best measure of a country's inequality." Discuss this statement.

Answers

Answer 1: Diagram: 1x1 box. Horizontal = cumulative % population (0-100). Vertical = cumulative % income (0-100). 45-degree line: diagonal from (0,0) to (100,100) - perfect equality. Lorenz curve: bowed below diagonal. Area A: between diagonal and Lorenz curve - degree of inequality (larger A = more inequality). Area B: under Lorenz curve. Gini = A/(A+B) = A/0.5 = 2A. Perfect equality: Lorenz IS the diagonal - A=0 - Gini=0. Perfect inequality: Lorenz runs along bottom then shoots up - B=0 - Gini=1.
Answer 2: Not necessarily. Same Gini = similar relative distribution. But: Country A bottom quintile per capita = 0.08 x $12,000 x 5 = ~$4,800. Country B bottom quintile = 0.08 x $55,000 x 5 = ~$22,000. The poor in B are far better off in absolute terms despite identical inequality. Gini measures relative inequality, not absolute poverty - this is a key limitation.
Answer 3: Cumulative: (0.2,0.06), (0.4,0.16), (0.6,0.32), (0.8,0.55), (1.0,1.00). Products = 0.012, 0.044, 0.096, 0.174, 0.310. Sum = 0.636. Gini = 1 - 0.636 = 0.364 (medium inequality). Bottom 60% earns 32% while top 20% earns 45% - moderately unequal, typical of developed countries with welfare states.
Answer 4: 1. Same Gini, different distributions: Country A (bottom 40%=15%, middle 40%=25%, top 20%=60%) vs Country B (bottom 40%=20%, middle 40%=45%, top 20%=35%) might produce similar Ginis. In A, the poor are desperate while ultra-wealthy dominate; in B, strong middle class and better-off poor. Policy responses should differ but Gini alone doesn't reveal this.
2. Ignores absolute income: Gini 0.35 in Switzerland vs Bangladesh. Bottom quintile in Switzerland ~$25K; Bangladesh ~$500. Gini treats as equivalent "inequality" but welfare implications vastly different. Absolute poverty may be more pressing in Bangladesh despite equal Gini.
Answer 5: Similarities: Both Gini 0.38 = moderate relative inequality.
Critical Differences: (1) Bottom 40% share: X (18%) vs Y (22%) - Y's income more evenly distributed in lower half. X's inequality more concentrated at bottom. (2) Absolute income: X bottom 40% per capita = 0.18 x $48,000 x (100/40) = $21,600 vs Y = 0.22 x $8,000 x (100/40) = $4,400. X's poorest enjoy nearly 5x absolute income despite harsher relative inequality.
Policy implications: Y needs growth-focused policies to raise absolute incomes. X needs redistribution-focused policies to improve relative fairness. Same Gini conceals entirely different policy priorities.
Answer 6 (Full IB-Style):
Introduction: Gini is the most widely recognized single-number inequality measure. "Single best" requires evaluation against alternatives.
Arguments For: (1) Simplicity - single number easily understood. (2) Comparability - standardized across countries and time. (3) Theoretical foundation - clear geometric interpretation A/(A+B). (4) Captures entire distribution - uses all income groups.
Arguments Against: (1) Same Gini, different distributions - not uniquely identifying. (2) Insensitive to extremes - most sensitive to middle; Palma ratio better captures rich-vs-poor. (3) No absolute income info - Gini 0.40 in Norway vs Niger. (4) Data quality - surveys underreport top incomes. (5) Doesn't distinguish income from wealth, pre-tax from post-tax.
What Best Depends On: International comparison/public communication = Gini is strong. Capturing plight of poorest = Palma ratio or absolute poverty measures better. Understanding structure = full Lorenz curve needed.
Conclusion: Gini is valuable and widely used, but "single best" overstates. Its strength (summarizing in one number) is also its weakness (masking structural details). Comprehensive assessment requires supplementing Gini with other measures and interpreting alongside absolute income data.

Evaluate

"The Gini coefficient is the single best measure of a country's inequality."

CLASPP Analysis (175 words)

Conclusion: The statement overstates. The Gini is a useful summary measure with strong theoretical foundations and broad comparability, but it is not uniquely "best" - it has significant limitations requiring supplementation with other measures.

Long-term vs Short-term: Over the long term, Gini is excellent for tracking broad inequality trends. In the short term, however, it may miss rapid changes at extremes (surging billionaire wealth) because it is most sensitive to middle-distribution changes.

Assumptions: Assumes inequality is one-dimensional and capturable in a single number. In reality, inequality is multi-dimensional (income vs wealth, pre-tax vs post-tax, relative vs absolute), and no single measure captures all.

Stakeholders: Policymakers benefit from Gini's simplicity for target-setting. Academics find it limiting. The poor are better served by measures focusing on the bottom (Palma ratio, absolute poverty lines).

Priorities: If priority is international benchmarking, Gini is most practical. If priority is understanding lived experience of inequality, multiple measures are essential.

Pros/Cons: Pro - simple, comparable, theoretically grounded, widely available. Con - non-unique, insensitive to extremes, ignores absolute income, depends on data quality.

Overall: The Gini is a necessary but not sufficient measure of inequality.
