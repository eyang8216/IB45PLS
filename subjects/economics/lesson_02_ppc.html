Lesson 2: The Production Possibilities Curve (PPC)

Syllabus reference: Unit 1.2–1.3 — Resource allocation and the PPC model

Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to:

  • Define the production possibilities curve (PPC) and explain what it represents
  • Explain why the PPC is typically drawn concave to the origin (increasing opportunity cost)
  • Interpret points on, inside, and outside the PPC in terms of efficiency, unemployment, and feasibility
  • Distinguish between actual growth (movement toward the curve) and potential growth (outward shift of the curve)
  • Analyse the causes and consequences of outward shifts of the PPC
  • Draw and fully label a PPC diagram, showing both consumer goods and capital goods

1. Prior Knowledge Check

Question 1: In Lesson 1, you learned that scarcity forces every economy to answer three basic questions. What are those three questions?

Show Answer

Answer: What to produce? How to produce? For whom to produce? Every economy must decide which goods and services to make, what combination of resources to use in making them, and who receives the output.

Question 2: What is the difference between capital goods and consumer goods? Think of one example of each.

Show Answer

Answer: Consumer goods are goods that directly satisfy human wants — food, clothing, smartphones. Capital goods are manufactured goods used to produce other goods — factory machinery, tractors, delivery vans. The key distinction: consumer goods are used up in consumption; capital goods are used to produce more goods in the future.

Question 3: Remember the definition of opportunity cost from Lesson 1. If a baker can produce either 50 loaves of bread or 30 cakes in an hour, what is the opportunity cost of producing one more loaf of bread?

Show Answer

Answer: The opportunity cost of one loaf of bread is 30/50 = 0.6 cakes. In the hour the baker uses to produce the loaf of bread, she could have produced a fraction of a cake instead. If we think of it the other way: to get 50 loaves, she gives up 30 cakes, so each loaf "costs" 0.6 cakes in terms of what is given up.

2. What the PPC Is and Why It Matters

The production possibilities curve (PPC), sometimes called the production possibilities frontier (PPF), is a diagram that shows the maximum combinations of two goods that an economy can produce, given its resources and technology, when all resources are fully and efficiently employed.

Why does this matter? The PPC is the simplest possible model of an entire economy. With just two goods, one diagram, and a few curves, it captures nearly every fundamental idea in economics: scarcity, choice, opportunity cost, efficiency, unemployment, and economic growth. If you understand the PPC deeply, you have a framework for thinking about every other topic in this course.

Imagine an economy that produces only two goods: capital goods (machines, factories, tools — goods used to produce other goods in the future) and consumer goods (food, clothing, entertainment — goods that directly satisfy wants today). By simplifying the economy to just these two categories, we can ask a powerful question: how much of each should the economy produce?

Check-in: An economy currently produces 200 units of capital goods and 500 units of consumer goods. If it wants to increase capital goods production to 250 units, what must happen to consumer goods production? Why?

Show Answer

Answer: Consumer goods production must fall. The economy's resources are fully employed producing 200 capital goods. To produce an additional 50 capital goods, resources (workers, machines, land) must be moved from consumer goods production to capital goods production. This reduces the output of consumer goods. This trade-off is the opportunity cost of producing more capital goods.

3. The Shape of the PPC — Why It Curves Outward

A PPC is almost always drawn as a curve that bows outward from the origin (concave to the origin). This shape is not an arbitrary choice. It represents a crucial economic idea: increasing opportunity cost.

Here is what that means. Suppose an economy moves resources from producing consumer goods to producing capital goods. The first few units of capital goods are produced by the resources best suited to making capital goods — the engineers, the factory sites, the machine tools. As the economy tries to produce even more capital goods, it must start using resources that are less and less suited to that task — perhaps farmland, or retail workers, or office buildings. These resources are much better at producing consumer goods. So to get each additional unit of capital goods, the economy must give up more and more consumer goods. The opportunity cost rises at the margin.

A straight-line PPC would mean constant opportunity cost — every unit of one good costs the same amount of the other good. This would imply that all resources are equally good at producing both goods, which is almost never true in reality.

Check-in: An economy is at point A on its PPC, producing lots of consumer goods and few capital goods. It moves to point B, producing more capital goods. At which point — A or B — is the opportunity cost of producing one more capital good higher? Why?

Show Answer

Answer: The opportunity cost is higher at point B. At point A, the economy is using resources that are well-suited to capital goods production (moving them away from consumer goods where they were less productive), so the cost in terms of consumer goods lost is relatively small. At point B, the economy has already moved its best capital-goods resources and is now forced to use resources that are much better at making consumer goods — so each additional capital good costs a larger amount of consumer goods.

4. Reading Points on the PPC

Every point on the PPC diagram tells you something about how the economy is performing.

Points on the curve: The economy is producing at its maximum potential. All resources — all workers, all machines, all land — are fully and efficiently employed. There is no unemployment and no wasted capacity. This is called productive efficiency: the economy cannot produce more of one good without producing less of the other.

Points inside the curve: The economy is producing below its potential. Some resources are unemployed (workers without jobs) or underemployed (machines sitting idle, factories running below capacity). The economy could produce more of both goods by putting these idle resources to work. This represents unemployment, recession, or inefficiency.

Points outside the curve: These combinations are unattainable given the economy's current resources and technology. The economy simply cannot produce that much. However, points outside the curve are not permanently out of reach — if the curve shifts outward (economic growth), points that were previously unattainable become possible.

5. Shifts of the PPC — Economic Growth

The PPC is not fixed forever. It can shift outward, representing economic growth — an increase in the economy's productive capacity.

There is an important distinction between two types of growth:

Actual growth occurs when an economy moves from a point inside the PPC to a point on the PPC. The economy was operating below its potential (unemployment, idle capacity) and now uses its idle resources. The curve itself does not move; the economy simply moves closer to it. This is a recovery from a recession.

Potential growth occurs when the PPC itself shifts outward. The economy's maximum possible output increases. This can happen for several reasons:

  • Increase in the quantity of resources: more workers (population growth, immigration), more capital goods (investment in new factories and machines), discovery of new natural resources.
  • Increase in the quality of resources: better education and training (human capital), technological progress, improved healthcare making workers more productive.
  • Improvements in efficiency: better institutions, reduced corruption, more competitive markets, improved infrastructure.

Check-in: A country discovers large offshore oil reserves. At the same time, it invests heavily in university education. Using PPC analysis, what kind of growth does each change represent? Will the PPC shift, and if so, how?

Show Answer

Answer: Both changes shift the PPC outward — they represent potential growth. The oil discovery increases the quantity of the land factor. The university investment increases the quality of the labour factor (human capital). The PPC shifts outward, likely asymmetrically: if oil is used more in capital goods industries, the capital goods axis might extend more than the consumer goods axis.

6. Worked Examples

Worked Example 1: Reading a PPC Table

Problem: An economy produces two goods: wheat (tonnes) and steel (tonnes). Five possible combinations are shown: A (0 wheat, 100 steel), B (20 wheat, 95 steel), C (40 wheat, 80 steel), D (60 wheat, 50 steel), E (80 wheat, 0 steel). Calculate the opportunity cost of moving from combination C to combination D.

Step 1: At combination C, the economy produces 40 tonnes of wheat and 80 tonnes of steel. At combination D, it produces 60 tonnes of wheat and 50 tonnes of steel. Moving from C to D increases wheat by 20 tonnes and decreases steel by 30 tonnes.

Step 2: The opportunity cost of the additional 20 tonnes of wheat is the 30 tonnes of steel given up. Per tonne of wheat, the opportunity cost is 30 ÷ 20 = 1.5 tonnes of steel.

Step 3: Notice that the opportunity cost of wheat increases as we move to the right along the PPC. Going from A to B: 20 wheat costs 5 steel (0.25 per wheat). From B to C: 20 wheat costs 15 steel (0.75 per wheat). From C to D: 20 wheat costs 30 steel (1.5 per wheat). This is increasing opportunity cost — the hallmark of a concave PPC.

⚠️ Common Wrong Approach: Students sometimes calculate opportunity cost as a simple ratio (80/40 = 2) and assume it is constant. This misses the key insight that opportunity cost changes along the curve. Always calculate opportunity cost for the specific movement between two points on the PPC.

Worked Example 2: Actual vs Potential Growth

Problem: The table below shows an economy's actual output of consumer goods and capital goods in three years. In Year 3, it invests 40% of its output in new machinery.

YearConsumer GoodsCapital GoodsUnemployment Rate
Year 1400 units100 units8%
Year 2480 units120 units3%
Year 3460 units180 units3%

Identify whether each transition represents actual growth, potential growth, both, or neither.

Step 1: Year 1 to Year 2. Both consumer goods (400→480) and capital goods (100→120) increase. Unemployment falls from 8% to 3%. The economy was inside its PPC (high unemployment) and moved closer to the curve. This is actual growth — using previously idle resources. The PPC itself may not have shifted.

Step 2: Year 2 to Year 3. Consumer goods fall (480→460) but capital goods rise sharply (120→180). Unemployment stays at 3% (the economy is on its PPC). The economy is shifting along the PPC, producing more capital goods at the expense of consumer goods. This is neither actual nor potential growth — it is a reallocation of resources along the existing PPC.

Step 3: Looking forward. The high investment in capital goods in Year 3 will increase the economy's capital stock. This will shift the PPC outward in future years, enabling potential growth — the economy sacrifices consumption today for greater productive capacity tomorrow.

⚠️ Common Wrong Approach: Some students see total output rise (640→640 — actually, 400+100=500, 480+120=600, 460+180=640) and assume potential growth has occurred. But total output can increase through actual growth (using idle resources) without the PPC shifting. Only an increase in productive capacity (not just output) shifts the PPC outward.

Worked Example 3: The Investment Trade-off (Exam Trap)

Problem: Two countries, X and Y, both produce at points on their respective PPCs. Country X produces mostly consumer goods (80% consumer, 20% capital). Country Y produces mostly capital goods (30% consumer, 70% capital). Both have identical resources and technology. Which country is likely to experience faster potential growth in the future? Explain why. (This targets the exam trap of thinking that producing at any point on the PPC is equivalent for long-term growth.)

Step 1: Both countries are on their PPCs — both are productively efficient. But productive efficiency is not the same as a good choice about the future.

Step 2: Country Y is devoting 70% of its resources to capital goods — machines, factories, infrastructure. These capital goods do not directly satisfy consumer wants today, but they increase the economy's capacity to produce in the future. Next year, Country Y will have more capital, so its PPC will shift outward by more.

Step 3: Country X is enjoying high consumption today at the expense of investment. Its PPC will shift outward by less (or not at all if it is not even replacing depreciating capital). Country X is choosing present consumption over future growth.

Step 4: Conclusion: Country Y will grow faster. Its citizens accept lower consumption today in exchange for higher consumption in the future. This is the fundamental trade-off the PPC illustrates: investment in capital goods requires sacrificing current consumption.

⚠️ Common Wrong Approach (The Exam Trap): Many students argue that because both are on their PPCs, they are equally efficient and will grow at the same rate. This confuses productive efficiency (being on the PPC) with allocative efficiency (choosing the right point on the PPC for long-term goals). In an IB exam, always ask: "What is being produced, not just how efficiently?" A country on its PPC that produces only consumer goods with zero investment will see its PPC shrink as capital depreciates.

⚠️ Common Mistakes to Avoid

  1. Mistake: Thinking a straight-line PPC is the norm. Why it's wrong: A straight PPC means constant opportunity cost — all resources are equally good at producing both goods. This almost never happens. Correct approach: Draw the PPC concave to the origin (bowed outward). Explain that this shape reflects increasing opportunity cost as resources are not equally suited to all types of production.
  2. Mistake: Confusing a movement toward the PPC (actual growth) with a shift of the PPC (potential growth). Why it's wrong: "Growth" in GDP numbers can come from either source, but they mean very different things for long-term prosperity. Correct approach: Ask: "Did the economy's maximum possible output increase, or did it just use its existing capacity better?" If unemployment fell, that is actual growth. If the capital stock, labour force, or technology improved, that is potential growth.
  3. Mistake: Assuming that a point on the PPC is always the best outcome. Why it's wrong: Being on the PPC means productive efficiency, but it says nothing about whether the right mix of goods is being produced. Correct approach: Distinguish productive efficiency (on the curve) from allocative efficiency (producing the combination society most values). A country on its PPC producing only weapons is productively efficient but may not be serving its citizens' needs.
  4. Mistake: Thinking that producing more capital goods always shifts the PPC outward by the same amount regardless of starting point. Why it's wrong: Investment in capital goods must at least replace depreciating capital before it adds net new capacity. Correct approach: If a country produces 100 units of capital goods but 80 units of existing capital wear out, net investment is only 20 units. The PPC shifts outward by the net addition to the capital stock.

Question 1. [Paper 1, MC] A point inside a country's PPC represents:

A. An unattainable combination of goods.
B. Full employment of all resources.
C. Unemployment or inefficient use of resources.
D. The maximum possible output of capital goods.

Question 2. [Paper 1, MC] Which of the following would shift a country's PPC outward?

A. A reduction in the unemployment rate.
B. An increase in the retirement age, expanding the labour force.
C. A movement of workers from consumer goods industries to capital goods industries.
D. An increase in consumer spending during a recovery.

Question 3. [Paper 2] A country produces two goods: food (tonnes) and electronics (units). Its PPC combinations are: A (0 food, 500 electronics), B (100 food, 480 electronics), C (200 food, 420 electronics), D (300 food, 300 electronics), E (400 food, 0 electronics). (a) Calculate the opportunity cost of moving from B to C. [2 marks] (b) Explain why the opportunity cost per unit of food increases as the country produces more food. [3 marks]

Question 4. [Paper 2] Using a PPC diagram, explain the difference between actual economic growth and potential economic growth. In your answer, identify one policy a government could use to achieve each type of growth. [6 marks]

Question 5. [Paper 2, Exam Style — 8 marks] Two countries, Alpha and Beta, have identical PPCs. Alpha chooses to produce at a point near the consumer goods axis (80% consumer goods, 20% capital goods). Beta chooses a point near the capital goods axis (30% consumer goods, 70% capital goods). (a) Draw a PPC diagram showing both countries' positions. [2 marks] (b) Explain which country is likely to experience faster potential growth over the next decade. [3 marks] (c) Discuss one reason why Alpha might rationally choose its position despite lower future growth. [3 marks]

Answers and Mark Scheme

Answer 1: C. Points inside the PPC represent underutilisation of resources — unemployment or inefficiency. The economy could produce more of both goods by putting idle resources to work.

Answer 2: B. Expanding the labour force increases the quantity of a factor of production, shifting the PPC outward (potential growth). A reduction in unemployment (A) represents actual growth — moving toward the existing PPC, not shifting it. C is a movement along the PPC. D increases spending but does not necessarily increase productive capacity.

Answer 3: (a) Moving B→C: food increases by 100 tonnes (from 100 to 200). Electronics decrease by 60 units (from 480 to 420). Opportunity cost = 60 electronics ÷ 100 food = 0.6 electronics per tonne of food. [✓ 1 mark for correct calculation, ✓ 1 mark for correct units.] (b) The PPC is concave because resources are not equally suited to producing both goods. As the country shifts more resources into food production, it must use land, labour, and capital that are increasingly better suited to producing electronics. Each additional tonne of food therefore requires giving up more electronics than the previous tonne. This is the principle of increasing opportunity cost. [✓ 3 marks — 1 for identifying resources are not equally suited, 1 for explaining the mechanism, 1 for linking to the shape of the PPC.]

Answer 4: Actual growth is movement from a point inside the PPC toward the curve, achieved by using previously idle resources (e.g., reducing unemployment through expansionary fiscal policy). The curve itself does not move. Potential growth is an outward shift of the entire PPC, achieved by increasing productive capacity — for example, investing in education to improve human capital (supply-side policy). [Diagram: show PPC with point A inside, point B on the curve (actual growth), and PPC₂ shifted outward (potential growth).] [✓ 6 marks — 2 for correct diagram, 2 for explaining actual growth + policy example, 2 for explaining potential growth + policy example.]

Answer 5: (a) [Diagram: PPC with Alpha near the consumer goods end, Beta near the capital goods end. Both on the curve.] [✓ 2 marks — 1 for correctly drawn PPC, 1 for showing both countries on the curve at different positions.] (b) Beta will experience faster potential growth. By devoting 70% of its resources to capital goods, Beta is building machines, factories, and infrastructure that increase future productive capacity. Alpha, consuming 80% of output today, invests little, so its PPC will shift outward more slowly. This is the investment-consumption trade-off: Beta sacrifices current consumption for greater future production. [✓ 3 marks — 1 for identifying Beta, 1 for explaining capital accumulation, 1 for linking to PPC shift.] (c) Alpha may rationally choose high current consumption if: its population is currently very poor and needs food and basic goods urgently; its citizens have a high time preference (they value present consumption much more than future consumption); it expects to receive foreign investment or aid that will shift its PPC outward regardless; or its political system favours short-term popularity over long-term planning. [✓ 3 marks — 1 for any valid reason, 2 for clear explanation with economic reasoning.]

10. Exam Tips

Paper 1 (Extended Response): PPC questions in Paper 1 often ask you to explain the concepts of scarcity, choice, and opportunity cost using the PPC model. Structure your answer around the diagram: start by drawing and labelling it, then explain what each feature of the diagram illustrates. The concave shape = increasing opportunity cost. The axes = the choice between two goods. The curve itself = scarcity (limited production possibilities). Points outside = unattainable given current resources.
Paper 2 (Data Response): If a Paper 2 question gives you a table of output combinations along a PPC, the examiner expects you to calculate opportunity costs between specific points and to recognise whether the data shows increasing, constant, or decreasing opportunity cost. Show all working. If the cost per unit is rising as you move along the table, explicitly state: "This demonstrates increasing opportunity cost, which explains why the PPC is concave."
General: The most common PPC exam error is confusing actual growth (movement toward the curve) with potential growth (shift of the curve). Before writing, ask yourself: "Did the productive capacity of the economy increase, or did it simply use existing capacity more fully?" This distinction is worth marks in every IB paper.
