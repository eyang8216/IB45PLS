Lesson 2: The Production Possibilities Curve (PPC)

What You'll Learn

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to:

  • Define and construct a Production Possibilities Curve (PPC)
  • Explain why the PPC is typically concave (bowed outward) due to increasing opportunity cost
  • Interpret points on, inside, and outside the PPC
  • Distinguish between actual growth and potential growth
  • Identify causes of outward shifts in the PPC
  • Differentiate between economic growth and economic development

1. The PPC: Definition and Construction

What Is the PPC?

The Production Possibilities Curve (PPC) — also called the Production Possibilities Frontier (PPF) — is a graph showing the maximum combinations of two goods an economy can produce in a given time period, using all its resources fully and efficiently, with a fixed state of technology.

Key assumptions of the PPC model:

  1. Only two goods are produced (to allow a two-dimensional graph); all others held constant
  2. Resources are fixed in quantity and quality
  3. Technology is fixed
  4. All resources are fully and efficiently employed
  5. The time period is specified (e.g., one year)

Constructing a PPC

Consider a simple economy producing only two goods: capital goods (machines, factories) and consumer goods (food, clothing). The table below shows possible combinations:

CombinationCapital Goods (units)Consumer Goods (units)
A0100
B1095
C2080
D3055
E4020
F450

Plotting these points on a graph with capital goods on the x-axis and consumer goods on the y-axis produces the PPC.

Notice what happens as we move from A → B → C → D → E → F:

  • Moving from A to B: gaining 10 capital goods costs 5 consumer goods → opportunity cost per unit = 0.5 consumer goods
  • Moving from B to C: gaining 10 capital goods costs 15 consumer goods → opportunity cost per unit = 1.5 consumer goods
  • Moving from C to D: gaining 10 capital goods costs 25 consumer goods → opportunity cost per unit = 2.5 consumer goods
  • Moving from D to E: gaining 10 capital goods costs 35 consumer goods → opportunity cost per unit = 3.5 consumer goods

The opportunity cost increases as we produce more capital goods. This is why the PPC is concave (bowed outward from the origin).

2. Why Is the PPC Concave? — Increasing Opportunity Cost

The PPC is typically drawn concave to the origin because of the law of increasing opportunity cost:

As an economy produces more of one good, the opportunity cost of producing additional units of that good (in terms of the other good sacrificed) increases.

Explanation: Not All Resources Are Equally Suited

Resources are not perfectly adaptable. Some resources are better suited to producing one type of good than another:

  • Land: Some land is better for grazing cattle (consumer good: food) while other land is better for factories (capital good). As you convert more land to factories, you start using land poorly suited for building.
  • Labour: Some workers are skilled at making consumer goods (bakers, tailors); others at making capital goods (engineers, machinists). Shifting workers from consumer to capital production eventually requires using workers whose skills don't match, reducing productivity.
  • Capital: Machines designed for consumer-good production (ovens, sewing machines) cannot easily produce capital goods (steel beams, engines).

When an economy starts shifting resources towards capital goods, it first reallocates the resources least suited to consumer goods — so the opportunity cost is low. But as it continues, it must reallocate resources that are highly suited to consumer goods but poorly suited to capital goods, so the output sacrificed (opportunity cost) rises.

A Straight-Line PPC?

If resources were perfectly adaptable between the two goods (identical productivity in both uses), the opportunity cost would be constant and the PPC would be a straight line. This is rare in reality but useful as a simplified benchmark.

3. Interpreting Points on the PPC

Point LocationMeaningResource StatusAttainable?
On the curveAll resources fully and efficiently employedFull employment, productive efficiencyYes
Inside the curveResources are unemployed or underemployed, or used inefficientlyUnemployment, inefficiency, recessionYes (but undesirable)
Outside the curveBeyond current productive capacityUnattainable with current resources and technologyNo (in the short run)

Points Inside the PPC

An economy producing at a point inside its PPC is not using all resources or not using them efficiently. Causes include:

  • Unemployment: Workers without jobs who could be producing output
  • Underemployment: Skilled workers in unskilled jobs, using only part of their capacity
  • Inefficient production: Waste, poor management, outdated methods
  • Recession: Reduced economic activity during a downturn

Points Outside the PPC

A point outside the PPC is unattainable given current resources and technology. It represents a combination of goods the economy cannot produce. However, through economic growth, the PPC can shift outward and make previously unattainable points achievable.

4. Shifts of the PPC: Actual vs Potential Growth

Actual Growth (Movement Toward the Curve)

Actual growth occurs when an economy moves from a point inside the PPC to a point on the PPC (or closer to it). This happens when:

  • Unemployed resources are put to work
  • Idle capacity is utilised
  • The economy recovers from a recession

The PPC itself does not shift — the economy simply moves closer to its existing frontier. This represents an increase in actual output (real GDP).

Potential Growth (Outward Shift of the Curve)

Potential growth occurs when the entire PPC shifts outward, increasing the economy's productive capacity. This represents an increase in potential output.

An outward shift can be:

  • Parallel (both goods increase equally) — if the improvement affects both sectors equally
  • Skewed (one good increases more) — if the improvement affects one sector more than the other

5. Causes of Outward PPC Shifts

The PPC shifts outward when there is an increase in the quantity or quality of resources, or an improvement in technology. Specifically:

1. Increase in Quantity of Resources

  • More land: Discovery of new mineral deposits, reclamation of land from the sea
  • More labour: Population growth, immigration, higher labour force participation
  • More capital: Investment in new machinery, factories, infrastructure (this also shifts the PPC in the future)

2. Increase in Quality of Resources

  • Better land: Improved soil fertility, irrigation
  • Better labour (human capital): Education, training, better healthcare → more productive workers
  • Better capital: Technological improvements embedded in new equipment

3. Technological Progress

  • New production methods that produce more output from the same inputs
  • Innovation that creates entirely new goods
  • Improvements in management and organisation

4. Improvements in Efficiency

  • Better allocation of resources
  • Reduced waste
  • Institutional improvements (legal system, property rights, political stability)

Important: Investment and Future Growth

An economy that produces more capital goods today sacrifices current consumption but builds productive capacity for the future. An economy producing at point C (more capital goods) will experience faster future PPC shifts than one at point B (more consumer goods). This is the growth trade-off: current consumption vs future growth.

6. Economic Growth vs Economic Development

These terms sound similar but have distinct meanings in IB Economics:

DimensionEconomic GrowthEconomic Development
DefinitionAn increase in the quantity of goods and services produced (real GDP)A broader improvement in living standards, well-being, and human capabilities
MeasurementQuantitative — % change in real GDP, GDP per capitaQualitative — HDI (health, education, income), poverty rates, life expectancy, literacy
PPC relationshipPPC shifts outward (potential growth) or movement toward the curve (actual growth)Not directly captured by the PPC; involves improvements in distribution, institutions, and human welfare
FocusOutput and incomeQuality of life

Example: A country may experience economic growth (PPC shifts outward due to oil discoveries) but limited economic development (if oil wealth goes to a small elite, health and education don't improve for the majority).

IB Exam Tip

When drawing a PPC in an IB exam:

  1. Label both axes clearly with the two goods
  2. Draw it concave to the origin (unless told otherwise)
  3. Mark points clearly on, inside, and outside the curve
  4. Use arrows to show shifts and explain the cause
  5. Always explain that the concave shape reflects increasing opportunity cost due to resource heterogeneity

Common mistake: Drawing a straight-line PPC without justification. Unless the question specifies constant opportunity cost, always draw it concave.

Problem 1: Interpreting the PPC

(a) What does Point Q represent?

(b) What must happen for the economy to reach Point R?

(c) If the economy moves from Q to P, what has occurred?

For each event, state whether the PPC shifts outward, shifts inward, or is unaffected. If it shifts, state whether it is parallel or skewed.

Two countries, X and Y, have identical PPCs. Country X produces at a point with 60% capital goods and 40% consumer goods. Country Y produces at 20% capital goods and 80% consumer goods. Both are on their PPCs.

(a) Which country will likely have higher consumption today?

(b) Which country will likely experience faster PPC growth in 10 years? Explain.

Problem 5: Growth vs Development

Classify each as an example of economic growth, economic development, both, or neither:

(a) Real GDP increases by 3%.

(b) Life expectancy rises from 65 to 72 years.

(c) A country discovers oil and its GDP per capita doubles, but income inequality worsens and literacy remains unchanged.

(d) The PPC shifts outward due to immigration of skilled workers.

Problem 6: IB-Style (Challenging)

An economy produces only two goods: healthcare (H) and education (E). Its PPC is given by the equation: H = 120 − 0.5E − 0.001E² (for 0 ≤ E ≤ 200).

(a) What is the maximum amount of healthcare that can be produced? What is the maximum amount of education?

(b) Calculate the opportunity cost of increasing education from 50 to 60 units.

(c) Calculate the opportunity cost of increasing education from 140 to 150 units.

(d) Explain why these two opportunity costs differ.

(e) If a new medical technology increases healthcare output by 20% at every level of education, write the new PPC equation.

Answers

Answer 1: Interpreting the PPC

(a) Point Q (8F, 25M) lies inside the PPC — the economy is producing below its potential. Resources are either unemployed, underemployed, or used inefficiently. This could indicate a recession.

(b) To reach Point R (15F, 50M) — which lies outside the current PPC — the economy must achieve potential growth: an outward shift of the PPC. This requires more/better resources or technological progress.

(c) Moving from Q to P represents actual growth — the economy moves from underemployment toward full employment on the existing PPC. No shift occurs; idle resources are put to work.

Answer 2: Calculating Opportunity Cost

(a) A → B: Gain 100 wheat, lose 50 steel → opportunity cost = 0.5 tonnes steel per tonne of wheat

(b) B → C: Gain 100 wheat, lose 100 steel → opportunity cost = 1.0 tonnes steel per tonne of wheat

(c) C → D: Gain 100 wheat, lose 150 steel → opportunity cost = 1.5 tonnes steel per tonne of wheat

(d) D → E: Gain 50 wheat, lose 200 steel → opportunity cost = 4.0 tonnes steel per tonne of wheat

Pattern: The opportunity cost of wheat rises steadily (0.5 → 1.0 → 1.5 → 4.0). This illustrates the law of increasing opportunity cost. Resources suited to steel production are increasingly diverted to wheat, where they are progressively less productive. The PPC is concave.

Answer 3: Shifts of the PPC

(a) Inward shift — destruction of factories reduces capital stock. Likely skewed (more impact on manufactured goods).

(b) Outward shift — education increases human capital (labour quality). Likely parallel over time as better-educated workers improve all sectors.

(c) Outward shift — discovery increases land resources. Likely skewed toward oil-related production.

(d) No shift; movement toward the curve — falling unemployment means the economy moves from inside the PPC toward the curve. This is actual growth, not potential growth. The PPC itself is unchanged.

(e) Outward shift, skewed — agricultural technology improves the maximum possible food output; the intercept on the agricultural axis extends outward. Non-agricultural maximum may be unchanged.

Answer 4: Capital vs Consumer Goods Trade-off

(a) Country Y will have higher consumption today — 80% of output is consumer goods vs 40% for Country X.

(b) Country X will likely experience faster PPC growth. By producing more capital goods (60% vs 20%), Country X is investing more in productive capacity. Capital goods (machines, factories) increase future production possibilities. Country X is sacrificing current consumption for future growth — the classic PPC trade-off.

Answer 5: Growth vs Development

(a) Economic growth — pure increase in output (real GDP).

(b) Economic development — improvement in a key human welfare indicator (health/life expectancy). This may accompany growth but is distinct.

(c) Economic growth without development — GDP per capita doubles (growth), but inequality worsens and literacy is unchanged, so broader living standards may not improve. This is a classic case of growth without development.

(d) Both — immigration of skilled workers increases productive capacity (potential growth, PPC shift) AND brings human capital that can contribute to broader development.

Answer 6: IB-Style Solution

(a) Maximums:

  • Maximum healthcare (H) when E = 0: H = 120 − 0 − 0 = 120 units
  • Maximum education (E): Set H = 0: 0 = 120 − 0.5E − 0.001E²

→ 0.001E² + 0.5E − 120 = 0

→ Using quadratic formula: E = [−0.5 ± √(0.25 + 0.48)] / 0.002

→ E = [−0.5 ± √0.73] / 0.002 = [−0.5 ± 0.8544] / 0.002

→ E ≈ 177.2 or E ≈ −677.2 (reject negative)

→ Maximum E ≈ 177 units

(b) Opportunity cost from E = 50 to E = 60:

  • At E = 50: H = 120 − 0.5(50) − 0.001(2500) = 120 − 25 − 2.5 = 92.5
  • At E = 60: H = 120 − 0.5(60) − 0.001(3600) = 120 − 30 − 3.6 = 86.4
  • Healthcare sacrificed = 92.5 − 86.4 = 6.1
  • Opportunity cost per unit of education = 6.1 / 10 = 0.61 healthcare units

(c) Opportunity cost from E = 140 to E = 150:

  • At E = 140: H = 120 − 0.5(140) − 0.001(19600) = 120 − 70 − 19.6 = 30.4
  • At E = 150: H = 120 − 0.5(150) − 0.001(22500) = 120 − 75 − 22.5 = 22.5
  • Healthcare sacrificed = 30.4 − 22.5 = 7.9
  • Opportunity cost per unit = 7.9 / 10 = 0.79 healthcare units

(d) The opportunity cost rises (0.61 → 0.79) because of increasing opportunity cost. The quadratic term (−0.001E²) in the PPC equation captures the fact that resources are not equally suited to both goods. As the economy produces more education, it must use resources progressively less suited to education (and more suited to healthcare), sacrificing ever-larger amounts of healthcare. This produces the concave shape.

(e) New PPC with 20% healthcare increase:

  • Original: H = 120 − 0.5E − 0.001E²
  • New: H = 1.2 × (120 − 0.5E − 0.001E²)
  • H = 144 − 0.6E − 0.0012E²

Evaluate

Question

"A country should always maximise its production of capital goods to achieve the highest possible economic growth." Evaluate this statement.

Model Answer

Conclusion: The statement is too simplistic. While producing more capital goods accelerates potential growth, maximising capital-goods production at the expense of all current consumption is neither desirable nor sustainable.

The PPC framework illustrates the core trade-off: an economy producing at a capital-goods-heavy point (e.g., 90% capital, 10% consumer goods) will experience faster outward PPC shifts than one consuming heavily today. Investment in machines, infrastructure, and technology builds tomorrow's productive capacity. South Korea's rapid post-war transformation from an agrarian economy to an industrial powerhouse exemplifies deliberate sacrifice of current consumption for capital accumulation and future growth.

However, "always maximise" ignores several critical considerations. First, there is a minimum consumption requirement — people need food, shelter, and healthcare today to survive and remain productive. An economy producing zero consumer goods would face malnutrition, social unrest, and collapsing labour productivity, undermining the very growth it seeks. Second, there are diminishing returns to capital: each additional unit of capital eventually adds less output, especially if complementary resources (skilled labour, energy) are constrained. Third, consumer spending itself can drive growth by creating demand that incentivises investment — the two types of goods are complementary, not purely competing.

Long-term vs short-term: In the short term, maximising capital goods causes severe welfare losses (poverty, inadequate healthcare). In the long term, the expanded PPC enables both more capital AND more consumption — but only if the population survives the short-term deprivation. The optimal balance depends on a society's time preference and existing consumption levels.

Assumptions: The statement assumes that all capital investment is productive and that the political/institutional framework (property rights, rule of law) exists to channel investment into growth. In countries with corruption or weak institutions, capital accumulation may be wasted or captured by elites, yielding little growth.

Stakeholders: Workers and consumers bear the immediate costs of reduced consumption, while future generations and owners of capital reap the benefits. This raises equity concerns — should today's poor sacrifice for tomorrow's rich?

Priorities: A balanced approach — producing enough capital goods to sustain robust growth while maintaining an acceptable standard of current consumption — is typically optimal. The precise mix depends on a society's values, development level, and growth objectives. Poorer countries may reasonably prioritise capital accumulation more heavily.

Pros/Cons: High capital investment accelerates growth (pro) and builds infrastructure for future generations (pro). But it creates current hardship (con), risks overinvestment in unproductive projects (con), and may neglect human capital (education, health) which is itself a form of productive investment (con).

Final judgment: Countries should pursue a high but not maximum rate of capital accumulation, calibrated to their development stage and societal preferences. The PPC is a tool for understanding the trade-off, not a prescription for an extreme position on it. The optimal point is somewhere in the middle of the curve, informed by both positive analysis (what are the returns to capital?) and normative judgment (how much current sacrifice is acceptable?).
