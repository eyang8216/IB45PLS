Lesson 1: Scarcity, Choice, and Opportunity Cost

Syllabus reference: Unit 1.1–1.2 — The foundations of economics

Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to:

  • Define scarcity and explain why it is the fundamental economic problem facing all societies
  • Distinguish between scarcity and a shortage, and between free goods and economic goods
  • Calculate opportunity cost in everyday decision-making scenarios
  • Identify the four factors of production and their corresponding factor payments
  • Distinguish between positive and normative economic statements
  • Compare microeconomics and macroeconomics as branches of economic study

1. Prior Knowledge Check

Question 1: Think about the last time you had to choose between two things you wanted — perhaps studying for a test versus going out with friends. What did you give up by making your choice?

Show Answer

Answer: You gave up the benefit of the option you did not choose. If you chose to study, you gave up the enjoyment of going out. If you chose to go out, you gave up the study time that might have improved your test score. This trade-off is the starting point for understanding opportunity cost, which we will define precisely in this lesson.

Question 2: A factory produces cars. What kinds of resources does the factory need to operate? List as many as you can think of.

Show Answer

Answer: The factory needs workers (labour), machinery and the factory building itself (capital), raw materials like steel and rubber that come from natural resources (land), and someone to organise the whole operation and take the risk of running the business (entrepreneurship). These four categories are the factors of production.

Question 3: A news headline reads: "The government should reduce taxes to boost economic growth." Another headline reads: "The inflation rate fell from 4.2% to 3.1% last quarter." Which one can be tested as true or false, and which one is a matter of opinion?

Show Answer

Answer: The inflation rate statement can be checked against data — it is a factual claim that is either true or false. The statement about what the government "should" do is a value judgment; reasonable people can disagree about it. These two types of statements have specific names in economics that we will learn in this lesson.

2. Scarcity — The Fundamental Economic Problem

Economics exists as a subject because of one inescapable fact about the world: human wants are unlimited, but the resources available to satisfy those wants are limited. This condition is called scarcity.

To understand scarcity, consider your own life. You have 24 hours in a day. In those 24 hours, you want to sleep, eat, study multiple subjects, exercise, spend time with friends and family, and pursue hobbies. No matter how efficiently you organise your day, you cannot do everything you want. Your time is scarce. The same is true for every person, every business, and every government on the planet.

Check-in: A government wants to provide free healthcare to all citizens, build new highways, increase military spending, and reduce taxes. Why can it not do all four simultaneously?

Show Answer

Answer: The government's budget is limited — its resources are scarce. Providing more healthcare requires spending money on doctors, hospitals, and medicine. Building highways requires spending on construction. Increasing military spending requires buying equipment and paying soldiers. Reducing taxes means collecting less revenue. The government faces a trade-off: spending more on one priority means spending less on another, or collecting more tax revenue (which leaves less money in citizens' pockets). Scarcity forces choices at every level.

A Critical Distinction: Scarcity vs. Shortage

Many students confuse scarcity with a shortage. These are different ideas, and the IB expects you to distinguish them clearly.

A shortage occurs when, at a particular price, the quantity of a good that buyers want to purchase exceeds the quantity that sellers are offering for sale. A shortage is:

  • Temporary: It can be eliminated by raising the price or producing more of the good.
  • Market-specific: A shortage affects a particular good in a particular market at a particular time.

For example, if a popular concert sells out in minutes, there is a shortage of tickets at the listed price. If the organisers raise the price or add a second show, the shortage may disappear.

Scarcity, by contrast, is:

  • Permanent: It can never be eliminated because human wants are insatiable and resources are finite.
  • Universal: It affects every society, rich or poor, at all times.

Even if concert tickets were free and unlimited, the musicians' time, the venue's land, and the equipment used would still be scarce — they could have been used for something else.

Scarcity forces choice. Because we cannot have everything, we must decide what to pursue and what to give up.

3. Opportunity Cost — The Heart of Economic Thinking

When scarcity forces you to choose, you sacrifice the next-best alternative. Economists give this sacrifice a precise name: opportunity cost.

Opportunity cost is the value of the single next-best alternative that you give up when you make a choice. Notice three important details in this definition:

  1. It is the next-best alternative only. If you have five options and choose one, the opportunity cost is the value of the best among the remaining four — not the sum of all four.
  2. It is measured in terms of value forgone. Opportunity cost is not necessarily money. If you spend an hour studying, the opportunity cost might be the enjoyment of the Netflix show you did not watch, or the £15 you could have earned working.
  3. It is subjective. Two people facing the same set of options may have different opportunity costs because they value the alternatives differently.

Check-in: You have £30 and a free evening. You can either buy a new economics revision guide (£30) or go to the cinema and buy dinner (£30 total). You value the revision guide at £50 worth of benefit and the cinema evening at £40. What is the opportunity cost of buying the revision guide?

Show Answer

Answer: The opportunity cost is the value of the cinema evening: £40. This is the value of the next-best alternative you give up. The revision guide is the better choice because its benefit (£50) exceeds its opportunity cost (£40).

Free Goods and Economic Goods

A free good is something that is not scarce. There is enough of it to satisfy all wants at a price of zero, and using it does not reduce the amount available to anyone else. Air (in most circumstances) is a free good — breathing does not use up the atmosphere in any meaningful sense. Free goods have zero opportunity cost because using them does not require giving up anything.

An economic good is something that is scarce. It has a price, and using it involves an opportunity cost. Almost everything in an economy — food, clothing, housing, education, healthcare, entertainment — is an economic good.

Check-in: Is a public park a free good or an economic good?

Show Answer

Answer: A public park is an economic good, even if entry is free. The land it occupies is scarce — it could have been used for housing, offices, or a school. Maintaining the park requires labour and equipment. The park has an opportunity cost: the next-best use of that land and those resources. The fact that it has no monetary price at the point of use does not make it a free good. "Free at the point of use" is not the same as "free good" in economics.

4. Factors of Production — The Inputs That Make Everything

To produce any good or service, an economy needs inputs. Economists classify these inputs into four categories called factors of production. Every product you use — the device you are reading this on, the chair you are sitting in, the food you ate today — was made by combining these four factors.

Land

Land includes all natural resources: the physical ground itself, minerals beneath it, water, forests, fish stocks, oil and gas deposits, and even the climate. The income earned by the owner of land is called rent.

Labour

Labour is human effort — both physical and mental — used in production. A construction worker swinging a hammer provides labour. A surgeon performing an operation provides labour. A teacher explaining a concept in a classroom provides labour. The income earned by workers is called wages (or salaries). The quality of labour — skills, education, health, experience — is called human capital.

Capital

Capital (or physical capital) is manufactured goods used to produce other goods and services. A tractor on a farm, a factory building, a sewing machine in a clothing workshop, and a computer used by an accountant are all capital. The income earned by the owner of capital is called interest.

A common point of confusion: In everyday language, "capital" often means money. In economics, capital means physical productive assets. Money itself is not capital; it is a medium of exchange that can be used to purchase capital goods. When an economist says "investment in capital," they mean building new factories and buying new machines — not putting money in the stock market.

Entrepreneurship

Entrepreneurship is the ability to combine land, labour, and capital to produce goods and services. Entrepreneurs take risks: they identify opportunities, organise resources, innovate, and bear the financial consequences if their ventures fail. The income earned by entrepreneurs is called profit.

5. Positive and Normative Statements

Economists make two fundamentally different kinds of statements, and knowing which is which is essential for clear thinking — and for IB exam marks.

A positive statement is a claim about what is. It can be tested against evidence and shown to be true or false. Positive statements do not depend on the speaker's values. They are factual.

Examples of positive statements:

  • "The unemployment rate in Spain was 11.8% in the fourth quarter of 2024." (This can be checked against data from Spain's statistical office.)
  • "An increase in the minimum wage is associated with reduced employment among teenage workers." (This is a testable claim about a relationship between two variables.)

A normative statement is a claim about what ought to be. It involves a value judgment and cannot be settled by facts alone. Reasonable people with different values can disagree about normative statements.

Examples of normative statements:

  • "The government should reduce unemployment even if it causes higher inflation."
  • "Income inequality is too high and must be reduced through higher taxes on the wealthy."

Words like "should," "ought," "fair," "unfair," "too high," and "too low" are strong signals that a statement is normative.

Check-in: Identify whether each statement is positive or normative: (a) "The inflation rate in Argentina exceeded 200% in 2023." (b) "The government should prioritise reducing inflation over reducing unemployment."

Show Answer

Answer: (a) Positive. This is a factual claim that can be verified or disproven using Argentina's official inflation data. (b) Normative. The word "should" signals a value judgment. Two economists could look at the same data on inflation and unemployment and reach different conclusions about which problem the government should address first.

6. Microeconomics and Macroeconomics

Economics is divided into two branches, distinguished by the scale of the questions they ask.

Microeconomics studies the behaviour of individual economic agents — households, firms, and specific industries. It asks questions like: why does the price of coffee rise when a frost damages the Brazilian coffee harvest? How does a firm decide whether to hire one more worker? What happens to rents in a city that introduces rent control? Microeconomics zooms in on individual markets.

Macroeconomics studies the economy as a whole. It asks questions about aggregates: what causes recessions, and why do some recoveries take longer than others? Why do some countries grow faster than others over decades? What determines the overall level of prices across the entire economy? Macroeconomics zooms out to look at the big picture.

The two branches are connected. You cannot fully understand a recession (a macroeconomic event) without understanding why individual firms lay off workers (a microeconomic decision). The distinction is a way of organising thought, not a hard boundary.

Check-in: Classify each topic as microeconomics or macroeconomics: (a) The effect of a new tax on the market for petrol. (b) The relationship between a country's money supply and its overall inflation rate. (c) A firm's decision about how many workers to hire.

Show Answer

Answer: (a) Microeconomics — examines a specific market (petrol). (b) Macroeconomics — examines economy-wide aggregates (money supply and overall price level). (c) Microeconomics — examines decisions of an individual firm.

7. Worked Examples

Worked Example 1: Calculating Opportunity Cost

Problem: A student has been offered two part-time jobs. Job A pays £12 per hour for 10 hours per week. Job B pays £15 per hour for 10 hours per week. However, Job B requires a 30-minute commute each way, while Job A is on campus. The student values her commuting time at £8 per hour. She can only accept one job. What is the opportunity cost of choosing Job B?

Step 1: Calculate the net benefit of each job. We need to account for both the wage income and the cost of commuting time. For Job A: wage income is £12 × 10 = £120. Commuting cost is zero. Net benefit = £120. For Job B: wage income is £15 × 10 = £150. Commuting time is 1 hour per shift × 5 shifts (we assume 5 workdays of 2 hours each?) — wait, let us re-examine. The problem says 10 hours per week. If she works 5 days of 2 hours each, that is 5 round trips of 1 hour each = 5 hours of commuting. Value of lost time = 5 × £8 = £40. Net benefit of Job B = £150 − £40 = £110.

Step 2: Identify the next-best alternative. If she chooses Job B (£110 net benefit), the forgone alternative is Job A (£120 net benefit).

Step 3: State the opportunity cost. The opportunity cost of choosing Job B is £120 — the net benefit of Job A.

Step 4: Which job should she choose? Job A has a higher net benefit (£120 > £110), so she should choose Job A.

⚠️ Common Wrong Approach: Many students compare only the wage rates (£15 vs. £12) and conclude Job B is better. This ignores the non-monetary cost of commuting. Opportunity cost includes all costs — not just money — because any resource used for one purpose (time spent commuting) cannot be used for another (leisure, studying, or additional work). The correct approach accounts for the full value of what is given up.

Worked Example 2: Distinguishing Positive from Normative

Problem: A newspaper editorial contains the following sentence: "The central bank's decision to raise interest rates from 3% to 4% will reduce consumer spending on durable goods, but the central bank should have waited until unemployment fell further before taking this action." Identify the positive and normative components.

Step 1: Find the testable claim. "The central bank's decision to raise interest rates from 3% to 4% will reduce consumer spending on durable goods." This is a prediction about a cause-and-effect relationship. It can be tested against data — economists can study what happened to durable goods spending after rate increases in the past. This part is positive.

Step 2: Find the value judgment. "The central bank should have waited until unemployment fell further before taking this action." The word "should" signals a normative claim. Two economists could agree on the facts about spending and still disagree about whether the central bank made the right decision, depending on how they weigh the competing goals of controlling inflation versus reducing unemployment. This part is normative.

⚠️ Common Wrong Approach: Students sometimes classify the entire sentence as positive because it contains factual elements. The presence of data (3% and 4%) does not make a sentence positive. Look for value-laden words — "should," "ought," "too high," "unfair" — to identify normative claims. A single sentence can contain both types.

Worked Example 3: Scarcity in Government Decisions (Exam Trap)

Problem: A government has a budget surplus of £10 billion. It is deciding between two spending proposals: expanding university places (estimated long-term economic benefit: £15 billion) and upgrading the national electricity grid (estimated benefit: £12 billion). The government's economic advisers say both projects are worthwhile. Explain why the government still faces an economic problem, and calculate the opportunity cost of choosing university expansion. (This targets the exam trap of confusing "we can afford it" with "there is no opportunity cost.")

Step 1: Recognise that a surplus does not eliminate scarcity. Even though the government has £10 billion available, it cannot fund both projects fully (they would cost more than £10 billion combined). But even if it could, the £10 billion itself has alternative uses — it could be returned to taxpayers as a tax cut, used to reduce national debt, or spent on health, defence, or other priorities. Scarcity persists regardless of the budget position.

Step 2: Identify the next-best alternative. If the government chooses university expansion, it gives up the electricity grid upgrade, which has a benefit of £12 billion.

Step 3: State the opportunity cost. The opportunity cost of expanding universities is the £12 billion in benefit from the electricity grid upgrade that is forgone.

Step 4: Make a recommendation. University expansion (£15 billion benefit) exceeds its opportunity cost (£12 billion), so it is the better choice on efficiency grounds. However, this is a positive analysis; a normative judgment about whether it is the "right" choice would depend on additional values — for example, how much the government prioritises education versus energy security.

⚠️ Common Wrong Approach (The Exam Trap): Students often argue that "because the government has a surplus, there is no opportunity cost — it can afford both." This is wrong for two reasons. First, even if both could be funded in money terms, the real resources (construction workers, engineers, steel, concrete) are scarce and cannot be used for both projects simultaneously. Second, the surplus itself has an opportunity cost: the best alternative use of that £10 billion. In an IB exam, always look for the hidden trade-off, even when the scenario appears to show abundance.

⚠️ Common Mistakes to Avoid

  1. Mistake: Thinking scarcity and shortage are the same thing. Why it's wrong: Scarcity is permanent and universal; a shortage is temporary and market-specific. Correct approach: Ask yourself: "Can this problem be solved by producing more or raising the price?" If yes, it is a shortage. If the problem would persist no matter how much is produced, it is driven by scarcity.
  2. Mistake: Calculating opportunity cost as the sum of all alternatives given up. Why it's wrong: Opportunity cost is the value of the single next-best alternative — the one you would have chosen if you had not made your actual choice. Correct approach: Rank all alternatives by their value to you. The opportunity cost of your chosen option is the value of the highest-ranked alternative among those not chosen.
  3. Mistake: Classifying money as capital. Why it's wrong: In economics, capital means physical productive assets — machines, buildings, tools. Money is a medium of exchange. Correct approach: When you see "capital" in an economics context, ask: "Is this a physical good used to produce other goods?" If it is money in a bank account or a stock portfolio, it is financial capital — not what economists mean by the factor of production.
  4. Mistake: Thinking that a "free at the point of use" service (like the NHS or a public library) is a free good. Why it's wrong: Free goods are not scarce and have zero opportunity cost. Public services consume scarce resources — land, labour, capital — that could have been used elsewhere. Correct approach: Ask: "Does providing this service use resources that could have been used for something else?" If yes, it is an economic good, regardless of whether users pay at the point of consumption.
  5. Mistake: Assuming a statement with numbers in it must be positive. Why it's wrong: Numbers can appear in normative statements. "The inflation rate of 5% is too high" contains a number but is normative because "too high" is a value judgment. Correct approach: Look for value-laden words — should, ought, fair, unfair, too much, too little — regardless of whether data is present.

11. Exam Tips

Paper 1 (Extended Response): When a question asks about the "basic economic problem," the examiner expects you to use the word scarcity — not "shortage," not "poverty," not "limited resources" without the context of unlimited wants. Define scarcity precisely at the start of your response: "Scarcity is the condition that arises because human wants are unlimited while the resources available to satisfy them are finite." Then build your answer from that definition. The difference between a 5 and a 7 often comes down to precise definitions in the opening paragraph.
Paper 2 (Data Response): Opportunity cost questions in Paper 2 typically present you with data about two or more alternatives and ask you to identify or calculate the opportunity cost of a specific choice. The trap: students often list every alternative or pick the wrong one. Always rank all alternatives by their value (monetary or otherwise). The opportunity cost is the highest-ranked alternative among those not chosen — not all of them, and not the lowest-valued one.
General: Positive vs. normative distinctions appear in every IB Economics paper. If a question includes the word "evaluate" or "discuss," your answer must include both positive analysis (what the evidence shows) and a normative conclusion (your judgment, supported by reasoning). Never present a normative claim as if it were a fact — say "this suggests that the policy is effective" rather than "the policy is obviously the right choice."
