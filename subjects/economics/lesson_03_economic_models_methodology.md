## 1. Economic Models — What They Are and Why We Build Them

### The Problem Models Solve

The real economy is incomprehensibly complex. At any moment, millions of households are making spending decisions, thousands of firms are setting prices and hiring workers, governments are adjusting taxes, and central banks are managing interest rates. Every decision interacts with every other decision. If an economist tried to think about all of this at once, she would be paralysed.

An **economic model** is a simplified, abstract representation of reality designed to isolate a few key relationships so that we can understand, explain, and predict economic phenomena. A model deliberately strips away detail. It is not a failure of a model that it is unrealistic. It is the point.

The philosopher Alfred North Whitehead put it well: "Seek simplicity, and distrust it." A model should be simple enough to analyse but rich enough to capture the essential mechanism at work. The art of modelling lies in knowing what to leave out.

### What Makes a Good Model?

Economists judge models by four criteria. First, **parsimony**: the model should use the minimum number of assumptions needed to generate its predictions. Extra assumptions add complexity without adding insight. Second, **tractability**: the model must be analysable — mathematically, graphically, or logically. A model you cannot solve is a model you cannot use. Third, **predictive power**: the model should generate hypotheses that can be tested against real-world data. A model that predicts nothing is not a scientific model. Fourth, **generality**: the model should apply to a range of situations, not just a single historical episode.

Milton Friedman, one of the most influential economists of the twentieth century, argued that models should be judged by the accuracy of their predictions, not by the realism of their assumptions. A model that assumes all consumers are perfectly rational and perfectly informed may still predict market behaviour with useful accuracy. The assumption is false, but the model may still work A road map omits trees, buildings, potholes, and weather — and because it omits them, it is useful for navigation. A map that included everything would be the territory itself, and it would be useless.

### Models You Will Encounter in IB Economics

The IB syllabus is built around a series of models, each designed to answer a different question. The **Production Possibilities Curve** models scarcity, choice, and opportunity cost. The **Demand and Supply model** predicts equilibrium prices and quantities in individual markets. The **Circular Flow model** maps the flows of goods, services, and payments between households, firms, the government, and the foreign sector. The **AD/AS model** analyses output, employment, and the price level for the economy as a whole. The **Market Structure models** (perfect competition, monopoly, oligopoly, monopolistic competition) explain how firms behave under different competitive conditions.

Each model is a lens. The question is not "which model is true?" but "which model is most useful for the question I am asking right now?"

## 2. The Ceteris Paribus Assumption

### What Ceteris Paribus Means

**Ceteris paribus** is a Latin phrase meaning "other things being equal." In economics, it is the assumption that when we analyse the relationship between two variables — say, price and quantity demanded — we hold all other relevant variables constant.

The assumption is not a claim about reality. It is a thinking tool. In reality, many things change at once. The price of petrol might rise on the same day that consumers learn about a new fuel-efficient car model, incomes fall due to a recession, and the government announces a forthcoming tax cut. Isolating the effect of the petrol price change alone is impossible without the ceteris paribus assumption.

### Why Ceteris Paribus Is Indispensable

Imagine trying to establish the law of demand without ceteris paribus. You observe that when the price of a good rises, quantity demanded sometimes falls (as the law predicts) but sometimes rises (because incomes happened to rise at the same time, swamping the price effect). Without holding other things equal, you observe only correlations — things that happen together — and you cannot identify causation.

Ceteris paribus allows the economist to say: "If the price of coffee rises from £3 to £4 per cup, and nothing else changes, the quantity of coffee demanded will fall." The "and nothing else changes" is the ceteris paribus clause. It turns an unmanageable mess of simultaneous changes into a clean, testable hypothesis.

### The Limitation

The limitation of ceteris paribus is that it is always false in the strict sense. Other things are never perfectly equal in a living economy. Critics of economics argue that this makes the entire modelling enterprise suspect. The defence is that ceteris paribus is a method, not a truth claim. Every science uses simplification. Physicists analyse projectiles in a vacuum even though vacuums do not exist on Earth. The test is whether the simplification produces predictions that are approximately correct. For the demand-and-supply model, the answer is yes.

A common mistake students make is to treat ceteris paribus as a minor technical detail. It is not. It is the foundation on which every causal claim in economics rests. Without it, economics would be pattern recognition without explanation.

## 3. Equilibrium as a Modelling Concept

### What Equilibrium Means

**Equilibrium** is a state of balance in which there is no inherent tendency for change. All the forces acting on the system — demand and supply, for example — are equalised. At the equilibrium price in a market, every buyer who is willing and able to pay that price finds a seller, and every seller who is willing and able to sell at that price finds a buyer. There are no frustrated buyers (no shortage) and no frustrated sellers (no surplus). The market clears.

Equilibrium is a modelling concept, not necessarily a description of what is happening at any given moment. Real markets are often in disequilibrium — adjusting toward equilibrium but not yet there. The concept of equilibrium tells us where the market is heading, even if it has not yet arrived.

### Types of Equilibrium

Economists distinguish several types of equilibrium. A **stable equilibrium** is one that the system returns to after a disturbance. If the price in a market is pushed above equilibrium, a surplus emerges, which pushes the price back down. The market is self-correcting. An **unstable equilibrium** is like a pencil balanced on its tip: a tiny disturbance sends it permanently away from balance. A **static equilibrium** is a snapshot — a single point in time with no tendency to change. A **dynamic equilibrium** is a balanced growth path in which variables change over time but in a stable relationship to each other, like a bicycle moving at constant speed.

### Equilibrium Is Not the Same as Optimal

Many students assume that if a market is in equilibrium, the outcome must be desirable. This is incorrect. Equilibrium simply means the forces are balanced. A market in equilibrium can involve child labour, environmental destruction, or gross inequality. It can be efficient or inefficient, fair or unfair. Equilibrium is a positive concept (what is), not a normative one (what ought to be). Keep this distinction sharp for the IB exam.

## 4. Economics and the Scientific Method

### The Steps of Scientific Inquiry

Economics, like the natural sciences, proceeds through a systematic method: observation, hypothesis formation, model building, prediction, empirical testing, and refinement or rejection of the theory. An economist notices a pattern — for example, that countries with higher levels of education tend to have higher GDP per capita. She forms a tentative explanation: education increases worker productivity, which raises output. She builds a model formalising this relationship. She deduces testable predictions: if education causes growth, then an increase in school enrolment in one region should, other things equal, lead to faster income growth in that region relative to others. She tests the prediction against data. If the data support the prediction, she tentatively accepts the model. If not, she revises or discards it.

### How Economics Differs from Natural Sciences

The differences are significant and worth understanding. In physics or chemistry, controlled laboratory experiments are routine. A chemist can hold temperature, pressure, and concentration constant while varying one factor and observing the result. An economist cannot put the British economy in a laboratory, hold everything constant, and change only the interest rate to see what happens.

This does not mean economics is unscientific. It means economics is a **social science** — it studies human behaviour, which is inherently less predictable than the behaviour of particles. People have expectations, emotions, and free will. They change their minds. They react not only to what happens but to what they expect to happen, and their expectations can be self-fulfilling or self-defeating.

Other differences include: low replicability (economic conditions are never exactly the same twice), the presence of value judgments (what economists choose to study and how they interpret results are influenced by normative views), and lower precision (economic predictions are approximate, not exact).

### The Status of Economics

Economics uses the methods of science — formal models, statistical testing, systematic evidence — applied to a subject matter that resists precise prediction. This makes it different from physics but not inferior to it. Astronomy cannot run experiments on stars. Evolutionary biology cannot replay evolution in a laboratory. Both are sciences. Economics deserves the same status, with the same caveats about the limits of its knowledge.

## 5. Why Economists Disagree

### Sources of Disagreement

Economists frequently offer contradictory policy advice. A journalist might present this as evidence that economics is useless — "if the experts cannot agree, how can anyone trust them?" The reality is more nuanced. Disagreements arise from four distinct sources, and understanding them helps you evaluate competing claims.

**Different value judgments.** Two economists may agree on every positive fact — the minimum wage will reduce employment among teenagers by 3% and raise wages for those who keep their jobs by 15% — and still disagree about whether the policy is desirable. One prioritises efficiency and opposes any policy that destroys jobs. The other prioritises equity and judges that the wage gains for the 97% outweigh the job losses for the 3%. This is a normative disagreement, not a scientific one. It cannot be settled by more data.

**Different models and assumptions.** Economists work within competing theoretical frameworks. A Keynesian economist assumes that prices and wages adjust slowly, so a drop in demand causes unemployment. A monetarist assumes that markets clear quickly, so a drop in demand causes prices to fall rather than employment. Both frameworks are internally consistent. They generate different policy prescriptions because they make different assumptions about how the world works.

**Different time horizons.** Short-run effects often differ from long-run effects. Tariffs on imported steel protect domestic steelworkers' jobs in the short run. In the long run, they raise costs for every industry that uses steel — automobile manufacturing, construction, machinery — and destroy more jobs than they save. An economist advising a politician facing an election in six months may emphasise the short-run benefit. A development economist may emphasise the long-run cost. Both are factually correct about different time frames.

**Different interpretations of data.** Economic data is noisy, incomplete, and subject to revision. Two economists can look at the same employment statistics after a minimum-wage increase and reach opposite conclusions because they weigh different pieces of evidence differently or use different statistical techniques to control for confounding factors.

### What Economists Agree On

The public perception of endless disagreement is misleading. Economists agree on a great deal: trade creates gains for all participating countries, inflation is caused by excessive money-supply growth, incentives matter for behaviour, price controls create shortages, and fiscal stimulus can reduce the depth of a recession. The disagreements, though real, are concentrated in areas where the data are ambiguous and the stakes are high.

## 6. A Brief History of Economic Thought

### Why the History Matters

The IB syllabus expects you to know the names of a few key thinkers and the broad contours of how economic ideas have evolved. This is not for the sake of trivia. Each school of thought represents a different answer to the central questions of economics: what drives prosperity? What causes recessions? What should the government do — and not do?

### Adam Smith and Classical Economics (Late 18th Century)

Adam Smith, a Scottish philosopher, published *The Wealth of Nations* in 1776. His central insight was that individuals pursuing their own self-interest in competitive markets unintentionally promote the social good — the famous "invisible hand." A baker does not bake bread out of benevolence. He bakes bread to earn a living. But in doing so, he feeds his community. The market coordinates millions of self-interested decisions into outcomes that, under the right conditions, serve everyone.

Smith also identified the power of specialisation and the division of labour. A pin factory in which each worker performs a single specialised task produces vastly more pins per worker than a factory in which each worker makes entire pins from start to finish. Specialisation raises productivity, which raises living standards. And specialisation requires trade.

The classical economists who followed Smith — David Ricardo, Thomas Malthus, John Stuart Mill — elaborated these ideas. They believed that markets are self-regulating, that supply creates its own demand (Say's Law), and that the economy tends toward full employment. The policy implication was laissez-faire: minimal government intervention.

### Karl Marx (Mid-19th Century)

Marx offered a radical critique of classical economics. Where Smith saw mutually beneficial exchange, Marx saw exploitation. Workers, he argued, produce more value than they receive in wages. The surplus is appropriated by the owners of capital as profit. Capitalism, in Marx's analysis, contained internal contradictions — falling rates of profit, recurring crises, growing inequality — that would eventually lead to its collapse and replacement by socialism. Marx's economic theories have largely been rejected by modern economics, but his emphasis on class conflict, power relations, and the instability of capitalism continues to influence heterodox traditions.

### Keynesian Economics (1930s–1970s)

The Great Depression of the 1930s shattered faith in classical self-regulation. Unemployment in the United States reached 25%. Output collapsed. Markets did not self-correct. John Maynard Keynes, a British economist, offered a new explanation in *The General Theory of Employment, Interest and Money* (1936). The problem, he argued, was insufficient **aggregate demand** — total spending in the economy. When households and firms cut spending during a downturn, the economy can settle into an equilibrium with high unemployment that has no natural tendency to correct itself.

Keynes prescribed government intervention: increase public spending and cut taxes to boost aggregate demand. During a recession, the government should run a budget deficit to put money into people's pockets, who would then spend it, which would induce firms to hire, which would further increase spending — a virtuous circle. This was a radical departure from laissez-faire.

### Monetarism and the Counter-Revolution (1960s–1980s)

Milton Friedman led a counter-movement against Keynesian dominance. He argued that inflation is "always and everywhere a monetary phenomenon" — caused by excessive growth in the money supply, not by excessive government spending per se. He was sceptical of the government's ability to fine-tune the economy through fiscal policy, pointing to long and variable lags between policy action and economic effect. His prescription: central banks should follow a simple rule — increase the money supply at a steady, low rate — rather than trying to actively manage the economy.

Friedman also revived the classical faith in markets. He argued that many apparent market failures were actually government failures, and that the burden of proof should lie with those who proposed intervention, not with those who defended markets.

### The Modern Mainstream

Today, most economists draw on insights from multiple schools. There is broad agreement that monetary policy should be conducted by independent central banks targeting low inflation, that free trade benefits participating countries, and that well-designed fiscal stimulus can reduce the severity of recessions. Active debates continue on the optimal size of government, the design of tax systems, and the best approach to regulating financial markets.

## 7. Correlation and Causation

### The Central Distinction

**Correlation** means that two variables move together in a systematic pattern. When ice cream sales rise, drowning deaths also rise. This is a positive correlation. When the price of a good rises, the quantity demanded falls. This is a negative correlation.

**Causation** means that a change in one variable produces a change in another. Turning on a light switch causes the light to go on. Correlation and causation are different things, and confusing them is one of the most persistent errors in empirical reasoning.

The mantra is: correlation does not imply causation. But economics goes further than the mantra. It identifies the specific mechanisms that produce correlation without causation.

### Why Correlations Can Be Spurious

Three mechanisms produce correlations that do not reflect causation.

**Reverse causation.** Suppose we observe that countries with more police officers have higher crime rates. Does this mean police cause crime? No. The causal arrow likely runs the other way: high-crime countries hire more police officers. The correlation is real, but the causation runs opposite to what a naive reading would suggest.

**Omitted variable bias.** A third factor causes both of the correlated variables. Ice cream sales and drowning deaths are correlated because both are caused by hot summer weather. When the weather is hot, people buy more ice cream and swim more. More swimming means more drowning. If we control for weather — if we compare ice cream sales and drowning rates only on days with the same temperature — the correlation disappears.

**Coincidence.** Given enough data, some pairs of variables will be correlated purely by chance. The divorce rate in the US state of Maine correlates with per capita consumption of margarine. No serious person believes margarine causes divorce. The correlation is a statistical artefact.

### How Economists Establish Causation

Because controlled experiments on entire economies are rarely possible, economists have developed sophisticated methods to extract causal inference from observational data. **Natural experiments** exploit situations in which a policy change or event affects one group but not another in a way that approximates random assignment. If one state raises its minimum wage and a neighbouring state does not, and the two states are otherwise similar, the difference in employment outcomes can be attributed to the policy. **Randomised controlled trials** are the gold standard, increasingly used in development economics to test interventions like bed nets, school vouchers, and microcredit. **Instrumental variables** use a third variable that affects the cause but not the effect directly. **Difference-in-differences** compares changes over time between treated and untreated groups. **Regression discontinuity** compares outcomes just above and just below an arbitrary threshold — for example, students who just barely pass an exam versus those who just barely fail.

These methods do not eliminate uncertainty. They reduce it. The honest economist reports not only an estimated effect but a confidence interval around that estimate, acknowledging the limits of what the data can say.

## IB Exam Tip

In Paper 1 essays and Paper 2 data-response questions, you may be asked to evaluate a model or discuss its limitations. When you do, address these points:

1. **The ceteris paribus assumption.** State what the model holds constant and explain what might change in reality that would complicate the prediction.
2. **The model's simplifying assumptions.** Are they reasonable in the context? A model that assumes perfect information is useful for understanding ideal market function but may mislead when applied to markets — like used cars or health insurance — where information is deeply asymmetric.
3. **Short-run versus long-run predictions.** A model may predict well in one time frame and poorly in another. The demand-and-supply model predicts short-run price movements well. It does not predict long-run technological change well.
4. **Reference key thinkers by name.** Adam Smith, Karl Marx, John Maynard Keynes, and Milton Friedman are the minimum. Knowing what each contributed shows the examiner you understand that economics is a living discipline with a history.

## Worked Example 1: Applying Ceteris Paribus

A researcher observes that over a five-year period, the price of housing in a city increased by 30% while the quantity of housing purchased also increased by 15%. She claims this contradicts the law of demand, which predicts that price and quantity demanded should move in opposite directions. Is her claim valid? Explain, using the ceteris paribus concept.

**Approach:** The law of demand states that, holding all else constant, a higher price leads to a lower quantity demanded. The researcher is observing raw correlations without controlling for other variables. We need to identify what else might have changed during the same period.

**Step 1:** State what ceteris paribus requires. The law of demand is a ceteris paribus statement. It predicts that if the price of housing rises and nothing else changes, the quantity of housing demanded will fall.

**Step 2:** Identify variables that probably changed. Over a five-year period, many things almost certainly changed: the city's population likely grew (more buyers), incomes likely rose (more purchasing power), interest rates may have fallen (cheaper mortgages), and the city's desirability may have increased (shift in tastes).

**Step 3:** Explain the observed pattern. The 30% price increase and 15% quantity increase are consistent with a scenario in which the demand curve shifted right (due to population growth, income growth, and falling interest rates) by more than any leftward movement along the demand curve caused by the price increase. The observed data do not contradict the law of demand. They reflect the combined effect of a price change (movement along the curve) and changes in non-price determinants (shifts of the curve).

**Why this makes sense:** The raw observation — price and quantity both rising — does not tell us whether the law of demand has been violated. We need to know what happened to the demand curve itself. This is exactly why ceteris paribus is essential: without it, we cannot separate movements along a curve from shifts of the curve.

## Worked Example 2: Distinguishing Correlation from Causation

A study finds that countries where people drink more coffee have lower rates of heart disease. A newspaper headline declares: "Scientists Say Coffee Prevents Heart Attacks." Identify at least two reasons why this conclusion might be invalid.

**Approach:** The study has established a correlation. To assess whether it implies causation, we need to examine possible omitted variables, reverse causation, and the mechanism linking coffee to heart health.

**Step 1:** Consider omitted variable bias. Wealthier countries tend to have higher coffee consumption (coffee is more expensive than alternatives in some regions) and also have better healthcare systems, healthier diets, and lower rates of smoking — all of which independently reduce heart disease. The observed correlation between coffee and heart health might be entirely explained by the fact that both are correlated with national income and the quality of healthcare.

**Step 2:** Consider reverse causation. It is theoretically possible that people in countries with lower heart disease rates have more disposable income (because they spend less on healthcare) and therefore buy more coffee. The causal arrow might run from health to coffee, not from coffee to health.

**Step 3:** Consider the need for a mechanism. Even if the correlation is robust, a causal claim requires a plausible mechanism. Does a specific compound in coffee affect cholesterol or blood pressure? In what quantity? Have these effects been demonstrated in randomised controlled trials? Without a mechanism, the correlation is suggestive at best.

**Why this makes sense:** The newspaper has leapt from correlation to causation without examining alternative explanations. This is the most common reasoning error in empirical economics, and the IB exam rewards students who can identify it and articulate the specific flaws in the inference.

## Practice Problems

**Problem 1.** The PPC model assumes only two goods, fixed resources, fixed technology, and full employment. For the assumption of fixed resources: (a) Explain why this assumption is made. (b) Explain what real-world complexity this assumption ignores. (c) Discuss whether this assumption weakens the model's usefulness for understanding economic growth. Write your answer as a full paragraph for each part.

**Problem 2.** A study finds that regions of a country with more hospitals per thousand residents have higher mortality rates. One commentator argues that this means hospitals are dangerous and the government should close some of them. Explain why this conclusion is likely wrong, using the concept of omitted variable bias. Identify a plausible omitted variable and explain the mechanism through which it creates a spurious correlation.

**Problem 3.** The market for rental apartments in a city is in equilibrium at a price of £1,200 per month and a quantity of 50,000 apartments rented. An earthquake damages 10% of the housing stock. Describe the immediate disequilibrium that results. Then describe the process by which the market adjusts to a new equilibrium. Finally, state whether the new equilibrium is likely to be stable and explain why or why not. Use full sentences throughout.

**Problem 4.** Two economists examine a proposal to increase the national minimum wage from £10.42 to £12.00 per hour. Economist A argues: "This will cause widespread job losses among young and low-skilled workers and should not be implemented." Economist B argues: "The effect on employment will be negligible, and the policy will substantially reduce in-work poverty." Identify and explain at least two distinct possible sources of their disagreement. One source should relate to positive economics, and one should relate to normative economics.

**Problem 5.** *(IB-style, 10 marks)* "The ceteris paribus assumption is simultaneously the greatest strength and the greatest weakness of economic analysis." Critically discuss this statement.

(a) Explain what the ceteris paribus assumption is and why it is necessary for economic modelling. (3 marks)
(b) Using a specific economic model you have studied, explain how ceteris paribus enables clear, testable predictions. (3 marks)
(c) Discuss the limitations of ceteris paribus as a basis for policy advice, with reference to a real-world policy issue. (4 marks)

## Answers

**Answer 1.** (a) The assumption of fixed resources is made so that the PPC model isolates the effect of resource allocation from the effect of changing resource endowments. If resources were allowed to change during the analysis, an observed increase in output could be caused either by better allocation (a movement along the PPC) or by more resources (a shift of the PPC), and we would not be able to separate the two effects. By holding resources fixed, the model ensures that any change in output combinations must represent a reallocation of existing resources, which is precisely the trade-off the model is designed to illuminate. (b) In reality, resources change constantly. A country's population grows through births and immigration. Capital equipment depreciates and is replaced, often with newer, more productive versions. New mineral deposits are discovered. Farmland degrades or improves through management practices. The assumption of fixed resources ignores all of these ongoing changes, which means the PPC, in its basic form, represents a snapshot of the economy at one moment rather than a moving picture of its evolution. (c) This assumption does not weaken the model's usefulness for understanding economic growth, provided the model is used correctly. The basic PPC with fixed resources explains the trade-off at a point in time. To analyse economic growth, economists extend the model: they show the PPC shifting outward when resources increase or technology improves. The fixed-resources assumption is the starting point for the analysis of growth, not a permanent limitation. It is like studying a still photograph before watching a film — understanding the static case is necessary for understanding the dynamic one. In IB terms, this is the distinction between the PPC model's basic form (which assumes fixed resources) and its extended form (which allows for shifts).

**Answer 2.** The commentator is mistaking correlation for causation, and the likely culprit is omitted variable bias. The omitted variable is the underlying health of the population in each region. Regions with worse population health — higher rates of chronic disease, poverty, poor sanitation, or an older population — have higher mortality rates for reasons that have nothing to do with hospitals. At the same time, regions with worse population health tend to have more hospitals per thousand residents because governments and private providers build hospitals where the need for medical care is greatest. The correlation between hospitals and mortality is therefore spurious: both are caused by the same underlying factor, namely the poor health of the population. If anything, more hospitals probably reduce mortality relative to what it would otherwise be. To test this, a researcher would need to control for the underlying health status of the population, perhaps by comparing regions with similar health profiles but different numbers of hospitals. The ceteris paribus logic — holding population health constant — would almost certainly reveal that hospitals reduce, rather than increase, mortality. A common mistake in interpreting such studies is to assume that the observed raw correlation tells a causal story, when in fact the allocation of hospitals is itself a response to the health needs of the population.

**Answer 3.** Immediately after the earthquake, the supply of rental apartments decreases. The supply curve shifts left because 10% of the housing stock has been destroyed — a negative supply shock. At the original equilibrium price of £1,200 per month, the quantity of apartments available for rent is now less than the quantity that tenants wish to rent. This creates excess demand, also called a shortage. Some tenants cannot find apartments at the existing price. The adjustment process begins as frustrated tenants offer to pay more than £1,200 to secure one of the remaining apartments. Landlords, observing that they can charge higher rents, raise prices. As rents rise, two things happen simultaneously: some tenants reduce their demand — perhaps by sharing apartments, moving to cheaper neighbourhoods, or leaving the city altogether (a contraction of quantity demanded along the demand curve) — and some landlords are incentivised to convert other buildings into rental units or to offer previously owner-occupied units for rent (an expansion of quantity supplied along the new supply curve). This process continues until the quantity demanded once again equals the quantity supplied at a new, higher equilibrium price and a lower equilibrium quantity. The new equilibrium is likely to be stable: if rents were temporarily above the new equilibrium level, a surplus would emerge and push rents back down; if rents were temporarily below it, a shortage would push rents back up. The market converges to the new equilibrium through the automatic operation of the price mechanism.

**Answer 4.** One possible source of disagreement is different positive assumptions about the responsiveness of labour demand to wage changes — what economists call the elasticity of labour demand. Economist A may believe that employers are highly sensitive to wage increases and will respond to a higher minimum wage by substituting machines for workers, reducing hours, or closing marginal operations. This would imply a large negative employment effect. Economist B may believe that labour demand is relatively unresponsive to wage changes in the sectors most affected (hospitality, retail), perhaps because machines cannot easily replace workers in these roles or because the wage increase represents a small fraction of total costs. This is a positive disagreement — it could, in principle, be settled by empirical evidence, though the evidence on the minimum wage is genuinely contested.

A second possible source is a normative disagreement about values. Both economists might agree on the exact magnitude of the employment effect — say, that the minimum wage increase will cause 50,000 workers to lose their jobs while raising wages for 1.5 million workers who retain employment. Economist A might value the principle that markets should determine wages without government interference and might believe that any job destruction, however small, is unacceptable. Economist B might value the reduction in poverty among working households and might judge that the welfare gain for 1.5 million workers outweighs the welfare loss for 50,000. This is a normative disagreement about what constitutes a desirable trade-off, and it cannot be settled by data alone. A common mistake students make is attributing all policy disagreements among economists to normative differences, when in fact many disagreements reflect genuinely contested positive questions for which the evidence is ambiguous.

**Answer 5.** (a) The ceteris paribus assumption is the analytical device of holding all variables constant except the one relationship being studied. When an economist says "an increase in price reduces quantity demanded, ceteris paribus," she means that the inverse relationship between price and quantity demanded holds when income, tastes, the prices of related goods, and all other influences on demand are held fixed. Ceteris paribus is necessary because without it, causal inference is impossible. In the real world, many variables change simultaneously. If the price of a good rises at the same time that consumer incomes rise and a new health study increases the good's popularity, we cannot tell by simple observation whether any change in quantity purchased was caused by the price change, the income change, or the taste change. Ceteris paribus isolates one causal channel so that it can be analysed and tested. (3 marks) (b) The demand-and-supply model depends entirely on ceteris paribus to generate testable predictions. The model predicts that if a government imposes a tax of £2 per unit on a good, the supply curve will shift left by exactly £2, and the equilibrium price will rise and equilibrium quantity will fall, with the exact changes depending on the relative elasticities of demand and supply. This is a precise, testable prediction. It is testable precisely because the model assumes ceteris paribus — that no other demand or supply determinants change during the period of analysis. An empirical researcher can test this prediction by comparing markets where the tax was imposed with otherwise similar markets where it was not, controlling for other observable changes. Without ceteris paribus, the model would generate no clear prediction at all. (3 marks) (c) The limitation of ceteris paribus for policy advice is that policies are implemented in a world where other things are emphatically not equal. A government considering a sugar tax to reduce obesity cannot assume that consumers' tastes, incomes, and the marketing strategies of food companies will remain unchanged after the tax is imposed. Food companies may reformulate products to avoid the tax (reducing sugar content, which achieves the policy goal without reducing consumption), or they may increase advertising of untaxed products, or consumers may switch to other high-calorie goods that are not taxed. All of these responses violate the ceteris paribus assumption. The model's prediction — "higher price, lower quantity demanded" — is directionally correct but may dramatically overstate or understate the real-world effect because it holds constant factors that will, in reality, adjust. For policy advice, the economist must move beyond the ceteris paribus model. She must ask: what else is likely to change? How will firms and consumers respond strategically? Are there feedback effects the simple model misses? This does not mean the ceteris paribus model is useless. It provides a baseline — a starting point for analysis. But the policy adviser who stops at the baseline has done only half the job. The other half is reasoning about how the real world, in which nothing is held constant, will modify the baseline prediction. (4 marks)

## Evaluate

**Question:** "Economics can never be a true science because it cannot conduct controlled laboratory experiments on entire economies." Evaluate this claim.

**Model Answer:**

The claim is flawed in its premise — that science requires controlled laboratory experiments — and in its conclusion — that economics therefore falls short of scientific status. When examined carefully, the claim confuses one specific scientific method with the broader definition of science itself.

Science is defined by its method, not by the presence of laboratories. The scientific method consists of systematic observation, hypothesis formation, empirical testing, and willingness to revise theories in light of evidence. Economics does all of these. Economists observe patterns in data — the negative correlation between inflation and unemployment in some periods, the positive correlation between education and earnings. They form hypotheses — inflation expectations drive wage-setting behaviour; education increases human capital and therefore productivity. They formalise these into models — the Phillips curve, the Mincer earnings equation. They test predictions against data, and they revise or discard models that fail empirical tests. This is the scientific method, applied to social rather than physical phenomena.

The claim's premise is also factually narrow. Astronomers cannot run experiments on stars. Evolutionary biologists cannot replay the history of life in a controlled setting. Climate scientists cannot subject the planet to alternative emissions scenarios in a laboratory. Yet astronomy, evolutionary biology, and climate science are recognised as sciences. They rely on careful observation, natural experiments, and statistical inference — precisely the tools economists use. The inability to run controlled macroeconomic experiments is a constraint shared by many disciplines, not a unique disqualification of economics.

Moreover, the claim ignores the rapid growth of experimental methods within economics. Development economists now routinely conduct randomised controlled trials to test the effects of interventions — school vouchers, microcredit, bed nets, fertiliser subsidies — on outcomes. Vernon Smith and Daniel Kahneman won Nobel Prizes for laboratory experiments in market behaviour and decision-making. These are controlled experiments in the strictest sense. They operate at the micro level rather than the macro level, but they are genuine experiments producing causal inference.

The deeper challenge for economics is not the absence of experiments but the nature of its subject matter. Human beings have expectations, emotions, and free will. They respond not only to what happens but to what they believe will happen. An economic policy announced today can change behaviour before it takes effect. A policy that is expected to fail may fail for that reason alone, through a self-fulfilling prophecy. Particles and planets do not have expectations. This makes economic prediction inherently more uncertain than prediction in physics, but it does not make economics unscientific. It makes it a science of a different kind — a social science — dealing with a subject matter that resists the precision available to the natural sciences.

The practical stakes of the claim matter. If students, policymakers, and the public believe that economics is not a science, they may dismiss economic evidence as mere opinion and make policy decisions on the basis of intuition or ideology. This would be a serious mistake. Economic evidence, while imperfect and contested, is vastly better than no evidence. The honest position is to treat economics as a genuine science — one that uses rigorous methods, generates testable predictions, accumulates knowledge over time, and communicates its conclusions with appropriate humility about the limits of that knowledge.

In conclusion, the claim rests on an arbitrarily narrow definition of science. Economics is a social science: it applies the scientific method to the study of human behaviour in conditions of scarcity. It shares the aspirations of the natural sciences — systematic knowledge, causal understanding, predictive power — while facing constraints that the natural sciences do not. The appropriate response is not to declare economics unscientific but to hold it to high standards of evidence, transparency, and intellectual honesty, and to demand that its practitioners communicate uncertainty as clearly as they communicate conclusions.
