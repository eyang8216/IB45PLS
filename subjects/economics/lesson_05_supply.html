Lesson 5: Supply — Law, Curve, and Determinants

Syllabus reference: Unit 2.3 — Supply in competitive markets

Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to:

  • Define supply and state the law of supply
  • Distinguish between a movement along the supply curve and a shift of the supply curve
  • Identify the non-price determinants of supply using the PINTS framework
  • Explain the concepts of joint supply and competitive supply
  • Draw and interpret supply curves and shifts in supply

1. Prior Knowledge Check

Question 1: In Lesson 4, you learned the law of demand: as price rises, quantity demanded falls. What do you think happens on the producer side — if the price of a good rises, do firms want to produce more or less of it? Why?

Show Answer

Answer: Firms typically want to produce more when the price rises. A higher price means higher revenue per unit sold, which makes production more profitable. This incentivises existing firms to expand output and new firms to enter the market. This is the intuition behind the law of supply.

Question 2: You learned the difference between a movement along and a shift of the demand curve. What caused each type of change for demand?

Show Answer

Answer: A movement along the demand curve is caused by a change in the good's own price. A shift of the demand curve is caused by a change in a non-price determinant (TRIBE: Tastes, Related goods, Income, Buyers, Expectations). The same logic applies to supply — we just use different non-price determinants.

2. What Supply Means in Economics

Supply is the quantity of a good or service that producers are willing and able to offer for sale at each possible price over a given period of time, ceteris paribus. As with demand, every word matters: "willing and able" (firms must have both the motivation and the capacity), "at each possible price" (supply is a relationship, not a single number), "over a given period" (supply is a flow), and "ceteris paribus" (we hold non-price factors constant).

The law of supply states that, ceteris paribus, as the price of a good rises, the quantity supplied of that good rises. As the price falls, the quantity supplied falls. Price and quantity supplied are directly (positively) related. This is the opposite of demand, where the relationship is inverse.

Why does supply slope up? There are two main reasons:

  1. The profit motive: At a higher price, producing the good is more profitable. Existing firms expand output, and new firms enter the market, attracted by the higher profits.
  2. Increasing marginal cost: As a firm produces more, the cost of producing each additional unit typically rises (overtime pay for workers, less efficient machinery pressed into service, less suitable land brought into cultivation). A higher price is needed to cover these higher costs.

Check-in: A bakery sells bread at £2 per loaf and produces 200 loaves per day. If the price rises to £3 per loaf, the bakery increases production to 280 loaves per day. Has supply increased? Use precise language.

Show Answer

Answer: No — supply has not increased. The quantity supplied has increased (from 200 to 280) in response to a higher price. This is a movement along the existing supply curve (up and to the right), not an increase in supply. An increase in supply would mean the entire curve shifts right — the bakery produces more at every price, not just at a higher price. This "supply vs. quantity supplied" distinction mirrors the "demand vs. quantity demanded" distinction from Lesson 4 and is equally mark-sensitive.

3. Movement Along vs. Shift of the Supply Curve

A movement along the supply curve occurs when the price of the good itself changes and producers adjust the quantity supplied in response. If price rises, we move up and right along the supply curve (higher quantity supplied). If price falls, we move down and left (lower quantity supplied).

A shift of the supply curve occurs when a non-price determinant of supply changes. The entire curve moves: right for an increase in supply (producers are willing to offer more at every price) or left for a decrease in supply (producers offer less at every price).

4. Non-Price Determinants of Supply — PINTS

Five categories shift the supply curve. We use PINTS:

P — Productivity. If workers or machines become more productive (producing more output per unit of input), the cost per unit falls, and firms can supply more at every price. Supply shifts right. Higher productivity can come from better training, improved technology, or more efficient organisation.

I — Indirect taxes and subsidies. These are government interventions that directly affect production costs:

  • An indirect tax (like VAT or a fuel duty) increases production costs. At every price, firms supply less. Supply shifts left.
  • A subsidy (a government payment to producers per unit) reduces production costs. At every price, firms supply more. Supply shifts right.

N — Number of firms. More firms in a market means more total supply at every price. New firms entering the market (attracted by profits) shift supply right. Firms leaving (due to losses) shift supply left.

T — Technology. Technological improvements reduce production costs. A bakery that invests in a faster oven can produce more bread with the same workers. Supply shifts right.

S — Supply shocks and costs of production. Changes in the prices of inputs (raw materials, energy, wages, rent) directly affect supply. A rise in the price of wheat (an input for bread) increases the bakery's costs. At every price for bread, the bakery offers less — supply shifts left. Supply shocks are sudden, unexpected events: a drought that destroys crops shifts agricultural supply left; the discovery of a new oil field shifts oil supply right.

Check-in: The government introduces a new tax of £0.50 on every litre of petrol sold. Separately, a new drilling technology reduces the cost of extracting crude oil by 30%. Analyse the effect of each on the supply of petrol.

Show Answer

Answer: The tax (I in PINTS) increases production costs for petrol refiners. At every price, they supply less — the supply curve for petrol shifts left. The new drilling technology (T in PINTS) reduces the cost of the key input (crude oil). At every price, refiners can supply more petrol — the supply curve shifts right. These two effects push in opposite directions. The net effect on petrol supply depends on which shift is larger.

5. Joint Supply and Competitive Supply

Joint supply occurs when producing one good automatically produces another. When a cow is slaughtered for beef, leather is produced as a by-product. You cannot produce more beef without also producing more leather. If the demand for beef rises (pushing up the price of beef), more cattle are slaughtered, and the supply of leather automatically increases — leather supply shifts right, even though nothing changed in the leather market directly.

Competitive supply occurs when the same resources can be used to produce two different goods, but not both at the same time. A farmer's field can grow wheat or barley. If the price of wheat rises, the farmer switches land from barley to wheat. The supply of wheat increases (shift right) and the supply of barley decreases (shift left), even though nothing changed in the barley market directly. The two goods compete for the same scarce resource.

Check-in: A sharp rise in the price of lamb causes sheep farmers to increase their flocks. What happens to the supply of wool? What type of supply relationship is this?

Show Answer

Answer: Wool supply increases (shifts right) — even though nothing changed in the wool market. When farmers raise more sheep for lamb meat, they also produce more wool as a by-product. This is joint supply: lamb and wool are produced together; you cannot increase one without increasing the other.

6. Worked Examples

Worked Example 1: Identifying Supply Shifters

Problem: For each scenario, state whether supply shifts right, left, or does not shift, and identify the PINTS determinant: (a) Wages for construction workers rise by 20%. (b) The government offers a £5,000 subsidy per electric vehicle produced. (c) Three new coffee shops open in a neighbourhood.

Step 1: (a) Higher wages increase production costs (S — costs of production). At every price, construction firms supply less. Supply shifts left.

Step 2: (b) The subsidy (I — indirect taxes and subsidies) reduces the effective cost per vehicle. At every price, firms supply more electric vehicles. Supply shifts right.

Step 3: (c) Three new firms (N — number of firms) mean more total supply at every price. Supply shifts right.

⚠️ Common Wrong Approach: For (a), some students say supply shifts right because "higher wages mean workers can afford more, so demand increases." This confuses supply (producers) with demand (consumers). Higher wages are a cost to the producer, so they reduce supply. The effect on workers' incomes is a demand-side story, not a supply-side one.

Worked Example 2: Joint Supply Trap

Problem: The price of beef rises sharply due to increased demand from export markets. Analyse the effect on the market for leather. (Exam trap: students analyse leather in isolation and miss the joint supply connection.)

Step 1: Beef and leather are goods in joint supply — both come from cattle. When more cattle are slaughtered for beef, more leather is automatically produced.

Step 2: The rise in the price of beef (driven by higher demand for beef) leads to an increase in cattle slaughter. This increases the supply of leather at every price — the leather supply curve shifts right. The price of leather falls and the quantity of leather traded rises.

Step 3: Notice that nothing changed in the leather market itself — no change in leather demand, no change in leather production technology. The supply shift in the leather market is entirely due to events in the beef market.

⚠️ Common Wrong Approach: Analysing the leather market in isolation as if the change in beef prices has no effect. The IB tests joint supply precisely because students forget to look for connections between markets.

Worked Example 3: Multiple Simultaneous Shifts

Problem: A severe drought destroys half of the wheat harvest. Simultaneously, the government removes a subsidy that had been paid to wheat farmers. Analyse the combined effect on wheat supply.

Step 1: The drought is a supply shock (S in PINTS) — it reduces the quantity of wheat that can be produced. Supply shifts left.

Step 2: Removing the subsidy (I in PINTS) increases the effective cost of production for farmers. Without the subsidy payment, farmers receive less per unit. Supply also shifts left.

Step 3: Both changes push supply in the same direction — left. The combined effect is a large leftward shift of the wheat supply curve. The equilibrium price of wheat will rise significantly, and the quantity will fall significantly.

⚠️ Common Wrong Approach: Failing to separate the two effects. Even though both push supply left, the IB examiner wants you to identify each one individually before stating the net result. "Supply decreases due to a supply shock (drought) and the removal of a production subsidy" earns more marks than "supply decreases."

⚠️ Common Mistakes to Avoid

  1. Mistake: Confusing "supply" with "quantity supplied" — exactly the parallel error from demand. Why it's wrong: "Supply" is the entire curve. "Quantity supplied" is a point on the curve. Correct approach: Own-price change → movement along. Any other factor → shift.
  2. Mistake: Drawing the supply curve sloping downward. Why it's wrong: The law of supply says price and quantity supplied are positively related — higher price, higher quantity. Correct approach: Always draw supply sloping upward, from bottom-left to top-right.
  3. Mistake: Treating a change in input costs as not affecting supply because "the firm can just raise prices." Why it's wrong: At any given price, higher costs mean the firm is less willing to supply. That is the definition of a supply decrease. Correct approach: Higher input costs shift the supply curve left. Whether firms actually do raise prices is determined by the interaction of supply and demand — that comes in Lesson 6.

7. End-of-Lesson Practice

Question 1. [Paper 1, MC] Which of the following would cause a rightward shift of the supply curve for furniture?

A. An increase in the price of furniture.
B. A decrease in the price of wood, a key input.
C. An increase in consumer incomes.
D. A new tax on furniture production.

Question 2. [Paper 1, MC] The government introduces a new regulation requiring all food manufacturers to install expensive safety equipment. What is the effect on the supply of processed food?

A. Movement up and right along the supply curve.
B. Movement down and left along the supply curve.
C. The supply curve shifts to the left.
D. The supply curve shifts to the right.

Question 3. [Paper 2] Distinguish between a movement along the supply curve and a shift of the supply curve. Provide one cause for each, using the market for coffee as an example. [4 marks]

Question 4. [Paper 2] Using the PINTS framework, explain how each of the following affects the supply of clothing: (a) The introduction of robot sewing machines that cut labour costs by 40%. (b) A rise in the world price of cotton. (c) Ten new clothing brands enter the market. [6 marks]

Question 5. [Paper 2, Exam Style] A drought in Brazil destroys 30% of the coffee harvest. At the same time, the Brazilian government introduces a new subsidy for coffee farmers to help them recover. (a) Use a supply diagram to show the effect of each change on the coffee market. [4 marks] (b) Explain why the net effect on the equilibrium price of coffee is uncertain. [2 marks]

Answer 1: B. Cheaper wood reduces input costs (S in PINTS), shifting supply right. A is an own-price change (movement along). C affects demand, not supply. D would shift supply left.

Answer 2: C. The expensive safety equipment increases production costs. At every price, firms are willing to supply less — supply shifts left.

Answer 3: A movement along occurs when the price of coffee itself changes — e.g., if coffee prices rise from £3 to £4 per pound, quantity supplied increases (movement up the supply curve). A shift occurs when a non-price determinant changes — e.g., if a new harvesting technology reduces costs, the entire supply curve shifts right.

Answer 4: (a) Technology (T) — lower labour costs shift supply right. (b) Cotton is an input; higher costs (S) shift supply left. (c) More firms (N) shift supply right.

Answer 5: (a) Drought: supply shock (S), supply shifts left. Subsidy: government payment reduces costs (I), supply shifts right. Diagram: show S₁ shifting left to S₂ (drought), then right to S₃ (subsidy). (b) The drought shifts supply left (pushing price up); the subsidy shifts supply right (pushing price down). The net effect depends on which shift is larger. If the subsidy is large enough to offset the drought damage, the price might not rise — but if the crop loss is severe, price will still rise despite the subsidy.

8. Key Terms

TermDefinition
SupplyThe quantity of a good that producers are willing and able to offer for sale at each possible price over a given time period, ceteris paribus
Law of supplyAs price rises, quantity supplied rises; as price falls, quantity supplied falls — ceteris paribus
Movement along the supply curveA change in quantity supplied caused by a change in the good's own price
Shift of the supply curveA change in supply caused by a change in a non-price determinant; the entire curve moves
Joint supplyWhen producing one good automatically produces another (e.g., beef and leather)

9. Exam Tips

Paper 1: When explaining the law of supply, draw the upward-sloping curve and label it "S." Explain why it slopes upward — the profit motive and increasing marginal cost. Reference ceteris paribus.
Paper 2: Supply questions in data response often involve a supply shock (drought, war, pandemic) combined with a government policy (tax, subsidy, regulation). Always separate the two effects before combining them.
