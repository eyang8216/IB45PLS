Lesson 5: Supply — Law, Curve, and Determinants

What You'll Learn

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to:

  • Define supply and state the law of supply
  • Construct and interpret supply schedules and curves
  • Distinguish between individual and market supply
  • Explain why supply curves slope upward
  • Differentiate between a movement along the curve and a shift of the curve
  • Identify and apply the non-price determinants of supply (PINTS)
  • Distinguish between joint supply and competitive supply

1. Definition of Supply and the Law of Supply

What Is Supply?

Supply is the quantity of a good or service that producers are willing and able to produce and sell at each possible price, over a given time period, ceteris paribus.

  • Willing: The firm finds it profitable or strategically desirable to produce
  • Able: The firm has the productive capacity (resources, technology) to produce

Supply reflects producers' behaviour — it is not about what producers could theoretically produce, but what they choose to produce given prices and costs.

The Law of Supply

As the price of a good rises, the quantity supplied rises; as the price falls, the quantity supplied falls — ceteris paribus.

There is a positive (direct) relationship between price and quantity supplied. Higher prices give producers both the incentive and the means to produce more.

Supply Curve

The supply curve is the graphical representation of the supply schedule. It slopes upward from left to right.

Individual Supply vs Market Supply

  • Individual supply: The supply of a single firm
  • Market supply: The horizontal summation of all individual firm supply curves in the market
Example: If Firm A supplies 200 cups at £3 and Firm B supplies 300 cups at £3, market supply at £3 is 200 + 300 = 500 cups.

3. Why Does the Supply Curve Slope Upward?

1. The Profit Motive

When the market price rises, producing the good becomes more profitable. Existing firms expand output to capture higher profits. New firms may enter the market, attracted by the profit opportunity. Higher prices signal to producers: "Make more of this."

2. Increasing Marginal Costs

In the short run, as a firm produces more, its marginal cost (cost of producing one more unit) typically rises. This is due to the law of diminishing marginal returns: with a fixed factor (e.g., factory size), adding more variable inputs (workers) eventually yields smaller and smaller increases in output. Each additional unit costs more to produce.

Firms will only produce an additional unit if the price they receive covers the marginal cost. Since marginal cost rises with output, a higher price is needed to justify higher output. Thus the supply curve is the marginal cost curve (above the shutdown point).

3. Time and Capacity Constraints

In the short run, firms cannot instantly expand capacity. To increase output, they must use existing capacity more intensively — pay workers overtime, run machines longer, use less efficient backup equipment — all of which raise per-unit costs.

4. Movement Along vs Shift of the Supply Curve

This parallels the demand-side distinction and is equally important for IB exams.

Movement Along the Supply Curve

A change in the good's own price causes a movement along the existing supply curve.

  • Price rises → movement up and right along the curve (higher Qs) — called an expansion of supply
  • Price falls → movement down and left along the curve (lower Qs) — called a contraction of supply

The supply curve itself does NOT shift.

Shift of the Supply Curve

A change in any non-price determinant causes the entire supply curve to shift.

  • Increase in supply → curve shifts RIGHT (more supplied at every price)
  • Decrease in supply → curve shifts LEFT (less supplied at every price)

5. Non-Price Determinants of Supply — PINTS

The factors that shift the supply curve can be remembered with the acronym PINTS:

P — Productivity

Higher productivity means more output from the same inputs (lower per-unit costs). This increases supply (shifts right). Productivity can rise due to:

  • Better worker training and education (human capital)
  • Improved management practices
  • Better organisation of production

Lower productivity (e.g., due to worker burnout, outdated methods) shifts supply left.

I — Indirect Taxes and Subsidies

Indirect taxes (e.g., VAT, excise duties on alcohol/tobacco/fuel) increase firms' costs. This decreases supply (shifts left).

Subsidies are payments from government to firms, reducing their costs. This increases supply (shifts right).

Important: The IB distinguishes between the imposition of a tax/subsidy (shift of supply) and the incidence (who actually bears the burden — covered in later lessons). For now, know that taxes shift supply left and subsidies shift supply right.

N — Number of Firms

More firms in the market → greater market supply (shifts right). Fewer firms (due to bankruptcies, mergers, exit) → market supply falls (shifts left).

Entry and exit depend on:

  • Barriers to entry: High barriers (licences, large capital requirements) limit entry
  • Profitability: If incumbent firms earn supernormal profits, new firms are attracted (in the long run, under competitive conditions)

T — Technology

Technological improvements allow firms to produce more output with the same inputs (or the same output with fewer inputs). This lowers production costs and increases supply (shifts right).

  • Automation replacing manual labour
  • Better software improving logistics and inventory management
  • New production techniques (e.g., 3D printing, precision agriculture)

Technological regression is rare but possible (e.g., loss of knowledge, destruction of infrastructure in war).

S — Supply Shocks and Input Costs

Input costs (raw materials, energy, wages, rent): If input prices rise, production costs rise, and supply decreases (shifts left). If input prices fall, supply increases (shifts right).

Supply shocks: Sudden, unexpected events affecting production:

  • Negative supply shock: Natural disaster destroying crops, pandemic disrupting supply chains, war cutting off energy supplies → supply shifts left
  • Positive supply shock: Unexpectedly good harvest, discovery of abundant cheap energy → supply shifts right

6. Joint Supply and Competitive Supply

Joint Supply

Joint supply occurs when the production of one good automatically yields another good as a by-product. The two goods are produced together — increasing production of one necessarily increases output of the other.

  • Examples: Beef and leather (slaughtering cattle yields both); crude oil and natural gas (often extracted together); wheat and straw
  • Implication: If demand (and price) for beef rises, production of beef increases → supply of leather automatically increases (shifts right)

Competitive Supply

Competitive supply occurs when two goods compete for the same resources in production. Producing more of one requires diverting resources from the other, reducing its output.

  • Examples: Wheat and corn (compete for the same farmland); a factory producing either cars or trucks on the same assembly line; a farmer choosing between dairy and crop farming
  • Implication: If the price of wheat rises, farmers allocate more land to wheat → supply of corn decreases (shifts left)

IB Exam Tip

When analysing supply changes, always specify why the curve slopes upward (profit motive, increasing marginal costs). IB examiners look for:

  1. Clear distinction between "quantity supplied" (movement along) and "supply" (shift)
  2. Correct use of non-price determinant names — use PINTS as a checklist
  3. Explanation of the mechanism: how does the determinant affect costs and thus supply?
  4. Recognition that supply-side factors often interact with demand — but analyse them separately first

Common mistake: Saying "supply increases" when you mean "quantity supplied increases due to a price rise." Be precise.

Practice Problems

Problem 1: Movement vs Shift

For each scenario, state whether it causes a movement along the supply curve or a shift, and in which direction.

(a) The price of wheat rises.

(b) New fertiliser technology doubles crop yields.

(c) The government imposes a new tax on sugary drinks.

(d) A drought destroys half the wheat crop.

(e) More farmers enter the wheat market.

(f) The price of wheat falls by 20%.

Problem 2: Identifying Determinants (PINTS)

For each scenario, identify which PINTS determinant is at work and the direction of the shift:

(a) Wage rates for construction workers rise by 15%. (Market: new housing)

(b) The government offers wind farm operators a subsidy of £50 per MWh. (Market: renewable electricity)

(c) Three major smartphone manufacturers exit the market. (Market: smartphones)

(d) AI-powered robots reduce assembly time by 60%. (Market: automobiles)

(e) Better training programmes improve worker skills. (Market: any manufactured good)

Problem 6: IB-Style (Challenging)

A market has two identical firms, each with supply: q = −50 + 25P.

(a) Derive the market supply function.

(b) If the government imposes a per-unit tax of £4, each firm's supply becomes q = −150 + 25P. Derive the new market supply function. At P = £10, calculate the change in market quantity supplied.

(c) Instead of a tax, suppose three new identical firms enter the market. Derive the new market supply function (5 firms total). At P = £10, calculate Qs. Compare with part (b) — which has a larger effect on quantity supplied at £10?

(d) Discuss one reason why the government might prefer a tax over allowing market entry, even if the entry would result in more supply at a given price.

Answers

Answer 1: Movement vs Shift

(a) Movement along — own price of wheat rises → expansion of supply (up and right along the curve).

(b) Shift right — technology (T in PINTS) improves → more wheat supplied at every price.

(c) Shift left — indirect tax (I in PINTS) raises costs → less supplied at every price.

(d) Shift left — negative supply shock (S in PINTS) → less wheat at every price.

(e) Shift right — number of firms (N in PINTS) increases → more market supply at every price.

(f) Movement along — own price falls → contraction of supply (down and left along the curve).

Answer 2: Identifying Determinants

(a) S — Supply shocks/Input costs. Wages are an input cost. Higher wages → higher production costs → supply shifts left (decrease).

(b) I — Indirect taxes/Subsidies. Subsidy reduces firms' effective costs → supply shifts right (increase).

(c) N — Number of firms. Fewer firms → lower market supply → supply shifts left (decrease).

(d) T — Technology. AI/robotics improve production efficiency → lower costs → supply shifts right (increase).

(e) P — Productivity. Better training → higher output per worker → lower per-unit costs → supply shifts right (increase).

Answer 3: Joint vs Competitive Supply

(a) Milk and cheese: Competitive supply. A dairy farmer can use milk to produce either cheese or sell it as liquid milk. The same milk is allocated between these competing uses.

(b) Wool and lamb: Joint supply. Sheep produce both wool and meat. Raising more sheep for wool automatically increases lamb production (and vice versa).

(c) Sugar cane and ethanol: Joint supply (from the same crop) — the same sugar cane can be processed into sugar or fermented into ethanol. However, after initial processing, the allocation between sugar and ethanol becomes competitive. The relationship is complex, but from the crop itself, it starts as joint supply.

(d) Chairs and tables: Competitive supply. The factory's production line can make one or the other (or switch between them). The same resources (machines, workers, space) compete between the two products.

Answer 4: Supply Schedule Analysis

(a) At P = £10: The firm will supply 50 T-shirts. The supply schedule shows that at P = £8, Qs = 50, and at P = £12, Qs = 150. A rational profit-maximising firm supplies up to the point where P ≥ MC. At P = £10, the firm produces 50 units (the last produced at MC = £8) but not 150 (which would require MC = £12, which exceeds P = £10). So Qs at £10 is between 50 and 150 — the firm would produce approximately 100 units if MC rises linearly.

(Note: With discrete data, the exact answer depends on the marginal cost function. The key insight: the firm supplies where P ≥ MC.)

(b) At P = £14: The firm supplies between 150 and 300 units. P = £14 covers MC up to £12 (150 units) but not MC = £16. So Qs ≈ 150–300, likely around 225 if MC is linear.

(c) At P = £5: The firm supplies zero. P = £5 is below the firm's minimum marginal cost (£8 at the first unit). The firm cannot cover even the variable costs of the first unit, so it shuts down.

(d) P = MC at the margin: At each output level, the price equals the marginal cost of the last unit produced (approximately). The supply curve is the marginal cost curve (above the shutdown point). This is because a profit-maximising firm produces where MR = MC, and in perfect competition, MR = P, so P = MC.

Answer 5: Supply Curve Shifts

(a) At P = £6: Qs = −200 + 50(6) = −200 + 300 = 100 tonnes.

(b) At P = £6 with new supply: Qs = −400 + 50(6) = −400 + 300 = −100 tonnes (effectively zero — the firm cannot supply at £6 with the new cost structure).

Supply has decreased (shifted left). This is a shift of the supply curve caused by a negative supply shock (drought → S in PINTS). At every price, less is supplied.

(c) At P = £10 with original supply: Qs = −200 + 50(10) = −200 + 500 = 300 tonnes.

This is a movement along the original supply curve (expansion of supply). The supply curve itself hasn't changed.

(d) The drought (shift left) means less avocado is supplied at every price — the entire supply relationship has deteriorated. To get the original Qs = 100, the price would now need to be higher. The price rise (movement along) is the market's response to some change — producers supply more because the price rose, not because they can produce more cheaply.

For consumers: a supply shift left means higher prices AND lower quantities (bad for consumers). A movement along triggered by higher demand means higher prices but also HIGHER quantities (consumers at least get more avocados, even at higher prices).

Answer 6: IB-Style Solution

(a) Market supply = 2 × q = 2(−50 + 25P) = −100 + 50P.

(b) New firm supply: q = −150 + 25P.

New market supply = 2(−150 + 25P) = −300 + 50P.

At P = £10:

  • Original Qs = −100 + 50(10) = −100 + 500 = 400
  • New Qs = −300 + 50(10) = −300 + 500 = 200
  • Change = −200 units (supply decreased).

(c) 5 firms, each with original supply:

Market supply = 5(−50 + 25P) = −250 + 125P.

At P = £10: Qs = −250 + 125(10) = −250 + 1250 = 1,000.

Comparison:

  • Tax (part b): Qs = 200
  • Entry (part c): Qs = 1,000

Entry has a much larger effect on quantity supplied at P = £10 (1,000 vs 200). This makes sense: the tax shifts each firm's supply left (higher costs), while entry adds entire new supply curves.

(d) Government might prefer a tax despite lower supply because:

  • Revenue: The tax generates government revenue (4 × 200 = £800 at P = £10) that can fund public services. Entry generates no direct government revenue.
  • Market stability: Entry may lead to overcapacity and eventual bankruptcies, creating instability. A tax is predictable.
  • Externalities: If the good has negative externalities (pollution from production), a tax internalises the external cost — reducing supply is actually the goal, not a side effect.
  • Political economy: Entry is a market process the government cannot directly control; a tax is a policy instrument under government control.

Evaluate

Question

"The law of supply is more reliable than the law of demand because producers are more rational than consumers." Evaluate this statement.

Model Answer

Conclusion: The claim is problematic on multiple levels. While the law of supply is indeed a robust empirical regularity, its "reliability" relative to the law of demand has nothing to do with producers being "more rational" than consumers — both laws rest on different mechanisms, and both have exceptions.

First, the premise that "producers are more rational than consumers" is questionable. Both producers and consumers generally act in their perceived self-interest given their constraints and information. Consumers may sometimes make inconsistent or impulse purchases, but so do firms — corporate history is replete with irrational investment decisions, herd behaviour, and managerial overconfidence. Behavioural economics has documented systematic deviations from rationality in both consumers (present bias, loss aversion) and firms (overoptimism, sunk cost fallacy). There is no compelling evidence that producers are systematically more rational.

Second, the reliability of the law of supply and the law of demand depends on different mechanisms. The law of demand rests on the income effect, substitution effect, and diminishing marginal utility — all of which are strong and general. The law of supply rests on the profit motive and increasing marginal costs. Both are well-supported empirically, but they can be disrupted by different factors.

Long-term vs short-term: In the short run, both laws have exceptions: the law of demand is challenged by Veblen and Giffen goods; the law of supply can be challenged when firms face fixed capacity (supply is perfectly inelastic in the very short run — a price rise cannot immediately increase output). In the long run, both laws become more reliable as consumers and producers fully adjust.

Assumptions: The law of supply assumes that firms are profit-maximisers with flexible production. In reality, some firms are not purely profit-driven (non-profits, state-owned enterprises), and some face capacity constraints that make supply unresponsive to price. Similarly, the law of demand assumes rational consumers with stable preferences — an assumption that behavioural economics complicates but does not destroy.

Stakeholders: For policymakers, both laws are sufficiently reliable for practical purposes. When designing a tax, they can reasonably expect that higher prices will reduce quantity demanded (law of demand) and that a tax will shift the supply curve left (law of supply). Neither law needs to be "more reliable" — they address different sides of the market.

Priorities: Rather than ranking the laws, students should understand that both are central tendencies with well-understood exceptions. The interesting questions are not "which law is more reliable?" but "under what conditions might each law fail, and why?"

Pros/Cons: The law of supply may appear more mechanical (cost curves are observable; preferences are not), giving it an illusion of greater reliability (pro for supply's perceived reliability). However, supply is heavily dependent on external factors — weather, geopolitics, technology shocks — that can cause sudden, unpredictable shifts (con). Demand tends to be more stable and predictable in the aggregate (pro for demand), though individual consumer behaviour may seem erratic (con).

Final judgment: The statement is unconvincing. Both the law of supply and the law of demand are well-established economic principles with strong theoretical foundations and extensive empirical support. Neither is "more reliable" in a meaningful sense, and the claim about producer rationality is an unsubstantiated stereotype. Good economics treats both laws as complementary tools for understanding market behaviour, each with its own domain of applicability and its own set of caveats.
