## 1. What Demand Means in Economics

### The Everyday Word vs. the Economic Concept

In everyday language, "demand" means wanting something. A person scrolling through photographs of tropical islands says, "I demand a holiday." This is not what economists mean.

In economics, **demand** is the quantity of a good or service that consumers are willing **and able** to purchase at each possible price, over a given time period, holding all other influences constant (ceteris paribus).

The phrase "willing and able" does half the work in this definition. A consumer must desire the good — that is the willing part. The consumer must also have the purchasing power to buy it — that is the able part. A student may desperately want a new games console priced at £450. If she has £35 in her bank account, she does not demand the console in the economic sense. Her desire is real. Her demand is zero. A millionaire may have the ability to buy a private island but no desire to do so. Again: no demand.

Demand is not a wish or a dream. It is a measurable intention backed by money, expressed at a specific price, in a specific time period. Change the price, change the time period, or change the consumer's income, and demand may change.

### Why Demand Matters

Consumer demand is the engine of market economies. Firms do not produce goods at random and hope someone buys them. They study demand — what consumers want, at what prices, in what quantities — and make production decisions accordingly. If demand for electric vehicles rises, manufacturers expand production, invest in battery technology, and compete for market share. If demand for printed newspapers falls, publishers cut print runs, lay off journalists, and shift resources to digital platforms. Understanding demand is understanding half of every market — the half that represents the people who ultimately pay for everything.

## 2. The Law of Demand

### The Statement

The **law of demand** is one of the most robust empirical regularities in all of economics. It states:

> As the price of a good rises, the quantity demanded of that good falls, ceteris paribus. As the price falls, the quantity demanded rises, ceteris paribus.

There is an **inverse relationship** between price and quantity demanded. When the price of a cup of coffee rises from £3 to £4, consumers buy fewer cups — not because they stop wanting coffee, but because at the higher price, they choose to economise: one cup instead of two, a smaller size, or a home-brewed alternative.

This relationship holds for virtually all goods virtually all of the time. There are rare exceptions — Veblen goods (where a higher price signals higher status and actually increases demand) and Giffen goods (extremely poor consumers forced to buy more of a staple when its price rises because they can no longer afford anything else). These exceptions are theoretically important and the IB exam expects you to know they exist. They do not undermine the law of demand as a practical guide to how markets work.

---

## 3. Why Demand Curves Slope Downward

### Three Effects That Produce the Inverse Relationship

The inverse relationship between price and quantity demanded is not an arbitrary fact. It is the result of three distinct effects that push consumers in the same direction when prices change.

**The income effect.** When the price of a good falls, the consumer experiences an increase in real income — her purchasing power rises because the same amount of money now buys more. A consumer with a weekly coffee budget of £15 can buy five cups at £3 per cup. If the price drops to £2 per cup, that same £15 buys seven and a half cups. Some of this increased purchasing power will be spent on buying more coffee. A common mistake students make is confusing this with an actual increase in money income. The consumer's salary has not changed. What has changed is what that salary can buy.

**The substitution effect.** When the price of a good falls, it becomes cheaper relative to goods that can satisfy a similar want. Consumers respond by substituting toward the now-cheaper good and away from its now-relatively-expensive alternatives. If the price of chicken falls from £6 to £4 per kilogram while the price of beef remains at £8, consumers shift some of their protein spending from beef to chicken. The substitution effect always pushes quantity demanded in the opposite direction of the price change: when price falls, quantity demanded rises through substitution; when price rises, quantity demanded falls through substitution away from the good.

**Diminishing marginal utility.** As a consumer consumes more units of a good in a given period, the additional satisfaction — the **marginal utility** — from each successive unit decreases. The first cup of coffee in the morning provides enormous satisfaction. The second cup is nice but less essential. By the fourth cup, the consumer might feel jittery rather than satisfied. Because each additional unit provides less benefit, the consumer is only willing to buy an additional unit if the price is low enough to justify the smaller gain. A consumer who would happily pay £4 for the first cup of the day might pay only £1 for a fifth cup. Lower prices are needed to induce purchases of additional units.

These three effects work together. For a normal good, all three push quantity demanded upward when price falls and downward when price rises. For an inferior good, the income effect works in the opposite direction (a fall in price makes the consumer effectively richer, and richer consumers buy less of inferior goods), but the substitution effect and diminishing marginal utility usually dominate, so the demand curve still slopes downward.

## 4. Demand Schedules and Demand Curves

### From Table to Graph

A **demand schedule** is a table showing the quantity of a good that consumers are willing and able to buy at each possible price, over a given time period. Consider the weekly demand for sandwiches at a university café:

| Price per sandwich (£) | Quantity demanded (sandwiches per week) |
|---|---|
| 6.00 | 80 |
| 5.00 | 150 |
| 4.00 | 250 |
| 3.00 | 400 |
| 2.00 | 600 |
| 1.00 | 900 |

When the price is £6, relatively few students buy sandwiches — only 80 per week. Many bring food from home or skip lunch. At £2, sandwiches are cheap relative to alternatives, and 600 are sold.

The **demand curve** is the graphical representation of this data. Place price on the vertical axis and quantity demanded on the horizontal axis — a convention that students sometimes find counterintuitive, since price is the independent variable. Plot each price-quantity pair as a point and connect them. The result is a downward-sloping curve.

A common mistake is labelling the axes with vague terms like "P" and "Q" without units. In IB exams, always write the units: "Price (£ per sandwich)" and "Quantity (sandwiches per week)." The examiner is looking for precision.

### Individual Demand and Market Demand

An individual consumer's demand for a good is called **individual demand**. The total demand from all consumers in a market is called **market demand**. Market demand is found by horizontally summing individual demand curves — at each price, add up the quantities demanded by every individual consumer.

If Alice wants 3 sandwiches when the price is £3, Bob wants 5, and Chen wants 2, the market quantity demanded at £3 is 3 + 5 + 2 = 10 sandwiches. Repeat this addition at every price to construct the market demand schedule.

## 5. Movement Along the Curve vs. Shift of the Curve

### The Distinction That Underpins All Market Analysis

This distinction is not a technical detail. It is the analytical foundation of every demand-and-supply question you will encounter in IB Economics. Confusing the two is the most common error in Paper 1 and Paper 2.

A **movement along the demand curve** occurs when the price of the good itself changes. Nothing else about the market has changed — incomes are the same, tastes are the same, the prices of related goods are the same. The consumer simply responds to the new price by moving to a different point on the existing demand curve.

- When price falls, there is an **expansion of demand**: quantity demanded increases as we move down and to the right along the curve.
- When price rises, there is a **contraction of demand**: quantity demanded decreases as we move up and to the left along the curve.

The demand curve itself does not move. The relationship between price and quantity demanded has not changed. We are simply observing a different point on the same relationship.

A **shift of the demand curve** occurs when something other than the good's own price changes — consumer income, tastes, the price of a related good, the number of buyers, or expectations about the future. The entire curve moves. At every single price, consumers now want a different quantity.

- When demand **increases**, the curve shifts to the right. At every price, quantity demanded is higher.
- When demand **decreases**, the curve shifts to the left. At every price, quantity demanded is lower.

### Why the Terminology Matters

The IB exam rewards precise language. Never write "demand increases" when you mean "quantity demanded increases." The first describes a shift of the curve (caused by a non-price determinant). The second describes a movement along the curve (caused by a change in the good's own price). Use the wrong phrase, and you will lose marks even if your diagram is correct.

## 6. The Non-Price Determinants of Demand — TRIBE

Five categories of factors, independent of the good's own price, can shift the entire demand curve. The acronym **TRIBE** helps you remember them.

### T — Tastes and Preferences

Anything that changes how much consumers desire a good shifts demand. Tastes can change because of advertising (a successful marketing campaign makes a brand more desirable), social trends (plant-based diets become fashionable, shifting demand for meat substitutes right), new health information (a study linking processed meat to cancer shifts demand for bacon left), or seasonal factors (demand for ice cream rises in summer, falls in winter).

A common mistake is to say that a change in "fashion" shifts demand. This is too narrow. Use the term "tastes and preferences" — it covers advertising, health information, social norms, seasonal patterns, and cultural change. The IB examiner is looking for the precise terminology.

### R — Related Goods Prices

The demand for one good can be affected by changes in the price of a different good, provided the two goods are related in consumption.

**Substitutes** are goods that can replace each other in satisfying a similar want. Tea and coffee are substitutes. If the price of tea rises from £2 to £4 per box, some tea drinkers switch to coffee. The demand for coffee shifts right. A change in the price of one substitute shifts the demand for the other substitute in the same direction. This relationship is reciprocal: if the price of coffee rises, demand for tea shifts right.

**Complements** are goods that are used together. Printers and ink cartridges are complements. If the price of printers falls from £200 to £120, more people buy printers. Each new printer owner needs ink cartridges, so the demand for ink cartridges shifts right. A change in the price of one complement shifts the demand for the other complement in the opposite direction. When the price of cars rises, fewer cars are sold, and the demand for petrol shifts left.

A common mistake students make is to treat a change in the price of a related good as causing a movement along the demand curve of the good in question. It does not. It causes a shift. The price of tea changes, and the demand curve for coffee shifts. The price of coffee itself has not changed, but at every price of coffee, consumers now want a different quantity.

### I — Income

Changes in consumer income shift demand, but the direction depends on the type of good.

A **normal good** is one for which demand increases when income rises and decreases when income falls. Most goods are normal. When a consumer gets a pay rise from £30,000 to £36,000 per year, she buys more restaurant meals, better quality clothing, and perhaps a newer car. Demand for normal goods shifts right as incomes rise.

Within normal goods, economists distinguish **necessities** and **luxuries**. A necessity is a normal good for which demand rises less than proportionally with income. If income rises by 10%, spending on basic food might rise by only 3%. A luxury is a normal good for which demand rises more than proportionally with income. If income rises by 10%, spending on international travel might rise by 20%.

An **inferior good** is one for which demand falls when income rises and rises when income falls. The word "inferior" is an economic classification, not a quality judgment. It simply means that as consumers become richer, they choose to buy less of this good because they can now afford something they prefer. Instant noodles are an inferior good for many consumers: when income is low, instant noodles are an affordable source of calories; when income rises, consumers switch to fresh pasta, restaurant meals, or higher-quality food. Bus travel is often an inferior good: when income rises, consumers switch to taxis, ride-sharing, or private cars. A common mistake is to assume that inferior goods are low-quality in some absolute sense. Some inferior goods — certain types of second-hand clothing, for example — may be perfectly functional. They are inferior only in the sense that consumers choose to buy less of them as their purchasing power grows.

### B — Buyers (Number of Consumers)

Market demand is the sum of individual demands. When the number of consumers in a market changes, market demand shifts, even if each individual consumer's demand remains unchanged. Population growth increases the number of buyers for housing, food, and energy — demand shifts right. The ageing of a population increases the number of buyers for healthcare and decreases the number of buyers for education — demand for healthcare shifts right, demand for education shifts left. Immigration expands the consumer base in the receiving country. Emigration shrinks it.

### E — Expectations

Consumer expectations about future prices, future income, or future availability affect how much they want to buy today. If consumers expect the price of petrol to rise sharply next month — perhaps because of an announced tax increase — they may fill their tanks today, increasing current demand. The demand curve for petrol this week shifts right. If consumers expect the price of a new smartphone model to fall after the holiday season, they may postpone their purchase, decreasing current demand. The demand curve shifts left.

Expectations about future income work similarly. If consumers fear a recession and possible job losses, they may reduce spending on durable goods and luxuries today, even though their income has not yet fallen. Precautionary saving shifts current demand left.

## 7. Normal Goods and Inferior Goods — A Closer Look

The distinction between normal and inferior goods is defined entirely by the relationship between income and demand. It is not about the good's absolute quality, price, or production cost. The table below summarises the relationship.

| Change in Income | Effect on Demand for a Normal Good | Effect on Demand for an Inferior Good |
|---|---|---|
| Income increases | Demand increases (curve shifts right) | Demand decreases (curve shifts left) |
| Income decreases | Demand decreases (curve shifts left) | Demand increases (curve shifts right) |

A good can be normal for one consumer and inferior for another. A wealthy professional might regard bus travel as inferior — she switches to driving as her income rises. A student with very low income might regard bus travel as normal — when she gets a part-time job and her income rises slightly, she might actually use the bus more to reach her new workplace. Context matters.

A good can also change its classification over time. Fifty years ago, mobile phones were luxuries. Today, in developed economies, a basic mobile phone is a necessity — demand rises with income but less than proportionally. The classification is not inherent in the good. It describes consumer behaviour in a particular time and place.

## 8. Substitutes and Complements — A Closer Look

The relationship between two goods in consumption determines whether they are substitutes or complements.

**Substitutes** satisfy a similar want. Consumers choose between them. Coca-Cola and Pepsi, butter and margarine, train travel and domestic flights, physical books and e-readers — each pair can replace the other. The key test: if you would buy more of Good A when the price of Good B rises, A and B are substitutes.

**Complements** are used together. Increasing consumption of one tends to increase consumption of the other. Cars and petrol, smartphones and mobile apps, tennis rackets and tennis balls, coffee machines and coffee pods — each pair is consumed jointly. The key test: if you would buy less of Good A when the price of Good B rises, A and B are complements.

A common mistake students make is confusing substitutes in consumption with substitutes in production. The demand-side classification (substitutes/complements) is about how consumers use goods. The supply-side classification (joint supply/competitive supply, covered in Lesson 5) is about how producers make goods. Keep these frameworks separate.

## IB Exam Tip

When an exam question asks you to analyse a change in a market, follow this sequence:

1. **Identify the nature of the change.** Is it a change in the good's own price? If yes, it is a movement along the demand curve. Is it a change in tastes, income, the price of a related good, the number of buyers, or expectations? If yes, it is a shift of the demand curve.

2. **Name the specific determinant.** Use the TRIBE terminology. Write "an increase in consumer income (I in TRIBE)" rather than "people have more money." Write "a rise in the price of a substitute good (R in TRIBE)" rather than "the other thing got more expensive." Precision matters.

3. **State the direction.** Use the exact phrases: "increase in demand" (shift right) or "decrease in demand" (shift left); "expansion of demand" (movement down and right along the curve) or "contraction of demand" (movement up and left along the curve).

4. **Explain the mechanism.** Why does the shift or movement occur? What are consumers doing differently? Connect the determinant to the behaviour of the people represented by the curve.

5. **Draw the diagram.** Label axes with names and units. Show initial and new positions clearly. Use arrows.

## Worked Example 1: Identifying Movement vs. Shift

The market for cinema tickets in a city has the following demand schedule at current consumer incomes:

| Price per ticket (£) | Quantity demanded (tickets per week) |
|---|---|
| 14 | 1,200 |
| 12 | 1,800 |
| 10 | 2,600 |
| 8 | 3,600 |
| 6 | 5,000 |

**Scenario A:** The cinema reduces its ticket price from £10 to £8. What happens to the quantity of tickets demanded? Is this a movement along the curve or a shift?

**Scenario B:** A new streaming service launches, offering new-release films at home for £5 per rental. At every ticket price, the quantity of cinema tickets demanded falls by 30%. What has happened to demand? Is this a movement or a shift?

**Approach:** For each scenario, identify whether the good's own price has changed or whether something else has changed. The own price triggers movement; any other determinant triggers a shift.

**Step 1, Scenario A:** The own price of cinema tickets falls from £10 to £8. This is a movement along the existing demand curve — specifically, an expansion of demand. At the original price of £10, quantity demanded was 2,600 tickets. At the new price of £8, quantity demanded is 3,600 tickets. The demand curve itself has not moved. Consumers are simply responding to the lower price by buying more tickets, which is exactly what the law of demand predicts.

**Step 2, Scenario B:** The price of cinema tickets has not changed. What has changed is the availability and price of a substitute — the streaming service. This is the R in TRIBE (Related goods prices). Because the streaming service is a substitute for cinema visits, its introduction reduces the demand for cinema tickets at every price. This is a shift of the demand curve to the left — a decrease in demand. At a ticket price of £10, consumers now demand 2,600 × 0.70 = 1,820 tickets instead of 2,600. At every other price, the same 30% reduction applies.

**Why this makes sense:** The distinction between movement and shift is not about whether quantity changes — in both scenarios, quantity changes. The distinction is about why it changes. In Scenario A, the cause is a change in the variable on the axis (price). In Scenario B, the cause is a change in a variable not on the axis (the price of a substitute). If you are unsure, ask: "Is the variable that changed labelled on one of the axes?" If yes, it is a movement. If no, it is a shift.

## Worked Example 2: Analysing the Effect of an Income Change

A consumer's monthly demand for restaurant meals is given by the schedule below. Her monthly income is £2,500.

| Price per meal (£) | Quantity demanded (meals per month) |
|---|---|
| 30 | 2 |
| 25 | 3 |
| 20 | 5 |
| 15 | 8 |
| 10 | 12 |

Her income then rises to £3,200 per month, and her demand schedule changes to the following:

| Price per meal (£) | Quantity demanded (meals per month) |
|---|---|
| 30 | 4 |
| 25 | 6 |
| 20 | 9 |
| 15 | 13 |
| 10 | 18 |

Determine whether restaurant meals are a normal good or an inferior good for this consumer. Calculate the percentage change in quantity demanded at a price of £20 per meal and explain what this tells you about the type of normal good (necessity or luxury) that restaurant meals might be.

**Approach:** If demand rises when income rises, the good is normal. To classify further, compare the percentage change in quantity demanded to the percentage change in income.

**Step 1:** Compare demand at a single price before and after the income change. At P = £20, quantity demanded rises from 5 meals to 9 meals. Since demand increased when income increased, restaurant meals are a normal good for this consumer.

**Step 2:** Calculate the percentage change in income. Income rose from £2,500 to £3,200, an increase of £700. Percentage change = (700 ÷ 2,500) × 100 = 28%.

**Step 3:** Calculate the percentage change in quantity demanded at P = £20. Quantity rose from 5 to 9 meals, an increase of 4 meals. Percentage change = (4 ÷ 5) × 100 = 80%.

**Step 4:** Compare the percentages. Quantity demanded rose by 80% while income rose by 28%. The percentage increase in demand is larger than the percentage increase in income, which suggests that restaurant meals act as a luxury good for this consumer. (For a necessity, demand would have risen by less than 28%.)

**Why this makes sense:** When a consumer receives a substantial income increase, she does not simply scale up all purchases proportionally. She reallocates spending toward goods she particularly values — in this case, restaurant meals — which is why the increase in meals (80%) far exceeds the income increase (28%). This is typical consumer behaviour: as people become richer, the composition of their spending shifts toward luxuries and away from basic necessities.

(d) Compare the effects on coffee demand at a price of £4 per cup of the income rise in part (b) and the tea price rise in part (c). Which change has the larger effect? Suggest one reason why a café owner might care more about one of these changes than the other. (3 marks)

## Answers

**Answer 1.** (a) This is a movement along the demand curve. The own price of cinema tickets falls from £12 to £9. As a result, there is an expansion of demand: quantity demanded increases as consumers move down and to the right along the existing demand curve. (b) This is a shift of the demand curve to the right. The determinant is T — Tastes and Preferences. The new health information makes cinema visits more desirable. At every ticket price, consumers now wish to visit the cinema more often. (c) This is a shift of the demand curve for cinema tickets — specifically, a shift to the right. The determinant is R — Related Goods Prices. Streaming services are substitutes for cinema visits. When the price of the substitute rises, some consumers switch from streaming to cinema, increasing the demand for cinema tickets at every price. (d) This is a shift of the demand curve to the left. The determinant is I — Income. Cinema visits are a normal good, meaning demand falls when income falls. During the recession, with household incomes 8% lower, consumers reduce their cinema visits at every ticket price. (e) This is a shift of the demand curve to the left. The determinant is B — Buyers (number of consumers). Market demand is the sum of all individual demands. When 20% of the core demographic leaves the city, there are fewer potential cinema-goers at every price, so the market demand curve shifts left.

**Answer 2.** (a) A twelve-year-old car with 140,000 miles is likely an inferior good. As consumer incomes rise, most people switch to newer, more reliable vehicles with lower mileage. The old car is purchased primarily because it is affordable, not because it is preferred. When a consumer can afford something better, she typically buys it. (b) Fresh organic vegetables are likely a normal good (and probably a luxury). As incomes rise, consumers devote more of their food budget to higher-quality, organic, and sustainably produced items. The shift from conventional to organic produce is a classic income-driven change in consumption patterns. (c) Ultra-processed frozen meals at £1.50 are likely an inferior good. These meals are typically bought by consumers with limited time and money. As incomes rise, consumers tend to replace them with fresh ingredients, meal-kit deliveries, or restaurant meals. The very low price point signals that these meals serve as a fallback option rather than a first choice. (d) Private tutoring is likely a normal good (and probably a luxury). As incomes rise, families invest more in their children's education beyond what the state provides. Private tutoring is one of the first additional educational expenditures families make when their disposable income increases. (e) A monthly bus pass is likely an inferior good for most consumers in a developed economy. As income rises, consumers switch from public transport to private cars, ride-sharing services, or taxis. Bus travel is often the affordable option that consumers abandon as soon as they can afford alternatives.

**Answer 3.** (a) Butter and margarine are substitutes. Both are spreads used on bread and in cooking, and most consumers choose one or the other for a given use. When the price of margarine rises by 25%, some margarine users switch to butter, so the demand for butter increases — the demand curve for butter shifts to the right. (b) Petrol and cars are complements. They are used together: a car without petrol does not function (for a conventional combustion-engine vehicle). When the price of cars rises by 25%, fewer people buy cars. Each new (or continuing) car owner needs petrol, so with fewer cars on the road, the demand for petrol decreases — the demand curve for petrol shifts to the left. (c) E-books and paperback books are substitutes. They satisfy the same underlying want — reading a book — through different formats. When the price of paperbacks rises by 25%, some readers switch to the relatively cheaper e-book versions, so the demand for e-books increases — the demand curve shifts to the right. (d) Coffee pods and the specific-brand coffee machine are complements. The pods are useless without the machine, and the machine is useless without the pods. When the price of the coffee machine rises by 25%, fewer machines are sold. Each machine owner would have been a continuing purchaser of pods, so with fewer machines in households, the demand for pods decreases — the demand curve shifts to the left.

**Answer 4.** (a) The subsidy has caused an increase in demand — a shift of the demand curve to the right. The subsidy is not a change in the own price of e-bikes as listed by sellers. It is a change in the effective price to consumers, which is a different thing. More precisely, the subsidy increases consumers' willingness and ability to buy at every listed price. At every original price point, the quantity demanded is higher than before. This is, by definition, a rightward shift of the demand curve. Some students mistakenly call this a movement along the curve because the price consumers pay has effectively fallen. The correct analysis is that the demand curve — the relationship between the listed price and quantity demanded — has shifted. At any given listed price, consumers demand more e-bikes. (b) The public-awareness campaign changes tastes and preferences — the T in TRIBE. The campaign makes e-bikes more desirable by providing information about their health and environmental advantages. The demand curve for e-bikes shifts to the right. At every price, consumers demand a larger quantity of e-bikes. This is distinct from the subsidy because the campaign does not change the effective price. It changes what consumers want.

**Answer 5.** (a) At P = £4: Qd = 800 − 25(4) = 800 − 100 = 700 cups per day. (1 mark) (b) At P = £4 with the new demand function: Qd = 950 − 25(4) = 950 − 100 = 850 cups per day. The original quantity at £4 was 700 cups. The increase from 700 to 850 cups represents a shift of the demand curve to the right — an increase in demand. The cause is a rise in consumer income (I in TRIBE), which is a non-price determinant. The price of coffee did not change. Consumers at every price now demand more coffee because they have more purchasing power. This is a shift, not a movement along the curve. (3 marks — 1 for the correct calculation, 1 for identifying it as a shift, 1 for the explanation referencing income as a non-price determinant.) ## Evaluate

**Question:** "The law of demand always holds true." Evaluate this statement, considering the theoretical exceptions of Veblen goods and Giffen goods.

**Model Answer:**

The law of demand — the inverse relationship between price and quantity demanded — is one of the most powerful and well-supported empirical regularities in economics. For virtually all goods, in virtually all markets, a higher price leads to a lower quantity demanded. But the word "always" is too strong. Both theory and evidence identify narrow but genuine exceptions.

The standard explanation for the law of demand rests on three mechanisms. The substitution effect pushes consumers away from a good when its price rises because alternatives become relatively cheaper. The income effect reduces consumers' real purchasing power when a price rises, leading them to buy less (for normal goods). Diminishing marginal utility means consumers value additional units less, so they are only willing to buy more units at lower prices. For the vast majority of goods — food, clothing, electronics, transport, entertainment — these three mechanisms work in the same direction, and the demand curve slopes downward.

**Veblen goods** violate the law of demand because the high price is itself the source of the good's desirability. Named after the economist Thorstein Veblen, who analysed "conspicuous consumption," a Veblen good is one where consumers derive utility from the fact that the good is expensive and therefore signals wealth, status, or exclusivity. A Rolex watch, an Hermès Birkin handbag, or a limited-edition supercar may be more desired precisely because they are priced beyond the reach of most people. If the price of a Birkin bag were halved, some status-seeking consumers might lose interest — the bag would no longer signal the same exclusivity. For Veblen goods, the demand curve can slope upward over certain price ranges. This does not mean Veblen goods are unaffected by price. It means the prestige effect can, in principle, dominate the standard substitution and income effects.

**Giffen goods** are a different and even rarer exception, operating through a perverse income effect. A Giffen good must be a strongly inferior good that accounts for a very large share of the consumer's budget, with no close substitutes. The classic historical example — debated but plausible — is potatoes during the Irish famine of the 1840s. When the price of potatoes rose, poor families experienced a severe reduction in real income because potatoes were their staple food. They could no longer afford meat or other superior foods. To survive, they had to buy even more potatoes — the inferior staple — despite the price increase. The income effect (so large and so perverse because the good was a huge share of the budget) overwhelmed the substitution effect. Quantity demanded rose as price rose. Economists have searched extensively for real-world Giffen behaviour. The evidence is thin — perhaps a handful of well-documented cases — precisely because the conditions required are so extreme.

These exceptions are real but narrow. Veblen effects operate only for luxury goods consumed for their status-signalling value. Giffen behaviour requires extreme poverty, a staple good with no substitutes, and a good that dominates the household budget. Neither exception describes the ordinary operation of markets for the vast majority of goods.

The statement "always holds true" therefore overstates the case. A more precise claim is that the law of demand is a near-universal empirical regularity with well-understood, narrowly circumscribed exceptions. The exceptions do not undermine the practical utility of the law of demand for understanding markets, setting prices, or designing policy. A tax on sugary drinks will reduce consumption. A fare increase on public transport will reduce ridership. A sale on clothing will increase purchases. These predictions hold reliably. The exceptions remind us that economic laws are tendencies, not iron necessities — a nuance that distinguishes careful economic thinking from dogmatic assertion.
