Lesson 23: Dynamic Equilibrium and Le Chatelier's Principle

What You'll Learn

  • Explain what a reversible reaction is and how it differs from a reaction that goes to completion
  • Define dynamic equilibrium and list the conditions required for a system to reach it
  • Apply Le Chatelier's Principle to predict how an equilibrium system responds to changes in concentration, pressure, and temperature
  • Explain why a catalyst has no effect on the position of equilibrium
  • Analyse the choice of industrial conditions for the Haber process in terms of both equilibrium yield and reaction rate

1. Reversible Reactions and Dynamic Equilibrium

What This Concept Is and Why It Matters

Some chemical reactions go only one way. When you burn methane (natural gas) in oxygen, you get carbon dioxide and water, and you cannot un-burn those products back into methane. These are called irreversible reactions.

But many reactions can go in both directions. Under the right conditions, the products can react with each other to re-form the original reactants. These are called reversible reactions. We use a special double arrow to show this: the symbol ⇌ means that both the forward reaction (left to right) and the reverse reaction (right to left) are happening.

For example, when nitrogen gas and hydrogen gas are mixed in a sealed container at high temperature:

\[\text{N}_2(\text{g}) + 3\text{H}_2(\text{g}) \rightleftharpoons 2\text{NH}_3(\text{g})\]

As soon as some ammonia (NH₃) is formed, some of those ammonia molecules start breaking apart to re-form N₂ and H₂. Both reactions — the formation of ammonia and its decomposition — are running simultaneously in the same container.

What Is Dynamic Equilibrium?

Imagine a sealed box with no way for matter to enter or leave — this is called a closed system. Inside this box, a reversible reaction is taking place. At the very beginning, only reactants are present, so the forward reaction runs at its maximum rate and the reverse reaction does not happen at all (there are no products yet). As products build up, the forward rate gradually slows down (because reactant concentrations are dropping) and the reverse rate gradually speeds up (because product concentrations are rising).

Eventually, the system reaches a state where the forward rate and the reverse rate become exactly equal. This state is called dynamic equilibrium.

The word "dynamic" is crucial. At equilibrium, the reactions have not stopped. Both forward and reverse reactions continue to occur at the molecular level. But because they are happening at exactly the same speed, there is no net change. For every molecule of NH₃ that forms, another molecule of NH₃ breaks apart somewhere else in the container. The result is that the amounts of all substances — N₂, H₂, and NH₃ — stay constant over time.

What Equilibrium Is NOT

A very common misconception is that at equilibrium, the concentrations of reactants and products must be equal. This is not true. The rates are equal, but the concentrations can be — and usually are — very different. In the Haber process at 450°C, at equilibrium you might have much more N₂ and H₂ than NH₃, or much more NH₃ than N₂ and H₂, depending on the conditions. What stays constant is each individual concentration, not their equality.

Another misconception is that equilibrium means the reaction has stopped. It has not stopped. If you could zoom in to the molecular level, you would see molecules constantly reacting in both directions. It is only at the macroscopic level — what you can measure with instruments — that nothing appears to be changing.

Requirements for Equilibrium

For a system to establish and maintain dynamic equilibrium, three conditions must be met:

  1. The system must be closed — no substances can enter or leave.
  2. The temperature must be constant — changing the temperature changes the equilibrium position.
  3. The reaction must be reversible — both forward and reverse reactions must be possible under the conditions.

2. Le Chatelier's Principle

What This Concept Is and Why It Matters

Le Chatelier's Principle is a powerful tool for predicting what will happen when you disturb a system that is at equilibrium. It was formulated by the French chemist Henri Louis Le Chatelier in 1884. The principle states:

When a system at equilibrium is subjected to a change in concentration, pressure, or temperature, the position of equilibrium shifts in the direction that tends to oppose (counteract) the change.

A simple way to think about it: the system does the opposite of what you do to it. If you push the system in one direction, it pushes back. If you add something, the system tries to remove it. If you remove something, the system tries to make more of it.

Effect of Changing Concentration

Suppose you have a system at equilibrium, and you add more of one of the reactants. According to Le Chatelier's Principle, the system will try to "use up" this extra reactant. It does this by shifting the position of equilibrium toward the products — the forward reaction speeds up relative to the reverse reaction, converting some of the added reactant (along with the other reactants) into products.

Conversely, if you remove a product from the equilibrium mixture (for example, by letting a gas escape or by precipitating it out of solution), the system tries to replace what was removed. It shifts toward the products to make more of that substance.

Here is a systematic summary:

  • Adding a reactant: The equilibrium shifts to the right (toward products) to consume the added reactant.
  • Removing a reactant: The equilibrium shifts to the left (toward reactants) to produce more of the removed reactant.
  • Adding a product: The equilibrium shifts to the left (toward reactants) to consume the added product.
  • Removing a product: The equilibrium shifts to the right (toward products) to replace the removed product.

A concrete example: the reaction between iron(III) ions and thiocyanate ions forms a blood-red complex ion:

\[\text{Fe}^{3+}(\text{aq}) + \text{SCN}^-(\text{aq}) \rightleftharpoons \text{FeSCN}^{2+}(\text{aq})\]

If you add more Fe³⁺ (a reactant) to a pale red solution at equilibrium, the solution becomes darker red. The added Fe³⁺ shifts the equilibrium to the right, producing more of the red FeSCN²⁺ complex. If you then add some SCN⁻, the colour deepens further. If you remove FeSCN²⁺ by some chemical means, the equilibrium shifts right to replace it.

3. Effect of Pressure (For Reactions Involving Gases)

What This Concept Is and Why It Matters

Pressure changes only affect equilibria that involve gases, and even then, only when the number of gas molecules on the left side of the equation is different from the number on the right side.

When you increase the pressure on a gaseous system (for example, by compressing the container to a smaller volume), the system responds by trying to reduce the pressure. It does this by shifting the equilibrium toward whichever side has fewer gas molecules. Fewer molecules in the same volume means lower pressure.

When you decrease the pressure (by expanding the container), the system tries to increase the pressure by shifting toward the side with more gas molecules.

Example: The Haber process reaction:

\[\text{N}_2(\text{g}) + 3\text{H}_2(\text{g}) \rightleftharpoons 2\text{NH}_3(\text{g})\]

Count the gas molecules on each side: on the left, there is 1 molecule of N₂ plus 3 molecules of H₂, for a total of 4 gas molecules. On the right, there are 2 molecules of NH₃. Increasing the pressure favours the side with fewer molecules — the right side. The equilibrium shifts right, producing more ammonia. This is why the Haber process is run at high pressure (around 200 atmospheres).

Compare this with a reaction where the number of gas molecules is the same on both sides:

\[\text{H}_2(\text{g}) + \text{I}_2(\text{g}) \rightleftharpoons 2\text{HI}(\text{g})\]

Left side: 2 gas molecules. Right side: 2 gas molecules. Changing the pressure has no effect on the position of equilibrium for this reaction, because neither direction reduces the total number of gas molecules.

Adding an Inert Gas

A note about a common exam scenario: if you add an inert gas (like argon or helium) to a container at constant volume, the total pressure increases, but the partial pressures of the reacting gases do not change — because the volume has not changed and no extra reacting molecules have been added. The equilibrium position does not shift. If you add an inert gas at constant total pressure (which means you must expand the volume), then the partial pressures of reactants decrease, and the equilibrium shifts toward the side with more molecules. The key is always whether the partial pressures of the reacting gases change.

4. Effect of Temperature

What This Concept Is and Why It Matters

Temperature is the only factor we have discussed so far that actually changes the value of the equilibrium constant K. Changing concentration or pressure shifts the position of equilibrium but does not change K. Changing temperature changes both the position of equilibrium AND the value of K.

To apply Le Chatelier's Principle to temperature changes, it helps to think of heat as if it were a chemical substance — either a reactant or a product, depending on whether the forward reaction is endothermic or exothermic.

If the forward reaction is exothermic (ΔH is negative): Heat is released when products form. You can think of heat as a product:

\[\text{reactants} \rightleftharpoons \text{products} + \text{heat}\]

  • If you increase the temperature (add heat), the system tries to remove heat by shifting left (toward reactants). The equilibrium yield of products decreases.
  • If you decrease the temperature (remove heat), the system tries to produce heat by shifting right (toward products). The equilibrium yield of products increases.

If the forward reaction is endothermic (ΔH is positive): Heat is absorbed when products form. You can think of heat as a reactant:

\[\text{reactants} + \text{heat} \rightleftharpoons \text{products}\]

  • If you increase the temperature (add heat), the system shifts right to absorb the added heat. Product yield increases.
  • If you decrease the temperature (remove heat), the system shifts left to produce heat. Product yield decreases.

Worked Example 1: Predicting Temperature Effects

Problem: The dimerisation of nitrogen dioxide is an equilibrium reaction:

\[2\text{NO}_2(\text{g}) \rightleftharpoons \text{N}_2\text{O}_4(\text{g}) \quad \Delta H = -58 \text{ kJ mol}^{-1}\]

NO₂ gas is brown. N₂O₄ gas is colourless. Predict and explain what happens to the colour of an equilibrium mixture of these gases when (a) the temperature is increased, and (b) the temperature is decreased.

Strategy: First, identify whether the forward reaction is exothermic or endothermic using the sign of ΔH. Then apply Le Chatelier's Principle. Finally, connect the shift direction to the colour observation.

Solution, part (a): ΔH = −58 kJ mol⁻¹, so the forward reaction (NO₂ → N₂O₄) is exothermic — it releases heat. Thinking of heat as a product: 2NO₂ ⇌ N₂O₄ + heat. Increasing the temperature adds heat to the system. The system opposes this by shifting in the direction that removes heat — which is the reverse (endothermic) direction, to the left. The equilibrium shifts left, converting N₂O₄ back into NO₂. Since NO₂ is brown, the mixture becomes darker brown.

Solution, part (b): Decreasing the temperature removes heat from the system. The system opposes this by shifting in the direction that produces heat — which is the forward (exothermic) direction, to the right. More N₂O₄ forms from NO₂. Since N₂O₄ is colourless, the brown colour fades and the mixture becomes paler.

Why this makes sense: This is a classic demonstration experiment. A sealed tube of NO₂/N₂O₄ turns darker when placed in hot water and paler when placed in ice water. The colour change is a direct visual indicator of the equilibrium shift.

5. Effect of a Catalyst

What This Concept Is and Why It Matters

A catalyst is a substance that speeds up a chemical reaction without being consumed. It works by providing an alternative pathway with a lower activation energy.

In the context of equilibrium, a catalyst has two important effects and one crucial non-effect:

  1. A catalyst lowers the activation energy for both the forward and reverse reactions — and by exactly the same amount.
  1. Because both directions are accelerated equally, a catalyst increases the speed at which equilibrium is reached. A system that might take hours to reach equilibrium without a catalyst might reach it in minutes with one.
  1. A catalyst has NO effect on the position of equilibrium. It does not change the equilibrium concentrations. It does not change the value of Kc or Kp. It does not increase the yield of products (at equilibrium). It simply gets you to equilibrium faster.

This is a common exam trap: students often think a catalyst increases yield. It does not. In an industrial process, a catalyst is valuable because it allows you to reach equilibrium quickly without needing excessively high temperatures (which might lower yield), but the equilibrium position itself is unaffected by the catalyst.

6. Industrial Case Study: The Haber Process

What This Concept Is and Why It Matters

The Haber process is the industrial method for producing ammonia (NH₃) from nitrogen and hydrogen. Ammonia is the starting material for making fertilisers, which are essential for feeding the world's population. The process is a perfect case study for applying everything we have learned about equilibrium, rates, and the trade-offs involved in industrial chemistry.

The reaction is:

\[\text{N}_2(\text{g}) + 3\text{H}_2(\text{g}) \rightleftharpoons 2\text{NH}_3(\text{g}) \quad \Delta H = -92 \text{ kJ mol}^{-1}\]

Let us examine each condition and why it was chosen:

Pressure: 200 atmospheres. According to Le Chatelier's Principle, high pressure favours the side with fewer gas molecules. Here, 4 gas molecules on the left versus 2 on the right. High pressure shifts equilibrium to the right, increasing the yield of ammonia. So why not use even higher pressure — 500 or 1000 atmospheres? Because building and operating equipment that can withstand extreme pressures is very expensive and dangerous. Beyond about 200 atmospheres, the extra yield does not justify the extra cost. This is an economic compromise.

Temperature: 450°C. The forward reaction is exothermic, so according to Le Chatelier's Principle, a low temperature would give a higher equilibrium yield of ammonia. So why not run the reaction at room temperature? Because at low temperatures, the rate is far too slow to be practical — it would take years to reach equilibrium. Raising the temperature increases the rate dramatically. At 450°C, the rate is fast enough for economical production, even though the equilibrium yield is lower than it would be at a cooler temperature. This is a kinetic-thermodynamic compromise between yield (thermodynamics, favoured by low T) and rate (kinetics, favoured by high T).

Catalyst: Finely divided iron. The iron catalyst lowers the activation energy, allowing the reaction to reach equilibrium much faster at 450°C. Without the catalyst, the reaction would still be too slow even at 450°C. The catalyst does not change the equilibrium yield — that is determined by the temperature and pressure — but it makes the process viable by speeding things up.

Removal of ammonia: As ammonia forms, it is continuously cooled, liquefied, and removed from the reaction mixture. This removal of a product shifts the equilibrium to the right (Le Chatelier), producing even more ammonia. Unreacted N₂ and H₂ are recycled back into the reactor.

Practice Problems

  1. A sealed container holds an equilibrium mixture of hydrogen gas, iodine gas, and hydrogen iodide gas according to the equation H₂(g) + I₂(g) ⇌ 2HI(g). The container is at a constant temperature. State three measurable characteristics of this system that indicate it is at dynamic equilibrium, and explain why each characteristic is a sign that equilibrium has been reached.
  1. The reaction CO(g) + 2H₂(g) ⇌ CH₃OH(g) has an enthalpy change ΔH = −91 kJ mol⁻¹. For each of the following changes, predict the direction in which the equilibrium will shift and explain your reasoning using Le Chatelier's Principle:

(a) The pressure is increased by compressing the gas mixture into a smaller volume.

(b) The temperature is increased.

(c) Methanol (CH₃OH) is continuously condensed and removed from the reaction vessel.

  1. Phosphorus pentachloride decomposes on heating according to the equation PCl₅(g) ⇌ PCl₃(g) + Cl₂(g). The enthalpy change for this decomposition is ΔH = +87.9 kJ mol⁻¹. Predict and explain the effect of each of the following changes on the equilibrium yield of PCl₃: (a) The temperature is increased. (b) The total pressure is increased by compressing the mixture.
  1. The Contact Process for manufacturing sulfuric acid involves the equilibrium 2SO₂(g) + O₂(g) ⇌ 2SO₃(g) with ΔH = −197 kJ mol⁻¹. A vanadium(V) oxide (V₂O₅) catalyst is used. For each of the following changes, state whether the equilibrium yield of SO₃ will increase, decrease, or remain unchanged, and briefly explain your answer: (a) adding more oxygen gas, (b) removing SO₃ as it forms, (c) increasing the temperature, (d) adding more V₂O₅ catalyst, (e) decreasing the volume of the container.
  1. (IB-Exam Style) The Haber process for the synthesis of ammonia is represented by the following equilibrium:

\[\text{N}_2(\text{g}) + 3\text{H}_2(\text{g}) \rightleftharpoons 2\text{NH}_3(\text{g}) \quad \Delta H = -92 \text{ kJ mol}^{-1}\]

The typical operating conditions are a temperature of about 450°C, a pressure of about 200 atmospheres, and the use of a finely divided iron catalyst.

(a) Explain, using Le Chatelier's Principle, why a high pressure is used. State one practical disadvantage of using pressures higher than 200 atmospheres.

(b) Explain why a temperature of 450°C is chosen even though a lower temperature would give a higher equilibrium yield of ammonia. Your answer should refer to both thermodynamics (equilibrium yield) and kinetics (reaction rate).

(c) A student claims that the iron catalyst increases the yield of ammonia at equilibrium because it lowers the activation energy of the forward reaction more than it lowers the activation energy of the reverse reaction. Identify the error in this statement and provide a correct explanation of the role of the catalyst.

(d) In the industrial process, ammonia is continuously removed from the reaction mixture by cooling and liquefaction, and unreacted nitrogen and hydrogen are recycled. Explain, using Le Chatelier's Principle, the purpose of continuously removing the ammonia product.

Answers

Answer 1

Three measurable characteristics of a system at dynamic equilibrium are as follows. First, the concentrations of all reactants and products remain constant over time. If you measure [H₂], [I₂], and [HI] at intervals of several minutes and the values do not change, this indicates that the forward and reverse reactions are proceeding at equal rates — for every HI molecule that forms, another HI molecule decomposes, so the concentrations are steady. Second, macroscopic properties such as colour remain constant. I₂ gas is purple, while H₂ and HI are colourless. If the purple colour intensity stays the same over time, the concentration of I₂ is not changing, which means the system is at equilibrium. Third, the system must be closed (sealed container) and at constant temperature, because if matter could escape or temperature could change, the equilibrium would shift and concentrations would not be stable.

Answer 2

(a) Increasing the pressure by compressing the mixture means the same number of particles now occupy a smaller volume, so the total pressure rises. The system opposes this by shifting toward the side with fewer gas molecules. On the left side of CO + 2H₂ ⇌ CH₃OH, there are 1 + 2 = 3 gas molecules. On the right side, there is 1 gas molecule. The equilibrium shifts to the right (toward CH₃OH), which reduces the total number of gas molecules in the container and partially relieves the pressure increase.

(b) The forward reaction is exothermic (ΔH = −91 kJ mol⁻¹), meaning heat is released when CH₃OH forms. Thinking of heat as a product, increasing the temperature adds heat to the system. The system opposes this by shifting in the direction that consumes heat, which is the reverse (endothermic) direction. The equilibrium shifts to the left, decreasing the yield of CH₃OH.

(c) Methanol is a product. Removing it from the reaction vessel decreases the concentration of CH₃OH in the equilibrium mixture. The system opposes this removal by shifting in the direction that produces more CH₃OH, which is the forward direction — to the right. This is one reason the industrial process is designed to continuously remove the product.

Answer 3

(a) ΔH = +87.9 kJ mol⁻¹ means the forward reaction (PCl₅ decomposing to PCl₃ and Cl₂) is endothermic — it absorbs heat. Thinking of heat as a reactant: PCl₅ + heat ⇌ PCl₃ + Cl₂. Increasing the temperature adds heat, and the system shifts in the direction that absorbs heat — the forward (endothermic) direction, to the right. The equilibrium yield of PCl₃ increases.

(b) The left side has 1 gas molecule (PCl₅). The right side has 1 + 1 = 2 gas molecules (PCl₃ and Cl₂). Increasing the pressure favours the side with fewer gas molecules, which is the left side (reactants). The equilibrium shifts left, decreasing the yield of PCl₃.

Answer 4

(a) Adding more O₂ (a reactant) shifts the equilibrium to the right to consume the added oxygen. The yield of SO₃ increases.

(b) Removing SO₃ (a product) shifts the equilibrium to the right to replace the removed SO₃. The yield of SO₃ increases.

(c) The forward reaction is exothermic (ΔH = −197 kJ mol⁻¹). Increasing temperature favours the reverse (endothermic) direction, shifting equilibrium left. The yield of SO₃ decreases.

(d) A catalyst speeds up both forward and reverse reactions equally. It has no effect on the position of equilibrium. The yield of SO₃ remains unchanged.

(e) Decreasing the volume increases the pressure. The left side has 2 + 1 = 3 gas molecules; the right side has 2 gas molecules. The equilibrium shifts toward the side with fewer molecules, which is the right. The yield of SO₃ increases.

Answer 5

(a) According to Le Chatelier's Principle, increasing the pressure causes the equilibrium to shift in the direction that reduces pressure — toward the side with fewer gas molecules. In this reaction, the left side has 4 gas molecules (1 N₂ + 3 H₂) and the right side has 2 gas molecules (2 NH₃). High pressure therefore shifts the equilibrium to the right, favouring ammonia production. A practical disadvantage of pressures significantly higher than 200 atmospheres is the cost: the reaction vessel must be built with extremely thick, strong walls, which is expensive. There is also an increased safety risk — high-pressure equipment can fail catastrophically.

(b) The forward reaction is exothermic (ΔH = −92 kJ mol⁻¹), so from a thermodynamic perspective, a low temperature would give a higher equilibrium yield of ammonia. However, at low temperatures, the reaction rate is very slow — it would take an impractically long time to reach equilibrium. At 450°C, the rate is fast enough for economic production even though the equilibrium yield is lower than it would be at, say, 200°C. The choice of 450°C represents a compromise between the opposing demands of thermodynamics (which favours low temperature for high yield) and kinetics (which favours high temperature for fast rate).

(c) The student's statement contains a fundamental error. A catalyst lowers the activation energy for both the forward and the reverse reaction by exactly the same amount. It provides an alternative reaction pathway with a lower energy barrier, but this barrier is lowered symmetrically — the transition state is lower for both directions. Because both directions are accelerated equally, the catalyst does not favour one direction over the other, and therefore it does not change the position of equilibrium or the equilibrium yield. The catalyst's role is purely to increase the rate at which equilibrium is attained, not to change where that equilibrium lies.

(d) Ammonia is a product of the reaction. By continuously removing it from the reaction mixture, the concentration of ammonia in the reactor is kept low. According to Le Chatelier's Principle, when a product is removed, the system responds by shifting the equilibrium position to the right — toward products — in order to replace what was taken away. This continuous removal therefore drives the reaction to produce more ammonia than would be present at a single-pass equilibrium. Combined with recycling of unreacted N₂ and H₂, this allows nearly complete conversion of the feed gases over multiple cycles.
