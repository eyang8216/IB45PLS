Lesson 23: Dynamic Equilibrium & Le Chatelier's Principle

Syllabus reference: 7.1 — Equilibrium

Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to:

  • Define dynamic equilibrium and explain why it requires a closed system.
  • State Le Chatelier's principle and apply it to predict the direction of shift when concentration, pressure, or temperature is changed.
  • Explain why a catalyst does not affect the position of equilibrium.
  • Analyse the Haber and Contact processes as industrial applications of equilibrium principles.
  • Sketch concentration–time graphs showing how a system responds to perturbations.

1. Prior Knowledge Check

Question 1: What is the difference between a reversible and an irreversible reaction? Give an example of each.

▸ Show
An irreversible reaction goes essentially to completion — the reverse reaction is negligible (e.g., $\ce{2Mg + O2 -> 2MgO}$, combustion). A reversible reaction can proceed in both directions under the same conditions (e.g., $\ce{N2 + 3H2 <=> 2NH3}$). Reversible reactions are denoted by the $\ce{<=>}$ arrow.

Question 2: For a reaction at equilibrium, is the reaction still occurring? Explain.

▸ Show
Yes. At equilibrium, the forward and reverse reactions continue at exactly equal rates. The concentrations of all species remain constant because they are being produced and consumed at the same rate. This is why equilibrium is called dynamic — it is active, not static. A common analogy: a busy shop where customers enter and leave at the same rate; the number inside is constant, but movement continues.

2. Dynamic Equilibrium — The Core Concept

A chemical system is at dynamic equilibrium when:

  1. The forward and reverse reactions occur at equal rates.
  2. The concentrations of all reactants and products remain constant (not necessarily equal).
  3. The system must be closed — no matter can enter or leave.
  4. Equilibrium can be approached from either direction.
Figure 1: Approach to equilibrium. Initially, forward rate is high (lots of reactant) and reverse rate is zero. As reactants are consumed, forward rate decreases and reverse rate increases until they become equal. At this point, concentrations stop changing — dynamic equilibrium is established.

Check-in: Why must the system be closed for equilibrium to be established?

▸ Show
In an open system, products (especially gases) can escape. This means the reverse reaction cannot occur at a rate sufficient to balance the forward reaction — products are continuously lost. The reaction goes to completion instead. For example, $\ce{CaCO3(s) <=> CaO(s) + CO2(g)}$: in an open container, $\ce{CO2}$ escapes and equilibrium is never reached; in a sealed container, equilibrium is established.

3. Le Chatelier's Principle

Le Chatelier's Principle: If a system at dynamic equilibrium is subjected to a change (in concentration, pressure, or temperature), the position of equilibrium shifts to oppose (partially counteract) that change.

3.1 Effect of Concentration

Adding a reactant shifts equilibrium to the right (towards products) to consume the added species. Removing a product also shifts equilibrium right. Adding a product shifts equilibrium left. The value of the equilibrium constant $K_c$ does not change — only the position shifts.

3.2 Effect of Pressure (Gaseous Systems Only)

Increasing pressure (by decreasing volume) shifts equilibrium towards the side with fewer moles of gas. If the number of gas moles is the same on both sides, pressure has no effect. This is because the system "wants" to reduce the total number of particles to lower the pressure.

Figure 2: Effect of pressure increase on the Haber process. High pressure shifts equilibrium towards fewer gas molecules (right, towards NH₃), increasing yield.

3.3 Effect of Temperature

This is the only perturbation that changes $K_c$. Treat heat as a reactant (endothermic) or product (exothermic):

  • Exothermic ($\Delta H < 0$): heat is a product. Increasing temperature shifts equilibrium left; $K_c$ decreases.
  • Endothermic ($\Delta H > 0$): heat is a reactant. Increasing temperature shifts equilibrium right; $K_c$ increases.

3.4 Effect of a Catalyst

A catalyst does NOT shift equilibrium. It lowers $E_a$ equally for the forward and reverse reactions, so both rates increase by the same factor. The system reaches equilibrium faster, but the equilibrium composition is unchanged. $K_c$ is unaffected.

Check-in: For $\ce{2SO2(g) + O2(g) <=> 2SO3(g)}$, $\Delta H = -197$ kJ mol⁻¹. Predict the effect of: (a) increasing pressure, (b) raising temperature, (c) adding a $\ce{V2O5}$ catalyst.

▸ Show
(a) 3 moles gas → 2 moles gas. Increasing pressure shifts equilibrium right, increasing $\ce{SO3}$ yield. (b) Exothermic — heat is a product. Raising temperature shifts equilibrium left, decreasing $\ce{SO3}$ yield and lowering $K_c$. (c) Catalyst increases the rate of both forward and reverse reactions equally — reaches equilibrium faster but no change in position or $K_c$.

4. Industrial Applications

4.1 The Haber Process: $\ce{N2(g) + 3H2(g) <=> 2NH3(g)}$, $\Delta H = -92$ kJ mol⁻¹

Pressure~200 atmHigh pressure favours NH₃ (fewer moles), but very high pressure is expensive and dangerous

4.2 The Contact Process: $\ce{2SO2(g) + O2(g) <=> 2SO3(g)}$, $\Delta H = -197$ kJ mol⁻¹

ConditionCompromise UsedReason
Pressure~2 atm (low)3→2 moles, but equilibrium already lies far right at low P — high P not needed economically

Check-in: In the Haber process, why not use a temperature of 200°C for a higher yield?

▸ Show
At 200°C, the equilibrium yield of NH₃ would indeed be higher (exothermic reaction favours lower T). However, the rate would be unacceptably slow — even with a catalyst, the reaction would take far too long to reach equilibrium. Industrial processes prioritise rate (productivity per unit time) over maximum theoretical yield. 450°C is the economical compromise: acceptable yield at an acceptable rate.

5. Worked Examples

Example 1 — Predicting Shifts:

$\ce{2NO2(g) <=> N2O4(g)}$, $\Delta H = -58$ kJ mol⁻¹. (Brown) ⇌ (Colourless). Predict the colour change when: (a) pressure is increased, (b) temperature is decreased, (c) a catalyst is added.

Solution: (a) 2 moles → 1 mole. Increasing P shifts right → more colourless $\ce{N2O4}$ → mixture becomes paler. (b) Exothermic. Decreasing T favours the exothermic direction (right) → more $\ce{N2O4}$ → mixture becomes paler. (c) Catalyst does not shift equilibrium → no colour change, but equilibrium is reached faster.

⚠️ Common wrong approach: Saying "the mixture turns colourless." At equilibrium, both species are present, so the mixture is never fully colourless — it becomes lighter brown (paler), not colourless. Equilibrium mixtures almost always contain all species.
Example 2 — Concentration–Time Graph:

For $\ce{A + B <=> C + D}$, the system is at equilibrium. At $t_1$, more A is added. At $t_2$, some C is removed. Sketch the concentration–time graph for all four species.

Solution: At $t_1$: [A] jumps up (added). Equilibrium shifts right: [A] and [B] decrease (B is consumed), [C] and [D] increase. New equilibrium established. At $t_2$: [C] jumps down (removed). Equilibrium shifts right: [A] and [B] decrease further, [C] and [D] increase. All concentrations eventually level off at new constant values. The key graph feature: after each perturbation, concentrations smoothly approach new plateau values — they do not return to original values (except in very specific cases).
Example 3 — Temperature and $K_c$:

For $\ce{CO(g) + 2H2(g) <=> CH3OH(g)}$, $K_c = 14.5$ at 500 K and $K_c = 1.6 \times 10^{-2}$ at 700 K. Is the forward reaction exothermic or endothermic? Explain.

Solution: As temperature increases from 500 K to 700 K, $K_c$ decreases dramatically (from 14.5 to 0.016). By Le Chatelier's principle, increasing temperature shifts equilibrium in the endothermic direction. Since $K_c$ decreases (products are less favoured), the equilibrium must be shifting left (towards reactants). Therefore, the reverse reaction is endothermic, meaning the forward reaction is exothermic.

⚠️ Common wrong approach: "Higher temperature favours products because molecules have more energy." This is wrong. Temperature effects depend on whether the reaction is exothermic or endothermic. For exothermic reactions, higher T decreases product yield. Always check the sign of $\Delta H$ or the trend in $K_c$ with temperature.

⚠️ Common Mistakes to Avoid

  1. "Equilibrium means equal concentrations of reactants and products." No. Equilibrium means constant concentrations — they are rarely equal. The ratio is determined by $K_c$, which can be huge (products favoured) or tiny (reactants favoured).
  2. "A catalyst increases the yield of products." A catalyst affects only the rate at which equilibrium is reached, not the position. It lowers $E_a$ equally for forward and reverse reactions, so both rates increase by the same factor and the equilibrium composition is unchanged.
  3. "Le Chatelier's principle says the system completely reverses the change." The key word is "partially" — the system opposes the change but never fully cancels it. Adding a reactant shifts equilibrium right, but some of the added reactant remains.
  4. "Pressure changes always shift equilibrium." Only if the number of moles of gas differs between the two sides. For $\ce{H2(g) + I2(g) <=> 2HI(g)}$, both sides have 2 moles — pressure changes have no effect on the position of equilibrium.
  5. "Adding an inert gas at constant volume shifts equilibrium." No. Adding an inert gas (e.g., Ar) at constant volume increases total pressure but does not change the partial pressures of the reacting gases. Therefore, equilibrium is unaffected.

6. End-of-Lesson Practice

Question 1 (Paper 1 style): $\ce{2X(g) + Y(g) <=> 2Z(g)}$, $\Delta H < 0$. Which change increases the equilibrium yield of Z?
A. Increasing temperature   B. Decreasing pressure   C. Adding a catalyst   D. Increasing pressure

Question 2: For $\ce{PCl5(g) <=> PCl3(g) + Cl2(g)}$, $\Delta H = +87$ kJ mol⁻¹, predict the effect on the equilibrium position and on $K_c$ of: (a) increasing temperature, (b) increasing pressure, (c) adding more $\ce{Cl2}$.

Question 3: The Haber process uses 450°C despite the forward reaction being exothermic. Justify this choice in terms of both equilibrium and kinetics.

Question 4: For $\ce{N2O4(g) <=> 2NO2(g)}$, a sealed syringe containing the equilibrium mixture is compressed. Predict and explain the colour change observed immediately and after re-establishment of equilibrium.

Question 5: $\ce{2HI(g) <=> H2(g) + I2(g)}$, $\Delta H = +53$ kJ mol⁻¹. The system is at equilibrium. State the effect (increase/decrease/no change) on [HI] of: (a) adding $\ce{H2}$, (b) increasing pressure, (c) raising temperature.

Question 6 (IB extended): For $\ce{CO(g) + H2O(g) <=> CO2(g) + H2(g)}$, $\Delta H = -41$ kJ mol⁻¹, explain: (a) why pressure changes have no effect, (b) the effect of cooling the mixture, (c) why a catalyst is used industrially despite having no effect on yield.

Answer 1: D. 3 moles → 2 moles. Increasing pressure shifts equilibrium right (towards fewer moles), increasing Z yield. A: exothermic — increasing T shifts left. B: decreasing P shifts left. C: catalyst does not affect position.

Answer 2: (a) Endothermic: increasing T shifts right, $K_c$ increases. (b) 1 mole → 2 moles: increasing P shifts left (towards fewer moles). $K_c$ unchanged. (c) Adding $\ce{Cl2}$ shifts equilibrium left (opposes the addition). $K_c$ unchanged.

Answer 3: Lower temperature would give a higher equilibrium yield (exothermic), but the rate would be too slow for economic viability. 450°C is a compromise: the yield is lower than at, say, 200°C, but the rate is fast enough for continuous industrial production. The iron catalyst further increases rate without affecting yield. Continuous removal of NH₃ also drives equilibrium right.

Answer 4: Immediately on compression, [NO₂] increases (same amount in smaller volume) → mixture appears darker brown. Then equilibrium shifts left (2 moles → 1 mole, towards fewer gas molecules), forming more N₂O₄ (colourless). After re-equilibration, the mixture is paler than immediately after compression, but still darker (or about as dark) as the original because the volume is smaller.

Answer 5: (a) Adding H₂ shifts equilibrium left → [HI] increases. (b) 2 moles ⇌ 2 moles → no change in position or [HI]. (c) Endothermic: raising T shifts equilibrium right → [HI] decreases.

Answer 6: (a) Both sides have 2 moles of gas (1+1 ⇌ 1+1) — pressure changes have no effect because the system cannot reduce the number of gas molecules by shifting either way. (b) Exothermic: cooling (decreasing T) shifts equilibrium right, increasing CO₂ and H₂ yield; $K_c$ increases. (c) The catalyst ($\ce{Fe3O4/Cr2O3}$) dramatically increases the rate, allowing equilibrium to be reached much faster. This reduces reactor size and energy costs. Although yield is unchanged, the rate of production is what matters economically.

7. Key Terms

TermDefinition
Dynamic equilibriumState in a closed system where forward and reverse reaction rates are equal and concentrations remain constant
Le Chatelier's principleA system at equilibrium, when disturbed, shifts to partially counteract the disturbance
Position of equilibriumThe relative amounts of reactants and products at equilibrium; described as "left" (reactant-favoured) or "right" (product-favoured)
Closed systemA system where no matter can enter or leave; energy may be exchanged with surroundings
Haber processIndustrial synthesis of ammonia: $\ce{N2 + 3H2 <=> 2NH3}$, using Fe catalyst at ~450°C, ~200 atm
Contact processIndustrial synthesis of sulfuric acid via $\ce{2SO2 + O2 <=> 2SO3}$, using $\ce{V2O5}$ catalyst at ~450°C
Compromise conditionsIndustrial conditions that balance equilibrium yield, reaction rate, and economic cost

8. Exam Tips

Paper 1 (Multiple Choice): When a question asks about the effect of a change on "the equilibrium yield" or "the amount of product," check whether $\Delta H$ is given. If it is exothermic, increasing T decreases yield. Students often pick the opposite — assuming heat always helps. Remember: treat heat as a chemical species (product for exothermic, reactant for endothermic).
Paper 2 (Structured): Industrial equilibrium questions (Haber/Contact) typically have 3–4 marks. You must mention (1) the effect on equilibrium position, (2) the effect on rate, (3) why a compromise is chosen. Simply saying "high pressure gives more product" is insufficient — you must state why (fewer moles on product side) and why it is not used even higher (cost, safety).
Graph Sketching: When asked to sketch concentration–time graphs after a perturbation: concentrations jump instantly when a substance is added/removed (vertical line), then change smoothly as equilibrium re-establishes (curved approach to new plateau). Temperature and pressure changes do not cause instantaneous jumps — only smooth transitions.
