Lesson 9: Ionic & Covalent Bonding — Lewis Structures, Formal Charge

Syllabus reference: 4.1/4.2/14.1 — Ionic and covalent bonding, Lewis structures, formal charge, resonance

Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to:

  • Describe ionic bonding as the electrostatic attraction between oppositely charged ions formed by electron transfer.
  • Draw Lewis (electron dot) structures for molecules and polyatomic ions, including those with multiple bonds.
  • Calculate and interpret formal charge to determine the most stable Lewis structure.
  • Identify resonance structures and explain why the true structure is a hybrid.
  • Apply the octet rule and recognise when expansion of the octet is possible (HL).

1. Prior Knowledge Check

Question 1: What is the difference between an ionic bond and a covalent bond?

▸ Show
An ionic bond is the electrostatic attraction between oppositely charged ions formed by electron transfer (usually metal + non-metal). A covalent bond is the electrostatic attraction between a shared pair of electrons and two positive nuclei, formed by electron sharing (usually non-metal + non-metal).

Question 2: State the octet rule. Which elements are common exceptions?

▸ Show
The octet rule: atoms tend to gain, lose, or share electrons to achieve a full outer shell of 8 electrons (noble gas configuration). Exceptions: H and He (duet, 2 electrons), Be (often 4), B (often 6), and elements in Period 3+ that can expand their octet using d orbitals (e.g., $\ce{PCl5}$, $\ce{SF6}$).

Question 3: Draw the Lewis structure of $\ce{CO2}$.

▸ Show
$\ce{O=C=O}$. Total valence electrons: C (4) + 2×O (6) = 16. Place C in centre, form two double bonds to satisfy octets. Each atom has a full octet. There are two double bonds and no lone pairs on carbon; each oxygen has two lone pairs.

2. Ionic Bonding — Electron Transfer

2.1 The Born–Haber Model

Ionic bonding occurs when a metal (low electronegativity, low IE) transfers electrons to a non-metal (high electronegativity, exothermic electron affinity). The resulting cation and anion are held together by electrostatic attraction in a giant ionic lattice.

Figure 1: Electron transfer from Na to Cl. The resulting Na⁺ and Cl⁻ ions attract electrostatically, forming the ionic compound NaCl. Both ions achieve noble gas configurations.

2.2 Properties of Ionic Compounds

  • High melting/boiling points — strong electrostatic forces throughout the lattice require much energy to overcome.
  • Brittle — when a layer of ions shifts, like charges align and repel, shattering the crystal.
  • Conduct electricity when molten or aqueous — ions are free to move; in the solid, they are locked in place.
  • Soluble in polar solvents (e.g., water) — hydration energy compensates for lattice energy.

Check-in: Why is $\ce{MgO}$ (m.p. 2852°C) so much higher-melting than $\ce{NaCl}$ (m.p. 801°C)?

▸ Show
$\ce{Mg^{2+}}$ and $\ce{O^{2-}}$ both carry double charges compared to $\ce{Na+}$ and $\ce{Cl-}$. The electrostatic force $F \propto (q_+ \times q_-)/r^2$ is roughly 4 times stronger in MgO. Additionally, the Mg²⁺ and O²⁻ ions are smaller (higher charge density), which further increases lattice enthalpy. Much more energy is needed to break the MgO lattice.

3. Covalent Bonding — Lewis Structures

3.1 The Systematic Method for Drawing Lewis Structures

  1. Count total valence electrons (adjust for charge in ions: add 1 for each negative, subtract 1 for each positive).
  2. Draw the skeleton — least electronegative atom in centre (except H, which is always terminal).
  3. Connect atoms with single bonds (2 electrons per bond).
  4. Distribute remaining electrons as lone pairs to satisfy octets (outer atoms first, then central).
  5. Form multiple bonds if any atom lacks an octet — convert lone pairs to bonding pairs.
  6. Check formal charges to identify the most stable structure.

3.2 Multiple Bonds and Bond Order

A single bond shares 2 electrons (1 pair), a double bond shares 4 electrons (2 pairs), and a triple bond shares 6 electrons (3 pairs). As bond order increases, bond length decreases and bond strength increases.

Check-in: Draw the Lewis structure of $\ce{HCN}$ (hydrogen cyanide). Count valence electrons and identify bond orders.

▸ Show
Valence electrons: H(1) + C(4) + N(5) = 10. Skeleton: H–C–N. After single bonds (4 e⁻ used), 6 e⁻ remain. Place on N (3 lone pairs = 6 e⁻). N has octet but C only has 4 e⁻. Convert two N lone pairs to bonding pairs: $\ce{H-C#N:}$ (triple bond between C and N). Structure: $\ce{H-C≡N}$ with one lone pair on N.

4. Formal Charge — Finding the Best Lewis Structure (HL)

4.1 Definition and Formula

Formal charge is the hypothetical charge an atom would have if all bonding electrons were shared equally:

$$\text{Formal charge} = (\text{valence e}^-) - (\text{non-bonding e}^-) - \frac{1}{2}(\text{bonding e}^-)$$

The best Lewis structure is the one where:

  • Formal charges are as close to zero as possible.
  • Any negative formal charge resides on the most electronegative atom.
  • The sum of all formal charges equals the overall charge of the species.

4.2 Example: $\ce{CO2}$ — Two Possible Structures

Figure 2: Formal charge comparison for two possible Lewis structures of CO₂. Structure A with all formal charges of zero is the correct and most stable representation.

Check-in: The nitrate ion $\ce{NO3-}$ can be drawn with one N=O double bond and two N–O single bonds. Calculate the formal charge on each oxygen in this structure. Why do we need resonance?

▸ Show
For the double-bonded O: FC = 6 − 4 − ½(4) = 0. For each single-bonded O: FC = 6 − 6 − ½(2) = −1. N: FC = 5 − 0 − ½(8) = +1. Sum = 0 + (−1) + (−1) + (+1) = −1 = charge on ion ✓. However, experimentally all three N–O bonds are identical (intermediate between single and double). We use resonance: the double bond is delocalised over all three oxygen positions, giving each N–O bond a bond order of 1⅓.

5. Resonance — When One Structure Isn't Enough

Resonance occurs when two or more valid Lewis structures can be drawn that differ only in the placement of electrons (not atoms). The true structure is a resonance hybrid — a weighted average of all contributing forms. Delocalised electrons are shown with a dashed circle or line.

Key examples: $\ce{NO3-}$, $\ce{CO3^{2-}}$, $\ce{O3}$ (ozone), $\ce{C6H6}$ (benzene), $\ce{SO3}$, $\ce{NO2-}$.

6. Worked Examples

Example 1 — Lewis Structure with Formal Charge:

Draw the best Lewis structure for $\ce{SO2}$ and calculate formal charges on all atoms.

Step 1: Valence electrons: S(6) + 2×O(6) = 18.
Step 2: Skeleton: O–S–O.
Step 3: Single bonds use 4 e⁻, leaving 14. Fill octets: each O gets 6 e⁻ as lone pairs = 12, leaving 2 e⁻ as one lone pair on S. Now S has only 8 e⁻ (octet satisfied) but there are 2 e⁻ left — place them on S as a second lone pair. S now has 10 e⁻ total (expanded octet — OK for Period 3).
Better approach: Form one double bond to give each atom an octet with minimal formal charge. Structure: O=S–O with one lone pair on S and two on each O.
Formal charges — Structure with one S=O double bond:
Double-bonded O: FC = 6 − 4 − ½(4) = 0.
Single-bonded O: FC = 6 − 6 − ½(2) = −1.
S: FC = 6 − 2 − ½(6) = +1.
Sum = 0. This is a resonance hybrid — both O positions are equivalent.
Example 2 — Identifying the Best Structure:

For the cyanate ion $\ce{OCN-}$, three possible skeletal arrangements exist: O–C–N, C–O–N, and C–N–O. Use formal charge to determine the most stable arrangement.

Valence electrons: O(6) + C(4) + N(5) + 1(charge) = 16.

O–C–N (cyanate): $\ce{^{-}O-C#N}$ with triple C≡N bond. FC: O = 6−6−½(2)=−1; C = 4−0−½(8)=0; N = 5−2−½(6)=0. Sum = −1 ✓
C–O–N: FC would place negative on C or N (less electronegative than O) → less stable.
C–N–O (isocyanate): $\ce{^{-}C=N^{+}=O}$ has FC: C = −1, N = +1, O = 0... or with different bonding. The most stable has negative charge on O (most electronegative).

Winner: O–C–N (cyanate) because the negative formal charge sits on oxygen, the most electronegative element.
Example 3 — Resonance in the Carbonate Ion:

Draw all resonance structures for $\ce{CO3^{2-}}$ and describe the true structure.

Total valence electrons: C(4) + 3×O(6) + 2(charge) = 24. With C central, three equivalent resonance forms each have one C=O double bond and two C–O⁻ single bonds. The double bond is delocalised over all three positions.

The true carbonate ion has three identical C–O bonds, each with bond order = 1⅓ (intermediate between single and double). All O atoms carry an equal partial negative charge (−⅔ each). The ion is trigonal planar with 120° bond angles. Experimental evidence: all three C–O bond lengths in $\ce{CO3^{2-}}$ are identical (≈129 pm, between C–O single 143 pm and C=O double 120 pm).

7. Common Misconceptions

⚠️ Common Mistakes to Avoid

  1. Confusing formal charge with oxidation state. Formal charge assumes equal sharing of bonding electrons (covalent model). Oxidation state assumes complete transfer to the more electronegative atom (ionic model). They are different concepts with different values for the same atom.
  2. Drawing resonance structures with different atom arrangements. Resonance structures must have identical atom connectivity — only electrons move. If atoms are rearranged, those are different isomers, not resonance forms.
  3. Forgetting to count the ion charge in total valence electrons. For $\ce{NO3-}$, add 1 electron. For $\ce{NH4+}$, subtract 1. This is the #1 cause of wrong Lewis structures in exams.
  4. Giving hydrogen an octet. Hydrogen follows the duet rule — it can only accommodate 2 electrons (1 bond). Never draw H with lone pairs or double bonds.
  5. Expanding the octet for Period 2 elements. C, N, O, F cannot expand their octet — they lack accessible d orbitals. Only Period 3+ elements (e.g., P, S, Cl) can have more than 8 electrons. A structure with 10 electrons around oxygen is always wrong.

8. End-of-Lesson Practice

Question 1: Draw the Lewis structure of $\ce{PCl3}$ and state the number of bonding pairs and lone pairs on the central atom.

Question 2: Draw the Lewis structure of $\ce{SO4^{2-}}$ and calculate the formal charge on each atom in your best structure.

Question 3: The thiocyanate ion $\ce{SCN-}$ can exist as $\ce{S-C#N}$ or $\ce{S=C=N}$. Use formal charges to determine which is more stable.

Question 4: Explain why $\ce{BeCl2}$ in the gas phase is linear and covalent, not ionic. Draw its Lewis structure and comment on whether it obeys the octet rule.

Question 5: Draw all resonance structures for $\ce{NO2-}$ (nitrite ion). What is the N–O bond order in the resonance hybrid?

Question 6 (HL multi-step): The molecule $\ce{XeF4}$ has xenon as the central atom. (a) Count total valence electrons. (b) Draw the Lewis structure. (c) How many electrons surround Xe? (d) Is this an example of octet expansion? Explain. (e) What is the formal charge on Xe?

Answer 1: $\ce{PCl3}$: P has 5 valence e⁻, each Cl has 7 → total = 5 + 21 = 26 e⁻. Three P–Cl single bonds + one lone pair on P, three lone pairs on each Cl. Central P: 3 bonding pairs, 1 lone pair. All atoms have octets.

Answer 2: $\ce{SO4^{2-}}$: S(6) + 4×O(6) + 2(charge) = 32 e⁻. Best structure: two S=O double bonds and two S–O⁻ single bonds (resonance hybrid of 6 forms). Formal charges: S = 6 − 0 − ½(12) = 0; each double-bonded O = 6 − 4 − ½(4) = 0; each single-bonded O = 6 − 6 − ½(2) = −1. Sum = 0+0+0+(−1)+(−1) = −2 = charge on ion ✓. In the resonance hybrid, all S–O bonds are equivalent with bond order 1.5.

Answer 3: S–C≡N (thiocyanate): S: FC = 6−6−½(2) = −1; C: FC = 4−0−½(8) = 0; N: FC = 5−2−½(6) = 0. Sum = −1.
S=C=N (isothiocyanate): S: FC = 6−4−½(4) = 0; C: FC = 4−0−½(8) = 0; N: FC = 5−4−½(4) = −1. Sum = −1.
Both have FC sum of −1, but in S–C≡N the negative charge sits on S (EN=2.5), while in S=C=N it sits on N (EN=3.0). The nitrogen-bearing negative charge (more electronegative atom) is slightly preferred for free ions, but both forms exist — the ion is ambidentate.

Answer 4: $\ce{BeCl2}$: Be(2) + 2×Cl(7) = 16 e⁻. Structure: Cl–Be–Cl with three lone pairs on each Cl. Be has only 4 electrons — it is electron-deficient and does not obey the octet rule. In the gas phase, it is a linear covalent molecule (sp hybridised). In the solid state, it polymerises via chlorine bridges to give Be a full octet. The electronegativity difference (Be=1.5, Cl=3.0) is moderate, so the bonding is polar covalent with significant ionic character.

Answer 5: $\ce{NO2-}$: N(5) + 2×O(6) + 1 = 18 e⁻. Two equivalent resonance structures with N=O double bond on one side and N–O⁻ single bond on the other. The double bond is delocalised — each N–O bond has bond order = 1.5. The ion is bent (V-shaped) with one lone pair on N.

Answer 6: (a) Xe(8) + 4×F(7) = 36 e⁻. (b) Square planar: four Xe–F single bonds, each F with three lone pairs, plus two lone pairs on Xe (above and below the plane). (c) Xe is surrounded by 8 bonding electrons + 4 non-bonding = 12 electrons total. (d) Yes — Xe is Period 5 with accessible 5d orbitals; it can expand beyond an octet. This is octet expansion. (e) FC on Xe = 8 − 4 − ½(8) = 0.

9. Key Terms

TermDefinition
Ionic bondElectrostatic attraction between oppositely charged ions in a lattice
Covalent bondElectrostatic attraction between a shared pair of electrons and two nuclei
Lewis structureA diagram showing all valence electrons as dots; bonding pairs as lines, lone pairs as dots
Formal chargeThe charge an atom would have if bonding electrons were shared equally: $V - N_{\text{non}} - \frac{1}{2}N_{\text{bond}}$
ResonanceThe representation of a molecule by two or more Lewis structures differing only in electron placement
Resonance hybridThe true structure, which is a weighted average of all resonance contributors
Delocalised electronsElectrons spread over three or more atoms, not confined to a single bond or atom
Octet ruleThe tendency of atoms to achieve 8 valence electrons (noble gas configuration)
Expanded octetWhen a central atom from Period 3+ accommodates more than 8 electrons using d orbitals
Electron-deficientA molecule where the central atom has fewer than 8 electrons (e.g., $\ce{BeCl2}$, $\ce{BF3}$)

10. Exam Tips

Paper 1 — Formal Charge Questions: You usually don't need to calculate formal charges from scratch in Paper 1. Instead, use the rule: the best structure has formal charges closest to zero, and any negative charge on the most electronegative atom. Look at the given structures and eliminate those with +2 or −2 formal charges. The correct answer is almost always the one with all FC = 0 or the most electronegative atom bearing the negative charge.
Paper 2 — Drawing Lewis Structures: You MUST show all valence electrons, including lone pairs. A structure without lone pairs loses marks even if connectivity is correct. For ions, put the structure in square brackets with the charge outside. For resonance, draw all contributing forms (usually 2-3) with double-headed arrows (↔) between them, then state the hybrid structure.
Common Exam Traps: (1) $\ce{CO}$ has a triple bond with a lone pair on each atom — the carbon carries a formal −1 and oxygen +1, which surprises many students. (2) $\ce{NO}$ is a free radical with 11 valence electrons — it cannot have a full octet on both atoms. (3) $\ce{BF3}$ is electron-deficient (6 e⁻ around B); it readily accepts a lone pair to form $\ce{BF4-}$.
