Lesson 19: Rate of Reaction & Collision Theory

Syllabus reference: 6.1 — Collision Theory and Rates of Reaction

Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to:

  • Define rate of reaction and express it in terms of the change in concentration of reactants or products over time.
  • State the key postulates of collision theory: particles must collide, with sufficient energy ($E \ge E_a$), and with correct orientation.
  • Explain how concentration, pressure, surface area, and temperature affect the rate of reaction using collision theory.
  • Sketch and interpret Maxwell–Boltzmann energy distribution curves and explain how temperature and catalysts shift the curve.
  • Define a catalyst and explain its role in providing an alternative pathway with lower activation energy.

1. Prior Knowledge Check

Question 1: What is meant by "rate of reaction"? Give an example of a fast and a slow reaction.

▸ Show
Rate of reaction is the change in concentration of a reactant or product per unit time, usually expressed in mol dm⁻³ s⁻¹. Fast reaction: explosion of $\ce{H2}$ with $\ce{O2}$ (milliseconds). Slow reaction: rusting of iron (days to years). The rate can be measured from the gradient of a concentration–time graph.

Question 2: What factors might you change to make a reaction go faster?

▸ Show
Five factors: (1) Concentration of reactants (more particles per volume = more collisions). (2) Pressure for gases (higher pressure = higher concentration). (3) Surface area of solids (more exposed particles). (4) Temperature (particles move faster, and more have $E \ge E_a$). (5) Catalyst (lowers $E_a$).

Question 3: What is activation energy ($E_a$) and why is it needed?

▸ Show
Activation energy ($E_a$) is the minimum kinetic energy that colliding particles must possess for a reaction to occur. It is needed to break existing bonds and reach the transition state. Even exothermic reactions need $E_a$ to get started — think of lighting a match: the combustion of wood is highly exothermic, but you must first supply the activation energy with a flame.

2. Defining and Measuring Rate of Reaction

2.1 Mathematical Definition

The rate of reaction is defined as the change in concentration of a specified species per unit time:

$$\text{Rate} = -\frac{\Delta[\text{reactant}]}{\Delta t} = +\frac{\Delta[\text{product}]}{\Delta t}$$ The negative sign ensures the rate is positive when using reactants (concentration decreases). Units: mol dm⁻³ s⁻¹. The initial rate is measured at $t = 0$ (steepest gradient), before concentrations change significantly.

Check-in: In the reaction $\ce{CaCO3(s) + 2HCl(aq) -> CaCl2(aq) + CO2(g) + H2O(l)}$, 0.440 g of $\ce{CO2}$ is produced in 50 seconds. Calculate the average rate of reaction in terms of $\ce{CO2}$ production (in mol s⁻¹). ($\ce{C}$ = 12.0, $\ce{O}$ = 16.0)

▸ Show
$M(\ce{CO2}) = 44.0$ g mol⁻¹. $n(\ce{CO2}) = 0.440 / 44.0 = 0.0100$ mol. Rate = $0.0100 / 50 = \mathbf{2.00 \times 10^{-4}\text{ mol s}^{-1}}$.

3. Collision Theory — Why Reactions Happen (and Don't)

3.1 The Three Conditions for a Successful Reaction

Collision theory states that for a reaction to occur between two particles:

  1. They must collide — particles that never meet cannot react.
  2. They must collide with sufficient energy — the combined kinetic energy must equal or exceed the activation energy ($E \ge E_a$).
  3. They must collide with the correct orientation — the atoms involved in bond-breaking/forming must be positioned appropriately.
Figure 1: Collision theory in action. Even if two particles collide, they only react if both energy AND orientation conditions are met. This is why not every collision leads to a reaction.

3.2 How Each Factor Affects Rate

FactorEffect on rateCollision theory explanation
Concentration (solutions)IncreasesMore particles per unit volume → higher collision frequency. The fraction of successful collisions stays the same, but there are more collisions overall.
Pressure (gases)IncreasesSame as concentration — compressing a gas increases the number of particles per unit volume → more collisions per second.
Surface area (solids)IncreasesMore particles are exposed at the surface → more collisions can occur between reactant particles. Powdered solids react faster than lumps.
TemperatureDramatically increasesTwo effects: (1) particles move faster → more frequent collisions (minor effect). (2) More particles exceed $E_a$ — the fraction of collisions with $E \ge E_a$ increases exponentially (major effect).
CatalystIncreasesProvides an alternative pathway with lower $E_a$. A larger fraction of particles now have sufficient energy to react. The catalyst is not consumed.

Check-in: A piece of magnesium ribbon reacts with HCl in 120 seconds. The same mass of magnesium powder reacts in 15 seconds under identical conditions. Explain the difference using collision theory.

▸ Show
Magnesium powder has a much larger surface area than a ribbon of the same mass. More Mg atoms are exposed on the surface and available to collide with $\ce{H+}$ ions in the acid. This dramatically increases the collision frequency — there are more successful collisions per second, so the reaction is faster. The activation energy and temperature are unchanged; only the number of exposed particles differs.

4. Maxwell–Boltzmann Distribution — The Energy Spread

4.1 What the Curve Shows

At any given temperature, gas particles do not all have the same kinetic energy — there is a distribution. The Maxwell–Boltzmann (MB) curve shows the number of particles ($N$) with each value of kinetic energy ($E$).

  • The curve starts at the origin (no particles have zero energy).
  • It rises to a peak (the most probable energy) then tails off to the right.
  • The area under the curve = total number of particles.
  • The area to the right of $E_a$ = number of particles with sufficient energy to react.

Figure 2: A Maxwell–Boltzmann distribution curve at a given temperature. Only the shaded area (particles with energy ≥ Eₐ) can react when they collide. The larger this shaded area, the faster the reaction.

4.2 Effect of Temperature and Catalyst on the MB Curve

Figure 3: Increasing temperature shifts the MB curve to the right and flattens it. The area with $E \ge E_a$ increases dramatically — this is why a 10°C rise typically doubles the reaction rate.

Check-in: A catalyst lowers $E_a$ from 100 kJ mol⁻¹ to 60 kJ mol⁻¹. On an MB curve, does the catalyst change the shape of the curve? Explain.

▸ Show
No — the curve itself does not change. A catalyst does not change the energy distribution of the particles (same temperature, same MB curve). What changes is the position of the $E_a$ line, which shifts left (from 100 to 60). The area under the unchanged curve to the right of the new, lower $E_a$ is much larger — so many more particles now have sufficient energy. This is why a catalyst increases rate without being consumed.

5. Worked Examples

Example 1 — Explaining Temperature Effect:

Explain why increasing temperature by 10°C roughly doubles the rate of many reactions, even though particles are only moving about 2% faster (based on $\sqrt{T}$).

Answer: The 2% increase in particle speed only slightly increases collision frequency — this alone would not double the rate. The dominant effect is that the fraction of particles with energy $\ge E_a$ increases exponentially with temperature. In the MB distribution, the high-energy tail grows dramatically even with a small temperature increase. Even though the most probable energy shifts only slightly, the area under the curve beyond $E_a$ can double or triple.

⚠️ Common wrong approach: Attributing the rate increase primarily to "particles moving faster". This only accounts for a tiny fraction of the effect. The key phrase to use in exams is: "a much larger fraction of particles have energy greater than or equal to the activation energy."
Example 2 — Rate from Experimental Data:

In an experiment, 0.050 mol of $\ce{H2}$ gas is produced in the first 40 seconds of a reaction. The solution volume is 250 cm³. Calculate: (a) the average rate in mol s⁻¹, (b) the average rate in mol dm⁻³ s⁻¹.

Step 1: Rate (mol s⁻¹) = $0.050 / 40 = \mathbf{1.25 \times 10^{-3}\text{ mol s}^{-1}}$.
Step 2: Concentration change = $0.050 / 0.250 = 0.200$ mol dm⁻³. Rate = $0.200 / 40 = \mathbf{5.00 \times 10^{-3}\text{ mol dm}^{-3}\text{ s}^{-1}}$.

⚠️ Common wrong approach: Forgetting to convert cm³ to dm³ (divide by 1000). 250 cm³ = 0.250 dm³. Using 250 directly gives a rate 1000× too small.
Example 3 — MB Curve Analysis:

The diagram below shows an MB distribution for a gas at 300 K. $E_a$ for the uncatalysed reaction is marked. (a) On the same axes, sketch the curve at 400 K. (b) Mark the new $E_a$ if a catalyst is added. (c) Explain why the area beyond $E_a$ increases.

(a) The curve shifts to the right, flattens (peak is lower and moves to higher energy), and the tail extends further. Total area remains the same (same number of particles).
(b) The catalyst provides a lower $E_{a(\text{cat})}$, marked further left on the energy axis — more of the curve lies to the right of this new threshold.
(c) The area beyond $E_a$ increases with temperature because more particles populate the high-energy tail of the distribution. With a catalyst, the area increases because the threshold moves left — the distribution itself does not change.

⚠️ Common wrong approach: Drawing the catalyst effect as shifting the MB curve. The curve does NOT change with a catalyst — only the $E_a$ marker moves.

⚠️ Common Mistakes to Avoid

  1. "A catalyst lowers the activation energy of the reaction." A catalyst does not change the $E_a$ of the original pathway. It provides an alternative pathway with a different, lower $E_a$. The original, uncatalysed pathway still exists — it's just that nearly all particles now use the catalysed route because it's easier.
  2. "Increasing temperature increases the number of particles with $E \ge E_a$ because particles speed up." The speed increase is negligible. The real reason is the distribution shifts — the MB curve spreads out. Use the phrase "the area under the curve to the right of $E_a$ increases" rather than "particles move faster".
  3. "Rate is constant throughout a reaction." Rate decreases over time as reactants are consumed — concentration falls, so collision frequency falls. Only the initial rate (gradient at $t = 0$) is the "true" rate for a given set of initial concentrations. Drawing a tangent at $t = 0$ on a concentration–time graph is how you measure it.
  4. "A catalyst is consumed in the reaction." A catalyst is not consumed — it is regenerated at the end of the catalytic cycle. It may be involved in the mechanism (forming an intermediate) but is chemically unchanged overall. This is why a tiny amount of catalyst can process large quantities of reactant.

6. End-of-Lesson Practice

Question 1 (Paper 1 style): Which combination of changes will always increase the rate of a gas-phase reaction?
A. Decrease temperature, add a catalyst
B. Increase pressure, decrease temperature
C. Increase temperature, increase concentration
D. Decrease concentration, increase surface area

Question 2: 0.024 mol of $\ce{O2}$ gas is produced in 30 seconds from the decomposition of $\ce{H2O2}$. The solution volume is 100 cm³. Calculate the average rate in (a) mol s⁻¹, (b) mol dm⁻³ s⁻¹.

Question 3: Explain why a lump of $\ce{CaCO3}$ reacts more slowly with HCl than the same mass of powdered $\ce{CaCO3}$. Use collision theory and reference the term "surface area".

Question 4: Sketch and label a Maxwell–Boltzmann distribution curve for a gas at 300 K. On the same axes, draw the curve at 500 K. Mark $E_a$ and explain which area on the graph represents particles that can react.

Question 5: The reaction $\ce{2HI(g) -> H2(g) + I2(g)}$ has $E_a = 183$ kJ mol⁻¹. With a platinum catalyst, $E_a$ drops to 58 kJ mol⁻¹. Using the MB distribution concept, explain why the rate increases by a factor of over $10^{10}$.

Question 6 (IB-style): A student investigates the rate of reaction between marble chips ($\ce{CaCO3}$) and hydrochloric acid by measuring the volume of $\ce{CO2}$ produced.
(a) Describe a suitable experimental setup and name the apparatus.
(b) Sketch the expected graph of gas volume vs. time. Explain why the curve levels off.
(c) The student repeats the experiment using the same mass of marble but in smaller chips. On the same axes, sketch the new curve.
(d) The student also repeats with the original marble chips but with a higher concentration of acid. Sketch this curve and compare.

Answer 1: C. Increasing temperature (more particles exceed $E_a$) and increasing concentration (more collision frequency) both always increase rate. A: decrease T decreases rate. B: decrease T decreases rate. D: decrease concentration decreases rate.

Answer 2: (a) Rate = 0.024 / 30 = $\mathbf{8.0 \times 10^{-4}\text{ mol s}^{-1}}$. (b) $V = 0.100$ dm³. $\Delta[\ce{O2}] = 0.024/0.100 = 0.240$ mol dm⁻³. Rate = $0.240 / 30 = \mathbf{8.0 \times 10^{-3}\text{ mol dm}^{-3}\text{ s}^{-1}}$.

Answer 3: Powdered $\ce{CaCO3}$ has a much larger surface area than a lump of the same mass. Collision theory states that a reaction can only occur when reactant particles collide. With a powder, far more $\ce{CaCO3}$ particles are exposed at the surface and available to collide with $\ce{H+}$ ions in solution. This gives a higher collision frequency (more collisions per second) and hence a faster rate of reaction. The activation energy is unchanged.

Answer 4: [Sketch description] Both curves start at origin. The 300 K curve is taller and narrower with a peak at lower energy. The 500 K curve is flatter, shifted right, with a lower peak height but the same total area. $E_a$ is marked as a vertical line to the right of both curve peaks. The area under each curve to the right of the $E_a$ line represents particles with $E \ge E_a$ — this area is significantly larger for the 500 K curve.

Answer 5: At 700 K, using $E_a = 183$: the fraction of particles with $E \ge E_a$ is given by $e^{-E_a/RT} = e^{-183000/(8.31 \times 700)} = e^{-31.5} \approx 2 \times 10^{-14}$. With the catalyst ($E_a = 58$): $e^{-58000/(8.31 \times 700)} = e^{-10.0} \approx 4.5 \times 10^{-5}$. The ratio is $\approx 2.3 \times 10^9$ — about $10^{10}$ times more particles have sufficient energy. The exponential dependence ($e^{-E_a/RT}$) means even modest reductions in $E_a$ produce enormous rate increases.

Answer 6: (a) Setup: Conical flask with marble chips and HCl, connected via delivery tube to a gas syringe (or inverted measuring cylinder in a water trough). Apparatus: conical flask, gas syringe (or water trough + measuring cylinder), stopwatch, balance. (b) Graph: gas volume rises steeply at first (fastest rate, highest concentration), then the gradient decreases and eventually levels off horizontally — the reaction is complete (limiting reagent consumed). (c) Smaller chips: steeper initial gradient (faster rate) but same final volume — the curve is higher at early times but converges to the same final volume. (d) Higher acid concentration: steeper initial gradient (more $\ce{H+}$ ions per volume → more collisions), faster rate, but same limiting reagent (marble) means the final gas volume may be the same IF the marble is still limiting. If acid was originally limiting, the final volume would increase.

7. Key Terms

TermDefinition
Rate of reactionThe change in concentration of a reactant or product per unit time; units: mol dm⁻³ s⁻¹
Initial rateThe instantaneous rate at the start of the reaction ($t = 0$), measured from the tangent to the concentration–time curve
Collision theoryThe model stating that for a reaction to occur, particles must collide with sufficient energy ($E \ge E_a$) and correct orientation
Activation energy ($E_a$)The minimum kinetic energy that colliding particles must possess for a successful reaction
Collision frequencyThe number of collisions per unit time per unit volume; increases with concentration, pressure, and temperature
Maxwell–Boltzmann distributionA graph showing the distribution of kinetic energies among particles in a gas at a given temperature
CatalystA substance that increases the rate of a reaction by providing an alternative pathway with lower $E_a$; is not consumed in the reaction
Surface areaThe total area of solid reactant exposed to the other reactants; larger surface area → more collisions → faster rate
Alternative pathwayA different reaction mechanism with a lower activation energy, provided by a catalyst

8. Exam Tips

Paper 1 (Multiple Choice): MB curve questions almost always appear. You will be asked to compare two curves (different T, or same T with catalyst), identify which one has more particles beyond $E_a$, or explain why a catalyst does not shift the curve. Remember: catalyst shifts the $E_a$ line — NOT the curve. Temperature shifts the entire curve.
Paper 2 — Explaining Rate Changes: You must use precise collision theory language. "Particles collide more" is too vague. Write: "Increasing concentration increases the number of particles per unit volume, which increases the frequency of collisions between reactant particles. A greater proportion of these collisions have sufficient energy ($E \ge E_a$) and correct orientation, so the rate increases." Link every factor back to collision frequency AND the fraction with sufficient energy.
Paper 3 — Rate Experiments: Be able to describe a method for measuring rate by gas collection or mass loss. Key marking points: (1) name the apparatus (gas syringe, balance), (2) record measurements at regular time intervals, (3) control variables (temperature, mass of solid, volume and concentration of acid), (4) repeat for reliability. The "initial rate" method uses the gradient of the tangent at $t = 0$.
