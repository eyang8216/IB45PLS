Lesson 17: Born-Haber Cycles & Enthalpy of Solution [HL]

Syllabus reference: 5.1 (HL) — Born-Haber Cycle; 5.4 (HL) — Enthalpy of Solution

Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to:

  • Define lattice enthalpy ($\Delta H_{\text{latt}}$) as the enthalpy change when one mole of an ionic solid is formed from its gaseous ions.
  • Construct a Born-Haber cycle for an ionic compound, identifying all enthalpy terms: atomisation, ionisation energy, electron affinity, and lattice enthalpy.
  • Calculate an unknown enthalpy term (typically lattice enthalpy) from a Born-Haber cycle using Hess's Law.
  • Define and calculate enthalpy of solution ($\Delta H_{\text{sol}}$) from hydration enthalpies and lattice enthalpy.
  • Explain why some ionic compounds are soluble and others are not, using the balance between lattice enthalpy and hydration enthalpy.

1. Prior Knowledge Check

Question 1: Define first ionisation energy and explain whether it is endothermic or exothermic.

▸ Show
First ionisation energy is the energy required to remove one mole of electrons from one mole of gaseous atoms: $\ce{X(g) -> X+(g) + e-}$. It is always endothermic (positive) because removing an electron requires overcoming the electrostatic attraction to the nucleus.

Question 2: What is electron affinity, and what is its sign?

▸ Show
Electron affinity is the enthalpy change when one mole of gaseous atoms gains one mole of electrons: $\ce{X(g) + e- -> X-(g)}$. The first electron affinity is usually exothermic (negative) because the electron is attracted to the nucleus. The second electron affinity (adding an electron to a negative ion) is endothermic (positive) — you are forcing an electron onto an already negative species, overcoming repulsion.

Question 3: What does "atomisation enthalpy" mean?

▸ Show
Standard enthalpy of atomisation ($\Delta H_{\text{at}}^\ominus$) is the enthalpy change when one mole of gaseous atoms is formed from the element in its standard state. For example: $\ce{Na(s) -> Na(g)}$ (sublimation of sodium metal, +107 kJ mol⁻¹) or $\ce{1/2 Cl2(g) -> Cl(g)}$ (half the bond enthalpy of Cl₂, +121 kJ mol⁻¹). It is always endothermic (positive) — you must break bonds or overcome intermolecular forces.

2. The Born-Haber Cycle — An Energy "Map" of Ionic Compound Formation

2.1 What Is Lattice Enthalpy?

Lattice enthalpy ($\Delta H_{\text{latt}}$) is the enthalpy change when one mole of a solid ionic compound is formed from its gaseous ions:

$$\ce{Na+(g) + Cl-(g) -> NaCl(s)}, \quad \Delta H_{\text{latt}}^\ominus = -790\text{ kJ mol}^{-1}$$

Lattice enthalpy is always negative (exothermic) because bringing oppositely charged ions together releases a large amount of energy. The magnitude depends on ionic charge and ionic radius — the stronger the electrostatic attraction, the more exothermic the lattice enthalpy.

Two conventions (IB uses both — read carefully):
• Lattice formation enthalpy: gaseous ions → solid, exothermic (−)
• Lattice dissociation enthalpy: solid → gaseous ions, endothermic (+), same magnitude, opposite sign.
The IB Data Booklet gives lattice dissociation enthalpies (positive values). In Born-Haber cycles, you must be consistent about which convention you use.

2.2 The Born-Haber Cycle for NaCl

A Born-Haber cycle is an application of Hess's Law to ionic compound formation. It breaks the overall formation reaction ($\ce{Na(s) + 1/2 Cl2(g) -> NaCl(s)}$) into a series of steps, each with a known or calculable enthalpy change.

f = −411] +107 Na(g) + ½Cl₂(g) +496 Na⁺(g) + e⁻ + ½Cl₂(g) +121 Na⁺(g) + Cl(g) + e⁻ −349 Na⁺(g) + Cl⁻(g) ΔHlatt NaCl(s) [Route: elements → compound = sum of steps]
Figure 1: Born-Haber cycle for NaCl. Starting from elements (top) to final ionic solid (bottom), the cycle traces five steps: atomisation of Na, ionisation of Na, atomisation of Cl, electron affinity of Cl, and lattice enthalpy. By Hess's Law, the sum of all steps equals $\Delta H_f^\ominus(\ce{NaCl}) = -411\text{ kJ mol}^{-1}$.

Check-in: Using the Born-Haber cycle for NaCl above, calculate the lattice formation enthalpy. $\Delta H_f^\ominus(\ce{NaCl}) = -411$, $\Delta H_{\text{at}}(\ce{Na}) = +107$, IE₁(Na) = +496, $\Delta H_{\text{at}}(\ce{1/2 Cl2}) = +121$, EA₁(Cl) = −349 kJ mol⁻¹.

▸ Show
By Hess's Law: $\Delta H_f = +107 + 496 + 121 + (-349) + \Delta H_{\text{latt}} = -411$. So $\Delta H_{\text{latt}} = -411 - 107 - 496 - 121 + 349 = \mathbf{-786\text{ kJ mol}^{-1}}$ (formation). This is the energy released when $\ce{Na+}$ and $\ce{Cl-}$ ions come together to form the lattice.

3. Enthalpy of Solution — Why Do Some Salts Dissolve?

3.1 The Two-Step Process

When an ionic solid dissolves in water, two energy changes compete:

  1. Breaking the lattice — separating gaseous ions from the solid. This is the lattice dissociation enthalpy (endothermic, $+\Delta H_{\text{latt diss}}$).
  2. Hydrating the ions — surrounding each gaseous ion with water molecules. This is the hydration enthalpy (exothermic, $\Delta H_{\text{hyd}}$).
Enthalpy of solution: $$\Delta H_{\text{sol}}^\ominus = \Delta H_{\text{latt diss}}^\ominus + \Delta H_{\text{hyd}}^\ominus$$ $\Delta H_{\text{latt diss}}^\ominus$ = lattice dissociation enthalpy (positive). $\Delta H_{\text{hyd}}^\ominus$ = sum of hydration enthalpies of cation and anion (negative). If $|\Delta H_{\text{hyd}}| > \Delta H_{\text{latt diss}}$, the salt dissolves exothermically — but solubility depends on $\Delta G$, not just $\Delta H$ (see Lesson 18).
latt diss (endothermic) ΔHhyd (exothermic, −) ΔHsol = ΔHlatt diss + ΔHhyd
Figure 2: Enthalpy of solution as the sum of lattice dissociation (up, endothermic) and hydration (down, exothermic). The balance determines whether dissolving is overall endothermic or exothermic.

Check-in: For NaCl, $\Delta H_{\text{latt diss}}^\ominus = +786$ kJ mol⁻¹ and $\Delta H_{\text{hyd}}^\ominus(\ce{Na+}) = -406$, $\Delta H_{\text{hyd}}^\ominus(\ce{Cl-}) = -378$ kJ mol⁻¹. Calculate $\Delta H_{\text{sol}}^\ominus$ and state whether dissolving NaCl is endothermic or exothermic.

▸ Show
$\Delta H_{\text{hyd}}^\ominus = (-406) + (-378) = -784$ kJ mol⁻¹. $\Delta H_{\text{sol}}^\ominus = +786 + (-784) = \mathbf{+2\text{ kJ mol}^{-1}}$. Dissolving NaCl is slightly endothermic. Yet NaCl dissolves readily — this tells you that entropy (disorder), not just enthalpy, drives solubility (see Lesson 18).

3.2 Factors Affecting Lattice and Hydration Enthalpies

FactorEffect on lattice enthalpyEffect on hydration enthalpy
Increasing ionic chargeMuch more exothermic (stronger attraction)Much more exothermic (stronger ion–dipole)
Increasing ionic radiusLess exothermic (weaker attraction)Less exothermic (weaker ion–dipole)

The balance between these competing effects determines solubility trends. For example, $\ce{MgO}$ has high lattice enthalpy (due to 2+/2− charges) and is insoluble — the lattice enthalpy dominates over hydration. $\ce{NaCl}$ (1+/1−) has moderate lattice enthalpy and dissolves.

Check-in: Explain why $\ce{CaCl2}$ is more soluble than $\ce{CaF2}$, even though both contain $\ce{Ca^2+}$.

▸ Show
$\ce{F-}$ is much smaller than $\ce{Cl-}$, so the lattice enthalpy of $\ce{CaF2}$ is significantly more exothermic (stronger electrostatic attraction). The hydration enthalpy of $\ce{F-}$ is also more exothermic (small ion = stronger hydration), but the increase in lattice enthalpy outweighs the increase in hydration enthalpy. The net $\Delta H_{\text{sol}}$ for $\ce{CaF2}$ is less favourable, and fluoride's small radius also means the entropy change upon dissolution is less favourable.

4. Worked Examples

Example 1 — Born-Haber for MgO:

Construct a Born-Haber cycle to calculate the lattice enthalpy of $\ce{MgO(s)}$.
Data (kJ mol⁻¹): $\Delta H_f^\ominus(\ce{MgO}) = -602$, $\Delta H_{\text{at}}(\ce{Mg}) = +148$, IE₁(Mg) = +738, IE₂(Mg) = +1451, $\Delta H_{\text{at}}(\ce{1/2 O2}) = +249$, EA₁(O) = −142, EA₂(O) = +844.

Step 1: Write the formation equation. $\ce{Mg(s) + 1/2 O2(g) -> MgO(s)}$, $\Delta H_f = -602$.
Step 2: Atomise Mg. $\ce{Mg(s) -> Mg(g)}$, +148.
Step 3: Ionise Mg — twice. $\ce{Mg(g) -> Mg+(g) + e-}$, +738. $\ce{Mg+(g) -> Mg^2+(g) + e-}$, +1451. Total IE = +2189.
Step 4: Atomise O. $\ce{1/2 O2(g) -> O(g)}$, +249.
Step 5: Add electrons to O — twice. EA₁ = −142, EA₂ = +844. Total EA = +702.
Step 6: Apply Hess's Law. $\Delta H_f = 148 + 2189 + 249 + 702 + \Delta H_{\text{latt}} = -602$. So $\Delta H_{\text{latt}} = -602 - 148 - 2189 - 249 - 702 = \mathbf{-3890\text{ kJ mol}^{-1}}$ (formation).

Note the enormous lattice enthalpy — direct consequence of the 2+/2− charges.

⚠️ Common wrong approach: Forgetting that IE and EA for doubly-charged ions require TWO steps. Mg needs IE₁ + IE₂; O needs EA₁ + EA₂. Using only one of each gives a wildly inaccurate lattice enthalpy.
Example 2 — Enthalpy of Solution of KBr:

Calculate $\Delta H_{\text{sol}}$ for $\ce{KBr(s)}$. Data (kJ mol⁻¹): $\Delta H_{\text{latt diss}} = +671$, $\Delta H_{\text{hyd}}(\ce{K+}) = -322$, $\Delta H_{\text{hyd}}(\ce{Br-}) = -336$.

Step 1: $\Delta H_{\text{hyd}}^\ominus = (-322) + (-336) = -658$ kJ mol⁻¹.
Step 2: $\Delta H_{\text{sol}} = +671 + (-658) = \mathbf{+13\text{ kJ mol}^{-1}}$.

⚠️ Common wrong approach: Using the lattice formation enthalpy (−671) instead of dissociation (+671). If you use −671, you get $\Delta H_{\text{sol}} = −671 − 658 = −1329$, which is absurdly large. Always confirm which convention you are using.
Example 3 — Predicting Solubility from Enthalpy Data:

Compare $\Delta H_{\text{sol}}$ of LiCl and NaCl. Explain the trend in terms of ionic radii.
Data (kJ mol⁻¹): LiCl: $\Delta H_{\text{latt diss}} = +853$, $\Delta H_{\text{hyd}}(\ce{Li+}) = -520$, $\Delta H_{\text{hyd}}(\ce{Cl-}) = -378$. NaCl: as calculated earlier ($+786$, $−406$, $−378$).

Step 1: LiCl: $\Delta H_{\text{sol}} = 853 + (-520 - 378) = 853 - 898 = \mathbf{-45\text{ kJ mol}^{-1}}$ (exothermic).
Step 2: NaCl: $\Delta H_{\text{sol}} = 786 + (-406 - 378) = 786 - 784 = \mathbf{+2\text{ kJ mol}^{-1}}$ (slightly endothermic).
Step 3: Explanation. Li⁺ has a smaller ionic radius than Na⁺, so both $\Delta H_{\text{latt diss}}$ and $|\Delta H_{\text{hyd}}|$ are larger for LiCl. However, $|\Delta H_{\text{hyd}}|$ increases more sharply for the small Li⁺ ion, outweighing the increased lattice enthalpy — making LiCl dissolution exothermic.

⚠️ Common wrong approach: Assuming that smaller ions = less soluble because of higher lattice enthalpy. This is only half the story — smaller ions also have more exothermic hydration. You must consider both.

⚠️ Common Mistakes to Avoid

  1. "Confusing lattice formation and lattice dissociation enthalpy." Lattice formation (gaseous ions → solid) is exothermic (−). Lattice dissociation (solid → gaseous ions) is endothermic (+). The IB Data Booklet historically gives dissociation values (positive). Always check which direction the question uses.
  2. "Forgetting that second electron affinity is endothermic." EA₁ is exothermic (electron attracted to neutral atom). EA₂ is always endothermic — you are forcing a second electron onto an already negative ion against electrostatic repulsion. For O²⁻, EA₁ = −142 but EA₂ = +844 kJ mol⁻¹.
  3. "Treating atomisation of diatomic gases as the bond enthalpy." For $\ce{1/2 Cl2(g) -> Cl(g)}$, you need half the bond enthalpy of $\ce{Cl-Cl}$ (half of +242 = +121), not the full +242. For $\ce{1/2 O2(g) -> O(g)}$, you need half of +498 = +249.
  4. "Assuming $\Delta H_{\text{sol}} < 0$ means soluble, and $\Delta H_{\text{sol}} > 0$ means insoluble." Solubility depends on $\Delta G$, not $\Delta H$. NaCl has $\Delta H_{\text{sol}} \approx +2$ kJ mol⁻¹ but is highly soluble because the large positive entropy change ($\Delta S \gg 0$) makes $\Delta G < 0$ at 298 K.

5. End-of-Lesson Practice

Question 1 (Paper 1 style): In a Born-Haber cycle, which step is always endothermic?
A. First electron affinity   B. Lattice formation   C. Ionisation energy   D. Hydration of ions

Question 2: Use the following data to calculate the lattice formation enthalpy of $\ce{KCl(s)}$ (kJ mol⁻¹): $\Delta H_f^\ominus(\ce{KCl}) = -437$, $\Delta H_{\text{at}}(\ce{K}) = +89$, IE₁(K) = +419, $\Delta H_{\text{at}}(\ce{1/2 Cl2}) = +121$, EA₁(Cl) = −349.

Question 3: Calculate $\Delta H_{\text{sol}}$ for $\ce{CaCl2(s)}$ given (kJ mol⁻¹): $\Delta H_{\text{latt diss}} = +2258$, $\Delta H_{\text{hyd}}(\ce{Ca^2+}) = -1650$, $\Delta H_{\text{hyd}}(\ce{Cl-}) = -378$.

Question 4: The lattice dissociation enthalpy of AgCl is +915 kJ mol⁻¹ and that of NaCl is +786 kJ mol⁻¹. Both have the same structure (rock salt). Explain why AgCl has a larger lattice enthalpy, even though Ag⁺ and Na⁺ have similar ionic radii.

Question 5: Construct a Born-Haber cycle for $\ce{CaO(s)}$ and calculate its lattice formation enthalpy. Data (kJ mol⁻¹): $\Delta H_f^\ominus(\ce{CaO}) = -635$, $\Delta H_{\text{at}}(\ce{Ca}) = +178$, IE₁(Ca) = +590, IE₂(Ca) = +1145, $\Delta H_{\text{at}}(\ce{1/2 O2}) = +249$, EA₁(O) = −142, EA₂(O) = +844.

Question 6 (IB-style): The solubility of Group 2 sulfates decreases down the group: $\ce{MgSO4}$ is soluble, $\ce{CaSO4}$ is sparingly soluble, $\ce{BaSO4}$ is insoluble.
(a) Explain this trend in terms of lattice and hydration enthalpies.
(b) Why does the trend reverse for Group 2 hydroxides?

Answer 1: C. Ionisation energy is always endothermic (positive). First electron affinity (A) is usually exothermic. Lattice formation (B) is exothermic. Hydration (D) is exothermic.

Answer 2: $\Delta H_f = +89 + 419 + 121 + (-349) + \Delta H_{\text{latt}} = -437$. $\Delta H_{\text{latt}} = -437 - 89 - 419 - 121 + 349 = \mathbf{-717\text{ kJ mol}^{-1}}$ (formation).

Answer 3: Total hydration = (−1650) + 2(−378) = −2406 kJ mol⁻¹ (note: 2 Cl⁻ ions). $\Delta H_{\text{sol}} = +2258 + (-2406) = \mathbf{-148\text{ kJ mol}^{-1}}$ (exothermic).

Answer 4: Ag⁺ has a much higher polarising power than Na⁺ because Ag⁺ has a d¹⁰ electron configuration that does not shield the nuclear charge effectively. This leads to significant covalent character in the Ag–Cl bond, which adds to the purely ionic lattice enthalpy. The AgCl lattice enthalpy is "extra" large because of this partial covalent bonding — not because of simple ionic electrostatics.

Answer 5: Total IE(Ca) = 590 + 1145 = +1735. Total EA(O) = −142 + 844 = +702. $\Delta H_f = 178 + 1735 + 249 + 702 + \Delta H_{\text{latt}} = -635$. $\Delta H_{\text{latt}} = -635 - 178 - 1735 - 249 - 702 = \mathbf{-3499\text{ kJ mol}^{-1}}$.

Answer 6: (a) As you go down Group 2, the cation radius increases from Mg²⁺ to Ba²⁺. Both lattice enthalpy and hydration enthalpy become less exothermic (weaker), but for sulfates, the lattice enthalpy decreases more slowly than hydration enthalpy because the large sulfate ion dominates the lattice spacing. The hydration enthalpy drops sharply as the cation gets larger, making $\Delta H_{\text{sol}}$ increasingly endothermic — and $\Delta G_{\text{sol}}$ becomes less negative/positive, reducing solubility.
(b) For Group 2 hydroxides, the trend reverses because OH⁻ is a small anion. As the cation gets larger, the lattice enthalpy decreases more sharply than for sulfates (since OH⁻ is small, the cation size matters more for lattice spacing). The hydration enthalpy still drops, but the lattice enthalpy drops faster, making $\Delta G_{\text{sol}}$ more favourable down the group — so solubility increases from Mg(OH)₂ (insoluble) to Ba(OH)₂ (soluble).

6. Key Terms

TermDefinition
Lattice enthalpy (formation)The enthalpy change when one mole of a solid ionic compound forms from its gaseous ions; always exothermic ($\Delta H < 0$)
Lattice dissociation enthalpyThe enthalpy change when one mole of an ionic solid separates into gaseous ions; always endothermic ($\Delta H > 0$), equal in magnitude to lattice formation enthalpy but opposite in sign
Born-Haber cycleA thermochemical cycle applying Hess's Law to the formation of an ionic compound, breaking it into atomisation, ionisation, electron affinity, and lattice enthalpy steps
Enthalpy of atomisationThe enthalpy change when one mole of gaseous atoms forms from an element in its standard state; always endothermic
Ionisation energy (IE)The energy required to remove an electron from a gaseous atom or ion; always endothermic
Electron affinity (EA)The enthalpy change when an electron is added to a gaseous atom or ion; first EA usually exothermic, second EA always endothermic
Hydration enthalpyThe enthalpy change when one mole of gaseous ions is surrounded by water molecules; always exothermic
Enthalpy of solution ($\Delta H_{\text{sol}}$)The enthalpy change when one mole of a solute dissolves in a solvent; $\Delta H_{\text{sol}} = \Delta H_{\text{latt diss}} + \Delta H_{\text{hyd}}$

7. Exam Tips

Paper 1 (Multiple Choice): Expect questions testing whether you can identify which Born-Haber step corresponds to a given equation. E.g., "$\ce{K(g) -> K+(g) + e-}$ represents..." Answer: first ionisation energy of potassium. Also, look for questions on the sign of EA₂ — second electron affinity is always endothermic, and this is a favourite trap.
Paper 2 — Born-Haber Calculation: Draw the cycle with clear, labelled arrows. List each step on a separate line with its value and sign. The data booklet gives ionisation energies (Section 8), bond enthalpies (Section 10), and some $\Delta H_f^\ominus$ values (Section 12). You are expected to halve bond enthalpies for diatomic atomisation yourself. Always check: does your final lattice enthalpy have the correct sign (negative for formation, positive for dissociation)?
Paper 2 — Solubility Explanation: When explaining solubility trends, you must discuss both lattice enthalpy and hydration enthalpy. "The lattice enthalpy becomes less exothermic" alone is worth only half the marks — you must compare the relative rates of change of the two factors. Use the phrases "lattice enthalpy decreases more slowly than hydration enthalpy" or vice versa to show you understand the balance.
