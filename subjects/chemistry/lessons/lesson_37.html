Lesson 37: Alkanes & Alkenes — Structure, Reactions & Mechanisms

Syllabus reference: 10.2, 20.2 (Alkanes and Alkenes)

Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to:

  • Describe the structure, bonding ($sp^3$ and $sp^2$ hybridisation), and physical properties of alkanes and alkenes.
  • Explain the free-radical substitution mechanism of alkanes with halogens, including initiation, propagation, and termination steps.
  • Apply Markovnikov's rule to predict the major product of electrophilic addition to unsymmetrical alkenes.
  • Draw curly-arrow mechanisms for electrophilic addition of $\ce{HX}$, $\ce{X2}$, and $\ce{H2O}$ (with $\ce{H+}$ catalyst) to alkenes.
  • Compare the reactivity of alkanes and alkenes in terms of bond energies and reaction types.

1. Prior Knowledge Check

Question 1: What is the general formula of alkanes and alkenes, and what type of bonds does each contain?

▸ Show
Alkanes: $\ce{C_nH_{2n+2}}$, all carbon–carbon bonds are single $\sigma$ bonds ($sp^3$ hybridised). Alkenes: $\ce{C_nH_{2n}}$, contain at least one $\ce{C=C}$ double bond — one $\sigma$ bond and one $\pi$ bond ($sp^2$ hybridised). The $\pi$ bond is weaker than the $\sigma$ bond and is the site of reactivity.

Question 2: What is a "curly arrow" in organic mechanism diagrams, and what does it represent?

▸ Show
A curly arrow represents the movement of an electron pair. It starts from a source of electrons (a lone pair, a $\pi$ bond, or a $\sigma$ bond) and points to where the electrons go (an atom, a bond-forming position). A "fish-hook" arrow (half-headed) represents the movement of a single electron — used only in free-radical mechanisms.

Question 3: What is an electrophile and why do alkenes react with electrophiles?

▸ Show
An electrophile is an electron-deficient species (electron-pair acceptor) that is attracted to regions of high electron density. Alkenes have a $\pi$ bond — a region of high electron density above and below the plane of the molecule. Electrophiles attack the $\pi$ electrons. Examples: $\ce{H+}$, $\ce{Br2}$ (polarised), $\ce{HBr}$.

2. Alkanes — The "Unreactive" Hydrocarbons

Alkanes are saturated hydrocarbons with the general formula $\ce{C_nH_{2n+2}}$. They were once called "paraffins" (Latin: parum affinis = little affinity) because they appeared unreactive. This is only partly true — they are unreactive towards polar reagents but undergo reactions with radicals.

2.1 Structure and Bonding in Alkanes

Every carbon in an alkane is $sp^3$ hybridised — four identical hybrid orbitals arranged tetrahedrally (109.5°). All bonds are $\sigma$ bonds: cylindrically symmetrical around the bond axis with free rotation. This is why alkanes can adopt many conformations (shapes by rotation around single bonds).

Figure 1: Tetrahedral geometry of $sp^3$ hybridised carbon. All four bonds are equivalent $\sigma$ bonds with 109.5° bond angles.

2.2 Free-Radical Substitution (FRS) — The Key Reaction of Alkanes

When alkanes react with halogens (especially $\ce{Cl2}$ or $\ce{Br2}$) in the presence of UV light, a hydrogen atom is replaced by a halogen atom. This is a free-radical chain reaction with three stages.

TERMINATION — two radicals combine, ending chains $\ce{Cl\cdot + Cl\cdot -> Cl2}$ $\ce{CH3\cdot + Cl\cdot -> CH3Cl}$   |   $\ce{CH3\cdot + CH3\cdot -> C2H6}$ Overall: $\ce{CH4 + Cl2 -> CH3Cl + HCl}$ (but further substitution to $\ce{CH2Cl2}$, $\ce{CHCl3}$, $\ce{CCl4}$ is common)

Check-in: Why does bromination of methane require UV light, while addition of $\ce{Br2}$ to ethene occurs instantly in the dark?

▸ Show
Two different mechanisms. Alkanes react via free-radical substitution — UV is needed to homolytically cleave $\ce{Br–Br}$ into $\ce{2 Br\cdot}$ (radical initiator). The $\ce{C–H}$ bond is strong and not polarised — electrophiles and nucleophiles have nothing to attack. Alkenes react via electrophilic addition — the $\pi$ bond is electron-rich and polarises the approaching $\ce{Br2}$ molecule. No UV needed. The reaction type is dictated by the functional group, not the reagent.

3. Alkenes — Electrophilic Addition

Alkenes contain a $\ce{C=C}$ double bond: one $\sigma$ bond (strong, $sp^2$–$sp^2$ overlap) and one $\pi$ bond (weaker, sideways overlap of unhybridised $p$ orbitals). The $\pi$ electrons sit above and below the molecular plane — exposed and available for attack by electrophiles.

3.1 Structure of the $\ce{C=C}$ Double Bond

Figure 3: Bonding in ethene. The $\sigma$ framework keeps the molecule planar; the $\pi$ bond is formed by sideways overlap of unhybridised $p$ orbitals — this creates electron density above and below the plane, making alkenes susceptible to electrophilic attack.

3.2 Electrophilic Addition Mechanism

The general mechanism for electrophilic addition to alkenes involves two steps:

Step 1 (rate-determining): The $\pi$ electrons attack the electrophile, forming a carbocation intermediate and expelling a leaving group or forming a new bond to the electrophile.

Step 2 (fast): A nucleophile (often the counter-ion or solvent) attacks the carbocation, forming the final addition product.

Figure 4: Electrophilic addition of HBr to ethene. Curly arrows show electron-pair movement: $\pi$ electrons → H; H–Br bond electrons → Br. The carbocation is a reactive intermediate — planar, $sp^2$ hybridised, with an empty $p$ orbital.

Check-in: Why does $\ce{Br2}$ addition to ethene require the $\ce{Br–Br}$ bond to be polarised, and how does this happen?

▸ Show
$\ce{Br2}$ is non-polar in isolation. As it approaches the electron-rich $\pi$ bond, the $\pi$ electrons repel the Br electrons, creating an induced dipole: the nearer Br becomes $\ce{Br^{\delta+}}$, the farther becomes $\ce{Br^{\delta-}}$. The $\pi$ electrons attack $\ce{Br^{\delta+}}$, breaking $\ce{Br–Br}$ heterolytically. A bromonium ion (three-membered ring) forms initially, then $\ce{Br-}$ attacks from the opposite side (anti addition).

3.3 Markovnikov's Rule

When $\ce{HX}$ adds to an unsymmetrical alkene, there are two possible products. Markovnikov's rule predicts the major product:

Markovnikov's Rule: In the addition of HX to an unsymmetrical alkene, the hydrogen atom attaches to the carbon that already has more hydrogen atoms (the less substituted carbon). The halogen attaches to the more substituted carbon. This is equivalent to: the more stable carbocation intermediate is formed.
electron-donating (+I effect) — they stabilise the positive charge on the cation. More alkyl groups = more stable cation.
Figure 5: Markovnikov addition to propene. The 2° carbocation (Path A) is more stable than the 1° carbocation (Path B) due to the +I effect of two alkyl groups vs. one. The more stable carbocation forms faster → major product.

Check-in: Predict the major product when $\ce{HCl}$ adds to 2-methylbut-2-ene.

▸ Show
2-methylbut-2-ene is $\ce{(CH3)2C=CHCH3}$. The $\ce{C=C}$ is between C2 (bonded to two methyls + one ethyl-like group) and C3 (bonded to one methyl + one H). The two carbons of the double bond have unequal substitution. Markovnikov: H⁺ adds to C3 (more H atoms there), forming a 3° carbocation at C2 (stabilised by 3 alkyl groups). Cl⁻ then attacks C2. Product: 2-chloro-2-methylbutane $\ce{(CH3)2CClCH2CH3}$.

4. Addition of $\ce{H2O}$ — Hydration of Alkenes

Alkenes react with steam in the presence of an acid catalyst ($\ce{H3PO4}$ or $\ce{H2SO4}$) to form alcohols. This follows Markovnikov's rule. Industrially, this is how ethanol is produced from ethene:

$$\ce{CH2=CH2 + H2O ->[H3PO4][300°C, 60 atm] CH3CH2OH}$$

For unsymmetrical alkenes, the –OH group attaches to the more substituted carbon.

5. Worked Examples

Example 1 — Free-Radical Substitution Product Distribution:

When ethane and chlorine react under UV light, a mixture of products is obtained: $\ce{C2H5Cl}$, $\ce{C2H4Cl2}$, $\ce{C2H3Cl3}$, etc. Explain why.

Answer: The free-radical chain mechanism is non-selective. Once $\ce{C2H5Cl}$ is formed, it can undergo further substitution because $\ce{Cl\cdot}$ radicals attack any $\ce{C–H}$ bond they encounter. The product $\ce{C2H5Cl}$ still has $\ce{C–H}$ bonds — the radicals don't "know" to stop. This is why FRS gives mixtures. In contrast, electrophilic addition stops after one addition because the product (e.g., bromoethane) no longer has a $\pi$ bond to attack.

⚠️ Common wrong approach: Writing $\ce{C2H6 + Cl2 -> C2H5Cl + HCl}$ as the only reaction. IB expects you to recognise that further chlorination occurs and that $\ce{C2H4Cl2}$ (dichloroethane, isomers) and higher chlorinated products are also formed.
Example 2 — Mechanism with Curly Arrows:

Draw the mechanism for the reaction of propene with $\ce{Br2}$ dissolved in $\ce{CCl4}$ (inert solvent). Name the product and state its stereochemistry.

Mechanism: The $\pi$ electrons of propene approach $\ce{Br2}$, inducing a dipole. $\pi$ electrons attack $\ce{Br^{\delta+}}$; $\ce{Br–Br}$ breaks heterolytically. A cyclic bromonium ion (three-membered $\ce{C–C–Br+}$ ring) forms — NOT an open carbocation for $\ce{Br2}$ addition. $\ce{Br-}$ then attacks from the opposite face of the bromonium ion (back-side attack), opening the ring. Product: 1,2-dibromopropane $\ce{CH2BrCHBrCH3}$. The stereochemistry: anti addition — the two Br atoms end up on opposite faces.

⚠️ Common wrong approach: Drawing a carbocation intermediate for $\ce{Br2}$ addition (this is correct for HBr but NOT for $\ce{Br2}$). $\ce{Br2}$ addition proceeds via a bromonium ion, not a carbocation — this is a key distinction IB examiners look for.
Example 3 — Predicting Major Product with Multiple $\pi$ Bonds:

Compound X is $\ce{CH3CH=CHCH2CH=CH2}$ (hexa-1,4-diene). When treated with 1 equivalent of $\ce{HBr}$, what is the major product?

Answer: The two $\ce{C=C}$ bonds are in different chemical environments. C1=C2 is a terminal alkene (monosubstituted). C4=C5 is also disubstituted (both C4 and C5 have one alkyl and one H… wait: C4 is $\ce{–CH=}$, attached to H and $\ce{CH2}$; C5 is $\ce{=CH2}$, terminal). Both are similar. The electrophilic addition is faster at the more electron-rich $\pi$ bond. The product after Markovnikov addition at C4=C5 gives a more stable carbocation. Major product: 4-bromohex-1-ene (addition at C4=C5, Markovnikov).

⚠️ Common wrong approach: Assuming addition always occurs at the terminal alkene because it is "less hindered." Steric effects matter, but carbocation stability is the dominant factor.

⚠️ Common Mistakes to Avoid

  1. Carbocation vs. bromonium ion. $\ce{HX}$ addition to alkenes proceeds via a carbocation intermediate. $\ce{X2}$ (especially $\ce{Br2}$ and $\ce{Cl2}$) addition proceeds via a cyclic halonium ion. Drawing a carbocation for $\ce{Br2}$ addition will lose marks on Paper 2.
  2. Markovnikov's rule is about carbocation stability, not "rich get richer." The H goes to the carbon with more H atoms because that leaves the more substituted (more stable) carbocation. It is a consequence of mechanism, not an arbitrary rule.
  3. Free-radical substitution produces mixtures. Unlike electrophilic addition, FRS is non-selective. The exam expects you to mention that further substitution occurs and that a mixture of mono-, di-, and poly-substituted products forms.
  4. Alkanes are not completely unreactive. They react with halogens under UV — but ONLY by free-radical substitution. They do not react with polar reagents (acids, bases, nucleophiles, electrophiles) because $\ce{C–C}$ and $\ce{C–H}$ $\sigma$ bonds are strong and non-polar.
  5. Curly arrows show electron movement, not atom movement. The arrow starts at the electron source (lone pair, $\pi$ bond) and points to where the electrons end up. Never draw an arrow from a positive charge or from a hydrogen atom.

6. End-of-Lesson Practice

Question 1 (Paper 1 style): Which species is the electrophile in the reaction of ethene with $\ce{Br2}$?
A. $\ce{Br2}$ (unpolarised)   B. $\ce{Br^{\delta+}}$ in polarised $\ce{Br2}$   C. $\ce{Br-}$   D. The $\pi$ electrons of ethene

Question 2: Write the full mechanism (with curly arrows) for the addition of $\ce{HBr}$ to 2-methylpropene $\ce{(CH3)2C=CH2}$.

Question 3: Draw and name all possible products when ethane reacts with excess chlorine under UV light.

Question 4: Predict the major product when $\ce{HI}$ adds to 3-methylbut-1-ene. Justify using carbocation stability.

Question 5: Explain the difference between homolytic and heterolytic bond fission. Give one example of each from alkane/alkene chemistry.

Question 6 (Paper 2 style): But-1-ene and but-2-ene are isomers. (a) Draw both structures. (b) Explain why but-2-ene can exist as E/Z stereoisomers but but-1-ene cannot. (c) Predict the major product when each reacts with $\ce{HBr}$, naming the products.

Answer 1: B. The $\pi$ electrons of ethene polarise the $\ce{Br2}$ molecule, creating $\ce{Br^{\delta+}–Br^{\delta-}}$. The $\pi$ electrons attack $\ce{Br^{\delta+}}$ — the electrophile. $\ce{Br-}$ is the nucleophile in step 2. The $\pi$ electrons are the nucleophile, not the electrophile.

Answer 2: $\ce{(CH3)2C=CH2 + HBr -> (CH3)2CBr–CH3}$ (2-bromo-2-methylpropane, Markovnikov). Step 1: $\pi$ electrons attack $\ce{H^{\delta+}}$ of $\ce{HBr}$; $\ce{Br-}$ leaves. A 3° carbocation $\ce{(CH3)2C+–CH3}$ forms. Step 2: $\ce{Br-}$ attacks the carbocation. Curly arrows: from $\pi$ bond → H; from H–Br bond → Br; from $\ce{Br-}$ lone pair → carbocation C⁺.

Answer 3: Ethane $\ce{C2H6}$ → mono-substitution: $\ce{C2H5Cl}$ (chloroethane). Di-substitution: $\ce{CH3CHCl2}$ (1,1-dichloroethane) and $\ce{CH2ClCH2Cl}$ (1,2-dichloroethane). Tri- through hexa-substitution up to $\ce{C2Cl6}$ (hexachloroethane). All C–H bonds are potential substitution sites.

Answer 4: 3-methylbut-1-ene = $\ce{(CH3)2CHCH=CH2}$. Markovnikov: H⁺ adds to C1 (more H), forming a 2° carbocation at C2 ($\ce{(CH3)2CHC+HCH3}$). However, a hydride shift can occur: 2° → 3° carbocation ($\ce{(CH3)2C+CH2CH3}$) via a 1,2-hydride shift. The 3° carbocation is more stable. $\ce{I-}$ attacks. Major product: 2-iodo-2-methylbutane $\ce{(CH3)2CI–CH2CH3}$. (IB may accept the 2° product if hydride shift is not considered — check context.)

Answer 5: Homolytic fission: Each atom retains one electron from the bond — produces two radicals. Example: $\ce{Cl–Cl ->[hv] 2 Cl\cdot}$ (initiation step of FRS). Heterolytic fission: One atom takes both electrons — produces a cation and an anion. Example: $\ce{H–Br}$ in electrophilic addition → $\ce{H+}$ and $\ce{:Br-}$ (both electrons go to Br).

Answer 6: (a) But-1-ene: $\ce{CH2=CHCH2CH3}$. But-2-ene: $\ce{CH3CH=CHCH3}$. (b) But-2-ene has different groups on each carbon of the C=C (H vs $\ce{CH3}$ on one, $\ce{CH3}$ vs H on the other), so E/Z isomerism is possible. But-1-ene: C1 has two identical H atoms → no E/Z possible. (c) But-1-ene + HBr → $\ce{CH3CHBrCH2CH3}$ (2-bromobutane, Markovnikov). But-2-ene + HBr → $\ce{CH3CHBrCH2CH3}$ (same product — both carbons of C=C are equally substituted, so same carbocation stability).

7. Key Terms

TermDefinition
$sp^3$ hybridisationMixing of one $s$ and three $p$ orbitals to form four equivalent hybrid orbitals arranged tetrahedrally (109.5°); found in alkanes
$sp^2$ hybridisationMixing of one $s$ and two $p$ orbitals to form three hybrid orbitals (120°, trigonal planar) with one unhybridised $p$ orbital for $\pi$ bonding; found in alkenes and carbocations
$\sigma$ bond (sigma)A covalent bond formed by end-on overlap of orbitals; cylindrically symmetrical about the bond axis; allows free rotation
$\pi$ bond (pi)A covalent bond formed by sideways overlap of parallel $p$ orbitals; electron density above and below the molecular plane; prevents rotation
Homolytic fissionBreaking a covalent bond so that each atom retains one electron, producing two free radicals
Heterolytic fissionBreaking a covalent bond so that one atom takes both electrons, producing a cation and an anion
Free radicalA species with an unpaired electron, e.g., $\ce{Cl\cdot}$, $\ce{CH3\cdot}$; highly reactive, produced by homolytic fission
ElectrophileAn electron-pair acceptor; attracted to regions of high electron density ($\pi$ bonds, lone pairs)
NucleophileAn electron-pair donor; attracted to regions of low electron density (positive charges, $\delta+$ atoms)
CarbocationA species with a positively charged carbon bearing only six valence electrons; $sp^2$ hybridised, planar, with an empty $p$ orbital
Markovnikov's ruleIn HX addition to an unsymmetrical alkene, H attaches to the less substituted carbon (more H atoms), X to the more substituted carbon — driven by carbocation stability
Bromonium ionA three-membered cyclic cation $\ce{[C–C–Br]+}$ formed during $\ce{Br2}$ addition to alkenes; leads to anti addition

8. Exam Tips

Paper 1 (Multiple Choice): Carbocation stability ordering (3° > 2° > 1° > methyl) is tested almost every year. Also, "which reagent could be used to distinguish an alkane from an alkene?" — the answer is bromine water (alkane: no reaction in dark; alkene: decolourises instantly). $\ce{Br2/CCl4}$ also works. Know the difference between $\ce{Br2}$ in water vs. $\ce{Br2}$ in $\ce{CCl4}$.
Paper 2 — Mechanism Drawing (4–6 marks): You MUST show: (1) the $\pi$ bond and all relevant lone pairs on the starting material; (2) curly arrows starting from the electron source; (3) the carbocation intermediate (with the + charge on the correct carbon); (4) the curly arrow from the nucleophile to the carbocation; (5) the final product. Miss any of these and you lose marks. Practice until it is automatic.
Free-radical substitution questions: IB examiners look for three distinct stages: initiation (UV + homolytic fission), propagation (two equations, radical consumed AND produced in each), and termination (any two radical–radical combinations). If you forget the termination step, you lose 1–2 marks. Remember: radicals combine in termination; they are not "neutralised" by anything else.
