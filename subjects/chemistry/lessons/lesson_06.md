## 1. Successive Ionisation Energies — Removing Electron After Electron

### What successive ionisation energies are

The first ionisation energy (IE₁) removes the outermost electron from a neutral atom. But after that electron is gone, you can continue to remove electrons — a second, a third, a fourth, and so on. These are called **successive ionisation energies**. Each step is defined by its own chemical equation:

\[
\begin{aligned}
\ce{X(g) &-> X^+(g) + e^-} \quad &\text{First ionisation energy, IE}_1 \\
\ce{X^+(g) &-> X^{2+}(g) + e^-} \quad &\text{Second ionisation energy, IE}_2 \\
\ce{X^{2+}(g) &-> X^{3+}(g) + e^-} \quad &\text{Third ionisation energy, IE}_3
\end{aligned}
\]

Notice that each successive step removes an electron from an ion that is one charge more positive than in the previous step. IE₂ removes an electron from X⁺ (a 1+ ion). IE₃ removes an electron from X²⁺ (a 2+ ion). And so on.

### Why every successive IE is larger than the previous one

For any element, IE₂ is always larger than IE₁, IE₃ is always larger than IE₂, and so on. There are two reinforcing reasons for this:

1. **Increasing positive charge on the ion.** After the first electron is removed, the remaining electrons are held by a species with a net positive charge (X⁺). The same number of protons is now attracting fewer electrons, so each remaining electron experiences a greater effective nuclear charge. Removing a second electron from an already positive ion requires more energy than removing the first from a neutral atom.

2. **Decreasing electron-electron repulsion.** With fewer electrons remaining, there is less repulsion among them. The remaining electrons can be drawn closer to the nucleus, which increases the attractive force they experience.

Together, these effects mean that successive ionisation energies always increase. The question is: do they increase smoothly, or are there sudden, dramatic jumps?

### The key insight: big jumps reveal electron shell structure

The successive ionisation energies of an element do not increase at a constant rate. There are places where the increase from one IE to the next is enormous — often by a factor of 5, 10, or even more. These huge jumps occur when you have removed all the electrons from the outermost shell and must now remove an electron from an inner shell. Inner-shell electrons are much closer to the nucleus and are shielded by far fewer electrons, so they are much more tightly held.

Consider sodium (Na), which has the electron configuration 1s² 2s² 2p⁶ 3s¹:

| IE₁ | IE₂ | IE₃ | IE₄ |
|-----|-----|-----|-----|
| 496 | 4562 | 6910 | 9543 |

The jump from IE₁ (496) to IE₂ (4562) is massive — IE₂ is more than 9 times IE₁. IE₁ removes the single 3s electron, which is far from the nucleus and shielded by 10 inner electrons. IE₂ must remove a 2p electron from the stable [Ne] core of Na⁺. The 2p electron is in an inner shell, much closer to the nucleus, and experiences far less shielding. Hence the enormous increase.

This pattern — one relatively low IE followed by a huge jump — immediately tells you that sodium has **one valence electron** and therefore belongs to Group 1.

### Worked Example 1: Identifying an Element's Group from Successive IE Data

**Problem:** An element X has the following successive ionisation energies, all measured in kJ mol⁻¹: IE₁ = 578, IE₂ = 1817, IE₃ = 2745, IE₄ = 11578, IE₅ = 14831. Determine the group number of element X.

**Approach:** Calculate the ratio of each IE to the previous one. The position of the largest ratio tells you how many valence electrons the element has.

**Solution:**
- IE₂ / IE₁ = 1817 / 578 ≈ 3.1
- IE₃ / IE₂ = 2745 / 1817 ≈ 1.5
- IE₄ / IE₃ = 11578 / 2745 ≈ 4.2 ← This is the largest jump
- IE₅ / IE₄ = 14831 / 11578 ≈ 1.3

The largest jump occurs between IE₃ and IE₄. This means the first three electrons are relatively easy to remove (they are valence electrons, all in the same outer shell), but the fourth electron is much harder (it comes from an inner shell). Therefore, element X has **3 valence electrons** and belongs to **Group 13** (also called Group 3 in older notation).

**Why this makes sense:** This element is aluminium (Al), which has the configuration [Ne] 3s² 3p¹. The first three ionisation energies remove the three n = 3 electrons (the two 3s electrons and the one 3p electron). The fourth ionisation energy must break into the n = 2 shell (the 2p⁶ sub-level), which is much closer to the nucleus and far more tightly held.

## 2. A Systematic Method for Analysing Successive Ionisation Energies

### Step-by-step procedure

When you are given a table of successive ionisation energies and asked to identify the group or the element, follow these steps:

1. **Write down all the ionisation energies in order.**

2. **Calculate the ratio of each IE to the previous one.** For example, IE₂/IE₁, IE₃/IE₂, IE₄/IE₃, and so on. The ratio tells you how much harder it is to remove the next electron compared to the previous one.

3. **Identify the largest ratio.** This is the point where you transition from removing valence electrons (outer shell) to removing core electrons (inner shell). The jump is usually obvious — ratios of 3 or higher typically indicate a shell change, while ratios below about 2 indicate electrons coming from the same shell.

4. **Count the number of ionisation energies before the big jump.** This number equals the number of valence electrons, which (for main-group elements in Groups 1, 2, and 13 through 18) equals the group number. For example, if the big jump comes between IE₂ and IE₃, the element has 2 valence electrons and is in Group 2.

5. **If the period is also given**, you can identify the specific element by combining the group number with the period number.

### Worked Example 2: Identifying a Specific Element

**Problem:** The successive ionisation energies of element Y, in kJ mol⁻¹, are: IE₁ = 738, IE₂ = 1451, IE₃ = 7733, IE₄ = 10543. Identify the group and, given that Y is in Period 3, name the element.

**Approach:** Follow the systematic procedure.

**Solution:**
- IE₂ / IE₁ = 1451 / 738 ≈ 2.0
- IE₃ / IE₂ = 7733 / 1451 ≈ 5.3 ← Large jump
- IE₄ / IE₃ = 10543 / 7733 ≈ 1.4

The largest jump occurs between IE₂ and IE₃. This means the first two electrons are valence electrons and the third comes from an inner shell. So Y has 2 valence electrons and is in **Group 2**. In Period 3, Group 2 is **magnesium (Mg)**.

**Why this makes sense:** Magnesium has the configuration [Ne] 3s². The first two ionisation energies remove the two 3s electrons. The third must break into the 2p⁶ inner shell, requiring much more energy.

## 3. Detailed Explanation of the Period 2 Ionisation Energy Anomalies

### Why the anomalies matter

Lesson 5 introduced the two anomalies in first ionisation energy across a period: the drop from beryllium to boron and the drop from nitrogen to oxygen. In this section, we examine the Period 2 anomalies in greater depth, using electron configurations and orbital theory to provide complete explanations. IB exam questions at Higher Level often ask you to explain these anomalies in detail.

The first ionisation energies of the Period 2 elements, in kJ mol⁻¹, are:

| Li | Be | B | C | N | O | F | Ne |
|-----|-----|-----|-----|-----|-----|-----|------|
| 520 | 900 | 801 | 1086 | 1402 | 1314 | 1681 | 2081 |

### Anomaly 1: Beryllium (900) to Boron (801) — A Drop of 99 kJ mol⁻¹

**Beryllium** has the electron configuration 1s² 2s². The electron being removed in the first ionisation is a **2s electron**. The 2s orbital is relatively low in energy, and its spherical shape keeps the electron relatively close to the nucleus on average.

**Boron** has the electron configuration 1s² 2s² 2p¹. The electron being removed is a **2p electron**. The 2p orbital is higher in energy than the 2s orbital. This is a fundamental property of orbitals: within the same shell (n = 2), the 2p sub-level lies at a higher energy than the 2s sub-level. Additionally, the dumbbell shape of the p orbital means the electron spends more time further from the nucleus than a 2s electron does.

Although boron has one more proton than beryllium (Z = 5 vs Z = 4), the fact that the electron is being removed from a higher-energy, more distant 2p orbital rather than a lower-energy 2s orbital outweighs the increased nuclear attraction. The net result is that the electron in boron is slightly easier to remove.

**Key phrase for IB answers:** "The electron removed from boron is in the 2p sub-level, which is higher in energy than the 2s sub-level from which beryllium's electron is removed. The higher energy of the 2p orbital outweighs the increase in nuclear charge."

### Anomaly 2: Nitrogen (1402) to Oxygen (1314) — A Drop of 88 kJ mol⁻¹

**Nitrogen** has the electron configuration 1s² 2s² 2p³. In the 2p sub-level, there are three electrons, and according to Hund's rule, each occupies a separate p orbital with parallel spin. This arrangement — one electron per orbital, all with the same spin — is called a **half-filled sub-level**. It is particularly stable because of a quantum mechanical effect called **exchange energy**. Exchange energy is the stabilisation that arises when electrons with parallel spins occupy different orbitals in the same sub-level; the more such parallel-spin electrons, the greater the stabilisation.

**Oxygen** has the electron configuration 1s² 2s² 2p⁴. The fourth electron in the 2p sub-level cannot occupy an empty orbital because all three p orbitals already have one electron each. It must pair up in an orbital that already contains an electron. When two electrons occupy the same orbital, they experience **electron-electron repulsion** because they are confined to the same small region of space. This repulsion raises the energy of the paired electrons and makes one of them easier to remove.

Even though oxygen has one more proton than nitrogen (Z = 8 vs Z = 7), the repulsion between the paired 2p electrons outweighs the small increase in nuclear attraction. The result is that oxygen has a slightly lower first ionisation energy than nitrogen.

**Key phrase for IB answers:** "In oxygen, one of the 2p orbitals contains a pair of electrons. The repulsion between these paired electrons makes one of them easier to remove than an unpaired electron from nitrogen's half-filled 2p sub-level. This electron-electron repulsion outweighs the increased nuclear charge."

### Worked Example 3: A Complete 6-Mark IB-Style Explanation

**Problem:** Explain the trend in first ionisation energy across Period 2 from lithium to neon. Your answer should include the general trend as well as explanations for both anomalies. (6 marks)

**Approach:** Structure the answer in three parts: the general trend and its cause, the beryllium-to-boron anomaly, and the nitrogen-to-oxygen anomaly.

**Solution:**

The first ionisation energy generally increases from lithium (520 kJ mol⁻¹) to neon (2081 kJ mol⁻¹). This increase occurs because the nuclear charge rises from +3 in lithium to +10 in neon, while all the electrons being added are placed in the same shell (n = 2), so shielding remains approximately constant. The increasing nuclear charge, with no increase in shielding, means the outer electrons are held progressively more tightly.

There are, however, two decreases that interrupt the general upward trend. The first decrease occurs between beryllium (900 kJ mol⁻¹) and boron (801 kJ mol⁻¹). Beryllium loses a 2s electron, whereas boron loses a 2p electron. The 2p sub-level is higher in energy than the 2s sub-level, so the 2p electron in boron is easier to remove than the 2s electron in beryllium. This effect outweighs the increase in nuclear charge from +4 to +5.

The second decrease occurs between nitrogen (1402 kJ mol⁻¹) and oxygen (1314 kJ mol⁻¹). Nitrogen has a half-filled 2p sub-level (2p³) in which each of the three p orbitals contains a single unpaired electron with parallel spin — an arrangement with extra stability. Oxygen (2p⁴) has one p orbital containing a pair of electrons. The repulsion between these paired electrons makes one of them easier to remove than an unpaired electron from nitrogen's half-filled sub-level. This electron-electron repulsion outweighs the increase in nuclear charge from +7 to +8.

## 4. Successive Ionisation Energies and Electron Configuration

### How IE data reveals electron configuration

The pattern of successive ionisation energies does more than just identify the group — it can reveal the detailed electron configuration of an element. Each "plateau" of relatively small increases corresponds to electrons being removed from the same sub-level or same shell. Each enormous jump corresponds to breaking into a new, inner shell.

For a main-group element like silicon (Si, Z = 14, configuration [Ne] 3s² 3p²), the first four ionisation energies remove the four n = 3 valence electrons (3p² and then 3s²). The increases among IE₁ through IE₄ are moderate because all four electrons come from the same shell. The fifth ionisation energy breaks into the n = 2 shell (2p⁶), causing a dramatic jump.

### Worked Example 4: Deducing Configuration from IE Data

**Problem:** The first eight ionisation energies of an unknown element are shown below (all values in kJ mol⁻¹). Identify the element and write its electron configuration.

| IE₁ | IE₂ | IE₃ | IE₄ | IE₅ | IE₆ | IE₇ | IE₈ |
|-----|-----|-----|-----|-----|-----|-----|-----|
| 1314 | 3388 | 5301 | 7469 | 10990 | 13327 | 71330 | 84080 |

**Approach:** Calculate successive ratios to find the big jump, then count valence electrons.

**Solution:**
- IE₂/IE₁ = 3388/1314 ≈ 2.6
- IE₃/IE₂ = 5301/3388 ≈ 1.6
- IE₄/IE₃ = 7469/5301 ≈ 1.4
- IE₅/IE₄ = 10990/7469 ≈ 1.5
- IE₆/IE₅ = 13327/10990 ≈ 1.2
- IE₇/IE₆ = 71330/13327 ≈ 5.4 ← Large jump
- IE₈/IE₇ = 84080/71330 ≈ 1.2

The large jump is between IE₆ and IE₇. This means the first six electrons are valence electrons and the seventh comes from an inner shell. Six valence electrons places the element in **Group 16** (also called Group 6). The element is **oxygen (O)**.

Electron configuration: the six valence electrons are 2s² 2p⁴, above the 1s² core. Full configuration: **1s² 2s² 2p⁴**.

**Why this makes sense:** Oxygen has 8 electrons total. The first six IEs remove the six n = 2 electrons (2s² 2p⁴) — these are relatively moderate in value. The seventh IE must remove a 1s electron from the K shell — the innermost, most tightly held electrons. Hence the massive jump from 13327 to 71330.

## 5. Applying Successive IE Analysis to Multiple Elements

### Comparing elements in the same period

When you are given successive IE data for multiple elements — especially elements in the same period — you can use the data to identify each element and to make predictions about relative values. This is a common higher-level IB exam task.

Consider the Period 3 elements and the large-jump pattern for each:

- Na ([Ne] 3s¹): 1 valence electron → big jump after IE₁
- Mg ([Ne] 3s²): 2 valence electrons → big jump after IE₂
- Al ([Ne] 3s² 3p¹): 3 valence electrons → big jump after IE₃
- Si ([Ne] 3s² 3p²): 4 valence electrons → big jump after IE₄
- P ([Ne] 3s² 3p³): 5 valence electrons → big jump after IE₅
- S ([Ne] 3s² 3p⁴): 6 valence electrons → big jump after IE₆
- Cl ([Ne] 3s² 3p⁵): 7 valence electrons → big jump after IE₇
- Ar ([Ne] 3s² 3p⁶): 8 valence electrons → big jump after IE₈

### Worked Example 5: Identifying Elements from Successive IE

**Problem:** An element from Period 3 has the following successive ionisation energies (in kJ mol⁻¹): IE₁ = 1012, IE₂ = 1907, IE₃ = 2914, IE₄ = 4964, IE₅ = 6274, IE₆ = 21267. Identify the element and explain your reasoning.

**Approach:** Find the large jump. The ratio IE₆/IE₅ = 21267/6274 ≈ 3.4. This is the largest jump, and it occurs between IE₅ and IE₆.

**Solution:** The large jump between IE₅ and IE₆ means the first five electrons are valence electrons and the sixth comes from an inner shell. Five valence electrons means the element is in **Group 15** (older notation: Group 5). In Period 3, Group 15 is **phosphorus (P)**.

**Verification:** Phosphorus has the configuration [Ne] 3s² 3p³. The first five ionisation energies remove the five n = 3 valence electrons (3p³ and 3s²). The sixth ionisation energy must break into the n = 2 shell (2p⁶), which is much more tightly held, hence the big jump.

**Why this makes sense:** The IE values themselves are also informative. IE₁ of phosphorus is 1012 kJ mol⁻¹, which matches the period trend — phosphorus has a relatively high IE₁ because of its half-filled 3p³ configuration.

## Answers

**Answer 1:** (a) Calculate the ratios: IE₂/IE₁ = 1145/590 ≈ 1.9. IE₃/IE₂ = 4912/1145 ≈ 4.3. This is the largest jump, occurring between IE₂ and IE₃. The first two electrons are valence electrons and the third comes from an inner shell. Therefore, the element has 2 valence electrons and belongs to Group 2.

(b) In Period 4, Group 2, the element is calcium (Ca, Z = 20). The electron configuration of calcium is [Ar] 4s², which is consistent with having two valence electrons.

---

**Answer 2:** The second ionisation energy (IE₂) is always larger than the first ionisation energy (IE₁) because IE₁ removes an electron from a neutral atom (X), whereas IE₂ removes an electron from a positively charged ion (X⁺). After the first electron has been removed, the remaining electrons are held by an ion with a net charge of +1. The same number of protons is now attracting one fewer electron, so each remaining electron experiences a greater effective nuclear charge. With this stronger attraction, more energy is required to remove the second electron. Additionally, there is one fewer electron contributing to electron-electron repulsion, allowing the remaining electrons to be drawn closer to the nucleus, which further increases the attraction.

**Answer 3:** (a) Silicon (Z = 14) has the electron configuration: 1s² 2s² 2p⁶ 3s² 3p², or [Ne] 3s² 3p² in condensed notation.

(b) The first four ionisation energies of silicon remove the four valence electrons from the n = 3 shell (first the two 3p electrons, then the two 3s electrons). Because all four of these electrons come from the same shell, the increases from IE₁ to IE₂, IE₂ to IE₃, and IE₃ to IE₄ are relatively moderate. The fifth ionisation energy, however, must remove an electron from the n = 2 shell (specifically, from the 2p⁶ sub-level of the [Ne] core). Electrons in the n = 2 shell are much closer to the nucleus than those in the n = 3 shell and experience far less shielding. The energy required to remove such a tightly held inner-shell electron is vastly greater, which is why the increase from IE₄ (4356) to IE₅ (16091) is so dramatic — an increase of more than 11,700 kJ mol⁻¹, or nearly a factor of 4.

**Answer 5:** (a) Element P: IE₁ = 496, then a huge jump to IE₂ = 4563. The jump after IE₁ means 1 valence electron → Group 1. Period 3, Group 1 → **sodium (Na)**.

Element Q: IE₁ = 738, IE₂ = 1451, then a large jump to IE₃ = 7733. The jump after IE₂ means 2 valence electrons → Group 2. Period 3, Group 2 → **magnesium (Mg)**.

Element R: IE₁ = 578, IE₂ = 1817, IE₃ = 2745, then a large jump to IE₄ = 11578. The jump after IE₃ means 3 valence electrons → Group 13. Period 3, Group 13 → **aluminium (Al)**.

(b) For all three elements, IE₂ removes an electron from a positively charged ion (X⁺) rather than from a neutral atom. The ion has the same nuclear charge as the neutral atom but one fewer electron, so the remaining electrons experience a greater effective nuclear attraction. This makes the second electron harder to remove than the first, giving IE₂ > IE₁ for every element.

(c) For element R (aluminium, [Ne] 3s² 3p¹), the first three ionisation energies all remove electrons from the n = 3 shell. IE₁ removes the 3p¹ electron, and IE₂ and IE₃ remove the two 3s² electrons. Because all three electrons come from the same principal shell (n = 3), the increases from IE₁ to IE₂ and from IE₂ to IE₃ are relatively modest. However, IE₄ must remove an electron from the n = 2 shell — specifically from the 2p⁶ sub-level of the neon core. This inner-shell electron is much closer to the nucleus, experiences far less shielding, and is held enormously more tightly. Hence the increase from IE₃ to IE₄ is dramatically larger than the increase from IE₂ to IE₃.

(d) Element Q (magnesium) would have the larger IE₃ value. The reason lies in their electron configurations. For magnesium ([Ne] 3s²), IE₃ removes the third electron, but magnesium has only two valence electrons. Therefore, magnesium's IE₃ must remove an electron from the n = 2 shell (the 2p⁶ inner core), which is extremely tightly held. For aluminium ([Ne] 3s² 3p¹), IE₃ removes the third valence electron from the still-accessible n = 3 shell (the final 3s electron). Removing an n = 3 electron is far easier than removing an n = 2 electron. In fact, the measured values confirm this: Mg IE₃ = 7733 kJ mol⁻¹ and Al IE₃ = 2745 kJ mol⁻¹. Magnesium's IE₃ is nearly three times larger because it must break into a noble gas core, while aluminium's IE₃ simply removes another valence electron.
