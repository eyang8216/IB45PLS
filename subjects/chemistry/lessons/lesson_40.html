Lesson 40: Benzene & Electrophilic Substitution (HL)

Syllabus reference: 20.1–20.2 (Aromatic Chemistry — HL only)

Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to:

  • Describe the delocalised $\pi$-electron system of benzene and explain the evidence against the Kekulé structure.
  • Calculate and interpret the delocalisation (resonance) energy of benzene from thermodynamic data.
  • Draw the general mechanism for electrophilic aromatic substitution ($\ce{S_EAr}$) including the Wheland intermediate.
  • Write mechanisms and equations for nitration, halogenation, Friedel–Crafts alkylation, and Friedel–Crafts acylation of benzene.
  • Explain why benzene undergoes substitution rather than addition, despite its unsaturation.

1. Prior Knowledge Check

Question 1: Benzene has the molecular formula $\ce{C6H6}$. Why was this formula problematic for 19th-century chemists trying to determine its structure?

▸ Show
With six carbons and only six hydrogens, benzene is highly unsaturated. For a comparable alkane ($\ce{C6H14}$), it is "missing" 8 hydrogen atoms. Chemists initially proposed structures with triple bonds or highly strained rings, but none matched benzene's chemical behaviour: it did NOT undergo addition reactions (decolourising bromine water) like alkenes, yet it was clearly unsaturated. The Kekulé structure (alternating single and double bonds in a six-membered ring, 1865) was the best fit — but it predicted two different 1,2-disubstituted isomers, which were never observed.

Question 2: What is the difference between an addition reaction (like alkene + $\ce{Br2}$) and a substitution reaction (like benzene + $\ce{Br2}$)?

▸ Show
Addition: atoms add across a $\pi$ bond; the unsaturation decreases; the carbon skeleton adds new atoms. Ethene + $\ce{Br2}$ → 1,2-dibromoethane. Substitution: one atom/group replaces another; the carbon skeleton and degree of unsaturation remain unchanged. Benzene + $\ce{Br2}$ → bromobenzene + $\ce{HBr}$ (Br replaces H; the aromatic ring is preserved). Benzene undergoes substitution because addition would destroy the aromaticity (delocalised $\pi$ system) — a large energetic penalty (~150 kJ mol⁻¹).

Question 3: What does "delocalisation" mean, and how does it affect the stability of a molecule?

▸ Show
Delocalisation means electrons are spread over more than two atoms — they are not confined to a single bond or lone pair site. This lowers the overall energy of the molecule. In benzene, six $\pi$ electrons are delocalised over all six carbon atoms in a continuous ring. This delocalisation energy (~150 kJ mol⁻¹) is why benzene is far more stable than the hypothetical "cyclohexatriene" with localised double bonds.

2. The Structure of Benzene — Evidence vs. Kekulé

Benzene, $\ce{C6H6}$, is the prototypical aromatic compound. Its structure was a major puzzle in 19th-century chemistry. The currently accepted model is based on molecular orbital theory: six $sp^2$ hybridised carbons form a planar ring, each contributing one unhybridised $p$ orbital to a continuous $\pi$-electron cloud above and below the plane.

2.2 The Delocalised $\pi$ System

Figure 2: The delocalised $\pi$ system of benzene. Six $p$ orbitals (one from each $sp^2$ carbon) overlap sideways to form a continuous $\pi$ cloud. The 6 $\pi$ electrons are fully delocalised — they belong to the ring as a whole, not to individual double bonds.

Check-in: Calculate the resonance energy of benzene given: $\Delta H_{hyd}$ (cyclohexene) = −120 kJ mol⁻¹; $\Delta H_{hyd}$ (benzene, experimental) = −208 kJ mol⁻¹.

▸ Show
Kekulé (cyclohexatriene) would have 3 isolated C=C bonds: predicted $\Delta H_{hyd}$ = 3 × (−120) = −360 kJ mol⁻¹. Experimental = −208 kJ mol⁻¹. Resonance (delocalisation) energy = |−360 − (−208)| = 152 kJ mol⁻¹. Benzene is 152 kJ mol⁻¹ more stable than the hypothetical Kekulé structure. This is the energy that would be lost if benzene underwent addition — explaining why it prefers substitution.

3. Electrophilic Aromatic Substitution ($\ce{S_EAr}$) — The General Mechanism

Benzene does NOT undergo electrophilic addition (like alkenes). Instead, it undergoes electrophilic aromatic substitution ($\ce{S_EAr}$) — the electrophile replaces a hydrogen while preserving the aromatic ring.

Wheland intermediate (arenium ion, $\sigma$ complex) Positive charge delocalised over 5 carbons Aromaticity is lost (but only temporarily) Step 2: Deprotonation (fast) $\ce{[C6H6E]+ -> C6H5E + H+}$ Base (often $\ce{FeBr4-}$ or $\ce{HSO4-}$) removes $\ce{H+}$ Aromaticity is restored Overall: $\ce{C6H6 + E+ -> C6H5E + H+}$ Wheland Intermediate Structure ⊕ E H $sp^3$ carbon at site of attack + charge delocalised by resonance over ortho and para positions (3 contributing resonance structures)
Figure 3: The general $S_EAr$ mechanism. The electrophile $\ce{E+}$ is generated in situ. Attack by the $\pi$ electrons forms a Wheland intermediate (non-aromatic, cationic). Loss of $\ce{H+}$ restores aromaticity — the driving force for substitution over addition.

Check-in: Why is a halogen carrier ($\ce{FeBr3}$ or $\ce{AlCl3}$) needed for benzene halogenation, but not for alkene halogenation?

▸ Show
Alkenes have localised $\pi$ electrons that are highly reactive — they can polarise $\ce{Br2}$ by induced dipole, leading to electrophilic addition without a catalyst. Benzene has delocalised $\pi$ electrons — they are stabilised by aromaticity (152 kJ mol⁻¹). The $\pi$ electrons are not sufficiently reactive to polarise $\ce{Br2}$ and initiate electrophilic attack. The halogen carrier ($\ce{FeBr3}$, a Lewis acid) polarises $\ce{Br–Br}$ by accepting a lone pair, generating the much stronger electrophile $\ce{Br+}$ (effectively). This stronger electrophile can overcome the aromatic stabilisation barrier.

4. Why Substitution, Not Addition?

This is the fundamental difference between benzene and alkenes — and a classic IB question.

If benzene underwent addition (e.g., $\ce{C6H6 + Br2 -> C6H6Br2}$), the product would be a non-aromatic cyclohexadiene derivative. The aromatic stabilisation energy (~152 kJ mol⁻¹) would be lost permanently. In substitution ($\ce{C6H6 + Br2 -> C6H5Br + HBr}$), aromaticity is only temporarily disrupted (Wheland intermediate) and then regained in the product. The energy "cost" of the Wheland intermediate is repaid when aromaticity is restored.

5. Worked Examples

Example 1 — Drawing the nitration mechanism (Paper 2 classic):

Draw the full mechanism (curly arrows) for the nitration of benzene to form nitrobenzene.

Step 0 (electrophile generation): $\ce{HNO3 + 2H2SO4 -> NO2+ + 2HSO4- + H3O+}$. The nitronium ion $\ce{NO2+}$ is the electrophile ($\ce{O=N+=O}$).
Step 1: $\pi$ electrons of benzene (curly arrow from the ring) attack $\ce{N+}$ of $\ce{NO2+}$. Curly arrow from $\ce{N=O}$ $\pi$ bond → $\ce{O}$ (to maintain octet). Wheland intermediate forms: $\ce{C6H6NO2+}$, with $\ce{NO2}$ attached to one $sp^3$ carbon and positive charge delocalised over ortho/para positions.
Step 2: $\ce{HSO4-}$ (the base) removes $\ce{H+}$ from the $sp^3$ carbon. Curly arrow from $\ce{C–H}$ $\sigma$ bond → ring (restoring $\pi$ system). Product: nitrobenzene $\ce{C6H5NO2}$ + $\ce{H2SO4}$ (regenerated).

⚠️ Common wrong approach: Forgetting to draw the generation of $\ce{NO2+}$ (step 0), or drawing $\ce{NO2+}$ attacking with the oxygen rather than the nitrogen. The electrophile is $\ce{NO2+}$ with the positive charge on nitrogen.
Example 2 — Comparing F–C alkylation vs. acylation:

Explain why Friedel–Crafts acylation is preferred over alkylation for introducing a 3-carbon side chain to benzene, if the desired product is propylbenzene.

F–C Alkylation directly: $\ce{C6H6 + CH3CH2CH2Cl ->[AlCl3] C6H5CH2CH2CH3}$ + HCl. Problem: the 1° carbocation $\ce{CH3CH2CH2+}$ can rearrange to the more stable 2° carbocation $\ce{CH3CH+CH3}$ → isopropylbenzene (major product), not propylbenzene. Also, polyalkylation is common.
F–C Acylation then reduction: Step 1: $\ce{C6H6 + CH3CH2COCl ->[AlCl3] C6H5COCH2CH3}$ (ethyl phenyl ketone). The acylium ion $\ce{CH3CH2CO+}$ is resonance-stabilised — NO rearrangement. Step 2: $\ce{C6H5COCH2CH3}$ is reduced using $\ce{Zn(Hg)/HCl}$ (Clemmensen) or $\ce{NH2NH2/KOH}$ (Wolff–Kishner) to give propylbenzene. Clean, no rearrangement, no polyacylation.

⚠️ Common wrong approach: Assuming FC alkylation always gives the straight-chain product. Carbocation rearrangements are a major limitation of FC alkylation.
Example 3 — Substitution vs. addition with thermodynamic numbers:

The enthalpy of hydrogenation of cyclohexene is −120 kJ mol⁻¹. The enthalpy of hydrogenation of benzene (to cyclohexane) is −208 kJ mol⁻¹. Cyclohexane → benzene + $\ce{3H2}$ has $\Delta H = +208$ kJ mol⁻¹. Using these numbers, explain why benzene does NOT undergo electrophilic addition of $\ce{Br2}$.

Analysis: Addition of $\ce{Br2}$ across one C=C of a hypothetical Kekulé benzene would be exothermic by roughly −120 kJ mol⁻¹ (analogous to cyclohexene). However, in REAL benzene, addition would destroy aromaticity — costing 152 kJ mol⁻¹ (the resonance energy). The net $\Delta H$ for addition would be approximately +32 kJ mol⁻¹ (endothermic). Substitution avoids this: the Wheland intermediate costs ~60 kJ mol⁻¹ (less than full aromaticity loss), and this is recovered when $\ce{H+}$ is lost and aromaticity is restored. The aromatic ring is preserved — the thermodynamic driving force.

⚠️ Common wrong approach: Saying "benzene doesn't react with $\ce{Br2}$." It does react — but only in the presence of a halogen carrier ($\ce{FeBr3}$), and it undergoes substitution, not addition. The distinction is crucial.

⚠️ Common Mistakes to Avoid

  1. Benzene undergoes substitution, NOT addition. This is the single most tested concept. Benzene + $\ce{Br2}$ → NO REACTION in the dark. With $\ce{FeBr3}$ → bromobenzene + HBr (substitution). Alkenes + $\ce{Br2}$ → instant decolourisation (addition). Know this difference cold.
  2. $\ce{FeBr3}$ (or $\ce{AlCl3}$) is a CATALYST — it is regenerated. The overall equation is $\ce{C6H6 + Br2 -> C6H5Br + HBr}$. $\ce{FeBr3}$ does not appear in the stoichiometric equation. The mechanism shows $\ce{FeBr4- + H+ -> FeBr3 + HBr}$ in the last step.
  3. The Wheland intermediate is NOT aromatic. It has an $sp^3$ carbon and only 4 $\pi$ electrons delocalised over 5 carbons (Hückel: 4 ≠ $4n+2$). Aromaticity is temporarily lost but regained in the product.
  4. Friedel–Crafts alkylation has limitations. Carbocations rearrange (1° → 2° → 3°). Polyalkylation occurs because the product (alkylbenzene) is more reactive than benzene (alkyl group is activating). Acylation avoids both problems.
  5. Hückel's rule: $4n+2$ $\pi$ electrons. Not "6 electrons makes it aromatic." Cyclopentadienyl anion ($\ce{C5H5-}$, 6 $\pi$ e⁻) and cycloheptatrienyl cation ($\ce{C7H7+}$, 6 $\pi$ e⁻) are also aromatic. $4n+2$ is the general rule.

Question 6 (Paper 2 style): Propose a synthesis of 4-nitroethylbenzene from benzene, showing all reagents, conditions, and the order of reactions. Explain why the order matters.

Answer 1: B. All C–C bonds in benzene are identical (139 pm, intermediate between single and double). A is wrong — benzene does NOT decolourise $\ce{Br2}$ water without a catalyst. C is wrong — the $\pi$ electrons are delocalised, not localised in three alternating double bonds. D is wrong — benzene is perfectly planar (all carbons $sp^2$).

Answer 2: Electrophile generation: $\ce{Cl2 + AlCl3 -> Cl+ + AlCl4-}$. Curly arrow from Cl–Cl bond to Al (forming Cl–Al bond); the other Cl becomes $\ce{Cl+}$. Step 1: $\pi$ electrons of benzene attack $\ce{Cl+}$ → Wheland intermediate with Cl on an $sp^3$ carbon, + charge delocalised. Step 2: $\ce{AlCl4-}$ removes $\ce{H+}$ → chlorobenzene $\ce{C6H5Cl + AlCl3 + HCl}$. $\ce{AlCl3}$ regenerated.

Answer 3: The predicted value (−360 kJ mol⁻¹) assumes three isolated C=C bonds (like cyclohexene × 3). But benzene is NOT cyclohexatriene — its $\pi$ electrons are delocalised. The delocalisation (resonance) energy = 360 − 208 = 152 kJ mol⁻¹. This is the extra stability from delocalisation. Because benzene is 152 kJ mol⁻¹ more stable than predicted, hydrogenation releases 152 kJ mol⁻¹ less energy.

Answer 4: (a) $\ce{C6H6 + HNO3 ->[H2SO4][50°C] C6H5NO2 + H2O}$. (b) $\ce{C6H6 + CH3COCl ->[AlCl3] C6H5COCH3 + HCl}$. (c) $\ce{C6H5COCH3 ->[Zn(Hg)][HCl,\Delta] C6H5CH2CH3}$ (Clemmensen reduction).

Answer 5: Cyclooctatetraene has 8 $\pi$ electrons: $4n+2 = 8 \implies 4n = 6 \implies n = 1.5$ (not an integer). So Hückel's rule predicts non-aromatic. Structurally, cyclooctatetraene is tub-shaped (not planar) — the $p$ orbitals cannot overlap effectively for delocalisation. It behaves as a typical polyene: undergoes addition reactions with $\ce{Br2}$, has alternating single and double bond lengths.

Answer 6: Target: 4-nitroethylbenzene (nitro and ethyl groups para to each other). Route: (1) FC acylation: $\ce{C6H6 + CH3COCl ->[AlCl3] C6H5COCH3}$ (acetophenone). The –COCH₃ group is meta-directing. Wait — need para product. Actually, ethyl is ortho/para-directing. So: (1) FC alkylation/acylation to introduce ethyl first? Better: FC acylation then reduction → ethylbenzene (ethyl is o/p-directing). (2) Nitration: $\ce{C6H5CH2CH3 + HNO3 ->[H2SO4]}$ → mixture of ortho and para nitroethylbenzene. Para is major (less steric hindrance). Order matters: if you nitrate first, the –NO₂ group is meta-directing, and FC alkylation would give the meta isomer. The activating o/p-directing group must be introduced first.

7. Key Terms

TermDefinition
Aromatic compoundA cyclic, planar, fully conjugated molecule with $4n+2$ $\pi$ electrons (Hückel's rule), exhibiting exceptional stability
DelocalisationSpreading of $\pi$ electrons over more than two atoms; in benzene, 6 $\pi$ electrons are delocalised over 6 carbons
Resonance energyThe extra stability of a molecule due to electron delocalisation; for benzene ≈ 152 kJ mol⁻¹ (difference between predicted and actual $\Delta H_{hyd}$)
Electrophilic aromatic substitution ($S_EAr$)Two-step mechanism: (1) $\pi$ electrons attack $\ce{E+}$ → Wheland intermediate, (2) loss of $\ce{H+}$ → aromatic product
Wheland intermediate (arenium ion, $\sigma$ complex)The non-aromatic cationic intermediate in $S_EAr$; positive charge delocalised but aromaticity is lost; one $sp^3$ carbon
Nitronium ion$\ce{NO2+}$ ($\ce{O=N+=O}$), the electrophile in nitration; generated from $\ce{HNO3 + H2SO4}$
Halogen carrierA Lewis acid catalyst ($\ce{FeBr3}$, $\ce{AlCl3}$, $\ce{FeCl3}$) that polarises $\ce{X2}$ or $\ce{RX}$ to generate a stronger electrophile; regenerated at the end
Friedel–Crafts alkylation$\ce{S_EAr}$ using $\ce{RCl + AlCl3}$ to introduce an alkyl group; limited by carbocation rearrangement and polyalkylation
Friedel–Crafts acylation$\ce{S_EAr}$ using $\ce{RCOCl + AlCl3}$ to introduce an acyl group; no rearrangement, no polyacylation; product is a ketone
Hückel's ruleA molecule is aromatic if it is cyclic, planar, fully conjugated, and has $4n+2$ $\pi$ electrons (where $n = 0, 1, 2,\ldots$)

8. Exam Tips

Paper 1 (Multiple Choice): "Which of these is NOT evidence against the Kekulé structure?" — bond lengths (all equal), one 1,2-isomer, enthalpy of hydrogenation, and preference for substitution are ALL evidence against. Questions often test whether you know benzene decolourises bromine water (it does NOT — without a catalyst). Also: Hückel's rule — counting $\pi$ electrons correctly (double bonds = 2 each, anions add electrons, cations remove).
Paper 2 — $S_EAr$ Mechanism (5–7 marks): You must draw: (1) generation of the electrophile using the catalyst, (2) curly arrow from benzene $\pi$ system to $\ce{E+}$, (3) the Wheland intermediate with + charge delocalised (draw resonance structures or indicate with dashed circle/partial charges), (4) curly arrow from C–H bond → ring with base removing $\ce{H+}$, (5) regeneration of the catalyst. This is a high-mark question — be thorough.
Multi-step synthesis (Paper 2): If asked to synthesise a disubstituted benzene, the ORDER of reactions is critical because the first substituent directs the second. Activating o/p-directors ($\ce{–CH3}$, $\ce{–NH2}$, $\ce{–OH}$) must be introduced before deactivating m-directors ($\ce{–NO2}$, $\ce{–COOH}$). Explain your reasoning — "nitration first would give the meta isomer, but the target is para."
