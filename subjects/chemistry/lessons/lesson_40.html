Lesson 40: Benzene and Electrophilic Substitution (HL)

What You'll Learn

In this lesson, you will study benzene — one of the most important molecules in organic chemistry. Benzene has a special stability that makes it behave very differently from alkenes, even though it contains double bonds. You will learn about the delocalised π electron system that gives benzene its unique properties. You will learn about electrophilic substitution, the main reaction type of benzene and its derivatives. You will learn about three important substitution reactions: nitration, halogenation, and Friedel-Crafts alkylation. You will also learn how groups already attached to a benzene ring direct incoming groups to specific positions.

1. What Is Benzene?

Benzene is a hydrocarbon with the molecular formula C₆H₆. At first glance, this formula seems puzzling: it has the same number of carbon atoms as cyclohexane (C₆H₁₂) but only half as many hydrogen atoms. Something about its structure must be different.

Benzene is a cyclic molecule — the six carbon atoms are arranged in a flat (planar) ring. Each carbon is bonded to one hydrogen atom. The ring is a regular hexagon with all bond angles equal to 120°. This tells us that each carbon uses sp² hybridisation — three sp² orbitals in a plane, 120° apart, forming σ bonds to two neighbouring carbons and one hydrogen. This leaves one unhybridised p orbital on each carbon, perpendicular to the plane of the ring.

The Key Insight: Delocalised π Electrons

In benzene, the six p orbitals (one from each carbon) overlap sideways with each other — not just in pairs, but all around the ring. This creates a continuous ring of electron density above and below the plane of the carbon atoms. The six electrons in these p orbitals (one from each carbon) are delocalised — they are not confined to a single bond between two atoms but are shared equally among all six carbon atoms.

This delocalisation is what makes benzene special. It gives benzene an extra stability that is not present in hypothetical "cyclohexa-1,3,5-triene" (a molecule with alternating single and double bonds).

Physical evidence: All six C−C bonds in benzene are identical in length — about 139 pm (picometres). This is intermediate between a typical C−C single bond (154 pm) and a typical C=C double bond (134 pm). If benzene had alternating single and double bonds, we would expect two different bond lengths. The fact that all six bonds are the same is strong evidence for delocalisation.

2. Evidence for Benzene's Special Stability

Evidence 1: Resistance to Addition Reactions

Alkenes react with bromine water at room temperature — the orange colour disappears as Br₂ adds across the double bond. Benzene does NOT react with bromine water under these conditions. If benzene had ordinary double bonds, it should add bromine just like an alkene. The fact that it does not tells us that the π electrons in benzene are not like ordinary alkene π electrons — they are delocalised and stabilised, making addition unfavourable.

To make benzene react with bromine, we need a halogen carrier catalyst (such as FeBr₃ or AlBr₃), and the reaction that occurs is substitution, not addition — one H is replaced by Br, and the aromatic ring is preserved. This tells us that the ring is so stable that the molecule will not sacrifice that stability by undergoing addition.

Evidence 2: Enthalpy of Hydrogenation

Hydrogenation is the addition of H₂ across a double bond. We can measure how much energy is released when it happens.

  • Cyclohexene (one C=C double bond) + H₂ → cyclohexane: ΔH = −120 kJ mol⁻¹.
  • If benzene were "cyclohexatriene" with three ordinary double bonds, hydrogenating it to cyclohexane should release 3 × (−120) = −360 kJ mol⁻¹.
  • The actual enthalpy change for C₆H₆ + 3H₂ → C₆H₁₂ is −210 kJ mol⁻¹.

The difference is 360 − 210 = 150 kJ mol⁻¹. Benzene is 150 kJ mol⁻¹ more stable than the hypothetical triene. This extra stability is called the delocalisation energy (or resonance energy). It is the energy you would need to put in to break the delocalisation — that is why benzene resists addition.

3. Electrophilic Substitution — The General Mechanism

Because benzene is electron-rich (the π cloud above and below the ring), it is attractive to electrophiles — electron-deficient species that seek out electrons. However, unlike alkenes, benzene does not undergo addition. Instead, it undergoes electrophilic substitution. The electrophile replaces one of the hydrogen atoms on the ring, and the stable aromatic system is preserved.

The general mechanism for electrophilic substitution of benzene (often abbreviated as SEAr, where Ar stands for "aromatic") has three stages:

Stage 1: Generation of the electrophile (E⁺). The electrophile is not present in the starting materials — it must be generated in the reaction mixture, usually by the catalyst. This stage varies depending on the specific reaction.

Stage 2: Attack of the electrophile on the ring. The electrophile is attracted to the π electron cloud of benzene. Two of the π electrons form a σ bond to the electrophile. This leaves only four π electrons spread over five carbon atoms (with a positive charge on the ring). The intermediate is called an arenium ion (also called a Wheland intermediate or σ-complex). The positive charge is delocalised over the ring, which stabilises this intermediate.

Stage 3: Loss of a proton (H⁺) and restoration of the aromatic system. A base present in the reaction mixture (often the counterion from Stage 1) removes a proton from the carbon that now carries the electrophile. The two electrons from the C−H bond move back into the π system, restoring the full aromatic sextet (six delocalised π electrons). The product is a substituted benzene, and the aromatic stability is recovered.

Why substitution and not addition? If benzene underwent addition (like alkenes), the product would be a cyclohexadiene derivative with only four π electrons — no longer aromatic, and much less stable. By undergoing substitution, benzene trades one H for the electrophile but keeps its aromaticity and its 150 kJ mol⁻¹ of delocalisation energy. The energy "cost" of losing aromaticity is too high for addition to be favourable.

4. Specific Electrophilic Substitution Reactions

Nitration of Benzene

Nitration replaces an H on the ring with a nitro group (−NO₂). The reagents are concentrated nitric acid (HNO₃) and concentrated sulfuric acid (H₂SO₄, the catalyst). The temperature is kept at about 50–55 °C.

Electrophile generation: Sulfuric acid protonates nitric acid, which leads to the formation of the nitronium ion (NO₂⁺):

$$\ce{HNO3 + H2SO4 -> NO2+ + HSO4- + H2O}$$

The NO₂⁺ is a powerful electrophile — the nitrogen carries a positive charge.

Overall reaction:

$$\ce{C6H6 + HNO3 ->[H2SO4] C6H5NO2 + H2O}$$

The product is nitrobenzene. If the temperature is raised and more nitric acid is added, further nitration can occur, giving mainly 1,3-dinitrobenzene (because the −NO₂ group directs incoming groups to the meta position, as explained later).

Halogenation of Benzene

Halogenation replaces an H with a halogen (Cl or Br). The reagent is the halogen (Cl₂ or Br₂) with a halogen carrier catalyst such as FeCl₃, FeBr₃, AlCl₃, or AlBr₃.

Electrophile generation: The catalyst polarises the halogen molecule, effectively generating a halogen cation (or a strongly polarised complex):

$$\ce{Br2 + FeBr3 -> Br+ + FeBr4-}$$

The Br⁺ (or the polarised Br–Br–FeBr₃ complex) acts as the electrophile.

Overall reaction:

$$\ce{C6H6 + Br2 ->[FeBr3] C6H5Br + HBr}$$

The product is bromobenzene. The FeBr₃ catalyst is regenerated: FeBr₄⁻ loses Br⁻ to the H⁺ removed from the ring, forming HBr and FeBr₃.

Friedel-Crafts Alkylation

Friedel-Crafts alkylation attaches an alkyl group (R−) to the benzene ring. The reagents are a halogenoalkane (RCl or RBr) and an aluminium chloride (AlCl₃) catalyst (anhydrous — no water).

Electrophile generation: The AlCl₃ accepts a chloride ion from the halogenoalkane, generating a carbocation (R⁺):

$$\ce{CH3Cl + AlCl3 -> CH3+ + AlCl4-}$$

The carbocation is the electrophile.

Overall reaction:

$$\ce{C6H6 + CH3Cl ->[AlCl3] C6H5CH3 + HCl}$$

The product is methylbenzene (common name: toluene). This reaction is very useful for building more complex molecules from benzene.

5. Directive Effects — Where Does the Second Group Go?

When a benzene ring already has one substituent (a group attached to the ring), and we do another electrophilic substitution, where does the second group attach? The existing substituent directs the incoming group to specific positions. There are two categories:

Ortho/Para Directing Groups (Direct to C-2, C-4, and C-6)

These groups direct incoming electrophiles to the ortho (C-2) and para (C-4) positions relative to themselves. Most are activating — they make the ring more reactive than benzene itself by donating electron density into the ring.

Examples:

  • −OH, −NH₂, −OCH₃: Strongly activating, strong ortho/para directors. The lone pair on the oxygen or nitrogen can overlap with the π system, increasing electron density at the ortho and para positions.
  • Alkyl groups (−CH₃, −C₂H₅, etc.): Weakly activating, ortho/para directing. They donate electron density through the inductive effect and hyperconjugation.
  • Halogens (−F, −Cl, −Br, −I): A special case. They are deactivating (they withdraw electron density through their electronegativity), but they are still ortho/para directing because their lone pairs can donate electron density through resonance. This is an HL detail worth noting: halogens deactivate the ring overall but direct ortho/para.

Meta Directing Groups (Direct to C-3 and C-5)

These groups direct incoming electrophiles to the meta (C-3) position. They are deactivating — they make the ring less reactive than benzene by withdrawing electron density.

Examples:

  • −NO₂, −COOH, −CHO, −CN, −SO₃H: These groups have a positive or partially positive atom attached directly to the ring, or they have strong electron-withdrawing π bonds. They pull electron density away from the ring, especially from the ortho and para positions, leaving the meta position as the least deactivated site for electrophilic attack.

Worked Example 1: Phenol (C₆H₅OH) reacts rapidly with bromine water without any catalyst, giving a white precipitate. Predict the product and explain why phenol is so much more reactive than benzene.

Phenol has an −OH group attached to the ring. The −OH group is strongly activating and ortho/para directing. The lone pair on the oxygen overlaps with the π system of the ring (this is called p-π conjugation), significantly increasing electron density, especially at the ortho and para positions.

With bromine water (a source of Br₂, which can act as an electrophile without a catalyst when the ring is activated enough), all three available ortho/para positions (relative to the −OH) are substituted. The product is 2,4,6-tribromophenol — a white precipitate with three bromine atoms on the ring at the 2, 4, and 6 positions.

No catalyst is needed because the −OH group makes the ring so electron-rich that Br₂ itself can act as the electrophile without needing FeBr₃ to generate Br⁺.

Worked Example 2: Predict the major product when benzoic acid (C₆H₅COOH) is nitrated with a mixture of concentrated nitric and sulfuric acids.

Benzoic acid has a −COOH group attached to the ring. The −COOH group is deactivating and meta directing. It withdraws electron density from the ring (the carbonyl C=O is electron-withdrawing through both inductive and resonance effects).

Nitration will therefore occur at the meta position (C-3) relative to the −COOH. The product is 3-nitrobenzoic acid.

If we tried to nitrate further, the second nitro group would also go meta to the −COOH (and meta to the first −NO₂), giving 3,5-dinitrobenzoic acid.

Practice Problems

Problem 1: Alkenes such as cyclohexene rapidly decolourise bromine water at room temperature, undergoing electrophilic addition. Benzene, however, does not react with bromine water under the same conditions. Explain this difference in reactivity. Your answer should refer to the delocalisation energy of benzene and the difference between addition and substitution.

Problem 2: Write balanced chemical equations for each of the following reactions, including the step in which the electrophile is generated:

(a) The nitration of benzene to form nitrobenzene.

(b) The reaction of benzene with chloromethane in the presence of anhydrous aluminium chloride (the Friedel-Crafts alkylation).

Problem 3: The enthalpy of hydrogenation of cyclohexene (one C=C double bond) has been measured as −120 kJ mol⁻¹.

(a) Calculate the enthalpy of hydrogenation that would be expected for benzene if it had the Kekulé structure with three localised C=C double bonds.

(b) The experimentally measured enthalpy of hydrogenation of benzene (C₆H₆ + 3H₂ → C₆H₁₂) is −210 kJ mol⁻¹. Calculate the delocalisation energy of benzene.

(c) Explain what this delocalisation energy represents in terms of the electronic structure of benzene, and how it explains benzene's resistance to addition reactions.

Problem 4: Predict the major organic product or products formed when each of the following substituted benzenes undergoes mononitration (addition of one −NO₂ group) using a mixture of concentrated nitric and sulfuric acids. In each case, explain whether the existing substituent is activating or deactivating and whether it directs ortho/para or meta.

(a) Methylbenzene (C₆H₅CH₃)

(b) Nitrobenzene (C₆H₅NO₂)

(c) Chlorobenzene (C₆H₅Cl)

Problem 5 (IB-style): Compound K is a liquid with the molecular formula C₇H₇NO₂. K is prepared by the nitration of methylbenzene (C₇H₈) at room temperature using a mixture of concentrated nitric acid and concentrated sulfuric acid. When K is heated under reflux with tin metal and concentrated hydrochloric acid (Sn/HCl), followed by the addition of sodium hydroxide solution, compound L with the formula C₇H₉N is formed. L reacts with ethanoyl chloride (CH₃COCl) to produce a white crystalline solid M.

(a) Methylbenzene has a −CH₃ group attached to the ring. Explain whether this group is activating or deactivating, and whether it directs incoming electrophiles to the ortho/para or meta positions.

(b) Draw the structures of the two main isomers of K that would be formed by mononitration of methylbenzene. Give the IUPAC name for each.

(c) Write a balanced equation for the reduction of K to L, using the symbol [H] to represent the reducing agent (hydrogen atoms provided by the Sn/HCl system). State the new functional group formed in L.

(d) L reacts with ethanoyl chloride (CH₃COCl). Identify the type of reaction that occurs between L and ethanoyl chloride, and name the functional group formed in M. Explain why L is able to react as a nucleophile in this reaction.

Answers

Answer 1.

Alkenes have localised π electrons — the π bond is concentrated between two specific carbon atoms. When an electrophile attacks, the π bond breaks and new σ bonds form (addition). The energy cost is about 120 kJ mol⁻¹ per double bond, and the product is a stable alkane. This is energetically favourable.

Benzene has delocalised π electrons spread over all six carbon atoms. If benzene were to undergo addition, two of the π electrons would be used to form bonds to the electrophile, but the remaining π system would have only four electrons spread over four carbons — a non-aromatic system. The ring would lose its aromatic stability, which is worth 150 kJ mol⁻¹. The energy penalty of losing aromaticity is greater than the energy gained by forming two new σ bonds, so addition is not favourable.

Instead, benzene undergoes substitution, where the electrophile replaces a hydrogen atom. After the electrophile attaches and the H⁺ departs, the π system is restored to six delocalised electrons — the aromatic stability is recovered. The 150 kJ mol⁻¹ of delocalisation energy is preserved, making substitution the thermodynamically favoured pathway.

Answer 2.

(a) Nitration:

Electrophile generation: $$\ce{HNO3 + H2SO4 -> NO2+ + HSO4- + H2O}$$

Overall reaction: $$\ce{C6H6 + HNO3 ->[H2SO4] C6H5NO2 + H2O}$$

(b) Friedel-Crafts alkylation:

Electrophile generation: $$\ce{CH3Cl + AlCl3 -> CH3+ + AlCl4-}$$

The carbocation CH₃⁺ (a methyl cation) is the electrophile.

Overall reaction: $$\ce{C6H6 + CH3Cl ->[AlCl3] C6H5CH3 + HCl}$$

In the mechanism, the AlCl₃ catalyst is regenerated: the H⁺ removed from the ring combines with AlCl₄⁻ to give AlCl₃ and HCl.

Answer 3.

(a) If benzene had three ordinary localised double bonds, the expected ΔH for complete hydrogenation would be: 3 × (−120 kJ mol⁻¹) = −360 kJ mol⁻¹.

(b) Delocalisation energy = expected − actual = 360 − 210 = 150 kJ mol⁻¹. Benzene is 150 kJ mol⁻¹ more stable than the hypothetical cyclohexa-1,3,5-triene.

(c) The delocalisation energy represents the extra stability gained by having six π electrons spread continuously around the ring rather than confined to three isolated double bonds. In benzene, each p orbital overlaps equally with both neighbours, creating a molecular orbital that encompasses all six carbon atoms. The electrons in this delocalised system are lower in energy than they would be in localised π bonds.

This extra 150 kJ mol⁻¹ of stability is why benzene resists addition. Addition would break the delocalised system, destroying this stabilisation — the energy cost is too high. Substitution preserves the delocalised π system, so the stability is retained throughout the reaction.

Answer 4.

(a) Methylbenzene: The −CH₃ group is activating (weakly) and ortho/para directing. Alkyl groups donate electron density through the inductive effect and through hyperconjugation, increasing electron density at ortho and para positions. The products of mononitration are 2-nitromethylbenzene (ortho-nitrotoluene) and 4-nitromethylbenzene (para-nitrotoluene).

(b) Nitrobenzene: The −NO₂ group is deactivating and meta directing. The nitro group withdraws electron density through both resonance (it can accept electrons into its π system) and the inductive effect (the highly electronegative oxygen atoms pull electron density). The meta position is the least deactivated. The product is 1,3-dinitrobenzene (meta-dinitrobenzene).

(c) Chlorobenzene: The −Cl group is deactivating (due to its electronegativity, it withdraws electron density inductively) but ortho/para directing (the lone pairs on chlorine can donate electron density into the ring through resonance, particularly at ortho and para positions). The products are 2-chloronitrobenzene and 4-chloronitrobenzene. The overall rate is slower than for benzene itself, but the regiochemistry is ortho/para.

Answer 5.

(a) The −CH₃ group in methylbenzene is an alkyl group. It is activating (weakly) because it donates electron density to the ring through the inductive effect and hyperconjugation. It is ortho/para directing — electrophiles attack preferentially at the ortho (C-2, C-6) and para (C-4) positions because these positions have higher electron density in the intermediate arenium ion.

(b) The two main isomers of K (C₇H₇NO₂, formed by mononitration of C₇H₈):

  • 2-nitromethylbenzene (ortho-nitrotoluene): the −NO₂ is at C-2, adjacent to the −CH₃.
  • 4-nitromethylbenzene (para-nitrotoluene): the −NO₂ is at C-4, opposite the −CH₃.

The meta isomer (3-nitromethylbenzene) is formed in only very small amounts.

(c) Reduction of K to L: The −NO₂ group is reduced to an −NH₂ group by the Sn/HCl system (which generates nascent hydrogen, [H]):

$$\ce{CH3C6H4NO2 + 6[H] -> CH3C6H4NH2 + 2H2O}$$

The new functional group in L is the amine group (−NH₂). L is a methylphenylamine (also called toluidine — specifically, depending on whether K was ortho or para, it is 2-methylphenylamine or 4-methylphenylamine).

(d) L contains an −NH₂ group (a primary amine). The nitrogen has a lone pair of electrons, which makes it a nucleophile. It reacts with ethanoyl chloride (CH₃COCl) in a nucleophilic addition-elimination (also called nucleophilic acyl substitution) reaction. The nitrogen attacks the carbonyl carbon of the acyl chloride, displacing chloride. The product M is an amide — specifically, N-(methylphenyl)ethanamide (an amide linkage: CH₃−CO−NH−C₆H₄CH₃). The reaction produces HCl as a by-product, which reacts with excess amine.

Equation: $$\ce{CH3C6H4NH2 + CH3COCl -> CH3C6H4NHCOCH3 + HCl}$$
