Lesson 1: Stoichiometry Review — The Mole and Reacting Quantities

Syllabus reference: 1.1–1.7 (Stoichiometric Relationships)

Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to:

  • Calculate the amount of substance (in moles) from mass, volume of gas, or concentration of solution.
  • Determine empirical and molecular formulas from percentage composition and combustion data.
  • Identify the limiting reagent in a reaction and calculate theoretical and percentage yield.
  • Apply the ideal gas equation $PV = nRT$ to calculate properties of gases.
  • Solve multi-step stoichiometry problems involving solutions, gases, and limiting reagents simultaneously.

1. Prior Knowledge Check

Question 1: How many atoms are in one molecule of $\ce{H2SO4}$?

▸ Show
7 atoms: 2 hydrogen + 1 sulfur + 4 oxygen. The subscript multiplies only the element it follows; if no subscript is written, it is 1.

Question 2: What does "STP" mean, and what is the molar volume of an ideal gas at STP?

▸ Show
STP = Standard Temperature and Pressure (273 K, 100 kPa). The molar volume of an ideal gas at STP is 22.7 dm³ mol⁻¹. This means one mole of any ideal gas occupies 22.7 dm³ under these conditions.

Question 3: Balance this equation: $\ce{Fe2O3 + CO -> Fe + CO2}$

▸ Show
$\ce{Fe2O3 + 3CO -> 2Fe + 3CO2}$. Strategy: balance Fe first (2 on left → need 2 on right), then balance O: left has 3 (from Fe₂O₃) + 3 (from 3CO) = 6; right needs 3CO₂ to get 6 O. Check C: 3 on left, 3 on right. ✓

2. The Mole Concept — the Chemist's Counting Unit

Chemistry happens between particles — atoms, molecules, and ions — but you cannot count them one by one. The mole solves this problem. One mole is simply a specific number of particles: $6.02 \times 10^{23}$, called Avogadro's number ($N_A$ or $L$).

Think of the mole like a dozen. A dozen eggs is 12 eggs. A mole of carbon atoms is $6.02 \times 10^{23}$ carbon atoms. The mole is just bigger — much bigger — because atoms are unimaginably small.

2.1 Connecting Mass to Moles

The molar mass ($M$) of a substance is the mass of one mole of that substance, expressed in grams per mole (g mol⁻¹). Numerically, it equals the relative atomic mass ($A_r$) or relative molecular mass ($M_r$) from the periodic table — but now it carries units.

Key formula — moles from mass: $$n = \frac{m}{M}$$ where $n$ = amount (mol), $m$ = mass (g), $M$ = molar mass (g mol⁻¹)
Figure 1: The three connected quantities — mass, moles, and number of particles. You can move between them using molar mass (M) and Avogadro's number (Nₐ).

Check-in: What is the mass of 0.500 mol of $\ce{NaOH}$? ($\ce{Na}$ = 23.0, $\ce{O}$ = 16.0, $\ce{H}$ = 1.00)

▸ Show
$M(\ce{NaOH}) = 23.0 + 16.0 + 1.00 = 40.0$ g mol⁻¹. $m = n \times M = 0.500 \times 40.0 = \mathbf{20.0\text{ g}}$.

2.2 Moles from Solution Concentration

When a substance is dissolved in water, you cannot weigh it directly — you measure the volume of the solution and its concentration.

Key formula — moles from solution: $$n = c \times V$$ where $c$ = concentration (mol dm⁻³) and $V$ = volume (dm³). Remember: 1 dm³ = 1000 cm³. If your volume is given in cm³, divide by 1000 first.

2.3 Moles from Gas Volumes

Gases are special: at the same temperature and pressure, equal volumes of all gases contain the same number of moles (Avogadro's Law). At STP (273 K, 100 kPa), one mole of any ideal gas occupies 22.7 dm³.

For conditions that are not STP, use the ideal gas equation:

Ideal gas equation: $$PV = nRT$$ $P$ = pressure (Pa or kPa), $V$ = volume (m³ or dm³), $n$ = moles, $R = 8.31$ J K⁻¹ mol⁻¹, $T$ = temperature (K). Units must match: if $P$ is in Pa, $V$ must be in m³. If $P$ is in kPa, $V$ must be in dm³.

Check-in: A gas occupies 49.3 cm³ at 102 kPa and 20°C. How many moles are present? ($R = 8.31$ J K⁻¹ mol⁻¹)

▸ Show
Convert: $V = 49.3\text{ cm}^3 = 4.93 \times 10^{-5}\text{ m}^3$, $P = 1.02 \times 10^5\text{ Pa}$, $T = 293\text{ K}$. $n = PV/RT = (1.02\times10^5 \times 4.93\times10^{-5}) / (8.31 \times 293) = \mathbf{2.07 \times 10^{-3}\text{ mol}}$.

3. Empirical and Molecular Formulas

3.1 Empirical Formula — the Simplest Ratio

The empirical formula tells you the simplest whole-number ratio of atoms in a compound. For example, glucose has molecular formula $\ce{C6H12O6}$, but its empirical formula is $\ce{CH2O}$ (divide all subscripts by 6).

How to find the empirical formula from percentage composition:

  1. Assume a 100 g sample (so percentages become masses in grams).
  2. Convert each mass to moles by dividing by the atomic mass.
  3. Divide all mole values by the smallest value to get the simplest ratio.
  4. If any ratio is not a whole number (e.g., 1.5), multiply ALL ratios by the same integer to make them whole (e.g., multiply by 2).
Worked: A compound is 40.0% C, 6.70% H, and 53.3% O by mass. Find its empirical formula.

Step 1: Assume 100 g → 40.0 g C, 6.70 g H, 53.3 g O.
Step 2: Moles: C = 40.0/12.0 = 3.33, H = 6.70/1.00 = 6.70, O = 53.3/16.0 = 3.33.
Step 3: Divide by smallest (3.33): C = 1, H = 2.01 ≈ 2, O = 1.
Empirical formula: $\ce{CH2O}$.

3.2 Molecular Formula — the Actual Formula

The molecular formula is a whole-number multiple of the empirical formula. To find it, you need the molar mass of the compound:

$$\text{Multiplier} = \frac{\text{molar mass of compound}}{\text{empirical formula mass}}$$

Check-in: A compound has empirical formula $\ce{CH2O}$ and molar mass 180 g mol⁻¹. What is its molecular formula?

▸ Show
Empirical formula mass = 12.0 + 2.00 + 16.0 = 30.0 g mol⁻¹. Multiplier = 180 / 30.0 = 6. Molecular formula = $\ce{C6H12O6}$ (glucose).

4. Limiting Reagents and Percentage Yield

4.1 Finding the Limiting Reagent

In most reactions, one reactant runs out first — that is the limiting reagent. The other reactants are in excess. The limiting reagent determines how much product can form.

Method: Divide the moles of each reactant by its coefficient in the balanced equation. The smallest result identifies the limiting reagent.

Figure 2: Identifying the limiting reagent. Divide moles by the coefficient — the smallest result wins. Here, Reactant A (2.0) is limiting despite having more moles initially.

4.2 Percentage Yield

The theoretical yield is the maximum amount of product possible (calculated from the limiting reagent). The actual yield is what you actually collect in the lab. No reaction is perfect — some product is always lost during transfer, purification, or due to side reactions.

$$\text{Percentage yield} = \frac{\text{actual yield}}{\text{theoretical yield}} \times 100\%$$

Check-in: A reaction has a theoretical yield of 25.0 g, but you only collect 18.5 g. What is the percentage yield?

▸ Show
Percentage yield = (18.5 / 25.0) × 100 = 74.0%. This means 26% of the potential product was lost — reasonable for many lab syntheses.

5. Worked Examples

Example 1 — Back-Titration (exam favorite):

1.50 g of impure $\ce{CaCO3}$ is reacted with 50.0 cm³ of 1.00 mol dm⁻³ $\ce{HCl}$ (an excess). The remaining $\ce{HCl}$ requires 28.0 cm³ of 1.00 mol dm⁻³ $\ce{NaOH}$ for neutralisation. Find the percentage purity of the $\ce{CaCO3}$ sample.

$\ce{CaCO3 + 2HCl -> CaCl2 + CO2 + H2O}$
$\ce{HCl + NaOH -> NaCl + H2O}$

Step 1: Moles of NaOH used. $n(\ce{NaOH}) = 1.00 \times 0.0280 = 0.0280$ mol.
Step 2: Moles of HCl remaining after CaCO₃ reaction. From the second equation, HCl and NaOH react 1:1, so $n(\ce{HCl\ remaining}) = 0.0280$ mol.
Step 3: Moles of HCl originally added. $n(\ce{HCl\ original}) = 1.00 \times 0.0500 = 0.0500$ mol.
Step 4: Moles of HCl that reacted with CaCO₃. $0.0500 - 0.0280 = 0.0220$ mol.
Step 5: Moles of CaCO₃. From equation, 1 mol $\ce{CaCO3}$ reacts with 2 mol $\ce{HCl}$. So $n(\ce{CaCO3}) = 0.0220 / 2 = 0.0110$ mol.
Step 6: Mass of pure CaCO₃. $M(\ce{CaCO3}) = 100$ g mol⁻¹. $m = 0.0110 \times 100 = 1.10$ g.
Step 7: Percentage purity. $(1.10 / 1.50) \times 100 = \mathbf{73.3\%}$.

⚠️ Common wrong approach: Students often forget that HCl reacts with both CaCO₃ AND NaOH, so they incorrectly use all 0.0500 mol of HCl as having reacted with CaCO₃. This gives a purity over 100% — an obvious clue something is wrong.
Example 2 — Limiting Reagent with Gas Volume:

2.00 g of $\ce{Mg}$ reacts with 500 cm³ of 0.200 mol dm⁻³ $\ce{HCl}$. What volume of $\ce{H2}$ is produced at STP? ($\ce{Mg}$ = 24.3)

$\ce{Mg + 2HCl -> MgCl2 + H2}$

Step 1: Moles of each reactant. $n(\ce{Mg}) = 2.00 / 24.3 = 0.0823$ mol. $n(\ce{HCl}) = 0.200 \times 0.500 = 0.100$ mol.
Step 2: Find limiting reagent. Mg: $0.0823 / 1 = 0.0823$. HCl: $0.100 / 2 = 0.0500$. HCl is limiting (0.0500 < 0.0823).
Step 3: Moles of H₂. From equation, 2 mol HCl → 1 mol H₂, so $n(\ce{H2}) = 0.100 / 2 = 0.0500$ mol.
Step 4: Volume of H₂ at STP. $V = n \times 22.7 = 0.0500 \times 22.7 = \mathbf{1.14\text{ dm}^3}$.

⚠️ Common wrong approach: Using Mg as limiting (because you have fewer grams) without converting to moles first. Grams are misleading — always convert to moles.
Example 3 — Ideal Gas Equation with Molar Mass:

0.586 g of an unknown gas occupies 282 cm³ at 102 kPa and −18°C. Find its molar mass. ($R = 8.31$ J K⁻¹ mol⁻¹)

Step 1: Convert all units. $P = 1.02 \times 10^5$ Pa, $V = 2.82 \times 10^{-4}$ m³, $T = 255$ K.
Step 2: Find moles using PV = nRT. $n = PV/RT = (1.02\times10^5 \times 2.82\times10^{-4}) / (8.31 \times 255) = 0.0136$ mol.
Step 3: Calculate molar mass. $M = m/n = 0.586 / 0.0136 = \mathbf{43.1\text{ g mol}^{-1}}$.

This could be $\ce{C3H8}$ (propane, $M = 44.1$) — close within experimental error.

⚠️ Common Mistakes to Avoid

  1. Confusing mass with moles when finding the limiting reagent. A reactant with a smaller mass is NOT necessarily limiting. You must convert to moles, then divide by the coefficient. A reactant that weighs less could still be in excess if it has a low molar mass.
  2. Forgetting to divide by 1000 when converting cm³ to dm³. 1 dm³ = 1000 cm³. If a volume is given as 250 cm³, that is 0.250 dm³. Using 250 in $n = cV$ will give an answer 1000 times too large.
  3. Using the wrong gas constant units. $R = 8.31$ J K⁻¹ mol⁻¹ requires pressure in Pa and volume in m³. If you use kPa and dm³, $R = 8.31$ works too — but you must be consistent. The common trap: mixing Pa with dm³.
  4. Not checking that the percentage yield is under 100%. If your calculation gives a yield above 100%, you have made an error — usually using the wrong limiting reagent or incorrect stoichiometric ratio.
  5. Using the molecular formula when only the empirical formula is needed. If the question does not give the molar mass, you can only find the empirical formula. Do not guess the multiplier.

6. End-of-Lesson Practice

Question 1 (Paper 1 style): 0.200 mol of $\ce{C3H8}$ contains how many atoms? ($L = 6.02 \times 10^{23}$)
A. $1.20 \times 10^{23}$   B. $1.32 \times 10^{24}$   C. $6.62 \times 10^{23}$   D. $1.02 \times 10^{24}$

Question 2: A compound contains 85.7% C and 14.3% H by mass. Its molar mass is 56.0 g mol⁻¹. Determine its empirical and molecular formulas.

Question 3: 3.00 g of $\ce{Zn}$ reacts with 50.0 cm³ of 2.00 mol dm⁻³ $\ce{HCl}$.
$\ce{Zn + 2HCl -> ZnCl2 + H2}$
(a) Identify the limiting reagent. (b) Calculate the volume of $\ce{H2}$ produced at STP. ($\ce{Zn}$ = 65.4)

Question 4: A solution of $\ce{Na2CO3}$ is made by dissolving 5.30 g of the solid in water and making up to 250 cm³. Calculate: (a) the concentration in mol dm⁻³, (b) the concentration of $\ce{Na+}$ ions in the solution. ($\ce{Na}$ = 23.0, $\ce{C}$ = 12.0, $\ce{O}$ = 16.0)

Question 5: 0.320 g of an ideal gas occupies 148 cm³ at 298 K and 101 kPa. Calculate its molar mass and suggest a possible identity. ($R = 8.31$ J K⁻¹ mol⁻¹)

Question 6 (IB-style multi-step): 2.50 g of an impure sample of $\ce{NaHCO3}$ is heated, decomposing according to:
$\ce{2NaHCO3 -> Na2CO3 + H2O + CO2}$
The $\ce{CO2}$ produced is collected and occupies 280 cm³ at STP.
(a) Calculate the moles of $\ce{CO2}$ produced.
(b) Calculate the moles of $\ce{NaHCO3}$ that decomposed.
(c) Calculate the mass of pure $\ce{NaHCO3}$ in the sample.
(d) Calculate the percentage purity of the original sample.
($\ce{Na}$ = 23.0, $\ce{H}$ = 1.00, $\ce{C}$ = 12.0, $\ce{O}$ = 16.0)

Answer 1: D. $\ce{C3H8}$ has 11 atoms per molecule. Number of molecules = $0.200 \times 6.02\times10^{23} = 1.204\times10^{23}$. Total atoms = $11 \times 1.204\times10^{23} = 1.32\times10^{24}$. Exam trap: Answer A forgets to multiply by 11 atoms. Answer C multiplies only by 3 carbon atoms.

Answer 2: Assume 100 g: C = 85.7/12.0 = 7.14 mol, H = 14.3/1.00 = 14.3 mol. Divide by 7.14: C = 1, H = 2.00. Empirical formula = $\ce{CH2}$, mass = 14.0. Multiplier = 56.0/14.0 = 4. Molecular formula = $\ce{C4H8}$.

Answer 3: (a) $n(\ce{Zn}) = 3.00/65.4 = 0.0459$ mol. $n(\ce{HCl}) = 2.00 \times 0.0500 = 0.100$ mol. Zn: 0.0459/1 = 0.0459. HCl: 0.100/2 = 0.0500. Zn is limiting. (b) 1 mol Zn → 1 mol H₂, so $n(\ce{H2}) = 0.0459$ mol. $V = 0.0459 \times 22.7 = 1.04$ dm³.

Answer 4: (a) $M(\ce{Na2CO3}) = (2\times23.0) + 12.0 + (3\times16.0) = 106$ g mol⁻¹. $n = 5.30/106 = 0.0500$ mol. $c = 0.0500/0.250 = \mathbf{0.200\text{ mol dm}^{-3}}$. (b) Each $\ce{Na2CO3}$ dissociates to give 2 $\ce{Na+}$ ions, so $[\ce{Na+}] = 2 \times 0.200 = \mathbf{0.400\text{ mol dm}^{-3}}$.

Answer 5: $P = 1.01\times10^5$ Pa, $V = 1.48\times10^{-4}$ m³, $T = 298$ K. $n = PV/RT = (1.01\times10^5 \times 1.48\times10^{-4})/(8.31 \times 298) = 0.00604$ mol. $M = 0.320/0.00604 = 53.0$ g mol⁻¹. Possible identity: $\ce{CH3CH2Cl}$ (chloroethane, 64.5) or $\ce{C4H6}$ (butyne, 54.0).

Answer 6: (a) $n(\ce{CO2}) = 0.280/22.7 = 0.01233$ mol. (b) From equation, 2 mol $\ce{NaHCO3}$ → 1 mol $\ce{CO2}$, so $n(\ce{NaHCO3}) = 2 \times 0.01233 = 0.02467$ mol. (c) $M(\ce{NaHCO3}) = 23.0+1.00+12.0+48.0 = 84.0$ g mol⁻¹. $m = 0.02467\times84.0 = 2.07$ g. (d) Purity = $(2.07/2.50)\times100 = \mathbf{82.9\%}$.

7. Key Terms

TermDefinition
Mole (mol)The amount of substance containing $6.02 \times 10^{23}$ particles (Avogadro's number)
Molar mass ($M$)The mass of one mole of a substance, in g mol⁻¹
Avogadro's number ($N_A$ or $L$)$6.02 \times 10^{23}$ mol⁻¹ — the number of particles in one mole
Empirical formulaThe simplest whole-number ratio of atoms in a compound
Molecular formulaThe actual number of atoms of each element in one molecule
Limiting reagentThe reactant that is completely consumed first, determining the maximum product formed
Theoretical yieldThe maximum mass of product calculated from the limiting reagent
Percentage yield(Actual yield ÷ theoretical yield) × 100%
STPStandard Temperature and Pressure: 273 K, 100 kPa. Molar volume at STP = 22.7 dm³ mol⁻¹
Ideal gasA gas that obeys $PV = nRT$ exactly; real gases deviate at high pressure and low temperature
ConcentrationAmount of solute per unit volume, typically mol dm⁻³

8. Exam Tips

Paper 1 (Multiple Choice): You will NOT have a calculator. Questions using the ideal gas equation or Avogadro's number are designed to be solved by estimation or by comparing powers of 10. The "atoms vs. molecules" trap appears almost every year — underline those words in the question before you answer.
Paper 2 (Structured): Stoichiometry questions in Section A are often 4–8 marks and require clear working. Even if your final answer is wrong, you earn method marks for: (1) correctly calculating moles of a reactant, (2) identifying the limiting reagent correctly, (3) using the correct mole ratio from the equation, (4) converting moles to the required quantity. Show every step.
Paper 2/3 — Back-Titrations: These are worth 5–7 marks and test whether you understand that the titrant reacts with the EXCESS acid, not the original acid. The key insight: $n(\ce{acid\ reacted}) = n(\ce{acid\ originally}) - n(\ce{acid\ remaining})$. If you write this line in your working, the rest is straightforward stoichiometry.
