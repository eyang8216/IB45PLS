Lesson 35: Electrolysis & Quantitative Electrochemistry (HL)

Syllabus reference: 9.2, 19.1 (HL) — Electrolytic Cells, Faraday's Laws, Quantitative Electrolysis

Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to:

  • Distinguish between voltaic (spontaneous) and electrolytic (non-spontaneous) cells.
  • Predict the products of electrolysis of molten salts and aqueous solutions using $E^{\ominus}$ values.
  • Apply Faraday's laws to calculate mass of product, current, or time in electrolysis.
  • Explain the relationship between charge, current, time, and amount of substance: $Q = It = nF$.
  • Compare electrolytic refining, electroplating, and the chlor-alkali industry.

1. Prior Knowledge Check

Question 1: What is the key difference between a voltaic cell and an electrolytic cell?

▸ Show
A voltaic cell uses a spontaneous redox reaction to generate electricity ($E^{\ominus}_{\text{cell}} > 0$). An electrolytic cell uses an external power source to drive a non-spontaneous redox reaction ($E^{\ominus}_{\text{cell}} < 0$). Voltaic: chemical → electrical. Electrolytic: electrical → chemical.

Question 2: In an electrolytic cell, which electrode is positive and which is negative?

▸ Show
In an electrolytic cell: anode = positive (+), cathode = negative (−). This is the opposite of a voltaic cell! Oxidation still occurs at the anode and reduction at the cathode in both types — the sign convention is what differs.

2. Electrolytic Cells — Driving Non-Spontaneous Reactions

An electrolytic cell uses a DC power source (battery) to force electrons through a circuit against the thermodynamic gradient. The external voltage must exceed the cell's $E^{\ominus}_{\text{cell}}$ (in magnitude) for electrolysis to occur.

Figure 1: Electrolysis of molten NaCl. External voltage forces Cl⁻ oxidation at the anode (+) and Na⁺ reduction at the cathode (−). The products are Cl₂ gas and liquid Na metal.

3. Predicting Products of Electrolysis

3.1 Molten Salts — Simple

Only one cation and one anion are present. The cation is reduced at the cathode; the anion is oxidised at the anode. Example: molten $\ce{PbBr2}$ → $\ce{Pb}$ at cathode, $\ce{Br2}$ at anode.

3.2 Aqueous Solutions — Competitive

In aqueous solutions, water can also be oxidised or reduced. You must compare $E^{\ominus}$ values to determine which reaction occurs preferentially.

Figure 2: Competing reactions in aqueous NaCl electrolysis. H₂O reduction wins at the cathode. At the anode, Cl⁻ oxidation is favoured in concentrated solutions due to kinetic overpotential effects.

Check-in: Predict products at each electrode for electrolysis of concentrated aqueous $\ce{CuSO4}$ using inert (graphite) electrodes.

▸ Show
Cathode (−): $\ce{Cu^{2+} + 2e- -> Cu(s)}$. Cu²⁺ ($E^{\ominus}$ = +0.34 V) is more easily reduced than water ($E^{\ominus}$ = −0.83 V). Copper metal deposits.
Anode (+): $\ce{2H2O -> O2 + 4H+ + 4e-}$. Water is more easily oxidised than SO₄²⁻. Oxygen gas evolves and solution becomes acidic near the anode. (Note: if Cu electrodes were used, the anode would dissolve: $\ce{Cu -> Cu^{2+} + 2e-}$.)

4. Faraday's Laws — Quantitative Electrolysis

Michael Faraday established the quantitative relationship between electricity and chemical change:

Faraday's Laws:
1st Law: The mass of substance produced is directly proportional to the quantity of electricity passed.
2nd Law: For a given quantity of electricity, the masses of different substances produced are proportional to their equivalent masses ($M/n$).

Key equations: $$Q = I \times t$$ $$n(e^-) = \frac{Q}{F} = \frac{I \times t}{F}$$ $$n(\text{product}) = \frac{n(e^-)}{\text{electrons in half-equation}}$$ where $Q$ = charge (C), $I$ = current (A), $t$ = time (s), $F = 96500\text{ C mol}^{-1}$.

4.1 The Calculation Pathway

Current ($I$, in A) and time ($t$, in s) → Charge ($Q$, in C) → Moles of electrons → Moles of product → Mass of product.

Check-in: A current of 2.00 A is passed through molten $\ce{MgCl2}$ for 30.0 minutes. Calculate the mass of magnesium produced. ($\ce{Mg}$ = 24.3, $F = 96500$)

▸ Show
$t = 30.0 \times 60 = 1800$ s. $Q = It = 2.00 \times 1800 = 3600$ C. $n(e^-) = 3600 / 96500 = 0.0373$ mol. $\ce{Mg^{2+} + 2e- -> Mg}$: 2 e⁻ per Mg. $n(\ce{Mg}) = 0.0373 / 2 = 0.01865$ mol. $m = 0.01865 \times 24.3 = \mathbf{0.453\text{ g}}$.

Check-in: Why are the carbon anodes consumed during aluminium extraction?

▸ Show
At the anode: $\ce{2O^{2-} -> O2 + 4e-}$. The oxygen produced at high temperature reacts with the carbon anodes: $\ce{C + O2 -> CO2}$ (and some CO). The anodes are gradually burnt away and must be replaced regularly — this is a major cost in aluminium production.

5. Worked Examples

Example 1 — Finding Time from Mass:

How long must a current of 5.00 A flow through $\ce{AgNO3(aq)}$ to deposit 2.16 g of silver? ($\ce{Ag}$ = 108, $F = 96500$)

Step 1: $n(\ce{Ag}) = 2.16 / 108 = 0.0200$ mol.
Step 2: $\ce{Ag+ + e- -> Ag}$, so $n(e^-) = n(\ce{Ag}) = 0.0200$ mol.
Step 3: $Q = n(e^-) \times F = 0.0200 \times 96500 = 1930$ C.
Step 4: $t = Q / I = 1930 / 5.00 = \mathbf{386\text{ s}}$ (6 min 26 s).

⚠️ Common wrong approach: Forgetting to convert mass to moles first, or using the wrong half-equation. For Ag⁺, it's 1 e⁻ per Ag — but for Cu²⁺ it's 2, for Al³⁺ it's 3. Always check the half-equation.
Example 2 — Comparing Products with Different $n$:

The same current is passed through $\ce{CuSO4}$ and $\ce{AgNO3}$ for the same time. If 0.635 g of Cu is deposited, what mass of Ag is deposited? ($\ce{Cu}$ = 63.5, $\ce{Ag}$ = 108)

Step 1: $n(\ce{Cu}) = 0.635/63.5 = 0.0100$ mol. Cu²⁺ + 2e⁻ → Cu, so $n(e^-) = 0.0200$ mol.
Step 2: Same $n(e^-)$ passed through AgNO₃. Ag⁺ + e⁻ → Ag, so $n(\ce{Ag}) = n(e^-) = 0.0200$ mol.
Step 3: $m(\ce{Ag}) = 0.0200 \times 108 = \mathbf{2.16\text{ g}}$.

⚠️ Common wrong approach: Assuming equal mass is deposited. Faraday's 2nd law: mass ∝ $M/n$. Cu: 63.5/2 = 31.75; Ag: 108/1 = 108. Ratio = 108/31.75 = 3.40, so Ag mass = 0.635 × 3.40 = 2.16 g.
Example 3 — Predicting Electrolysis Products (Active Electrodes):

Electrolysis of $\ce{CuSO4(aq)}$ is carried out using copper electrodes (not inert). Predict products and electrode mass changes.

Cathode (−): $\ce{Cu^{2+} + 2e- -> Cu(s)}$. Copper is deposited — cathode gains mass.
Anode (+): With copper electrodes, the anode itself is oxidised: $\ce{Cu(s) -> Cu^{2+} + 2e-}$. Anode loses mass. This is why CuSO₄ concentration stays constant — Cu²⁺ removed at cathode is replaced at anode.
This is electrorefining: impure copper anode → pure copper cathode. Impurities (Ag, Au, Pt) fall as anode sludge.

⚠️ Common wrong approach: Predicting O₂ at the anode as for inert electrodes. The nature of the electrode material matters — active electrodes (Cu, Ag, Zn) can be oxidised instead of the anion or water.

⚠️ Common Mistakes to Avoid

  1. Confusing anode/cathode signs between voltaic and electrolytic cells. In electrolytic: anode = +, cathode = −. In voltaic: anode = −, cathode = +. Oxidation is always at the anode and reduction at the cathode in both — the sign convention is what flips.
  2. Forgetting to convert time to seconds. $Q = It$ requires time in seconds. If minutes or hours are given, multiply by 60 or 3600 respectively.
  3. Using the wrong number of electrons in the half-equation. Al³⁺ requires 3 e⁻; Cu²⁺ requires 2; Ag⁺ requires 1. The number of electrons determines the mole ratio: $n$(product) = $n(e^-) / n$.
  4. Ignoring water as a competing reactant in aqueous electrolysis. In aqueous solutions, $\ce{H2O}$ can be reduced (to $\ce{H2}$ + $\ce{OH-}$) or oxidised (to $\ce{O2}$ + $\ce{H+}$). These reactions compete with the dissolved ions.
  5. Assuming inert electrode behaviour when electrodes are active. Copper, silver, and zinc electrodes can dissolve at the anode. This is exploited in electroplating and electrorefining.

Question 2: A current of 0.500 A is passed through $\ce{CuSO4(aq)}$ for 2.00 hours. Calculate the mass of copper deposited. ($\ce{Cu}$ = 63.5, $F = 96500$)

Question 3: Predict products at both electrodes for electrolysis of: (a) molten $\ce{KBr}$, (b) dilute aqueous $\ce{KBr}$ using inert electrodes.

Question 4: In the chlor-alkali process, concentrated $\ce{NaCl(aq)}$ is electrolysed. Write equations for the cathode and anode reactions and explain why the solution becomes alkaline.

Question 5: How long (in minutes) must a current of 3.00 A flow to deposit 5.00 g of chromium from $\ce{Cr^{3+}(aq)}$? ($\ce{Cr}$ = 52.0, $F = 96500$)

Question 6 (IB-style): Two electrolytic cells are connected in series — one contains $\ce{AgNO3(aq)}$ and the other $\ce{CuSO4(aq)}$. After electrolysis, 1.08 g of Ag is deposited in the first cell. (a) Calculate the charge passed. (b) Calculate the mass of Cu deposited. (c) If the current was 0.200 A, how long did the electrolysis last?

Answer 1: D. Cathode, which is negative. Al³⁺ is reduced at the cathode: $\ce{Al^{3+} + 3e- -> Al}$. In electrolytic cells, the cathode is the negative electrode.

Answer 2: $t = 2.00 \times 3600 = 7200$ s. $Q = 0.500 \times 7200 = 3600$ C. $n(e^-) = 3600/96500 = 0.0373$ mol. $\ce{Cu^{2+} + 2e- -> Cu}$, so $n(\ce{Cu}) = 0.0373/2 = 0.01865$ mol. $m = 0.01865 \times 63.5 = \mathbf{1.18\text{ g}}$.

Answer 3: (a) Molten KBr: Cathode: $\ce{K+ + e- -> K(l)}$. Anode: $\ce{2Br- -> Br2 + 2e-}$. (b) Dilute aqueous KBr: Cathode: $\ce{2H2O + 2e- -> H2 + 2OH-}$ (water reduced, not K⁺). Anode: $\ce{2H2O -> O2 + 4H+ + 4e-}$ (water oxidised, not Br⁻ in dilute solution). Products: H₂ and O₂.

Answer 4: Cathode: $\ce{2H2O + 2e- -> H2 + 2OH-}$. Anode: $\ce{2Cl- -> Cl2 + 2e-}$. The cathode produces OH⁻ ions, making the solution alkaline. Overall: $\ce{2NaCl + 2H2O -> H2 + Cl2 + 2NaOH}$. The NaOH accumulates in the solution.

Answer 5: $n(\ce{Cr}) = 5.00/52.0 = 0.09615$ mol. $\ce{Cr^{3+} + 3e- -> Cr}$, so $n(e^-) = 3 \times 0.09615 = 0.2885$ mol. $Q = 0.2885 \times 96500 = 27840$ C. $t = 27840/3.00 = 9280$ s = 155 minutes.

Answer 6: (a) $n(\ce{Ag}) = 1.08/108 = 0.0100$ mol. Ag⁺ + e⁻ → Ag, so $n(e^-) = 0.0100$ mol. $Q = 0.0100 \times 96500 = \mathbf{965\text{ C}}$. (b) Same charge in series. $n(\ce{Cu}) = n(e^-)/2 = 0.00500$ mol. $m = 0.00500 \times 63.5 = \mathbf{0.318\text{ g}}$. (c) $t = Q/I = 965/0.200 = 4825$ s = 80.4 min.

7. Key Terms

TermDefinition
ElectrolysisThe use of electrical energy to drive a non-spontaneous chemical reaction
Electrolytic cellA cell where an external power source forces a redox reaction to occur in the non-spontaneous direction
Faraday constant ($F$)$9.65 \times 10^4\text{ C mol}^{-1}$ — the charge on one mole of electrons
$Q = It$The relationship between charge (C), current (A), and time (s)
$n(e^-) = Q/F$The number of moles of electrons transferred, given the total charge
OverpotentialThe extra voltage beyond the thermodynamic requirement needed to drive electrolysis at a practical rate

8. Exam Tips

Paper 1 (Multiple Choice): Questions on electrolysis often test the sign convention (anode = + in electrolytic cell) and the electron-to-product mole ratio. Also common: identifying which species is discharged at each electrode based on $E^{\ominus}$ values. Check whether the question specifies "molten" or "aqueous" — the answer changes completely.
Paper 2 (Structured): Quantitative electrolysis calculations follow a fixed pathway: $I$ and $t$ → $Q = It$ → $n(e^-) = Q/F$ → moles of product → mass. Show each step clearly — method marks are awarded even if your arithmetic is wrong. Always check: are you using the correct $n$ (number of electrons in the half-equation)?
Electrode material matters: The IB frequently tests the distinction between inert electrodes (graphite, platinum) and active electrodes (copper, silver). With inert electrodes, water or the anion is oxidised. With active electrodes, the electrode metal itself may oxidise. This is crucial for electroplating and electrorefining questions.
