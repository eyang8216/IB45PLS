2. Energy Levels, Subshells, and Orbitals

Electrons do not orbit randomly. They occupy specific energy levels (n = 1, 2, 3, 4‚Ä¶), and within each level are subshells (s, p, d, f). Each subshell contains orbitals ‚Äî regions of space where electrons are most likely to be found.

Figure 1: The hierarchy of electron arrangement. Each energy level n contains n subshells. Each subshell contains specific numbers of orbitals, and each orbital holds up to 2 electrons.

2.1 Orbital Shapes

An s orbital is spherical. Every energy level has one s orbital. A p orbital is shaped like a dumbbell (two lobes on opposite sides of the nucleus). Every level from n = 2 upward has three p orbitals, oriented along the x, y, and z axes. d orbitals (from n = 3 upward) have more complex cloverleaf shapes ‚Äî there are five of them.

Figure 2: Approximate shapes. s = spherical, p = two-lobed dumbbell, d = four-lobed cloverleaf (one of five orientations shown).

3. The Three Rules for Filling Orbitals

3.1 The Aufbau Principle

Electrons fill orbitals starting from the lowest energy and moving upward. The order is NOT simply 1s ‚Üí 2s ‚Üí 2p ‚Üí 3s ‚Üí 3p ‚Üí 3d. Because of subtle energy differences, 4s fills before 3d. The full order (up to Z = 36) is:

Figure 3: The Aufbau order. Yellow = 4s fills before 3d. Blue = 3d fills after 4s. This is the order for neutral atoms.

3.2 Hund's Rule

When filling orbitals of equal energy (like the three 2p orbitals), electrons occupy separate orbitals with parallel spins before pairing up. Think of it like passengers on a bus: everyone takes an empty seat before sharing.

3.3 The Pauli Exclusion Principle

No two electrons in an atom can have the same set of four quantum numbers. Practically: each orbital can hold at most two electrons, and they must have opposite spins (represented by ‚Üë and ‚Üì in orbital diagrams).

Figure 4: Nitrogen's 2p¬≥ configuration. Each of the three p orbitals gets one electron with the same spin before any pairing occurs.

4. Writing Electron Configurations

4.1 Full Configuration

List every occupied subshell with the number of electrons as a superscript. Oxygen (Z = 8): $\ce{1s^2 2s^2 2p^4}$.

4.2 Condensed (Noble Gas Core) Configuration

Replace the inner-shell configuration with the symbol of the preceding noble gas in brackets. Iron (Z = 26): $\ce{[Ar] 4s^2 3d^6}$.

4.3 The Cr and Cu Exceptions

Chromium (Z = 24) is not $\ce{[Ar] 4s^2 3d^4}$ ‚Äî it is $\ce{[Ar] 4s^1 3d^5}$. Copper (Z = 29) is not $\ce{[Ar] 4s^2 3d^9}$ ‚Äî it is $\ce{[Ar] 4s^1 3d^{10}}$. In both cases, one electron from 4s moves into 3d to create a half-filled (d‚Åµ) or fully-filled (d¬π‚Å∞) d subshell. These configurations are more stable because half-filled and fully-filled d subshells have extra stability from exchange energy and symmetrical electron distribution.

Example 3 (exam trap): A student writes the electron configuration of $\ce{V^{2+}}$ (vanadium, Z = 23) as $\ce{[Ar] 3d^3}$. Is this correct?

Step 1: Neutral V: $\ce{[Ar] 4s^2 3d^3}$ (23 electrons).
Step 2: To form $\ce{V^{2+}}$, remove 2 electrons. Electrons are lost from the highest energy level (n = 4) first, which is 4s.
Step 3: Remove both 4s electrons ‚Üí $\ce{[Ar] 3d^3}$. Yes, the student is correct.

‚öÝÔ∏è Common wrong approach: Some students remove from 3d first because "3d is higher energy than 4s" ‚Äî but for cations, the 4s electrons are lost first because they are in the outermost (highest n) shell.

‚öÝÔ∏è Common Mistakes to Avoid

  1. Filling 3d before 4s for neutral atoms. For neutral atoms, 4s fills before 3d. Cr is $\ce{[Ar] 4s^1 3d^5}$, not $\ce{[Ar] 3d^5 4s^1}$ (though the order is less important in condensed form, examiners expect 4s written first for neutral atoms).
  2. Forgetting that 4s electrons are lost first when forming cations. Even though 4s fills before 3d, it is also the first to empty because it has the higher principal quantum number (n = 4 vs n = 3). Fe¬≤‚Å∫: $\ce{[Ar] 3d^6}$, NOT $\ce{[Ar] 4s^2 3d^4}$.
  3. Pairing electrons before filling all orbitals in a subshell. Hund's rule: for p¬≥, all three p orbitals get one electron each (‚Üë ‚Üë ‚Üë) before any pairing. For d‚Åµ in Cr, all five d orbitals get one electron.
  4. Memorizing Cr and Cu without understanding WHY. The driving force is the extra stability of half-filled (d‚Åµ) and fully-filled (d¬π‚Å∞) d subshells. The energy gained by this arrangement slightly exceeds the energy cost of promoting a 4s electron.

8. Exam Tips

Paper 1: Watch for "excited state" questions ‚Äî they look nearly identical to ground-state configurations but have one electron in a higher-energy subshell than expected (e.g., an electron in 3s when 2p isn't full). The trap: the total electron count is correct for the element, but the distribution is wrong.
Paper 2: When writing configurations for ions, the exam expects you to remove electrons from the highest n first (4s before 3d). This is worth a mark. State this explicitly: "Electrons are removed from the 4s orbital because it has the highest principal quantum number."
