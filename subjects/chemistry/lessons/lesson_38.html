Lesson 38: Alcohols, Halogenoalkanes, SN1/SN2 & Elimination (HL)

Syllabus reference: 10.2, 20.2 (Alcohols, Halogenoalkanes, Nucleophilic Substitution, Elimination)

Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to:

  • Describe the preparation, oxidation, and substitution reactions of alcohols and halogenoalkanes.
  • Distinguish between SN1 and SN2 mechanisms based on kinetics, stereochemistry, and substrate structure.
  • Predict whether a given halogenoalkane will undergo SN1, SN2, or elimination under specified conditions.
  • Draw curly-arrow mechanisms for SN1, SN2, E1, and E2 reactions including transition states.
  • Explain how the choice of solvent, temperature, and nucleophile/base strength determines the reaction pathway.

1. Prior Knowledge Check

Question 1: What is a nucleophile? Give three examples relevant to this lesson.

▸ Show
A nucleophile is an electron-pair donor — a Lewis base. It has a lone pair or $\pi$ electrons and attacks electron-deficient sites. Examples: $\ce{OH-}$ (hydroxide), $\ce{CN-}$ (cyanide), $\ce{NH3}$ (ammonia), $\ce{:Br-}$, and $\ce{H2O:}$. Nucleophiles are central to substitution reactions at saturated carbon.

Question 2: How does the classification of an alcohol (1°/2°/3°) affect its oxidation products?

▸ Show
1° alcohol → aldehyde → carboxylic acid (with excess oxidant). 2° alcohol → ketone (no further oxidation without breaking C–C). 3° alcohol → no oxidation under normal conditions (no H atom on the carbon bearing –OH to remove). This is a classic distinguishing test: only 1° and 2° alcohols are oxidised by acidified $\ce{K2Cr2O7}$ (orange → green).

Question 3: What is the difference between "substitution" and "elimination" as reaction types?

▸ Show
Substitution: one atom or group is replaced by another; the carbon skeleton remains unchanged. $\ce{CH3CH2Br + OH- -> CH3CH2OH + Br-}$. Elimination: atoms/groups are removed from adjacent carbons, forming a $\pi$ bond. $\ce{CH3CH2Br + OH- -> CH2=CH2 + H2O + Br-}$. Substitution and elimination are competing pathways — the outcome depends on the substrate, reagent, and conditions.

2. Alcohols — Preparation, Reactions, and Oxidation

Alcohols contain the hydroxyl group $\ce{–OH}$ bonded to an $sp^3$ carbon. They are classified as 1°, 2°, or 3°. The $\ce{–OH}$ group makes alcohols weak Brønsted acids ($pK_a \approx 16$ for primary alcohols) and also provides a lone pair for nucleophilic behaviour.

2.2 Preparation of Halogenoalkanes from Alcohols

Alcohols can be converted to halogenoalkanes by nucleophilic substitution — but the $\ce{–OH}$ group is a poor leaving group (hydroxide $\ce{OH-}$ is a strong base). It must first be protonated to form $\ce{–OH2+}$, where water is an excellent leaving group.

Using $\ce{PCl5}$ or $\ce{SOCl2}$: $\ce{ROH + SOCl2 -> RCl + SO2 + HCl}$ (no acid needed; clean reaction).

Using $\ce{HX}$: $\ce{ROH + HBr -> RBr + H2O}$ (via protonation of –OH, then SN1 or SN2).

Check-in: Why can't you simply use $\ce{NaCl}$ and an alcohol to make a chloroalkane?

▸ Show
$\ce{Cl-}$ is a good nucleophile, but $\ce{–OH}$ is a terrible leaving group because $\ce{OH-}$ is a strong base. The reaction $\ce{ROH + Cl- -> RCl + OH-}$ is thermodynamically unfavourable (strong base displaced by weaker base). By adding acid, $\ce{–OH}$ becomes $\ce{–OH2+}$; now the leaving group is $\ce{H2O}$ (weak base, excellent leaving group). This is why halogenoalkane synthesis from alcohols requires acidic conditions or specialised reagents ($\ce{SOCl2}$, $\ce{PCl5}$, $\ce{PBr3}$).

3. Nucleophilic Substitution — SN1 vs. SN2 (HL)

Nucleophilic substitution is the reaction of a nucleophile ($\ce{Nu-}$ or $\ce{Nu:}$) with a halogenoalkane ($\ce{R–X}$) to replace X with Nu. But there are two fundamentally different mechanisms — and which one operates depends on the structure of the halogenoalkane.

3.2 SN1 — Unimolecular, Stepwise

N1 Mechanism: $\ce{(CH3)3CBr + H2O -> (CH3)3COH + HBr}$ Step 1: RATE-DETERMINING $\ce{(CH3)3C–Br -> (CH3)3C+ + Br-}$ heterolytic fission — slow, unimolecular Rate = $k[(\ce{CH3)3CBr}]$ — 1st order curly arrow: C–Br bond electrons → Br Step 2: FAST $\ce{(CH3)3C+ + H2O: -> (CH3)3C–OH2+}$ water (nucleophile) attacks planar carbocation Step 3 (fast): deprotonation → $\ce{(CH3)3COH}$ Planar carbocation (sp²) empty p orbital ⊥ to plane Nu can attack from EITHER face → racemisation (if chiral centre) SN1 favoured by: 3° > 2° >> 1° (carbocation stability) Weak nucleophiles (e.g., H₂O, ROH) Polar protic solvents (stabilise ions)
Figure 3: The SN1 mechanism. The leaving group departs first (slow step), forming a planar carbocation. The nucleophile then attacks from either face — leading to racemisation if the carbon is chiral. Rate depends only on [RX].

Check-in: Why does 1-bromobutane undergo SN2 while 2-bromo-2-methylpropane undergoes SN1 with the same nucleophile?

▸ Show
1-bromobutane is a 1° halogenoalkane. SN2 requires back-side attack — minimal steric hindrance at a 1° centre, so SN2 is fast. Forming a 1° carbocation for SN1 is very unfavourable. 2-bromo-2-methylpropane is 3°: SN2 is impossible — the carbon is completely blocked for back-side attack (steric hindrance from 3 methyl groups). But SN1 is fast because the 3° carbocation is highly stabilised by three +I alkyl groups. The substrate determines the mechanism.

4. Elimination Reactions (HL) — Competing with Substitution

When a nucleophile can also act as a base (e.g., $\ce{OH-}$, $\ce{RO-}$, $\ce{NH2-}$), it may remove a $\beta$-hydrogen instead of substituting. This is elimination — forming an alkene.

N2 Good Nu, less basic 1° RX (low steric) Aprotic solvent → Inversion SN1 Weak Nu (H₂O, ROH) 3° RX (stable C⁺) Protic solvent → Racemisation E2 Strong base Heat Concerted → anti-periplanar E1 Weak base, 3° RX Heat Stepwise (C⁺ first) → Zaitsev product Key Decision Factors • 1° RX + strong Nu⁻/weak base (e.g., $\ce{CN-}$, $\ce{I-}$) → SN2 dominates • 3° RX + strong base (e.g., $\ce{OH-}$, $\ce{EtO-}$) → E2 dominates (steric hindrance blocks SN2) • Heat favours elimination (ΔS positive: 2 particles → 3 particles)  |  Cold favours substitution
Figure 4: Decision flowchart for SN1/SN2/E1/E2. The substrate (1° vs. 2° vs. 3°), the nucleophile/base strength, the solvent, and the temperature all influence the pathway.

Check-in: Predict the major organic product when 2-bromopropane is heated with (a) aqueous $\ce{NaOH}$ and (b) ethanolic $\ce{NaOH}$.

▸ Show
(a) Aqueous $\ce{NaOH}$ at moderate temperature: $\ce{OH-}$ acts as a nucleophile → SN2 at 2° centre → propan-2-ol. (b) Ethanolic $\ce{NaOH}$ at reflux: $\ce{OH-}$ in ethanol acts as a strong base; heat favours elimination → E2 → propene $\ce{CH3CH=CH2}$. The solvent is a key clue: aqueous = substitution; ethanolic = elimination. IB expects you to know this distinction.

5. Zaitsev's Rule for Elimination

When elimination can produce more than one alkene, Zaitsev's rule predicts the major product:

Zaitsev's Rule: The major product of elimination is the more substituted alkene — the one with more alkyl groups attached to the $\ce{C=C}$ carbons. This alkene is thermodynamically more stable (hyperconjugation from alkyl groups stabilises the $\pi$ bond).

6. Worked Examples

Example 1 — Predicting SN2 stereochemistry:

(S)-2-bromobutane reacts with $\ce{NaOH}$ (aq). Draw the product and state its stereochemistry.

Analysis: 2-bromobutane is a 2° halogenoalkane with a chiral centre at C2. $\ce{OH-}$ is a good nucleophile. SN2 is possible (2° substrates can go SN1 or SN2 depending on conditions; aqueous $\ce{NaOH}$ favours SN2). Mechanism: $\ce{OH-}$ attacks from the back side → Walden inversion. (S)-2-bromobutane becomes (R)-butan-2-ol. CIP priority: $\ce{OH > CH2CH3 > CH3 > H}$. With inversion, the configuration flips from S to R.

⚠️ Common wrong approach: Forgetting that SN2 causes inversion. Students sometimes draw the same stereochemistry, giving (S)-butan-2-ol.
Example 2 — Competition between substitution and elimination:

1-bromopropane is treated with $\ce{KOH}$ in ethanol under reflux. The product mixture contains two organic compounds. Identify them and explain which is major.

Analysis: 1° halogenoalkane + strong base + heat + ethanolic solvent. $\ce{OH-}$/$\ce{EtO-}$ can act as base (E2) or nucleophile (SN2). Both occur. E2 product: propene $\ce{CH3CH=CH2}$. SN2 product: propan-1-ol (or ethoxypropane via $\ce{EtO-}$ if $\ce{EtOH}$ solvent participates). With ethanolic $\ce{KOH}$, elimination dominates → propene is major (Zaitsev not applicable — only one elimination product possible). Minor product: propan-1-ol (substitution).

⚠️ Common wrong approach: Writing only the elimination product. IB expects you to recognise that substitution and elimination compete and that the conditions (solvent, temperature) influence the ratio.
Example 3 — E2 stereochemistry (anti-periplanar requirement):

Explain why (1R,2R)-1-bromo-1,2-diphenylethane undergoes E2 elimination with $\ce{NaOEt}$ to give exclusively (E)-1,2-diphenylethene, while the (1S,2R) diastereomer gives exclusively the (Z)-alkene.

Analysis: E2 requires the H and the leaving group (Br) to be anti-periplanar — in the same plane but on opposite sides (dihedral angle ≈ 180°). This allows optimal orbital overlap for the forming $\pi$ bond. In the (1R,2R) isomer, when Br and H are anti, the two phenyl groups end up on opposite sides of the C=C → E configuration. In (1S,2R), anti arrangement places phenyl groups on the same side → Z configuration. Key point: E2 stereochemistry is stereospecific — the diastereomer determines the alkene geometry.

⚠️ Common wrong approach: Treating E2 as non-stereospecific and predicting a mixture of E and Z for each diastereomer. The anti-periplanar requirement makes E2 highly stereospecific.

⚠️ Common Mistakes to Avoid

  1. SN2 is NOT simply "1° substrates." While SN2 is fastest for 1° substrates, it can also occur with 2° substrates (especially with strong nucleophiles like $\ce{I-}$, $\ce{CN-}$). It is impossible for 3° substrates due to steric hindrance. The transition from SN2 to SN1 is gradual, not abrupt.
  2. Aqueous vs. ethanolic conditions. $\ce{NaOH(aq)}$ = nucleophilic substitution (OH⁻ as Nu). $\ce{NaOH}$ in ethanol + heat = elimination (OH⁻/EtO⁻ as base). This is a classic IB distinction that appears every year.
  3. Oxidation states. 1° alcohol → aldehyde → acid involves oxidation at the SAME carbon twice. 2° → ketone is a single oxidation. 3° alcohol cannot be oxidised (no C–H on the C–OH). Don't confuse this with complete combustion (which burns everything).
  4. "Nucleophile" vs. "Base." All bases are nucleophiles, but nucleophilicity and basicity are different. $\ce{I-}$ is an excellent nucleophile but a very weak base (its conjugate acid $\ce{HI}$ is strong). $\ce{t-BuO-}$ is a strong base but a poor nucleophile (too bulky). This distinction determines SN2 vs E2.
  5. SN1 racemisation is not always complete. Ion-pair effects mean the leaving group can partially block one face, giving more inversion than retention. IB accepts "racemisation" as the expected answer, but be aware that real SN1 reactions often give a slight excess of inversion product.

Question 1 (Paper 1 style): Which halogenoalkane reacts fastest by the SN2 mechanism?
A. $\ce{(CH3)3CBr}$   B. $\ce{CH3CH2CH2Br}$   C. $\ce{(CH3)2CHBr}$   D. $\ce{CH3Br}$

Question 2: Draw the SN2 mechanism for the reaction of $\ce{CH3CH2I}$ with $\ce{CN-}$. Show curly arrows and the transition state.

Question 3: Predict the products when 2-bromo-2-methylbutane is heated with aqueous $\ce{NaOH}$. Explain the mechanism.

Question 4: Classify each reaction as SN1, SN2, E1, or E2: (a) $\ce{(CH3)3CCl + H2O}$ (warm) → $\ce{(CH3)3COH}$, (b) $\ce{CH3CH2Br + NaOCH3}$ (in methanol, reflux) → $\ce{CH2=CH2}$.

Question 5: (R)-2-chlorobutane reacts with aqueous $\ce{KOH}$. Draw the stereochemistry of the product and state its absolute configuration (R/S).

Question 6 (Paper 2 style): The reaction of 1-iodobutane with $\ce{NH3}$ yields butan-1-amine. (a) Draw the SN2 mechanism. (b) Explain why a large excess of $\ce{NH3}$ is needed to minimise further substitution. (c) What by-product forms if excess 1-iodobutane is used instead?

Answer 1: D. $\ce{CH3Br}$ has the least steric hindrance — three small H atoms around the carbon. SN2 rate: $\ce{CH3X}$ > 1° > 2° > 3°. $\ce{(CH3)3CBr}$ (3°) undergoes SN1, not SN2.

Answer 2: $\ce{NC-}$ attacks C of $\ce{CH3CH2I}$ from the back (180° to C–I). Curly arrow from $\ce{CN-}$ lone pair → C; curly arrow from C–I bond → I. Transition state: pentacoordinate C with partial C–Nu and C–I bonds. Product: $\ce{CH3CH2CN}$ (propanenitrile) + $\ce{I-}$.

Answer 3: 2-bromo-2-methylbutane is 3°. Aqueous $\ce{NaOH}$: water is the solvent and nucleophile. SN1 mechanism: Br⁻ leaves → 3° carbocation $\ce{(CH3CH2)(CH3)2C+}$. $\ce{H2O}$ attacks → protonated alcohol → deprotonation → 2-methylbutan-2-ol. Also: some E1 → 2-methylbut-2-ene and 2-methylbut-1-ene (Zaitsev: the more substituted alkene is major).

Answer 4: (a) 3° RX, weak nucleophile (H₂O), protic solvent → SN1 (with some E1 as minor). Rate = $k[\ce{RX}]$ only. (b) $\ce{CH3CH2Br}$ is 1° RX, $\ce{NaOCH3}$ is a strong base, methanol solvent, reflux (heat) → E2 dominates. The methoxide acts as a base, abstracting the β-H while Br leaves.

Answer 5: SN2 → Walden inversion. (R)-2-chlorobutane becomes (S)-butan-2-ol. CIP priority: $\ce{OH}$ (highest) > $\ce{CH2CH3}$ > $\ce{CH3}$ > H. In the (R) enantiomer of the starting material, the priority order is viewed with H pointing away; inversion flips all relative positions, giving S.

Answer 6: (a) SN2: $\ce{NH3}$ lone pair attacks C, I⁻ leaves, then deprotonation → $\ce{CH3CH2CH2CH2NH2}$. (b) The product butan-1-amine is itself a nucleophile (has a lone pair). It can react with remaining 1-iodobutane → N-butylbutan-1-amine ($\ce{R2NH}$). Excess $\ce{NH3}$ outcompetes the amine product statistically. (c) If excess 1-iodobutane, secondary and tertiary amines form (and even quaternary ammonium salt $\ce{R4N+ I-}$).

8. Key Terms

TermDefinition
Nucleophilic substitutionA reaction where a nucleophile replaces a leaving group on an $sp^3$ carbon
SN2Bimolecular nucleophilic substitution: concerted (one-step), back-side attack, Walden inversion, rate = $k[\ce{RX}][\ce{Nu-}]$
SN1Unimolecular nucleophilic substitution: stepwise via carbocation, racemisation, rate = $k[\ce{RX}]$
E2Bimolecular elimination: concerted, anti-periplanar geometry, rate = $k[\ce{RX}][\ce{Base}]$
E1Unimolecular elimination: stepwise via carbocation, Zaitsev product, rate = $k[\ce{RX}]$
Walden inversionInversion of configuration at a chiral centre during SN2 — the nucleophile attacks from the side opposite the leaving group
Leaving groupThe atom or group that departs with the bonding electron pair during substitution or elimination; good leaving groups are weak bases (e.g., $\ce{I-}$, $\ce{Br-}$, $\ce{H2O}$, $\ce{OTs-}$)
Zaitsev's ruleIn elimination, the major product is the more substituted (more stable) alkene
Anti-periplanarA conformation where the H and leaving group are in the same plane but on opposite sides (dihedral angle ≈ 180°), required for E2
Oxidation (organic)Increase in oxygen content or decrease in hydrogen content; for alcohols: 1° → aldehyde → acid; 2° → ketone
Protic solventA solvent capable of hydrogen-bond donation (e.g., $\ce{H2O}$, $\ce{ROH}$); stabilises carbocations and anions, favouring SN1
Aprotic solventA solvent without H-bond donor ability (e.g., acetone, DMSO, DMF); nucleophiles are not solvated as strongly, favouring SN2

9. Exam Tips

Paper 1 (Multiple Choice): SN2 rate comparison questions are common: "which reacts fastest with $\ce{CN-}$?" Remember: $\ce{CH3X}$ > 1° > 2° > 3° for SN2. For SN1: reverse order (3° > 2° > 1°). Also, the leaving group ability: $\ce{I- > Br- > Cl- >> F-}$ (weaker base = better leaving group).
Paper 2 — Mechanism questions (4–6 marks): You must show: curly arrows (electron pair movement), the transition state/ intermediate structure, stereochemistry where relevant (inversion for SN2, racemisation for SN1), and the correct product. For E2, show the anti-periplanar arrangement. IB examiners deduct marks for missing stereochemistry.
Aqueous vs. ethanolic KOH: This is one of the most frequently tested distinctions. $\ce{KOH(aq)}$, warm = hydrolysis (substitution, OH⁻ as nucleophile). $\ce{KOH}$ in ethanol, reflux = elimination (OH⁻/EtO⁻ as base). If the question specifies "ethanolic" and "reflux", the answer is elimination — do not write substitution.
