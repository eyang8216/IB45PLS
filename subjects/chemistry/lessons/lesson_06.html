Lesson 6: Ionisation Energy II — Successive IE, Anomalies, and Group Identification (HL)

Syllabus reference: 12.1 (HL — Successive Ionisation Energies)

Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to:

  • Define successive ionisation energies and explain why each successive IE is larger than the previous one.
  • Analyse successive IE data to determine the group of an unknown element.
  • Explain why large jumps in successive IE data indicate a change of principal quantum number (electron shell).
  • Apply your understanding of electron configuration to predict and explain IE anomalies.

1. Prior Knowledge Check

Q1: Write the electron configuration of $\ce{Na+}$ and explain why its second ionisation energy is much larger than its first.

▸ Show
Na: $\ce{1s^2 2s^2 2p^6 3s^1}$. $\ce{Na+}$: $\ce{1s^2 2s^2 2p^6}$ ([Ne] configuration). IE₁ removes the 3s electron (outer shell, n=3). IE₂ must remove a 2p electron from the [Ne] core (n=2) — much closer to the nucleus, much less shielding → much larger IE.

Q2: Why does an $\ce{Al+}$ ion (configuration $\ce{[Ne] 3s^2}$) hold its remaining electrons more tightly than a neutral Al atom?

▸ Show
After losing one electron, $\ce{Al+}$ has 12 electrons held by 13 protons. Each remaining electron experiences a GREATER effective nuclear charge — more protons pulling on fewer electrons. This makes the remaining electrons harder to remove.

2. Successive Ionisation Energies — The Concept

After removing one electron, you can remove a second, then a third, and so on. Each removal requires more energy because the remaining electrons are held by the same number of protons but experience less electron-electron repulsion. The effective nuclear charge ($Z_{\text{eff}}$) on each remaining electron steadily increases.

IEEquationProcess
IE₁$\ce{X(g) -> X+(g) + e-}$Remove 1st electron from neutral atom
IE₂$\ce{X+(g) -> X^{2+}(g) + e-}$Remove 2nd electron from 1+ ion
IE₃$\ce{X^{2+}(g) -> X^{3+}(g) + e-}$Remove 3rd electron from 2+ ion

2.1 The Power of the Big Jump

The most useful feature of successive IE data is the dramatic jump that occurs when you finish removing valence electrons and start breaking into the inner, filled shell (the noble gas core). This jump tells you exactly how many valence electrons the element has — and therefore which group it belongs to.

Figure 1: Aluminium (Z=13). The first three IEs remove the 3s²3p¹ valence electrons. IE₄ breaks into the 2p subshell — a new shell → dramatically larger.

Check-in: An element has IEs (kJ mol⁻¹): IE₁=578, IE₂=1817, IE₃=2745, IE₄=11578, IE₅=14831. In which group is this element?

▸ Show
The BIG jump is between IE₃ (2745) and IE₄ (11578) — IE₄ is over 4× larger. This means electrons 1-3 come from the outer shell, and electron 4 comes from an inner shell. 3 valence electrons → Group 13 (or 3). This is aluminium.

3. Deeper Explanation of Anomalies (HL)

In Lesson 5 you learned that Al has a lower IE₁ than Mg (3p vs 3s electron), and S has a lower IE₁ than P (paired electron repulsion). These same principles apply to successive IEs. For example, when you look at the IE data for chromium ($\ce{[Ar] 4s^1 3d^5}$), the pattern of successive IEs reflects the unusual stability of the half-filled d⁵ subshell.

4. Worked Examples

Example 1: The successive IEs of magnesium (kJ mol⁻¹): IE₁=738, IE₂=1451, IE₃=7733, IE₄=10543. (a) Why is IE₂ > IE₁? (b) Why is the jump from IE₂ to IE₃ much larger than the jump from IE₁ to IE₂?

(a) After IE₁, $\ce{Mg+}$ has 11 electrons experiencing the pull of 12 protons — greater $Z_{\text{eff}}$ → harder to remove the next electron.
(b) Mg is $\ce{[Ne] 3s^2}$. IE₁ and IE₂ both remove 3s electrons from n=3. IE₃ must remove a 2p electron from the [Ne] core (n=2). This electron is in a lower shell, much closer to the nucleus, with far less shielding from inner electrons. The jump between IE₂ (1451) and IE₃ (7733) — over 5× — marks the transition from the outer shell to the inner shell. ⚠️ Common wrong approach: Saying "because the ion is more positive." That's true for every successive IE, so it doesn't explain why THIS jump is so much larger. You must mention the change in principal quantum number (shell).
Example 2: Identify the group of element Z from its IEs: 1000, 2251, 3361, 4556, 6991, 8512, 27106 kJ mol⁻¹.

Step 1: Find the large jump. IE₆=8512 → IE₇=27106. That's over 3×.
Step 2: The first SIX electrons are valence electrons. 6 valence electrons → Group 16 (or 6).
Step 3: Check: element Z has around 8 electrons total (6 valence + 2 in inner shell from [He] or [Ne]). Likely oxygen or sulfur. The values suggest sulfur (S, Z=16: $\ce{[Ne] 3s^2 3p^4}$).

⚠️ Common Mistakes to Avoid

  1. Counting the jump position wrong. If the jump occurs BETWEEN IE₃ and IE₄, the element has 3 valence electrons (Group 13), NOT 4. Count the IEs BEFORE the jump.
  2. Explaining a large jump as "because the atom is more positively charged." This is true for EVERY successive IE. The correct explanation must mention: (a) the electron is being removed from a DIFFERENT (inner) shell, (b) this shell is closer to the nucleus, (c) inner electrons experience far less shielding.
  3. Confusing "jump" with "steady increase." IE always increases. Look for a jump that is 3-10× the previous value, not just 1.2-2×. A 2× increase is moderate and likely within the same shell.
  4. Forgetting state symbols in equations. All IE equations require gaseous species: $\ce{Ca+(g) -> Ca^{2+}(g) + e-}$. The (g) symbol is essential and often worth a mark.

6. Key Terms

TermDefinition
Successive ionisation energyThe energy to remove the nth electron: $\ce{X^{(n-1)+}(g) -> X^{n+}(g) + e-}$
Large jump (in IE data)A dramatic (3-10×) increase between successive IEs indicating removal from a new, inner electron shell
Valence electronsThe electrons in the outermost shell — those removed before the large jump
Noble gas coreThe inner-shell electrons with a noble gas configuration; very stable, very hard to remove

7. Exam Tips

Paper 1: When given a table of successive IEs, count the number of IEs BEFORE the first dramatic jump (3× or more). That number equals the number of valence electrons. For main group elements, that gives you the group directly (e.g., 3 valence = Group 13).
Paper 2: An "explain" question about a large jump in IE data requires THREE points: (1) state which shell the electron is being removed from, (2) mention the difference in distance from the nucleus, (3) mention the difference in shielding. Simply saying "it's from an inner shell" will only get 1 of 3 marks.
State symbols are non-negotiable: Every IE equation must use (g) for all species. Writing $\ce{Na(s) -> Na+(aq) + e-}$ will receive zero marks because ionisation energies are defined for gaseous atoms only.
