Lesson 20: Factors Affecting Rate and Maxwell-Boltzmann Distribution

What You'll Learn

  • Name five factors that change the rate of a chemical reaction and explain why each one works
  • Interpret a Maxwell-Boltzmann energy distribution curve and identify its key features
  • Explain how changing the temperature shifts the Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution
  • Explain how a catalyst changes the fraction of particles that can react without changing the energy distribution itself
  • Use Maxwell-Boltzmann distributions to compare reaction rates under different conditions

1. Five Factors That Affect Reaction Rate

What This Concept Is and Why It Matters

If you are making a product in a chemical plant, you want the reaction to go as fast as possible — faster production means more product per day and lower costs. If you are trying to preserve food, you want the reactions that cause spoilage to go as slowly as possible. To control reaction rates, you need to know what makes them change. There are five key factors, and each one can be explained using collision theory — the idea that particles must collide with enough energy and correct orientation to react.

Factor 1: Concentration of Reactants in Solution

When you increase the concentration of a reactant in a solution, there are more particles of that reactant dissolved in each unit of volume. Imagine a room with only three people in it — they will bump into each other only occasionally. Now imagine the same room with three hundred people — collisions become far more frequent. The same thing happens with particles in solution. Higher concentration means more particles per unit volume, which means more collisions happen every second. More collisions per second means more successful collisions per second, so the rate goes up.

Important note: Changing the concentration does not change the fraction of particles that have enough energy to react. It simply increases the total number of collisions, which proportionally increases the number of successful collisions.

Factor 2: Pressure of Gaseous Reactants

For reactions involving gases, increasing the pressure has the same effect as increasing the concentration. When you compress a gas into a smaller volume, the same number of particles now occupy less space. The particles are closer together, so they collide more often. The rate increases. Conversely, lowering the pressure spreads the particles out, collisions become less frequent, and the rate decreases.

Factor 3: Surface Area of Solid Reactants

When a solid reacts with a liquid or a gas, the reaction can only happen at the surface of the solid — the place where solid particles meet the other reactant. The particles buried deep inside the solid are not exposed to the other reactant and cannot react until the outer layers have been consumed.

If you take a solid lump and break it into many small pieces, you expose far more of its particles at the surface. A powdered solid has an enormous total surface area compared to a single large chunk of the same mass. The larger the surface area, the more solid particles are available to collide with the other reactant at any moment, so more collisions happen per second and the rate increases.

A common analogy: A single large log burns slowly, but sawdust made from that same log can burst into flames almost instantly because the tiny particles of sawdust expose so much more surface area to oxygen in the air.

Factor 4: Temperature

Changing the temperature affects the reaction rate in two ways, but one of these effects is far more important than the other.

First, raising the temperature makes particles move faster. Faster-moving particles collide more often — if you raise the temperature by 10°C, the collision frequency increases by only about 2% to 3%. This small increase in collision frequency contributes only a small amount to the rate increase.

Second — and this is the dominant effect — raising the temperature increases the fraction of particles that have kinetic energy greater than or equal to the activation energy. This fraction increases much more dramatically than the collision frequency. For a typical reaction, a 10°C rise in temperature roughly doubles the rate. This near-doubling comes almost entirely from the fact that many more particles now have enough energy to react, not from the slight increase in how often they collide.

The mathematical way to describe this is through the Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution, which we will study in the next section.

Factor 5: Addition of a Catalyst

A catalyst is a substance that increases the rate of a chemical reaction without being used up itself. It works by providing an alternative reaction pathway — a different sequence of bond-breaking and bond-forming steps — that has a lower activation energy than the uncatalysed pathway.

Because the activation energy is lower, a much larger fraction of the colliding particles now have enough energy to react. The catalyst does not change the energy of the particles themselves. It simply lowers the "entry fee" for the reaction. At the end of the reaction, the catalyst is regenerated unchanged and can be used again.

Many students confuse a catalyst with a reactant. A catalyst is not a reactant — it is not consumed. It participates in the reaction mechanism (it interacts with reactants during the process) but it emerges unchanged at the end.

2. The Maxwell-Boltzmann Distribution

What This Concept Is and Why It Matters

In any sample of gas or liquid, not all particles have the same kinetic energy. Some are moving slowly (low energy), most are moving at moderate speeds, and a few are moving very fast (high energy). The Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution is a graph that shows how the kinetic energies of particles in a sample are spread out — how many particles have each possible value of energy. Understanding this distribution is essential because it lets you see visually why temperature and catalysts have such large effects on reaction rates.

Key Features of the Maxwell-Boltzmann Curve

The curve is always drawn with kinetic energy on the horizontal x-axis and some measure of "how many particles have this energy" on the vertical y-axis (often labelled "number of particles" or "fraction of particles").

Feature 1: The curve starts at the origin. No particles have exactly zero kinetic energy. The curve begins at (0,0) on the graph.

Feature 2: The curve rises to a peak. The energy at which the curve reaches its highest point is called the most probable energy, sometimes written as E_mp. More particles have this energy than any other single energy value. But note: the most probable energy is not the same as the average energy. Because the curve is not symmetric, the average energy lies slightly to the right of the peak.

Feature 3: The curve falls off gradually to the right. As you move to higher and higher energies, fewer and fewer particles have those energies. The curve never quite reaches zero — in theory, there is always a tiny chance that a particle could have an extremely high energy, but the number of such particles is vanishingly small.

Feature 4: The total area under the curve equals the total number of particles in the sample. This is a fundamental property of any distribution curve: the area under the curve represents the count.

Feature 5: The curve is asymmetric. The tail on the right (high-energy side) is much longer than the tail on the left (low-energy side). This means there are more particles with energies significantly above the most probable energy than there are with energies significantly below it.

Activation Energy on the Maxwell-Boltzmann Curve

To use the Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution to think about reaction rates, we draw a vertical line at the value of the activation energy (Ea). All particles to the right of this line have energy ≥ Ea and can react if they collide with correct orientation. All particles to the left of this line have energy below Ea and cannot react no matter how they collide.

The shaded area under the curve to the right of the Ea line represents the number (or fraction) of particles capable of reacting. The larger this shaded area, the faster the reaction — because more particles are able to participate in successful collisions.

3. Effect of Temperature on the Maxwell-Boltzmann Distribution

What This Concept Is and Why It Matters

When you increase the temperature of a gas or liquid, the particles gain kinetic energy. But they do not all gain the same amount of energy. The Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution changes its shape in a specific way. Understanding these changes explains why a small temperature rise can cause a large increase in reaction rate.

When the temperature increases from, say, 300 K to 330 K:

Change 1: The peak moves to the right. The most probable energy shifts to a higher value because particles are, on average, moving faster.

Change 2: The peak becomes lower. Since the total number of particles is the same (the area under the curve stays constant), and the curve spreads out more, the peak cannot be as tall. The curve flattens.

Change 3: The curve broadens. The distribution of energies becomes wider. There is more spread in how fast different particles are moving.

Change 4: The tail extends further to the right. At the higher temperature, there are now many more particles with very high energies than there were at the lower temperature.

The critical point: The fraction of particles with energy ≥ Ea increases dramatically. On the graph, if you shade the area to the right of the Ea line for both the low-temperature curve and the high-temperature curve, the shaded area for the higher temperature is much larger — often two or three times larger. This is why the rate can double with only a 10°C temperature increase: the number of particles that can react roughly doubles, even though the total number of particles is unchanged.

Worked Example 1: Sketching Maxwell-Boltzmann Curves at Two Temperatures

Problem: A sample of nitrogen gas is held at 300 K. The temperature is then raised to 330 K. On the same set of axes, sketch the Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution curves for the gas at both temperatures. Label each curve with its temperature. Draw a vertical line representing the activation energy Ea for a particular reaction of nitrogen. Shade the region under each curve that represents particles with sufficient energy to react.

Strategy: We need to draw two curves that have the same total area (same number of particles). The higher-temperature curve must be shifted to the right with a lower, broader peak and a longer tail.

Solution and description of the sketch:

  • Draw the horizontal axis (labelled "Kinetic energy") and the vertical axis (labelled "Number of particles").
  • Draw the first curve for 300 K: it has a relatively tall peak positioned toward the left side of the graph, and its right-hand tail is fairly short.
  • Draw the second curve for 330 K: its peak is lower and shifted noticeably to the right. The curve is wider, and its right-hand tail extends much further than the tail of the 300 K curve.
  • Draw a vertical line at some energy value to the right of both peaks, and label it Ea.
  • Shade the area under the 300 K curve to the right of Ea. This area is small.
  • Shade the area under the 330 K curve to the right of Ea. This area is visibly much larger than the shaded area for 300 K.
  • Label the first curve "300 K" and the second curve "330 K."

Why this makes sense: At a higher temperature, more particles have high energies, so a larger proportion exceed any given threshold energy.

4. Effect of a Catalyst on the Maxwell-Boltzmann Distribution

What This Concept Is and Why It Matters

A catalyst changes the reaction pathway, not the particles. The particles have the same distribution of energies at a given temperature regardless of whether a catalyst is present. What changes is the position of the activation energy line on the graph.

When a catalyst is added, the activation energy decreases from Ea (uncatalysed) to Ea_cat (catalysed). On the Maxwell-Boltzmann graph, the vertical Ea line moves to the left. The curve itself does not change at all — the temperature is the same, so the particles have exactly the same energy distribution.

But because the threshold has moved to a lower energy, the area under the curve to the right of the new Ea_cat line is much larger than the area to the right of the original Ea line. More particles now qualify as having "enough energy to react." This is why a catalyst increases the rate without changing the temperature.

A common student mistake is thinking that a catalyst shifts the Maxwell-Boltzmann curve. It does not. The curve stays exactly where it was. Only the vertical Ea line moves.

Worked Example 2: Comparing Temperature Increase with Catalyst Addition

Problem: The reaction H₂(g) + I₂(g) → 2HI(g) has an activation energy of 173 kJ mol⁻¹ when uncatalysed. A platinum catalyst lowers the activation energy to 105 kJ mol⁻¹. The reaction is carried out at 700 K. Explain, using the Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution, why the catalysed reaction is faster than the uncatalysed reaction at the same temperature.

Strategy: We need to explain that at 700 K, there is one Maxwell-Boltzmann curve (same temperature). The only difference is where the Ea line is drawn. We compare the shaded areas.

Solution: At 700 K, the sample of H₂ and I₂ gas molecules has a specific Maxwell-Boltzmann energy distribution. This distribution is the same whether a catalyst is present or not, because the temperature is 700 K in both cases.

For the uncatalysed reaction, a vertical line is drawn at Ea = 173 kJ mol⁻¹. This line is far to the right on the energy axis, deep in the tail of the distribution. The area under the curve to the right of this line — representing particles with energy ≥ 173 kJ mol⁻¹ — is very small. Only a tiny fraction of collisions involve particles with this much energy.

For the catalysed reaction, a vertical line is drawn at Ea_cat = 105 kJ mol⁻¹. This line is shifted to the left, closer to the peak of the distribution. The area under the curve to the right of this line is much larger — a significantly greater fraction of particles now have sufficient energy. Many more collisions are now successful.

The collision frequency and the orientation requirements are unchanged. The only thing that has changed is the energy threshold, and this single change dramatically increases the number of effective collisions per second.

Why this makes sense: Lowering the activation energy by 68 kJ mol⁻¹ is a substantial reduction, and because the Maxwell-Boltzmann curve is steep in this region, moving the threshold leftward captures a much larger slice of the area under the curve.

Practice Problems

(a) Increasing the concentration of a reactant in solution.

(b) Increasing the temperature of the reaction mixture.

(c) Adding a suitable catalyst to the reaction.

  1. On a single set of axes, sketch the Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution curves for nitrogen gas (N₂) at a temperature of 300 K and at a temperature of 500 K. Label both curves clearly with their temperatures. Label the most probable energy for each curve. Draw and label a vertical line representing an activation energy Ea. Shade the area under each curve that corresponds to particles with enough energy to react.
  1. A student crushes a large marble chip into a fine powder and then reacts both the chip and the powder (separately) with hydrochloric acid of the same concentration. The powder reacts much faster. Explain this observation using collision theory. Your explanation should include the phrases "surface area" and "exposed particles" and should make clear why the powder produces a faster rate.
  1. The decomposition of hydrogen peroxide, 2H₂O₂(aq) → 2H₂O(l) + O₂(g), is slow at room temperature. When a small amount of solid manganese dioxide (MnO₂) is added, the solution bubbles vigorously as oxygen gas is produced rapidly. The MnO₂ can be filtered out at the end and appears unchanged. (a) What is the role of MnO₂ in this reaction? (b) Draw a Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution curve for H₂O₂ at room temperature. On the same graph, draw and label two vertical lines: one showing the activation energy without MnO₂ and one showing the activation energy with MnO₂. Shade the region of particles that can react in each case. (c) Explain why the Maxwell-Boltzmann curve itself does not change when MnO₂ is added.
  1. (IB-Exam Style) Two different chemical reactions, Reaction A and Reaction B, are carried out at the same temperature of 350 K. Reaction A has an activation energy of 50 kJ mol⁻¹. Reaction B has an activation energy of 100 kJ mol⁻¹.

(a) On a single sketch of a Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution curve for this temperature, draw and label vertical lines at Ea = 50 kJ mol⁻¹ and Ea = 100 kJ mol⁻¹. Shade the regions representing particles that can react in each reaction. Use your sketch to explain which reaction will have the faster rate and why.

(b) The temperature of Reaction B is raised from 350 K to 370 K, while Reaction A remains at 350 K. After this change, is it possible that Reaction B now has a faster rate than Reaction A? Explain your reasoning carefully. Include a description of how the Maxwell-Boltzmann curve for Reaction B changes when the temperature is raised.

(c) A student says the following: "When you raise the temperature, every single particle moves faster. That means every collision now has enough energy to react, so the reaction rate becomes infinite." Identify and correct two distinct scientific errors in this student's statement.

Answers

Answer 1

(a) Increasing the concentration of a reactant in solution increases the number of particles of that reactant in each unit of volume. This means particles of different reactants encounter each other more frequently — the collision frequency goes up. However, the energies of the individual particles are not changed by adding more of them. The fraction of particles that have energy ≥ Ea remains exactly the same as before. The rate increases because there are more collisions overall, and a fixed fraction of those collisions are successful. So concentration increases collision frequency only.

(b) Increasing the temperature has two effects, but they are very different in magnitude. First, particles move faster at higher temperature, so they collide slightly more often — collision frequency increases by a few percent for a 10°C rise. Second, and far more importantly, the entire Maxwell-Boltzmann energy distribution shifts to higher energies. This means the fraction of particles with energy ≥ Ea increases substantially — often enough to double the rate for just a 10°C rise. So temperature increases both collision frequency (a little) and the fraction of particles with E ≥ Ea (a lot).

(c) Adding a catalyst provides an alternative reaction pathway with a lower activation energy. The particles themselves do not change — they have the same energies and the same collision frequency at the same temperature. But because the energy threshold is now lower, the fraction of particles with energy ≥ Ea_cat (the catalysed activation energy) is much larger than the fraction with energy ≥ Ea (the uncatalysed activation energy). So the catalyst increases the fraction of particles that can react, without changing the collision frequency at all.

Answer 2

The sketch should show two curves on the same axes. The curve labelled "300 K" has a tall peak positioned more to the left and a relatively short right-hand tail. The curve labelled "500 K" has a lower, broader peak shifted to the right, and its tail extends much further to the right than the 300 K curve's tail. Both curves begin at the origin and have the same total area underneath. The most probable energy for the 500 K curve (the x-coordinate of its peak) is to the right of the most probable energy for the 300 K curve. The vertical Ea line is drawn to the right of both peaks. The shaded area under the 500 K curve to the right of Ea is visibly larger than the shaded area under the 300 K curve, indicating that more particles can react at the higher temperature.

Answer 3

When a solid reacts with a solution, the reaction can only occur at the surface of the solid, where the solid particles are in contact with the reactant particles in the solution. A large marble chip has most of its calcium carbonate particles buried inside the chip, where the hydrochloric acid cannot reach them. Only the particles on the outer surface are exposed and available to collide with H⁺ ions. When the marble is crushed into a fine powder, the same total mass of calcium carbonate now has an enormously larger total surface area. Many more CaCO₃ particles are exposed at the surfaces of the tiny powder grains. These exposed particles can collide with H⁺ ions from the acid simultaneously across the entire powder. Because there are many more collisions happening per second between reactants, the rate of reaction is much faster with the powder than with the single large chip. The powder uses up the acid more quickly because more solid particles are accessible at any given moment.

Answer 4

(a) Manganese dioxide (MnO₂) acts as a catalyst. It provides an alternative pathway for the decomposition of hydrogen peroxide with a lower activation energy. The MnO₂ participates in the reaction mechanism but is regenerated at the end, which is why it can be filtered out and appears unchanged — it is not consumed.

(b) The sketch shows one Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution curve (the temperature is the same whether MnO₂ is present or not, so there is only one curve). Two vertical lines are drawn: one labelled "Ea (uncatalysed)" further to the right, and one labelled "Ea (catalysed)" further to the left. The shaded area to the right of the catalysed Ea line is visibly larger than the shaded area to the right of the uncatalysed Ea line. Both shaded regions lie under the same curve; the only difference is the position of the vertical threshold.

(c) The Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution describes the kinetic energies of the H₂O₂ molecules themselves. This distribution depends only on the temperature of the sample. Adding MnO₂ does not change the temperature of the solution (or changes it negligibly), so the H₂O₂ molecules continue to have the same range and distribution of kinetic energies. The curve does not shift because the particles' energies have not changed. What has changed is not the particles but the reaction pathway — the catalyst provides a different transition state with a lower energy barrier. On the graph, this is represented by moving the Ea line, not by moving the curve.

Answer 5

(a) The sketch shows one Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution curve at 350 K. A vertical line at 50 kJ mol⁻¹ (Reaction A) is drawn closer to the peak. A vertical line at 100 kJ mol⁻¹ (Reaction B) is drawn further to the right in the tail of the curve. The shaded area to the right of 50 kJ mol⁻¹ is much larger than the shaded area to the right of 100 kJ mol⁻¹. This means that at 350 K, a much larger fraction of particles have energy ≥ 50 kJ mol⁻¹ than have energy ≥ 100 kJ mol⁻¹. Since both reactions are at the same temperature, the collision frequencies are similar, but many more collisions are successful in Reaction A because many more particles exceed its lower activation energy. Therefore, Reaction A has the faster rate.

(b) When the temperature of Reaction B is raised from 350 K to 370 K, the Maxwell-Boltzmann curve for Reaction B shifts to the right (the peak moves rightward, the curve flattens and broadens, and the tail extends further). The fraction of particles with energy ≥ 100 kJ mol⁻¹ increases. However, Reaction A still has an activation energy of only 50 kJ mol⁻¹, which is half of Reaction B's activation energy. Even after heating Reaction B by 20 K, the fraction of its particles exceeding 100 kJ mol⁻¹ is very likely still much smaller than the fraction of Reaction A's particles exceeding 50 kJ mol⁻¹ at 350 K. The difference in activation energy (a factor of 2) generally outweighs a modest 20 K temperature increase. So it is very unlikely that Reaction B overtakes Reaction A; Reaction A almost certainly remains faster.

(c) The student's statement contains two fundamental errors. First error: "Every single particle moves faster." This is incorrect. Raising the temperature shifts the entire distribution of energies to higher values, so the average energy increases. But there are still individual particles with low energies — the distribution still starts near the origin, and some particles are always moving slowly at any temperature. Temperature describes the average, not the individual behaviour of every particle. Second error: "Every collision now has enough energy to react, so the reaction rate becomes infinite." Even at extremely high temperatures, not all collisions have energy ≥ Ea (there is still a distribution, and some collisions involve low-energy particles). Furthermore, even collisions that do have enough energy still require correct orientation — particles must collide in the right geometry for bonds to break and form. The steric factor means that some fraction of high-energy collisions will always be unsuccessful because the molecules were not aligned properly. The rate can never become infinite.
