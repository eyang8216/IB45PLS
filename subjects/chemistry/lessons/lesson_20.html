Lesson 20: Factors Affecting Rate & Maxwell-Boltzmann Distribution

Syllabus reference: 6.1 — Collision Theory and Rates of Reaction

Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to:

  • Explain how concentration, pressure, surface area, temperature, and catalysts affect the rate of a chemical reaction using collision theory.
  • Sketch and interpret the Maxwell-Boltzmann energy distribution curve for particles in a gas.
  • Relate the area under the curve beyond $E_a$ to the proportion of particles with energy sufficient to react.
  • Predict how changing temperature shifts the Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution and affects reaction rate.
  • Define activation energy ($E_a$) as the minimum kinetic energy required for colliding particles to react.

1. Prior Knowledge Check

Question 1: What must happen for two particles to react when they collide?

▸ Show
Two conditions must be met: (1) the particles must collide with the correct orientation (the reactive parts of the molecules must face each other), and (2) they must possess at least the activation energy ($E_a$). A collision meeting both criteria is called an effective collision.

Question 2: Predict whether increasing the concentration of a reactant will increase or decrease the reaction rate, and briefly explain why.

▸ Show
Increasing concentration increases the rate. Higher concentration means more particles per unit volume, so the frequency of collisions increases. More collisions per second → more effective collisions per second → faster rate.

2. Collision Theory — The Foundation of Kinetics

For a reaction to occur, particles must collide. But not every collision produces products. Only collisions with (a) sufficient energy ($\ge E_a$) and (b) correct orientation lead to reaction. These are called effective collisions.

The rate of reaction is proportional to the frequency of effective collisions per unit time. Anything that increases the frequency of effective collisions increases the rate.

Check-in: Why does grinding a solid into a powder increase its reaction rate with a liquid?

▸ Show
Grinding increases the surface area of the solid. More surface area means more particles are exposed and available to collide with the liquid reactant. This increases the frequency of collisions and therefore the rate of effective collisions. The same mass reacts faster simply because more of it is accessible at any moment.

3. The Five Factors Affecting Reaction Rate

3.1 Concentration (and Pressure for Gases)

Increasing concentration places more particles in the same volume → higher collision frequency → more effective collisions per second → faster rate. For gases, increasing pressure (at constant temperature) compresses the gas, which is equivalent to increasing concentration.

3.2 Temperature

Raising temperature has two simultaneous effects. First, particles move faster (higher average kinetic energy), so collisions are more frequent — but this alone accounts for only a small fraction of the rate increase. The dominant effect is that the proportion of particles possessing energy $\ge E_a$ increases dramatically. This is best understood through the Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution.

3.3 Surface Area

For heterogeneous reactions (e.g., solid + liquid), only particles at the surface of the solid can react. Increasing surface area (by powdering, grating, or using smaller pieces) exposes more reactive sites, increasing collision frequency.

3.4 Catalysts

A catalyst provides an alternative reaction pathway with a lower activation energy ($E_{a,\text{cat}} < E_a$). The catalyst itself is not consumed. By lowering $E_a$, a much larger fraction of particles now have sufficient energy — the rate increases dramatically, often by factors of $10^3$ to $10^6$.

Check-in: A reaction at 30°C is slow. At 50°C, it proceeds rapidly. Explain this using collision theory and the Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution.

▸ Show
At the higher temperature, the Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution flattens and shifts to the right. Although the average kinetic energy increases only modestly, the area under the curve to the right of $E_a$ (the fraction of particles with $E \ge E_a$) increases exponentially. This exponential increase in the proportion of sufficiently energetic particles is the primary reason for the dramatic rate increase — far more than the modest increase in collision frequency alone.

4. The Maxwell-Boltzmann Distribution

The Maxwell-Boltzmann (M-B) distribution describes how kinetic energies are distributed among particles in a gas or liquid at a given temperature. Key features:

  • The curve starts at the origin — no particles have zero energy.
  • It rises to a peak (the most probable energy), then tails off to the right.
  • The curve is asymmetric — the tail extends to high energies.
  • The area under the entire curve represents the total number of particles.
  • The area to the right of $E_a$ represents particles with enough energy to react.
Figure 2: Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution at a single temperature. Only particles in the shaded region (energy ≥ Eₐ) can react upon collision. At moderate temperatures, this is a small fraction.

4.1 Effect of Temperature on the M-B Distribution

When temperature increases from $T_1$ to $T_2$ ($T_2 > T_1$):

  • The peak shifts right (higher most probable energy) and lowers.
  • The curve flattens and spreads out.
  • The total area remains constant (same number of particles).
  • The area beyond $E_a$ increases significantly — this is the key insight.
Figure 3: Effect of temperature on the Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution. At T₂, the shaded area beyond Eₐ (pink + blue) is much larger than at T₁ (blue only). The blue region represents particles that already had sufficient energy at T₁; the pink region shows the additional particles that now exceed Eₐ at the higher temperature.

Check-in: On a Maxwell-Boltzmann diagram, where does the activation energy of a catalysed reaction appear?

▸ Show
The catalysed pathway has a lower activation energy, labelled $E_{a,\text{cat}}$, drawn as a vertical line to the left of the uncatalysed $E_a$. Because $E_{a,\text{cat}}$ is lower, a much larger proportion of the area under the M-B curve lies to its right — hence a much larger fraction of particles can react, explaining why catalysts dramatically increase the rate without raising the temperature.

5. Worked Examples

Example 1 — Interpreting the M-B Curve:

On a Maxwell-Boltzmann diagram for a reaction at 298 K, the activation energy $E_a = 80$ kJ mol⁻¹ is marked. The curve shows that approximately 2% of particles have $E \ge E_a$. When the temperature is raised to 310 K, the fraction rises to about 8%. By what factor has the proportion of sufficiently energetic particles increased?

Solution: Factor = $8\% / 2\% = 4$. The proportion of particles with $E \ge E_a$ has quadrupled for a temperature rise of only 12 K. This illustrates why a ~10°C rise often doubles the reaction rate — the effect is exponential, not linear.

⚠️ Common wrong approach: Students sometimes claim the increased rate is "because particles move faster and collide more." While collision frequency does increase with temperature, the increase is only about 2% for a 10°C rise. The dominant effect — roughly 98% of the rate increase — comes from the exponential increase in the fraction of particles exceeding $E_a$.
Example 2 — Identifying the Limiting Factor:

Marble chips ($\ce{CaCO3}$) react with 2.0 mol dm⁻³ $\ce{HCl}$ producing $\ce{CO2}$. In Experiment A, large chips are used. In Experiment B, the same mass of powdered $\ce{CaCO3}$ is used. Both use the same volume and concentration of $\ce{HCl}$ at the same temperature. Sketch the expected volume–time graphs for both experiments on the same axes.

Solution: Both curves plateau at the same final volume of $\ce{CO2}$ (same mass of $\ce{CaCO3}$ means same moles of $\ce{CO2}$ produced). However, Experiment B (powder) has a steeper initial gradient and reaches the plateau sooner because the greater surface area gives a higher initial rate. The curve for B lies entirely above that of A until they converge at the plateau.
Example 3 — Collision Theory in Context:

The reaction $\ce{2HI(g) -> H2(g) + I2(g)}$ has $E_a = 183$ kJ mol⁻¹. At 500 K, the rate constant is $k = 6.4 \times 10^{-7}$ dm³ mol⁻¹ s⁻¹. Explain why this reaction is slow even at 500 K, using collision theory.

Solution: The activation energy of 183 kJ mol⁻¹ is very high. Even at 500 K, which corresponds to an average kinetic energy of only about 6.2 kJ mol⁻¹, only an extremely small fraction of $\ce{HI}$ molecules possess kinetic energy $\ge 183$ kJ mol⁻¹. Despite frequent collisions at this temperature, almost none of them are effective. The tiny value of $k$ ($6.4 \times 10^{-7}$) quantitatively reflects this.

⚠️ Common wrong approach: "The reaction is slow because HI is a stable molecule." Stability is a thermodynamic concept ($\Delta G$), not a kinetic one. A reaction can be thermodynamically favourable ($\Delta G < 0$) but kinetically slow due to high $E_a$. Do not confuse thermodynamics with kinetics.

⚠️ Common Mistakes to Avoid

  1. "Higher temperature increases rate because particles move faster and collide more." This is only a minor contributor (~2% per 10°C). The dominant reason is the exponential increase in the proportion of particles with $E \ge E_a$. Always mention the Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution when explaining temperature effects.
  2. "A catalyst lowers the activation energy by raising the energy of the reactants." No. A catalyst provides an entirely alternative pathway with a different (lower) transition state. The energies of reactants and products are unchanged — catalysis does not alter $\Delta H$.
  3. "The Maxwell-Boltzmann curve is symmetrical." It is not. It is skewed — the tail extends further to the right (high energies) than to the left. The mean energy lies to the right of the most probable energy.
  4. "Increasing concentration changes the shape of the M-B distribution." No. Concentration affects collision frequency, not the energy distribution. The M-B distribution depends only on temperature and molar mass. Concentration and pressure shift the rate by a different mechanism (more collisions per second).
  5. "Surface area affects the activation energy." No. Surface area affects how many particles are available to collide; $E_a$ is unchanged. Powdering a solid does not make individual collisions more likely to succeed — it just creates more opportunities for collisions.

6. End-of-Lesson Practice

Question 1 (Paper 1 style): Which change always increases the rate of a chemical reaction?
A. Increasing temperature   B. Increasing concentration of a reactant   C. Adding a catalyst   D. I, II, and III

Question 2: Sketch a Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution and label: (a) the most probable energy, (b) the mean energy, (c) the activation energy $E_a$, and (d) shade the area representing particles with sufficient energy to react.

Question 3: On the same M-B axes, draw curves for a reaction at 300 K and 320 K. Mark $E_a$ and explain which curve has the greater reaction rate and why.

Question 4: Explain why a 10°C rise typically doubles the reaction rate, even though the average kinetic energy increases by only about 3%.

Question 5: Hydrogen peroxide decomposes: $\ce{2H2O2(aq) -> 2H2O(l) + O2(g)}$. The reaction is slow at room temperature but rapid when $\ce{MnO2}$ powder is added. The $\ce{MnO2}$ is recovered unchanged at the end. Explain these observations using collision theory and catalysis.

Question 6: Magnesium ribbon reacts with dilute $\ce{HCl}$. State and explain the effect on the initial rate of: (a) using 2.0 mol dm⁻³ $\ce{HCl}$ instead of 1.0 mol dm⁻³, (b) cutting the ribbon into smaller pieces, (c) carrying out the reaction at 40°C instead of 20°C.

Answer 1: D. All three changes (increasing temperature, increasing concentration, adding a catalyst) always increase the rate. Note: For zero-order reactions, increasing concentration does not increase rate — but this question is at the pre-HL kinetics level (6.1). In Topic 6.1, all three are valid.

Answer 2: Diagram should show: asymmetric curve rising from origin, peak labelled "most probable energy," a vertical line further right labelled "mean energy," a vertical dashed line further right labelled "$E_a$," and the area under the curve to the right of $E_a$ shaded. The mean energy is always to the right of the most probable energy because the distribution is skewed.

Answer 3: The 320 K curve is flatter, with its peak shifted right and lower. The shaded area beyond $E_a$ is significantly larger for the 320 K curve. The reaction at 320 K is faster because a much greater proportion of particles now possess $E \ge E_a$, leading to more effective collisions per second.

Answer 4: The key is that the fraction of particles with $E \ge E_a$ depends exponentially on temperature (as described by the Arrhenius equation). A 3% increase in average KE translates to an exponential increase in the number of particles in the high-energy tail of the Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution. The area beyond $E_a$ can double or triple for a small temperature rise because the distribution is not linear — it has an exponential tail.

Answer 5: $\ce{MnO2}$ acts as a heterogeneous catalyst. It provides an alternative pathway with a lower $E_a$. On the Maxwell-Boltzmann diagram, $E_{a,\text{cat}}$ is drawn to the left of the uncatalysed $E_a$, so a much larger proportion of $\ce{H2O2}$ molecules now have sufficient energy to react. The catalyst is recovered unchanged because it is not consumed — it lowers $E_a$ without being used up. Catalysts do not alter $\Delta H$ or the equilibrium position.

Answer 6: (a) Doubling [HCl] approximately doubles the rate: more $\ce{H+}$ ions per unit volume → higher collision frequency between $\ce{H+}$ and $\ce{Mg}$ atoms → more effective collisions per second. (b) Cutting the ribbon increases surface area: more $\ce{Mg}$ atoms exposed to $\ce{H+}$ → higher collision frequency → faster rate. (c) Raising T from 20°C to 40°C approximately quadruples the rate: the Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution shifts, dramatically increasing the proportion of collisions with $E \ge E_a$.

7. Key Terms

TermDefinition
Activation energy ($E_a$)The minimum kinetic energy that colliding particles must possess for a reaction to occur
Effective collisionA collision that results in a reaction — must have correct orientation and energy $\ge E_a$
Collision frequencyThe number of collisions occurring per unit volume per unit time
Maxwell-Boltzmann distributionA graph showing the distribution of kinetic energies among particles in a gas or liquid at a given temperature
Most probable energyThe kinetic energy possessed by the largest number of particles (the peak of the M-B curve)
Mean energyThe arithmetic average kinetic energy of all particles; lies to the right of the most probable energy
CatalystA substance that increases the rate of a reaction by providing an alternative pathway with a lower $E_a$, without being consumed
Heterogeneous reactionA reaction where reactants are in different phases (e.g., solid + liquid); surface area affects rate
Homogeneous reactionA reaction where all reactants are in the same phase (e.g., all gases); concentration/pressure affects rate

8. Exam Tips

Paper 1 (Multiple Choice): M-B questions often show two curves and ask you to identify which corresponds to the higher temperature, or which change (catalyst vs. temperature) is being illustrated. Remember: a catalyst shifts $E_a$ left (the curve itself does NOT change shape); temperature changes the shape of the curve (flatter, shifted right). Do not confuse the two.
Paper 2 (Structured): When asked to "explain using collision theory," you must explicitly mention three things: (1) the change in collision frequency (if applicable), (2) the change in the proportion of particles with $E \ge E_a$ (if temperature or catalyst is involved), and (3) the resulting change in the frequency of effective collisions, which directly determines rate.
Common Data-Based Question: You may be given a table showing reaction times at different temperatures and asked to explain the trend. A complete answer: "At higher temperatures, the Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution broadens and shifts right. The area under the curve beyond $E_a$ increases significantly, meaning a much larger fraction of collisions are effective. This exponentially increases the rate, which is observed as a much shorter reaction time."
