Lesson 28: Weak Acids вЂ” Ka, pKa, and Equilibrium Calculations

Syllabus reference: 18.2 (HL) вЂ” Weak Acids and Bases, Ka and pKa

Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to:

  • Write the equilibrium expression ($K_a$) for any weak monoprotic acid and state what $K_a$ tells you about acid strength.
  • Calculate $[\ce{H+}]$ and pH of a weak acid solution given $K_a$ and initial concentration, using the approximation when appropriate.
  • Convert between $K_a$ and $\mathrm{p}K_a$ and use $\mathrm{p}K_a$ to compare the relative strengths of acids.
  • Determine $K_a$ experimentally from pH measurements at known concentration.

1. Prior Knowledge Check

Question 1: What is the difference between a strong acid and a weak acid?

в–ё Show
A strong acid (e.g., $\ce{HCl}$, $\ce{HNO3}$, $\ce{H2SO4}$) dissociates completely in water: every molecule releases its proton. A weak acid (e.g., $\ce{CH3COOH}$, $\ce{HF}$) dissociates only partially вЂ” an equilibrium is established between the undissociated acid and its ions.

Question 2: Write the equilibrium expression $K_c$ for the reaction $\ce{HA <=> H+ + A-}$.

в–ё Show
$$K_c = \frac{[\ce{H+}][\ce{A-}]}{[\ce{HA}]}$$ This is the general form. When the reaction is specifically an acid dissociation, we call this $K_a$ instead of $K_c$.

2. The Acid Dissociation Constant, $K_a$

For a weak monoprotic acid $\ce{HA}$, dissociation in water is incomplete вЂ” it reaches a dynamic equilibrium:

$$\ce{HA(aq) + H2O(l) <=> H3O+(aq) + A-(aq)}$$

The acid dissociation constant, $K_a$, is the equilibrium constant for this reaction:

Key formula вЂ” Acid Dissociation Constant: $$K_a = \frac{[\ce{H3O+}][\ce{A-}]}{[\ce{HA}]}$$ Often abbreviated as: $\displaystyle K_a = \frac{[\ce{H+}][\ce{A-}]}{[\ce{HA}]}$

$K_a$ is a constant at a given temperature (usually reported at 298 K). It does NOT depend on concentration вЂ” it is an intrinsic property of the acid. The smaller $K_a$, the weaker the acid: fewer products, more reactants at equilibrium.

2.1 The pKa Scale

Since $K_a$ values span many orders of magnitude (e.g., $\ce{HF}$: $K_a \approx 6.8\times10^{-4}$; $\ce{HCN}$: $K_a \approx 4.9\times10^{-10}$), chemists use a logarithmic scale:

$$\mathrm{p}K_a = -\log_{10} K_a \qquad \text{and} \qquad K_a = 10^{-\mathrm{p}K_a}$$

Key intuition: smaller $\mathrm{p}K_a$ = stronger acid. Each unit decrease in $\mathrm{p}K_a$ means the acid is 10 times stronger. Carboxylic acids have $\mathrm{p}K_a \approx 4-5$; phenols have $\mathrm{p}K_a \approx 10$.

Figure 1: The pKa scale. Stronger acids have smaller pKa values. Moving right в†’ weaker acids.

Check-in: Acid X has $K_a = 1.8 \times 10^{-5}$. Acid Y has $\mathrm{p}K_a = 3.2$. Which is stronger?

в–ё Show
$\mathrm{p}K_a$(X) = $-\log(1.8\times10^{-5}) = 4.74$. $\mathrm{p}K_a$(Y) = 3.2. Since smaller $\mathrm{p}K_a$ means stronger acid, Y is stronger. You can also compare: $K_a$(Y) = $10^{-3.2} = 6.3\times10^{-4}$, which is larger than $K_a$(X).

3. Calculating [HвЃє] and pH from $K_a$

For a weak acid with initial concentration $c$, we set up an ICE table (Initial, Change, Equilibrium):

$\ce{HA}$$\ce{H+}$$\ce{A-}$
Initial$c$$0$$0$
Change$-x$$+x$$+x$
Equilibrium$c - x$$x$$x$

Substituting into $K_a$:

$$K_a = \frac{(x)(x)}{c-x} = \frac{x^2}{c-x}$$

This is a quadratic. However, for weak acids where $K_a \ll c$, only a tiny fraction dissociates вЂ” meaning $x \ll c$. In this case we can approximate $c - x \approx c$, giving:

Approximation (valid when $K_a/c \leq 10^{-3}$): $$K_a \approx \frac{x^2}{c} \quad\Rightarrow\quad x = [\ce{H+}] = \sqrt{K_a \cdot c}$$ $$\text{pH} = -\log_{10}[\ce{H+}]$$
Figure 2: When to use the approximation. The rule of thumb: if Kв‚ђ/c is smaller than 10вЃ»Ві, the approximation introduces less than ~3% error.

Check-in: Calculate the pH of 0.100 mol dmвЃ»Ві $\ce{CH3COOH}$ ($K_a = 1.8 \times 10^{-5}$).

в–ё Show
Check: $K_a / c = 1.8\times10^{-5} / 0.100 = 1.8\times10^{-4} \leq 10^{-3}$ вњ“ вЂ” approximation valid.
$x = \sqrt{1.8\times10^{-5} \times 0.100} = \sqrt{1.8\times10^{-6}} = 1.34\times10^{-3}$ mol dmвЃ»Ві.
$\text{pH} = -\log(1.34\times10^{-3}) = \mathbf{2.87}$.

4. Finding $K_a$ from Experimental Data

If you measure the pH of a weak acid solution of known concentration, you can calculate $K_a$:

  1. From pH, calculate $[\ce{H+}] = 10^{-\text{pH}}$.
  2. Since $[\ce{H+}] = [\ce{A-}] = x$ and $[\ce{HA}]_{\text{eq}} = c - x$, substitute into $K_a = x^2 / (c - x)$.
  3. If $x \ll c$, use $K_a \approx x^2 / c$ (then verify the assumption).

Check-in: A 0.0500 mol dmвЃ»Ві solution of a weak acid has pH = 3.46. Calculate its $K_a$ and $\mathrm{p}K_a$.

в–ё Show
$[\ce{H+}] = 10^{-3.46} = 3.47\times10^{-4}$ mol dmвЃ»Ві. Since $x = 3.47\times10^{-4}$ and $c = 0.0500$, indeed $x \ll c$.
$K_a = (3.47\times10^{-4})^2 / 0.0500 = 1.20\times10^{-8} / 0.0500 = \mathbf{2.41\times10^{-6}}$
$\mathrm{p}K_a = -\log(2.41\times10^{-6}) = \mathbf{5.62}$.

5. Worked Examples

Example 1 вЂ” Direct pH from Ka (approximation):

Calculate the pH of 0.250 mol dmвЃ»Ві $\ce{HCOOH}$ (methanoic acid, $K_a = 1.8 \times 10^{-4}$).

Step 1: Check approximation: $K_a/c = 1.8\times10^{-4}/0.250 = 7.2\times10^{-4}$. This is close to $10^{-3}$ вЂ” let's use approximation first, then verify.
Step 2: $[\ce{H+}] = \sqrt{K_a \cdot c} = \sqrt{1.8\times10^{-4}\times0.250} = \sqrt{4.5\times10^{-5}} = 6.71\times10^{-3}$ mol dmвЃ»Ві.
Step 3: $\text{pH} = -\log(6.71\times10^{-3}) = \mathbf{2.17}$.
Verification: $x/c = 6.71\times10^{-3}/0.250 = 0.0268 = 2.68\%$. This is just above the usual 5% threshold вЂ” a full quadratic would give $x = 6.61\times10^{-3}$ (pH 2.18), a negligible difference.

вљ пёЏ Common wrong approach: Treating HCOOH as a strong acid and using $\text{pH} = -\log(0.250) = 0.60$. This is completely wrong вЂ” weak acids do NOT fully dissociate.
Example 2 вЂ” Finding Ka from pH (no approximation needed):

A 0.0200 mol dmвЃ»Ві solution of $\ce{HF}$ has pH = 2.47. Calculate $K_a$ and $\mathrm{p}K_a$ for $\ce{HF}$.

Step 1: $[\ce{H+}] = 10^{-2.47} = 3.39\times10^{-3}$ mol dmвЃ»Ві.
Step 2: $[\ce{F-}] = [\ce{H+}] = 3.39\times10^{-3}$ mol dmвЃ»Ві. $[\ce{HF}]_{\text{eq}} = 0.0200 - 0.00339 = 0.01661$ mol dmвЃ»Ві.
Step 3: $K_a = (3.39\times10^{-3})^2 / 0.01661 = 1.15\times10^{-5} / 0.01661 = \mathbf{6.92\times10^{-4}}$.
Step 4: $\mathrm{p}K_a = -\log(6.92\times10^{-4}) = \mathbf{3.16}$.

вљ пёЏ Common wrong approach: Using $K_a = [\ce{H+}]^2 / c$ without subtracting $x$ from $c$. This gives $(3.39\times10^{-3})^2 / 0.0200 = 5.75\times10^{-4}$, which is a 17% error вЂ” unacceptable for a moderate acid like HF where dissociation is ~17%.
Example 3 вЂ” Comparing Acid Strengths with pKa:

Three acids: $\ce{CH3COOH}$ (pKa = 4.76), $\ce{HClO}$ (pKa = 7.54), and $\ce{HNO2}$ (pKa = 3.34).
(a) Rank them from strongest to weakest.
(b) How many times stronger is $\ce{HNO2}$ than $\ce{CH3COOH}$?

(a) Strongest = smallest pKa: $\ce{HNO2}$ (3.34) > $\ce{CH3COOH}$ (4.76) > $\ce{HClO}$ (7.54).
(b) $\Delta\mathrm{p}K_a = 4.76 - 3.34 = 1.42$. Each unit is a factor of 10, so $\ce{HNO2}$ is $10^{1.42} \approx \mathbf{26\times}$ stronger than $\ce{CH3COOH}$.

вљ пёЏ Common wrong approach: Saying pKa difference of 1.42 means the acid is 1.42Г— stronger. It's on a logarithmic scale вЂ” the factor is $10^{\Delta\mathrm{p}K_a}$, not $\Delta\mathrm{p}K_a$ itself.

вљ пёЏ Common Mistakes to Avoid

  1. Treating weak acids as strong acids. You cannot simply say $\text{pH} = -\log c$ for a weak acid. A 0.1 mol dmвЃ»Ві solution of a weak acid does not have pH = 1 вЂ” it will typically be pH 2вЂ“4 depending on $K_a$.
  2. Confusing $K_a$ with acid strength. Larger $K_a$ = stronger acid. Larger $\mathrm{p}K_a$ = weaker acid. Students frequently reverse this.
  3. Using the approximation when it is not valid. If $K_a/c > 10^{-3}$ (or dissociation > 5%), you must solve the quadratic. IB markers will check whether you verified your assumption.
  4. Forgetting that $K_a$ is temperature-dependent. All equilibrium constants vary with temperature. The $K_a$ values in data booklets are for 298 K. If the question states a different temperature, the values change.
  5. Using $[\ce{HA}]_{\text{initial}}$ instead of $[\ce{HA}]_{\text{equilibrium}}$ in $K_a$. $K_a = [\ce{H+}][\ce{A-}]/[\ce{HA}]_{\textbf{eq}}$, not $[\ce{HA}]_{\text{initial}}$. The "a" in $K_a$ is always about the equilibrium concentrations.

(b) If both are at the same concentration, which solution has the lower pH? (c) Explain why a solution of $\ce{HA}$ is a better conductor of electricity than $\ce{HB}$ at equal concentrations.

Answer 1: D. Weak acids have pKa > 3 typically. A is wrong (weak acids have small Ka). B describes a strong acid. C is wrong (weak acid в†’ weak conjugate base, or more precisely, the conjugate base of a weak acid is relatively strong but still a weak base).

Answer 2: $[\ce{H+}] = 10^{-3.12} = 7.59\times10^{-4}$. $K_a = (7.59\times10^{-4})^2 / 0.150 = \mathbf{3.84\times10^{-6}}$. $\mathrm{p}K_a = -\log(3.84\times10^{-6}) = \mathbf{5.42}$.

Answer 3: Check: $K_a/c = 6.3\times10^{-5}/0.0500 = 1.26\times10^{-3}$ вЂ” borderline, use quadratic or approximation with check. Using approximation: $x = \sqrt{6.3\times10^{-5} \times 0.0500} = \sqrt{3.15\times10^{-6}} = 1.77\times10^{-3}$. pH = $-\log(1.77\times10^{-3}) = \mathbf{2.75}$. Verification: $1.77\times10^{-3}/0.0500 = 3.5\%$, acceptable.

Answer 4: (a) $K_a = 10^{-4.88} = 1.32\times10^{-5}$. (b) $x = \sqrt{1.32\times10^{-5}\times0.200} = \sqrt{2.64\times10^{-6}} = 1.62\times10^{-3}$ mol dmвЃ»Ві. pH = 2.79. (c) % dissociated = $(1.62\times10^{-3}/0.200)\times100 = \mathbf{0.81\%}$.

Answer 5: (a) $[\ce{H+}] = 10^{-2.67} = 2.14\times10^{-3}$. $K_a = (2.14\times10^{-3})^2/(0.0800 - 0.00214) = 4.58\times10^{-6}/0.07786 = 5.88\times10^{-5}$. $\mathrm{p}K_a = 4.23$. (b) Lactate pKa = 3.86 vs. our 4.23 вЂ” these differ by 0.37 units, so HX is probably not pure lactic acid (though isomers with different pKa values exist).

Answer 6: (a) $K_a(\ce{HA})/K_a(\ce{HB}) = 10^{-4.20}/10^{-5.80} = 10^{1.60} \approx 39.8$. HA is about 40Г— stronger than HB. (b) HA has the lower pH (stronger acid в†’ more $\ce{H+}$ at same concentration). (c) HA produces more ions (higher degree of dissociation), so it has more charge carriers and conducts electricity better.

7. Key Terms

TermDefinition
Weak acidAn acid that dissociates only partially in aqueous solution, establishing an equilibrium
$K_a$ (acid dissociation constant)The equilibrium constant for the dissociation of a weak acid: $K_a = [\ce{H+}][\ce{A-}]/[\ce{HA}]$
$\mathrm{p}K_a$The negative base-10 logarithm of $K_a$: $\mathrm{p}K_a = -\log_{10}K_a$. Smaller $\mathrm{p}K_a$ = stronger acid
ICE tableA structured method (Initial, Change, Equilibrium) for calculating equilibrium concentrations
ApproximationAssuming $c - x \approx c$ when $x$ is very small compared to $c$ (valid when $K_a/c \leq 10^{-3}$)
Degree of dissociation ($\alpha$)The fraction of acid molecules that have dissociated: $\alpha = x/c = [\ce{H+}]/[\ce{HA}]_{\text{initial}}$
Monoprotic acidAn acid that can donate only one proton per molecule (e.g., $\ce{HCl}$, $\ce{CH3COOH}$)
Conjugate baseThe species formed when an acid donates a proton; $\ce{A-}$ is the conjugate base of $\ce{HA}$

8. Exam Tips

Paper 1 (Multiple Choice): Expect questions that ask you to compare acid strengths given $K_a$ or $\mathrm{p}K_a$ values. Remember: larger $K_a$ = stronger, smaller $\mathrm{p}K_a$ = stronger. A common distractor reverses this. Also look for questions where you estimate $[\ce{H+}]$ using $K_a$ and concentration without a calculator вЂ” use $\sqrt{K_a c}$ and round sensibly.
Paper 2 (Structured): When calculating pH from $K_a$, always state your assumption explicitly (e.g., "assuming $x \ll c$, so $c-x \approx c$") and then verify it. IB examiners award a mark for checking that the assumption is valid. If your calculated $x$ is more than 5% of $c$, you must note that the quadratic should be used.
Data Booklet: The IB Chemistry Data Booklet contains $K_a$ and $\mathrm{p}K_a$ values for common weak acids. Familiarise yourself with the location вЂ” you may need to find a value quickly. $\ce{CH3COOH}$: $K_a = 1.8\times10^{-5}$, $\mathrm{p}K_a = 4.76$ is the most commonly tested weak acid.
