Lesson 4: Atomic Emission Spectra — Hydrogen Spectrum & Convergence

Syllabus reference: 2.4 (Atomic Emission Spectra) and 2.5 (HL — Atomic Spectra Calculations)

Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to:

  • Distinguish between continuous and line spectra and explain how a line spectrum is produced.
  • Describe the hydrogen emission spectrum, naming the Lyman, Balmer, and Paschen series.
  • Explain why the hydrogen spectrum converges at high energy.
  • Calculate the energy and wavelength of spectral lines using $\Delta E = h\nu$ and $c = \lambda\nu$.
  • Determine the ionisation energy of hydrogen from the convergence limit (HL).

1. Prior Knowledge Check

Q1: What happens when an electron in an atom absorbs energy?

▸ Show
The electron jumps to a higher energy level (higher n). The atom is now in an excited state. When the electron falls back to a lower energy level, it releases the absorbed energy as a photon of light.

Q2: What is the relationship between energy and frequency of light?

▸ Show
$\Delta E = h\nu$, where $h = 6.63 \times 10^{-34}$ J s (Planck's constant) and $\nu$ is frequency in Hz (s⁻¹). Higher frequency = higher energy. Also: $c = \lambda\nu$, where $c = 3.00 \times 10^8$ m s⁻¹ and $\lambda$ is wavelength in meters.

2. Continuous vs. Line Spectra

When white light passes through a prism, it spreads into a continuous spectrum — a smooth rainbow containing all wavelengths. This is what you see from the Sun, a light bulb, or any hot solid/liquid. But when you heat a single element as a gas and pass its light through a prism, you see something completely different: a line spectrum — discrete, sharp, coloured lines on a dark background.

Figure 1: A continuous spectrum (top) contains all wavelengths blended together. A line spectrum (bottom) contains only specific, discrete wavelengths — each appears as a sharp line. The position of each line corresponds to a specific electron transition.

3. How a Line Spectrum is Produced

The process has three steps:

  1. Excitation: Energy (heat or electricity) is supplied to the gaseous atoms. An electron absorbs this energy and jumps to a higher energy level (further from the nucleus). The atom is now in an excited state.
  2. Relaxation: The excited electron is unstable and falls back to a lower energy level. The energy difference between the two levels is released as a photon of light.
  3. Detection: The photon has a specific energy ($\Delta E = E_{\text{higher}} - E_{\text{lower}}$), which corresponds to a specific wavelength (colour). Each possible transition produces one line.
Figure 2: An electron absorbs energy to jump UP (red arrow). When it falls DOWN (green and blue arrows), it emits a photon. The energy of the photon depends on which two levels are involved. Larger jumps = higher energy = shorter wavelength.

5. Convergence

As the upper energy level gets higher (n → ∞), the energy difference between levels gets smaller and smaller. This means the spectral lines get closer together at high energy until they eventually merge into a continuum. This point is called the convergence limit.

The convergence limit represents the energy needed to completely remove the electron from the atom — that is, the ionisation energy. When an electron is excited to n = ∞, it is no longer bound to the nucleus.

Check-in: Why do the lines in the Lyman series get closer together as you move toward higher energy (shorter wavelength)?

▸ Show
The energy levels get closer together as n increases. The energy gap between n = 1 and n = 2 is large; the gap between n = 9 and n = 10 is tiny. Since spectral lines represent the energy gaps between levels, the lines get closer together and merge at the convergence limit (n = ∞).

6. Calculations (HL)

The energy of a photon is related to its frequency and wavelength:

$$\Delta E = h\nu \quad \text{and} \quad c = \lambda\nu \quad \Rightarrow \quad \Delta E = \frac{hc}{\lambda}$$

$h = 6.63 \times 10^{-34}$ J s, $c = 3.00 \times 10^8$ m s⁻¹

Worked: A line in the Balmer series has wavelength $\lambda = 656$ nm. Calculate its energy in joules and in kJ mol⁻¹.

Step 1: Convert wavelength to meters. $\lambda = 656\text{ nm} = 6.56 \times 10^{-7}\text{ m}$.
Step 2: Calculate energy per photon. $\Delta E = hc/\lambda = (6.63\times10^{-34} \times 3.00\times10^8) / (6.56\times10^{-7}) = 3.03 \times 10^{-19}\text{ J}$.
Step 3: Convert to kJ mol⁻¹. Multiply by Avogadro's number and divide by 1000: $E = 3.03\times10^{-19} \times 6.02\times10^{23} / 1000 = \mathbf{182\text{ kJ mol}^{-1}}$.

⚠️ Common wrong approach: Using nm directly in $\Delta E = hc/\lambda$ without converting to meters. This gives an answer off by a factor of 10⁹.

6.1 Finding Ionisation Energy from Convergence (HL)

If the convergence limit is at frequency $\nu_{\text{conv}}$, then the ionisation energy is simply $\Delta E = h\nu_{\text{conv}}$. This equals the energy needed to excite the electron from the ground state (n = 1) to n = ∞.

Check-in (HL): The convergence limit of the Lyman series occurs at $\nu = 3.29 \times 10^{15}$ Hz. Calculate the ionisation energy of hydrogen in kJ mol⁻¹.

▸ Show
$\Delta E = h\nu = 6.63\times10^{-34} \times 3.29\times10^{15} = 2.18 \times 10^{-18}$ J per atom. In kJ mol⁻¹: $(2.18\times10^{-18} \times 6.02\times10^{23}) / 1000 = \mathbf{1310\text{ kJ mol}^{-1}}$. This is the experimental ionisation energy of hydrogen — the energy to remove its electron from n = 1.

7. Worked Examples

Example 1: A line in the hydrogen spectrum has energy $4.09 \times 10^{-19}$ J. (a) Calculate its frequency. (b) Calculate its wavelength. (c) In which region of the EM spectrum does it lie?

(a) $\nu = \Delta E / h = 4.09\times10^{-19} / 6.63\times10^{-34} = 6.17 \times 10^{14}$ Hz.
(b) $\lambda = c / \nu = 3.00\times10^8 / 6.17\times10^{14} = 4.86 \times 10^{-7}$ m = 486 nm.
(c) 486 nm is in the visible region (blue-green). This is actually the Hβ line in the Balmer series.
Example 2 (HL): The convergence limit in the Balmer series occurs at $\lambda = 365$ nm. Calculate the energy difference between n = 2 and n = ∞ in kJ mol⁻¹. Explain what this energy represents.

Step 1: $\lambda = 3.65 \times 10^{-7}$ m. $\Delta E = hc/\lambda = (6.63\times10^{-34}\times3.00\times10^8)/(3.65\times10^{-7}) = 5.45\times10^{-19}$ J.
Step 2: In kJ mol⁻¹: $(5.45\times10^{-19}\times6.02\times10^{23})/1000 = 328$ kJ mol⁻¹.
Interpretation: This is the energy needed to remove an electron from the n = 2 level (not the ground state!). It is smaller than the ionisation energy from n = 1 (1310 kJ mol⁻¹) because the electron is already partially removed from the nucleus.

⚠️ Common Mistakes to Avoid

  1. Confusing absorption and emission spectra. An emission spectrum has coloured lines on a dark background. An absorption spectrum has dark lines on a rainbow background. The IB asks about emission spectra unless stated otherwise.
  2. Thinking "convergence" means lines converge to zero energy. Lines converge at high energy (short wavelength) — as the energy gap between higher and higher n levels approaches a maximum (the ionisation limit).
  3. Forgetting to convert wavelength to meters. $\lambda$ in $\Delta E = hc/\lambda$ must be in meters. If you are given nm, divide by $10^9$. This is the #1 mistake on calculation questions.
  4. Mixing up the series. The Balmer series is the ONLY series with lines in the visible region. If a question mentions "visible light," the lower level must be n = 2.

8. End-of-Lesson Practice

Q1 (Paper 1): The convergence limit in the Lyman series of hydrogen occurs at 91.2 nm. What does this energy correspond to?
A. The energy to excite from n=1 to n=2   B. The energy to remove the electron from n=1   C. The energy to remove the electron from n=2   D. The energy difference between n=2 and n=3

Q2: Explain why the hydrogen emission spectrum consists of discrete lines rather than a continuous spectrum.

Q3: A line in the Balmer series has wavelength 434 nm. Calculate its: (a) frequency, (b) energy per photon in joules, (c) energy in kJ mol⁻¹.

Q4: Describe the difference between the Lyman series and the Balmer series in terms of (a) the lower energy level involved, (b) the region of the electromagnetic spectrum in which they appear.

Q5 (HL): The energy required to remove the electron from a hydrogen atom in the n = 2 state is 328 kJ mol⁻¹. (a) Explain why this is smaller than the ionisation energy from n = 1. (b) Calculate the wavelength of the convergence limit of the Balmer series.

A1: B. The convergence limit of the Lyman series (transitions to n = 1) corresponds to completely removing the electron from the ground state — the ionisation energy.

A2: Electrons in an atom can only exist in specific quantised energy levels. When an electron falls from a higher level to a lower level, it releases energy equal to the exact difference between those two levels. Since only specific energy transitions are possible, only specific photon wavelengths (lines) are produced — never a continuous range.

A3: (a) $\lambda = 4.34\times10^{-7}$ m. $\nu = c/\lambda = 3.00\times10^8 / 4.34\times10^{-7} = 6.91\times10^{14}$ Hz. (b) $\Delta E = h\nu = 6.63\times10^{-34} \times 6.91\times10^{14} = 4.58\times10^{-19}$ J. (c) $(4.58\times10^{-19} \times 6.02\times10^{23}) / 1000 = 276$ kJ mol⁻¹.

A4: (a) Lyman: electrons fall to n = 1. Balmer: electrons fall to n = 2. (b) Lyman series appears in the ultraviolet (UV) region. Balmer series appears in the visible (and near-UV) region. Transitions to n = 1 release more energy (shorter λ) than transitions to n = 2.

A5 (HL): (a) The electron at n = 2 is further from the nucleus and partially shielded by the n = 1 electrons. Less energy is needed to remove it compared to an electron at n = 1. (b) $\Delta E = 328$ kJ mol⁻¹ = $3.28\times10^5$ J mol⁻¹. Per atom: $3.28\times10^5 / 6.02\times10^{23} = 5.45\times10^{-19}$ J. $\lambda = hc/\Delta E = (6.63\times10^{-34}\times3.00\times10^8)/(5.45\times10^{-19}) = 3.65\times10^{-7}$ m = 365 nm.

10. Exam Tips

Paper 1: Questions often ask you to identify which series a given wavelength belongs to. Remember: visible = Balmer. If the wavelength is in nm and between ~400-700, it's Balmer (n=2). Below 400 nm could be Balmer near-UV or Lyman.
Paper 2 (HL): Calculation questions on $\Delta E = hc/\lambda$ are worth 2-3 marks. Always show the unit conversion from nm to m explicitly — it's worth a method mark. The energy per atom → kJ mol⁻¹ conversion (multiply by $N_A$, divide by 1000) is also a common mark point.
