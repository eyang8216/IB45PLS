Lesson 30: Titration Curves & Indicators

Syllabus reference: 18.3–18.4 (HL) — pH Curves, Buffer Regions, Acid–Base Indicators

Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to:

  • Sketch and interpret pH titration curves for all four acid–base combinations (SA–SB, SA–WB, WA–SB, WA–WB).
  • Identify key features: initial pH, buffer region, equivalence point, and the vertical jump.
  • Select an appropriate indicator for a given titration using pKIn values.
  • Explain why the equivalence point pH varies depending on the acid–base combination.

1. Prior Knowledge Check

Question 1: Define the equivalence point and the end point in a titration.

▸ Show
Equivalence point: The point where exactly enough titrant has been added to react completely with the analyte (stoichiometric equivalence). End point: The point where the indicator changes colour. Ideally these coincide, but in practice there is a small difference (titration error).

Question 2: What is the pH of pure water at 298 K? What happens to this pH if salt is dissolved in it?

▸ Show
pH = 7.00 at 298 K. Dissolving a salt may change the pH if the ions undergo hydrolysis — e.g., $\ce{CH3COONa}$ produces a basic solution (pH > 7), while $\ce{NH4Cl}$ produces an acidic solution (pH < 7).

2. The Four Types of Titration Curves

A pH titration curve plots pH (y-axis) against volume of titrant added (x-axis). The shape reveals whether the acid and base are strong or weak. The IB expects you to recognise and sketch all four combinations.

2.1 Strong Acid + Strong Base (SA–SB)

Example: $\ce{HCl + NaOH -> NaCl + H2O}$. The salt ($\ce{NaCl}$) is neutral — no hydrolysis. Equivalence point pH = 7.00. The vertical jump is large: pH ~3 to ~11 over a few drops.

Figure 1: SA–SB titration curve. Starts low (pH ~1 for 0.1 mol dm⁻³ HCl), ends high (pH ~13). Equivalence at pH 7. Largest vertical jump of all four types.

2.2 Weak Acid + Strong Base (WA–SB)

Example: $\ce{CH3COOH + NaOH -> CH3COONa + H2O}$. The salt ($\ce{CH3COONa}$) is basic — acetate hydrolyses: $\ce{CH3COO- + H2O <=> CH3COOH + OH-}$. Equivalence point pH > 7 (typically 8–10).

Key features: starts higher than SA–SB, has a buffer region (gentle slope near half-equivalence), pH = pKₐ at half-equivalence.

Figure 2: WA–SB titration. Note the buffer region (gentle slope), half-equivalence point at pH = pKₐ, and equivalence point above pH 7.

2.3 Strong Acid + Weak Base (SA–WB)

Example: $\ce{HCl + NH3 -> NH4Cl}$. The salt ($\ce{NH4Cl}$) is acidic — ammonium hydrolyses: $\ce{NH4+ + H2O <=> NH3 + H3O+}$. Equivalence point pH < 7 (typically 4–6). The buffer region after half-equivalence.

2.4 Weak Acid + Weak Base (WA–WB)

No sharp vertical jump — you cannot use indicators for WA–WB titrations. The curve is very gradual, and the equivalence point is ill-defined. These titrations are done by conductometry, not indicators.

Check-in: For the titration of $\ce{NH3}$ (weak base) with $\ce{HCl}$ (strong acid), would the equivalence point pH be above, below, or equal to 7?

▸ Show
Below 7 (typically pH 4–6). The product $\ce{NH4Cl}$ is acidic — $\ce{NH4+}$ is a weak acid that donates H⁺ to water, making the solution acidic at the equivalence point.

3. Acid–Base Indicators

An indicator is itself a weak acid ($\ce{HIn}$) whose conjugate base ($\ce{In-}$) has a different colour:

$$\ce{HIn <=> H+ + In-}$$ $$\text{colour 1} \qquad \text{colour 2}$$

The indicator's colour depends on the pH of the solution. The indicator dissociation constant $K_{\mathrm{In}}$ determines the transition range:

$$\mathrm{pH} = \mathrm{p}K_{\mathrm{In}} \pm 1$$

At $\mathrm{pH} = \mathrm{p}K_{\mathrm{In}}$, $[\ce{HIn}] = [\ce{In-}]$ and the indicator shows an intermediate colour. For a useful titration, the indicator must change colour within the vertical jump of the titration curve.

Check-in: Phenolphthalein has pKIn = 9.3 (colourless → pink). Methyl orange has pKIn = 3.7 (red → yellow). Which indicator would you use for (a) HCl + NaOH, (b) CH₃COOH + NaOH?

▸ Show
(a) HCl + NaOH: equivalence at pH 7, vertical jump from ~3 to ~11 — both work (phenolphthalein better since its range 8.3–10 lies squarely in the jump). (b) CH₃COOH + NaOH: equivalence at pH ~8.7 — phenolphthalein (range 8.3–10) works well; methyl orange (range 3.1–4.4) would change colour far too early, well before the equivalence point.

5. Worked Examples

Example 1 — Sketching a Titration Curve:

Sketch the pH curve for 25.0 cm³ of 0.100 mol dm⁻³ $\ce{HCl}$ titrated with 0.100 mol dm⁻³ $\ce{NaOH}$. Label the initial pH, equivalence volume, equivalence pH, and the vertical region.

Step 1 — Initial pH: $[\ce{H+}] = 0.100$ mol dm⁻³, pH = 1.00.
Step 2 — Equivalence volume: $n(\ce{HCl}) = 0.100 \times 0.0250 = 0.00250$ mol. Same moles of NaOH needed: $V = 0.00250/0.100 = 0.0250$ dm³ = 25.0 cm³.
Step 3 — Equivalence pH: NaCl is neutral, pH = 7.00.
Sketch: Start at (0, 1), gentle rise to ~pH 3 by 24.5 cm³, vertical jump from ~3 to ~11 at 25.0 cm³, then gentle rise to pH ~13 at 50 cm³.

⚠️ Common wrong approach: Drawing the equivalence point at 12.5 cm³ (half the final volume) instead of the stoichiometric equivalence volume (25.0 cm³).
Example 2 — Choosing an Indicator:

A student titrates 0.100 mol dm⁻³ $\ce{CH3COOH}$ with 0.100 mol dm⁻³ $\ce{NaOH}$. The equivalence point occurs at pH 8.7. The available indicators are: bromocresol green (pKIn = 4.7), bromothymol blue (pKIn = 7.0), and phenolphthalein (pKIn = 9.3). Which indicator should she choose?

Analysis: The indicator's transition range is pKIn ± 1. Phenolphthalein: range 8.3–10.0. The equivalence pH (8.7) falls within this range. Bromothymol blue (6.0–8.0) misses slightly. Bromocresol green (3.7–5.7) is far too low — it would change colour during the buffer region.
Answer: Phenolphthalein.

⚠️ Common wrong approach: Choosing the indicator whose pKIn is closest to 7 regardless of the equivalence point pH. The indicator must match the actual equivalence point pH, not pH 7.
Example 3 — Analysing an Unknown Titration Curve:

A titration curve shows: initial pH ≈ 2.9, a buffer region from 5–22 cm³, half-equivalence at 12.5 cm³ where pH = 4.76, equivalence at 25.0 cm³, equivalence pH ≈ 8.7, and final pH ≈ 12.5. (a) Identify the acid as strong or weak. (b) Identify the base as strong or weak. (c) What is the pKₐ of the acid?

(a) Weak acid. Initial pH 2.9 (not 1.0 for 0.1 mol dm⁻³ strong acid) and presence of a buffer region confirm it's weak.
(b) Strong base. Equivalence pH > 7 and final pH ~12.5 (not ~11 for a weak base) indicate a strong base.
(c) pKₐ = 4.76 (pH at half-equivalence). This matches ethanoic acid.

⚠️ Common wrong approach: Assuming the acid is strong because the initial pH is low. Even weak acids can have pH 2–3 at 0.1 mol dm⁻³ — the telltale sign of a weak acid is the buffer region.

⚠️ Common Mistakes to Avoid

  1. Thinking all equivalence points are at pH 7. Only SA–SB titrations have equivalence at pH 7. WA–SB gives pH > 7 (basic salt). SA–WB gives pH < 7 (acidic salt).
  2. Believing the buffer region makes the acid strong. Only weak acids show a buffer region. Strong acids have no buffer region because they are fully dissociated — there is no HA reservoir to buffer with.
  3. Selecting an indicator without checking the vertical jump pH range. The indicator's transition range (pKIn ± 1) must overlap with the vertical section of the curve. If not, the end point and equivalence point will differ significantly.
  4. Confusing the half-equivalence point with the equivalence point. At half-equivalence, half the acid has been neutralised — this is where pH = pKₐ. At equivalence, the neutralisation is complete — this is where the vertical jump occurs.

6. End-of-Lesson Practice

Question 1 (Paper 1 style): The equivalence point of a titration between 0.100 mol dm⁻³ $\ce{HCl}$ and 0.100 mol dm⁻³ $\ce{NH3}$ occurs at pH:
A. 7.0   B. Below 7.0   C. Above 7.0   D. Cannot be determined

Question 2: Sketch and label the pH curve for 20.0 cm³ of 0.200 mol dm⁻³ $\ce{CH3COOH}$ titrated with 0.200 mol dm⁻³ $\ce{NaOH}$. Mark: initial pH (~2.7), buffer region, half-equivalence (10.0 cm³, pH = 4.76), equivalence (20.0 cm³, pH ~8.7).

Question 3: A student titrates $\ce{HCl}$ with $\ce{NaOH}$ and uses phenolphthalein (range 8.3–10.0). (a) Explain whether this is appropriate. (b) Would methyl orange (range 3.1–4.4) also work?

Question 4: A titration curve has these features: initial pH = 11.1, buffer region, equivalence pH = 5.3, final pH ~1. (a) What is being titrated (acid or base in the flask)? (b) Is it strong or weak? (c) Is the titrant strong or weak?

Question 5: Explain why WA–WB titrations cannot use indicators. What alternative method is used?

Answer 1: B. The product NH₄Cl is acidic because NH₄⁺ hydrolyses: NH₄⁺ + H₂O ⇌ NH₃ + H₃O⁺. The equivalence point is below pH 7 (typically pH ~5).

Answer 2: The curve should show: starts at pH ~2.7, gentle rise (buffer) from ~5–18 cm³, half-equivalence at 10.0 cm³ (pH = 4.76), vertical jump from ~pH 6 to ~11 at 20.0 cm³, then levelling to pH ~13. Equivalence at 20.0 cm³ and pH ~8.7.

Answer 3: (a) Yes. HCl + NaOH has equivalence at pH 7.0, with a vertical jump from ~3 to ~11. Phenolphthalein (8.3–10.0) lies within the vertical jump — appropriate. (b) Yes, methyl orange (3.1–4.4) also lies within the jump, so both work for SA–SB.

Answer 4: (a) A base is in the flask (initial pH is basic). (b) Weak base (buffer region present). (c) Strong acid (low final pH, sharp drop at equivalence). This is a WB–SA titration (e.g., NH₃ + HCl).

Answer 5: WA–WB titrations have no sharp vertical jump — the pH changes very gradually through the equivalence point. Without a sharp jump, any indicator will change colour gradually over a range of volumes, making the end point impossible to locate precisely. Conductometric titration (measuring conductivity) or using a pH meter and plotting the first derivative are alternatives.

7. Key Terms

TermDefinition
Titration curveA graph of pH vs. volume of titrant added during a titration
Equivalence pointThe point where stoichiometrically equivalent amounts of acid and base have been mixed
End pointThe point where the indicator changes colour; should be as close as possible to the equivalence point
Buffer regionThe portion of a WA–SB or SA–WB titration curve where pH changes slowly; centred on the half-equivalence point
Half-equivalence pointThe point where half the acid has been neutralised; at this point pH = pKₐ
Vertical jumpThe steep section of a titration curve where pH changes rapidly with small additions of titrant
pKInThe negative logarithm of the indicator's dissociation constant; determines the pH range of colour change
PhenolphthaleinIndicator with pKIn = 9.3, colourless in acid, pink in base; used for WA–SB titrations
Methyl orangeIndicator with pKIn = 3.7, red in acid, yellow in base; used for SA–WB titrations

8. Exam Tips

Paper 1 (Multiple Choice): Expect questions asking which indicator is suitable for a given titration. First identify the titration type (SA–SB, WA–SB, etc.), recall the equivalence pH, then match the indicator whose pKIn ± 1 range covers it. A common trick: phenolphthalein for WA–SB is the most frequently tested combination.
Paper 2 (Structured): When asked to sketch a titration curve, you must show: (1) correct initial pH (not just "low" — use actual numbers), (2) buffer region for weak species, (3) equivalence volume correctly calculated, (4) equivalence pH above/below 7 as appropriate, (5) vertical jump. Label all features. Even a rough sketch earns most marks if key features are present.
Data Analysis questions: In Paper 3 or Section B, you may be given a titration curve and asked to determine pKₐ, concentration, or identify the acid. Read pKₐ at the half-equivalence volume (halfway to the vertical jump). Concentration is found from the equivalence volume and the known titrant concentration.
