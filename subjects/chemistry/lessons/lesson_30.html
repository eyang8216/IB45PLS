Lesson 30: Titration Curves and Indicators

What You'll Learn

  • Describe what a titration is and explain the purpose of performing one
  • Interpret pH titration curves for all four possible combinations of strong and weak acids with strong and weak bases
  • Identify and explain the significance of the equivalence point, the half-equivalence point, and the buffer region on a titration curve
  • Use the half-equivalence point on a weak acid–strong base titration curve to determine the pKa of the weak acid experimentally
  • Select an appropriate acid-base indicator for a given titration by comparing the indicator's pH colour-change range with the vertical jump of the titration curve
  • Explain how an acid-base indicator works at the molecular level

1. What Is a Titration?

The purpose of a titration

A titration is a laboratory technique used to determine the unknown concentration of a solution. The word comes from the French "titrer," meaning to determine the concentration of a substance. In an acid-base titration, you react an acid with a base. One of these solutions has a known concentration (this is called the standard solution) and the other has an unknown concentration. By measuring exactly how much of the standard solution is needed to completely react with the unknown solution, you can calculate the concentration of the unknown.

The equipment and procedure

In a typical titration, you place a measured volume of the unknown solution in a conical flask using a pipette. You fill a burette with the standard solution of known concentration. The burette is a long glass tube with a tap at the bottom that lets you add the standard solution drop by drop. You add the standard solution slowly to the flask while swirling it. As you add, you monitor the pH of the mixture — either by using a pH meter (which gives you continuous readings) or by adding a few drops of a chemical called an indicator, which changes colour at a particular pH.

The equivalence point and the end point

Two terms that students often confuse are the equivalence point and the end point. They are related but different.

The equivalence point is the moment when the number of moles of base added exactly equals the number of moles of acid originally present, according to the stoichiometry of the neutralisation reaction. At the equivalence point, the acid and base have reacted in exactly the right proportions. This is a theoretical point — it exists whether you measure it or not.

The end point is the moment when the indicator changes colour. This is what you actually observe in the laboratory. Ideally, the end point and the equivalence point should be the same — the indicator should change colour right at the equivalence point. A good titration experiment is designed so that these two points coincide as closely as possible.

2. pH Titration Curves — The Four Types

What a titration curve shows

A pH titration curve is a graph that plots the pH of the solution (on the vertical axis) against the volume of the standard solution added from the burette (on the horizontal axis). The shape of the curve reveals a great deal of information about the acid and the base being used, including their strengths, the pH at the equivalence point, and the pKa of any weak acid involved.

There are four possible combinations of acid and base, and each produces a titration curve with a distinctive shape. Let us examine each one.

Curve 1: Strong Acid titrated with Strong Base

Example: hydrochloric acid, HCl (aq), titrated with sodium hydroxide, NaOH (aq).

The neutralisation reaction: HCl (aq) + NaOH (aq) → NaCl (aq) + H₂O (l).

What the curve looks like:

The curve starts at a low pH. For a 0.10 mol dm⁻³ solution of hydrochloric acid, the initial pH is 1.00. As you add sodium hydroxide, the pH rises very slowly at first. This is because the solution starts with a huge excess of H⁺ ions, and adding a small amount of OH⁻ barely makes a dent in that supply.

After enough base has been added, the curve starts to rise more steeply. Near the equivalence point, there is an extremely sharp, nearly vertical jump in pH. For a 0.10 mol dm⁻³ strong acid and 0.10 mol dm⁻³ strong base, this vertical region spans from approximately pH 3 to pH 11 — a change of about 8 pH units with the addition of just a few drops.

At the equivalence point, the pH is exactly 7.00. This happens because the salt produced, sodium chloride (NaCl), is made from a strong acid (HCl) and a strong base (NaOH). Neither the Na⁺ ion nor the Cl⁻ ion reacts with water to produce H⁺ or OH⁻. The solution is neutral.

After the equivalence point, the curve flattens out again at a high pH (approaching 13 for 0.10 mol dm⁻³ solutions) as excess hydroxide ions accumulate.

Curve 2: Weak Acid titrated with Strong Base

Example: ethanoic acid, CH₃COOH (aq), titrated with sodium hydroxide, NaOH (aq).

The neutralisation reaction: CH₃COOH (aq) + NaOH (aq) → CH₃COONa (aq) + H₂O (l).

What the curve looks like:

The curve starts at a higher pH than the strong acid curve. For a 0.10 mol dm⁻³ solution of ethanoic acid, the initial pH is approximately 2.87 (because the acid is weak and only partially dissociates). This is an important observation: for the same concentration, a weak acid has a higher pH than a strong acid because it produces fewer H⁺ ions.

As base is added, the pH rises more quickly at first than in the strong acid case. But then something interesting happens: the curve enters a buffer region, where the pH rises very gradually. During this region, the solution contains both unreacted CH₃COOH and the CH₃COO⁻ produced by the neutralisation. This mixture forms a buffer, which resists changes in pH.

At the half-equivalence point — the point where exactly half of the acid has been neutralised — the concentrations of CH₃COOH and CH₃COO⁻ are equal. At this point, the pH equals the pKa of the weak acid. This is an extremely useful experimental measurement: if you can find the half-equivalence point on a titration curve, you know the pKa of the acid.

The equivalence point occurs at a pH greater than 7 — typically between 8 and 9 for most weak acids. The reason is that at the equivalence point, all of the CH₃COOH has been converted to CH₃COO⁻. The ethanoate ion is the conjugate base of a weak acid, and it reacts with water in a hydrolysis reaction:

$$\ce{CH3COO-(aq) + H2O(l) <=> CH3COOH(aq) + OH-(aq)}$$

This reaction produces OH⁻ ions, making the solution basic.

After the equivalence point, excess strong base dominates and the pH rises toward 13.

Curve 3: Strong Acid titrated with Weak Base

Example: hydrochloric acid, HCl (aq), titrated with ammonia, NH₃ (aq).

The neutralisation reaction: HCl (aq) + NH₃ (aq) → NH₄Cl (aq).

What the curve looks like:

The curve starts at a low pH (strong acid). As weak base is added, the pH rises. Near the equivalence point, there is a vertical jump, but it is less dramatic than in the strong-strong titration — typically spanning from about pH 3 to pH 7.5.

At the equivalence point, the pH is less than 7 — typically around 5.3. This is because the salt produced, ammonium chloride (NH₄Cl), contains the ammonium ion (NH₄⁺), which is the conjugate acid of the weak base ammonia. The ammonium ion hydrolyses:

$$\ce{NH4+(aq) + H2O(l) <=> NH3(aq) + H3O+(aq)}$$

This reaction produces H₃O⁺ ions, making the solution acidic.

After the equivalence point, excess weak base is present. Since NH₃ is a weak base, the pH does not rise as high as it would with a strong base — it typically levels off around pH 10 to 11 rather than approaching 13.

Curve 4: Weak Acid titrated with Weak Base

Example: ethanoic acid, CH₃COOH (aq), titrated with ammonia, NH₃ (aq).

The neutralisation reaction: CH₃COOH (aq) + NH₃ (aq) → CH₃COONH₄ (aq).

What the curve looks like:

This type of titration produces a curve with no sharp vertical jump. The pH changes gradually throughout the entire titration. Because there is no sudden, dramatic change in pH at the equivalence point, it is impossible to detect the end point using a chemical indicator. Any indicator would change colour gradually over a wide range of added titrant volume, making the result inaccurate.

For IB Chemistry, the key point to remember is: weak acid–weak base titrations are NOT suitable for analysis using indicators. If you need to titrate a weak acid against a weak base, you would use a pH meter (a potentiometric titration) rather than a visual indicator.

3. The Half-Equivalence Point — Finding pKa Experimentally

The significance of the half-equivalence point

The half-equivalence point is reached when the volume of titrant added is exactly half the volume required to reach the equivalence point. At this moment, exactly half of the weak acid (or weak base) has been neutralised. The remaining half is still in its original form.

For a monoprotic weak acid HA being titrated with a strong base: at the half-equivalence point, [HA] = [A⁻]. The Henderson-Hasselbalch equation tells us that when [A⁻] = [HA], the log term is log(1) = 0, and therefore:

pH = pKa

This means you can read the pKa of a weak acid directly from the titration curve: find the volume at the equivalence point, divide by two, and read the pH at that volume. That pH is the pKa.

This is one of the most common experimental methods for determining Ka values in the laboratory.

Worked Example 1: A student titrates 25.0 cm³ of an unknown weak monoprotic acid with 0.10 mol dm⁻³ sodium hydroxide, NaOH (aq). The equivalence point is reached after adding 25.0 cm³ of NaOH (aq). From the titration curve, the student reads that after adding 12.5 cm³ of NaOH (aq), the pH of the solution is 4.20. (a) Determine the pKa of this acid. (b) Calculate the Ka of the acid. (c) Identify the acid from the following list: ethanoic acid (pKa = 4.74), benzoic acid (pKa = 4.20), or methanoic acid (pKa = 3.74).

Strategy: At the half-equivalence point (12.5 cm³, which is half of 25.0 cm³), pH = pKa.

(a) pKa = 4.20.

(b) Ka = 10⁻⁴·²⁰ = 6.3 × 10⁻⁵ mol dm⁻³.

(c) The pKa of 4.20 matches benzoic acid, C₆H₅COOH.

Why this makes sense: At the half-equivalence point, exactly half the acid has been converted to its conjugate base. The remaining solution contains equal concentrations of HA and A⁻, and the Henderson-Hasselbalch equation reduces to pH = pKa.

4. Acid-Base Indicators

How an indicator works

An acid-base indicator is itself a weak acid (or sometimes a weak base) that has one colour in its acid form and a different colour in its conjugate base form. If we represent the indicator as HIn (where "In" stands for indicator), the equilibrium is:

$$\ce{HIn(aq) <=> H+(aq) + In-(aq)}$$

(colour 1) ............ (colour 2)

HIn is the acid form of the indicator and has one colour. In⁻ is the conjugate base form of the indicator and has a distinctly different colour. The human eye can detect the colour change when one form is about ten times more abundant than the other.

The pH range over which the colour change occurs is given by the indicator's own pKa:

Colour change range = pKa(indicator) ± 1

Common indicators and their pH ranges

How to choose the right indicator

The rule for selecting an indicator is simple but important: the pH range over which the indicator changes colour must lie entirely within the vertical (steep) portion of the titration curve. If the indicator changes colour before or after the equivalence point, the end point and the equivalence point will not match, and your result will be inaccurate.

Worked Example 2: For each of the following titrations, state which indicator (methyl orange, bromothymol blue, or phenolphthalein) would be suitable, and explain your reasoning. (a) HCl (aq) + NaOH (aq), both at 0.10 mol dm⁻³. (b) CH₃COOH (aq) + NaOH (aq), both at 0.10 mol dm⁻³. (c) HCl (aq) + NH₃ (aq), both at 0.10 mol dm⁻³.

(a) HCl + NaOH (strong acid + strong base): The vertical jump on the titration curve spans approximately pH 3 to pH 11. The equivalence point is at pH 7. All three indicators have their colour-change ranges somewhere between pH 3 and pH 11. Methyl orange (3.1–4.4), bromothymol blue (6.0–7.6), and phenolphthalein (8.2–10.0) are all within the vertical jump. Any of the three would be suitable. In practice, phenolphthalein is commonly used because its colourless-to-pink change is very easy to see.

(b) CH₃COOH + NaOH (weak acid + strong base): The equivalence point is at approximately pH 8.7. The vertical jump goes from about pH 6.5 to pH 11. Methyl orange changes colour at pH 3.1–4.4, which is below the vertical jump. If you used methyl orange, the colour change would occur well before the equivalence point, giving an inaccurate result. Bromothymol blue (6.0–7.6) starts within the jump but its full range extends below it — it is marginal. Phenolphthalein changes at pH 8.2–10.0, which falls completely within the vertical jump. Phenolphthalein is the correct choice.

(c) HCl + NH₃ (strong acid + weak base): The equivalence point is at approximately pH 5.3. The vertical jump goes from about pH 3 to pH 7.5. Phenolphthalein changes colour at pH 8.2–10.0, which is above the vertical jump — most of the colour change would happen after the equivalence point. Methyl orange changes at pH 3.1–4.4, which falls within the vertical jump. Methyl orange is the correct choice.

Why this makes sense: The equivalence point pH determines which indicator works. Weak acid + strong base gives a basic equivalence point, so you need an indicator that changes colour in the basic range (phenolphthalein). Strong acid + weak base gives an acidic equivalence point, so you need an indicator that changes colour in the acidic range (methyl orange).

Answers

Answer 1

The titration of a strong acid (HCl) with a strong base (NaOH) produces a curve with these features:

The initial pH is 1.00 because the HCl concentration is 0.10 mol dm⁻³ and HCl is a strong acid that dissociates completely: [H⁺] = 0.10 mol dm⁻³, so pH = −log(0.10) = 1.00.

As NaOH is added, the pH rises very slowly at first. This is because there is a large excess of H⁺, and the added OH⁻ ions are neutralised immediately.

Near the equivalence point, the pH rises extremely steeply — almost vertically. The vertical jump spans from approximately pH 3 to pH 11. This means that adding just a few drops of NaOH around the equivalence point changes the pH by about 8 units.

The equivalence point occurs at 25.0 cm³ of NaOH added (because the concentrations are equal and both solutions are monoprotic, the volumes must be equal). At the equivalence point, the pH is exactly 7.00 because the salt NaCl is neutral — neither Na⁺ nor Cl⁻ undergoes hydrolysis.

After the equivalence point, excess NaOH accumulates and the pH levels off, approaching 13 (since the concentration of excess NaOH is 0.10 mol dm⁻³ and pOH = 1, so pH = 13).

Answer 2

(a) The half-equivalence point occurs when 12.5 cm³ of NaOH has been added (half of the 25.0 cm³ needed for the equivalence point). At this point, pH = pKa. Since the pH at 12.5 cm³ is 5.00, pKa = 5.00.

(b) Ka = 10⁻⁵·⁰⁰ = 1.0 × 10⁻⁵ mol dm⁻³.

(c) The pH at the equivalence point will be greater than 7. At the equivalence point, all of the weak acid HA has been converted to its conjugate base A⁻. The anion A⁻ is the conjugate base of a weak acid, which means it is a weak base. It undergoes hydrolysis with water: A⁻ (aq) + H₂O (l) ⇌ HA (aq) + OH⁻ (aq). This reaction produces hydroxide ions, OH⁻, making the solution basic. For a typical weak acid with pKa around 5, the pH at the equivalence point would be approximately 8.5 to 9.0.

Answer 3

(a) HNO₃ + KOH is a strong acid + strong base titration. The equivalence point pH is 7. Any of the three indicators (methyl orange, bromothymol blue, or phenolphthalein) would be suitable because the vertical jump spans from about pH 3 to pH 11, covering all three indicator ranges.

(b) HCOOH + NaOH is a weak acid + strong base titration. The equivalence point pH is greater than 7, typically around 8 to 9. Phenolphthalein (8.2–10.0) is the suitable indicator because its colour change range falls within the vertical jump of the titration curve. Methyl orange would change colour too early, and bromothymol blue's range starts before the vertical jump.

(c) HCl + NH₃ is a strong acid + weak base titration. The equivalence point pH is less than 7, typically around 5 to 5.5. Methyl orange (3.1–4.4) is the suitable indicator because its colour change range falls within the vertical jump. Phenolphthalein would change colour too late (after the equivalence point), giving inaccurate results.

Answer 4

The student's claim is incorrect. Phenolphthalein is not always the best indicator — in fact, it would be a poor choice for the titration of HCl with NH₃.

In the HCl + NH₃ titration, the equivalence point occurs at a pH of approximately 5.3 because the ammonium chloride (NH₄Cl) produced is acidic — the NH₄⁺ ion undergoes hydrolysis, releasing H₃O⁺. The vertical jump of the titration curve extends from approximately pH 3 to pH 7.5.

Phenolphthalein changes colour over the pH range 8.2 to 10.0. This entire range lies above the vertical jump. This means the indicator would not begin to change colour until well after the equivalence point had been passed. By the time the student saw the pink colour appear, they would have added significantly more NH₃ than was needed to neutralise the HCl. The titre volume would be too large, and the calculated concentration of HCl would be too high.

For this titration, methyl orange (pH range 3.1–4.4) is the correct choice because its colour change range lies squarely within the vertical jump, ensuring that the end point coincides closely with the equivalence point.

Answer 5

(a) The half-equivalence point occurs when 12.5 cm³ of NaOH has been added (half of 25.0 cm³). At this volume, the pH is 4.74. Since pH = pKa at the half-equivalence point, pKa = 4.74. This value matches ethanoic acid, CH₃COOH. Therefore, acid HA is ethanoic acid.

(b) At the equivalence point (25.0 cm³ of NaOH added), all of the ethanoic acid has been neutralised and converted to ethanoate ions: CH₃COOH (aq) + NaOH (aq) → CH₃COONa (aq) + H₂O (l). The ethanoate ion, CH₃COO⁻, is the conjugate base of a weak acid, so it acts as a weak base. It reacts with water in a hydrolysis reaction: CH₃COO⁻ (aq) + H₂O (l) ⇌ CH₃COOH (aq) + OH⁻ (aq). This equilibrium produces hydroxide ions, OH⁻, which make the solution basic. That is why the pH at the equivalence point (8.72) is greater than 7.

(c) The vertical jump of this titration curve runs from approximately pH 6.5 to pH 11, and the equivalence point pH is 8.72. Phenolphthalein changes colour over the pH range 8.2 to 10.0, which falls entirely within the vertical jump. Therefore, phenolphthalein is suitable.

Methyl orange changes colour over pH 3.1 to 4.4, which is far below the vertical jump. It would change colour long before the equivalence point, giving a titre volume that is too small. Bromothymol blue changes colour over pH 6.0 to 7.6. While part of this range overlaps with the beginning of the vertical jump, most of it lies below. This indicator would produce an indistinct colour change that starts before the equivalence point, leading to inaccurate results. Only phenolphthalein is suitable.

(d) At 0.0 cm³ of NaOH added, the solution contains only the weak acid HA at a concentration of 0.10 mol dm⁻³. The pH is 2.87.

[H⁺] = 10⁻²·⁸⁷.

We can calculate: 10⁻²·⁸⁷ = 10⁻⁰·⁸⁷ × 10⁻².

10⁻⁰·⁸⁷ is approximately 0.135 (since 10⁻⁰·⁸⁷ = 1 ÷ 10⁰·⁸⁷ and 10⁰·⁸⁷ ≈ 7.4).

So [H⁺] = 0.135 × 10⁻² = 1.35 × 10⁻³ mol dm⁻³.

Using the Ka expression: Ka = [H⁺]² ÷ ([HA] − [H⁺]).

Ka = (1.35 × 10⁻³)² ÷ (0.10 − 1.35 × 10⁻³).

(1.35 × 10⁻³)² = 1.82 × 10⁻⁶.

0.10 − 0.00135 = 0.09865 ≈ 0.0987.

Ka = 1.82 × 10⁻⁶ ÷ 0.0987 ≈ 1.84 × 10⁻⁵ mol dm⁻³.

pKa = −log(1.84 × 10⁻⁵) ≈ 4.74.

This matches the pKa of ethanoic acid, confirming the identification made in part (a).
