## 1. What Is a Transition Element? The IUPAC Definition

The International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry (IUPAC) defines a **transition element** as an element whose atom has a partially filled d subshell in its ground state, OR an element that forms at least one stable ion with a partially filled d subshell.

This definition is precise and has important consequences. Let us examine it carefully. A d subshell contains five orbitals, and each orbital can hold two electrons, so a full d subshell holds 10 electrons. A "partially filled" d subshell means the d subshell has at least one electron and at most nine electrons—it is neither completely empty (d⁰) nor completely full (d¹⁰).

All elements in the d-block of the periodic table (Groups 3 through 12) have d electrons. However, not all of them meet the IUPAC definition of a transition element. Two elements in the first row of the d-block are specifically excluded: scandium (Sc) and zinc (Zn).

### Why Scandium Is Not a Transition Element

A scandium atom has the electron configuration [Ar] 3d¹ 4s². The atom does have one electron in its 3d subshell, so its ground state has a partially filled d subshell. However, the IUPAC definition also considers the ions that the element commonly forms. Scandium almost exclusively forms the Sc³⁺ ion in its compounds. The Sc³⁺ ion has the electron configuration [Ar], which is simply the argon core with no d electrons at all—the 3d subshell is completely empty (3d⁰). Since scandium has no common ion with a partially filled d subshell (its only common ion has an empty d subshell), scandium is NOT classified as a transition element according to the IUPAC definition. Compounds of Sc³⁺ are typically white or colourless because there are no d electrons to undergo d–d transitions.

### Why Zinc Is Not a Transition Element

A zinc atom has the electron configuration [Ar] 3d¹⁰ 4s². The 3d subshell is completely full. Zinc almost exclusively forms the Zn²⁺ ion, which has the electron configuration [Ar] 3d¹⁰. The 3d subshell remains completely full in the ion. Since zinc has a full d subshell both in its atomic ground state and in its common ion, it does NOT meet the IUPAC definition. Zinc is a d-block element but not a transition element. Compounds of Zn²⁺ are typically white or colourless—just like Sc³⁺ compounds—because a full d subshell cannot undergo d–d electronic transitions.

### The True Transition Elements of the First Row

The first-row transition elements are therefore: titanium (Ti), vanadium (V), chromium (Cr), manganese (Mn), iron (Fe), cobalt (Co), nickel (Ni), and copper (Cu). All eight of these elements have partially filled 3d subshells in at least one of their common oxidation states.

Copper deserves special note. A copper atom has the electron configuration [Ar] 3d¹⁰ 4s¹—notice that the 3d subshell is full and the 4s has only one electron. Based on the atom alone, copper might seem to be excluded like zinc. However, copper commonly forms the Cu²⁺ ion, which has the configuration [Ar] 3d⁹. The Cu²⁺ ion has a partially filled d subshell (nine electrons out of a possible ten), so copper meets the IUPAC definition of a transition element. This is why copper(II) compounds are coloured (usually blue or green), while zinc compounds are white.

### Worked Example 13.1: Identifying Transition Elements

**Problem:** For each of the following elements, state whether it is a transition element according to the IUPAC definition, and explain your reasoning: (a) iron (Fe), (b) zinc (Zn), (c) copper (Cu).

**To solve this, we need to check the electron configuration of each atom and its common ions for a partially filled d subshell.**

(a) Iron: The Fe atom has electron configuration [Ar] 3d⁶ 4s². The 3d subshell has six electrons (partially filled). Iron commonly forms Fe²⁺ ([Ar] 3d⁶) and Fe³⁺ ([Ar] 3d⁵), both of which have partially filled d subshells. Iron IS a transition element.

(b) Zinc: The Zn atom has electron configuration [Ar] 3d¹⁰ 4s². The 3d subshell is completely full (10 electrons). Zinc's common ion is Zn²⁺ ([Ar] 3d¹⁰), which also has a completely full d subshell. Neither the atom nor any common ion has a partially filled d subshell. Zinc is NOT a transition element.

(c) Copper: The Cu atom has electron configuration [Ar] 3d¹⁰ 4s¹. The atom has a full 3d subshell. However, copper commonly forms the Cu²⁺ ion, which has configuration [Ar] 3d⁹. This ion has a partially filled d subshell (nine electrons). Copper IS a transition element because it forms at least one stable ion with a partially filled d subshell.

**Why this makes sense:** The key distinction is whether any common oxidation state produces an ion with an incomplete d subshell. Zinc and scandium fail this test: zinc's d subshell is always full, and scandium's d subshell is empty in its only common ion.

## 2. Characteristic Properties of Transition Elements

Transition elements share a set of characteristic properties that arise directly from their partially filled d subshells.

### Variable Oxidation States

Transition metals can exist in several different oxidation states. This is possible because the 3d and 4s electrons are relatively close in energy. An atom can lose different numbers of electrons from these subshells, producing ions with different charges.

Manganese provides a particularly clear example. It forms compounds in oxidation states ranging from +2 to +7: Mn²⁺ (in MnCl₂, pale pink), MnO₂ (manganese in the +4 oxidation state, a brown solid), MnO₄²⁻ (manganate ion, manganese in the +6 oxidation state, green), and MnO₄⁻ (permanganate ion, manganese in the +7 oxidation state, intensely purple). Notice that each oxidation state is associated with a different colour.

As a general trend, the stability of the highest oxidation states decreases across the first row of the transition series. Early transition metals (such as titanium and vanadium) can reach high oxidation states more readily than later ones.

### Formation of Coloured Compounds

Many compounds of transition metals are brightly coloured. For example, copper(II) sulfate pentahydrate (CuSO₄·5H₂O) is blue, nickel(II) chloride (NiCl₂) is green, and potassium permanganate (KMnO₄) is intensely purple. The origin of this colour is a phenomenon called **d–d electronic transitions**, which we will explain in detail in Section 4 using Crystal Field Theory. For now, the key point is that a partially filled d subshell allows electrons to absorb visible light and jump between d orbitals of different energies. If the d subshell is empty (as in Sc³⁺, 3d⁰) or completely full (as in Zn²⁺, 3d¹⁰), no d–d transitions are possible, and the compound appears white or colourless.

### Catalytic Activity

Transition metals and their compounds are widely used as catalysts—substances that increase the rate of a chemical reaction without being consumed. Transition metals are effective catalysts because they can provide a surface for reactant molecules to adsorb (heterogeneous catalysis), or they can change their oxidation state during the reaction to form intermediate compounds, providing an alternative reaction pathway with a lower activation energy (homogeneous catalysis).

Examples include: iron in the Haber process for ammonia synthesis (heterogeneous), nickel in the hydrogenation of alkenes (heterogeneous), vanadium(V) oxide (V₂O₅) in the Contact process for sulfuric acid production (heterogeneous), and manganese(IV) oxide (MnO₂) in the catalytic decomposition of hydrogen peroxide (heterogeneous).

### Formation of Complex Ions

Transition metal ions in solution do not exist as bare ions. They are surrounded by molecules or ions called ligands, which bond to the metal ion through coordinate (dative) covalent bonds. The resulting species is called a complex ion. We examine complex ions in detail in Section 3.

## 3. Complex Ions: Structure and Terminology

A **complex ion** (or coordination complex) consists of a central metal ion bonded to a set of surrounding molecules or ions called **ligands**. The bond between the metal ion and each ligand is a **coordinate covalent bond** (also called a dative covalent bond): the ligand donates a lone pair of electrons to the metal ion, which acts as a Lewis acid (an electron pair acceptor). The ligand acts as a Lewis base (an electron pair donor).

### Key Terminology

The **coordination number** is the number of coordinate bonds that the central metal ion forms with its ligands. It is NOT simply the number of ligands. If a ligand can form only one coordinate bond (a monodentate ligand), then the coordination number equals the number of ligands. But if a ligand can form multiple coordinate bonds (a polydentate ligand), the coordination number is larger than the number of ligands.

A **ligand** is any molecule or ion that has at least one lone pair of electrons that it can donate to a metal ion. Common ligands include water (H₂O, with lone pairs on oxygen), ammonia (NH₃, with a lone pair on nitrogen), chloride ion (Cl⁻, with four lone pairs), cyanide ion (CN⁻, with a lone pair on carbon and one on nitrogen), and hydroxide ion (OH⁻).

### Types of Ligands

A **monodentate ligand** (from Latin "one tooth") donates exactly one lone pair to the metal ion. It forms one coordinate bond. Examples: H₂O (water), NH₃ (ammonia), Cl⁻ (chloride), CN⁻ (cyanide), OH⁻ (hydroxide).

A **bidentate ligand** (two teeth) has two different atoms, each with a lone pair, and both can donate to the same metal ion simultaneously. Important examples for IB Chemistry:

- **Ethane-1,2-diamine** (abbreviated "en"): H₂N–CH₂–CH₂–NH₂. Each nitrogen atom has a lone pair, so one en ligand forms two coordinate bonds to the metal.

- **The ethanedioate ion (oxalate)**: C₂O₄²⁻, with the structure ⁻OOC–COO⁻. Each of the two negatively charged oxygen atoms can donate a lone pair.

A **polydentate ligand** has more than two donor atoms. The most famous example is EDTA⁴⁻ (ethylenediaminetetraacetate), which can donate six lone pairs and is a hexadentate ligand.

### Common Geometries of Complex Ions

The geometry of a complex ion depends primarily on its coordination number:

**Coordination number 6: Octahedral.** This is the most common geometry for transition metal complexes. Six ligands are arranged at the corners of an octahedron around the central metal ion, with bond angles of 90°. Examples: [Fe(H₂O)₆]³⁺ (hexaaquairon(III) ion), [Cu(H₂O)₆]²⁺ (hexaaquacopper(II) ion), [Co(NH₃)₆]³⁺ (hexaamminecobalt(III) ion).

**Coordination number 4: Tetrahedral.** Four ligands arranged tetrahedrally, with bond angles of approximately 109.5°. Examples: [CuCl₄]²⁻ (tetrachlorocuprate(II) ion), [CoCl₄]²⁻ (tetrachlorocobaltate(II) ion).

**Coordination number 4: Square planar.** Four ligands arranged in a flat square around the metal, with bond angles of 90°. This geometry is common for d⁸ metal ions, especially those of platinum(II), palladium(II), gold(III), and sometimes nickel(II) with strong-field ligands. Examples: [Pt(NH₃)₂Cl₂] (cisplatin, an important anticancer drug), [Ni(CN)₄]²⁻ (tetracyanonickelate(II) ion).

**Coordination number 2: Linear.** Rare, but found in some silver(I) and copper(I) complexes. Example: [Ag(NH₃)₂]⁺ (diamminesilver(I) ion).

### Worked Example 13.2: Analyzing a Complex Ion

**Problem:** The complex ion [Cu(NH₃)₄(H₂O)₂]²⁺ contains four ammonia ligands and two water ligands attached to a copper(II) ion. State the coordination number of the copper ion, name the geometry of the complex, and explain whether this complex can exist in isomeric forms.

**To solve this, we need to identify each ligand, count the bonds, and determine the geometry.**

The four ammonia molecules (NH₃) are monodentate ligands—each donates one lone pair from its nitrogen atom, forming one coordinate bond. The two water molecules (H₂O) are also monodentate ligands—each donates one lone pair from its oxygen atom. The total number of coordinate bonds from all ligands to the copper ion is 4 (from NH₃) + 2 (from H₂O) = 6. The coordination number is therefore 6.

A coordination number of 6 nearly always corresponds to an octahedral geometry. The six ligands are arranged at the corners of an octahedron around the central Cu²⁺ ion. All bond angles are approximately 90°.

This complex can exist in two isomeric forms because the two water molecules can occupy different relative positions. In the **cis isomer**, the two water molecules occupy adjacent corners of the octahedron (they are at 90° to each other). In the **trans isomer**, the two water molecules occupy opposite corners of the octahedron (they are at 180° to each other). These are cis–trans geometric isomers, analogous to cis–trans isomerism in alkenes.

**Why this makes sense:** The coordination number is always the total number of bonds from ligands to the metal. Since all six ligands are monodentate, the coordination number equals the total number of ligands (6), and the geometry is octahedral.

## 4. Crystal Field Theory: Why Transition Metal Complexes Are Coloured

Crystal Field Theory (CFT) is a relatively simple model that explains the colour and magnetic properties of transition metal complexes. It considers the electrostatic interaction between the d orbitals of the central metal ion and the lone pairs of the surrounding ligands.

### The Five d Orbitals: Their Shapes and Orientations

To understand CFT, you need to know the shapes and orientations of the five d orbitals. The five d orbitals are named d_(xy), d_(xz), d_(yz), d_(x²−y²), and d_(z²).

Three of these—d_(xy), d_(xz), and d_(yz)—have their lobes oriented between the Cartesian axes. For example, the d_(xy) orbital has its four lobes lying in the xy plane, between the x and y axes (at 45° to each axis). These three orbitals are collectively called the **t₂g** set (the name comes from group theory, but for IB purposes, you just need to know that t₂g refers to the lower-energy set of d orbitals in an octahedral field).

Two of the d orbitals—d_(x²−y²) and d_(z²)—have their lobes oriented directly along the Cartesian axes. The d_(x²−y²) orbital has its four lobes pointing directly along the +x, −x, +y, and −y directions. The d_(z²) orbital has two large lobes pointing along the +z and −z directions, with a small ring (torus) of electron density in the xy plane. These two orbitals are collectively called the **e_g** set (the higher-energy set in an octahedral field).

### Octahedral Crystal Field Splitting

Imagine a metal ion at the center of an octahedron with six ligands approaching along the x, y, and z axes—one ligand from the +x direction, one from −x, one from +y, one from −y, one from +z, and one from −z.

The electrons in the d_(x²−y²) and d_(z²) orbitals are concentrated directly along these axes. When the ligands approach, the lone pair electrons of the ligands repel the electrons in these two orbitals very strongly. The energy of the d_(x²−y²) and d_(z²) orbitals is raised. These two orbitals form the **e_g** set.

The electrons in the d_(xy), d_(xz), and d_(yz) orbitals are concentrated between the axes. When the ligands approach along the axes, the electrons in these three orbitals are not directly in the path of the ligands, so they experience less repulsion. Their energy is lowered relative to the hypothetical "unsplit" d orbital energy. These three orbitals form the **t₂g** set.

The energy gap between the t₂g set and the e_g set is called the **crystal field splitting energy** and is given the symbol **ΔE** (or Δ_oct for an octahedral complex). The size of ΔE depends on the identity of the metal ion, its oxidation state, and the nature of the ligands.

### How Colour Arises from d–d Transitions

When white light (which contains all visible wavelengths) passes through a solution of a transition metal complex, an electron in a lower-energy t₂g orbital can absorb a photon of light whose energy exactly matches ΔE. The electron absorbs this photon and is promoted (excited) to a higher-energy e_g orbital. This is called a **d–d electronic transition**.

The wavelength (λ) of the absorbed photon is related to ΔE by the equation:

**ΔE = hc / λ**

where h is Planck's constant (6.63 × 10⁻³⁴ J s) and c is the speed of light (3.00 × 10⁸ m s⁻¹).

The light that is NOT absorbed is transmitted through the solution and reaches our eyes. We perceive the **complementary colour** of the absorbed light. For example, if a complex absorbs orange light (wavelength around 600 nm), the transmitted light lacks orange, and we see the complementary colour, which is blue.

The complementary colour pairs are: red ↔ green, orange ↔ blue, and yellow ↔ violet. If a solution absorbs violet light, it appears yellow. If it absorbs blue light, it appears orange. If it absorbs green light, it appears red.

## 5. The Spectrochemical Series: How Ligands Affect Colour

Different ligands produce different values of ΔE. Ligands can be arranged in order of the size of the splitting they produce. This ordering is called the **spectrochemical series**.

A simplified version of the spectrochemical series, from weakest field (smallest ΔE) to strongest field (largest ΔE), is:

**I⁻ < Br⁻ < Cl⁻ < F⁻ < H₂O < NH₃ < en (ethane-1,2-diamine) < CN⁻**

Ligands on the left (I⁻, Br⁻, Cl⁻) are called **weak-field ligands**. They produce a small ΔE. Because ΔE is small, the absorbed photons have low energy, which means they have long wavelengths (toward the red end of the spectrum).

Ligands on the right (CN⁻, en) are called **strong-field ligands**. They produce a large ΔE. Because ΔE is large, the absorbed photons have high energy, which means they have short wavelengths (toward the violet end of the spectrum).

This explains why changing the ligands can change the colour of a complex. Consider the following copper(II) complexes:

- [Cu(H₂O)₆]²⁺ is pale blue. H₂O is a moderate-field ligand (middle of the spectrochemical series). The complex absorbs in the orange-red region, transmitting blue.

- [Cu(NH₃)₄(H₂O)₂]²⁺ (formed when ammonia is added to aqueous Cu²⁺) is a deeper blue-violet. NH₃ is a stronger-field ligand than H₂O, producing a larger ΔE. The absorption shifts to shorter wavelengths (higher energy)—from orange toward yellow-green—and the transmitted colour shifts toward violet.

- [CuCl₄]²⁻ is yellow-green. Cl⁻ is a weaker-field ligand than H₂O, producing a smaller ΔE. The absorption shifts to longer wavelengths (lower energy)—toward violet—and the transmitted colour is yellow-green.

### Worked Example 13.3: Comparing Colours Using the Spectrochemical Series

**Problem:** The complex [Co(H₂O)₆]²⁺ is pink, while the complex [CoCl₄]²⁻ is a deep blue. Using Crystal Field Theory and the spectrochemical series, explain why these two cobalt(II) complexes have different colours.

**To solve this, we need to consider two factors: the geometry of each complex and the position of the ligands in the spectrochemical series.**

[Co(H₂O)₆]²⁺ is an octahedral complex. Water (H₂O) is a moderate-field ligand, appearing near the middle of the spectrochemical series. In an octahedral field, the d orbitals of Co²⁺ split into t₂g (lower) and e_g (higher) sets with a moderate ΔE. This ΔE corresponds to the absorption of light in the blue-green region of the spectrum. The complementary colour of blue-green is pink (or red-orange), so the complex appears pink.

[CoCl₄]²⁻ is a tetrahedral complex (coordination number 4, with four chloride ligands). This is important because tetrahedral complexes have a crystal field splitting that is fundamentally different from octahedral splitting. In a tetrahedral field, the splitting pattern is inverted (the orbitals that point more directly at the ligands are the t₂ set, approximately, and they are raised), and the magnitude of the splitting (Δ_tet) is only about 4/9 of the octahedral splitting (Δ_oct) for the same metal and ligands. Additionally, Cl⁻ is a weaker-field ligand than H₂O—it appears further to the left in the spectrochemical series, meaning it produces a smaller ΔE even for the same geometry.

The combination of tetrahedral geometry (inherently smaller splitting) and weak-field chloride ligands produces a much smaller ΔE in [CoCl₄]²⁻ than in [Co(H₂O)₆]²⁺. The smaller ΔE means that lower-energy (longer-wavelength) light is absorbed. The complex absorbs in the red-orange region, and the complementary colour transmitted is blue.

**Why this makes sense:** Both the geometry change (octahedral to tetrahedral) and the ligand change (H₂O to Cl⁻) act in the same direction—both reduce ΔE. This shifts the absorption to longer wavelengths and the perceived colour from pink to blue. This example illustrates why you must consider both geometry and ligand identity when comparing colours.

## 6. Calculating the Crystal Field Splitting Energy

If you know the wavelength of light that a complex absorbs most strongly, you can calculate ΔE using the equation ΔE = hc/λ.

The steps are:
1. Convert the wavelength λ from nanometers to meters: 1 nm = 10⁻⁹ m.
2. Calculate the energy per photon: ΔE_photon = (h × c) / λ, where h = 6.63 × 10⁻³⁴ J s and c = 3.00 × 10⁸ m s⁻¹. The result is in joules (J).
3. Convert to energy per mole by multiplying by Avogadro's number (N_A = 6.02 × 10²³ mol⁻¹): ΔE_per mole = ΔE_photon × N_A. The result is in J mol⁻¹. Divide by 1000 to convert to kJ mol⁻¹ if needed.

---

### Worked Example 13.4: Calculating ΔE

**Problem:** An aqueous solution of copper(II) sulfate appears blue because the [Cu(H₂O)₆]²⁺ complex ion absorbs light most strongly at a wavelength of approximately 600 nanometers. Calculate the crystal field splitting energy ΔE in kilojoules per mole. Use h = 6.63 × 10⁻³⁴ J s, c = 3.00 × 10⁸ m s⁻¹, and N_A = 6.02 × 10²³ mol⁻¹.

**To solve this, we need to apply the equation ΔE = hc/λ and then convert to a per-mole basis.**

Step 1: Convert the wavelength to meters. λ = 600 nm = 600 × 10⁻⁹ m = 6.00 × 10⁻⁷ m.

Step 2: Calculate the energy per photon.

ΔE_photon = (6.63 × 10⁻³⁴ J s × 3.00 × 10⁸ m s⁻¹) / (6.00 × 10⁻⁷ m)

First, multiply h and c: 6.63 × 10⁻³⁴ × 3.00 × 10⁸ = 1.989 × 10⁻²⁵ J m.

Then divide by λ: 1.989 × 10⁻²⁵ / 6.00 × 10⁻⁷ = 3.315 × 10⁻¹⁹ J.

This is the energy absorbed by a single photon, which corresponds to the energy needed to excite one electron from a t₂g orbital to an e_g orbital in a single [Cu(H₂O)₆]²⁺ complex.

Step 3: Convert to energy per mole.

ΔE_per mole = (3.315 × 10⁻¹⁹ J) × (6.02 × 10²³ mol⁻¹) = 1.996 × 10⁵ J mol⁻¹.

Convert to kJ mol⁻¹: 1.996 × 10⁵ J mol⁻¹ ÷ 1000 = 199.6 kJ mol⁻¹ ≈ 200 kJ mol⁻¹.

**Why this makes sense:** The crystal field splitting energy is on the order of 200 kJ mol⁻¹ for this complex. This is intermediate between typical intermolecular forces (a few kJ mol⁻¹ for hydrogen bonds, up to about 40 kJ mol⁻¹) and typical covalent bond energies (200–500 kJ mol⁻¹). The energy is in the visible region of the electromagnetic spectrum, which is why d–d transitions produce colours visible to the human eye. A value around 200 kJ mol⁻¹ is typical for octahedral complexes with moderate-field ligands like water.

## Answers

1. (a) Scandium: NOT a transition element. A scandium atom has the electron configuration [Ar] 3d¹ 4s². The atom has one electron in its 3d subshell, which is a partially filled d subshell. However, scandium almost exclusively forms the Sc³⁺ ion in its compounds. The Sc³⁺ ion has the electron configuration [Ar], with no electrons in the 3d subshell at all (3d⁰). Since the only common ion of scandium has an empty d subshell rather than a partially filled one, scandium does not satisfy the IUPAC definition of a transition element.

   (b) Iron: IS a transition element. The Fe atom has the configuration [Ar] 3d⁶ 4s². The 3d subshell contains six electrons, which is partially filled (neither empty nor full). Iron also forms Fe²⁺ ([Ar] 3d⁶) and Fe³⁺ ([Ar] 3d⁵), both of which have partially filled d subshells.

   (c) Copper: IS a transition element. The Cu atom has the configuration [Ar] 3d¹⁰ 4s¹, which has a full 3d subshell. However, copper commonly forms the Cu²⁺ ion with configuration [Ar] 3d⁹. The Cu²⁺ ion has nine electrons in the 3d subshell, which is partially filled. Since copper forms a stable ion with a partially filled d subshell, it meets the IUPAC definition.

   (d) Zinc: NOT a transition element. The Zn atom has the configuration [Ar] 3d¹⁰ 4s², with a completely full 3d subshell. Zinc almost exclusively forms Zn²⁺, which has the configuration [Ar] 3d¹⁰—still a completely full d subshell. Neither the atom nor the common ion has a partially filled d subshell, so zinc fails the IUPAC definition. This is a common exam trap: zinc is a d-block element, but it is not a transition element.

2. (a) [Fe(CN)₆]⁴⁻: The name is hexacyanoferrate(II) ion. The iron is in the +2 oxidation state (hence "ferrate(II)"). There are six cyanide (CN⁻) ligands, each monodentate, bonded to the central Fe²⁺ ion. Coordination number = 6. Geometry = octahedral.

   (b) [Ag(NH₃)₂]⁺: The name is diamminesilver(I) ion. The silver is in the +1 oxidation state. There are two ammonia ligands, each monodentate, bonded to Ag⁺. Coordination number = 2. Geometry = linear.

   (c) [Co(NH₃)₆]³⁺: The name is hexaamminecobalt(III) ion. The cobalt is in the +3 oxidation state. There are six ammonia ligands, each monodentate. Coordination number = 6. Geometry = octahedral.

3. The chromium(III) ion, Cr³⁺, has the electron configuration [Ar] 3d³. It has three electrons in its 3d subshell, which is partially filled. In the octahedral complex [Cr(H₂O)₆]³⁺, the six water molecules approach the Cr³⁺ ion along the x, y, and z axes. The electrostatic repulsion between the ligand lone pairs and the d electrons causes the five d orbitals to split into two sets: the lower-energy t₂g set (d_(xy), d_(xz), d_(yz)) and the higher-energy e_g set (d_(x²−y²), d_(z²)). The three d electrons of Cr³⁺ occupy the lower-energy t₂g orbitals (one electron in each, following Hund's rule). When white light is shone on the solution, an electron in a t₂g orbital absorbs a photon whose energy matches the gap ΔE between the t₂g and e_g sets. The electron is promoted to an e_g orbital. The complex absorbs light in the yellow-green region of the spectrum (wavelength approximately 560–580 nm). The light that is not absorbed is transmitted, and this transmitted light is the complementary colour of yellow-green, which is violet. The solution therefore appears violet.

4. All three complexes contain Ni²⁺ (electron configuration [Ar] 3d⁸) in an octahedral geometry with a coordination number of 6. The only difference is the identity of the ligands: H₂O in the first, NH₃ in the second, and en (ethane-1,2-diamine) in the third. According to the spectrochemical series, these three ligands are ordered as H₂O < NH₃ < en, with H₂O being the weakest-field ligand (producing the smallest ΔE) and en being the strongest-field ligand (producing the largest ΔE).

   In [Ni(H₂O)₆]²⁺, the weak-field H₂O ligands produce a relatively small ΔE. The complex absorbs low-energy (long-wavelength) light in the red region, transmitting green (the complementary colour of red).

   In [Ni(NH₃)₆]²⁺, the stronger-field NH₃ ligands produce a larger ΔE. The absorption shifts to higher energy (shorter wavelength)—into the orange-yellow region—and the transmitted colour shifts from green to blue (complementary to orange).

   In [Ni(en)₃]²⁺, the even stronger-field en ligands produce the largest ΔE. The absorption shifts to still higher energy (even shorter wavelength)—into the yellow-green region—and the transmitted colour is violet (the complementary colour of yellow-green).

   This systematic shift from green to blue to violet directly reflects the increasing position of the ligands in the spectrochemical series: as the field strength increases, ΔE increases, the absorbed wavelength decreases, and the observed colour shifts toward the violet end of the spectrum.

5. (a) The yellow-green complex formed in Step 1 is [CuCl₄]²⁻, the tetrachlorocuprate(II) ion. The geometry is tetrahedral (coordination number = 4). When concentrated HCl is added to aqueous Cu²⁺, the high concentration of Cl⁻ ions displaces the water ligands, forming the tetrahedral chloro complex. The colour change is due to both the change in ligand (from H₂O to Cl⁻) and the change in geometry (from octahedral to tetrahedral).

   (b) In the original blue solution, the complex is [Cu(H₂O)₆]²⁺, which is octahedral with H₂O as the ligand. Water is a moderate-field ligand and appears in the middle of the spectrochemical series. The octahedral crystal field splitting ΔE is moderate, and the complex absorbs in the orange-red region (around 600 nm), transmitting blue.

   When HCl is added, the chloride ions (Cl⁻) replace the water ligands. Chloride is a weaker-field ligand than water, meaning it appears further to the left in the spectrochemical series (I⁻ < Br⁻ < Cl⁻ < F⁻ < H₂O). A weaker-field ligand produces a smaller ΔE. Additionally, the geometry changes from octahedral to tetrahedral, and tetrahedral complexes have an inherently smaller crystal field splitting than octahedral complexes (Δ_tet is approximately 4/9 of Δ_oct). Both effects—the weaker-field ligand and the tetrahedral geometry—reduce ΔE significantly.

   A smaller ΔE means that the energy of the photons that can be absorbed is lower. Lower photon energy corresponds to longer wavelength. The absorption therefore shifts from the orange-red region toward the red or near-infrared region. The transmitted light shifts from blue toward yellow-green, which is the complementary colour of violet/blue absorption. The solution appears yellow-green.

   (c) When excess concentrated ammonia is added, the ammonia molecules (NH₃) replace the chloride and water ligands. Ammonia is a stronger-field ligand than both Cl⁻ and H₂O, appearing further to the right in the spectrochemical series. The complex formed is [Cu(NH₃)₄(H₂O)₂]²⁺, with four ammonia ligands and two water ligands. The geometry returns to octahedral (coordination number = 6). The stronger-field NH₃ ligand produces a larger ΔE than H₂O, so the absorption shifts to shorter wavelengths (higher energy)—further into the yellow-green region—and the transmitted colour becomes a deeper blue-violet.

   (d) Step 1: Convert λ = 600 nm to meters: 600 × 10⁻⁹ m = 6.00 × 10⁻⁷ m.

   Step 2: Calculate energy per photon:
   ΔE_photon = hc / λ = (6.63 × 10⁻³⁴ J s × 3.00 × 10⁸ m s⁻¹) / (6.00 × 10⁻⁷ m)
   = (1.989 × 10⁻²⁵ J m) / (6.00 × 10⁻⁷ m)
   = 3.315 × 10⁻¹⁹ J

   Step 3: Convert to kJ mol⁻¹:
   ΔE_per mole = 3.315 × 10⁻¹⁹ J × 6.02 × 10²³ mol⁻¹
   = 1.996 × 10⁵ J mol⁻¹
   = 199.6 kJ mol⁻¹ ≈ 200 kJ mol⁻¹

   The crystal field splitting energy for [Cu(H₂O)₆]²⁺ is approximately 200 kJ mol⁻¹.

   (e) Zinc sulfate solutions are colourless because the Zn²⁺ ion has the electron configuration [Ar] 3d¹⁰. The 3d subshell is completely full. In a d¹⁰ ion, all five d orbitals are occupied by two electrons each (spin-paired). There is no vacant orbital within the d subshell into which an electron can be promoted. A d–d electronic transition, which is what produces colour in transition metal complexes, requires that an electron can absorb a photon and move from one d orbital to another. But in Zn²⁺, every d orbital is already full—the Pauli exclusion principle forbids adding an electron to an already-filled orbital. Therefore, no d–d transition can occur, and no visible light is absorbed. The solution appears colourless.

   Copper(II) sulfate solutions are blue because the Cu²⁺ ion has the configuration [Ar] 3d⁹. With nine electrons in five d orbitals, there is one "hole" (one orbital with only one electron). An electron from a lower-energy d orbital can absorb visible light and be promoted to the higher-energy orbital that has the vacancy. This d–d transition absorbs orange-red light, and the transmitted light appears blue.

   A common pitfall is to think that zinc is colourless because it is "not a transition metal" without giving the electronic reason. The full explanation must identify that the d¹⁰ configuration prevents d–d transitions, which is the electronic basis for zinc's exclusion from the transition metals.
