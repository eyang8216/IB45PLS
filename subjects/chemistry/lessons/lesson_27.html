Lesson 27: Brønsted-Lowry Theory, pH, Strong Acids & Bases

Syllabus reference: 8.1 — Theories of Acids and Bases; 8.2 — Properties of Acids and Bases

Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to:

  • Define acids and bases according to the Brønsted-Lowry theory: proton donors and proton acceptors.
  • Identify conjugate acid–base pairs in reactions and explain amphiprotic behaviour.
  • Calculate pH from $[\ce{H+}]$: $\text{pH} = -\log_{10}[\ce{H+}]$, and $[\ce{H+}]$ from pH: $[\ce{H+}] = 10^{-\text{pH}}$.
  • Determine the pH of strong acids and strong bases, accounting for complete dissociation and stoichiometry.
  • Apply the ionic product of water: $K_w = [\ce{H+}][\ce{OH-}] = 1.0 \times 10^{-14}$ at 298 K, and calculate pH of strong bases via pOH.

1. Prior Knowledge Check

Question 1: What is a proton in the context of acid–base chemistry? Why is $\ce{H+}$ called a proton?

▸ Show
A hydrogen atom consists of one proton and one electron. When hydrogen loses its electron, $\ce{H+}$ is simply a bare proton (for the isotope $\ce{^1H}$). In aqueous solution, $\ce{H+}$ immediately bonds to a water molecule to form $\ce{H3O+}$ (hydronium ion). The Brønsted-Lowry theory defines acid–base reactions as proton transfers from one species to another.

Question 2: What is a logarithm? Calculate $\log_{10}(1000)$, $\log_{10}(0.01)$, and $\log_{10}(2.0)$.

▸ Show
$\log_{10}(1000) = 3$ (since $10^3 = 1000$). $\log_{10}(0.01) = -2$ (since $10^{-2} = 0.01$). $\log_{10}(2.0) \approx 0.301$. The pH scale is logarithmic: a decrease of 1 pH unit means the $[\ce{H+}]$ has increased by a factor of 10. Small changes in pH correspond to large changes in acidity.

2. Brønsted-Lowry Theory — Proton Transfer

Brønsted-Lowry definitions:
  • Acid: A proton ($\ce{H+}$) donor.
  • Base: A proton ($\ce{H+}$) acceptor.
Every acid–base reaction involves the transfer of a proton from the acid to the base. The acid becomes its conjugate base (after losing $\ce{H+}$); the base becomes its conjugate acid (after gaining $\ce{H+}$).
Figure 1: The Brønsted-Lowry acid–base reaction. The acid (HA) donates a proton to the base (B). After the transfer, HA becomes its conjugate base (A⁻) and B becomes its conjugate acid (HB⁺). The two species on the left (HA and A⁻) form a conjugate acid–base pair.

2.1 Conjugate Acid–Base Pairs

A conjugate pair consists of two species that differ by exactly one proton ($\ce{H+}$). Examples:

  • $\ce{HCl}$ / $\ce{Cl-}$: HCl is the acid; Cl⁻ is its conjugate base.
  • $\ce{NH3}$ / $\ce{NH4+}$: NH₃ is the base; NH₄⁺ is its conjugate acid.
  • $\ce{H2O}$ / $\ce{OH-}$: H₂O acts as an acid; OH⁻ is its conjugate base.
  • $\ce{H2O}$ / $\ce{H3O+}$: H₂O acts as a base; H₃O⁺ is its conjugate acid.

2.2 Amphiprotic Species

An amphiprotic species can both donate and accept a proton — it can act as either an acid or a base. $\ce{H2O}$ is the classic example: it can accept a proton to become $\ce{H3O+}$ or donate a proton to become $\ce{OH-}$. Other examples: $\ce{HCO3-}$, $\ce{HSO4-}$, $\ce{H2PO4-}$.

Check-in: Identify the conjugate acid–base pairs in: $\ce{HNO3 + H2O -> H3O+ + NO3-}$

▸ Show
Two conjugate pairs: (1) $\ce{HNO3}$ (acid) / $\ce{NO3-}$ (conjugate base). (2) $\ce{H2O}$ (base) / $\ce{H3O+}$ (conjugate acid). HNO₃ donates a proton to H₂O. After the transfer: HNO₃ becomes NO₃⁻ (lost H⁺), H₂O becomes H₃O⁺ (gained H⁺).

3. The pH Scale

pH is a logarithmic measure of the hydrogen ion concentration:

$$\text{pH} = -\log_{10}[\ce{H+}] \quad \text{and} \quad [\ce{H+}] = 10^{-\text{pH}}$$ where $[\ce{H+}]$ is in mol dm⁻³. Analogously: $\text{pOH} = -\log_{10}[\ce{OH-}]$.
Figure 2: The pH scale. Each unit decrease in pH represents a 10× increase in [H⁺]. pH 0 (1 mol dm⁻³ H⁺) to pH 14 (10⁻¹⁴ mol dm⁻³ H⁺). At 298 K, pH 7 is neutral where [H⁺] = [OH⁻] = 10⁻⁷.

Check-in: Solution A has pH = 3.0 and Solution B has pH = 5.0. How many times more acidic is Solution A than B?

▸ Show
$[\ce{H+}]_A = 10^{-3} = 1.0 \times 10^{-3}$ mol dm⁻³. $[\ce{H+}]_B = 10^{-5} = 1.0 \times 10^{-5}$ mol dm⁻³. Ratio = $10^{-3} / 10^{-5} = 10^2 = 100$. Solution A is 100 times more acidic than B. The difference of 2 pH units = factor of $10^2 = 100$ in [H⁺].

4. Strong Acids and Bases — Complete Dissociation

4.1 Strong Acids

A strong acid dissociates completely in aqueous solution. The concentration of $\ce{H+}$ equals the concentration of the acid (for monoprotic acids like HCl) or a multiple of it (for diprotic acids like $\ce{H2SO4}$).

Strong AcidDissociation Equation$[\ce{H+}]$ from $c$ mol dm⁻³
$\ce{HCl}$$\ce{HCl -> H+ + Cl-}$$[\ce{H+}] = c$
$\ce{HNO3}$$\ce{HNO3 -> H+ + NO3-}$$[\ce{H+}] = c$
$\ce{H2SO4}$$\ce{H2SO4 -> 2H+ + SO4^{2-}}$$[\ce{H+}] = 2c$ (first dissociation is strong; second is also strong in dilute solution for IB purposes)

4.2 Strong Bases

A strong base dissociates completely, releasing $\ce{OH-}$ ions. Common strong bases: $\ce{NaOH}$, $\ce{KOH}$, $\ce{Ba(OH)2}$.

Strong BaseDissociation Equation$[\ce{OH-}]$ from $c$ mol dm⁻³
$\ce{NaOH}$$\ce{NaOH -> Na+ + OH-}$$[\ce{OH-}] = c$
$\ce{Ba(OH)2}$$\ce{Ba(OH)2 -> Ba^{2+} + 2OH-}$$[\ce{OH-}] = 2c$

4.3 The Ionic Product of Water, $K_w$

$$K_w = [\ce{H+}][\ce{OH-}] = 1.0 \times 10^{-14} \text{ at 298 K}$$ This relationship holds in all aqueous solutions — acidic, neutral, or basic. For a strong base, find $[\ce{OH-}]$, then $[\ce{H+}] = K_w / [\ce{OH-}]$, then pH = $-\log_{10}[\ce{H+}]$. Alternatively: pOH = $-\log_{10}[\ce{OH-}]$, and pH + pOH = 14 (at 298 K).

Check-in: Calculate the pH of 0.025 mol dm⁻³ $\ce{Ba(OH)2}$ at 298 K.

▸ Show
Ba(OH)₂ → Ba²⁺ + 2OH⁻, so $[\ce{OH-}] = 2 \times 0.025 = 0.050$ mol dm⁻³. $[\ce{H+}] = K_w / [\ce{OH-}] = 1.0\times10^{-14} / 0.050 = 2.0\times10^{-13}$ mol dm⁻³. pH = $-\log_{10}(2.0\times10^{-13}) = 12.70$. Alternatively: pOH = $-\log_{10}(0.050) = 1.30$, pH = $14 - 1.30 = 12.70$.

5. Worked Examples

Example 1 — pH of a Strong Acid:

Calculate the pH of 0.0030 mol dm⁻³ $\ce{HNO3}$.

Solution: HNO₃ is a strong monoprotic acid → complete dissociation: $[\ce{H+}] = 0.0030$ mol dm⁻³. pH = $-\log_{10}(0.0030) = -\log_{10}(3.0 \times 10^{-3}) = 2.52$.

⚠️ Common wrong approach: Reporting pH = 3.0 (just taking the exponent). $-\log(3.0\times10^{-3}) = 3 - \log(3.0) = 3 - 0.48 = 2.52$. The mantissa (decimal part) matters — it is not simply the exponent.
Example 2 — pH of a Strong Diprotic Acid:

Calculate $[\ce{H+}]$ and pH of 0.050 mol dm⁻³ $\ce{H2SO4}$.

Solution: H₂SO₄ is a strong diprotic acid. Both protons dissociate completely in dilute solution: $[\ce{H+}] = 2 \times 0.050 = 0.100$ mol dm⁻³. pH = $-\log_{10}(0.100) = 1.00$.

Note: At IB level, treat the second dissociation of H₂SO₄ as complete unless told otherwise. In reality, the second dissociation ($K_{a2} \approx 1.2\times10^{-2}$) is not fully complete, but this simplification is accepted at HL.
Example 3 — Finding [H⁺] from pH and Dilution:

A solution of HCl has pH = 1.30. (a) Calculate [H⁺]. (b) If 10.0 cm³ of this solution is diluted to 250 cm³, calculate the new pH.

Solution:
(a) $[\ce{H+}] = 10^{-1.30} = 0.0501$ mol dm⁻³. (Since HCl is monoprotic and strong, [HCl] = 0.0501 mol dm⁻³.)
(b) Dilution factor = 250/10.0 = 25. New [H⁺] = 0.0501/25 = 0.00200 mol dm⁻³. pH = $-\log_{10}(0.00200) = 2.70$.

⚠️ Common wrong approach: "Diluting by factor 25 changes pH by factor 25." pH is logarithmic: a dilution by factor 10 changes pH by +1. Dilution by 25 changes pH by $\log_{10}(25) = 1.40$. Verify: $1.30 + 1.40 = 2.70$ ✓.

⚠️ Common Mistakes to Avoid

  1. "pH + pOH always equals 14." Only at 298 K ($K_w = 1.0\times10^{-14}$). At higher temperatures, $K_w$ increases (water autoionisation is endothermic), so pH + pOH < 14 at higher T. Always check the temperature before using 14.
  2. "Strong acids have low pH; weak acids are less acidic." "Strong" and "weak" refer to the extent of dissociation, not the concentration. A 10⁻⁵ mol dm⁻³ strong acid (pH 5) has a higher pH than a 1 mol dm⁻³ weak acid (pH could be ~2). "Strong" ≠ "concentrated."
  3. "$\ce{H2SO4}$ always gives $[\ce{H+}] = 2c$." The second dissociation has $K_{a2} = 1.2\times10^{-2}$, so it is not 100% complete in concentrated solutions. However, IB generally treats H₂SO₄ as fully dissociating in both steps unless a question specifically tests the partial dissociation.
  4. "Water is neutral because $[\ce{H+}] = [\ce{OH-}]$." Yes, but this does not mean pH = 7 at all temperatures. At 373 K, $K_w \approx 5\times10^{-13}$, so neutral pH ≈ 6.1. Neutral means $[\ce{H+}] = [\ce{OH-}]$, whatever that value is at the given temperature.
  5. "pH can be negative." Yes — if $[\ce{H+}] > 1$ mol dm⁻³, pH is negative (e.g., 10 mol dm⁻³ HCl has pH = −1). The pH scale typically ranges 0–14, but it extends beyond these limits for very concentrated solutions.

Question 6 (IB multi-step): 0.400 g of $\ce{NaOH}$ is dissolved in water and made up to 250 cm³. ($M(\ce{NaOH}) = 40.0$ g mol⁻¹)
(a) Calculate the concentration of the NaOH solution.
(b) Write the dissociation equation and find $[\ce{OH-}]$.
(c) Calculate pOH and hence pH at 298 K.
(d) This solution is diluted by a factor of 10. Calculate the new pH.

Answer 1: B. The conjugate base of HSO₄⁻ is formed by removing one H⁺: HSO₄⁻ → SO₄²⁻ + H⁺. SO₄²⁻ is the conjugate base. H₂SO₄ is the conjugate acid of HSO₄⁻.

Answer 2: Two conjugate pairs: (1) NH₃ (base) / NH₄⁺ (conjugate acid) — NH₃ accepts a proton. (2) H₂O (acid) / OH⁻ (conjugate base) — H₂O donates a proton. In this reaction, H₂O acts as an acid and NH₃ acts as a base.

Answer 3: (a) [H⁺] = 0.0015, pH = $-\log(1.5\times10^{-3}) = 2.82$. (b) [OH⁻] = 0.020, pOH = $-\log(0.020) = 1.70$, pH = 14 − 1.70 = 12.30. (c) [H⁺] = 2×0.010 = 0.020, pH = $-\log(0.020) = 1.70$.

Answer 4: $[\ce{H+}] = 10^{-4.70} = 2.00 \times 10^{-5}$ mol dm⁻³. $[\ce{OH-}] = K_w/[\ce{H+}] = 1.0\times10^{-14} / 2.00\times10^{-5} = 5.00 \times 10^{-10}$ mol dm⁻³.

Answer 5: Original: [H⁺] = 0.10, pH = 1.00. Dilution factor = 100/5.0 = 20. New [H⁺] = 0.10/20 = 0.0050, pH = $-\log(5.0\times10^{-3}) = 2.30$. Check: dilution×20 → pH increase = $\log_{10}(20) = 1.30$ → $1.00 + 1.30 = 2.30$ ✓.

Answer 6: (a) n(NaOH) = 0.400/40.0 = 0.0100 mol. c = 0.0100/0.250 = 0.0400 mol dm⁻³. (b) NaOH → Na⁺ + OH⁻ → [OH⁻] = 0.0400 mol dm⁻³. (c) pOH = $-\log(0.0400) = 1.40$, pH = 14 − 1.40 = 12.60. (d) New [OH⁻] = 0.00400, pOH = $-\log(0.00400) = 2.40$, pH = 14 − 2.40 = 11.60. Each 10× dilution of a strong base reduces pH by 1 unit.

8. Exam Tips

Paper 1 (Multiple Choice): Conjugate pair identification is a classic question. Look for the two species that differ by exactly one $\ce{H+}$. The acid has one more H than its conjugate base. Common trap: $\ce{H3PO4}$ and $\ce{HPO4^{2-}}$ differ by two H⁺ — they are not a conjugate pair. The conjugate base of H₃PO₄ is H₂PO₄⁻.
Paper 2 pH Calculations: Always check whether the acid/base is monoprotic or diprotic before calculating [H⁺] or [OH⁻]. For H₂SO₄, $[\ce{H+}] = 2c$. For Ba(OH)₂, $[\ce{OH-}] = 2c$. Missing the factor of 2 is the most common error. Also: show the $K_w$ step explicitly when calculating pH of bases — it earns a method mark.
Sig Figs for pH: The number of decimal places in pH equals the number of significant figures in [H⁺]. If [H⁺] = $3.0 \times 10^{-4}$ (2 sig figs), pH = 3.52 (2 decimal places). The integer part of pH (the characteristic) corresponds to the exponent and does not count towards significant figures.
