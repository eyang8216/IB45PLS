1. BrГёnsted-Lowry Theory вЂ” A Way to Define Acids and Bases

What is an acid? What is a base?

The first thing you need to know about acids and bases is how to recognise them. In 1923, two chemists named Johannes BrГёnsted and Thomas Lowry independently proposed the same idea. They said: when you see a chemical reaction, look for which species gives away a hydrogen ion and which species receives it.

A hydrogen ion is a single proton. Its chemical symbol is HвЃғ. (Sometimes you will see it written as Hв‚ѓOвЃғ, which is a hydrogen ion attached to a water molecule. For most of our calculations, HвЃғ and Hв‚ѓOвЃғ mean the same thing.)

A BrГёnsted-Lowry acid is any chemical species (an atom, a molecule, or an ion) that gives away a hydrogen ion. Think of it as a proton donor. The word "donor" means it hands over a proton to something else.

A BrГёnsted-Lowry base is any chemical species that receives a hydrogen ion. Think of it as a proton acceptor. The word "acceptor" means it takes a proton from something else.

This idea is more useful than older definitions because it does not require water to be present. It works for reactions in any solvent, and even for reactions in the gas phase.

A concrete example

Let us look at what happens when hydrogen chloride gas dissolves in water. The chemical equation with state symbols is:

$$\ce{HCl(g) + H2O(l) -> H3O+(aq) + Cl-(aq)}$$

The symbol (g) means gas, (l) means liquid, and (aq) means aqueous вЂ” dissolved in water.

In this reaction, the hydrogen chloride molecule, HCl, gives away its hydrogen ion to the water molecule. Because HCl donates a proton, HCl is acting as the BrГёnsted-Lowry acid. The water molecule, Hв‚‚O, receives that proton. Because Hв‚‚O accepts a proton, Hв‚‚O is acting as the BrГёnsted-Lowry base.

Many students think that acids are always liquids that burn your skin, and bases are always slippery solids. But actually, according to BrГёnsted-Lowry theory, being an acid or a base is a behaviour вЂ” it is something a species does in a specific reaction, not something it always is. Water, for example, can act as a base (as it does here) or as an acid in other reactions.

What the "p" in pH means

The letter "p" in chemistry notation is an operator that means "take the negative logarithm of whatever comes after it." So when you see "pH," it means the negative logarithm (base 10) of the hydrogen ion concentration. This is a mathematical tool for making very small numbers easier to work with. Instead of writing the hydrogen ion concentration as 0.0000001, we can write the pH as 7.

2. Conjugate Acid-Base Pairs вЂ” The Other Side of Every Reaction

What "conjugate" means

When a BrГёnsted-Lowry acid gives away its proton, whatever is left behind is called its conjugate base. The word "conjugate" here means "linked together" or "paired." A conjugate base is simply the acid after it has lost a proton.

When a BrГёnsted-Lowry base receives a proton, the new species that forms is called its conjugate acid. It is the base after it has gained a proton.

A conjugate acid-base pair is two species that differ from each other by exactly one hydrogen ion. For example, HCl and ClвЃ» are a conjugate acid-base pair. HCl has one more HвЃғ than ClвЃ» does. Every acid has a conjugate base, and every base has a conjugate acid.

A species that can act as either a BrГёnsted-Lowry acid or a BrГёnsted-Lowry base is called amphiprotic. The word comes from "amphi" meaning "both" and "protic" referring to protons.

Strategy for solving this problem: We need to find who lost a proton and who gained a proton. Start by comparing the left side (reactants) and the right side (products) of the equation.

Why this makes sense: In any BrГёnsted-Lowry reaction, two conjugate pairs must exist. One species donates a proton (acid becoming conjugate base) and another species accepts that same proton (base becoming conjugate acid).

3. pH and the Ionic Product of Water, Kw

What pH actually measures

The pH of a solution is a number that tells you how acidic or basic (alkaline) that solution is. The pH scale usually runs from 0 to 14, although values outside this range are possible for very concentrated acids or bases.

The mathematical definition of pH is:

$$\text{pH} = -\log_{10}[\ce{H+}]$$

The square brackets are a standard notation in chemistry meaning "concentration of." The function "logв‚Ѓв‚Ђ" is the base-10 logarithm. The negative sign means that a higher concentration of HвЃғ gives a lower pH.

The reverse calculation is equally important. If you know the pH, you can find the hydrogen ion concentration:

$$[\ce{H+}] = 10^{-\text{pH}} \ \text{mol dm}^{-3}$$

Even the purest water you can find contains a tiny number of hydrogen ions and hydroxide ions. This is because water molecules can transfer a proton from one water molecule to another. This process is called the self-ionisation of water:

4. Strong Acids вЂ” Complete Dissociation

What "strong" means for an acid

The word "strong" in chemistry has a very specific meaning that is different from everyday language. A strong acid is an acid that dissociates completely when it dissolves in water. "Dissociate" means the acid molecules split apart into ions. "Completely" means that every single acid molecule breaks apart вЂ” there are no undissociated acid molecules left in the solution.

This is different from "concentrated," which tells you how much acid was dissolved. You can have a dilute strong acid (a small amount of HCl dissolved in lots of water) or a concentrated weak acid (a large amount of ethanoic acid dissolved in water, where most molecules stay intact).

The common strong acids

For IB Chemistry HL, you need to know these strong acids:

Hydrochloric acid: HCl (aq) в†’ HвЃғ (aq) + ClвЃ» (aq)

Nitric acid: HNOв‚ѓ (aq) в†’ HвЃғ (aq) + NOв‚ѓвЃ» (aq)

Sulfuric acid (first dissociation): Hв‚‚SOв‚„ (aq) в†’ HвЃғ (aq) + HSOв‚„вЃ» (aq)

Notice that I wrote a single arrow (в†’) for these reactions rather than a double arrow (в‡Њ). The single arrow means the reaction goes to completion вЂ” it does not establish an equilibrium because there is nothing to establish an equilibrium with. All of the acid dissociates.

Sulfuric acid, Hв‚‚SOв‚„, is a diprotic acid, meaning it has two hydrogen ions it can give away. The first hydrogen ion comes off completely (strong acid behaviour). The second hydrogen ion comes off the HSOв‚„вЃ» ion and this dissociation is only partial вЂ” it behaves as a weak acid. For IB HL, when asked about Hв‚‚SOв‚„ pH calculations, the syllabus typically expects you to treat both protons as fully dissociated unless told otherwise.

Calculating pH of a strong acid

Because a strong monoprotic acid (an acid with one HвЃғ to give away) dissociates completely, the concentration of HвЃғ in the solution equals the original concentration of the acid.

Why this makes sense: A concentration of 0.010 mol dmвЃ»Ві is exactly 10вЃ»ВІ mol dmвЃ»Ві, so pH should be exactly 2. The calculation confirms this.

5. Strong Bases вЂ” Complete Dissociation

What "strong" means for a base

A strong base is a base that dissociates completely when it dissolves in water, releasing hydroxide ions, OHвЃ». Just as with strong acids, every formula unit of the base separates into its ions.

The strong bases you need to know for IB Chemistry HL are:

Sodium hydroxide: NaOH (s) в†’ NaвЃғ (aq) + OHвЃ» (aq)

Dilution вЂ” What happens when you add water

When you add water to an acid or base solution, you are diluting it. The amount (number of moles) of acid or base stays the same, but the volume increases, so the concentration decreases. To calculate the new concentration after dilution, use the formula:
