Lesson 21: Rate Equations, Orders of Reaction & Rate Constant (HL)

Syllabus reference: 16.1 — Rate Expression and Reaction Mechanism (HL)

Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to:

  • Write the rate equation (rate law) for a reaction given experimental data: $\text{rate} = k[\ce{A}]^m[\ce{B}]^n$.
  • Determine orders of reaction (zero, first, second) with respect to each reactant using the method of initial rates.
  • Calculate the rate constant $k$ and its units from experimental data.
  • Sketch and interpret concentration–time graphs for zero-, first-, and second-order reactions.
  • Deduce the overall order of a reaction and predict how changing concentration affects the rate.

1. Prior Knowledge Check

Question 1: Write a general expression showing how reaction rate depends on the concentration of a reactant A if the reaction is (a) zero order in A, (b) first order in A.

▸ Show
(a) Zero order: rate is independent of [A] — doubling [A] does not change the rate. (b) First order: rate $\propto$ [A] — doubling [A] doubles the rate. The order tells you the power to which the concentration is raised in the rate equation.

Question 2: In the reaction $\ce{2A + B -> C}$, why can we NOT write rate $= k[\ce{A}]^2[\ce{B}]$ based solely on the stoichiometric equation?

▸ Show
The stoichiometric coefficients are not necessarily the orders. Reaction order must be determined experimentally — it depends on the mechanism, not the overall equation. The reaction could proceed through multiple elementary steps, and the slowest step (rate-determining step) dictates the rate law. Only for elementary (single-step) reactions do stoichiometric coefficients equal the orders.

2. The Rate Equation — Definition and Meaning

The rate equation (or rate law) is a mathematical expression that relates the rate of a reaction to the concentrations of reactants raised to some powers:

General rate equation: $$\text{rate} = k[\ce{A}]^m[\ce{B}]^n$$ $k$ = rate constant (temperature-dependent); $m$ = order with respect to A; $n$ = order with respect to B; $m + n$ = overall order.

The orders $m$ and $n$ are usually small integers (0, 1, or 2) — they are not the stoichiometric coefficients. The rate constant $k$ is independent of concentration but increases with temperature. Its units depend on the overall order.

Overall OrderUnits of $k$Example Rate Equation
0mol dm⁻³ s⁻¹rate = $k$
1s⁻¹rate = $k[\ce{A}]$
2dm³ mol⁻¹ s⁻¹rate = $k[\ce{A}]^2$ or $k[\ce{A}][\ce{B}]$
3dm⁶ mol⁻² s⁻¹rate = $k[\ce{A}]^2[\ce{B}]$

Check-in: What are the units of $k$ for a reaction with rate = $k[\ce{A}][\ce{B}]^2$?

▸ Show
Overall order = $1 + 2 = 3$. Rate has units mol dm⁻³ s⁻¹. $k$ = rate / ($[\ce{A}][\ce{B}]^2$) = (mol dm⁻³ s⁻¹) / (mol dm⁻³ × (mol dm⁻³)²) = (mol dm⁻³ s⁻¹) / (mol³ dm⁻⁹) = dm⁶ mol⁻² s⁻¹. Always derive units from the rate equation — never memorise them.

3. Determining Orders: The Method of Initial Rates

The initial rate is the instantaneous rate at $t = 0$, measured before concentrations change significantly. By systematically varying one reactant's concentration while keeping others constant, you isolate its effect on the rate.

Figure 1: Concentration–time curves for three experiments with different initial [A]. The dashed tangents at t = 0 give the initial rates. Comparing how the initial rate changes as [A] changes reveals the order with respect to A.

3.1 The Systematic Approach

  1. Find two experiments where only one concentration changes while all others are constant.
  2. Compare the factor change in concentration to the factor change in rate.
  3. If rate does not change → zero order. If rate changes by same factor → first order. If rate changes by factor² → second order.
  4. Repeat for each reactant.
  5. Substitute data from any experiment into the rate equation to find $k$.

Check-in: For a reaction A + B → products, experiments give:
Expt 1: [A]=0.10, [B]=0.10, rate=2.0×10⁻⁴
Expt 2: [A]=0.20, [B]=0.10, rate=4.0×10⁻⁴
Expt 3: [A]=0.10, [B]=0.20, rate=2.0×10⁻⁴
Determine the orders and rate equation.

▸ Show
Comparing Expt 1→2: [A] doubles, [B] constant, rate doubles → first order in A. Comparing Expt 1→3: [B] doubles, [A] constant, rate unchanged → zero order in B. Rate equation: rate = $k[\ce{A}]$. Overall order = 1. $k$ = 2.0×10⁻⁴ / 0.10 = 2.0×10⁻³ s⁻¹.

4. Graphical Determination of Order

4.1 Concentration–Time Graphs

The shape of a concentration–time graph reveals the order:

  • Zero order: Linear decrease — constant gradient. [A] vs. $t$ is a straight line with slope = $-k$.
  • First order: Exponential decay — constant half-life. $\ln[\ce{A}]$ vs. $t$ is a straight line with slope = $-k$.
  • Second order: Curve steeper initially than first order. $1/[\ce{A}]$ vs. $t$ is a straight line with slope = $+k$.
Figure 2: Concentration–time curves for zero-, first-, and second-order reactions (same initial concentration, equal $k$ for comparison). Zero-order is linear; first-order has constant half-life; second-order drops fastest initially but takes longest to reach zero.

Check-in: A plot of $\ln[\ce{A}]$ against time gives a straight line. What is the order and how do you find $k$?

▸ Show
The reaction is first order with respect to A. For a first-order reaction, $\ln[\ce{A}]_t = \ln[\ce{A}]_0 - kt$, which is of the form $y = c + mx$. The slope of the $\ln[\ce{A}]$ vs. $t$ graph = $-k$. So $k = -\text{slope}$. The half-life $t_{1/2} = \ln2/k = 0.693/k$, and is constant throughout the reaction — a unique feature of first-order kinetics.

5. Worked Examples

Example 1 — Determining Order from Data:

For $\ce{2NO(g) + O2(g) -> 2NO2(g)}$, the following initial rate data were collected at 300 K:
Expt[NO] / mol dm⁻³[O₂] / mol dm⁻³Initial rate / mol dm⁻³ s⁻¹
10.0100.0102.5 × 10⁻⁵
20.0200.0101.0 × 10⁻⁴
30.0100.0205.0 × 10⁻⁵
Determine the rate equation and calculate $k$.

Solution:
Expt 1→2: [NO] ×2, [O₂] constant, rate ×4 ($1.0\times10^{-4} / 2.5\times10^{-5} = 4$). Since 2² = 4, second order in NO.
Expt 1→3: [O₂] ×2, [NO] constant, rate ×2 ($5.0\times10^{-5} / 2.5\times10^{-5} = 2$). First order in O₂.
Rate equation: rate = $k[\ce{NO}]^2[\ce{O2}]$. Overall order = 3.
$k$ = rate / ($[\ce{NO}]^2[\ce{O2}]$) = $2.5\times10^{-5} / ((0.010)^2(0.010))$ = $2.5\times10^{-5} / 1.0\times10^{-6}$ = 25 dm⁶ mol⁻² s⁻¹.

⚠️ Common wrong approach: Writing rate = $k[\ce{NO}]^2[\ce{O2}]$ because the coefficients are 2 and 1. This happens to be correct here, but it is coincidence — never assume orders equal coefficients. The data must confirm it.
Example 2 — Units of $k$:

A reaction is first order in A and second order in B. Derive the units of $k$.

Solution: Rate = $k[\ce{A}][\ce{B}]^2$. Overall order = 3.
Rate has units: mol dm⁻³ s⁻¹.
$[\ce{A}]$ has units: mol dm⁻³. $[\ce{B}]^2$ has units: mol² dm⁻⁶.
So $k$ = rate / ($[\ce{A}][\ce{B}]^2$) has units: (mol dm⁻³ s⁻¹) / (mol³ dm⁻⁹) = dm⁶ mol⁻² s⁻¹.

⚠️ Common wrong approach: Memorising a table of units without understanding. If the question gives a rate equation you haven't seen before, you must derive the units from scratch. Always start from: units of $k$ = units of rate ÷ (units of each concentration term raised to its order).
Example 3 — First-Order Half-Life:

The decomposition of $\ce{N2O5}$ is first order: $\ce{2N2O5 -> 4NO2 + O2}$. The rate constant is $k = 3.4 \times 10^{-5}$ s⁻¹ at 298 K. Calculate the half-life and the time for 75% decomposition.

Solution:
$t_{1/2} = \ln 2 / k = 0.693 / (3.4 \times 10^{-5}) = \mathbf{2.04 \times 10^4\text{ s}}$ (about 5.7 hours).
75% decomposed means 25% remains. After one half-life, 50% remains. After two half-lives, 25% remains. So $t_{75\%} = 2 \times t_{1/2} = \mathbf{4.08 \times 10^4\text{ s}}$ (about 11.3 hours).
This illustrates a key property of first-order reactions: each successive half-life takes the same amount of time. This is not true for zero- or second-order reactions.

⚠️ Common Mistakes to Avoid

  1. "The order equals the stoichiometric coefficient." This is only true for elementary (single-step) reactions. For most reactions, the order must be determined experimentally. The coefficients in the overall equation provide no information about the orders.
  2. "Zero order means the rate is zero." No. Zero order means the rate is independent of the concentration of that reactant. The reaction still proceeds at a constant rate (until the reactant runs out).
  3. "The rate constant $k$ is truly constant." $k$ is independent of concentration but depends strongly on temperature (Arrhenius equation) and may depend on the presence of a catalyst. "Constant" means concentration-independent.
  4. "If doubling [A] doubles the rate, it must be first order." Check at least two concentration changes. A single comparison could be misleading if there is experimental error. Always look for a pattern across multiple experiments.
  5. "Overall order cannot be fractional." It can be — fractional orders (e.g., 0.5, 1.5) occur in some mechanisms, particularly those involving dissociation equilibria before the rate-determining step. However, at IB HL, integer orders (0, 1, 2) are primarily expected.

6. End-of-Lesson Practice

Question 1 (Paper 1 style): The rate equation for a reaction is rate = $k[\ce{X}][\ce{Y}]^2$. If [X] is doubled and [Y] is halved, the rate:
A. stays the same   B. is halved   C. is doubled   D. is quartered

Question 2: For the reaction $\ce{A + 2B -> C}$, data: Expt 1: [A]=0.10, [B]=0.20, rate=1.0×10⁻³; Expt 2: [A]=0.20, [B]=0.20, rate=2.0×10⁻³; Expt 3: [A]=0.10, [B]=0.40, rate=4.0×10⁻³. Determine: (a) the orders, (b) the rate equation, (c) the value and units of $k$.

Question 3: A reaction is zero order in reactant P. Sketch the concentration–time graph for [P] and explain its shape.

Question 4: The half-life of a first-order reaction is 230 s. Calculate the rate constant $k$ and the time for the concentration to fall to 12.5% of its original value.

Question 5: For rate = $k[\ce{A}]^2$, derive the units of $k$ and predict the factor by which the rate increases if [A] is tripled.

Question 6 (IB multi-step): A student studies $\ce{2X + Y -> Z}$ and collects: Expt 1: [X]=0.20, [Y]=0.10, rate=8.0×10⁻⁴; Expt 2: [X]=0.40, [Y]=0.10, rate=3.2×10⁻³; Expt 3: [X]=0.20, [Y]=0.30, rate=8.0×10⁻⁴.
(a) Determine the order with respect to X and Y.
(b) Write the rate equation.
(c) Calculate $k$ with units.
(d) Predict the rate when [X]=0.30, [Y]=0.25.

Answer 1: B (halved). New rate = $k(2[\ce{X}])(\frac{1}{2}[\ce{Y}])^2 = k(2[\ce{X}])(\frac{1}{4}[\ce{Y}]^2) = \frac{1}{2}k[\ce{X}][\ce{Y}]^2$. The factor is $2 \times \frac{1}{4} = \frac{1}{2}$.

Answer 2: (a) Expt 1→2: [A] ×2, rate ×2 → first order in A. Expt 1→3: [B] ×2, rate ×4 → second order in B. (b) rate = $k[\ce{A}][\ce{B}]^2$. Overall order = 3. (c) $k = 1.0\times10^{-3} / (0.10 \times (0.20)^2) = 0.25$ dm⁶ mol⁻² s⁻¹.

Answer 3: A straight line with constant negative slope. Zero order: rate = $k$, so [P] decreases at a constant rate: $[\ce{P}]_t = [\ce{P}]_0 - kt$. The gradient = $-k$. The graph is linear because the rate does not depend on [P] — even as [P] falls, the rate stays the same.

Answer 4: $k = \ln2 / t_{1/2} = 0.693 / 230 = 3.01 \times 10^{-3}$ s⁻¹. 12.5% = 1/8 remains → 3 half-lives. $t = 3 \times 230 = \mathbf{690\text{ s}}$.

Answer 5: $k$ = rate/$[\ce{A}]^2$ → units = (mol dm⁻³ s⁻¹)/(mol² dm⁻⁶) = dm³ mol⁻¹ s⁻¹. If [A] is tripled, rate changes by factor $3^2 = 9$, so the rate increases 9-fold.

Answer 6: (a) Expt 1→2: [X] ×2, rate ×4 → second order in X. Expt 1→3: [Y] ×3, rate unchanged → zero order in Y. (b) rate = $k[\ce{X}]^2$. (c) $k = 8.0\times10^{-4} / (0.20)^2 = 0.020$ dm³ mol⁻¹ s⁻¹. (d) rate = $0.020 \times (0.30)^2 = \mathbf{1.8 \times 10^{-3}}$ mol dm⁻³ s⁻¹. Note that [Y] does not affect the rate.

7. Key Terms

TermDefinition
Rate equationMathematical expression: rate = $k[\ce{A}]^m[\ce{B}]^n$, relating rate to reactant concentrations
Order of reactionThe power to which a concentration is raised in the rate equation (0, 1, or 2 typically)
Overall orderThe sum of all individual orders in the rate equation
Rate constant ($k$)Proportionality constant in the rate equation; independent of concentration but temperature-dependent
Method of initial ratesTechnique: vary one concentration at a time, measure initial rate, deduce order from rate-change factors

8. Exam Tips

Paper 1 (Multiple Choice): Order-determination questions typically present a table of 3 experiments. Identify the pair where only one concentration changes. If the rate doubles when concentration doubles → first order. If the rate quadruples → second order. If the rate is unchanged → zero order. Watch for questions where both concentrations change — you must account for the known order of one reactant to isolate the other.
Paper 2 (Structured): When calculating $k$, you must include correct units. At least one mark is allocated for units. Derive them: units of $k$ = (mol dm⁻³ s⁻¹) / (mol dm⁻³)^overall order. If you cannot remember this, do dimensional analysis: rearrange the rate equation to $k = \text{rate}/([\ce{A}]^m[\ce{B}]^n)$ and cancel units.
Graphical Analysis: If given a graph, identify the linearised form: straight-line [A] vs. $t$ → zero order; straight-line $\ln[\ce{A}]$ vs. $t$ → first order; straight-line $1/[\ce{A}]$ vs. $t$ → second order. The slope gives $k$ (or $-k$, depending on the plot). Exam questions often give raw [A] vs. $t$ data and ask you to determine the order by testing which transformation gives a straight line.
