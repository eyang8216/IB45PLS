## 1. Lewis Theory — The Broadest Definition of Acids and Bases

### Why we need a broader definition

The Brønsted-Lowry theory defines acids as proton donors and bases as proton acceptors. This works for almost all acid-base reactions you encounter in aqueous solution. However, some reactions look and behave like acid-base reactions even though no protons are transferred. For example, when boron trifluoride (BF₃) reacts with ammonia (NH₃), a new bond forms between the boron atom and the nitrogen atom. No hydrogen ion is given or taken. Yet BF₃ behaves in a way that is chemically similar to how H⁺ behaves.

To account for reactions like this, the American chemist Gilbert N. Lewis proposed a more general definition in 1923, the same year Brønsted and Lowry published their theory.

### The Lewis definitions

A **Lewis acid** is a chemical species that **accepts an electron pair**. The key word is "accepts." A Lewis acid has an empty orbital or a region of positive charge that can receive a pair of electrons.

A **Lewis base** is a chemical species that **donates an electron pair**. The key word is "donates." A Lewis base has a lone pair of electrons that it can share with the Lewis acid.

When a Lewis base donates its electron pair to a Lewis acid, the two species form a new **coordinate covalent bond** (also called a dative bond). In a coordinate covalent bond, both electrons in the bond come from the same atom — the Lewis base.

### The relationship between Lewis and Brønsted-Lowry

Every Brønsted-Lowry base is also a Lewis base, because to accept a proton (H⁺), a species must donate an electron pair to form a bond with the proton. Similarly, every Brønsted-Lowry acid reaction can be described in Lewis terms, because the proton (H⁺) itself is a Lewis acid — it has an empty 1s orbital and accepts an electron pair from the base.

However, many Lewis acids are not Brønsted-Lowry acids. BF₃, AlCl₃, CO₂, and transition metal ions such as Fe³⁺ and Cu²⁺ are all Lewis acids, but none of them donates a proton in the Brønsted-Lowry sense. The Lewis definition is therefore broader and more general.

### Examples of Lewis acid-base reactions

**Example 1:** Boron trifluoride and ammonia.

$$\ce{BF3 + NH3 -> F3B-NH3}$$

In BF₃, the boron atom has only six electrons in its valence shell (three single bonds to fluorine). This means boron has an empty orbital and can accept an electron pair. BF₃ is the Lewis acid. Ammonia, NH₃, has a lone pair of electrons on the nitrogen atom. NH₃ is the Lewis base. The nitrogen donates its lone pair to the boron, forming a new B—N bond.

**Example 2:** A proton and ammonia.

$$\ce{H+ + NH3 -> NH4+}$$

The proton H⁺ has no electrons at all — it is an empty orbital waiting to be filled. H⁺ is the Lewis acid. NH₃ donates its lone pair. NH₃ is the Lewis base. This is also a Brønsted-Lowry reaction, illustrating the overlap between the two theories.

**Example 3:** A transition metal ion forming a complex.

$$\ce{Cu^{2+}(aq) + 4NH3(aq) -> [Cu(NH3)4]^{2+}(aq)}$$

The copper(II) ion, Cu²⁺, has empty orbitals in its 3d, 4s, and 4p subshells. It accepts electron pairs from four ammonia molecules, acting as a Lewis acid. Each ammonia molecule donates its lone pair, acting as a Lewis base. The resulting species, [Cu(NH₃)₄]²⁺, is a complex ion. This reaction is a Lewis acid-base reaction but not a Brønsted-Lowry one, because no protons are transferred.

## 2. Polyprotic Acids — More Than One Ionisable Proton

### What a polyprotic acid is

A **polyprotic acid** is an acid that has more than one hydrogen atom that can be donated as a proton. The prefix "poly" means many, and "protic" refers to protons.

A **diprotic** acid has two ionisable protons. Examples include sulfuric acid (H₂SO₄), carbonic acid (H₂CO₃), and hydrogen sulfide (H₂S).

A **triprotic** acid has three ionisable protons. The most important example for IB Chemistry HL is phosphoric acid, H₃PO₄.

### Successive dissociation steps

When a polyprotic acid dissociates, it does not lose all its protons at once. Instead, the protons come off one at a time in a series of steps, and each step has its own Ka value.

For phosphoric acid, H₃PO₄, the three dissociation steps are:

Step 1: H₃PO₄ (aq) ⇌ H⁺ (aq) + H₂PO₄⁻ (aq) — Ka₁ = 7.5 × 10⁻³ mol dm⁻³

Step 2: H₂PO₄⁻ (aq) ⇌ H⁺ (aq) + HPO₄²⁻ (aq) — Ka₂ = 6.2 × 10⁻⁸ mol dm⁻³

Step 3: HPO₄²⁻ (aq) ⇌ H⁺ (aq) + PO₄³⁻ (aq) — Ka₃ = 4.8 × 10⁻¹³ mol dm⁻³

Look at the pattern: Ka₁ is about 10⁵ times larger than Ka₂, and Ka₂ is about 10⁵ times larger than Ka₃. Each successive dissociation is much weaker than the one before it. This is a universal pattern for polyprotic acids.

### Why successive Ka values decrease

There are two reasons why it becomes progressively harder to remove each additional proton:

First, **electrostatic attraction**: after the first proton is removed, the remaining species (H₂PO₄⁻) carries a negative charge. Removing a positively charged proton from a negatively charged ion is much harder than removing it from a neutral molecule, because the opposite charges attract each other. After the second proton is removed, the species (HPO₄²⁻) has a charge of −2, making the third proton even harder to remove.

Second, the **common ion effect**: the H⁺ ions produced in the first dissociation suppress the second and third dissociations through Le Chatelier's principle. The equilibrium for step 2 is shifted to the left by the high concentration of H⁺ already present from step 1.

### Calculating pH of a polyprotic acid solution

For most polyprotic acids, only the **first dissociation** contributes significantly to [H⁺]. The second and third dissociations produce such tiny amounts of additional H⁺ that they can be neglected in pH calculations. The rule is: if Ka₂ is at least 1000 times smaller than Ka₁, consider only Ka₁.

<p><strong>Strategy:</strong> Since Ka₂ is about 8000 times smaller than Ka₁ (5.6 × 10⁻¹¹ vs 4.3 × 10⁻⁷), the second dissociation contributes negligibly to [H⁺]. We only need to consider the first dissociation: H₂CO₃ (aq) ⇌ H⁺ (aq) + HCO₃⁻ (aq).</p>

## 3. Salt Hydrolysis — Why Salt Solutions Are Not Always Neutral

### The misconception about salts

Many students think that all salt solutions are neutral because salts are the product of acid-base neutralisation. This is incorrect. When a salt dissolves in water, its ions may react with water molecules in a process called **hydrolysis**. The word "hydrolysis" comes from "hydro" (water) and "lysis" (breaking). In this context, it means that the ions break apart water molecules by pulling off either H⁺ or OH⁻.

Whether a salt solution is acidic, basic, or neutral depends entirely on the acid and base from which the salt was formed.

### The four categories of salts

**Category 1: Salt from a strong acid and a strong base.**

Example: sodium chloride, NaCl, formed from HCl (strong acid) and NaOH (strong base).

When NaCl dissolves: NaCl (s) → Na⁺ (aq) + Cl⁻ (aq). The Na⁺ ion comes from NaOH, a strong base. Ions from strong bases (Na⁺, K⁺, Ba²⁺) do not hydrolyse because their conjugate bases (NaOH, KOH, Ba(OH)₂) are completely dissociated — there is no tendency for the reverse reaction. The Cl⁻ ion comes from HCl, a strong acid. Ions from strong acids (Cl⁻, NO₃⁻, Br⁻, I⁻) do not hydrolyse because their conjugate acids are completely dissociated. Since neither ion hydrolyses, the solution is **neutral** (pH ≈ 7).

**Category 2: Salt from a strong acid and a weak base.**

Example: ammonium chloride, NH₄Cl, formed from HCl (strong acid) and NH₃ (weak base).

The Cl⁻ ion does not hydrolyse (from strong acid). The NH₄⁺ ion comes from the weak base NH₃, so NH₄⁺ is the conjugate acid of a weak base. It hydrolyses:

$$\ce{NH4+(aq) + H2O(l) <=> NH3(aq) + H3O+(aq)}$$

This reaction produces H₃O⁺, making the solution **acidic** (pH < 7).

**Category 3: Salt from a weak acid and a strong base.**

Example: sodium ethanoate, CH₃COONa, formed from CH₃COOH (weak acid) and NaOH (strong base).

The Na⁺ ion does not hydrolyse (from strong base). The CH₃COO⁻ ion comes from the weak acid CH₃COOH, so CH₃COO⁻ is the conjugate base of a weak acid. It hydrolyses:

$$\ce{CH3COO-(aq) + H2O(l) <=> CH3COOH(aq) + OH-(aq)}$$

This reaction produces OH⁻, making the solution **basic** (pH > 7).

**Category 4: Salt from a weak acid and a weak base.**

Example: ammonium ethanoate, CH₃COONH₄, formed from CH₃COOH (weak acid) and NH₃ (weak base).

Both ions hydrolyse. The NH₄⁺ ion produces H₃O⁺ (acidic), and the CH₃COO⁻ ion produces OH⁻ (basic). The overall pH depends on the relative strengths of the acid and base: if Ka of the weak acid is greater than Kb of the weak base, the solution is acidic; if Kb is greater than Ka, the solution is basic; if they are equal, the solution is approximately neutral (this is rare).

### A quick rule

To predict whether a salt solution is acidic or basic, identify the "parent" acid and base. If the parent acid is strong and the parent base is strong, the salt is neutral. If the parent acid is strong and the parent base is weak, the salt is acidic. If the parent acid is weak and the parent base is strong, the salt is basic.

<p><strong>Worked Example 3:</strong> Predict whether an aqueous solution of each of the following salts will be acidic, basic, or neutral. (a) Potassium nitrate, KNO₃. (b) Copper(II) sulfate, CuSO₄. (c) Sodium carbonate, Na₂CO₃.</p>

<p><strong>Strategy:</strong> Identify the parent acid and base for each ion. Determine whether each ion hydrolyses.</p>

<p><strong>(a) KNO₃:</strong> K⁺ comes from KOH (strong base) — does not hydrolyse. NO₃⁻ comes from HNO₃ (strong acid) — does not hydrolyse. Both ions are spectators. Solution is <strong>neutral</strong>.</p>

SO₄²⁻ comes from H₂SO₄ — the first proton of H₂SO₄ is strong, but the second (from HSO₄⁻) is weak, so SO₄²⁻ can hydrolyse slightly: SO₄²⁻ (aq) + H₂O (l) ⇌ HSO₄⁻ (aq) + OH⁻ (aq). ## 4. Calculating the pH of a Salt Solution

### The method

When a salt contains an ion that hydrolyses, the pH calculation is similar to a weak acid or weak base calculation, but you must first find the appropriate Ka or Kb for the hydrolysing ion.

For the anion of a weak acid (A⁻), the hydrolysis reaction is: A⁻ (aq) + H₂O (l) ⇌ HA (aq) + OH⁻ (aq). The equilibrium constant for this reaction is Kb for A⁻:

$$K_b(\ce{A-}) = \frac{K_w}{K_a(\ce{HA})}$$

Once you have Kb, treat it as a weak base problem: find [OH⁻] using [OH⁻] = √(Kb × C₀), then pOH = −log[OH⁻], and pH = 14 − pOH (at 298 K).

For the cation of a weak base (for example, NH₄⁺), the hydrolysis is: NH₄⁺ (aq) + H₂O (l) ⇌ NH₃ (aq) + H₃O⁺ (aq). Here, Ka for NH₄⁺ is:

$$K_a(\ce{NH4+}) = \frac{K_w}{K_b(\ce{NH3})}$$

Treat this as a weak acid problem: find [H⁺] directly.

<div class="worked">
<p><strong>Worked Example 4:</strong> Calculate the pH of a solution of sodium ethanoate, CH₃COONa, with a concentration of 0.10 mol dm⁻³ at 298 K. The Ka of ethanoic acid, CH₃COOH, is 1.8 × 10⁻⁵ mol dm⁻³ and Kw is 1.0 × 10⁻¹⁴.</p>

<p><strong>Strategy:</strong> CH₃COONa dissolves to give Na⁺ (spectator) and CH₃COO⁻ (which hydrolyses). First, find Kb for CH₃COO⁻, then treat as a weak base problem.</p>

<p>Kb(CH₃COO⁻) = Kw ÷ Ka(CH₃COOH) = (1.0 × 10⁻¹⁴) ÷ (1.8 × 10⁻⁵).</p>
<p>Kb = (1.0 ÷ 1.8) × (10⁻¹⁴ ÷ 10⁻⁵) = 0.556 × 10⁻⁹ = 5.6 × 10⁻¹⁰ mol dm⁻³.</p>

<p>The hydrolysis reaction is: CH₃COO⁻ (aq) + H₂O (l) ⇌ CH₃COOH (aq) + OH⁻ (aq).</p>

<p>Using the approximation: [OH⁻] = √(Kb × C₀) = √(5.6 × 10⁻¹⁰ × 0.10) = √(5.6 × 10⁻¹¹).</p>
<p>[OH⁻] = √5.6 × √10⁻¹¹ = 2.37 × 10⁻⁵·⁵ ... Let me be more careful: √(5.6 × 10⁻¹¹) = √(56 × 10⁻¹²) = √56 × 10⁻⁶ ≈ 7.48 × 10⁻⁶ mol dm⁻³.</p>

<p>pOH = −log(7.48 × 10⁻⁶) = −[log(7.48) + log(10⁻⁶)] = −[0.874 + (−6.00)] = −(−5.126) = 5.13.</p>

<p>pH = 14.00 − 5.13 = 8.87.</p>

<p><strong>Why this makes sense:</strong> Sodium ethanoate is the salt of a weak acid and a strong base, so we expect a basic solution. A pH of 8.87 is mildly basic, which is consistent with this expectation. The conjugate base of a weak acid with Ka of 10⁻⁵ should produce a mildly basic solution — this is confirmed by the calculation.</p>

## 5. Comparing the Three Acid-Base Theories

| Theory | Definition of an acid | Definition of a base | Limitation |
|--------|----------------------|---------------------|------------|
| Arrhenius (1884) | Produces H⁺ in water | Produces OH⁻ in water | Only applies to aqueous solutions; cannot explain why NH₃ is a base |
| Brønsted-Lowry (1923) | Proton (H⁺) donor | Proton (H⁺) acceptor | Requires H⁺ transfer; cannot explain reactions like BF₃ + NH₃ |
| Lewis (1923) | Electron pair acceptor | Electron pair donor | The most general — covers all acid-base reactions |

The Lewis theory is the broadest. It includes all Brønsted-Lowry acids and bases, which in turn include all Arrhenius acids and bases. As you move from Arrhenius to Brønsted-Lowry to Lewis, the definitions become progressively more general and encompass more chemical reactions.

## Answers

### Answer 1

**(a) CO₂ + OH⁻ → HCO₃⁻:** In carbon dioxide, the carbon atom is bonded to two oxygen atoms by double bonds. The carbon atom has a partial positive charge because oxygen is more electronegative. The carbon atom in CO₂ can accept an electron pair from a nucleophile. The hydroxide ion, OH⁻, has three lone pairs on the oxygen atom. The hydroxide ion donates one of its lone pairs to the carbon atom of CO₂, forming a new C—O bond. Therefore, CO₂ is the Lewis acid (electron pair acceptor) and OH⁻ is the Lewis base (electron pair donor).

**(b) Fe³⁺ + 6H₂O → [Fe(H₂O)₆]³⁺:** The iron(III) ion, Fe³⁺, has empty orbitals in its 3d, 4s, and 4p subshells. It can accept electron pairs. Each water molecule has two lone pairs on its oxygen atom. Six water molecules each donate one lone pair to the iron(III) ion, forming six Fe—O coordinate covalent bonds. Therefore, Fe³⁺ is the Lewis acid and H₂O is the Lewis base.

**(c) SO₃ + H₂O → H₂SO₄:** In sulfur trioxide, the sulfur atom is bonded to three oxygen atoms. The sulfur atom in SO₃ has an incomplete octet (or can expand its octet to accept electron pairs). It accepts an electron pair from the oxygen atom of a water molecule. Water donates a lone pair from its oxygen atom. Therefore, SO₃ is the Lewis acid and H₂O is the Lewis base.

### Answer 2

**(a) NaCl:** Na⁺ comes from NaOH (strong base) — does not hydrolyse. Cl⁻ comes from HCl (strong acid) — does not hydrolyse. The solution is neutral.

**(b) NH₄NO₃:** NH₄⁺ comes from NH₃ (weak base) — hydrolyses: NH₄⁺ (aq) + H₂O (l) ⇌ NH₃ (aq) + H₃O⁺ (aq). NO₃⁻ comes from HNO₃ (strong acid) — does not hydrolyse. The solution is acidic.

**(c) K₂CO₃:** K⁺ comes from KOH (strong base) — does not hydrolyse. CO₃²⁻ comes from HCO₃⁻ (very weak acid) — hydrolyses: CO₃²⁻ (aq) + H₂O (l) ⇌ HCO₃⁻ (aq) + OH⁻ (aq). The solution is basic.

**(d) NH₄Cl:** NH₄⁺ comes from NH₃ (weak base) — hydrolyses: NH₄⁺ (aq) + H₂O (l) ⇌ NH₃ (aq) + H₃O⁺ (aq). Cl⁻ comes from HCl (strong acid) — does not hydrolyse. The solution is acidic.

### Answer 3

Only the first dissociation (Ka₁ = 1.0 × 10⁻⁷) is considered. Ka₂ (1.0 × 10⁻¹⁴) is 10⁷ times smaller and contributes negligibly.

The first dissociation: H₂S (aq) ⇌ H⁺ (aq) + HS⁻ (aq), Ka₁ = 1.0 × 10⁻⁷.

Using the approximation: x = √(Ka₁ × C₀) = √(1.0 × 10⁻⁷ × 0.050) = √(5.0 × 10⁻⁹).

x = √5.0 × √10⁻⁹ = 2.236 × 10⁻⁴·⁵ = 7.07 × 10⁻⁵ mol dm⁻³.

Check: percentage dissociation = (7.07 × 10⁻⁵ ÷ 0.050) × 100% = 0.14%. This is far less than 5%, so the approximation is valid.

[H⁺] = 7.07 × 10⁻⁵ mol dm⁻³.

pH = −log(7.07 × 10⁻⁵) = −[log(7.07) + log(10⁻⁵)] = −[0.849 + (−5.00)] = −(−4.151) = 4.15.

### Answer 4

NaCN dissolves to give Na⁺ (spectator) and CN⁻ (which hydrolyses).

Kb(CN⁻) = Kw ÷ Ka(HCN) = (1.0 × 10⁻¹⁴) ÷ (6.2 × 10⁻¹⁰).

Kb = (1.0 ÷ 6.2) × (10⁻¹⁴ ÷ 10⁻¹⁰) = 0.1613 × 10⁻⁴ = 1.61 × 10⁻⁵ mol dm⁻³.

The hydrolysis reaction is: CN⁻ (aq) + H₂O (l) ⇌ HCN (aq) + OH⁻ (aq).

[OH⁻] = √(Kb × C₀) = √(1.61 × 10⁻⁵ × 0.20) = √(3.22 × 10⁻⁶).

[OH⁻] = √3.22 × √10⁻⁶ = 1.795 × 10⁻³ mol dm⁻³.

pOH = −log(1.795 × 10⁻³) = −[log(1.795) + log(10⁻³)] = −[0.254 + (−3.00)] = −(−2.746) = 2.75.

pH = 14.00 − 2.75 = 11.25.

This strongly basic pH makes sense because HCN is an extremely weak acid (Ka = 6.2 × 10⁻¹⁰, pKa = 9.21), meaning its conjugate base CN⁻ is a relatively strong base (Kb ≈ 1.6 × 10⁻⁵, similar to ammonia).

### Answer 5

**(a)** When iron(III) chloride dissolves in water, it dissociates: FeCl₃ (s) → Fe³⁺ (aq) + 3Cl⁻ (aq). The chloride ions do not hydrolyse because they come from HCl, a strong acid. However, the Fe³⁺ ion is small and highly charged (charge density is high). In water, Fe³⁺ becomes hydrated, forming the complex ion [Fe(H₂O)₆]³⁺ (aq). The high positive charge on the iron pulls electron density away from the O—H bonds in the coordinated water molecules, weakening them. This allows one of the water molecules to lose a proton:

[Fe(H₂O)₆]³⁺ (aq) ⇌ [Fe(H₂O)₅(OH)]²⁺ (aq) + H⁺ (aq)

The release of H⁺ makes the solution acidic. This is a general property of small, highly charged metal cations (such as Fe³⁺, Al³⁺, Cr³⁺, and Cu²⁺).

**(b)** Ka₁ (7.5 × 10⁻³) is approximately 1.2 × 10⁵ times larger than Ka₂ (6.2 × 10⁻⁸). The reason is electrostatic: it is much harder to remove a positively charged proton from a negatively charged ion than from a neutral molecule. In the first dissociation, a proton is removed from neutral H₃PO₄. In the second dissociation, a proton must be removed from H₂PO₄⁻, which carries a charge of −1. The electrostatic attraction between the negatively charged ion and the positively charged proton makes the second proton much harder to remove. By the third dissociation, the proton must be removed from HPO₄²⁻ (charge −2), making it even harder — Ka₃ is only 4.8 × 10⁻¹³. Each successive negative charge increases the electrostatic grip on the remaining protons.

Using the approximation: x = √(Ka₁ × C₀) = √(7.5 × 10⁻³ × 0.10) = √(7.5 × 10⁻⁴).

**(d)** The student's claim is incorrect. Sodium dihydrogen phosphate, NaH₂PO₄, dissociates in water to Na⁺ (aq) and H₂PO₄⁻ (aq). Na⁺ is a spectator — it comes from NaOH, a strong base, and does not hydrolyse. However, H₂PO₄⁻ is amphiprotic: it can act as both an acid and a base.

As an acid (donating a proton): H₂PO₄⁻ (aq) ⇌ HPO₄²⁻ (aq) + H⁺ (aq). The equilibrium constant for this is Ka₂ = 6.2 × 10⁻⁸.

As a base (accepting a proton): H₂PO₄⁻ (aq) + H⁺ (aq) ⇌ H₃PO₄ (aq). The equilibrium constant for this is Kb = Kw ÷ Ka₁ = (1.0 × 10⁻¹⁴) ÷ (7.5 × 10⁻³) = 1.33 × 10⁻¹².

Now compare the two tendencies: Ka₂ (6.2 × 10⁻⁸) is much larger than Kb (1.33 × 10⁻¹²). Since Ka₂ >> Kb, the acidic behaviour of H₂PO₄⁻ dominates over its basic behaviour. The solution produces more H⁺ than OH⁻, making it acidic. The actual pH of a NaH₂PO₄ solution is approximately 4.7, which is indeed acidic (pH < 7). The solution is not neutral.
