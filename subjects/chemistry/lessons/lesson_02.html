Lesson 2: Subatomic Particles, Isotopes & Mass Spectrometry

Syllabus reference: 2.1–2.2 (Atomic Structure)

Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to:

  • Describe the three subatomic particles and their relative masses and charges.
  • Calculate relative atomic mass from isotope abundance data.
  • Explain how a mass spectrometer works and interpret a simple mass spectrum.
  • Determine the number of protons, neutrons, and electrons in any atom or ion given its nuclide notation.

1. Prior Knowledge Check

Question 1: What does the atomic number (Z) of an element tell you?

▸ Show
The atomic number (Z) is the number of protons in the nucleus. In a neutral atom, it also equals the number of electrons. Z defines which element an atom is — change Z, and you change the element.

Question 2: An atom of carbon-12 has 6 protons. How many neutrons does it have?

▸ Show
12 − 6 = 6 neutrons. The mass number (12) is protons + neutrons. If there are 6 protons, the remaining 6 must be neutrons.

2.1 Nuclide Notation

Every atom can be described using nuclide notation. The atomic number (Z) is the number of protons. The mass number (A) is the total number of protons plus neutrons. They are written like this:

Figure 2: Nuclide notation. A = protons + neutrons (mass number), Z = protons (atomic number), X = element symbol. Example: $\ce{^{56}_{26}Fe}$ has 26 protons, 30 neutrons (56−26), and (if neutral) 26 electrons.

Check-in: How many protons, neutrons, and electrons are in the ion $\ce{^{56}_{26}Fe^{3+}}$?

▸ Show
Protons: 26 (Z never changes for a given element). Neutrons: 56 − 26 = 30. Electrons: 26 − 3 = 23 (the 3+ charge means it has lost 3 electrons).

3. Isotopes — Same Element, Different Mass

Isotopes are atoms of the same element (same number of protons) that have different numbers of neutrons. Because neutrons contribute mass but not charge, isotopes of the same element have identical chemical properties but different masses.

Figure 3: Carbon-12 and carbon-14 are isotopes. Both have 6 protons (they are carbon), but ¹²C has 6 neutrons while ¹⁴C has 8 neutrons.

4. Relative Atomic Mass — the Weighted Average

Most elements exist as a mixture of isotopes. The mass you see on the periodic table is NOT the mass of any single isotope — it is the weighted average of all naturally occurring isotopes, taking into account how abundant each isotope is.

Relative atomic mass ($A_r$): $$A_r = \frac{\sum (\text{isotope mass} \times \text{percentage abundance})}{100}$$ If abundances are given as fractions (0–1), omit the ÷100.
Worked: Gallium has two isotopes: ⁶⁹Ga (60%) and ⁷¹Ga (40%). Calculate $A_r$.

Step 1: Apply the weighted average formula.
$A_r = \frac{(69 \times 60) + (71 \times 40)}{100} = \frac{4140 + 2840}{100} = \frac{6980}{100} = \mathbf{69.8}$

Why this makes sense: The answer (69.8) is closer to 69 than 71 because ⁶⁹Ga is more abundant. A weighted average always leans toward the more heavily weighted value.

Check-in: Chlorine has two isotopes: ³⁵Cl (75%) and ³⁷Cl (25%). Calculate $A_r$ of chlorine. Then check your periodic table — does it match?

▸ Show
$A_r = \frac{(35 \times 75) + (37 \times 25)}{100} = \frac{2625 + 925}{100} = 35.5$. Yes — chlorine's $A_r$ on the periodic table is 35.45, which rounds to 35.5.

4.1 Working Backwards — Finding Abundance from $A_r$

Sometimes you are given $A_r$ and the masses of the isotopes, and you need to find the percentage abundance of each isotope. Set up an equation with one unknown.

Worked: Lithium has $A_r = 6.94$ and two isotopes: ⁶Li and ⁷Li. Find the percentage of each.

Step 1: Let $x$ = fraction of ⁷Li (more massive isotope). Then $(1-x)$ = fraction of ⁶Li.
Step 2: $6.94 = 6(1-x) + 7x = 6 - 6x + 7x = 6 + x$
Step 3: $x = 6.94 - 6 = 0.94$. So ⁷Li = 94% and ⁶Li = 6%.
Why this makes sense: $A_r$ (6.94) is very close to 7, so ⁷Li must dominate.

5. The Mass Spectrometer — How It Works

A mass spectrometer is an instrument that separates particles by their mass-to-charge ratio ($m/z$). It tells us which isotopes exist and in what abundance. The process has five stages:

Figure 4: The five stages of a mass spectrometer. The key physics: lighter ions and more highly charged ions deflect more in the magnetic field (Stage 4).

5.1 Interpreting a Mass Spectrum

The output is a mass spectrum: a graph of relative abundance (y-axis) against mass-to-charge ratio, $m/z$ (x-axis). For singly charged ions (1+), $m/z$ equals the mass of the ion.

Check-in: Bromine gas ($\ce{Br2}$) has a mass spectrum showing three peaks at $m/z$ = 158, 160, and 162 with intensity ratio 1:2:1. Bromine has two isotopes: ⁷⁹Br and ⁸¹Br, each ~50% abundant. Why three peaks? Why is 160 tallest?

▸ Show
Each $\ce{Br2}$ molecule contains two Br atoms. The three possible combinations are: ⁷⁹Br–⁷⁹Br (mass 158), ⁷⁹Br–⁸¹Br (mass 160), and ⁸¹Br–⁸¹Br (mass 162). The probabilities: 158 = 0.5×0.5 = 0.25 (1 part), 160 = 2×0.5×0.5 = 0.50 (2 parts — two ways to get one of each isotope), 162 = 0.5×0.5 = 0.25 (1 part). The 160 peak is tallest because the mixed-isotope molecule is twice as likely as either pure-isotope molecule. This is like flipping two coins: HT is twice as likely as HH or TT.

6. Worked Examples

Example 1 — Subatomic Particle Count:

How many protons, neutrons, and electrons are in one $\ce{^{31}_{15}P^{3-}}$ ion?

Step 1: Protons. Z = 15, so there are 15 protons.
Step 2: Neutrons. A − Z = 31 − 15 = 16 neutrons.
Step 3: Electrons. A neutral P atom would have 15 electrons. The 3− charge means the ion has GAINED 3 electrons, so it has 15 + 3 = 18 electrons.

⚠️ Common wrong approach: Subtracting electrons because the charge is negative. A negative charge means MORE electrons, not fewer. Think: "negative = extra electrons."
Example 2 — Mass Spectrum Interpretation:

A mass spectrum of magnesium shows three peaks: $m/z$ 24 (79%), $m/z$ 25 (10%), $m/z$ 26 (11%). Calculate $A_r$ of magnesium.

Step 1: Apply the weighted average formula.
$A_r = \frac{(24 \times 79) + (25 \times 10) + (26 \times 11)}{100}$
Step 2: $= \frac{1896 + 250 + 286}{100} = \frac{2432}{100} = \mathbf{24.3}$

This matches the periodic table. Magnesium exists as three stable isotopes.
Example 3 — Finding Abundance (Paper 1 Style):

An element X has two isotopes: ¹⁰X and ¹¹X. Its $A_r$ is 10.8. What is the percentage of ¹¹X?

Step 1: Let $x$ = fraction of ¹¹X. Then $10.8 = 10(1-x) + 11x = 10 + x$.
Step 2: $x = 0.8$, so ¹¹X = 80%.

⚠️ Common wrong approach: Guessing 50:50 from two isotopes. Always calculate — the weighted average tells you the true proportion.

⚠️ Common Mistakes to Avoid

  1. Confusing mass number with atomic number. The mass number (A) is the superscript (protons + neutrons). The atomic number (Z) is the subscript (protons). A common trap: seeing $\ce{^{56}_{26}Fe}$ and saying it has 56 protons. It has 26 protons and 56 total nucleons.
  2. Forgetting that a charge changes the electron count. A positive charge means the atom has LOST electrons. $\ce{Fe^{3+}}$ has FEWER electrons than Fe, not more. Count: electrons = Z − (charge). For $\ce{Fe^{3+}}$: 26 − 3 = 23 electrons.
  3. Treating $A_r$ as the mass of the most common isotope. Chlorine's $A_r$ is 35.5, but no chlorine atom actually has mass 35.5. It's a weighted average of ³⁵Cl and ³⁷Cl. The .5 tells you the two isotopes are not equally abundant.
  4. Thinking the mass spectrometer measures mass directly. It measures the mass-to-charge ratio ($m/z$). If an ion has a 2+ charge, it appears at half its mass (e.g., a ²⁺ ion of mass 90 would appear at $m/z$ = 45).

7. End-of-Lesson Practice

Question 1 (Paper 1 style): An atom has 19 protons and 20 neutrons. Which of the following is its nuclide notation?
A. $\ce{^{39}_{19}K}$   B. $\ce{^{20}_{19}K}$   C. $\ce{^{39}_{20}Ca}$   D. $\ce{^{19}_{39}K}$

Question 2: State the number of protons, neutrons, and electrons in: (a) $\ce{^{79}_{35}Br}$, (b) $\ce{^{79}_{35}Br^{-}}$, (c) $\ce{^{32}_{16}S^{2-}}$.

Question 3: Copper has two isotopes: ⁶³Cu (69%) and ⁶⁵Cu (31%). Calculate the relative atomic mass of copper.

Question 4: An element has $A_r = 79.9$ and two isotopes of mass 79 and 81. Calculate the percentage abundance of each isotope.

Question 5: A mass spectrum of chlorine gas ($\ce{Cl2}$) shows peaks at $m/z$ 70, 72, and 74. Chlorine has two isotopes: ³⁵Cl (75%) and ³⁷Cl (25%). (a) Explain why there are three peaks. (b) Predict the relative intensities of these three peaks. Show your probability calculation.

Question 6 (IB-style): The mass spectrum of element X shows peaks at $m/z$ 50 (4.3%), $m/z$ 52 (83.8%), $m/z$ 53 (9.5%), and $m/z$ 54 (2.4%). (a) Calculate the relative atomic mass of X to two decimal places. (b) Identify element X using the periodic table. (c) The peak at $m/z$ 53 does NOT come from a cation of a ⁵³X isotope. Suggest what ion produces this peak, given that X forms $\ce{X^{2+}}$ ions in the mass spectrometer.

Answer 1: A. A = protons + neutrons = 19 + 20 = 39. Z = 19 (potassium, K). $\ce{^{39}_{19}K}$.

Answer 2: (a) $\ce{^{79}_{35}Br}$: 35 protons, 79−35=44 neutrons, 35 electrons. (b) $\ce{^{79}_{35}Br^{-}}$: 35 protons, 44 neutrons, 36 electrons (gained one). (c) $\ce{^{32}_{16}S^{2-}}$: 16 protons, 32−16=16 neutrons, 18 electrons (gained two).

Answer 3: $A_r = \frac{(63 \times 69) + (65 \times 31)}{100} = \frac{4347 + 2015}{100} = \mathbf{63.6}$.

Answer 4: Let $x$ = fraction of ⁸¹Br. $79.9 = 79(1-x) + 81x = 79 + 2x$, so $x = 0.45$. ⁸¹Br = 45%, ⁷⁹Br = 55%.

Answer 5: (a) Three peaks come from three possible combinations of two Cl atoms: ³⁵Cl–³⁵Cl (m/z 70), ³⁵Cl–³⁷Cl or ³⁷Cl–³⁵Cl (m/z 72), and ³⁷Cl–³⁷Cl (m/z 74). (b) P(70) = 0.75×0.75 = 0.5625. P(72) = 2×0.75×0.25 = 0.375. P(74) = 0.25×0.25 = 0.0625. Ratio ≈ 9:6:1. The 70 peak is tallest because ³⁵Cl is more abundant.

Answer 6: (a) $A_r = \frac{(50\times4.3)+(52\times83.8)+(53\times9.5)+(54\times2.4)}{100} = \frac{215+4357.6+503.5+129.6}{100} = 52.06$. (b) Chromium (Cr), $A_r = 52.00$. (c) The peak at m/z 53 probably comes from $\ce{^{52}Cr^{2+}}$: a doubly charged ion of ⁵²Cr. m/z = 52/2 = 26? Wait, that doesn't work. Actually: a $\ce{^{53}Cr+}$ ion would give m/z 53. But if it's NOT ⁵³X⁺, it could be $\ce{^{106}X^{2+}}$ or a fragment. More likely: it's $\ce{^{52}CrH+}$ — a hydride ion where a ⁵²Cr atom picks up a proton, giving m/z 53.

8. Key Terms

TermDefinition
Atomic number (Z)The number of protons in the nucleus; defines the element
Mass number (A)The total number of protons and neutrons in the nucleus
IsotopeAtoms of the same element (same Z) with different numbers of neutrons (different A)
Nuclide notationA symbolic way to represent an atom: $\ce{^{A}_{Z}X}$
Relative atomic mass ($A_r$)The weighted average mass of naturally occurring isotopes relative to 1/12 of a carbon-12 atom
Mass spectrometerAn instrument that separates particles by mass-to-charge ratio ($m/z$)
Mass spectrumA graph of relative abundance against $m/z$, showing the isotopic composition of a sample

9. Exam Tips

Paper 1 (Multiple Choice): The "atom vs ion electron count" trap appears frequently. When you see a charge (e.g., $\ce{^{56}Fe^{3+}}$), immediately note: electrons = Z − charge. A positive charge means lost electrons — fewer, not more.
Paper 2 (Structured): Mass spectrum interpretation questions are worth 2–4 marks. The key is explaining the probability pattern for diatomic molecules like $\ce{Cl2}$ or $\ce{Br2}$ — show the multiplication clearly ($0.75 \times 0.75 = 0.5625$) and mention that the mixed-isotope molecule can form in TWO ways.
Common command term — "Explain": When asked to "explain" the presence of peaks in a mass spectrum, you must link the mass of each peak to a specific isotope combination AND discuss relative intensities. Marks are split: 1 for identifying combinations, 1 for probability reasoning.
