Lesson 2: Subatomic Particles, Isotopes & Mass Spectrometry

What You'll Learn

  • Identify the three subatomic particles, state their charges and relative masses, and explain where each is located inside the atom
  • Interpret nuclide notation (A and Z numbers) to determine the number of protons, neutrons, and electrons in any atom or ion
  • Explain what isotopes are, describe why isotopes of the same element have identical chemical properties, and compare their physical properties
  • Calculate the relative atomic mass (Aᵣ) of an element from the masses and percentage abundances of its isotopes
  • Describe each of the five stages of a mass spectrometer and explain, with reference to mass and charge, why ions are deflected
  • Interpret a mass spectrum to identify the isotopes present in a sample and calculate relative atomic mass

1. The Three Particles That Build Every Atom

What subatomic particles are and why knowing them matters

All matter is made of atoms, and every atom is built from just three types of smaller particles. These particles are called subatomic particles because they are smaller than an atom. The three subatomic particles are the proton, the neutron, and the electron. Understanding these three particles is the foundation of all chemistry, because the way atoms behave — how they bond, how they react, and what properties they have — is entirely determined by the number and arrangement of their protons, neutrons, and electrons.

The table below summarises the key facts about each particle:

Particle Symbol Charge Relative Mass Location in the Atom
---------- -------- -------- --------------- ----------------------
Proton p⁺ +1 (positive) 1 Inside the nucleus
Neutron n⁰ 0 (neutral, no charge) 1 Inside the nucleus
Electron e⁻ −1 (negative) Approximately 1/1840 (negligible) Outside the nucleus, in orbitals

The nucleus is the tiny, dense centre of the atom. It contains all the protons and neutrons, which together are called nucleons. Nearly all the mass of an atom is concentrated in the nucleus. The electrons occupy the much larger space surrounding the nucleus. To give you a sense of scale: if an atom were the size of a football stadium, the nucleus would be about the size of a marble sitting at the centre.

Atomic number and mass number — the identity card of an element

Every element is defined by its atomic number, which is given the symbol Z. The atomic number is simply the number of protons in the nucleus. This number never changes for a given element. Any atom with 6 protons is carbon. Any atom with 26 protons is iron. If you change the number of protons, you change the element.

The mass number, given the symbol A, is the total number of nucleons in the nucleus — that is, the number of protons plus the number of neutrons:

\[ A = \text{number of protons} + \text{number of neutrons} \]

In a neutral atom, the number of electrons equals the number of protons. This balance of positive and negative charges makes the atom electrically neutral overall. An ion is formed when an atom gains or loses electrons. A cation is a positively charged ion (fewer electrons than protons). An anion is a negatively charged ion (more electrons than protons). The number of protons never changes when an ion is formed — only electrons are gained or lost.

Nuclide notation — how we write an atom's identity

Chemists use a compact notation to show the composition of any atom or ion. The symbol for the element is written with the mass number as a superscript on the left and the atomic number as a subscript on the left:

\[ \ce{^{A}_{Z}X} \]

For example, \(\ce{^{23}_{11}Na}\) represents a sodium atom with mass number 23 and atomic number 11.

A common mistake is confusing the mass number with the atomic number. Remember: the larger number (A) goes on top, and it equals protons plus neutrons. The smaller number (Z) goes on the bottom, and it equals protons only.

Worked Example 1: Reading Nuclide Notation

Problem: An atom is represented by the symbol \(\ce{^{23}_{11}Na}\). State the number of protons, neutrons, and electrons in this atom.

Approach: The bottom number (Z) gives the number of protons. The top number (A) gives the total number of nucleons, so neutrons = A − Z. For a neutral atom, electrons equal protons.

Solution:

  • Protons = Z = 11.
  • Neutrons = A − Z = 23 − 11 = 12.
  • Electrons = protons = 11 (because the atom is neutral).

Why this makes sense: Sodium is element number 11 on the periodic table, so it must have 11 protons. The mass number 23 tells us there are 12 neutrons bringing the total nucleons to 23. The neutral atom must have 11 electrons to balance the 11 positive charges in the nucleus.

2. Isotopes — Same Element, Different Number of Neutrons

What isotopes are and why they exist

The number of protons in an atom is fixed for each element, but the number of neutrons can vary. Atoms of the same element that have different numbers of neutrons (and therefore different mass numbers) are called isotopes.

Consider chlorine. Every chlorine atom has 17 protons. But in nature, chlorine exists as a mixture of two isotopes:

  • \(\ce{^{35}_{17}Cl}\): 17 protons and 18 neutrons (mass number = 35)
  • \(\ce{^{37}_{17}Cl}\): 17 protons and 20 neutrons (mass number = 37)

Both isotopes are chlorine — they have the same atomic number (Z = 17), so they are the same element. They differ only in their mass numbers (A = 35 vs A = 37).

Chemical properties of isotopes — why they are identical

The chemical behaviour of an element is determined by the number and arrangement of its electrons. Since isotopes of the same element have exactly the same number of electrons (and the same electron configuration), they have identical chemical properties. Chlorine-35 and chlorine-37 undergo exactly the same reactions, form exactly the same compounds, and behave identically in all chemical processes.

Physical properties of isotopes — why they differ slightly

Some physical properties depend on mass. Heavier isotopes diffuse more slowly, have slightly higher densities, and have slightly higher boiling points than lighter isotopes of the same element. These differences are small but measurable. For example, water made with deuterium (hydrogen-2, also called heavy hydrogen) is called "heavy water" and has a density about 10% higher than ordinary water. It also has slightly different boiling and freezing points. These physical differences are the basis for separating isotopes in processes like uranium enrichment.

Worked Example 2: Counting Subatomic Particles in an Ion of an Isotope

Problem: How many protons, neutrons, and electrons are present in the chloride ion \(\ce{^{37}_{17}Cl^{-}}\)?

Approach: Z gives protons. A − Z gives neutrons. For a negative ion, we add the charge magnitude to the number of electrons that a neutral atom would have.

Solution:

  • Protons = Z = 17. The atomic number never changes for chlorine.
  • Neutrons = A − Z = 37 − 17 = 20.
  • A neutral chlorine atom would have 17 electrons. The Cl⁻ ion has gained one extra electron because it has a 1− charge, so electrons = 17 + 1 = 18.

Why this makes sense: The −1 charge means there is one more electron than protons. Since there are 17 protons (+17 charge), we need 18 electrons (−18 charge) to produce a net charge of −1.

3. Relative Atomic Mass — The Weighted Average of Isotopes

What relative atomic mass means and why the periodic table shows decimal numbers

If you look at the periodic table, you will notice that most atomic masses are not whole numbers. Chlorine, for example, has an atomic mass of 35.5. But no single chlorine atom has a mass of 35.5 — every chlorine atom has a mass that is either approximately 35 or approximately 37. The value 35.5 is a weighted average of the masses of all naturally occurring isotopes, taking into account how abundant each isotope is.

The quantity on the periodic table is called the relative atomic mass, symbol Aᵣ. It is defined as the weighted average mass of all the naturally occurring isotopes of an element, measured relative to 1/12 of the mass of a carbon-12 atom. Because Aᵣ is a relative measure, it has no units.

How to calculate relative atomic mass from isotope data

The formula for relative atomic mass is:

\[ A_r = \frac{\sum (\text{isotopic mass} \times \text{percentage abundance})}{100} \]

An equivalent way that uses fractional abundances (where 75% = 0.75) is:

\[ A_r = \sum (\text{isotopic mass} \times \text{fractional abundance}) \]
Worked Example 3: Calculating Relative Atomic Mass from Percentages

Problem: Naturally occurring chlorine consists of 75% of the isotope chlorine-35 (with a mass of 35.0) and 25% of the isotope chlorine-37 (with a mass of 37.0). Calculate the relative atomic mass of chlorine.

Approach: Multiply each isotopic mass by its percentage, add the products, and divide by 100.

\[ A_r = \frac{(35.0 \times 75) + (37.0 \times 25)}{100} \] \[ A_r = \frac{2625 + 925}{100} = \frac{3550}{100} = 35.5 \]

Why this makes sense: The value 35.5 is closer to 35 than to 37 because chlorine-35 is three times more abundant than chlorine-37 (75% vs 25%). The weighted average is pulled more strongly toward the more abundant isotope.

Worked Example 4: Working Backwards — Finding Isotope Abundances from Aᵣ

Problem: The element boron has two naturally occurring isotopes, boron-10 (with a mass of 10.0) and boron-11 (with a mass of 11.0). The relative atomic mass of boron on the periodic table is 10.8. Estimate the percentage abundance of each isotope.

Approach: Let the fraction of boron-11 be x, so the fraction of boron-10 is (1 − x). Set up a weighted average equation and solve for x.

\[ 10.8 = 10.0 \times (1 - x) + 11.0 \times x \] \[ 10.8 = 10.0 - 10.0x + 11.0x \] \[ 10.8 = 10.0 + 1.0x \] \[ x = 0.80 \]

So the fraction of boron-11 is 0.80, meaning 80% of boron atoms are boron-11 and 20% are boron-10.

Why this makes sense: The relative atomic mass of 10.8 is much closer to 11 than to 10, so the heavier isotope (boron-11) must be much more abundant. An 80:20 split is consistent with this observation.

4. The Mass Spectrometer — How We Measure Isotope Abundances

What a mass spectrometer does and why it is essential

A mass spectrometer is an instrument that separates atoms (or molecules) according to their mass-to-charge ratio. It produces a mass spectrum, which is a graph that shows the relative abundances of particles with different masses. The mass spectrometer is the tool that allows chemists to determine the isotopic composition of an element and to measure relative atomic masses with high precision.

The five stages of mass spectrometry

The operation of a mass spectrometer can be broken down into five sequential stages. Each stage performs a specific function.

Stage 1: Vaporisation. The sample is heated until it becomes a gas. This is necessary because the instrument can only process individual, separated particles. A solid or liquid sample must first be vaporised so that its atoms or molecules are free to move independently.

Stage 2: Ionisation. The gaseous atoms (or molecules) are bombarded with a beam of high-energy electrons. When a high-energy electron collides with an atom, it can knock out one of the atom's electrons, leaving behind a positively charged ion:

\[ \ce{X(g) + e^- -> X^+(g) + 2e^-} \]

This stage is essential because the next two stages — acceleration and deflection — rely on electric and magnetic fields, which only exert forces on charged particles. Neutral particles would pass straight through undetected.

A common question is why ions with a 1+ charge are the most common. The answer is that removing a second electron requires much more energy than removing the first, so under typical ionisation conditions, most ions formed carry a single positive charge.

Stage 3: Acceleration. The positive ions are attracted toward negatively charged plates, which accelerates them to a high speed. All ions with the same charge gain the same kinetic energy. However, because kinetic energy depends on both mass and velocity, lighter ions end up moving faster than heavier ions.

Stage 4: Deflection. The beam of fast-moving ions passes through a strong magnetic field. A magnetic field exerts a force on any moving charged particle, causing its path to bend. The amount of deflection depends on two factors: the mass of the ion and its charge. For ions with the same charge, lighter ions are deflected more than heavier ones. For ions with the same mass, ions with a higher charge (for example, 2+ instead of 1+) are deflected more. The ratio that determines deflection is m/z, where m is the mass of the ion and z is its charge.

Stage 5: Detection. Ions strike a detector, generating a small electrical current. The strength of the current is proportional to the number of ions hitting the detector. By gradually varying the strength of the magnetic field, ions with different m/z values are brought to the detector one after another. The instrument then produces a mass spectrum: a graph with m/z on the horizontal axis and relative abundance (or relative intensity) on the vertical axis.

Worked Example 5: Interpreting a Simple Mass Spectrum

Problem: The mass spectrum of the element magnesium shows three peaks at the following m/z values, with relative abundances given as percentages: m/z = 24 (79%), m/z = 25 (10%), and m/z = 26 (11%). Calculate the relative atomic mass of magnesium.

Approach: Each peak corresponds to a different isotope of magnesium. The peak height (relative abundance) tells us how much of that isotope is present in the sample. We use the same weighted average formula as before.

\[ A_r = \frac{(24 \times 79) + (25 \times 10) + (26 \times 11)}{100} \] \[ A_r = \frac{1896 + 250 + 286}{100} = \frac{2432}{100} = 24.3 \]

Why this makes sense: The periodic table value for magnesium is 24.3, matching our calculation. Magnesium has three naturally occurring stable isotopes: magnesium-24, magnesium-25, and magnesium-26. Magnesium-24 is by far the most abundant (79%), which is why the relative atomic mass is close to 24.

Practice Problems

Problem 1: (a) State the number of protons, neutrons, and electrons in a neutral atom of iron-56, which is written as \(\ce{^{56}_{26}Fe}\). (b) State the number of protons, neutrons, and electrons in the iron(III) ion, \(\ce{^{56}_{26}Fe^{3+}}\).

Problem 2: The element gallium has two naturally occurring isotopes: gallium-69 with a mass of 69.0 and an abundance of 60%, and gallium-71 with a mass of 71.0 and an abundance of 40%. Calculate the relative atomic mass of gallium.

Problem 3: The element lithium has a relative atomic mass of 6.94 on the periodic table. Lithium has two naturally occurring isotopes: lithium-6 (mass = 6.0) and lithium-7 (mass = 7.0). Calculate the percentage abundance of each isotope.

Problem 4: When bromine gas (Br₂) is analysed in a mass spectrometer, the mass spectrum shows three molecular ion peaks at m/z values of 158, 160, and 162. The relative intensities of these three peaks are approximately 1 : 2 : 1. Bromine has two stable isotopes, bromine-79 and bromine-81, which are present in roughly equal abundance (about 50% each). (a) Explain why three peaks are observed in the mass spectrum of Br₂ rather than just two peaks. (b) Explain why the peak at m/z = 160 is the tallest of the three peaks.

Problem 5 (IB-Style): A sample of an unknown element X is analysed in a mass spectrometer. The mass spectrum shows five peaks with the following m/z values and relative abundances: m/z = 90 (51.5%), m/z = 91 (11.2%), m/z = 92 (17.1%), m/z = 94 (17.4%), and m/z = 96 (2.8%). All peaks correspond to ions with a 1+ charge. (a) Calculate the relative atomic mass of element X to two decimal places. (b) The peak at m/z = 90 corresponds to the isotope written as \(\ce{^{90}X}\). Suggest the identity (mass number) of the isotope responsible for each of the other four peaks. (c) If some of the ions of the \(\ce{^{90}X}\) isotope carried a charge of 2+ instead of 1+, at what m/z value would these doubly charged ions appear in the mass spectrum? Explain your reasoning.

Answers

Answer 1: (a) For neutral \(\ce{^{56}_{26}Fe}\): protons = Z = 26. Neutrons = A − Z = 56 − 26 = 30. Electrons = protons = 26 (the atom is neutral, so the positive and negative charges balance).

(b) For the iron(III) ion \(\ce{^{56}_{26}Fe^{3+}}\): protons = 26 (the atomic number never changes). Neutrons = 30 (the number of neutrons is not affected by ionisation). A neutral iron atom has 26 electrons. The ion has a charge of 3+, meaning it has lost three electrons. Therefore, electrons = 26 − 3 = 23.

Answer 2: Using the relative atomic mass formula:

\[ A_r = \frac{(69.0 \times 60) + (71.0 \times 40)}{100} = \frac{4140 + 2840}{100} = \frac{6980}{100} = 69.8 \]

Answer 3: Let x be the fraction of lithium-7. Then (1 − x) is the fraction of lithium-6. Set up the weighted average:

\[ 6.94 = 6.0 \times (1 - x) + 7.0 \times x \] \[ 6.94 = 6.0 - 6.0x + 7.0x \] \[ 6.94 = 6.0 + 1.0x \] \[ x = 0.94 \]

So lithium-7 has an abundance of 94% and lithium-6 has an abundance of 6%. This makes sense because the relative atomic mass (6.94) is very close to 7, meaning the heavier isotope must dominate.

Answer 4: (a) Bromine gas exists as diatomic molecules, meaning each molecule contains two bromine atoms bonded together. Since there are two isotopes of bromine (bromine-79 and bromine-81), a Br₂ molecule can be assembled from these isotopes in three different combinations: both atoms can be bromine-79 (giving a total mass of 79 + 79 = 158), one atom can be bromine-79 and the other bromine-81 (giving a total mass of 79 + 81 = 160), or both atoms can be bromine-81 (giving a total mass of 81 + 81 = 162). Each of these three possible molecules produces a peak at the corresponding m/z value, which is why three peaks appear.

(b) The peak at m/z = 160 is tallest because the mixed-isotope molecule (one bromine-79 and one bromine-81) can be formed in two ways: the first atom could be bromine-79 and the second bromine-81, or the first could be bromine-81 and the second bromine-79. If each isotope has a 50% probability, the probability of getting both atoms as bromine-79 is 0.5 × 0.5 = 0.25 (25%), the probability of getting both as bromine-81 is also 0.25 (25%), and the probability of getting one of each is 2 × (0.5 × 0.5) = 0.50 (50%). The 50% probability for the mixed molecule is twice the 25% for either pure-isotope molecule, which is why the 160 peak has twice the intensity — exactly the 1:2:1 ratio observed in the spectrum.

Answer 5: (a) Calculate the weighted average:

\[ A_r = \frac{(90 \times 51.5) + (91 \times 11.2) + (92 \times 17.1) + (94 \times 17.4) + (96 \times 2.8)}{100} \] \[ A_r = \frac{4635 + 1019.2 + 1573.2 + 1635.6 + 268.8}{100} = \frac{9131.8}{100} = 91.32 \]

(b) The peaks correspond to isotopes: m/z = 91 is \(\ce{^{91}X}\), m/z = 92 is \(\ce{^{92}X}\), m/z = 94 is \(\ce{^{94}X}\), and m/z = 96 is \(\ce{^{96}X}\). This element is zirconium (Zr), which has a relative atomic mass of approximately 91.22 on the periodic table, consistent with our calculation.

(c) The mass spectrometer separates ions by their mass-to-charge ratio, m/z. For an ion with mass 90 and charge 1+, the m/z value is 90/1 = 90. If the same ion carries a charge of 2+ instead, its m/z value becomes 90/2 = 45. The doubly charged ion would therefore appear at m/z = 45. This is a common feature in mass spectra: doubly charged ions appear at half the m/z value of the corresponding singly charged ion. Students sometimes mistakenly think the m/z value is just the mass, but it is the ratio of mass to charge, so changing the charge shifts the peak.
