Lesson 8: The Periodic Table & Periodic Trends

Syllabus reference: 2.2/3.1/3.2 — The periodic table, periodic trends, oxides, group properties

Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to:

  • Explain how the periodic table is organised by atomic number into groups (vertical) and periods (horizontal).
  • Describe and rationalise trends across Period 3 and down groups: atomic radius, ionic radius, ionisation energy, electron affinity, and electronegativity.
  • Relate the acid–base character of Period 3 oxides to bonding type and position in the periodic table.
  • Compare the chemical behaviour of Group 1 (alkali metals) and Group 17 (halogens), including reactivity trends.
  • Predict properties of elements based on their position in the periodic table using trends.

1. Prior Knowledge Check

Question 1: What determines the group and period of an element from its electron configuration?

▸ Show
The period is the highest principal quantum number ($n$) occupied. The group is determined by the number of valence electrons: for s- and p-block elements, it equals the sum of electrons in the outermost s and p sub-levels. For example, Cl: [Ne]3s²3p⁵ → Period 3, Group 17 (7 valence electrons).

Question 2: Define electronegativity and state its trend across Period 3.

▸ Show
Electronegativity is the ability of an atom to attract a bonding pair of electrons in a covalent bond. Across Period 3 (Na → Cl), electronegativity increases because nuclear charge increases while shielding remains roughly constant, so the nucleus pulls bonding electrons more strongly.

Question 3: Why does atomic radius decrease across a period?

▸ Show
Across a period, electrons are added to the same principal energy level while the nuclear charge increases. The increased effective nuclear charge pulls the electron cloud closer to the nucleus, decreasing the atomic radius. Shielding from inner electrons remains approximately constant.

2.2 The Diagonal Relationship

Elements on a diagonal sometimes show similar properties despite being in different groups — e.g., Li and Mg, Be and Al, B and Si. This arises because moving right (increasing $Z$) increases polarising power, while moving down (increasing size) decreases it — the diagonal balances these effects.

Check-in: Lithium is in Group 1, yet it resembles magnesium (Group 2) more than sodium. Explain two chemical similarities.

▸ Show
(1) Both Li and Mg form normal oxides ($\ce{Li2O}$, $\ce{MgO}$), whereas Na forms a peroxide ($\ce{Na2O2}$). (2) Both Li and Mg form nitrides when heated in nitrogen ($\ce{Li3N}$, $\ce{Mg3N2}$), unlike other Group 1 metals. (3) Both have relatively high melting points and are harder than their group neighbours. The diagonal relationship arises because Li⁺ and Mg²⁺ have similar charge-to-radius ratios (similar polarising power).

3. Key Periodic Trends Explained

3.1 Atomic Radius

Figure 2: Atomic radius decreases across a period (more protons pulling on same shell) and increases down a group (additional electron shells).

Across a period: Atomic radius decreases — increasing effective nuclear charge pulls the same outer shell inward.
Down a group: Atomic radius increases — new principal energy levels are added, so outer electrons are further from the nucleus.

3.2 Ionic Radius — a Subtlety

Cations are smaller than their parent atoms (lost electrons, less repulsion, sometimes lost the entire outer shell). Anions are larger than their parent atoms (gained electrons, increased repulsion expands the electron cloud).

For isoelectronic species (same number of electrons): the one with the higher nuclear charge is smallest. Example: $\ce{N^{3-}}$, $\ce{O^{2-}}$, $\ce{F-}$, $\ce{Ne}$, $\ce{Na+}$, $\ce{Mg^{2+}}$, $\ce{Al^{3+}}$ all have 10 electrons. Ionic radius decreases in this order because $Z$ increases from 7 to 13.

Check-in: Arrange $\ce{S^{2-}}$, $\ce{Cl-}$, $\ce{K+}$, and $\ce{Ca^{2+}}$ in order of increasing ionic radius. All are isoelectronic with Ar (18 electrons).

▸ Show
All have 18 electrons. Nuclear charges: $\ce{Ca^{2+}}$ (20), $\ce{K+}$ (19), $\ce{Cl-}$ (17), $\ce{S^{2-}}$ (16). Higher $Z$ = stronger pull = smaller radius. Order of increasing radius: $\ce{Ca^{2+}} < \ce{K+} < \ce{Cl-} < \ce{S^{2-}}$.

3.3 Electronegativity Trends

Electronegativity is the tendency of an atom to attract a bonding pair of electrons. The Pauling scale is the most common. Fluorine (4.0) is the most electronegative element.

Across a period: Increases (stronger effective nuclear charge).
Down a group: Decreases (larger atomic radius, more shielding, bonding pair is further from nucleus).

Check-in: Why does sulfur (S₈, m.p. 119°C) have a higher melting point than phosphorus (P₄, m.p. 44°C), given both are simple molecular?

▸ Show
S₈ molecules are larger than P₄ molecules (8 atoms vs. 4 atoms), so they have more electrons and therefore stronger London (dispersion) forces between molecules. More energy is required to overcome these stronger intermolecular forces, giving sulfur a higher melting point.

5. Worked Examples

Example 1 — Predicting Properties from Position:

Element X is in Period 4, Group 16. Predict: (a) its electron configuration, (b) the formula of its hydride, (c) whether its oxide is acidic or basic, (d) its electronegativity relative to sulfur.

(a) Period 4, Group 16 → selenium (Se): [Ar] 3d¹⁰ 4s² 4p⁴.
(b) Group 16 hydride: $\ce{H2Se}$ (like $\ce{H2O}$, $\ce{H2S}$).
(c) Non-metal oxide → acidic: $\ce{SeO2}$ and $\ce{SeO3}$ react with bases.
(d) Electronegativity: Se is below S in Group 16, so electronegativity decreases (Se ≈ 2.4 vs. S ≈ 2.5). The bonding pair is further from the nucleus.
Example 2 — Period 3 Oxide Chemistry:

Write balanced equations for: (a) the reaction of $\ce{Na2O}$ with water, (b) the reaction of $\ce{SO3}$ with water, (c) the reaction of $\ce{Al2O3}$ with aqueous NaOH.

(a) $\ce{Na2O + H2O -> 2NaOH}$ (basic oxide, forms alkali).
(b) $\ce{SO3 + H2O -> H2SO4}$ (acidic oxide, forms sulfuric acid).
(c) $\ce{Al2O3 + 2NaOH + 3H2O -> 2NaAl(OH)4}$ (amphoteric — note that Al₂O₃ is insoluble in water but dissolves in alkali solution).
Example 3 — Ionic Radius Comparison:

Explain why $\ce{K+}$ (138 pm) is smaller than $\ce{Cl-}$ (181 pm), even though K (Z=19) has more protons than Cl (Z=17).

Answer: $\ce{K+}$ and $\ce{Cl-}$ are isoelectronic (both have 18 electrons), but $\ce{K+}$ has 19 protons while $\ce{Cl-}$ has only 17. The greater nuclear charge of $\ce{K+}$ exerts a stronger electrostatic pull on the same number of electrons, contracting the electron cloud. Additionally, $\ce{K+}$ has lost its 4s electron entirely, so its outer shell is now $n=3$ (same as $\ce{Cl-}$), not $n=4$.

6. Common Misconceptions

⚠️ Common Mistakes to Avoid

  1. Thinking atomic radius increases across a period. No — more protons in the same shell pull electrons inward. The radius decreases. This is one of the most counterintuitive trends for students.
  2. Claiming electronegativity increases down a group. It decreases because the bonding pair is further from the nucleus and more shielded. Fluorine (top of Group 17) is the most electronegative, not iodine.
  3. Forgetting that $\ce{Al2O3}$ is amphoteric. Many students classify it as basic because Al is a metal, but its position at the metal–nonmetal boundary gives it intermediate character. It reacts with both HCl and NaOH.
  4. Assuming all Group 1 metals react identically. While trends are similar, reactivity increases significantly down the group. Potassium reacts far more violently with water than lithium. This is because the outer electron is easier to remove (lower IE) and the atom is larger.
  5. Misidentifying the cause of the Period 3 melting point trend. The giant drop after Si is NOT about bond strength within molecules — it's about the type of structure. $\ce{P4}$, $\ce{S8}$, and $\ce{Cl2}$ are held together by weak intermolecular forces, not the strong covalent bonds within each molecule.

Question 2: Arrange each set in order of increasing size and explain: (a) $\ce{Na, Na+}$, (b) $\ce{Cl, Cl-}$, (c) $\ce{O^{2-}, F-, Ne, Na+, Mg^{2+}}$.

Question 3: Explain why the first ionisation energy of neon ($2081$ kJ mol⁻¹) is higher than that of fluorine ($1681$ kJ mol⁻¹), despite both being in Period 2.

Question 4: Describe and explain the trend in acid–base character of the oxides of Period 3 elements from sodium to chlorine.

Question 5: Astatine (At) is below iodine in Group 17. Predict: (a) its physical state at room temperature, (b) the formula of its compound with sodium, (c) how its reactivity compares with iodine, (d) the colour of its vapour.

Question 6 (HL-style): The melting points (K) of Period 3 elements are: Na 371, Mg 923, Al 933, Si 1687, P 317, S 392, Cl 172, Ar 84. Explain the trend in terms of bonding and structure, identifying the type of bonding/intermolecular force in each case.

Answer 1: B. Across Period 3, radius decreases from left to right, so Cl (smallest) < S < P (largest of the three). In A, the order is reversed (radius decreases Na→Al). In C, Mg is smaller than Na. In D, O is smaller than F (and S is larger than both).

Answer 2: (a) $\ce{Na+}$ < $\ce{Na}$ — Na⁺ has lost its 3s electron entirely, so its outer shell is now n=2, and there are 11 protons pulling on 10 electrons. (b) $\ce{Cl}$ < $\ce{Cl-}$ — Cl⁻ has an extra electron, increasing electron-electron repulsion and expanding the cloud. (c) $\ce{Mg^{2+}}$ < $\ce{Na+}$ < $\ce{Ne}$ < $\ce{F-}$ < $\ce{O^{2-}}$ — all isoelectronic (10 e⁻), size decreases as $Z$ increases (12→11→10→9→8).

Answer 3: Both Ne and F have electrons in the $n=2$ shell. Ne has 10 protons vs. F's 9, so the effective nuclear charge is greater. The 2p electrons in neon are therefore held more tightly, requiring more energy to remove. Additionally, removing an electron from neon disrupts a stable full octet.

Answer 4: $\ce{Na2O}$, $\ce{MgO}$: basic (ionic oxides of metals, react with acids). $\ce{Al2O3}$: amphoteric (aluminium sits at the metal–nonmetal boundary). $\ce{SiO2}$: acidic (giant covalent, reacts with hot concentrated bases). $\ce{P4O10}$, $\ce{SO3}$, $\ce{Cl2O7}$: acidic (covalent oxides of non-metals, react with water to form acids and with bases to form salts). The trend reflects the change from metallic to non-metallic character across Period 3.

Answer 5: (a) Solid — halogens become increasingly dense and less volatile down the group; iodine is a solid, so astatine should be too. (b) $\ce{NaAt}$ (1:1 ratio, like NaCl). (c) Less reactive than iodine — reactivity decreases down Group 17 because the atom is larger and attracts an extra electron less strongly. (d) Dark/black — halogens darken down the group; astatine is expected to be near-black.

Answer 6: Na, Mg, Al: metallic bonding. Strength increases Na→Mg→Al because more delocalised valence electrons (1, 2, 3) and smaller, more highly charged cations strengthen the metallic lattice. Si: giant covalent — very strong Si–Si covalent bonds throughout the lattice require a lot of energy to break (highest m.p.). P₄, S₈, Cl₂, Ar: simple molecular with van der Waals' forces only. S₈ (392 K) > P₄ (317 K) because S₈ molecules are larger (more electrons, stronger London forces). Cl₂ (172 K) < S₈ because Cl₂ molecules have far fewer electrons. Ar (84 K) is monatomic with only very weak instantaneous dipole–induced dipole forces.

8. Key Terms

TermDefinition
Atomic radiusHalf the distance between the nuclei of two bonded atoms of the same element
Ionic radiusThe radius of an ion in an ionic crystal; cations are smaller, anions larger than parent atoms
Ionisation energyThe minimum energy required to remove one mole of electrons from one mole of gaseous atoms
ElectronegativityA measure of the tendency of an atom to attract a bonding pair of electrons (Pauling scale)
Electron affinityThe energy change when one mole of electrons is added to one mole of gaseous atoms
Effective nuclear charge ($Z_{\text{eff}}$)The net positive charge experienced by an electron after accounting for shielding
IsoelectronicSpecies with the same number of electrons (e.g., $\ce{O^{2-}}$, $\ce{F-}$, $\ce{Ne}$, $\ce{Na+}$ all have 10)
AmphotericA substance that can react with both acids and bases (e.g., $\ce{Al2O3}$, $\ce{Al(OH)3}$)
Diagonal relationshipSimilarities between certain elements on a diagonal (e.g., Li/Mg, Be/Al, B/Si)
PeriodicityThe repeating pattern of physical and chemical properties across periods

9. Exam Tips

Paper 1 — Trend Questions: The IB loves "which is in the correct order?" questions. Your strategy: identify what property is being compared, recall the trend direction (across = decrease for radius, increase for IE/EN; down = increase for radius, decrease for IE/EN), then scan for the answer that matches. Eliminate options where two elements in the same period are in the wrong order.
Paper 2 — "Explain the trend" = 3 marks: The IB marking scheme expects three things: (1) state the direction of the trend, (2) mention nuclear charge and shielding, (3) relate to the specific property. E.g., "Atomic radius decreases across Period 3 because nuclear charge increases while shielding remains constant, so the outer electrons are pulled closer to the nucleus."
Data-Based Questions: If you're given a table of melting points and asked to explain the trend, always classify each substance by its structure type first (metallic, giant covalent, simple molecular). The explanation follows directly from structure — don't try to explain metallic trends with covalent arguments, or vice versa.
