Lesson 33: Balancing Redox Equations — The Half-Equation Method

What You'll Learn

In this lesson, you will learn how to write and balance redox equations. You will learn the half-equation method, which splits a full redox reaction into two separate pieces — one for oxidation and one for reduction. You will learn a systematic five-step procedure to balance each half-equation in acidic solution. You will learn how to combine half-equations to get the full balanced equation. This skill is essential for electrochemistry and for understanding how electrons move in chemical reactions.

1. What Is a Half-Equation?

A half-equation is a chemical equation that shows only one half of a redox reaction — either the oxidation part or the reduction part. In a half-equation, electrons are shown explicitly as either reactants or products. This is different from a full equation, where electrons are not shown because they cancel out.

Why do we use half-equations? Because it is much easier to balance a complicated redox reaction by breaking it into two simpler pieces, balancing each one separately, and then putting them back together. This method is systematic — it works step by step, and if you follow the steps in order, you will always arrive at the correct answer.

Rule for placing electrons:

  • In an oxidation half-equation, electrons are products (written on the right side) because the species being oxidised loses electrons.
  • In a reduction half-equation, electrons are reactants (written on the left side) because the species being reduced gains electrons.

Simple examples:

  • Oxidation half-equation: $$\ce{Zn(s) -> Zn^{2+}(aq) + 2e-}$$ — zinc loses two electrons.
  • Reduction half-equation: $$\ce{Cu^{2+}(aq) + 2e- -> Cu(s)}$$ — copper(II) ions gain two electrons.

2. The Five-Step Method for Balancing Half-Equations in Acidic Solution

The IB Chemistry course requires you to balance half-equations in acidic solution. "Acidic solution" means there are H⁺ ions available, and we can use H₂O and H⁺ when we need extra oxygen or hydrogen atoms. The method has five steps, and you must do them in this exact order:

Step 1: Write the skeleton. Write down the formulas of the species that are changing. This is a "before and after" — what the atom or ion looks like at the start and at the end.

Step 2: Balance all atoms except oxygen and hydrogen. Count the atoms of each element that is not O or H, and make sure both sides have the same number.

Step 3: Balance oxygen atoms by adding H₂O molecules. Count how many oxygen atoms are on each side. For every oxygen atom you need to add, add one H₂O molecule to the side that needs it.

Step 4: Balance hydrogen atoms by adding H⁺ ions. After step 3, count hydrogen atoms on both sides. For every hydrogen atom you need to add, add one H⁺ ion to the side that needs it.

Step 5: Balance the electrical charge by adding electrons (e⁻). Calculate the total charge on the left side and the total charge on the right side. Add enough electrons to the more positive side to make the charges equal.

Why this order matters: You must balance atoms before you balance charge. If you try to balance charge first, you will get the wrong answer because adding H₂O or H⁺ changes the charge.

3. Worked Examples of Half-Equation Balancing

Worked Example 1: Balance the reduction half-equation where manganate(VII) ions become manganese(II) ions in acidic solution.

The manganate(VII) ion is MnO₄⁻. The manganese(II) ion is Mn²⁺.

Step 1 — Skeleton: Write what changes.

$$\ce{MnO4- -> Mn^{2+}}$$

Step 2 — Balance atoms other than O and H: Only manganese needs checking. There is 1 Mn on the left and 1 Mn on the right. Manganese is already balanced.

Step 3 — Balance oxygen by adding H₂O: Count oxygen. Left: 4 O atoms (in MnO₄⁻). Right: 0 O atoms. We need to add 4 oxygen atoms to the right side. Add 4 H₂O molecules:

$$\ce{MnO4- -> Mn^{2+} + 4H2O}$$

Step 4 — Balance hydrogen by adding H⁺: Count hydrogen. Left: 0 H atoms. Right: 4 H₂O molecules, each with 2 H atoms, so 8 H atoms total. We need 8 H atoms on the left. Add 8 H⁺ ions:

$$\ce{MnO4- + 8H+ -> Mn^{2+} + 4H2O}$$

Step 5 — Balance charge by adding electrons: Calculate the total charge on each side. Left: MnO₄⁻ has charge −1, plus 8 H⁺ each with charge +1. Total = (−1) + 8(+1) = +7. Right: Mn²⁺ has charge +2, plus 4 H₂O which are neutral (charge 0). Total = +2. The left side is more positive by 5 units. To make them equal, we must add 5 electrons to the left side (electrons are negative, so adding them reduces the charge):

$$\ce{MnO4- + 8H+ + 5e- -> Mn^{2+} + 4H2O}$$

Check: Left charge: −1 + 8(+1) + 5(−1) = −1 + 8 − 5 = +2. Right charge: +2. Equal. All atoms balanced.

This is the famous half-equation for the reduction of purple manganate(VII) ions to colourless manganese(II) ions.

Worked Example 2: Balance the oxidation half-equation where oxalate ions become carbon dioxide in acidic solution.

The oxalate ion is C₂O₄²⁻. The product is carbon dioxide, CO₂.

Step 1 — Skeleton:

$$\ce{C2O4^{2-} -> CO2}$$

Step 2 — Balance atoms other than O and H: Look at carbon. Left: 2 C (in C₂O₄²⁻). Right: 1 C (in CO₂). We need 2 C on the right, so multiply CO₂ by 2:

$$\ce{C2O4^{2-} -> 2CO2}$$

Step 3 — Balance oxygen: Left: 4 O. Right: 2 × 2 = 4 O. Oxygen is already balanced.

Step 4 — Balance hydrogen: No hydrogen atoms anywhere. Skip this step.

Step 5 — Balance charge: Left charge: −2. Right charge: 0 (two neutral CO₂ molecules). The left is more negative by 2 units. To make both sides equal, we add 2 electrons to the right side (adding negative charge to the side that is more positive makes them match):

$$\ce{C2O4^{2-} -> 2CO2 + 2e-}$$

Check: Left charge: −2. Right charge: 2(−1) = −2. Equal. All atoms balanced.

Worked Example 3: Balance the reduction half-equation where dichromate(VI) ions become chromium(III) ions in acidic solution.

The dichromate(VI) ion is Cr₂O₇²⁻. The chromium(III) ion is Cr³⁺.

Step 1 — Skeleton:

$$\ce{Cr2O7^{2-} -> Cr^{3+}}$$

Step 2 — Balance atoms other than O and H: Left has 2 Cr. Right has 1 Cr. Multiply Cr³⁺ by 2:

$$\ce{Cr2O7^{2-} -> 2Cr^{3+}}$$

Step 3 — Balance oxygen: Left: 7 O. Right: 0 O. Add 7 H₂O to the right:

$$\ce{Cr2O7^{2-} -> 2Cr^{3+} + 7H2O}$$

Step 4 — Balance hydrogen: Left: 0 H. Right: 7 × 2 = 14 H. Add 14 H⁺ to the left:

$$\ce{Cr2O7^{2-} + 14H+ -> 2Cr^{3+} + 7H2O}$$

Step 5 — Balance charge: Left: −2 + 14(+1) = +12. Right: 2(+3) = +6. The left is more positive by 6. Add 6 e⁻ to the left:

$$\ce{Cr2O7^{2-} + 14H+ + 6e- -> 2Cr^{3+} + 7H2O}$$

Check: Left charge: −2 + 14 − 6 = +6. Right charge: +6. Equal.

4. Combining Half-Equations into a Full Redox Equation

Once you have balanced both half-equations separately, you combine them to get the balanced equation for the overall reaction. Here is the procedure:

  1. Write both balanced half-equations, one above the other.
  2. Look at the number of electrons in each half-equation. They must be equal because electrons lost in oxidation must equal electrons gained in reduction.
  3. If the electron counts are different, multiply one or both half-equations by the smallest whole numbers that make the electron counts equal.
  4. Add the two half-equations together. Everything on the left side of both stays on the left. Everything on the right of both stays on the right.
  5. Cancel anything that appears on both sides. Electrons will always cancel completely. Sometimes H⁺, H₂O, or other species also appear on both sides and must be cancelled.

Worked Example 4: Balance the full equation for the reaction of manganate(VII) ions with iron(II) ions in acidic solution.

The skeleton reaction is: MnO₄⁻ reacts with Fe²⁺ to produce Mn²⁺ and Fe³⁺.

To solve this, we need to: write and balance each half-equation, then combine them by making the electron counts equal.

Reduction half-equation (MnO₄⁻ → Mn²⁺), from Worked Example 1:

$$\ce{MnO4- + 8H+ + 5e- -> Mn^{2+} + 4H2O}$$

Oxidation half-equation (Fe²⁺ → Fe³⁺):

Step 1: $$\ce{Fe^{2+} -> Fe^{3+}}$$

Step 2: Fe already balanced.

Step 3: No O.

Step 4: No H.

Step 5: Left charge = +2, right charge = +3. Add 1 e⁻ to the right:

$$\ce{Fe^{2+} -> Fe^{3+} + e-}$$

Now we have 5 electrons in the reduction half and 1 electron in the oxidation half. The least common multiple is 5. Multiply the oxidation half-equation by 5:

$$\ce{5Fe^{2+} -> 5Fe^{3+} + 5e-}$$

Now add the reduction half-equation and the multiplied oxidation half-equation:

Left sides: $$\ce{MnO4- + 8H+ + 5e- + 5Fe^{2+}}$$

Right sides: $$\ce{Mn^{2+} + 4H2O + 5Fe^{3+} + 5e-}$$

The 5e⁻ appear on both sides — cancel them:

$$\ce{MnO4- + 8H+ + 5Fe^{2+} -> Mn^{2+} + 4H2O + 5Fe^{3+}}$$

Why this makes sense: One MnO₄⁻ gains 5 electrons, which is enough to oxidise 5 Fe²⁺ ions (each losing 1 electron). The stoichiometric ratio 1:5 matches the electron count.

Worked Example 5: Balance the full equation for the reaction of dichromate(VI) ions with sulfur dioxide in acidic solution.

The skeleton reaction: Cr₂O₇²⁻ reacts with SO₂ to produce Cr³⁺ and SO₄²⁻.

Reduction half-equation (Cr₂O₇²⁻ → Cr³⁺), from Worked Example 3:

$$\ce{Cr2O7^{2-} + 14H+ + 6e- -> 2Cr^{3+} + 7H2O}$$

Oxidation half-equation (SO₂ → SO₄²⁻):

Step 1: $$\ce{SO2 -> SO4^{2-}}$$

Step 2: S already balanced (1 each side).

Step 3: Left has 2 O, right has 4 O. Add 2 H₂O to the left:

$$\ce{SO2 + 2H2O -> SO4^{2-}}$$

Step 4: Left now has 4 H (from 2 H₂O), right has 0 H. Add 4 H⁺ to the right:

$$\ce{SO2 + 2H2O -> SO4^{2-} + 4H+}$$

Step 5: Left charge = 0. Right charge = −2 + 4(+1) = +2. Add 2 e⁻ to the right:

$$\ce{SO2 + 2H2O -> SO4^{2-} + 4H+ + 2e-}$$

Now we have 6 electrons (reduction) and 2 electrons (oxidation). Multiply the oxidation half by 3 to get 6 electrons:

$$\ce{3SO2 + 6H2O -> 3SO4^{2-} + 12H+ + 6e-}$$

Add the two half-equations:

Left: $$\ce{Cr2O7^{2-} + 14H+ + 6e- + 3SO2 + 6H2O}$$

Right: $$\ce{2Cr^{3+} + 7H2O + 3SO4^{2-} + 12H+ + 6e-}$$

Cancel 6e⁻ from both sides. Cancel H⁺: 14 on left, 12 on right → 2 H⁺ remain on left. Cancel H₂O: 6 on left, 7 on right → 1 H₂O remains on right:

$$\ce{Cr2O7^{2-} + 2H+ + 3SO2 -> 2Cr^{3+} + H2O + 3SO4^{2-}}$$

Check: Left charge: −2 + 2(+1) = 0. Right charge: 2(+3) + 3(−2) = 6 − 6 = 0. All atoms balanced.

Why this makes sense: Three SO₂ molecules each lose 2 electrons (total 6), which matches the 6 electrons gained by one Cr₂O₇²⁻.

5. Hydrogen Peroxide — A Special Case

Hydrogen peroxide, H₂O₂, is special because the oxygen in it has oxidation number −1. This is an intermediate oxidation number — it can go up to 0 (being oxidised to O₂) or down to −2 (being reduced to H₂O). Because of this, H₂O₂ can act as either a reducing agent or an oxidising agent, depending on what it reacts with.

When H₂O₂ acts as a reducing agent (it is oxidised to O₂):

$$\ce{H2O2 -> O2 + 2H+ + 2e-}$$

Let us verify this half-equation: Step 1: skeleton H₂O₂ → O₂. Step 2: 2 O on each side. Step 3: O balanced. Step 4: Left has 2 H, right has 0 H — add 2 H⁺ to right. Step 5: Left charge 0, right charge +2 — add 2 e⁻ to right.

When H₂O₂ acts as an oxidising agent (it is reduced to H₂O):

$$\ce{H2O2 + 2H+ + 2e- -> 2H2O}$$

Verify: Step 1: H₂O₂ → H₂O. Step 2: nothing else. Step 3: Left 2 O, right 1 O — multiply H₂O by 2. Step 4: Left 2 H, right 4 H — add 2 H⁺ to left. Step 5: Left charge +2, right charge 0 — add 2 e⁻ to left.

Worked Example 6: Hydrogen peroxide is added to an acidified solution of iron(II) ions, and the Fe²⁺ is oxidised to Fe³⁺. Determine which role H₂O₂ plays and write the balanced overall equation.

To solve this, we need to: decide whether H₂O₂ is oxidised or reduced. Since Fe²⁺ is oxidised (it loses electrons to become Fe³⁺), those electrons must be gained by something else. H₂O₂ must gain those electrons — therefore H₂O₂ is reduced. This means H₂O₂ acts as the oxidising agent.

Reduction half (H₂O₂ → H₂O):

$$\ce{H2O2 + 2H+ + 2e- -> 2H2O}$$

Oxidation half (Fe²⁺ → Fe³⁺):

$$\ce{Fe^{2+} -> Fe^{3+} + e-}$$

Multiply oxidation half by 2 and add:

$$\ce{H2O2 + 2H+ + 2Fe^{2+} -> 2H2O + 2Fe^{3+}}$$

Why this makes sense: The peroxide oxygen goes from −1 to −2 (reduction), and iron goes from +2 to +3 (oxidation). Two Fe²⁺ each lose one electron, providing the two electrons needed to reduce one H₂O₂.

Practice Problems

Problem 1: Balance each of the following half-equations in acidic solution using the five-step method. Show each step clearly.

(a) $$\ce{NO3- -> NO}$$

(b) $$\ce{SO4^{2-} -> SO2}$$

(c) $$\ce{Cl2 -> ClO-}$$

Problem 2: Zinc metal reacts with nitrate ions in acidic solution to produce zinc ions and ammonium ions. The skeleton ionic equation is:

$$\ce{Zn(s) + NO3-(aq) -> Zn^{2+}(aq) + NH4+(aq)}$$

Balance this redox equation by writing and combining the two half-equations. (The nitrate ion, NO₃⁻, is reduced to NH₄⁺. This requires balancing N, then O with H₂O, then H with H⁺, then charge with e⁻.)

Problem 3: Balance the following redox equation, which occurs in acidic solution:

$$\ce{MnO4-(aq) + H2O2(aq) -> Mn^{2+}(aq) + O2(g)}$$

Write both half-equations, balance them, combine them, and then state whether hydrogen peroxide is acting as the oxidising agent or the reducing agent in this reaction. Explain how you can tell from the oxidation number changes.

Problem 4: Copper metal reacts with dilute nitric acid, HNO₃, producing copper(II) nitrate, nitrogen monoxide gas (NO), and water. The balanced molecular equation is not yet known, but the ionic skeleton is:

$$\ce{Cu(s) + NO3-(aq) + H+(aq) -> Cu^{2+}(aq) + NO(g) + H2O(l)}$$

Balance this ionic equation using the half-equation method. The nitrate ion is reduced to NO.

Problem 5 (IB-style): An unknown metal M reacts with acidified potassium dichromate(VI) solution. The metal M forms the ion M²⁺, and the dichromate(VI) ion, Cr₂O₇²⁻, is reduced to Cr³⁺. The unbalanced ionic equation is:

$$\ce{M(s) + Cr2O7^{2-}(aq) + H+(aq) -> M^{2+}(aq) + Cr^{3+}(aq) + H2O(l)}$$

(a) Write the balanced oxidation half-equation for M and the balanced reduction half-equation for Cr₂O₇²⁻. Combine them to obtain the balanced overall equation.

(b) In an experiment, a 0.50 g sample of metal M is added to excess acidified potassium dichromate(VI). The reaction requires exactly 25.0 cm³ of 0.100 mol dm⁻³ K₂Cr₂O₇ solution for complete reaction. Calculate the amount, in moles, of Cr₂O₇²⁻ used.

(c) Using your balanced equation from part (a), determine the amount, in moles, of metal M that reacted.

(d) Calculate the molar mass of metal M in g mol⁻¹. Suggest the identity of M by comparing your calculated molar mass to values on the periodic table. (If your calculated value is close to that of a known metal, state which metal it is likely to be.)

Answers

Answer 1.

(a) NO₃⁻ → NO.

Step 1: $$\ce{NO3- -> NO}$$

Step 2: N already balanced (1 each side).

Step 3: Left 3 O, right 1 O. Add 2 H₂O to right: $$\ce{NO3- -> NO + 2H2O}$$

Step 4: Left 0 H, right 4 H. Add 4 H⁺ to left: $$\ce{NO3- + 4H+ -> NO + 2H2O}$$

Step 5: Left charge: −1 + 4(+1) = +3. Right charge: 0. Add 3 e⁻ to left: $$\ce{NO3- + 4H+ + 3e- -> NO + 2H2O}$$

(b) SO₄²⁻ → SO₂.

Step 1: $$\ce{SO4^{2-} -> SO2}$$

Step 2: S balanced.

Step 3: Left 4 O, right 2 O. Add 2 H₂O to right: $$\ce{SO4^{2-} -> SO2 + 2H2O}$$

Step 4: Left 0 H, right 4 H. Add 4 H⁺ to left: $$\ce{SO4^{2-} + 4H+ -> SO2 + 2H2O}$$

Step 5: Left: −2 + 4(+1) = +2. Right: 0. Add 2 e⁻ to left: $$\ce{SO4^{2-} + 4H+ + 2e- -> SO2 + 2H2O}$$

(c) Cl₂ → ClO⁻.

Step 1: $$\ce{Cl2 -> ClO-}$$

Step 2: Left 2 Cl, right 1 Cl. Multiply ClO⁻ by 2: $$\ce{Cl2 -> 2ClO-}$$

Step 3: Left 0 O, right 2 O. Add 2 H₂O to left: $$\ce{Cl2 + 2H2O -> 2ClO-}$$

Step 4: Left 4 H, right 0 H. Add 4 H⁺ to right: $$\ce{Cl2 + 2H2O -> 2ClO- + 4H+}$$

Step 5: Left charge: 0. Right: 2(−1) + 4(+1) = +2. Add 2 e⁻ to right: $$\ce{Cl2 + 2H2O -> 2ClO- + 4H+ + 2e-}$$

Answer 2.

First, balance the reduction half-equation: NO₃⁻ → NH₄⁺.

Step 1: $$\ce{NO3- -> NH4+}$$

Step 2: N balanced (1 each).

Step 3: Left 3 O, right 0 O. Add 3 H₂O to right: $$\ce{NO3- -> NH4+ + 3H2O}$$

Step 4: Left 0 H, right 4 + 6 = 10 H. Add 10 H⁺ to left: $$\ce{NO3- + 10H+ -> NH4+ + 3H2O}$$

Step 5: Left charge: −1 + 10(+1) = +9. Right: +1. Add 8 e⁻ to left: $$\ce{NO3- + 10H+ + 8e- -> NH4+ + 3H2O}$$

Oxidation half: $$\ce{Zn -> Zn^{2+} + 2e-}$$

Multiply oxidation half by 4 to get 8 e⁻: $$\ce{4Zn -> 4Zn^{2+} + 8e-}$$

Add: $$\ce{4Zn + NO3- + 10H+ -> 4Zn^{2+} + NH4+ + 3H2O}$$

Answer 3.

Reduction half (MnO₄⁻ → Mn²⁺):

$$\ce{MnO4- + 8H+ + 5e- -> Mn^{2+} + 4H2O}$$

Oxidation half (H₂O₂ → O₂):

$$\ce{H2O2 -> O2 + 2H+ + 2e-}$$

Electrons: 5 vs 2. Least common multiple = 10. Multiply reduction by 2 (10 e⁻), oxidation by 5 (10 e⁻):

Reduction ×2: $$\ce{2MnO4- + 16H+ + 10e- -> 2Mn^{2+} + 8H2O}$$

Oxidation ×5: $$\ce{5H2O2 -> 5O2 + 10H+ + 10e-}$$

Add and cancel 10 e⁻:

$$\ce{2MnO4- + 16H+ + 5H2O2 -> 2Mn^{2+} + 8H2O + 5O2 + 10H+}$$

Cancel H⁺: 16 on left, 10 on right → 6 H⁺ left on left:

$$\ce{2MnO4- + 6H+ + 5H2O2 -> 2Mn^{2+} + 8H2O + 5O2}$$

Hydrogen peroxide is acting as the reducing agent. We can tell because the oxygen in H₂O₂ has oxidation number −1 and becomes 0 in O₂ — an increase in oxidation number means H₂O₂ is oxidised, and the species that is oxidised is the reducing agent.

Answer 4.

Reduction half (NO₃⁻ → NO):

Already balanced in Answer 1(a): $$\ce{NO3- + 4H+ + 3e- -> NO + 2H2O}$$

Oxidation half (Cu → Cu²⁺):

$$\ce{Cu -> Cu^{2+} + 2e-}$$

Electrons: 3 vs 2. Multiply reduction by 2 (6 e⁻), oxidation by 3 (6 e⁻):

Reduction ×2: $$\ce{2NO3- + 8H+ + 6e- -> 2NO + 4H2O}$$

Oxidation ×3: $$\ce{3Cu -> 3Cu^{2+} + 6e-}$$

Add and cancel 6 e⁻:

$$\ce{3Cu + 2NO3- + 8H+ -> 3Cu^{2+} + 2NO + 4H2O}$$

Answer 5.

(a) Oxidation half (M → M²⁺):

$$\ce{M -> M^{2+} + 2e-}$$

Reduction half (Cr₂O₇²⁻ → Cr³⁺):

$$\ce{Cr2O7^{2-} + 14H+ + 6e- -> 2Cr^{3+} + 7H2O}$$

Electrons: 2 vs 6. Multiply oxidation by 3: $$\ce{3M -> 3M^{2+} + 6e-}$$

Add and cancel 6 e⁻:

$$\ce{3M(s) + Cr2O7^{2-}(aq) + 14H+(aq) -> 3M^{2+}(aq) + 2Cr^{3+}(aq) + 7H2O(l)}$$

(b) Amount of Cr₂O₇²⁻ = concentration × volume. But volume must be in dm³: 25.0 cm³ = 0.0250 dm³.

n(Cr₂O₇²⁻) = 0.100 mol dm⁻³ × 0.0250 dm³ = 0.00250 mol.

(c) From the balanced equation, 1 mol Cr₂O₇²⁻ reacts with 3 mol M.

n(M) = 3 × 0.00250 = 0.00750 mol.

(d) Molar mass of M = mass / amount = 0.50 g / 0.00750 mol = 66.7 g mol⁻¹.

This is close to the molar mass of zinc, which is 65.38 g mol⁻¹. The metal M is likely to be zinc, Zn. The small discrepancy is expected due to experimental uncertainty.
