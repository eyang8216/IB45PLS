# Lesson 37: Alkanes and Alkenes — Structure, Reactions, and Mechanisms

## What You'll Learn

In this lesson, you will learn about two of the most fundamental families of organic compounds: the alkanes and the alkenes. You will learn about their structures — how the atoms are arranged in three-dimensional space. You will learn about their physical properties and why boiling points change with molecular size. You will learn the chemical reactions that alkanes and alkenes undergo, including combustion, free-radical substitution, and electrophilic addition. You will learn Markovnikov's rule for predicting which product forms when HX adds to an unsymmetrical alkene.

## 1. Alkanes — The Simplest Hydrocarbons

**Alkanes** are hydrocarbons (compounds containing only carbon and hydrogen) in which every carbon-carbon bond is a **single bond**. Because they contain only single bonds, alkanes are described as **saturated** — each carbon atom is bonded to the maximum possible number of hydrogen atoms.

**General formula:** For a straight-chain or branched alkane with no rings, the formula is **CₙH₂ₙ₊₂**. For example, if n = 1 (one carbon), the formula is CH₄ (methane). If n = 2 (two carbons), C₂H₆ (ethane). If n = 3, C₃H₈ (propane).

### Structure and Bonding in Alkanes

Each carbon atom in an alkane forms four single covalent bonds. To understand how these bonds are arranged in space, we use the concept of **hybridisation**. Hybridisation is a model that explains the shapes of molecules by combining (mixing) the atomic orbitals of the central atom.

In alkanes, each carbon is **sp³ hybridised**. This means the one 2s orbital and the three 2p orbitals of carbon combine to form four identical sp³ hybrid orbitals. These four orbitals point toward the corners of a **tetrahedron** — a triangular pyramid with the carbon atom at the centre. The angle between any two bonds is approximately **109.5°**.

Every bond in an alkane is a **sigma (σ) bond**. A sigma bond is formed when two atomic orbitals overlap head-on, with the electron density concentrated along the line between the two nuclei. Sigma bonds are strong and allow free rotation — the atoms connected by a sigma bond can spin around the bond axis without breaking the bond.

### Physical Properties of Alkanes

Alkanes are **non-polar** molecules. The electronegativity difference between carbon (2.5 on the Pauling scale) and hydrogen (2.1) is very small, so C−C and C−H bonds are essentially non-polar. As a result, the only forces holding alkane molecules together in the liquid and solid states are **London dispersion forces** (also called instantaneous dipole-induced dipole forces).

London dispersion forces arise from temporary fluctuations in electron distribution. As molecules get larger (more electrons, longer chains), these forces become stronger, and the boiling point increases. This is why methane (CH₄, boiling point −162 °C) is a gas at room temperature, while octane (C₈H₁₈, boiling point +125 °C) is a liquid, and eicosane (C₂₀H₄₂, boiling point +343 °C) is a waxy solid.

**Branching lowers the boiling point.** A branched alkane has a more compact, more spherical shape than its straight-chain isomer (same molecular formula, different structure). The compact shape means less surface area is in contact with neighbouring molecules, so London forces are weaker. For example, pentane (straight chain, C₅H₁₂) boils at 36 °C, while 2,2-dimethylpropane (highly branched, same formula C₅H₁₂) boils at only 9.5 °C.

Alkanes are **insoluble in water** because water molecules are held together by strong hydrogen bonds, and alkanes cannot form hydrogen bonds. Alkanes are soluble in non-polar solvents such as hexane.

## 2. Combustion of Alkanes

**Combustion** is a chemical reaction in which a substance reacts rapidly with oxygen, releasing energy as heat and light. Alkanes are excellent fuels because their combustion is highly exothermic (releases a lot of energy).

**Complete combustion** occurs when there is plenty of oxygen available. The products are carbon dioxide and water:

$$\ce{CH4(g) + 2O2(g) -> CO2(g) + 2H2O(l)}$$

This is the equation for burning methane (natural gas). The general equation for complete combustion of any alkane is:

$$\ce{C_nH_{2n+2} + \frac{3n+1}{2} O2 -> n CO2 + (n+1) H2O}$$

In words: each carbon atom becomes a CO₂ molecule, and each pair of hydrogen atoms becomes an H₂O molecule.

**Incomplete combustion** occurs when the oxygen supply is limited. Instead of CO₂, the products may include **carbon monoxide (CO)** — a toxic gas — or even **solid carbon (soot)**:

$$\ce{2CH4(g) + 3O2(g) -> 2CO(g) + 4H2O(l)}$$

$$\ce{CH4(g) + O2(g) -> C(s) + 2H2O(l)}$$

Carbon monoxide is dangerous because it binds to haemoglobin in blood more than 200 times more strongly than oxygen does, preventing the blood from carrying oxygen around the body. Incomplete combustion in a poorly ventilated room can be fatal.

## 3. Free-Radical Substitution — The Reaction of Alkanes with Halogens

Alkanes are generally unreactive. They do not react with acids, bases, oxidising agents, or reducing agents under normal conditions. However, they do react with **halogens** (chlorine, Cl₂, and bromine, Br₂) in the presence of **ultraviolet (UV) light**. This reaction is called **free-radical substitution**.

**What is a free radical?** A free radical (often just called a radical) is an atom or molecule that has an **unpaired electron**. Radicals are extremely reactive because they want to pair up their unpaired electron. In chemical equations, a radical is shown with a dot (•) next to it to indicate the unpaired electron.

The reaction has three distinct stages:

### Stage 1: Initiation

UV light provides the energy to break a chlorine molecule apart. The bond breaks **homolytically** — each chlorine atom takes one of the two electrons from the bond:

$$\ce{Cl2 ->[h\nu] 2Cl\bullet}$$

The symbol "hν" means light energy (h is Planck's constant, ν is the frequency of the light). Each chlorine atom produced is a **chlorine radical**, Cl•, with an unpaired electron.

### Stage 2: Propagation

Propagation steps are the "chain" part of the reaction. Each step produces a new radical, which can go on to react again. This is called a **chain reaction**.

Step 2a: A chlorine radical collides with a methane molecule, takes a hydrogen atom, and forms HCl. The methyl group that remains is a **methyl radical**, •CH₃:
$$\ce{Cl\bullet + CH4 -> HCl + \bullet CH3}$$

Step 2b: The methyl radical collides with a Cl₂ molecule, takes a chlorine atom, and forms chloromethane. This also regenerates a chlorine radical:
$$\ce{\bullet CH3 + Cl2 -> CH3Cl + Cl\bullet}$$

The chlorine radical produced in step 2b can now go back to step 2a and react with another methane molecule. The chain continues, potentially through thousands of cycles.

### Stage 3: Termination

Eventually, two radicals collide and combine, ending their radical character. This stops the chain:

$$\ce{Cl\bullet + Cl\bullet -> Cl2}$$
$$\ce{Cl\bullet + \bullet CH3 -> CH3Cl}$$
$$\ce{\bullet CH3 + \bullet CH3 -> C2H6}$$

Any of these termination steps stops the chain reaction. The products include not just CH₃Cl (the monosubstituted product) but also ethane (C₂H₆) from methyl radical combination, and potentially products with more than one chlorine if the chain continues on the same molecule.

**Important limitation:** Free-radical substitution produces a **mixture of products** because the propagation steps can happen multiple times on the same molecule. This means the reaction is not useful for making a single pure product in high yield.

## 4. Alkenes — The Unsaturated Hydrocarbons

**Alkenes** are hydrocarbons that contain at least one **carbon-carbon double bond (C=C)**. Because they have a double bond, alkenes are described as **unsaturated** — they do not have the maximum possible number of hydrogen atoms for their carbon count.

**General formula:** For an alkene with one double bond and no rings: **CₙH₂ₙ**. Compare this with the alkane formula CₙH₂ₙ₊₂: the alkene has two fewer hydrogen atoms because two carbons share four electrons (a double bond) instead of two.

### Structure and Bonding in Alkenes

The two carbon atoms involved in the double bond are **sp² hybridised**. In sp² hybridisation, one 2s orbital and two 2p orbitals combine to form three identical sp² hybrid orbitals, which lie in a plane and are 120° apart. The remaining 2p orbital (unhybridised) is perpendicular to this plane.

The double bond consists of two different kinds of bonds:
- A **sigma (σ) bond** formed by head-on overlap of an sp² orbital from each carbon.
- A **pi (π) bond** formed by sideways overlap of the unhybridised p orbitals from each carbon.

The pi bond is weaker than the sigma bond and the pi electrons are located above and below the plane of the molecule — they are more exposed. Because of the pi bond, there is **restricted rotation** about the C=C double bond. You cannot twist one end of the double bond relative to the other without breaking the pi bond. This restricted rotation is the reason that geometric (cis-trans/E-Z) isomerism exists in alkenes.

The bond angles around the double-bonded carbons are approximately **120°**, giving a trigonal planar geometry at each of these carbons.

## 5. Electrophilic Addition — The Main Reaction of Alkenes

The pi electrons in the C=C double bond form a region of high electron density above and below the plane. This makes alkenes attractive to **electrophiles**. An **electrophile** is a chemical species that is electron-deficient and can accept a pair of electrons to form a new covalent bond. "Electrophile" literally means "electron-loving."

When an electrophile attacks an alkene, the pi bond breaks and the two electrons from the pi bond form new sigma bonds to the electrophile and to another atom. The overall result is that the double bond becomes a single bond, and two new atoms or groups are added across the former double bond. This type of reaction is called **electrophilic addition**.

Here are the key addition reactions of alkenes:

**Addition of hydrogen (hydrogenation):** Alkene + H₂ → alkane. Requires a metal catalyst such as nickel, palladium, or platinum, and heating.
$$\ce{C2H4(g) + H2(g) ->[Ni, 150°C] C2H6(g)}$$

**Addition of halogens (halogenation):** Alkene + Br₂ or Cl₂ → dihalogenoalkane. Occurs readily at room temperature. The classic test for unsaturation: bromine water (orange) is decolourised by alkenes.
$$\ce{C2H4(g) + Br2(aq) -> CH2BrCH2Br(l)}$$
(The colour change from orange to colourless is a positive test for an alkene.)

**Addition of hydrogen halides:** Alkene + HX (where X = Cl, Br, I) → halogenoalkane. Occurs at room temperature.
$$\ce{C2H4(g) + HBr(g) -> CH3CH2Br(l)}$$

**Addition of water (hydration):** Alkene + H₂O → alcohol. Requires an acid catalyst (phosphoric acid, H₃PO₄), high temperature (about 300 °C), and high pressure (about 60 atmospheres).
$$\ce{C2H4(g) + H2O(g) ->[H3PO4, 300°C, 60 atm] CH3CH2OH(l)}$$

## 6. Markovnikov's Rule — Predicting the Major Product

When HX adds to a **symmetrical** alkene (where both carbons of the double bond have the same groups attached), only one product is possible. For example, ethene (CH₂=CH₂) gives bromoethane (CH₃CH₂Br) regardless of which carbon gets the H and which gets the Br — both give the same product.

But when the alkene is **unsymmetrical** — the two carbons of the double bond have different groups attached — there are two possible products. Which one is favoured?

**Markovnikov's rule** states: when a hydrogen halide (HX) adds to an unsymmetrical alkene, the hydrogen atom goes to the carbon that already has more hydrogen atoms, and the halogen goes to the carbon that has fewer hydrogen atoms. A phrase to help you remember: "The rich get richer" — the carbon with more H atoms gets the H.

The product that follows Markovnikov's rule is called the **Markovnikov product** and is the **major** product. The other product (anti-Markovnikov) is the **minor** product.

**Why does this happen?** The mechanism goes through a **carbocation intermediate** (a carbon with a positive charge and only 6 electrons around it). Carbocations are more stable when they are more substituted — that is, when the positively charged carbon is attached to more alkyl groups. Alkyl groups donate electron density, helping to stabilise the positive charge. So the reaction path that goes through the more stable (more substituted) carbocation is favoured.

<div class="worked">
<strong>Worked Example: Predict the major product when HBr adds to propene (CH₃CH=CH₂).</strong>

Propene is unsymmetrical. Let us look at the two carbons of the double bond:
- C-1 (the CH₂ end): this carbon has 2 hydrogen atoms.
- C-2 (the CH end): this carbon has 1 hydrogen atom.

According to Markovnikov's rule, the H from HBr goes to the carbon with more hydrogen atoms. That is C-1. The Br therefore goes to C-2.

The major product is: CH₃−CHBr−CH₃, which is **2-bromopropane**.

The minor product (anti-Markovnikov) would be: CH₃−CH₂−CH₂Br, which is **1-bromopropane**.

**Why this makes sense:** If H⁺ adds to C-1, the resulting carbocation is on C-2: CH₃−C⁺H−CH₃. This is a **secondary carbocation** (the positive carbon is attached to two other carbons), which is relatively stable. If H⁺ added to C-2 instead, the carbocation would be on C-1: CH₃−CH₂−C⁺H₂. This is a **primary carbocation**, which is less stable. The more stable carbocation forms faster, so the major product comes from that pathway.
</div>

## 7. Addition Polymerisation

Alkenes are the building blocks (called **monomers**) for a huge class of materials called **addition polymers**. In addition polymerisation, many alkene molecules join together by opening their double bonds and forming single bonds to each other. A polymer is a very long chain molecule made from repeating monomer units.

**Poly(ethene)** (also called polythene or polyethylene):
$$n\ce{CH2=CH2 -> [-CH2-CH2-]_n}$$

**Poly(propene)** (polypropylene):
$$n\ce{CH3CH=CH2 -> [-CH(CH3)-CH2-]_n}$$

**Poly(chloroethene)** (PVC):
$$n\ce{CH2=CHCl -> [-CHCl-CH2-]_n}$$

**Poly(tetrafluoroethene)** (PTFE, also known as Teflon):
$$n\ce{CF2=CF2 -> [-CF2-CF2-]_n}$$

The letter "n" represents a very large number — typically thousands or tens of thousands of monomer units.

## Answers

**Answer 1.**

(a) Complete combustion of propane:
$$\ce{C3H8(g) + 5O2(g) -> 3CO2(g) + 4H2O(l)}$$
How to balance: 3 carbons need 3 CO₂. 8 hydrogens need 4 H₂O. That uses 3×2 + 4×1 = 10 oxygen atoms on the right, so 5 O₂ on the left.

(b) Incomplete combustion of ethane producing CO:
$$\ce{2C2H6(g) + 5O2(g) -> 4CO(g) + 6H2O(l)}$$
How to balance: 2 ethane molecules have 4 carbons → 4 CO. 12 hydrogens → 6 H₂O. Right side has 4 + 6 = 10 oxygen atoms → 5 O₂ on left.

**Answer 2.**

(a) Initiation: UV light breaks the Br–Br bond homolytically.
$$\ce{Br2 ->[h\nu] 2Br\bullet}$$
Each bromine atom takes one electron from the bond, forming two bromine radicals.

(b) Propagation step 1: A bromine radical abstracts a hydrogen atom from ethane.
$$\ce{Br\bullet + C2H6 -> HBr + \bullet C2H5}$$
The product is hydrogen bromide and an ethyl radical (•C₂H₅).

Propagation step 2: The ethyl radical reacts with a bromine molecule.
$$\ce{\bullet C2H5 + Br2 -> C2H5Br + Br\bullet}$$
This forms bromoethane and regenerates a bromine radical, which can continue the chain.

(c) Termination (any two radicals combine):
$$\ce{Br\bullet + Br\bullet -> Br2}$$
$$\ce{Br\bullet + \bullet C2H5 -> C2H5Br}$$
$$\ce{\bullet C2H5 + \bullet C2H5 -> C4H10}$$
The last one produces butane (C₄H₁₀) — a dimerisation product.

**Answer 3.**

(a) But-1-ene is CH₃CH₂CH=CH₂. The double bond is between C-1 (two H's) and C-2 (one H). Markovnikov's rule: H goes to C-1 (more H's), Br goes to C-2. Major product: **2-bromobutane**, CH₃CH₂CHBrCH₃. Minor product: 1-bromobutane, CH₃CH₂CH₂CH₂Br.

(b) Ethene is symmetrical (CH₂=CH₂). Adding H–OH (water) across the double bond gives ethanol regardless of which carbon gets H and which gets OH. Product: **ethanol**, CH₃CH₂OH.

(c) 2-methylpropene is (CH₃)₂C=CH₂. This is addition of Br₂ (not HBr, so Markovnikov's rule does not apply for the regiochemistry because both ends get Br). The Br₂ adds across the double bond. Product: **1,2-dibromo-2-methylpropane**, (CH₃)₂CBr−CH₂Br.

**Answer 4.**

Geometric (cis-trans) isomerism requires two conditions, both of which must be met:
1. There must be **restricted rotation**, which is provided by the C=C double bond (the pi bond prevents free rotation).
2. Each carbon of the double bond must have **two different groups** attached.

In but-2-ene (CH₃CH=CHCH₃), each carbon of the double bond is attached to an H atom and a CH₃ group — two different groups. Therefore, cis and trans isomers are possible: in cis-but-2-ene the two CH₃ groups are on the same side; in trans-but-2-ene they are on opposite sides.

In but-1-ene (CH₃CH₂CH=CH₂), the first carbon of the double bond (C-1) has two identical H atoms attached. Because the two groups on C-1 are the same (both H), condition 2 is not met. There are no cis and trans forms of but-1-ene — switching the H atoms on C-1 does not create a different spatial arrangement.

**Answer 5.**

(a) X decolourises bromine water. This is the classic test for unsaturation — the presence of a C=C double bond. X is an **alkene**. The orange colour of bromine water is due to dissolved Br₂. When Br₂ adds across the double bond, the Br₂ is consumed and the colour disappears. Alkanes do not react with bromine water in the dark, so the colour change confirms X is an alkene.

(b) X has formula C₅H₁₀, which fits the general alkene formula CₙH₂ₙ (5 × 2 = 10). It must be unsymmetrical to give two different products with HBr. One possible structure is **pent-1-ene**: CH₃CH₂CH₂CH=CH₂. (Another possibility: 3-methylbut-1-ene or 2-methylbut-1-ene.) Name: pent-1-ene.

(c) HBr addition to pent-1-ene:
Double bond is between C-1 (CH₂, two H's) and C-2 (CH, one H).
- H goes to C-1 (more H's), Br goes to C-2. Major product Y: **2-bromopentane**, CH₃CH₂CH₂CHBrCH₃.
- Minor product Z: **1-bromopentane**, CH₃CH₂CH₂CH₂CH₂Br.

Y is the major product because the mechanism goes through a carbocation intermediate. When H⁺ adds to C-1, the carbocation forms at C-2. This carbocation (CH₃CH₂CH₂−C⁺H−CH₃) is **secondary** — the positive carbon is attached to two other carbons. When H⁺ adds to C-2, the carbocation forms at C-1 (CH₃CH₂CH₂CH₂−C⁺H₂), which is **primary** — the positive carbon is attached to only one other carbon. Secondary carbocations are more stable than primary carbocations (alkyl groups donate electron density, stabilising the positive charge), so the pathway through the secondary carbocation is faster, giving more of the Markovnikov product Y.

(d) Complete combustion of C₅H₁₀:
$$\ce{2C5H10(g) + 15O2(g) -> 10CO2(g) + 10H2O(l)}$$
Or equivalently: $$\ce{C5H10(g) + 7.5O2(g) -> 5CO2(g) + 5H2O(l)}$$

Using whole numbers: 2 molecules of C₅H₁₀ need 15 molecules of O₂ to produce 10 molecules each of CO₂ and H₂O.
