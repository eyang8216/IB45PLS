# Lesson 12: Metallic Bonding, Giant Covalent Structures, Sigma and Pi Bonds, and Hybridisation — IB Chemistry HL

## What You'll Learn

This lesson covers four interconnected topics that all deal with how atoms are held together in different types of substances, with a focus on the HL material for orbital overlap and hybridisation. First, you will learn about metallic bonding: what it is, how the electron sea model explains the properties of metals (malleability, electrical conductivity, thermal conductivity, lustre), and why alloys are harder and less malleable than pure metals. Second, you will study giant covalent structures—diamond, graphite, graphene, and silicon dioxide—and learn how differences in bonding at the atomic level produce dramatically different macroscopic properties. Third, you will learn the distinction between sigma (σ) bonds and pi (π) bonds: how each type of bond is formed through orbital overlap, why sigma bonds are stronger than pi bonds, and why pi bonds prevent rotation. Fourth, you will learn about hybridisation: the mixing of atomic orbitals on a single atom to form new, equivalent hybrid orbitals, and how the type of hybridisation (sp, sp², sp³, sp³d, sp³d²) is directly determined by the number of electron domains around the central atom.

## 1. Metallic Bonding: The Electron Sea Model

A metal consists of a regular, repeating lattice of metal cations (positively charged ions) immersed in a "sea" of delocalized electrons. To understand what this means, let us build the model step by step.

Imagine a block of sodium metal. Each sodium atom has one valence electron in its 3s orbital. When many sodium atoms come together to form a solid, each atom loses its single valence electron. The atom becomes a sodium cation, Na⁺. The electrons that are lost do not stay attached to any particular atom. Instead, they become **delocalized**: they are free to move throughout the entire metal lattice, belonging to the lattice as a whole rather than to any individual atom.

The metal cations (Na⁺ ions, in our sodium example) are arranged in a regular, repeating three-dimensional pattern called a metallic lattice. The delocalized electrons form a negatively charged "fluid" that fills the spaces between the cations. The electrostatic attraction between the positive metal cations and the negative sea of delocalized electrons holds the entire structure together. This attraction is **metallic bonding**.

### Properties of Metals Explained by the Electron Sea Model

**Malleability and ductility.** A metal is malleable (it can be hammered into thin sheets) and ductile (it can be drawn into wires). When a force is applied to a metal, the layers of cations can slide past each other. In an ionic solid, such sliding would bring ions of the same charge next to each other, causing repulsion and shattering. In a metal, however, no matter where the cations slide, they remain surrounded by the delocalized electron sea. The metallic bonding is **non-directional**—it does not depend on a specific geometric arrangement of bonds. The attraction between cations and electrons is maintained even when the lattice is deformed, which allows the metal to change shape without breaking.

**Electrical conductivity.** Metals conduct electricity in the solid state. When a potential difference (voltage) is applied across a metal, the delocalized electrons, which are already mobile, move in a coordinated way toward the positive terminal. This flow of electrons is an electric current. In contrast, ionic compounds can only conduct when molten or dissolved, because the ions themselves must be free to move. In metals, it is electrons, not ions, that carry the current.

**Thermal conductivity.** Metals conduct heat well because the delocalized electrons can transfer kinetic energy rapidly through the lattice. When one part of a metal is heated, the electrons in that region gain kinetic energy and move quickly to cooler parts of the metal, distributing the energy.

**Lustre (shininess).** Metals have a shiny appearance because the delocalized electrons at the surface can absorb and re-emit light across a wide range of wavelengths. When light strikes a metal surface, the electrons oscillate and re-radiate the light, giving metals their characteristic reflective appearance.

### Alloys: Why Mixing Metals Makes Them Harder

An **alloy** is a mixture of a metal with one or more other elements, which may be other metals or non-metals such as carbon. The added atoms have a different size from the atoms of the main metal. When these differently sized atoms occupy positions in the metallic lattice, they disrupt the regular arrangement of the layers.

In a pure metal, the layers of cations are smooth and can slide past each other relatively easily. In an alloy, the differently sized atoms act like bumps or obstacles in the otherwise smooth layers. When a force tries to slide one layer past another, the "bumps" catch on each other and prevent the layers from moving freely. The alloy is therefore harder, stronger, and less malleable than the pure metal.

For example, pure gold (24-carat gold) is very soft and can be scratched or bent easily. Jewelry is usually made from 18-carat gold, which is an alloy of gold with copper and sometimes silver. The copper atoms are smaller than gold atoms and disrupt the gold lattice, making the alloy much harder and more durable. Similarly, steel is an alloy of iron with a small amount of carbon (and often other metals such as chromium and nickel). The carbon atoms occupy the small spaces (interstitial sites) between the iron atoms, locking the lattice and greatly increasing the hardness and strength compared to pure iron.

### Worked Example 12.1: Pure Metals vs. Alloys

**Problem:** A jeweler explains to a customer that a ring made from pure (24-carat) gold would be too soft for everyday wear, and recommends an 18-carat gold ring instead. The 18-carat gold contains approximately 75 percent gold and 25 percent copper by mass. Explain, using the electron sea model of metallic bonding, why the 18-carat gold alloy is harder than pure gold.

**To solve this, we need to compare what happens when a force is applied to pure gold versus the gold–copper alloy.** In pure gold, all the atoms in the metallic lattice are gold atoms. They are all the same size, so the layers of cations form smooth planes. When a force is applied, these smooth layers can slide past each other without much resistance. The delocalized electrons adjust their positions to maintain the metallic bonding throughout the deformation.

In the 18-carat gold alloy, roughly one-quarter of the lattice positions are occupied by copper atoms. Copper atoms have a different atomic radius from gold atoms. The presence of these differently sized atoms disrupts the regularity of the lattice. The layers are no longer smooth; the copper atoms protrude or create indentations relative to the surrounding gold atoms. When a force tries to slide one layer past another, the irregularities catch against each other, resisting the movement. More force is needed to cause deformation, meaning the alloy is harder than pure gold.

**Why this makes sense:** You can think of the difference between a stack of perfectly flat, smooth tiles (which slide easily) and a stack of tiles that have bumps and protrusions (which lock together and resist sliding). The copper atoms are the "bumps" that prevent the gold layers from gliding freely.

## 2. Giant Covalent Structures (Network Covalent Solids)

In a giant covalent structure, also called a network covalent solid, atoms are joined together by covalent bonds that extend continuously throughout the entire solid. There are no individual molecules—the entire crystal is essentially one enormous molecule. The different arrangements of atoms in giant covalent structures produce very different macroscopic properties.

### Diamond: sp³ Carbon, Tetrahedral Network

In diamond, each carbon atom is covalently bonded to four other carbon atoms. The four bonds are arranged in a tetrahedral geometry with bond angles of exactly 109.5°. This tetrahedral arrangement repeats throughout the entire crystal, creating a rigid three-dimensional network.

Every valence electron on every carbon atom is used to form a C–C sigma (single) bond. There are no delocalized electrons anywhere in the diamond structure. All electrons are confined to the covalent bonds between specific pairs of atoms.

The properties of diamond follow directly from this structure. Diamond is the hardest naturally occurring substance because every atom is locked into a rigid three-dimensional framework. To scratch or deform a diamond, you would need to break covalent bonds, which are very strong. Diamond has a very high melting point (approximately 3550 °C) because melting would require breaking covalent bonds throughout the entire lattice. Diamond does not conduct electricity because there are no delocalized electrons or mobile charge carriers—every electron is localized in a covalent bond. Diamond is also an excellent thermal conductor (vibrations travel quickly through the rigid lattice) and is transparent to visible light.

### Graphite: sp² Carbon, Layered Structure with Delocalized Electrons

In graphite, each carbon atom is covalently bonded to only three other carbon atoms. These three bonds are arranged in a trigonal planar geometry with 120° bond angles, forming flat hexagonal sheets. This means each carbon atom uses three of its four valence electrons for sigma bonding within its layer.

The fourth valence electron on each carbon atom occupies a p orbital that is perpendicular to the plane of the layer. These p orbitals on adjacent carbon atoms overlap sideways with each other, forming a delocalized pi (π) electron system that extends across the entire layer. The electrons in this pi system are free to move within the layer, although they cannot easily jump from one layer to another.

The layers of graphite are held together only by weak London dispersion forces. There are no covalent bonds between layers.

The properties of graphite are very different from those of diamond because of this layered structure. Graphite is soft and slippery—the layers can slide over each other easily because only weak intermolecular forces hold them together. This makes graphite an excellent dry lubricant and the reason pencil "lead" (which is actually graphite mixed with clay) leaves a mark on paper: layers of graphite shear off onto the paper. Graphite conducts electricity within each layer because the delocalized pi electrons are mobile charge carriers. Graphite does not conduct electricity perpendicular to the layers. Graphite is opaque and black, unlike transparent diamond.

### Graphene: A Single Layer of Graphite

Graphene is exactly what its name suggests: a single atomic layer of graphite. It is a sheet of carbon atoms arranged in hexagons, exactly one atom thick. Because it is only one layer, all the properties that graphite exhibits within a single layer are expressed to an extreme degree in graphene. Graphene has exceptional tensile strength (it is stronger than steel for its weight), extremely high electrical conductivity (electrons travel through graphene with almost no resistance), and high thermal conductivity. It is also transparent because it is only one atom thick. Graphene has potential applications in electronics, composite materials, and many other fields.

### Silicon Dioxide (SiO₂): A Three-Dimensional Network Like Diamond

Silicon dioxide, also known as silica, is the main component of sand and quartz. In SiO₂, each silicon atom is covalently bonded to four oxygen atoms arranged tetrahedrally (bond angle 109.5°). Each oxygen atom is bonded to two silicon atoms, bridging between them. This creates a continuous three-dimensional network, similar in concept to diamond, but with oxygen atoms acting as bridges between silicon atoms.

The formula SiO₂ is an empirical formula, not a molecular formula. It tells you the ratio of silicon to oxygen (1:2) in the network, but there are no discrete SiO₂ molecules. Like diamond, silicon dioxide has a very high melting point (about 1710 °C), is very hard, does not conduct electricity, and is insoluble in water.

### Worked Example 12.2: Diamond vs. Graphite Electrical Conductivity

**Problem:** Diamond and graphite are both pure forms of the element carbon. Diamond is an electrical insulator while graphite is a conductor of electricity. Explain this difference in terms of the bonding and structure of the two allotropes.

**To solve this, we need to examine where the valence electrons are located in each structure and whether any electrons are free to move.**

In diamond, every carbon atom forms four sigma bonds to four neighboring carbon atoms. All four valence electrons of every carbon atom are used in these covalent bonds. Every electron is confined to the region between two specific carbon nuclei. There are no free or delocalized electrons anywhere in the structure. For an electric current to flow, charged particles must be able to move through the material. Since diamond has no mobile charge carriers, it cannot conduct electricity—it is an electrical insulator.

In graphite, each carbon atom forms only three sigma bonds to three neighboring carbon atoms within its layer. This uses three of the four valence electrons. The fourth valence electron is in a p orbital oriented perpendicular to the layer. The p orbitals on all the carbon atoms in a layer overlap sideways, creating a continuous pi (π) electron system that extends throughout the layer. The electrons in this delocalized pi system are not bound to any particular carbon–carbon bond; they are free to move anywhere within the layer. These delocalized electrons are mobile charge carriers, so graphite can conduct electricity—but only within each layer, not perpendicular to them.

**Why this makes sense:** The critical difference is the number of bonds each carbon atom forms. In diamond, carbon forms four bonds and uses all its electrons, leaving none free. In graphite, carbon forms three bonds and has one electron left over, which becomes delocalized and mobile. The same element, carbon, can be either an insulator or a conductor, purely because of how its atoms are arranged.

## 3. Sigma (σ) and Pi (π) Bonds: Two Ways Orbitals Overlap

All covalent bonds involve the overlap of atomic orbitals, but not all overlaps are the same. There are two fundamentally different ways that orbitals can overlap, producing two types of bonds: sigma bonds and pi bonds.

### Sigma (σ) Bonds: End-On Overlap

A sigma bond is formed when two atomic orbitals overlap head-on (also called end-on overlap). Imagine two s orbitals approaching each other along the line connecting the two nuclei. They overlap directly, and the electron density is concentrated symmetrically around the axis that connects the two nuclei. This means the electron density is highest in the region directly between the two nuclei, which is the most effective place for electrons to attract both nuclei and hold them together.

Sigma bonds can be formed by several combinations of orbitals: s–s overlap (as in the H–H bond), s–p overlap (as in the H–F bond), p–p end-on overlap (as in the F–F bond), or overlap involving hybrid orbitals.

**Properties of sigma bonds:** A sigma bond is cylindrically symmetrical, meaning that if you rotate the atoms around the bond axis, the appearance of the bond does not change. Because of this symmetry, atoms joined by a sigma bond can rotate freely relative to each other. Sigma bonds are the strongest type of covalent bond because the overlap is direct and extensive.

**Key rule:** The first bond between any two atoms is always a sigma bond. Every single bond is a sigma bond. Every double bond contains one sigma bond and one pi bond. Every triple bond contains one sigma bond and two pi bonds.

### Pi (π) Bonds: Sideways Overlap

A pi bond is formed when two parallel p orbitals overlap sideways. Imagine two p orbitals on adjacent atoms, both oriented perpendicular to the bond axis. Their lobes extend above and below (or in front of and behind) the bond axis. When these lobes overlap, the electron density is concentrated in two regions: one above the plane containing the bond axis and one below it. Critically, there is no electron density directly along the bond axis in a pi bond—the electron density is entirely off the axis.

**Properties of pi bonds:** A pi bond is not cylindrically symmetrical. If you try to rotate one end of the molecule relative to the other around a bond that has pi character, the parallel p orbitals will no longer be aligned, and the pi overlap will be broken. Pi bonds therefore **prevent free rotation.** This is why molecules with double bonds (such as ethene, H₂C=CH₂) cannot rotate around the C=C bond. The restricted rotation is the reason that alkenes can exist as cis–trans (E/Z) isomers.

Pi bonds are weaker than sigma bonds because the sideways overlap is less extensive than the head-on overlap of a sigma bond. This is why the bond enthalpy of a C=C double bond (614 kJ mol⁻¹) is less than twice that of a C–C single bond (2 × 348 = 696 kJ mol⁻¹). The sigma component is strong, but the pi component adds less than a full sigma bond's worth of strength.

### Examples of Sigma and Pi Bond Counting

In ethene, C₂H₄: There is a C=C double bond between the two carbon atoms. This consists of one C–C sigma bond and one C–C pi bond. Each carbon is also bonded to two hydrogen atoms by C–H sigma bonds. Total in the molecule: 5 sigma bonds (one C–C + four C–H) and 1 pi bond.

In ethyne (acetylene), C₂H₂: The C≡C triple bond consists of one sigma bond and two pi bonds. Each carbon is also bonded to one hydrogen atom by a C–H sigma bond. Total: 3 sigma bonds (one C–C + two C–H) and 2 pi bonds.

In nitrogen gas, N₂: The N≡N triple bond consists of one sigma bond and two pi bonds. The molecule is diatomic, so there is only this one bond between the two nitrogen atoms.

## 4. Hybridisation: Mixing Atomic Orbitals to Form Bonding Orbitals

In many molecules, the bond angles predicted by considering pure s and p orbitals do not match the experimentally observed angles. For example, if carbon used one s and three p orbitals to bond in methane, we might expect three bonds at 90° (from p orbitals) and one bond with no directional preference (from the s orbital). But methane is known to have four identical bonds at 109.5° angles.

The concept of **hybridisation** resolves this discrepancy. Hybridisation is the mathematical mixing of atomic orbitals on a single atom to produce a set of new, equivalent orbitals called hybrid orbitals. The number of hybrid orbitals formed always equals the number of atomic orbitals that were mixed.

### How to Determine Hybridisation

The hybridisation of an atom is determined by the number of electron domains around that atom. The number of electron domains equals the number of hybrid orbitals.

| Number of electron domains | Hybridisation | Electron domain geometry |
|:---:|---|---|
| 2 | sp | Linear |
| 3 | sp² | Trigonal planar |
| 4 | sp³ | Tetrahedral |
| 5 | sp³d | Trigonal bipyramidal |
| 6 | sp³d² | Octahedral |

### sp Hybridisation: 2 Electron Domains

One s orbital and one p orbital mix to form two equivalent sp hybrid orbitals. These two sp orbitals are oriented 180° apart, giving a linear geometry. The remaining two p orbitals on the atom stay unhybridized and are available for pi bonding.

Example: In beryllium chloride, BeCl₂, the beryllium atom has two electron domains (two single bonds) and is sp hybridised. In ethyne, C₂H₂, each carbon atom has two electron domains (one sigma bond to the other carbon and one sigma bond to a hydrogen) and is sp hybridised. The two unhybridized p orbitals on each carbon overlap sideways to form the two pi bonds of the C≡C triple bond.

### sp² Hybridisation: 3 Electron Domains

One s orbital and two p orbitals mix to form three equivalent sp² hybrid orbitals. These three orbitals lie in a plane and are oriented 120° apart, giving a trigonal planar geometry. The one remaining p orbital is perpendicular to this plane and is available for pi bonding.

Example: In boron trifluoride, BF₃, the boron atom has three electron domains and is sp² hybridised. In ethene, C₂H₄, each carbon atom has three electron domains (two C–H sigma bonds and one C–C sigma bond) and is sp² hybridised. The unhybridized p orbital on each carbon overlaps sideways to form the pi bond of the C=C double bond.

### sp³ Hybridisation: 4 Electron Domains

One s orbital and three p orbitals mix to form four equivalent sp³ hybrid orbitals. These four orbitals point toward the corners of a tetrahedron, with angles of 109.5°.

Example: In methane, CH₄, the carbon has four electron domains and is sp³ hybridised. In ammonia, NH₃, the nitrogen has four electron domains (three bonds + one lone pair) and is sp³ hybridised. The lone pair occupies one of the sp³ hybrid orbitals. In water, H₂O, the oxygen has four electron domains (two bonds + two lone pairs) and is sp³ hybridised.

### sp³d Hybridisation (HL): 5 Electron Domains

One s orbital, three p orbitals, and one d orbital mix to form five sp³d hybrid orbitals. These orbitals are directed toward the corners of a trigonal bipyramid.

Example: In phosphorus pentachloride, PCl₅, the phosphorus has five electron domains and is sp³d hybridised.

### sp³d² Hybridisation (HL): 6 Electron Domains

One s orbital, three p orbitals, and two d orbitals mix to form six sp³d² hybrid orbitals. These orbitals are directed toward the corners of an octahedron.

Example: In sulfur hexafluoride, SF₆, the sulfur has six electron domains and is sp³d² hybridised.

### Important Clarification

The hybridisation is determined by the total number of electron domains, which includes both bonding pairs and lone pairs. A nitrogen atom in NH₃, which has four electron domains (three bonds and one lone pair), is sp³ hybridised—exactly like carbon in CH₄. The lone pair occupies an sp³ hybrid orbital. The difference in molecular shape (trigonal pyramidal vs. tetrahedral) is due to the presence of the lone pair, not a difference in hybridisation.

### Worked Example 12.3: Determining Hybridisation

**Problem:** Determine the hybridisation of the central atom in each of the following species: (a) CO₂, (b) SO₂, (c) XeF₄.

**To solve this, we need to count the total electron domains around the central atom in each species.**

(a) CO₂: The carbon atom is bonded to two oxygen atoms by double bonds. Each double bond counts as one electron domain. There are no lone pairs on carbon. Total electron domains = 2. For 2 electron domains, the hybridisation is sp.

(b) SO₂: The sulfur atom has one double bond to one oxygen, one single bond to the other oxygen (or, in the resonance hybrid, two equivalent bonds of bond order 1.5—but still two bonding domains total), and one lone pair. Total electron domains = 2 (bonding) + 1 (non-bonding) = 3. For 3 electron domains, the hybridisation is sp².

(c) XeF₄: The xenon atom has four single bonds to fluorine atoms and two lone pairs. Total electron domains = 4 (bonding) + 2 (non-bonding) = 6. For 6 electron domains, the hybridisation is sp³d². The molecular shape is square planar.

**Why this makes sense:** The hybridisation is always determined by the total number of electron domains, not by the number of atoms bonded. This is why XeF₄ is sp³d², not sp³—even though it has only four atoms attached, it has six electron domains when you count the lone pairs.

## Answers

1. In sodium metal, the bonding is metallic. Each sodium atom loses its single valence electron to become a Na⁺ cation. The lost electrons become delocalized and form a mobile "sea" of electrons that can move freely throughout the metallic lattice. When a voltage is applied, these delocalized electrons flow toward the positive terminal, constituting an electric current. In solid sodium chloride, the bonding is ionic. The Na⁺ and Cl⁻ ions are held in fixed positions in a giant ionic lattice. Although the ions carry electric charge, they cannot move from their lattice positions in the solid state. Without mobile charge carriers, no electric current can flow. Sodium chloride conducts electricity only when molten or dissolved in water, because in those states the ions become free to move.

2. (a) The three carbon atoms in propene, CH₃–CH=CH₂:
   - The leftmost carbon (CH₃–): This carbon forms four sigma bonds (three to hydrogen, one to the middle carbon). Total electron domains = 4. Hybridisation = sp³.
   - The middle carbon (–CH=): This carbon forms three sigma bonds (one to the left carbon, one to hydrogen, one to the right carbon as part of the double bond) and has no lone pairs. Total electron domains = 3. Hybridisation = sp².
   - The rightmost carbon (=CH₂): This carbon forms three sigma bonds (one to the middle carbon as part of the double bond, two to hydrogen) and has no lone pairs. Total electron domains = 3. Hybridisation = sp².

   (b) Sigma bonds: There are six C–H bonds (all sigma), plus two C–C single bonds? Actually, there is one C–C single bond (between the sp³ carbon and the sp² carbon) and one C=C double bond (between the two sp² carbons). The C=C double bond contains one sigma bond. So the C–C sigma bonds = 1 (from the single) + 1 (from the double) = 2. Total sigma bonds = 6 (C–H) + 2 (C–C) = 8 sigma bonds.
   
   Pi bonds: The C=C double bond contains one pi bond. Total pi bonds = 1.

3. (a) Diamond has a rigid three-dimensional network in which every carbon atom is covalently bonded to four other carbon atoms in a tetrahedral arrangement. There are no layers or planes of weakness—the entire crystal is a single continuous covalent structure. To use diamond as a lubricant, you would need to shear covalent bonds, which requires enormous force. Graphite has a layered structure. Within each layer, carbon atoms are strongly bonded by covalent bonds in hexagonal rings. Between the layers, however, the forces are only weak London dispersion forces. When graphite is used as a lubricant, the layers slide past each other easily because only the weak intermolecular forces need to be overcome. This is also why a pencil leaves a mark: layers of graphite shear off onto the paper.

   (b) Graphite conducts electricity because each carbon atom uses only three of its four valence electrons for sigma bonding within the layer. The fourth valence electron occupies a p orbital perpendicular to the layer. These p orbitals overlap sideways to form a delocalized pi electron system extending across the entire layer. The delocalized electrons are mobile and can carry electric current—but only parallel to the layers. Diamond does not conduct electricity because every carbon atom uses all four valence electrons to form sigma bonds. All electrons are localized in specific covalent bonds between pairs of atoms. There are no mobile electrons, so no current can flow.

4. (a) BeCl₂: The beryllium atom has 2 valence electrons and forms two single bonds to two chlorine atoms. Total electron domains = 2, with zero lone pairs. Hybridisation = sp. The molecular shape is linear.

   (b) SF₄: The sulfur atom has 6 valence electrons and forms four single bonds to four fluorine atoms, using 4 electrons, leaving 2 electrons as one lone pair. Total electron domains = 4 (bonding) + 1 (non-bonding) = 5. Hybridisation = sp³d. The molecular shape is seesaw (disphenoidal).

   (c) BrF₅: The bromine atom has 7 valence electrons and forms five single bonds to five fluorine atoms, using 5 electrons, leaving 2 electrons as one lone pair. Total electron domains = 5 (bonding) + 1 (non-bonding) = 6. Hybridisation = sp³d². The molecular shape is square pyramidal.

5. (a) The Lewis structure of NO₃⁻ has a central nitrogen atom bonded to three oxygen atoms. The total valence electrons are: 5 (from N) + 3 × 6 (from three O) + 1 (from the −1 charge) = 24 electrons. The most stable Lewis structure shows nitrogen with one double bond to one oxygen and single bonds to the other two oxygens. The singly bonded oxygens each carry a −1 formal charge. Three equivalent resonance structures can be drawn by rotating the double bond among the three oxygen atoms.

   (b) The nitrogen atom in NO₃⁻ has three electron domains: the three bonds to the three oxygen atoms (the double bond counts as one domain, just like the single bonds). There are no lone pairs on the nitrogen atom. With three electron domains, the hybridisation of nitrogen is sp². This accounts for the planar geometry of the ion.

   (c) The nitrogen atom uses its three sp² hybrid orbitals to form three sigma bonds—one to each oxygen atom. These three sigma bonds lie in a plane with 120° angles. The nitrogen atom also has one unhybridized p orbital, which is oriented perpendicular to the plane of the molecule. This p orbital overlaps sideways with the p orbitals on all three oxygen atoms. The result is a delocalized pi (π) system that extends over all four atoms (one nitrogen and three oxygens). This is sometimes described as a four-centre pi bond. The four electrons that participate in this delocalized pi system (two from the N=O double bond in any one resonance structure, plus the two negative charges distributed over the oxygens) are shared among all four atoms.

   (d) In the resonance hybrid, the pi electrons are delocalized equally over all three N–O bonds. No single N–O bond has a full double bond character, and no single N–O bond is a pure single bond. Each bond has a bond order of 1⅓ (or 4/3). Because the electron distribution is identical for all three bonds, all three bonds have the same length and the same strength. If the electrons were localized (stuck in one N=O double bond), one bond would be shorter and two would be longer, but experiment shows all three are equal.

   (e) The nitrogen atom in NO₃⁻ has three electron domains (all bonding) and is sp² hybridised, giving a trigonal planar shape. The nitrogen atom in NH₃ has five valence electrons: it forms three single bonds to three hydrogen atoms (using three electrons) and has one lone pair (the remaining two electrons). Total electron domains = 4 (three bonding, one non-bonding). The hybridisation is sp³. The electron domain geometry is tetrahedral. Because one of the four tetrahedral positions is occupied by a lone pair, the molecular shape is trigonal pyramidal. The difference in the number of electron domains (3 in NO₃⁻ versus 4 in NH₃) leads to different hybridisation (sp² versus sp³) and different molecular shapes (planar versus pyramidal). Additionally, NO₃⁻ has delocalized pi bonding across all three N–O bonds, whereas NH₃ has only sigma bonds (the lone pair occupies an sp³ orbital, and all bonding electrons are in sigma bonds).
