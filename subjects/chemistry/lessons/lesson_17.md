## 1. What Is Lattice Enthalpy?

### The Meaning of Lattice Enthalpy

An ionic compound such as sodium chloride, NaCl, exists as a solid crystal at room temperature. In this crystal, sodium ions (Na‚Āļ) and chloride ions (Cl‚ĀĽ) are arranged in a regular repeating pattern called a crystal lattice. The ions are held together by strong electrostatic attractions ‚ÄĒ the positive Na‚Āļ ions attract the negative Cl‚ĀĽ ions, and vice versa. To pull this lattice apart into separate, free-moving gaseous ions would require a great deal of energy.

**Lattice enthalpy** (symbol: őĒH_lat‚¶Ķ) is a measure of how strongly the ions are held together in the crystal. There are two equivalent ways to define it, and you must be careful to use the definition expected in IB Chemistry:

The **IB definition** (which we will use throughout this lesson) is: Lattice enthalpy is the enthalpy change when **one mole of a solid ionic compound is formed from its gaseous ions**.

For sodium chloride, this is written as:

Na‚Āļ(g) + Cl‚ĀĽ(g) ‚Üí NaCl(s)

Because bringing oppositely charged ions together releases energy, lattice enthalpy defined this way is always **exothermic** ‚ÄĒ it always has a **negative** sign. The more negative the lattice enthalpy, the stronger the ionic bonding.

### An Alternative Definition

Some textbooks and exam boards define lattice enthalpy as the energy required to **separate** one mole of a solid ionic compound into its gaseous ions (the reverse of the IB definition). Under this alternative definition, lattice enthalpy is endothermic (positive). The two definitions describe the same process but in opposite directions, so the numerical value is the same, only the sign is different. In this lesson and in IB examinations, we use the formation definition (exothermic, negative).

### Why Lattice Enthalpy Matters

The magnitude of the lattice enthalpy tells you how stable the ionic crystal is. A compound with a very exothermic lattice enthalpy (large negative value) has very strong ionic bonds. This affects many properties: melting point, solubility in water, and hardness. For example, magnesium oxide (MgO) has a lattice enthalpy of approximately ‚ąí3845 kJ mol‚ĀĽ¬Ļ and melts at about 2852¬įC, while sodium chloride (NaCl) has a lattice enthalpy of only ‚ąí788 kJ mol‚ĀĽ¬Ļ and melts at 801¬įC. The much larger lattice enthalpy of MgO explains its much higher melting point.

## 2. The Born-Haber Cycle: An Energy Map for Ionic Compounds

### What Is a Born-Haber Cycle?

A Born-Haber cycle is a special type of enthalpy cycle (an application of Hess's Law) that is used to determine the lattice enthalpy of an ionic compound. It breaks the overall formation of the ionic compound from its elements into a series of individual steps, each with a known or measurable enthalpy change. By adding up all these steps, we can calculate the lattice enthalpy ‚ÄĒ a quantity that cannot be measured directly.

### The Steps of a Born-Haber Cycle

Every Born-Haber cycle for a compound with +1 and ‚ąí1 ions (such as NaCl) follows the same sequence of steps. I will describe each step in the order they appear.

**Step 1: Atomisation of the metal.** The solid metal must be converted into individual gaseous atoms. Atomisation is always endothermic (őĒH positive) because you must supply energy to overcome the metallic bonding that holds the atoms together in the solid.

For sodium: Na(s) ‚Üí Na(g), őĒH_at‚¶Ķ = +108 kJ mol‚ĀĽ¬Ļ.

**Step 2: Ionisation of the gaseous metal atom.** The gaseous metal atom must lose one (or more) electrons to become a positive ion. This is the ionisation energy, and it is always endothermic (positive) because energy is needed to remove an electron from an atom. For elements that form 2+ ions (like Mg¬≤‚Āļ), both the first and second ionisation energies must be included.

For sodium: Na(g) ‚Üí Na‚Āļ(g) + e‚ĀĽ, IE‚āĀ = +496 kJ mol‚ĀĽ¬Ļ.

**Step 3: Atomisation of the non-metal.** If the non-metal is diatomic in its standard state (like Cl‚āā, O‚āā, N‚āā), it must be split into individual gaseous atoms. This is the atomisation enthalpy (sometimes called bond dissociation enthalpy) and is endothermic.

For chlorine: ¬ĹCl‚āā(g) ‚Üí Cl(g), őĒH_at‚¶Ķ = +122 kJ mol‚ĀĽ¬Ļ.

Note the coefficient of ¬Ĺ: we are making exactly 1 mole of Cl atoms because the formula NaCl has one Cl‚ĀĽ per formula unit.

**Step 4: Electron affinity of the non-metal.** The gaseous non-metal atom gains one (or more) electrons to become a negative ion. The first electron affinity is usually exothermic (negative) because energy is released when an electron is attracted to a neutral atom. However, the second electron affinity (adding an electron to an already negative ion, as in O‚ĀĽ + e‚ĀĽ ‚Üí O¬≤‚ĀĽ) is endothermic because you are forcing a negatively charged electron onto an already negatively charged ion, which repels it.

For chlorine: Cl(g) + e‚ĀĽ ‚Üí Cl‚ĀĽ(g), EA = ‚ąí349 kJ mol‚ĀĽ¬Ļ.

**Step 5: Formation of the ionic solid from gaseous ions.** This is the lattice enthalpy ‚ÄĒ the step we usually calculate. It is exothermic.

For NaCl: Na‚Āļ(g) + Cl‚ĀĽ(g) ‚Üí NaCl(s), őĒH_lat‚¶Ķ = ? (this is what we want to find).

**Step 6 (reference): Overall formation from elements.** This is the standard enthalpy of formation of the ionic compound, őĒH_f‚¶Ķ, which is usually measured experimentally.

For NaCl: Na(s) + ¬ĹCl‚āā(g) ‚Üí NaCl(s), őĒH_f‚¶Ķ = ‚ąí411 kJ mol‚ĀĽ¬Ļ.

### The Born-Haber Cycle Diagram

The Born-Haber cycle visually connects all these steps. It starts at the bottom with the elements in their standard states, goes up through atomisation and ionisation to gaseous ions, and then comes back down through lattice formation to the ionic solid.

```
    Na‚Āļ(g) + Cl‚ĀĽ(g)
         ‚ÜĎ
         ‚Ēā + EA(Cl) = ‚ąí349
         ‚Ēā
    Na‚Āļ(g) + Cl(g) + e‚ĀĽ
         ‚ÜĎ
         ‚Ēā + IE‚āĀ(Na) = +496
         ‚Ēā
    Na(g) + Cl(g)
         ‚ÜĎ                  ‚Üė
         ‚Ēā + őĒH_at(Cl)       ‚Ēā őĒH_lat‚¶Ķ (unknown)
         ‚Ēā = +122            ‚Ēā
    Na(g) + ¬ĹCl‚āā(g)          ‚Ēā
         ‚ÜĎ                   ‚Ēā
         ‚Ēā + őĒH_at(Na)       ‚Ēā

    Na(s) + ¬ĹCl‚āā(g) ‚ĒÄ‚ĒÄ‚ĒÄ‚ĒÄ‚Üí NaCl(s)
```

### The Calculation

By Hess's Law, going up the left side and then down the right side must give the same total őĒH as going directly across the bottom:

őĒH_f‚¶Ķ = őĒH_at‚¶Ķ(Na) + IE‚āĀ(Na) + őĒH_at‚¶Ķ(Cl) + EA(Cl) + őĒH_lat‚¶Ķ

Now we substitute the numbers:

‚ąí411 = +108 + 496 + 122 + (‚ąí349) + őĒH_lat‚¶Ķ
‚ąí411 = +377 + őĒH_lat‚¶Ķ
őĒH_lat‚¶Ķ = ‚ąí411 ‚ąí 377 = ‚ąí788 kJ mol‚ĀĽ¬Ļ

The lattice enthalpy of sodium chloride is ‚ąí788 kJ mol‚ĀĽ¬Ļ.

### Worked Example 1: Lattice Enthalpy of Potassium Chloride

**Problem:** Calculate the lattice enthalpy of potassium chloride, KCl(s). Use the following data (all values in kJ mol‚ĀĽ¬Ļ):
- őĒH_f‚¶Ķ[KCl(s)] = ‚ąí437
- őĒH_at‚¶Ķ[K(s)] = +90
- IE‚āĀ[K(g)] = +419
- őĒH_at‚¶Ķ[Cl‚āā(g)] = +244 (this is for Cl‚āā ‚Üí 2Cl, so for ¬ĹCl‚āā ‚Üí Cl it is +122)
- EA[Cl(g)] = ‚ąí349

**Strategy:** Set up the Born-Haber equation. The atomisation enthalpy of chlorine is given for the full Cl‚āā molecule; since we need only ¬ĹCl‚āā (to make one Cl atom), we divide by 2: +244/2 = +122 kJ mol‚ĀĽ¬Ļ.

**Calculation:**
őĒH_f‚¶Ķ = őĒH_at‚¶Ķ(K) + IE‚āĀ(K) + őĒH_at‚¶Ķ(Cl) + EA(Cl) + őĒH_lat‚¶Ķ
‚ąí437 = +90 + 419 + 122 + (‚ąí349) + őĒH_lat‚¶Ķ
‚ąí437 = +282 + őĒH_lat‚¶Ķ
őĒH_lat‚¶Ķ = ‚ąí437 ‚ąí 282 = ‚ąí719 kJ mol‚ĀĽ¬Ļ.

**Why this makes sense:** KCl has a lattice enthalpy of ‚ąí719 kJ mol‚ĀĽ¬Ļ, which is less exothermic than NaCl (‚ąí788 kJ mol‚ĀĽ¬Ļ). This is expected because K‚Āļ is a larger ion than Na‚Āļ (potassium is in period 4, sodium in period 3). A larger cation means the ions cannot get as close together, so the electrostatic attraction is weaker and the lattice enthalpy is less exothermic.

## 3. Born-Haber Cycle for a 2+ / 2‚ąí Compound: Magnesium Oxide

### Why MgO Is Different

Magnesium oxide, MgO, contains Mg¬≤‚Āļ and O¬≤‚ĀĽ ions. Because both ions carry double charges, we must include two ionisation energies for magnesium (to remove two electrons) and two electron affinity steps for oxygen (to add two electrons). The second electron affinity of oxygen is particularly interesting: it is endothermic, not exothermic.

### Data for MgO (all values in kJ mol‚ĀĽ¬Ļ)

- őĒH_f‚¶Ķ[MgO(s)] = ‚ąí602
- őĒH_at‚¶Ķ[Mg(s)] = +148
- IE‚āĀ[Mg(g)] = +738 (first electron removed)
- IE‚āā[Mg‚Āļ(g)] = +1451 (second electron removed)
- őĒH_at‚¶Ķ[O‚āā(g)] = +498 (for O‚āā ‚Üí 2O, so ¬ĹO‚āā ‚Üí O = +249)
- EA‚āĀ[O(g)] = ‚ąí141 (first electron added to neutral oxygen)
- EA‚āā[O‚ĀĽ(g)] = +798 (second electron added to O‚ĀĽ ‚ÄĒ endothermic!)

### Understanding the Second Electron Affinity of Oxygen

The first electron affinity of oxygen is exothermic (‚ąí141 kJ mol‚ĀĽ¬Ļ) because a neutral oxygen atom attracts an extra electron. But adding a second electron to O‚ĀĽ is endothermic (+798 kJ mol‚ĀĽ¬Ļ). Why? The O‚ĀĽ ion already has a negative charge. The second electron being added is also negatively charged. The O‚ĀĽ ion repels the incoming electron. Energy must be supplied to overcome this repulsion. This is a common pattern: the first electron affinity is usually exothermic, but the second (and any beyond) is always endothermic.

### The Calculation

őĒH_f‚¶Ķ = őĒH_at‚¶Ķ(Mg) + IE‚āĀ(Mg) + IE‚āā(Mg) + őĒH_at‚¶Ķ(O) + EA‚āĀ(O) + EA‚āā(O) + őĒH_lat‚¶Ķ

First, sum all the "uphill" steps (the endothermic ones that take us from elements to gaseous ions):

Sum = +148 + 738 + 1451 + 249 + (‚ąí141) + 798
Sum = +148 + 738 + 1451 + 249 ‚ąí 141 + 798
Sum = +3243 kJ mol‚ĀĽ¬Ļ.

Now use the formation enthalpy:

‚ąí602 = +3243 + őĒH_lat‚¶Ķ
őĒH_lat‚¶Ķ = ‚ąí602 ‚ąí 3243 = ‚ąí3845 kJ mol‚ĀĽ¬Ļ.

The lattice enthalpy of MgO is ‚ąí3845 kJ mol‚ĀĽ¬Ļ. This is enormously exothermic ‚ÄĒ nearly five times the magnitude of NaCl's lattice enthalpy.

### Why Is MgO's Lattice Enthalpy So Large?

There are two reinforcing reasons, both coming from Coulomb's Law, which governs the force between charged particles. Coulomb's Law states that the force of attraction between two oppositely charged ions is proportional to the product of their charges (q‚āĀ √ó q‚āā) and inversely proportional to the square of the distance between them (1/r¬≤).

For MgO: q‚āĀ √ó q‚āā = (+2) √ó (‚ąí2) = 4 in magnitude.
For NaCl: q‚āĀ √ó q‚āā = (+1) √ó (‚ąí1) = 1 in magnitude.

So the charge product for MgO is four times larger than for NaCl. Additionally, Mg¬≤‚Āļ is smaller than Na‚Āļ (magnesium is in period 3 but has lost two electrons, making the ion very compact), and O¬≤‚ĀĽ is smaller than Cl‚ĀĽ (oxygen is in period 2, chlorine is in period 3). Smaller ions can approach more closely, which makes the 1/r¬≤ term in Coulomb's Law larger. Both the charge effect and the size effect work in the same direction, making MgO's lattice enthalpy dramatically larger.

## 4. Factors That Affect Lattice Enthalpy

### Factor 1: Ionic Charge

This is the most important factor. The magnitude of lattice enthalpy increases rapidly as the ionic charges increase. Going from +1/‚ąí1 charges (like NaCl) to +2/‚ąí2 charges (like MgO) increases the charge product from 1 to 4. Going to +3/‚ąí2 (like Al‚āāO‚āÉ) increases it even further. This is why compounds with highly charged ions, such as aluminium oxide, have extremely high melting points.

### Factor 2: Ionic Radius

For ions with the same charge, smaller ions produce a more exothermic lattice enthalpy because they can pack closer together. The closer the ions, the stronger the electrostatic attraction. For example:

- NaF has a more exothermic lattice enthalpy than NaCl, which is more exothermic than NaBr, which is more exothermic than NaI. In this series, the fluoride ion (F‚ĀĽ) is the smallest halide ion and the iodide ion (I‚ĀĽ) is the largest.

- MgO has a more exothermic lattice enthalpy than CaO. Mg¬≤‚Āļ (ionic radius ‚Čą 72 pm) is smaller than Ca¬≤‚Āļ (ionic radius ‚Čą 100 pm), so the Mg¬≤‚Āļ/O¬≤‚ĀĽ distance is shorter.

### Comparing Lattice Enthalpies: A Systematic Approach

When asked to compare the lattice enthalpies of two ionic compounds, check two things in order:

1. **Charges:** If the charges are different, the compound with higher charges will have the larger lattice enthalpy magnitude. Charge almost always dominates.

2. **Radii:** If the charges are the same, the compound with the smaller ions will have the larger lattice enthalpy magnitude.

**Example:** Which has the more exothermic lattice enthalpy, NaF or KBr? Both have +1/‚ąí1 charges. But Na‚Āļ is smaller than K‚Āļ, and F‚ĀĽ is smaller than Br‚ĀĽ. Therefore NaF has the more exothermic (more negative) lattice enthalpy.

**Example:** Which has the more exothermic lattice enthalpy, NaCl or MgO? MgO has 2+/2‚ąí charges versus 1+/1‚ąí for NaCl. The charge effect dominates, so MgO has a much more exothermic lattice enthalpy despite Mg¬≤‚Āļ being similar in size to Na‚Āļ.

## 5. Enthalpy of Solution and Enthalpy of Hydration

### What Happens When an Ionic Compound Dissolves?

When you add an ionic solid like NaCl to water, the solid dissolves. Two things must happen:

1. The ionic lattice must be broken apart. This requires energy ‚ÄĒ it is endothermic. The energy needed is the opposite of the lattice enthalpy: ‚ąíőĒH_lat‚¶Ķ (which is positive, since őĒH_lat‚¶Ķ is negative).

2. The separated gaseous ions become surrounded by water molecules. Water is a polar molecule: the oxygen end has a partial negative charge and the hydrogen ends have partial positive charges. The water molecules orient themselves around the ions ‚ÄĒ the oxygen side toward the cation, the hydrogen side toward the anion. This process is called **hydration**, and it releases energy (it is exothermic).

### Definition of Hydration Enthalpy

The **standard enthalpy of hydration** (őĒH_hyd‚¶Ķ) is the enthalpy change when **one mole of gaseous ions** is dissolved in water to form an infinitely dilute solution. Because ion-dipole attractions are formed, hydration enthalpy is always **exothermic** (negative).

Each ion has its own hydration enthalpy. For NaCl:
- őĒH_hyd‚¶Ķ[Na‚Āļ] = ‚ąí406 kJ mol‚ĀĽ¬Ļ
- őĒH_hyd‚¶Ķ[Cl‚ĀĽ] = ‚ąí364 kJ mol‚ĀĽ¬Ļ

### Definition of Enthalpy of Solution

The **standard enthalpy of solution** (őĒH_sol‚¶Ķ) is the enthalpy change when **one mole of a substance** dissolves in water to form an infinitely dilute solution. Unlike lattice enthalpy or hydration enthalpy, őĒH_sol can be either exothermic (negative) or endothermic (positive), depending on the balance between the two competing processes.

### The Energy Cycle for Dissolution

Using Hess's Law, we can relate these quantities:

```
NaCl(s) ‚ĒÄ‚ĒÄ‚ĒÄ‚ĒÄ‚ĒÄ‚ĒÄ‚ĒÄ‚ĒÄ‚Üí Na‚Āļ(aq) + Cl‚ĀĽ(aq)
   ‚Ēā                    ‚ÜĎ
   ‚Ēā                    ‚Ēā
   ‚Ēā ‚ąíőĒH_lat‚¶Ķ           ‚Ēā ő£őĒH_hyd‚¶Ķ
   ‚Ēā (lattice broken)   ‚Ēā (ions hydrated)
   ‚Ēā                    ‚Ēā
   ‚ĒĒ‚ĒÄ‚ĒÄ‚Üí Na‚Āļ(g) + Cl‚ĀĽ(g) ‚ĒÄ‚Ēė
```

The enthalpy of solution is the sum of:
- Breaking the lattice: ‚ąíőĒH_lat‚¶Ķ (endothermic, because őĒH_lat‚¶Ķ is negative, so ‚ąíőĒH_lat‚¶Ķ is positive)
- Hydrating the ions: ő£őĒH_hyd‚¶Ķ (exothermic, negative)

So: **őĒH_sol‚¶Ķ = ‚ąíőĒH_lat‚¶Ķ + ő£őĒH_hyd‚¶Ķ**

Or equivalently: **őĒH_sol‚¶Ķ = ő£őĒH_hyd‚¶Ķ ‚ąí őĒH_lat‚¶Ķ**

### Worked Example 2: Enthalpy of Solution of NaCl

**Problem:** Calculate the enthalpy of solution of sodium chloride using the following data:
- őĒH_lat‚¶Ķ[NaCl(s)] = ‚ąí788 kJ mol‚ĀĽ¬Ļ
- őĒH_hyd‚¶Ķ[Na‚Āļ(g)] = ‚ąí406 kJ mol‚ĀĽ¬Ļ
- őĒH_hyd‚¶Ķ[Cl‚ĀĽ(g)] = ‚ąí364 kJ mol‚ĀĽ¬Ļ

**Strategy:** Apply the formula őĒH_sol = ő£őĒH_hyd ‚ąí őĒH_lat.

**Calculation:**
ő£őĒH_hyd = (‚ąí406) + (‚ąí364) = ‚ąí770 kJ mol‚ĀĽ¬Ļ.
őĒH_sol = ‚ąí770 ‚ąí (‚ąí788) = ‚ąí770 + 788 = +18 kJ mol‚ĀĽ¬Ļ.

The enthalpy of solution of NaCl is +18 kJ mol‚ĀĽ¬Ļ, meaning it is **slightly endothermic**. When NaCl dissolves, the energy required to break the lattice (788 kJ) is almost ‚ÄĒ but not quite ‚ÄĒ compensated by the energy released during hydration (770 kJ). The net result is a small absorption of heat. This is why you might notice a very slight cooling when you dissolve table salt in water, although the effect is subtle compared to, say, ammonium nitrate.

### Worked Example 3: Enthalpy of Solution of Calcium Chloride

**Problem:** Calculate the enthalpy of solution of calcium chloride, CaCl‚āā(s). Data:
- őĒH_lat‚¶Ķ[CaCl‚āā(s)] = ‚ąí2258 kJ mol‚ĀĽ¬Ļ
- őĒH_hyd‚¶Ķ[Ca¬≤‚Āļ(g)] = ‚ąí1592 kJ mol‚ĀĽ¬Ļ
- őĒH_hyd‚¶Ķ[Cl‚ĀĽ(g)] = ‚ąí364 kJ mol‚ĀĽ¬Ļ (per mole of Cl‚ĀĽ ions)

**Strategy:** CaCl‚āā produces one Ca¬≤‚Āļ ion and two Cl‚ĀĽ ions per formula unit. So the total hydration enthalpy includes the hydration of one Ca¬≤‚Āļ plus two Cl‚ĀĽ.

**Calculation:**
ő£őĒH_hyd = (‚ąí1592) + 2 √ó (‚ąí364) = ‚ąí1592 ‚ąí 728 = ‚ąí2320 kJ mol‚ĀĽ¬Ļ.
őĒH_sol = ‚ąí2320 ‚ąí (‚ąí2258) = ‚ąí2320 + 2258 = ‚ąí62 kJ mol‚ĀĽ¬Ļ.

The enthalpy of solution is ‚ąí62 kJ mol‚ĀĽ¬Ļ, which is **exothermic**. When CaCl‚āā dissolves, the hydration energy released (‚ąí2320 kJ) exceeds the energy required to break the lattice (+2258 kJ), so there is a net release of heat.

### When Is Dissolution Exothermic vs. Endothermic?

The sign of őĒH_sol depends on the competition between lattice enthalpy and hydration enthalpy:

- If the magnitude of the hydration enthalpy (|ő£őĒH_hyd|) is **greater** than the magnitude of the lattice enthalpy (|őĒH_lat|), then őĒH_sol is negative and dissolution is **exothermic** (the solution gets warm). CaCl‚āā is an example.

- If |ő£őĒH_hyd| is **smaller** than |őĒH_lat|, then őĒH_sol is positive and dissolution is **endothermic** (the solution gets cold). NaCl is a borderline case; NH‚āĄNO‚āÉ and KCl are clearer examples of endothermic dissolution.

### Common Misconception

Many students think that if a compound is soluble, its dissolution must be exothermic. This is not true. Solubility depends on Gibbs free energy (őĒG = őĒH ‚ąí TőĒS), not on őĒH alone. A compound can dissolve even if őĒH_sol is positive, as long as the entropy increase (őĒS) is large enough to make őĒG negative. We will study this in detail in Lesson 18.
