Lesson 36: Organic Fundamentals — Functional Groups & Nomenclature

Syllabus reference: 10.1, 20.1 (Fundamentals of Organic Chemistry)

Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to:

  • Identify and name at least 12 functional groups by their general formula, suffix, and prefix.
  • Apply systematic IUPAC nomenclature rules to organic molecules with up to 10 carbon atoms.
  • Classify alcohols, halogenoalkanes, and amines as primary (1°), secondary (2°), or tertiary (3°).
  • Draw structural, condensed, and skeletal formulas for a given molecular formula.
  • Recognise homologous series and predict trends in physical properties within a series.

1. Prior Knowledge Check

Question 1: What does the term "homologous series" mean, and why are alkanes described as a homologous series?

▸ Show
A homologous series is a family of compounds with the same general formula, same functional group, and a regular gradation in physical properties. Each successive member differs by a $\ce{–CH2–}$ unit. Alkanes ($\ce{C_nH_{2n+2}}$) are a homologous series — methane ($\ce{CH4}$), ethane ($\ce{C2H6}$), propane ($\ce{C3H8}$), etc. — each differing by $\ce{CH2}$. Boiling points increase steadily due to increasing London dispersion forces.

Question 2: Draw the displayed formula of $\ce{C2H6O}$ — there are two possible isomers. What are they?

▸ Show
Two isomers: ethanol $\ce{CH3CH2OH}$ (an alcohol) and methoxymethane $\ce{CH3OCH3}$ (an ether). They have the same molecular formula but different connectivity — this is functional group isomerism. Ethanol boils at 78°C (hydrogen bonding); methoxymethane boils at −24°C (dipole–dipole only).

Question 3: What is the difference between "saturated" and "unsaturated" hydrocarbons?

▸ Show
Saturated hydrocarbons contain only carbon–carbon single bonds ($\ce{C–C}$). Every carbon is bonded to the maximum number of hydrogen atoms possible. Alkanes are saturated. Unsaturated hydrocarbons contain at least one multiple bond — a $\ce{C=C}$ double bond (alkenes) or a $\ce{C#C}$ triple bond (alkynes). These multiple bonds are sites of reactivity.

2. The Language of Organic Chemistry — Functional Groups

Organic chemistry is the chemistry of carbon compounds. Carbon is uniquely versatile: it forms four covalent bonds, catenates (chains to itself), and forms stable bonds with H, O, N, S, P, and halogens. Over 20 million organic compounds are known — but they are systematically organised by their functional groups.

A functional group is an atom or group of atoms within a molecule that gives it characteristic chemical reactivity. The rest of the molecule — the carbon skeleton — influences physical properties (boiling point, solubility) but the functional group determines the chemistry.

Check-in: Which functional group is present in both aldehydes and ketones, and how do you distinguish them?

▸ Show
Both contain the carbonyl group $\ce{>C=O}$. The distinction: in an aldehyde, the carbonyl carbon is bonded to at least one H atom ($\ce{–CHO}$) and is always at the end of a chain. In a ketone, the carbonyl carbon is bonded to two carbon atoms ($\ce{>C=O}$) and is never at the end of a chain. This difference is crucial — aldehydes oxidise to carboxylic acids; ketones do not (unless the chain breaks).

3. IUPAC Nomenclature — Systematic Naming

The International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry (IUPAC) provides a systematic way to name organic compounds. Every name encodes the structure. Mastering nomenclature is not memorisation — it is decoding. A name like 2-methylpropan-1-ol tells you everything about the molecule.

3.1 The Three Parts of Every IUPAC Name

Figure 2: Every IUPAC name = Prefix + Stem + Suffix. The stem tells you the carbon chain length; the suffix tells you the functional group family; the prefix tells you what substituents are attached and where.

3.2 Step-by-Step Naming Rules

Rule 1 — Find the longest continuous carbon chain that contains the principal functional group. This determines the stem name.

Rule 2 — Number the chain so that the principal functional group gets the lowest possible number. If there is no functional group (alkane), number so substituents get the lowest combination of numbers.

Rule 3 — Identify and name substituents (side chains, halogen atoms, etc.). List them alphabetically with their position numbers. Use di–, tri–, tetra– for multiple identical substituents.

Rule 4 — Assemble the name: (number)–(substituent)(number)–(substituent)…STEM(number)–(suffix). Use commas between numbers, hyphens between numbers and words.

Check-in: Name the following molecule: $\ce{CH3CH(CH3)CH2CH2OH}$

▸ Show
The longest chain containing –OH is 4 carbons: butan–. Number from –OH end: C1 = $\ce{CH2OH}$, C2 = $\ce{CH2}$, C3 = $\ce{CH(CH3)}$, C4 = $\ce{CH3}$. At C3, a methyl substituent. Name: 3-methylbutan-1-ol. (The "1" is necessary because –OH could be on C2 in an isomer.)

4. Representing Organic Molecules — Three Types of Formula

Organic molecules can be drawn in three ways. IB exam questions may use any of them — you must be fluent in all three.

Figure 3: Three ways to represent butanoic acid. IB exams may use any of these. Skeletal formulas are most efficient — each vertex and line-end represents a carbon atom with sufficient hydrogens to make four bonds.

Check-in: What is the difference between the molecular formula $\ce{C4H8O2}$ and the structural formula $\ce{CH3CH2CH2COOH}$?

▸ Show
$\ce{C4H8O2}$ tells you only which atoms are present and how many. It does not tell you how they are connected. $\ce{CH3CH2CH2COOH}$ is a structural formula that shows connectivity: a 3-carbon chain ending in a –COOH group. $\ce{C4H8O2}$ could also represent ethyl ethanoate ($\ce{CH3COOCH2CH3}$), methyl propanoate, or butanoic acid — all different compounds with the same molecular formula (isomers).

5. Classification — Primary, Secondary, Tertiary

For several functional groups — alcohols, halogenoalkanes, and amines — the reactivity depends critically on the degree of substitution at the carbon bearing the functional group.

ONE other carbon OH ethanol (1° alcohol) 2° (Secondary) Carbon with –OH is attached to TWO other carbons OH propan-2-ol (2° alcohol) 3° (Tertiary) Carbon with –OH is attached to THREE other carbons OH 2-methylpropan-2-ol (3°)
Figure 4: Classification of alcohols. The same logic applies to halogenoalkanes (1°, 2°, 3° based on the halogen-bearing carbon) and amines (1°, 2°, 3° based on how many carbons are attached to the nitrogen). Note: amine classification uses a different rule!

Important distinction for amines: For amines, classification counts the number of carbon atoms directly attached to the nitrogen atom, NOT the carbon bearing the functional group (since the nitrogen IS the functional group).

6. Worked Examples

Example 1 — IUPAC naming with multiple substituents:

Name: $\ce{CH3CH(Cl)CH(CH3)CH2CH3}$

Step 1: Draw or expand the structure. The chain is: C1($\ce{CH3}$)–C2($\ce{CHCl}$)–C3($\ce{CH(CH3)}$)–C4($\ce{CH2}$)–C5($\ce{CH3}$). Longest chain = 5 carbons → pentane.
Step 2: Number from the end that gives substituents the lowest numbers. From left: Cl on C2, methyl on C3 → set {2,3}. From right: Cl on C4, methyl on C3 → set {3,4}. Choose left (2,3 is lower than 3,4).
Step 3: Alphabetical: chloro– before methyl–. Name: 2-chloro-3-methylpentane.
Step 4: Check: pentane = 5 C, chloro at C2, methyl at C3. ✓

⚠️ Common wrong approach: Numbering from the wrong end gives 4-chloro-3-methylpentane — wrong because {3,4} > {2,3}. Always minimise the first point of difference.
Example 2 — Naming compounds with two functional groups:

Name: $\ce{HOCH2CH2CH2COOH}$

Step 1: Priority: carboxylic acid > alcohol, so –COOH determines the suffix and numbering. Stem = 4 carbons → butanoic acid.
Step 2: Number from –COOH (C1). $\ce{–OH}$ is on C4.
Step 3: Prefix for alcohol: hydroxy–. Name: 4-hydroxybutanoic acid.
Step 4: The IUPAC priority order (descending): –COOH > –COOR > –CONH₂ > –CN > –CHO > >C=O > –OH > –NH₂ > >C=C< > –X. Use this always when multiple functional groups are present.

⚠️ Common wrong approach: Naming this as "butanoic-4-ol" — incorrect because –COOH outranks –OH, so –OH becomes a prefix.
Example 3 — Drawing from a name:

Draw the skeletal formula of 3-ethyl-2,2-dimethylhexane.

Step 1: "hexane" = 6-carbon chain. Draw a zigzag with 6 vertices.
Step 2: "2,2-dimethyl" = two methyl groups on C2.
Step 3: "3-ethyl" = an ethyl group ($\ce{–CH2CH3}$) on C3.
Step 4: Number 1→6. C1 at one end, C6 at the other.

⚠️ Common wrong approach: Forgetting that "dimethyl" means the two methyls are on the SAME carbon (C2). Some students incorrectly place them on C2 and C3.

⚠️ Common Mistakes to Avoid

  1. Amine classification is different from alcohol/halogenoalkane classification. For alcohols and halogenoalkanes, you look at the carbon bearing the –OH/–X group. For amines, you count how many carbon atoms are directly attached to the nitrogen. $\ce{CH3NH2}$ is primary; $\ce{(CH3)2NH}$ is secondary; $\ce{(CH3)3N}$ is tertiary. Do NOT apply the carbon-bearing rule to amines.
  2. "Longest chain" must contain the principal functional group. If the longest chain passes through but does not include the –OH group, it is NOT the parent chain. The parent is the longest chain containing the functional group.
  3. Alphabetical order ignores di–, tri–, tetra– prefixes. "3,3-dimethyl" comes before "2-ethyl" because 'm' (methyl) comes before 'e' (ethyl). The di– is ignored for alphabetical ordering but NOT for naming.
  4. Skeletal formulas: every vertex and line-end is a carbon. Students sometimes forget that the end of a line represents $\ce{CH3}$ (not nothing). A line with two vertices represents $\ce{CH3–CH2–CH3}$ (3 carbons), not 2.
  5. Carbonyl in aldehydes vs ketones. $\ce{–CHO}$ is always terminal; $\ce{>C=O}$ in ketones is never terminal. An exam trap: drawing a ketone at the end of a chain and calling it an aldehyde.

Question 1 (Paper 1 style): Which compound is a secondary alcohol?
A. $\ce{CH3CH2CH2OH}$   B. $\ce{(CH3)2CHOH}$   C. $\ce{(CH3)3COH}$   D. $\ce{CH3OH}$

Question 2: Give the systematic IUPAC name for $\ce{CH3CH2CH(CH3)CH2COOH}$.

Question 3: Draw the skeletal formula of 2-bromo-3-chloro-4-methylhexane.

Question 4: Classify the following as 1°, 2°, or 3°: (a) $\ce{CH3CH2CH2Br}$, (b) $\ce{(CH3)2CHNH2}$, (c) $\ce{(CH3)3CCl}$, (d) $\ce{(CH3)2NH}$.

Question 5: A compound has molecular formula $\ce{C3H6O}$. Draw and name ALL possible isomers, identifying the functional group in each. (Hint: consider alcohols, ethers, aldehydes, and ketones.)

Question 6 (IB-style): Compound X has the molecular formula $\ce{C5H10O2}$. It reacts with sodium carbonate to produce $\ce{CO2}$ gas. It does NOT decolourise bromine water. Deduce two possible structures for X, giving systematic names.

Answer 1: B. $\ce{(CH3)2CHOH}$ is propan-2-ol — the carbon bearing the –OH is attached to TWO other carbons → secondary (2°). A is propan-1-ol (1°). C is 2-methylpropan-2-ol (3°). D is methanol (1°).

Answer 2: Longest chain with –COOH: 5 carbons → pentanoic acid. Number from –COOH (C1). Methyl on C3. Name: 3-methylpentanoic acid.

Answer 3: hexane = 6C backbone. Number so substituents get lowest set: Br at C2, Cl at C3, methyl at C4. Skeletal: zigzag 6 carbons, with Br branch at C2, Cl branch at C3, $\ce{CH3}$ branch at C4.

Answer 4: (a) $\ce{CH3CH2CH2Br}$ — Br on C1 which is attached to 1 carbon → 1°. (b) $\ce{(CH3)2CHNH2}$ — N attached to 1 carbon (the isopropyl group) → 1° amine. (c) $\ce{(CH3)3CCl}$ — Cl on C attached to 3 carbons → 3° halogenoalkane. (d) $\ce{(CH3)2NH}$ — N attached to 2 carbons → 2° amine. Note the difference between (b) and (d): in (b), the NH₂ is on a secondary carbon (2° halogenoalkane-like classification not applicable — amines are classified by N-attachment). Actually, $\ce{(CH3)2CHNH2}$: N is attached to ONE carbon → primary amine.

Answer 5: $\ce{C3H6O}$ isomers: (1) Propanal $\ce{CH3CH2CHO}$ — aldehyde. (2) Propanone $\ce{CH3COCH3}$ — ketone. (3) Prop-2-en-1-ol $\ce{CH2=CHCH2OH}$ — alcohol + alkene. (4) Prop-1-en-1-ol (enol, unstable, tautomerises). (5) Methoxyethene $\ce{CH2=CHOCH3}$ — ether + alkene. (6) Cyclopropanol — cyclic alcohol. Most important for IB: propanal and propanone (functional group isomers).

Answer 6: $\ce{C5H10O2}$. Reacts with $\ce{Na2CO3}$ to give $\ce{CO2}$ → must be a carboxylic acid (–COOH). Does not decolourise $\ce{Br2}$ water → no $\ce{C=C}$ double bond. So it is a saturated carboxylic acid. Two possible structures: pentanoic acid $\ce{CH3CH2CH2CH2COOH}$ and 3-methylbutanoic acid $\ce{(CH3)2CHCH2COOH}$. (Also 2-methylbutanoic acid and 2,2-dimethylpropanoic acid are possible.)

8. Key Terms

TermDefinition
Functional groupAn atom or group of atoms within a molecule that gives it characteristic chemical reactivity (e.g., –OH, –COOH, >C=O)
Homologous seriesA family of compounds with the same general formula and functional group, differing by successive $\ce{–CH2–}$ units
IUPAC nomenclatureThe international system for naming organic compounds systematically (prefix + stem + suffix)
Carbonyl groupThe $\ce{>C=O}$ functional group, found in aldehydes, ketones, carboxylic acids, esters, and amides
Carboxyl groupThe $\ce{–COOH}$ functional group of carboxylic acids, consisting of a carbonyl and a hydroxyl on the same carbon
Primary (1°) carbonA carbon atom bonded to exactly one other carbon atom
Secondary (2°) carbonA carbon atom bonded to exactly two other carbon atoms
Tertiary (3°) carbonA carbon atom bonded to exactly three other carbon atoms
Skeletal formulaA line-angle representation of an organic molecule where vertices and line-ends represent carbon atoms; hydrogen atoms are omitted unless part of a functional group
Structural isomerCompounds with the same molecular formula but different connectivity of atoms (different structural formulas)
Functional group isomerStructural isomers that belong to different homologous series (e.g., propanal and propanone, both $\ce{C3H6O}$)

9. Exam Tips

Paper 1 (Multiple Choice): Nomenclature questions appear almost every year — usually testing whether you can distinguish between functional group isomers. The trap: a molecular formula like $\ce{C3H6O}$ could be an aldehyde OR a ketone. Read the name carefully and match it to the correct functional group. Also, primary/secondary/tertiary classification of alcohols and halogenoalkanes is a frequent focus — memorise the rule for each.
Paper 2 (Structured): You may be asked to draw structural formulas from IUPAC names or vice versa. These are "easy marks" if you are systematic. Always: (1) identify the stem (carbon count), (2) draw the backbone, (3) number it, (4) add substituents and functional groups at the correct positions, (5) check every carbon has four bonds.
IUPAC priority order is essential: When a molecule has multiple functional groups, the highest-priority group becomes the suffix. The order is: –COOH > –COOR > –CONH₂ > –CN > –CHO > >C=O > –OH > –NH₂ > >C=C< > –X. IB expects you to know this for naming compounds with multiple functional groups on Paper 2.
