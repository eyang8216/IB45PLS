Lesson 36: Organic Chemistry Fundamentals ‚ÄĒ Functional Groups and IUPAC Nomenclature

What You'll Learn

This lesson is your introduction to organic chemistry ‚ÄĒ the chemistry of carbon compounds. You will learn why carbon is special and can form millions of different compounds. You will learn what a functional group is and how to recognise common ones. Most importantly, you will learn the IUPAC system ‚ÄĒ the international rules for naming organic molecules. By the end of this lesson, you will be able to look at a molecular formula or a structural diagram and give the molecule its correct systematic name.

1. What Is Organic Chemistry?

Organic chemistry is the branch of chemistry that studies compounds containing the element carbon. The name "organic" comes from history ‚ÄĒ scientists once believed that these compounds could only be made by living organisms. We now know that is not true, but the name has stayed. Organic chemistry today is about the structure, properties, reactions, and synthesis of carbon-based molecules, whether they come from nature or are made in a laboratory.

Why carbon is special. Carbon atoms have the unique ability to form strong covalent bonds with other carbon atoms. A covalent bond is a bond in which two atoms share a pair of electrons. Carbon can form long chains of carbon atoms linked together ‚ÄĒ a property called catenation. Carbon can also form rings, branched structures, and bonds with many other elements like hydrogen, oxygen, nitrogen, and the halogens (fluorine, chlorine, bromine, iodine).

Carbon has 4 electrons in its outer shell (its valence shell). Because of this, a carbon atom always forms 4 covalent bonds in its stable molecules. It can do this by forming four single bonds, or two single bonds and one double bond, or one single bond and one triple bond, or two double bonds. The ability to form double and triple bonds (where two or three pairs of electrons are shared) adds to the diversity of organic molecules.

Hydrocarbons are the simplest organic compounds ‚ÄĒ they contain only carbon and hydrogen. All other organic compounds can be thought of as hydrocarbons in which one or more hydrogen atoms have been replaced by other atoms or groups of atoms.

2. Homologous Series ‚ÄĒ Families of Organic Compounds

A homologous series is a family of organic compounds that share the same general features:

  • All members of the series have the same functional group (explained below).
  • Each member differs from the next by one CH‚āā group ‚ÄĒ that is, by one carbon atom and two hydrogen atoms.
  • The series has a general molecular formula that describes any member.
  • All members have similar chemical properties because they have the same functional group.
  • Physical properties (such as boiling point and melting point) show a gradual trend as the chain gets longer.

Think of a homologous series like a ladder. Each rung is one CH‚āā unit higher than the previous one. The chemistry is similar at every rung because the same functional group is present.

3. Functional Groups ‚ÄĒ The Reactive Parts of Molecules

A functional group is a specific atom or group of atoms within a molecule that gives the molecule its characteristic chemical reactions. The rest of the molecule (the hydrocarbon skeleton) affects physical properties but does not usually participate directly in reactions.

When you look at an organic molecule, the first thing to identify is the functional group because it tells you what kind of reactions the molecule can do. Two molecules with the same functional group but different chain lengths will undergo the same kinds of reactions.

4. The IUPAC Naming System ‚ÄĒ Rules for Giving Every Molecule a Unique Name

IUPAC stands for the International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry. The IUPAC naming system (also called systematic nomenclature) is a set of rules that assigns a unique name to every possible organic molecule. If you follow the rules correctly, anyone anywhere in the world can draw the molecule from just its name.

Here are the steps, explained one at a time. You must follow them in this order.

Step 1: Find the longest continuous chain of carbon atoms. This chain is called the parent chain. The number of carbon atoms in the parent chain determines the root of the name:

  • 1 carbon: meth‚ąí
  • 2 carbons: eth‚ąí
  • 3 carbons: prop‚ąí
  • 4 carbons: but‚ąí
  • 5 carbons: pent‚ąí
  • 6 carbons: hex‚ąí
  • 7 carbons: hept‚ąí
  • 8 carbons: oct‚ąí
  • 9 carbons: non‚ąí
  • 10 carbons: dec‚ąí

Step 2: Identify the principal functional group. Among all the functional groups present, one is the "principal" one ‚ÄĒ it determines the suffix (ending) of the name. The priority order (highest priority first) is: carboxylic acid > ester > amide > nitrile > aldehyde > ketone > alcohol > amine > alkene > alkyne > alkane > halogenoalkane. The highest-priority group gets the suffix; lower-priority groups are named as prefixes.

Step 3: Number the carbon atoms in the parent chain. Start numbering from the end of the chain that gives the principal functional group the lowest possible number. If there is no functional group (alkane), number from the end that gives the first substituent the lowest number.

Step 4: Identify and name substituents. A substituent is any atom or group attached to the parent chain that is not part of the principal functional group. Common substituents include:

  • Alkyl groups (branches from the main chain): methyl (‚ąíCH‚āÉ), ethyl (‚ąíC‚āāH‚āÖ), propyl (‚ąíC‚āÉH‚āá)
  • Halogens: fluoro (‚ąíF), chloro (‚ąíCl), bromo (‚ąíBr), iodo (‚ąíI)
  • Lower-priority functional groups named as prefixes: hydroxy (‚ąíOH when not the principal group), amino (‚ąíNH‚āā when not principal)

Step 5: Write the name as one word (with hyphens and commas). The format is: (substituent positions)-(substituent names)(parent chain)(functional group suffix). If there are multiple identical substituents, use di‚ąí (2), tri‚ąí (3), tetra‚ąí (4). List substituents in alphabetical order (ignoring di‚ąí, tri‚ąí when alphabetising). Use commas between numbers and hyphens between numbers and words.

5. Worked Examples ‚ÄĒ Naming Molecules Step by Step

Worked Example 1: Name the molecule with the structural formula CH‚āÉCH‚āāCH‚āāCOOH.

To solve this, we need to: follow the IUPAC steps.

Step 1: Count the longest carbon chain that includes the functional group. The chain is CH‚āÉ‚ąíCH‚āā‚ąíCH‚āā‚ąíCOOH. That is 4 carbon atoms. Root: but‚ąí.

Step 2: The functional group is ‚ąíCOOH, which is a carboxylic acid. This is the highest priority group. Suffix: ‚ąíoic acid. Replace the "e" of butane: butanoic acid.

Step 3: Numbering: The ‚ąíCOOH carbon is always number 1 in carboxylic acids. No need to write "1" in the name.

Answer: butanoic acid.

There are no substituents, so the name is simply the root plus the suffix.

Worked Example 2: Name the molecule CH‚āÉCH(Cl)CH‚āÉ.

To solve this, we need to: identify the parent chain and substituents.

Step 1: The longest carbon chain: CH‚āÉ‚ąíCH‚ąíCH‚āÉ. That is 3 carbon atoms. Root: prop‚ąí.

Step 2: There are only single bonds and a chlorine atom. No multiple bonds, no oxygen or nitrogen groups. The molecule is an alkane with a halogen substituent. The principal group is the alkane. Suffix: ‚ąíane.

Step 3: Numbering: Number from the end that gives the chlorine the lowest number. If we number from the left: carbon 1 is CH‚āÉ, carbon 2 is CH(Cl), carbon 3 is CH‚āÉ. Chlorine is at position 2. If we number from the right, chlorine is also at position 2. So chlorine is at C-2.

Step 4: The substituent is a chlorine atom. As a prefix: chloro‚ąí. Its position is 2.

Step 5: Put it together: 2-chloropropane.

Notice: numbers and words are separated by hyphens. No space between "chloro" and "propane."

Worked Example 3: Name the molecule CH‚āÉCH=CHCH‚āÉ.

Step 1: The longest chain with the double bond: 4 carbon atoms. Root: but‚ąí.

Step 2: The functional group is the C=C double bond ‚ÄĒ an alkene. Suffix: ‚ąíene.

Step 3: Numbering: We number to give the double bond the lowest possible number. The double bond starts at C-2 (whether we number from left or right). So the double bond is between C-2 and C-3.

Step 4: No substituents.

Step 5: Put it together: but‚ąí2‚ąíene. More commonly written as but-2-ene.

The "2" indicates the position of the double bond ‚ÄĒ specifically, it starts at carbon 2.

Worked Example 4: Name the molecule CH‚āÉCH‚āāCOCH‚āÉ.

Step 1: Longest chain including the C=O group: CH‚āÉ‚ąíCH‚āā‚ąíCO‚ąíCH‚āÉ. That is 4 carbon atoms. Root: but‚ąí.

Step 2: The functional group is >C=O (carbonyl) in the middle of the chain ‚ÄĒ a ketone. Suffix: ‚ąíone.

Step 3: Numbering: Number from the end that gives the C=O the lowest number. From the left: C-1 is CH‚āÉ, C-2 is CH‚āā, C-3 is CO, C-4 is CH‚āÉ. C=O is at C-3. From the right: C=O is at C-2. So number from the right, giving C=O position 2.

Step 4: No substituents.

Step 5: Put it together: butan‚ąí2‚ąíone. More commonly: butan-2-one.

Note the "an" between "but" and "one" ‚ÄĒ this comes from the alkane name "butane." The "e" of "ane" is dropped before adding "one": butane ‚Üí butanone.

Worked Example 5: Name the molecule CH‚āÉCH(OH)CH‚āāCH‚āÉ.

Step 1: Longest chain: 4 carbon atoms. Root: but‚ąí.

Step 2: Functional group: ‚ąíOH (hydroxyl), which is an alcohol. Suffix: ‚ąíol.

Step 3: Numbering: Number from the end that gives the ‚ąíOH the lowest number. From the left: ‚ąíOH is at C-2. From the right: ‚ąíOH is at C-3. So number from the left.

Step 4: No other substituents.

Step 5: Put it together: butan-2-ol. The "e" is kept before "ol": butane ‚Üí butanol, and the number is inserted.

Worked Example 6: Name the molecule CH‚āÉCH(CH‚āÉ)CH‚āāCH‚āÉ.

Step 1: Longest chain: The backbone is CH‚āÉ‚ąíCH‚ąíCH‚āā‚ąíCH‚āÉ. That is 4 carbons. Root: but‚ąí.

Step 2: Only single bonds ‚ÄĒ alkane. Suffix: ‚ąíane.

Step 3: Numbering: Number from the end closer to the branch. The branch (methyl group) is on C-2 from the left, or C-3 from the right. So number from the left. The methyl group is at position 2.

Step 4: Substituent: a methyl group (‚ąíCH‚āÉ) at position 2. Prefix: methyl‚ąí.

Step 5: 2-methylbutane.

Worked Example 7: Name the ester CH‚āÉCH‚āāCOOCH‚āÉ.

An ester has the general structure R‚ąíCOO‚ąíR', where R is the part from the carboxylic acid and R' is the part from the alcohol. To name an ester, we name the alkyl group from the alcohol first, then the acid part with the suffix ‚ąíoate.

The molecule: CH‚āÉCH‚āā‚ąíCOO‚ąíCH‚āÉ.

The alcohol part (the ‚ąíOR' group): ‚ąíO‚ąíCH‚āÉ comes from methanol. The alkyl group is methyl.

The acid part (the RCO‚ąí group): CH‚āÉCH‚āā‚ąíCO‚ąí comes from propanoic acid (CH‚āÉCH‚āāCOOH). As an ester suffix, propanoic acid becomes propanoate.

Put together: methyl propanoate.

(Note: in the full name, the two words are separated by a space ‚ÄĒ this is an exception to the one-word rule.)

Worked Example 8: Name the molecule CH‚āÉCHBrCH‚āāCH‚āÉ.

Step 1: Longest chain: 4 carbon atoms. Root: but‚ąí.

Step 2: Only single bonds ‚ÄĒ alkane. Suffix: ‚ąíane.

Step 3: Numbering: Number to give Br the lowest number. From the left: Br at C-2. From the right: Br at C-3. Number from the left.

Step 4: Substituent: bromine at position 2. Prefix: bromo‚ąí.

Step 5: 2-bromobutane.

Problem 5 (IB-style): Two organic compounds, A and B, both have the molecular formula C‚āĄH‚āĀ‚āÄO.

  • Compound A does not react when heated with acidified potassium dichromate(VI) solution (K‚āāCr‚āāO‚āá dissolved in dilute sulfuric acid). The solution remains orange.
  • Compound B does react with acidified potassium dichromate(VI): the orange solution turns green, and a new compound C with the formula C‚āĄH‚āąO is formed. Compound C gives a positive result with Tollens' reagent (a silver mirror forms).

(a) Explain what the reaction (or lack of reaction) with acidified potassium dichromate(VI) tells you about the type of alcohol present in compounds A and B. Classify each as primary (1¬į), secondary (2¬į), or tertiary (3¬į), or state if it is not an alcohol at all.

(b) Compound C gives a positive Tollens' test. What functional group does this indicate is present in compound C? Based on this, what type of alcohol must compound B be?

(c) Suggest one possible structural formula and IUPAC name for compound A, one for compound B, and one for compound C. Explain how you arrived at each answer.

(d) Explain, in terms of bonding, why compound A does not react with acidified dichromate(VI), whereas compound B does.

Answers

Answer 1.

(a) Alkenes (one double bond, straight chain): C‚āôH‚āā‚āô. Each double bond reduces the hydrogen count by 2 compared to the alkane with the same number of carbons.

(b) Alcohols (one ‚ąíOH, straight chain): C‚āôH‚āā‚āô‚āä‚āĀOH. The general formula can also be written as C‚āôH‚āā‚āô‚āä‚āāO, but C‚āôH‚āā‚āô‚āä‚āĀOH makes the functional group explicit.

(c) Carboxylic acids (one ‚ąíCOOH, straight chain): C‚āôH‚āā‚āôO‚āā. For example, methanoic acid is HCOOH (n=1 gives CH‚āāO‚āā, which matches: 1 carbon, 2 hydrogens, 2 oxygens).

Answer 2.

(a) CH‚āÉCH‚āāCHO: The group at the end is ‚ąíCHO (carbonyl with H attached). This is an aldehyde group. The compound belongs to the aldehyde homologous series.

(b) CH‚āÉCOCH‚āāCH‚āÉ: The C=O group has carbon atoms on both sides (CH‚āÉ‚ąí on one side, ‚ąíCH‚āāCH‚āÉ on the other). This is a ketone group. The compound belongs to the ketone homologous series. Its IUPAC name is butanone.

(c) CH‚āÉCH‚āāNH‚āā: The ‚ąíNH‚āā group is an amine (amino group). The compound belongs to the amine homologous series. Its IUPAC name is ethylamine or aminoethane.

(d) CH‚āÉCN: The ‚ąíC‚Č°N group is a nitrile (cyano group). The compound belongs to the nitrile homologous series. Its IUPAC name is ethanenitrile.

Answer 3.

(a) CH‚āÉCH‚āāCH‚āāCH‚āÉ: 4 carbon chain, all single bonds ‚Üí alkane. Root: but‚ąí, suffix: ‚ąíane. Name: butane.

(b) CH‚āÉCH‚āāCH‚āāOH: 3 carbon chain. Functional group: ‚ąíOH (alcohol). Root: prop‚ąí, suffix: ‚ąíol. Numbering: ‚ąíOH is at C-1 (numbering from the ‚ąíOH end). Name: propan-1-ol.

(c) CH‚āÉCH=CH‚āā: 3 carbon chain. Functional group: C=C (alkene). Root: prop‚ąí, suffix: ‚ąíene. Numbering: double bond starts at C-1. Name: propene (no number needed since the double bond can only be at C-1 in a 3-carbon chain).

(d) CH‚āÉCHClCH‚āāCH‚āÉ: 4 carbon chain. All single bonds ‚Üí alkane. Root: but‚ąí, suffix: ‚ąíane. Numbering: Cl is at C-2 (number from end closest to Cl). Substituent: chloro at C-2. Name: 2-chlorobutane.

(e) CH‚āÉCH‚āāCOOH: 3 carbon chain including the ‚ąíCOOH carbon. Functional group: ‚ąíCOOH (carboxylic acid). Root: prop‚ąí, suffix: ‚ąíoic acid. The ‚ąíCOOH carbon is automatically number 1. Name: propanoic acid.

Answer 4.

(a) Pentan-3-one: "Pent" = 5 carbons. "3-one" = ketone (C=O) at C-3. Structure: CH‚āÉCH‚āā‚ąíCO‚ąíCH‚āāCH‚āÉ.

(b) 2-methylpropan-1-ol: "Prop" = 3 carbon parent chain. "1-ol" = ‚ąíOH at C-1. "2-methyl" = methyl group at C-2. Structure: (CH‚āÉ)‚āāCH‚ąíCH‚āāOH. The central carbon (C-2) is bonded to H, CH‚āÉ, CH‚āÉ, and CH‚āāOH.

(c) Ethyl ethanoate: Ester. "Ethyl" = alkyl group from ethanol (CH‚āÉCH‚āā‚ąí). "Ethanoate" = from ethanoic acid (CH‚āÉCOO‚ąí). Structure: CH‚āÉCOOCH‚āāCH‚āÉ.

(d) 1,2-dibromoethane: "Eth" = 2 carbon chain. "1,2-dibromo" = bromine atoms at C-1 and C-2. Structure: CH‚āāBr‚ąíCH‚āāBr.

Answer 5.

(a) Acidified potassium dichromate(VI) is an oxidising agent. In organic chemistry, it can oxidise primary (1¬į) alcohols (first to aldehydes and then to carboxylic acids) and secondary (2¬į) alcohols (to ketones). It cannot oxidise tertiary (3¬į) alcohols. Compound A does not react, which suggests A is a tertiary alcohol. Compound B does react, which suggests B is either a primary or a secondary alcohol.

(b) A positive Tollens' test (silver mirror) indicates the presence of an aldehyde. Aldehydes are formed by oxidation of primary alcohols. Therefore, compound B must be a primary (1¬į) alcohol. Compound C is the aldehyde produced by oxidation of B.

(c) Possible answers (there are several isomers of C‚āĄH‚āĀ‚āÄO):

Compound A (tertiary alcohol, C‚āĄH‚āĀ‚āÄO): 2-methylpropan-2-ol, also written as (CH‚āÉ)‚āÉCOH. The carbon bearing the ‚ąíOH is attached to three other carbon atoms (three methyl groups) and has no hydrogen atom attached to it.

Compound B (primary alcohol, C‚āĄH‚āĀ‚āÄO): butan-1-ol, CH‚āÉCH‚āāCH‚āāCH‚āāOH. This is a straight-chain primary alcohol.

Compound C (aldehyde, C‚āĄH‚āąO): butanal, CH‚āÉCH‚āāCH‚āāCHO. This is formed by oxidation of butan-1-ol at the ‚ąíCH‚āāOH end.

(d) Oxidation of alcohols by dichromate(VI) involves the removal of two hydrogen atoms: one from the ‚ąíOH group and one from the carbon atom that is bonded to the ‚ąíOH group. For this to happen, that carbon atom must have at least one hydrogen atom attached to it. In a tertiary alcohol such as 2-methylpropan-2-ol, the carbon bearing the ‚ąíOH group is bonded to three other carbon atoms and has no C‚ąíH bond at that position. Since there is no hydrogen atom to remove from that carbon, oxidation cannot occur. The dichromate remains orange because no Cr(VI) is reduced to Cr(III).
