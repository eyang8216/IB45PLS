Lesson 10: VSEPR Theory — Molecular Shapes & Bond Angles

Syllabus reference: 4.3/14.1 — VSEPR theory, molecular shapes, bond angles, polarity of molecules

Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to:

  • State the central principle of VSEPR theory: electron pairs repel and arrange themselves to minimise repulsion.
  • Determine the electron domain geometry and molecular geometry for any molecule or ion given its Lewis structure.
  • Predict bond angles and explain deviations from ideal angles due to lone-pair repulsion.
  • Identify all 11 molecular shapes tested by the IB from 2 to 6 electron domains.
  • Apply VSEPR to deduce shape, then use shape to determine molecular polarity.

1. Prior Knowledge Check

Question 1: What does VSEPR stand for and what is its basic premise?

▸ Show
VSEPR = Valence Shell Electron Pair Repulsion. The premise: electron pairs (both bonding and non-bonding) around a central atom repel each other and arrange themselves as far apart as possible in three-dimensional space to minimise repulsion. The resulting arrangement determines the molecular shape.

Question 2: What is the difference between an electron domain and a bonding pair?

▸ Show
An electron domain is any region of electron density around the central atom — it can be a single bond, double bond, triple bond, or lone pair. All count as ONE domain for VSEPR purposes. A double bond counts as one domain, not two.

Question 3: Draw the Lewis structure of $\ce{NH3}$ and $\ce{NH4+}$. How many electron domains around N in each?

▸ Show
$\ce{NH3}$: N has 3 bonding pairs + 1 lone pair = 4 electron domains. $\ce{NH4+}$: N has 4 bonding pairs + 0 lone pairs = 4 electron domains. Both have tetrahedral electron domain geometry, but different molecular shapes (trigonal pyramidal vs. tetrahedral).

2. The VSEPR Method — Step by Step

2.1 The Five Steps

  1. Draw the Lewis structure — this is non-negotiable; you cannot do VSEPR without it.
  2. Count electron domains around the central atom: each bond (single, double, triple) = 1 domain; each lone pair = 1 domain.
  3. Determine the electron domain geometry (the arrangement of all domains) based on the count.
  4. Determine the molecular geometry (the arrangement of atoms only, ignoring lone pairs).
  5. Predict bond angles and account for lone-pair compression.

2.2 The Repulsion Hierarchy

Not all electron pair repulsions are equal:

$$\text{Lone pair–lone pair} > \text{Lone pair–bonding pair} > \text{Bonding pair–bonding pair}$$

Lone pairs are held closer to the nucleus and occupy more space than bonding pairs (which are stretched between two nuclei). Therefore, lone pairs compress bond angles — the more lone pairs, the smaller the bond angle.

Figure 1: The progressive compression of bond angles with increasing lone pairs. All three molecules have 4 electron domains (tetrahedral arrangement), but the bond angle shrinks from 109.5° → 107° → 104.5° as lone pair count increases from 0 → 1 → 2.

Check-in: Why is the bond angle in $\ce{NH3}$ (107°) smaller than in $\ce{CH4}$ (109.5°)?

▸ Show
Both have 4 electron domains (tetrahedral). However, $\ce{NH3}$ has one lone pair which occupies more space than a bonding pair. The lone pair–bonding pair repulsion is stronger than bonding pair–bonding pair repulsion, so the N–H bonds are pushed closer together, compressing the H–N–H angle from 109.5° to about 107°.

4. Special Considerations

4.1 Multiple Bonds Count as ONE Domain

A double or triple bond is treated as one electron domain in VSEPR, because all the electron density is concentrated in the same region between the two nuclei. $\ce{CO2}$ has 2 domains (two double bonds) → linear; $\ce{SO2}$ has 3 domains (one double bond, one single bond, one lone pair) → bent.

4.2 5-Domain Geometries — Axial vs. Equatorial

In a trigonal bipyramid, there are two types of positions: axial (180° apart, 3 × 90° interactions with equatorial ligands) and equatorial (120° apart, 2 × 90° interactions). Lone pairs always occupy equatorial positions first because they minimise the number of 90° LP–BP repulsions (2 vs. 3).

Check-in: In $\ce{SF4}$ (seesaw shape), where is the lone pair located — axial or equatorial? Why?

▸ Show
The lone pair occupies an equatorial position. If it were axial, it would have three 90° LP–BP repulsions with the equatorial fluorine atoms. In the equatorial position, it has only two 90° LP–BP repulsions. This minimises overall repulsion, making the seesaw shape more stable.

Check-in: $\ce{XeF2}$ has 5 electron domains but is linear. Explain.

▸ Show
$\ce{XeF2}$: Xe(8) + 2×F(7) = 22 valence electrons. Lewis structure: two Xe–F bonds + three lone pairs on Xe. That's 5 electron domains (trigonal bipyramidal). The three lone pairs occupy all three equatorial positions (to minimise 90° repulsions), leaving the two F atoms in axial positions — exactly 180° apart. The molecular geometry (atoms only) is linear.

5. Worked Examples

Example 1 — Complete VSEPR Analysis of $\ce{PCl3}$:

Lewis structure: P(5) + 3×Cl(7) = 26 e⁻. Three P–Cl single bonds + 1 lone pair on P. Each Cl has 3 lone pairs.
Electron domains: 3 bonding + 1 lone = 4 → tetrahedral electron domain geometry.
Molecular geometry: 3 bonding + 1 lone → trigonal pyramidal.
Bond angle: ~107° (less than 109.5° due to lone pair compression).
Polarity: The molecule is polar — the Cl–P bonds are polar (Cl more EN) and the trigonal pyramidal shape prevents cancellation of bond dipoles. The net dipole points downward (toward the lone pair).
Example 2 — $\ce{XeF4}$ (Square Planar, HL):

Lewis structure: Xe(8) + 4×F(7) = 36 e⁻. Four Xe–F bonds (8 e⁻), each F with 3 lone pairs (24 e⁻), remaining 4 e⁻ = 2 lone pairs on Xe.
Electron domains: 4 bonding + 2 lone = 6 → octahedral electron domain geometry.
Lone pair placement: The two lone pairs must be placed opposite each other (180° apart, axial positions) to minimise LP–LP repulsion.
Molecular geometry: 4 bonding pairs in a square plane → square planar.
Bond angle: 90° (between adjacent F atoms).
Polarity: Non-polar — the four bond dipoles are symmetrically arranged in a plane and cancel.
Example 3 — $\ce{SO4^{2-}}$ vs. $\ce{SO3^{2-}}$:

These two sulfur oxoanions have very different shapes despite both having a central S atom bonded to oxygen. Explain.

$\ce{SO4^{2-}}$ (sulfate): S(6) + 4×O(6) + 2 = 32 e⁻. Four S–O bonds (with resonance), no lone pair on S. 4 domains → tetrahedral, 109.5°.
$\ce{SO3^{2-}}$ (sulfite): S(6) + 3×O(6) + 2 = 26 e⁻. Three S–O bonds + 1 lone pair on S. 4 domains → trigonal pyramidal, ~107°.

The difference: adding one more O atom to sulfite uses the lone pair in bonding, converting trigonal pyramidal to tetrahedral. The bond angle increases and the polarity changes.

6. Common Misconceptions

⚠️ Common Mistakes to Avoid

  1. Counting a double bond as two electron domains. A double bond is one domain. $\ce{CO2}$ has 2 domains (not 4), so it's linear, not bent. This is the single most common VSEPR error.
  2. Confusing electron domain geometry with molecular geometry. The electron domain geometry includes lone pairs; the molecular geometry describes atom positions only. $\ce{NH3}$ has tetrahedral electron geometry but trigonal pyramidal molecular geometry.
  3. Thinking ionic compounds have molecular shapes. VSEPR applies only to discrete covalent molecules and polyatomic ions. NaCl does not have a "shape" — it has a crystal lattice structure.
  4. Forgetting that lone pairs on terminal atoms do not affect the central atom's geometry. Only lone pairs on the central atom influence shape. The three lone pairs on each Cl in $\ce{PCl3}$ do not affect the trigonal pyramidal shape — only the one lone pair on P matters.
  5. Assuming the ideal angle without accounting for lone pair compression. If asked for the bond angle in $\ce{NH3}$, writing 109.5° is wrong. It's 107°. The IB expects you to know that lone pairs compress bond angles by about 2.5° per lone pair.

7. End-of-Lesson Practice

Question 1: State the electron domain geometry, molecular geometry, and approximate bond angle for: (a) $\ce{BeCl2}$, (b) $\ce{BF3}$, (c) $\ce{CH4}$.

Question 2: Predict the shape and bond angle of $\ce{SO2}$. Explain why it is not linear like $\ce{CO2}$.

Question 3: Draw the Lewis structure of $\ce{IF5}$ and predict its molecular shape. Where is the lone pair? (HL)

Question 4: Compare the shapes and bond angles of $\ce{NH3}$, $\ce{PH3}$, and $\ce{AsH3}$. All are Group 15 hydrides — explain any differences.

Question 5: $\ce{ClF3}$ has the molecular formula $\ce{AX3E2}$ (3 bonding, 2 lone pairs). Predict its shape and explain the positions of the lone pairs. (HL)

Question 6 (HL synthesis): For $\ce{XeOF4}$ (xenon oxytetrafluoride): (a) draw the Lewis structure, (b) determine the number of electron domains around Xe, (c) predict the molecular geometry, (d) state the approximate O–Xe–F and F–Xe–F bond angles.

Answer 1: (a) $\ce{BeCl2}$: 2 domains (2 bonding, 0 lone) → linear, 180°. (b) $\ce{BF3}$: 3 domains (3 bonding, 0 lone) → trigonal planar, 120°. (c) $\ce{CH4}$: 4 domains (4 bonding, 0 lone) → tetrahedral, 109.5°.

Answer 2: $\ce{SO2}$: 3 electron domains (S=O double bond, S–O single bond, 1 lone pair on S) → bent/V-shaped, ~119°. $\ce{CO2}$ has only 2 domains (two C=O double bonds, no lone pair on C) → linear. The lone pair on S is the crucial difference.

Answer 3: $\ce{IF5}$: I(7) + 5×F(7) = 42 e⁻. Five I–F bonds (10 e⁻), each F with 3 lone pairs (30 e⁻), 2 e⁻ left = 1 lone pair on I. Six electron domains (octahedral), 5 bonding + 1 lone → square pyramidal. The lone pair is opposite one F, pushing the four equatorial F atoms slightly down. Bond angles: slightly less than 90°.

Answer 4: All three are trigonal pyramidal (4 domains, 3 bonding + 1 lone). Bond angles: $\ce{NH3}$ (107°) > $\ce{PH3}$ (93.5°) > $\ce{AsH3}$ (91.8°). As the central atom gets larger down the group, the bonding pairs are further from the central atom, reducing BP–BP repulsion. Consequently, the lone pair compresses the bond angle more severely in the heavier hydrides. Additionally, the H–X–H angle approaches 90° as the central atom uses almost pure p orbitals for bonding (little hybridisation in heavier elements).

Answer 5: $\ce{ClF3}$: 5 electron domains — trigonal bipyramidal. The two lone pairs occupy equatorial positions (to minimise 90° LP–LP and LP–BP repulsions). The three F atoms occupy the remaining two axial positions and one equatorial position → T-shaped. F–Cl–F angles are approximately 87.5° (between axial and equatorial F) and 175° (between axial F atoms, slightly bent due to equatorial lone pairs).

Answer 6: (a) Xe(8) + O(6) + 4×F(7) = 42 e⁻. One Xe=O double bond, four Xe–F single bonds, one lone pair on Xe. (b) 6 electron domains (1 double bond + 4 single bonds + 1 lone pair). (c) Octahedral electron geometry, 5 bonding + 1 lone → square pyramidal — but the oxygen occupies one position. The preferred structure places the double-bonded O opposite the lone pair (to minimise LP–double bond repulsion), with the four F atoms in the square plane. (d) O–Xe–F ≈ 90° (axial-equatorial), F–Xe–F ≈ 90° (adjacent in square plane). The F atoms are slightly pushed away from the lone pair.

8. Key Terms

TermDefinition
VSEPR theoryValence Shell Electron Pair Repulsion — electron pairs repel and arrange to minimise repulsion
Electron domainA region of electron density around the central atom (bond or lone pair); counts as ONE
Electron domain geometryThe spatial arrangement of ALL electron domains (bonding + non-bonding) around the central atom
Molecular geometryThe spatial arrangement of ATOMS only (ignoring lone pairs)
Bond angleThe angle between two bonds originating from the same central atom
Axial positionIn a trigonal bipyramid, the two positions aligned vertically on the symmetry axis (180° apart)
Equatorial positionIn a trigonal bipyramid, the three positions in the horizontal plane (120° apart)
Lone pair compressionThe reduction of bond angles caused by lone pairs occupying more space than bonding pairs
Trigonal planar3 electron domains, 120° apart in a plane (e.g., $\ce{BF3}$)
Octahedral6 electron domains, 90° apart, arranged around a central point like the vertices of an octahedron

9. Exam Tips

Paper 1 — "Which species has shape X?": These questions test whether you can quickly count electron domains. Strategy: (1) identify the central atom, (2) count groups around it (each bond = 1, double bond = 1), (3) subtract from group valence to find lone pairs, (4) match to shape. Time-saving: $\ce{NH4+}$ = tetrahedral, $\ce{H3O+}$ = trigonal pyramidal, $\ce{CO3^{2-}}$ = trigonal planar — memorise these.
Paper 2 — Drawing shapes: You MUST use wedges and dashes to show 3D structure. Solid wedge = coming out of page; dashed wedge = going into page; solid line = in plane. For tetrahedral: two in plane, one out, one in. For trigonal planar: all in plane (no wedges needed). For octahedral: four in plane, one out, one in. Label bond angles and name the shape explicitly.
HL — 5 and 6 domain shapes: The IB only tests seesaw, T-shaped, square pyramidal, and square planar for 5- and 6-domain species. Know how lone pairs are placed: equatorial first for 5-domain, opposite each other for 6-domain. $\ce{XeF2}$ (linear), $\ce{SF4}$ (seesaw), $\ce{ClF3}$ (T-shaped), $\ce{BrF5}$ (square pyramidal), and $\ce{XeF4}$ (square planar) are the classic examples.
