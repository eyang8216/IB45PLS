Lesson 31: Advanced Acid–Base — Lewis Theory, Polyprotic Acids & Hydrolysis

Syllabus reference: 18.1–18.3 (HL) — Lewis Acids/Bases, Polyprotic Acids, Salt Hydrolysis

Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to:

  • Define a Lewis acid (electron-pair acceptor) and Lewis base (electron-pair donor), extending beyond Brønsted–Lowry theory.
  • Identify Lewis acid–base reactions, including complex ion formation with transition metals.
  • Write stepwise dissociation equations and $K_a$ expressions for polyprotic acids.
  • Predict whether a salt solution is acidic, basic, or neutral based on the hydrolysis behaviour of its ions.

1. Prior Knowledge Check

Question 1: Define a Brønsted–Lowry acid and base.

▸ Show
A Brønsted–Lowry acid is a proton ($\ce{H+}$) donor. A Brønsted–Lowry base is a proton acceptor. This theory focuses exclusively on proton transfer.

Question 2: What is a polyprotic acid? Give an example.

▸ Show
A polyprotic acid can donate more than one proton per molecule. Examples: $\ce{H2SO4}$ (diprotic), $\ce{H3PO4}$ (triprotic). Each proton is lost in a separate step with its own $K_a$ value.

2. Lewis Acid–Base Theory

Brønsted–Lowry theory explains most acid–base chemistry but is limited to proton transfer. In 1923, Gilbert N. Lewis proposed a more general definition:

Lewis definitions:
• Lewis acid: An electron-pair acceptor — has an empty orbital to receive electrons.
• Lewis base: An electron-pair donor — has a lone pair of electrons to donate.
• Coordinate (dative) covalent bond forms when the base donates its lone pair to the acid.

All Brønsted–Lowry bases are also Lewis bases (they have lone pairs). But Lewis acids include species that have no protons at all — e.g., $\ce{BF3}$, $\ce{AlCl3}$, and transition metal cations like $\ce{Cu^{2+}}$ and $\ce{Fe^{3+}}$.

Figure 1: BF₃ (electron-deficient boron) accepts a lone pair from NH₃. No proton is transferred — this is purely a Lewis acid–base reaction.

Check-in: Classify each as Lewis acid (LA) or Lewis base (LB): (a) $\ce{OH-}$, (b) $\ce{AlCl3}$, (c) $\ce{Fe^{3+}}$, (d) $\ce{H2O}$.

▸ Show
(a) LB — has lone pairs on O. (b) LA — Al has an incomplete octet (6 electrons). (c) LA — Fe³⁺ is a cation with empty d-orbitals. (d) LB — water has two lone pairs on oxygen (and can act as a ligand).

3. Polyprotic Acids

A polyprotic acid dissociates in steps, each with its own $K_a$. The first proton is always the easiest to lose; subsequent dissociations are progressively weaker because it's harder to remove $\ce{H+}$ from a negatively charged species.

3.1 Diprotic Example: Carbonic Acid ($\ce{H2CO3}$)

$$\ce{H2CO3 <=> H+ + HCO3-} \qquad K_{a1} = 4.3 \times 10^{-7}$$ $$\ce{HCO3- <=> H+ + CO3^{2-}} \qquad K_{a2} = 5.6 \times 10^{-11}$$

Notice: $K_{a2} \ll K_{a1}$ (by a factor of ~10⁴). The second dissociation is far weaker.

3.2 Triprotic Example: Phosphoric Acid ($\ce{H3PO4}$)

$K_{a1} = 7.1 \times 10^{-3}$, $K_{a2} = 6.3 \times 10^{-8}$, $K_{a3} = 4.2 \times 10^{-13}$. Each step is ~10⁵ weaker than the previous one.

Figure 2: Stepwise dissociation of a triprotic acid. In practice, only the first dissociation contributes significantly to [H⁺].

Check-in: Calculate the pH of 0.100 mol dm⁻³ $\ce{H2CO3}$ ($K_{a1} = 4.3\times10^{-7}$, $K_{a2} = 5.6\times10^{-11}$). State why you can ignore the second dissociation.

▸ Show
Using $K_{a1}$ only: $x = \sqrt{4.3\times10^{-7} \times 0.100} = \sqrt{4.3\times10^{-8}} = 2.07\times10^{-4}$ mol dm⁻³. pH = 3.68. $K_{a2}$ is ~10⁴ times smaller — the $\ce{H+}$ from the second dissociation is negligible (~$5.6\times10^{-11}$ mol dm⁻³).

4. Salt Hydrolysis — Acidic, Basic, or Neutral?

When a salt dissolves, its ions may react with water — this is called hydrolysis. The pH of the resulting solution depends on the parent acid and base:

Salt fromExampleSolution pHExplanation
Strong acid + Strong base$\ce{NaCl}$, $\ce{KNO3}$7 (neutral)Neither ion hydrolyses
Strong acid + Weak base$\ce{NH4Cl}$, $\ce{FeCl3}$< 7 (acidic)Cation hydrolyses: $\ce{NH4+ + H2O <=> NH3 + H3O+}$
Weak acid + Strong base$\ce{CH3COONa}$, $\ce{Na2CO3}$> 7 (basic)Anion hydrolyses: $\ce{CH3COO- + H2O <=> CH3COOH + OH-}$
Weak acid + Weak base$\ce{CH3COONH4}$Depends on $K_a$ vs. $K_b$Both ions hydrolyse; compare strengths

Check-in: Predict whether solutions of these salts are acidic, basic, or neutral: (a) $\ce{KCl}$, (b) $\ce{NH4NO3}$, (c) $\ce{Na2CO3}$, (d) $\ce{AlCl3}$.

▸ Show
(a) Neutral — KOH (strong base) + HCl (strong acid). (b) Acidic — NH₃ (weak base) + HNO₃ (strong acid); NH₄⁺ hydrolyses. (c) Basic — NaOH (strong base) + H₂CO₃ (weak acid); CO₃²⁻ hydrolyses. (d) Acidic — Al(OH)₃ (weak base) + HCl (strong acid); Al³⁺ hydrolyses: $\ce{[Al(H2O)6]^{3+} + H2O <=> [Al(H2O)5(OH)]^{2+} + H3O+}$.

5. Worked Examples

Example 1 — Identifying Lewis Acid–Base Reactions:

Which of these is a Lewis acid–base reaction but NOT a Brønsted–Lowry reaction?
(a) $\ce{NH3 + HCl -> NH4Cl}$
(b) $\ce{Cu^{2+} + 4NH3 -> [Cu(NH3)4]^{2+}}$
(c) $\ce{CH3COOH + NaOH -> CH3COONa + H2O}$

Analysis: (a) Involves proton transfer (HCl donates H⁺ to NH₃) — this is Brønsted–Lowry. (b) Cu²⁺ accepts electron pairs from NH₃, forming coordinate bonds. No proton transfer occurs — this is purely Lewis. (c) Involves proton transfer — Brønsted–Lowry.
Answer: (b) only.

⚠️ Common wrong approach: Thinking all acid–base reactions are proton transfers. Lewis theory is broader — any electron-pair donation/acceptance counts, including complex ion formation.
Example 2 — pH of a Polyprotic Acid:

Calculate the pH of 0.0500 mol dm⁻³ $\ce{H2SO3}$ (sulfurous acid). $K_{a1} = 1.5\times10^{-2}$, $K_{a2} = 1.0\times10^{-7}$.

Step 1: Check if approximation valid for $K_{a1}$: $K_{a1}/c = 1.5\times10^{-2}/0.0500 = 0.30$. This is far above $10^{-3}$ — must solve quadratic!
Step 2 — Quadratic: $1.5\times10^{-2} = x^2/(0.0500 - x)$. $x^2 + 1.5\times10^{-2}x - 7.5\times10^{-4} = 0$.
Step 3: $x = [-0.015 + \sqrt{(0.015)^2 + 4\times7.5\times10^{-4}}]/2 = [-0.015 + \sqrt{2.25\times10^{-4} + 3.00\times10^{-3}}]/2 = [-0.015 + 0.0568]/2 = 0.0209$ mol dm⁻³.
Step 4: pH = $-\log(0.0209) = \mathbf{1.68}$. Ignore $K_{a2}$ — it contributes negligibly.

⚠️ Common wrong approach: Using the approximation for a moderately strong polyprotic acid like H₂SO₃. With $K_a/c = 0.30$, the error would be huge.
Example 3 — Predicting Salt Hydrolysis pH:

Predict whether a 0.100 mol dm⁻³ solution of $\ce{CH3COONH4}$ is acidic, basic, or neutral. $K_a(\ce{CH3COOH}) = 1.8\times10^{-5}$, $K_b(\ce{NH3}) = 1.8\times10^{-5}$.

Analysis: Both ions hydrolyse. For $\ce{CH3COO-}$: $K_b = K_w/K_a = 10^{-14}/1.8\times10^{-5} = 5.6\times10^{-10}$. For $\ce{NH4+}$: $K_a = K_w/K_b = 10^{-14}/1.8\times10^{-5} = 5.6\times10^{-10}$. Since $K_a$ of $\ce{NH4+}$ equals $K_b$ of $\ce{CH3COO-}$, the solution is neutral (pH = 7.00).

⚠️ Common wrong approach: Assuming all salts of a weak acid and weak base are neutral. This is only true if $K_a = K_b$. If $K_a > K_b$, the solution is acidic; if $K_b > K_a$, it's basic.

⚠️ Common Mistakes to Avoid

  1. Limiting Lewis acids to proton-containing species. Lewis acids include metal cations ($\ce{Cu^{2+}}$, $\ce{Fe^{3+}}$) and electron-deficient molecules ($\ce{BF3}$, $\ce{AlCl3}$) that have no protons at all.
  2. Treating polyprotic acids as if all protons contribute equally. The first dissociation dominates. Unless the acid is strong in its first step (like $\ce{H2SO4}$), $K_{a2}$ and $K_{a3}$ can be ignored for pH calculations.
  3. Forgetting that highly charged metal cations (Al³⁺, Fe³⁺) hydrolyse to produce acidic solutions. These cations are surrounded by water ligands that become acidic due to the high charge density polarising O–H bonds.
  4. Thinking all salts of weak acid + weak base are neutral. The pH depends on the relative magnitudes of $K_a$ and $K_b$ of the ions involved.

6. End-of-Lesson Practice

Question 1 (Paper 1 style): Which species can act as a Lewis acid but NOT as a Brønsted–Lowry acid?
A. $\ce{HCl}$   B. $\ce{NH3}$   C. $\ce{BF3}$   D. $\ce{H3O+}$

Question 2: Write the two dissociation equations and $K_a$ expressions for the diprotic acid $\ce{H2C2O4}$ (oxalic acid, $K_{a1}=5.9\times10^{-2}$, $K_{a2}=6.4\times10^{-5}$).

Question 3: Calculate the pH of 0.0200 mol dm⁻³ $\ce{H2C2O4}$ using $K_{a1}=5.9\times10^{-2}$. Explain why you cannot use the approximation.

Question 4: Classify aqueous solutions of these salts as acidic, basic, or neutral: (a) $\ce{K2SO4}$, (b) $\ce{CuSO4}$, (c) $\ce{NaHCO3}$, (d) $\ce{NH4CN}$ ($K_a$ HCN = $6.2\times10^{-10}$, $K_b$ NH₃ = $1.8\times10^{-5}$).

Question 5: Write the hydrolysis equation and explain why $\ce{FeCl3}$ solution is acidic. What is the role of $\ce{Fe^{3+}}$ as a Lewis acid?

Answer 1: C. BF₃ has no protons to donate (so it's not Brønsted–Lowry), but boron has an empty 2p orbital and can accept an electron pair — making it a Lewis acid. HCl and H₃O⁺ are both Brønsted–Lowry and Lewis acids. NH₃ is a base.

Answer 2: Step 1: $\ce{H2C2O4 <=> H+ + HC2O4-}$, $K_{a1} = [\ce{H+}][\ce{HC2O4-}]/[\ce{H2C2O4}]$. Step 2: $\ce{HC2O4- <=> H+ + C2O4^{2-}}$, $K_{a2} = [\ce{H+}][\ce{C2O4^{2-}}]/[\ce{HC2O4-}]$.

Answer 3: $K_{a1}/c = 0.059/0.0200 = 2.95 \gg 10^{-3}$, so approximation fails. Solve quadratic: $5.9\times10^{-2} = x^2/(0.0200-x)$. $x^2 + 0.059x - 0.00118 = 0$. $x = [-0.059 + \sqrt{0.003481 + 0.00472}]/2 = [-0.059 + 0.0906]/2 = 0.0158$. pH = $-\log(0.0158) = \mathbf{1.80}$.

Answer 4: (a) Neutral — KOH (SB) + H₂SO₄ (SA). (b) Acidic — Cu(OH)₂ (WB) + H₂SO₄ (SA); Cu²⁺ hydrolyses. (c) Basic — NaOH (SB) + H₂CO₃ (WA); HCO₃⁻ hydrolyses to give OH⁻. (d) $K_a$(NH₄⁺) = $10^{-14}/1.8\times10^{-5} = 5.6\times10^{-10}$, $K_b$(CN⁻) = $10^{-14}/6.2\times10^{-10} = 1.6\times10^{-5}$. Since $K_b$(CN⁻) > $K_a$(NH₄⁺), the solution is basic.

Answer 5: $\ce{[Fe(H2O)6]^{3+} + H2O <=> [Fe(H2O)5(OH)]^{2+} + H3O+}$. Fe³⁺ has a high charge density (+3) and polarises the O–H bonds of coordinated water molecules, weakening them so they release H⁺. Fe³⁺ acts as a Lewis acid because it accepts electron pairs from water molecules to form the hexaaqua complex — the hydrolysis is a consequence of this Lewis acidity.

7. Key Terms

TermDefinition
Lewis acidAn electron-pair acceptor; has an empty orbital (e.g., $\ce{BF3}$, $\ce{Cu^{2+}}$)
Lewis baseAn electron-pair donor; has a lone pair (e.g., $\ce{NH3}$, $\ce{OH-}$, $\ce{H2O}$)
Coordinate bondA covalent bond where both electrons come from one atom (the Lewis base)
Polyprotic acidAn acid that can donate more than one proton per molecule, in stepwise fashion
$K_{a1}$, $K_{a2}$, $K_{a3}$Successive acid dissociation constants; $K_{a1} > K_{a2} > K_{a3}$ always
HydrolysisReaction of an ion with water, producing $\ce{H3O+}$ or $\ce{OH-}$ and changing the pH
Amphiprotic speciesA species that can both donate and accept a proton (e.g., $\ce{HCO3-}$, $\ce{H2O}$)
Charge densityThe ratio of an ion's charge to its radius; high charge density cations (Al³⁺, Fe³⁺) produce acidic solutions

8. Exam Tips

Paper 1 (Multiple Choice): Questions on Lewis theory often ask "which is a Lewis acid but not a Brønsted acid?" Look for electron-deficient molecules (BF₃, AlCl₃) or transition metal cations. The key is the absence of protons.
Paper 2 (Structured): For polyprotic acid pH calculations, always state "Assuming $\ce{H+}$ from the second (and third) dissociation is negligible." This shows you understand that $K_{a1}$ dominates. If $K_{a1}/c$ is large, you must solve the quadratic — show the quadratic equation explicitly to earn method marks.
Salt hydrolysis: When asked to explain why a salt solution is acidic/basic, write the hydrolysis equation, identify the ion responsible, and explain using Brønsted–Lowry (proton transfer) or Lewis (electron-pair acceptance) theory as appropriate. For metal cations like Al³⁺ and Fe³⁺, referencing the hexaaqua complex shows deeper understanding.
