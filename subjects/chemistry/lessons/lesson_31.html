1. Lewis Theory вЂ” The Broadest Definition of Acids and Bases

Why we need a broader definition

The BrГёnsted-Lowry theory defines acids as proton donors and bases as proton acceptors. This works for almost all acid-base reactions you encounter in aqueous solution. However, some reactions look and behave like acid-base reactions even though no protons are transferred. For example, when boron trifluoride (BFв‚ѓ) reacts with ammonia (NHв‚ѓ), a new bond forms between the boron atom and the nitrogen atom. No hydrogen ion is given or taken. Yet BFв‚ѓ behaves in a way that is chemically similar to how HвЃє behaves.

To account for reactions like this, the American chemist Gilbert N. Lewis proposed a more general definition in 1923, the same year BrГёnsted and Lowry published their theory.

The Lewis definitions

A Lewis acid is a chemical species that accepts an electron pair. The key word is "accepts." A Lewis acid has an empty orbital or a region of positive charge that can receive a pair of electrons.

A Lewis base is a chemical species that donates an electron pair. The key word is "donates." A Lewis base has a lone pair of electrons that it can share with the Lewis acid.

When a Lewis base donates its electron pair to a Lewis acid, the two species form a new coordinate covalent bond (also called a dative bond). In a coordinate covalent bond, both electrons in the bond come from the same atom вЂ” the Lewis base.

The relationship between Lewis and BrГёnsted-Lowry

Every BrГёnsted-Lowry base is also a Lewis base, because to accept a proton (HвЃє), a species must donate an electron pair to form a bond with the proton. Similarly, every BrГёnsted-Lowry acid reaction can be described in Lewis terms, because the proton (HвЃє) itself is a Lewis acid вЂ” it has an empty 1s orbital and accepts an electron pair from the base.

However, many Lewis acids are not BrГёnsted-Lowry acids. BFв‚ѓ, AlClв‚ѓ, COв‚‚, and transition metal ions such as FeВівЃє and CuВІвЃє are all Lewis acids, but none of them donates a proton in the BrГёnsted-Lowry sense. The Lewis definition is therefore broader and more general.

Examples of Lewis acid-base reactions

Example 1: Boron trifluoride and ammonia.

$$\ce{BF3 + NH3 -> F3B-NH3}$$

In BFв‚ѓ, the boron atom has only six electrons in its valence shell (three single bonds to fluorine). This means boron has an empty orbital and can accept an electron pair. BFв‚ѓ is the Lewis acid. Ammonia, NHв‚ѓ, has a lone pair of electrons on the nitrogen atom. NHв‚ѓ is the Lewis base. The nitrogen donates its lone pair to the boron, forming a new BвЂ”N bond.

Example 2: A proton and ammonia.

$$\ce{H+ + NH3 -> NH4+}$$

The proton HвЃє has no electrons at all вЂ” it is an empty orbital waiting to be filled. HвЃє is the Lewis acid. NHв‚ѓ donates its lone pair. NHв‚ѓ is the Lewis base. This is also a BrГёnsted-Lowry reaction, illustrating the overlap between the two theories.

Example 3: A transition metal ion forming a complex.

$$\ce{Cu^{2+}(aq) + 4NH3(aq) -> [Cu(NH3)4]^{2+}(aq)}$$

The copper(II) ion, CuВІвЃє, has empty orbitals in its 3d, 4s, and 4p subshells. It accepts electron pairs from four ammonia molecules, acting as a Lewis acid. Each ammonia molecule donates its lone pair, acting as a Lewis base. The resulting species, [Cu(NHв‚ѓ)в‚„]ВІвЃє, is a complex ion. This reaction is a Lewis acid-base reaction but not a BrГёnsted-Lowry one, because no protons are transferred.

2. Polyprotic Acids вЂ” More Than One Ionisable Proton

What a polyprotic acid is

A polyprotic acid is an acid that has more than one hydrogen atom that can be donated as a proton. The prefix "poly" means many, and "protic" refers to protons.

A diprotic acid has two ionisable protons. Examples include sulfuric acid (Hв‚‚SOв‚„), carbonic acid (Hв‚‚COв‚ѓ), and hydrogen sulfide (Hв‚‚S).

A triprotic acid has three ionisable protons. The most important example for IB Chemistry HL is phosphoric acid, Hв‚ѓPOв‚„.

First, electrostatic attraction: after the first proton is removed, the remaining species (Hв‚‚POв‚„вЃ») carries a negative charge. Removing a positively charged proton from a negatively charged ion is much harder than removing it from a neutral molecule, because the opposite charges attract each other. After the second proton is removed, the species (HPOв‚„ВІвЃ») has a charge of в€’2, making the third proton even harder to remove.

Second, the common ion effect: the HвЃє ions produced in the first dissociation suppress the second and third dissociations through Le Chatelier's principle. The equilibrium for step 2 is shifted to the left by the high concentration of HвЃє already present from step 1.

3. Salt Hydrolysis вЂ” Why Salt Solutions Are Not Always Neutral

The misconception about salts

Many students think that all salt solutions are neutral because salts are the product of acid-base neutralisation. This is incorrect. When a salt dissolves in water, its ions may react with water molecules in a process called hydrolysis. The word "hydrolysis" comes from "hydro" (water) and "lysis" (breaking). In this context, it means that the ions break apart water molecules by pulling off either HвЃє or OHвЃ».

Whether a salt solution is acidic, basic, or neutral depends entirely on the acid and base from which the salt was formed.

The four categories of salts

Category 1: Salt from a strong acid and a strong base.

Example: sodium chloride, NaCl, formed from HCl (strong acid) and NaOH (strong base).

When NaCl dissolves: NaCl (s) в†’ NaвЃє (aq) + ClвЃ» (aq). The NaвЃє ion comes from NaOH, a strong base. Since neither ion hydrolyses, the solution is neutral (pH в‰€ 7).

Category 2: Salt from a strong acid and a weak base.

Example: ammonium chloride, NHв‚„Cl, formed from HCl (strong acid) and NHв‚ѓ (weak base).

The ClвЃ» ion does not hydrolyse (from strong acid). The NHв‚„вЃє ion comes from the weak base NHв‚ѓ, so NHв‚„вЃє is the conjugate acid of a weak base. It hydrolyses:

$$\ce{NH4+(aq) + H2O(l) <=> NH3(aq) + H3O+(aq)}$$

This reaction produces Hв‚ѓOвЃє, making the solution acidic (pH < 7).

Category 3: Salt from a weak acid and a strong base.

Example: sodium ethanoate, CHв‚ѓCOONa, formed from CHв‚ѓCOOH (weak acid) and NaOH (strong base).

The NaвЃє ion does not hydrolyse (from strong base). The CHв‚ѓCOOвЃ» ion comes from the weak acid CHв‚ѓCOOH, so CHв‚ѓCOOвЃ» is the conjugate base of a weak acid. It hydrolyses:

$$\ce{CH3COO-(aq) + H2O(l) <=> CH3COOH(aq) + OH-(aq)}$$

This reaction produces OHвЃ», making the solution basic (pH > 7).

Category 4: Salt from a weak acid and a weak base.

Example: ammonium ethanoate, CHв‚ѓCOONHв‚„, formed from CHв‚ѓCOOH (weak acid) and NHв‚ѓ (weak base).

Both ions hydrolyse. The NHв‚„вЃє ion produces Hв‚ѓOвЃє (acidic), and the CHв‚ѓCOOвЃ» ion produces OHвЃ» (basic). The overall pH depends on the relative strengths of the acid and base: if Ka of the weak acid is greater than Kb of the weak base, the solution is acidic; if Kb is greater than Ka, the solution is basic; if they are equal, the solution is approximately neutral (this is rare).

A quick rule

To predict whether a salt solution is acidic or basic, identify the "parent" acid and base. If the parent acid is strong and the parent base is strong, the salt is neutral. If the parent acid is strong and the parent base is weak, the salt is acidic. If the parent acid is weak and the parent base is strong, the salt is basic.
