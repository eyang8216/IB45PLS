## 1. What Is Rate of Reaction?

### What This Concept Is and Why It Matters

When a chemical reaction takes place, the substances you start with (the reactants) are gradually turned into different substances (the products). Some reactions finish in a fraction of a second — an explosion, for example. Other reactions take hours, days, or even millions of years — like the rusting of iron or the formation of fossil fuels. The "rate of reaction" is the measurement that tells us how quickly this transformation happens. Understanding rate is essential because chemists need to control how fast reactions proceed — whether they want to speed up the production of a useful medicine or slow down the spoilage of food.

### Defining Rate of Reaction

The rate of reaction tells us how much the concentration of a reactant or product changes in a given period of time. Concentration is the amount of a substance dissolved in a certain volume of solution. In chemistry, we usually measure concentration in moles per cubic decimetre, which is written as mol dm⁻³. (One cubic decimetre, 1 dm³, is the same as one litre.)

To calculate the rate at which a reactant is being used up, we take the change in its concentration and divide by the time it took for that change to happen. Since the concentration of a reactant goes down as the reaction proceeds, the change in concentration would be a negative number. To make the rate a positive number, we include a minus sign:

\[
\text{Rate} = -\frac{\Delta [\text{A}]}{\Delta t}
\]

Here the symbol Δ (the Greek letter delta) means "change in." So Δ[A] means "change in concentration of substance A" and Δt means "change in time." The square brackets [A] mean "the concentration of A in mol dm⁻³."

For a product, whose concentration increases as the reaction proceeds, we do not need the minus sign:

\[
\text{Rate} = +\frac{\Delta [\text{C}]}{\Delta t}
\]

The units of rate are mol dm⁻³ s⁻¹, which you read as "moles per cubic decimetre per second." This unit tells you the change in concentration that happens every second.

Many students think that rate means "how much product is made." That is not quite right. Rate is specifically about how much the concentration changes per unit of time. A reaction that makes a lot of product but does so very slowly has a low rate. A reaction that makes a small amount of product almost instantly has a high rate.

## 2. Measuring Reaction Rates

### What This Concept Is and Why It Matters

If you want to study how fast a reaction goes, you need a way to measure something that changes as the reaction proceeds. Different reactions call for different measuring techniques. Choosing the right method depends on what the reaction produces or consumes that you can detect with an instrument.

### Method 1: Collecting a Gas and Measuring Its Volume

Some reactions produce a gas as one of the products. When a gas is produced, you can channel it into a gas syringe (a sealed syringe with a plunger that moves outward as gas fills it) or collect it over water in an inverted measuring cylinder. By recording the volume of gas at regular time intervals — for example, every 30 seconds — you can track how fast the reaction is going. This method works well for reactions like the one between calcium carbonate (marble chips) and hydrochloric acid:

\[
\text{CaCO}_3(\text{s}) + 2\text{HCl}(\text{aq}) \rightarrow \text{CaCl}_2(\text{aq}) + \text{CO}_2(\text{g}) + \text{H}_2\text{O}(\text{l})
\]

Carbon dioxide gas bubbles out of the solution, and you can measure how much is produced over time.

### Method 2: Measuring the Loss of Mass

If a reaction produces a gas that escapes into the air, the total mass of the reaction container will decrease as the gas leaves. You can place the reaction flask on a digital balance and record the mass at regular time intervals. The mass loss over time tells you the rate at which gas is being produced and lost. This method is straightforward and works for reactions like magnesium metal reacting with hydrochloric acid:

\[
\text{Mg}(\text{s}) + 2\text{HCl}(\text{aq}) \rightarrow \text{MgCl}_2(\text{aq}) + \text{H}_2(\text{g})
\]

The hydrogen gas escapes, and the mass on the balance drops.

### Method 3: Measuring Colour Change

Some reactions involve a substance that has a distinct colour. As the reaction proceeds, the intensity of that colour changes — either the colour appears, disappears, or shifts. You can use an instrument called a spectrophotometer, which passes light through the solution and measures how much light is absorbed. The absorbance reading is proportional to the concentration of the coloured substance. This method is especially useful for reactions involving iodine (which is brown in solution) or transition metal complexes (which are often brightly coloured).

### Method 4: Measuring pH Change

If a reaction produces or consumes hydrogen ions (H⁺) or hydroxide ions (OH⁻), the pH of the solution will change over time. A pH meter — an electronic device with a probe that you dip into the solution — can measure the pH continuously. The rate at which the pH changes tells you the rate of the reaction.

### Method 5: Measuring Electrical Conductivity

The electrical conductivity of a solution depends on how many ions are present and what kinds of ions they are. If a reaction changes the total number of ions in solution, the conductivity will change. For example, in the reaction between propanone (acetone) and iodine:

\[
\text{CH}_3\text{COCH}_3(\text{aq}) + \text{I}_2(\text{aq}) \rightarrow \text{CH}_3\text{COCH}_2\text{I}(\text{aq}) + \text{H}^+(\text{aq}) + \text{I}^-(\text{aq})
\]

The number of ions increases as the reaction proceeds, so the conductivity goes up. A conductivity probe can track this change.

### Average Rate versus Instantaneous Rate

When you calculate rate over a fairly long time interval — say, from the start of the reaction to the end — you get what is called the **average rate**. But the rate of a reaction usually changes as it goes along; most reactions slow down as the reactants get used up. To find the rate at a specific moment in time, you need the **instantaneous rate**. You get this by plotting a graph of concentration against time and drawing a tangent line — a straight line that just touches the curve at the point you are interested in. The gradient (slope) of that tangent is the instantaneous rate at that moment.

## 3. Rate and Stoichiometry

### What This Concept Is and Why It Matters

In a chemical reaction, different reactants are consumed at different rates, and different products are formed at different rates — simply because the balanced equation may have different numbers in front of each substance. The stoichiometric coefficients (the numbers in front of each formula in the balanced equation) tell you the proportion in which substances react and form. The rate of reaction must be the same number no matter which substance you use to measure it, provided you account for these stoichiometric coefficients.

For a general reaction written as:

\[
a\text{A} + b\text{B} \rightarrow c\text{C} + d\text{D}
\]

The letters a, b, c, and d are the stoichiometric coefficients. The relationship between the rates measured using different substances is:

\[
\text{Rate} = -\frac{1}{a}\frac{\Delta[\text{A}]}{\Delta t} = -\frac{1}{b}\frac{\Delta[\text{B}]}{\Delta t} = +\frac{1}{c}\frac{\Delta[\text{C}]}{\Delta t} = +\frac{1}{d}\frac{\Delta[\text{D}]}{\Delta t}
\]

The negative signs for A and B are there because reactants are consumed (their concentrations decrease). Dividing by the stoichiometric coefficient adjusts for the fact that, for example, if a = 2, then A is used up twice as fast as the "reaction event" itself.

A common misconception is that the rate is simply −Δ[A]/Δt for any reactant. But if a = 2, the raw change in [A] is twice the reaction rate, because two particles of A are consumed per reaction event. You must divide by the coefficient to get the true rate.

### Worked Example 1: Connecting Rates of Different Species

**Problem:** In the reaction shown below, the rate of formation of nitrogen dioxide (NO₂) is measured as 0.0080 mol dm⁻³ s⁻¹.

\[
2\text{N}_2\text{O}_5(\text{g}) \rightarrow 4\text{NO}_2(\text{g}) + \text{O}_2(\text{g})
\]

Calculate (a) the rate of decomposition of N₂O₅ and (b) the overall rate of reaction.

**Strategy:** We will use the stoichiometric relationship that connects the rate of change of each species. We know that the rate of reaction equals +(1/4) multiplied by the rate of formation of NO₂ (since the coefficient of NO₂ is 4), and it also equals −(1/2) multiplied by the rate of decomposition of N₂O₅ (coefficient is 2).

**Solution, part (a):** We set up the equality:

\[
-\frac{1}{2}\frac{\Delta[\text{N}_2\text{O}_5]}{\Delta t} = +\frac{1}{4}\frac{\Delta[\text{NO}_2]}{\Delta t}
\]

We substitute the known value for the rate of formation of NO₂, which is 0.0080 mol dm⁻³ s⁻¹:

\[
-\frac{1}{2}\frac{\Delta[\text{N}_2\text{O}_5]}{\Delta t} = \frac{1}{4}(0.0080)
\]

We calculate the right-hand side: 0.0080 divided by 4 equals 0.0020. So:

\[
-\frac{1}{2}\frac{\Delta[\text{N}_2\text{O}_5]}{\Delta t} = 0.0020
\]

Now multiply both sides by −2:

\[
\frac{\Delta[\text{N}_2\text{O}_5]}{\Delta t} = -0.0040 \text{ mol dm}^{-3}\text{ s}^{-1}
\]

The negative sign tells us that the concentration of N₂O₅ is decreasing. The rate of decomposition is the absolute value: **0.0040 mol dm⁻³ s⁻¹**.

**Solution, part (b):** The overall rate of reaction is:

\[
\text{Rate} = \frac{1}{4}\frac{\Delta[\text{NO}_2]}{\Delta t} = \frac{0.0080}{4} = 0.0020 \text{ mol dm}^{-3}\text{ s}^{-1}
\]

**Why this makes sense:** For every 2 molecules of N₂O₅ that break apart, 4 molecules of NO₂ are formed. So NO₂ appears twice as fast as N₂O₅ disappears. That is exactly what our numbers show: 0.0080 is twice 0.0040.

## 4. Collision Theory

### What This Concept Is and Why It Matters

Chemical reactions happen when particles interact with each other. But not every interaction leads to a reaction. Collision theory explains what must happen for particles to actually react when they meet. This theory is the foundation for understanding why some reactions are fast, some are slow, and how we can control reaction rates.

### The Three Requirements for a Successful Reaction

For two particles to react, three conditions must all be satisfied at the same time:

**Condition 1: The particles must collide.** This sounds simple, but it is worth stating explicitly. If two particles never come into contact with each other, they cannot react. In a gas or a liquid, particles are constantly moving and bumping into each other — there are billions of collisions every second in a typical sample.

**Condition 2: The particles must have enough energy when they collide.** Not just any collision will do. The particles must approach each other with enough kinetic energy (energy of motion) to break the existing chemical bonds in the reactants. The minimum energy required for this is called the **activation energy**, given the symbol Ea. If two particles collide with less energy than Ea, they simply bounce off each other without reacting — no bonds are broken and no new bonds are formed.

**Condition 3: The particles must collide with the correct orientation.** Even if particles collide with enough energy, they must be positioned in the right way relative to each other. Imagine trying to fit a key into a lock. You need both enough force (energy) and the right alignment (orientation). In chemical terms, the atoms that will form new bonds need to be facing each other when the collision happens. If the wrong side of a molecule hits another particle, nothing happens even if there is plenty of energy.

### Why Most Collisions Do Not Lead to Reaction

In a typical gas at room temperature, each particle collides with others about a billion times per second. Yet most reactions do not happen instantly. The reason is that only a very tiny fraction of all those collisions satisfy both the energy requirement and the orientation requirement. The fraction of collisions that have the correct orientation is called the **steric factor**. For many reactions, the steric factor is much less than 1 — sometimes as low as 10⁻⁶, meaning only one in a million collisions has the right geometry.

## 5. Activation Energy (Ea)

### What This Concept Is and Why It Matters

Activation energy is the minimum kinetic energy that two colliding particles must have in order for their collision to result in a chemical reaction. Think of activation energy as the "entry fee" for a reaction: if the particles do not bring at least this much energy to the collision, no reaction occurs and they simply separate unchanged.

On an energy profile diagram (a graph showing how the energy of the chemical system changes as the reaction progresses), the activation energy is the vertical distance from the energy level of the reactants up to the highest point on the curve — the **transition state** (also called the activated complex). The transition state is the fleeting arrangement of atoms at the very moment bonds are breaking and forming. It exists for an unimaginably short time before collapsing into either products or back into reactants.

The mathematical relationship is:

\[
E_a = E_{\text{transition state}} - E_{\text{reactants}}
\]

### How Activation Energy Affects Reaction Rate

If a reaction has a low activation energy, a large fraction of the colliding particles will have enough energy to react. The reaction will be fast.

If a reaction has a high activation energy, only a tiny fraction of particles — those in the high-energy "tail" of the energy distribution — will have enough energy. The reaction will be slow.

This explains why a mixture of hydrogen gas and oxygen gas can sit at room temperature for years without reacting. The activation energy for the reaction 2H₂ + O₂ → 2H₂O is quite high, so almost no H₂ and O₂ molecules have enough energy when they collide at room temperature. But if you provide a spark — a concentrated burst of energy — you supply enough energy to get the first few molecules over the barrier. Once the reaction starts, it releases a large amount of heat (it is highly exothermic), and that heat activates more molecules, creating a chain reaction. That is why a small spark can trigger an explosion.

### Worked Example 2: Applying Collision Theory to Explain an Observation

**Problem:** Magnesium metal reacts rapidly with hydrochloric acid at room temperature, producing bubbles of hydrogen gas. Copper metal does not react with hydrochloric acid at all under the same conditions, even though both metals are placed in the same acid. Use collision theory and the concept of activation energy to explain this difference.

**Strategy:** We need to consider what must happen for a metal atom to react with a hydrogen ion. The key question is: what is different about the collision between a magnesium atom and H⁺ compared to a collision between a copper atom and H⁺?

**Solution:** For either metal to react, a metal atom must collide with a hydrogen ion from the acid with enough energy to transfer electrons — the metal atom must lose electrons (be oxidized) and the hydrogen ion must gain electrons (be reduced). The activation energy for the reaction between magnesium atoms and H⁺ ions is relatively low. At room temperature, a large fraction of the collisions between magnesium atoms at the metal surface and H⁺ ions in solution have enough energy to overcome this barrier and react. The reaction proceeds rapidly, producing hydrogen gas.

The activation energy for the reaction between copper atoms and H⁺ ions is very high. At room temperature, practically no collisions between copper atoms and H⁺ ions have sufficient energy to reach the transition state. Even though collisions are happening constantly, none of them are successful. Therefore, no reaction is observed.

**Why this makes sense:** Magnesium is a more reactive metal than copper. In chemistry, "more reactive" essentially means "has a lower activation energy for giving away electrons." The collision theory provides the molecular-level explanation for what we observe at the macroscopic level.

### Worked Example 3: Average Rate Calculation from Experimental Data

**Problem:** A student places 0.50 grams of magnesium ribbon into an excess of hydrochloric acid with a concentration of 1.0 mol dm⁻³. The reaction produces 510 cm³ of hydrogen gas over a period of 120 seconds, measured at room temperature and pressure (RTP). At RTP, one mole of any gas occupies 24.0 dm³. Calculate the average rate of reaction in (a) cm³ of hydrogen gas per second, and (b) moles of magnesium consumed per second.

**Strategy:** For part (a), the rate in terms of gas volume is simply the total volume divided by the total time — this is a straightforward average rate. For part (b), we need to convert the volume of hydrogen gas to moles using the molar volume at RTP. Then, since the balanced equation tells us that one mole of magnesium produces one mole of hydrogen gas, the moles of magnesium consumed equal the moles of hydrogen produced.

**Solution, part (a):**

\[
\text{Average rate (volume)} = \frac{\text{total volume of H}_2}{\text{total time}} = \frac{510 \text{ cm}^3}{120 \text{ s}} = 4.25 \text{ cm}^3 \text{ s}^{-1}
\]

**Solution, part (b):** First, we convert the gas volume from cm³ to dm³ because the molar volume is given in dm³. There are 1000 cm³ in 1 dm³:

\[
\text{Volume in dm}^3 = \frac{510}{1000} = 0.510 \text{ dm}^3
\]

Next, we calculate the number of moles of hydrogen gas produced using the molar volume at RTP:

\[
\text{Moles of H}_2 = \frac{\text{volume}}{\text{molar volume}} = \frac{0.510 \text{ dm}^3}{24.0 \text{ dm}^3 \text{ mol}^{-1}} = 0.02125 \text{ mol}
\]

The balanced equation for this reaction is:

\[
\text{Mg}(\text{s}) + 2\text{HCl}(\text{aq}) \rightarrow \text{MgCl}_2(\text{aq}) + \text{H}_2(\text{g})
\]

From the equation, 1 mole of Mg produces 1 mole of H₂. So the number of moles of magnesium consumed is also 0.02125 mol.

Now we divide by the time to get the average rate:

\[
\text{Average rate (moles Mg)} = \frac{0.02125 \text{ mol}}{120 \text{ s}} = 1.77 \times 10^{-4} \text{ mol s}^{-1}
\]

**Why this makes sense:** The rate in moles per second is a very small number because individual molecules are incredibly small. Even though the reaction looks vigorous — bubbling and fizzing — the actual number of moles reacting per second is tiny, on the order of ten-thousandths of a mole.

## Answers

### Answer 1

**(a)** To find the rate of consumption of B, we use the stoichiometric relationship from the balanced equation 2A + B → 3C. The rate of reaction can be expressed as −(1/2)Δ[A]/Δt using A, or as −(1/1)Δ[B]/Δt using B. Setting these equal gives −(1/2)Δ[A]/Δt = −Δ[B]/Δt. Since Δ[A]/Δt = −0.0060 (the negative sign indicates A is being consumed), we have −(1/2)(−0.0060) = −Δ[B]/Δt, which simplifies to 0.0030 = −Δ[B]/Δt, meaning Δ[B]/Δt = −0.0030. So B is consumed at a rate of **0.0030 mol dm⁻³ s⁻¹**. A common mistake is to forget the factor of 1/2 and report 0.0060 for B — but B is consumed half as fast as A because the coefficient of A is 2 and the coefficient of B is 1.

**(b)** Using the same approach, we relate A to C: −(1/2)Δ[A]/Δt = +(1/3)Δ[C]/Δt. Substituting: −(1/2)(−0.0060) = (1/3)Δ[C]/Δt, so 0.0030 = (1/3)Δ[C]/Δt, giving Δ[C]/Δt = 0.0090. C is formed at a rate of **0.0090 mol dm⁻³ s⁻¹**. This is the fastest rate of the three because each reaction event produces 3 molecules of C while consuming only 2 of A and 1 of B.

### Answer 2

The three conditions of collision theory are: (1) The particles must collide — they must physically come into contact. (2) The colliding particles must possess kinetic energy equal to or greater than the activation energy (Ea) — the minimum energy needed to break existing bonds and begin forming new ones. (3) The particles must collide with the correct orientation — the atoms that will form new bonds must be positioned appropriately relative to each other at the moment of impact.

For the third condition, a useful analogy is that of a key and a lock. You can push a key into a keyhole with a lot of force (analogous to having enough energy), but if the key is upside down or sideways (analogous to wrong orientation), it will not turn and the door will not open. Similarly, two molecules can collide with more than enough energy, but if the reactive parts of the molecules are not facing each other, no bonds will break or form and the molecules will simply bounce apart unchanged.

### Answer 3

**Method 1 — Measuring the volume of gas produced:** Carbon dioxide is a gaseous product of this reaction. The chemist can set up the reaction in a conical flask connected by a delivery tube to a gas syringe. As CO₂ is produced, the plunger of the gas syringe moves outward. The chemist records the volume of gas collected at regular time intervals, such as every 30 seconds. The rate of reaction can be calculated from the volume of gas produced per unit time. As the reaction proceeds, the volume of gas increases rapidly at first, then more slowly as the reactants are used up.

**Method 2 — Measuring the loss of mass:** Since carbon dioxide gas escapes from the reaction vessel into the air, the total mass of the apparatus decreases over time. The chemist places the reaction flask containing the marble chips and acid on a digital balance. Cotton wool is placed loosely in the neck of the flask to prevent liquid from splashing out while allowing CO₂ gas to escape. The mass is recorded at regular time intervals. The loss in mass corresponds to the mass of CO₂ that has escaped. The rate can be calculated from the mass loss per unit time. The mass decreases quickly at first and then more gradually.

### Answer 4

**(a)** First, convert the volume from cm³ to dm³: 450 cm³ = 450/1000 = 0.450 dm³. Then use the molar volume to find moles of H₂: moles = volume / molar volume = 0.450 dm³ / 24.0 dm³ mol⁻¹ = **0.01875 mol**.

**(b)** The average rate in moles of H₂ per second is the total moles divided by the total time: 0.01875 mol / 150 s = **1.25 × 10⁻⁴ mol s⁻¹**.

**(c)** From the balanced equation Zn(s) + 2HCl(aq) → ZnCl₂(aq) + H₂(g), one mole of zinc produces one mole of hydrogen gas. Therefore, the moles of zinc consumed are also 0.01875 mol. The mass of zinc consumed is: mass = moles × Mr = 0.01875 mol × 65.4 g mol⁻¹ = 1.22625 g. The average rate in grams of zinc per second is 1.22625 g / 150 s = **0.00818 g s⁻¹** (or 8.18 × 10⁻³ g s⁻¹). Note that the initial mass of zinc was 1.20 g, and 1.226 g was calculated to have reacted — the slight excess (due to rounding in the gas measurement) means essentially all the zinc reacted during these 150 seconds.

### Answer 5

**(a)** Over the first 60 seconds, the concentration of H₂O₂ changes from 0.200 mol dm⁻³ to 0.140 mol dm⁻³. The change in concentration, Δ[H₂O₂], is 0.140 − 0.200 = −0.060 mol dm⁻³. The average rate is −Δ[H₂O₂]/Δt = −(−0.060) / 60 = 0.060 / 60 = **1.00 × 10⁻³ mol dm⁻³ s⁻¹**. The negative sign in the change accounts for the fact that H₂O₂ is being consumed; the rate itself is a positive number.

**(b)** Over the interval from 180 s to 240 s, the concentration changes from 0.072 mol dm⁻³ to 0.052 mol dm⁻³. The change is 0.052 − 0.072 = −0.020 mol dm⁻³. The average rate is −(−0.020) / 60 = 0.020 / 60 = **3.33 × 10⁻⁴ mol dm⁻³ s⁻¹**. This rate is about one-third of the rate during the first 60 seconds.

**(c)** The rate decreases over time because the concentration of hydrogen peroxide decreases as it decomposes. According to collision theory, when the concentration of a reactant is lower, there are fewer H₂O₂ particles per unit volume of solution. This means that H₂O₂ particles collide with each other less frequently. Since the rate of reaction depends on how often particles collide with enough energy and correct orientation, fewer collisions per second means fewer successful collisions per second, and therefore a slower rate.

**(d)** To find the instantaneous rate at t = 120 seconds, you draw a tangent line to the curve at the point where t = 120 s. A tangent is a straight line that just touches the curve at that single point without crossing through it. The gradient (slope) of this tangent line equals Δ[H₂O₂]/Δt at exactly that moment. Because the curve is downward-sloping, the gradient will be a negative number. The instantaneous rate is the absolute value of this gradient (you take the negative of the gradient to get a positive rate). The steeper the tangent, the faster the rate at that moment. Since the curve becomes less steep as time goes on (it flattens out), the tangent at 120 s will be less steep than the tangent at 0 s but steeper than the tangent at 240 s.
