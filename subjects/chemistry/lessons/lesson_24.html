Lesson 24: The Equilibrium Constant $K_c$ — Expression, Magnitude, Q

Syllabus reference: 7.1 — Equilibrium; 17.1 — The Equilibrium Law (HL)

Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to:

  • Write the equilibrium constant expression $K_c$ for any homogeneous reaction from its balanced equation.
  • Explain why solids and pure liquids are omitted from the $K_c$ expression.
  • Interpret the magnitude of $K_c$ ($K_c \gg 1$, $K_c \approx 1$, $K_c \ll 1$) in terms of the equilibrium position.
  • Define the reaction quotient $Q$ and use it to predict the direction a reaction mixture will shift to reach equilibrium.
  • Relate $Q$ and $K_c$: if $Q < K_c$, reaction proceeds forwards; if $Q > K_c$, reaction proceeds backwards.

1. Prior Knowledge Check

Question 1: Write the mathematical expression for an equilibrium constant in terms of concentrations. What does the balanced equation tell you about the exponents?

▸ Show
For $\ce{aA + bB <=> cC + dD}$: $K_c = \frac{[\ce{C}]^c[\ce{D}]^d}{[\ce{A}]^a[\ce{B}]^b}$. Products go in the numerator, reactants in the denominator. The exponents are the stoichiometric coefficients from the balanced equation. Note: unlike rate equations, the exponents in $K_c$ are always the stoichiometric coefficients — $K_c$ is derived from thermodynamics, not kinetics.

Question 2: The equilibrium $\ce{CaCO3(s) <=> CaO(s) + CO2(g)}$ is established in a sealed container. Write the $K_c$ expression. Why are some species omitted?

▸ Show
$K_c = [\ce{CO2}]$. Solids ($\ce{CaCO3}$ and $\ce{CaO}$) are omitted because their "concentration" is essentially constant — it depends on density, not on how much solid is present. The activity of a pure solid or pure liquid is defined as 1. Only gases and aqueous species appear in $K_c$ expressions.

2. The $K_c$ Expression — Rules and Conventions

For a general homogeneous equilibrium: $\ce{aA + bB <=> cC + dD}$ $$K_c = \frac{[\ce{C}]^c_{\text{eq}}[\ce{D}]^d_{\text{eq}}}{[\ce{A}]^a_{\text{eq}}[\ce{B}]^b_{\text{eq}}}$$ All concentrations are equilibrium concentrations in mol dm⁻³. $K_c$ has units of (mol dm⁻³)$^{\Delta n}$ where $\Delta n = (c+d) - (a+b)$ = moles of gaseous/aqueous products minus moles of gaseous/aqueous reactants.

Check-in: Write $K_c$ for $\ce{CH3COOH(l) + C2H5OH(l) <=> CH3COOC2H5(l) + H2O(l)}$. What about units?

▸ Show
All species are pure liquids. In principle, all activities equal 1 and $K_c = 1$ — but this is not practically useful. In reality, for esterification in an organic solvent, we treat them as solutes: $K_c = \frac{[\ce{CH3COOC2H5}][\ce{H2O}]}{[\ce{CH3COOH}][\ce{C2H5OH}]}$. For IB, when all species are in the same liquid phase, they are treated as if in solution and appear in $K_c$. The key point: pure solids are definitely omitted; liquid mixtures (where concentrations vary) are included.

3. Interpreting the Magnitude of $K_c$

The numerical value of $K_c$ tells you about the equilibrium position — the relative amounts of products and reactants at equilibrium:

$K_c$ ValueInterpretationEquilibrium Position
$K_c \gg 1$ (e.g., $10^3$, $10^{10}$)Products dominate; equilibrium lies far rightReaction essentially "goes to completion"
$K_c \approx 1$ (e.g., 0.1 to 10)Significant amounts of both reactants and productsEquilibrium mixture contains all species
$K_c \ll 1$ (e.g., $10^{-3}$, $10^{-10}$)Reactants dominate; equilibrium lies far leftReaction "does not occur" to a significant extent
Figure 2: The $K_c$ spectrum. $K_c$ can range from extremely small to extremely large. A $K_c$ of $10^{30}$ means the reaction is effectively irreversible under those conditions.

Check-in: At 298 K, $K_c = 1.0 \times 10^{-14}$ for $\ce{2H2O(l) <=> H3O+(aq) + OH-(aq)}$. What does this tell you about the extent of water autoionisation?

▸ Show
$K_c = 1.0 \times 10^{-14} \ll 1$ means the equilibrium lies extremely far to the left. At equilibrium, only about $1 \times 10^{-7}$ mol dm⁻³ of $\ce{H3O+}$ and $\ce{OH-}$ are present — negligible compared to the ~55.5 mol dm⁻³ of water. This is why pure water is effectively neutral and non-conductive. The tiny $K_c$ quantifies what "water barely dissociates" means numerically.

4. The Reaction Quotient $Q$

The reaction quotient $Q$ has the same mathematical form as $K_c$, but uses non-equilibrium concentrations — i.e., the concentrations at any arbitrary point, not necessarily at equilibrium.

Reaction quotient: $$Q = \frac{[\ce{C}]^c[\ce{D}]^d}{[\ce{A}]^a[\ce{B}]^b} \quad \text{(using current, non-equilibrium concentrations)}$$ Compare $Q$ to $K_c$:
  • $Q < K_c$: Too few products / too many reactants → reaction proceeds forward (→) to reach equilibrium.
  • $Q = K_c$: System is at equilibrium.
  • $Q > K_c$: Too many products / too few reactants → reaction proceeds backward (←) to reach equilibrium.

Check-in: For $\ce{N2(g) + 3H2(g) <=> 2NH3(g)}$, $K_c = 0.50$ at 400°C. A mixture contains: $[\ce{N2}] = 0.80$, $[\ce{H2}] = 1.2$, $[\ce{NH3}] = 0.30$ mol dm⁻³. Is the mixture at equilibrium? If not, which way will it shift?

▸ Show
$Q = [\ce{NH3}]^2 / ([\ce{N2}][\ce{H2}]^3) = (0.30)^2 / (0.80 \times (1.2)^3) = 0.090 / (0.80 \times 1.728) = 0.090 / 1.382 = 0.065$. Since $Q (0.065) < K_c (0.50)$, the reaction proceeds forward (→), producing more NH₃ until $Q = K_c = 0.50$.

5. Worked Examples

Example 1 — Writing $K_c$ Expressions:

Write the $K_c$ expression and state its units for each reaction:
(a) $\ce{2SO2(g) + O2(g) <=> 2SO3(g)}$
(b) $\ce{NH4HS(s) <=> NH3(g) + H2S(g)}$
(c) $\ce{CH3COOH(aq) + H2O(l) <=> CH3COO-(aq) + H3O+(aq)}$

Solution:
(a) $K_c = \dfrac{[\ce{SO3}]^2}{[\ce{SO2}]^2[\ce{O2}]}$. Δn = 2 − 3 = −1. Units: (mol dm⁻³)⁻¹ = dm³ mol⁻¹.
(b) $K_c = [\ce{NH3}][\ce{H2S}]$. Solid NH₄HS omitted. Δn = 2 − 0 = 2. Units: mol² dm⁻⁶.
(c) $K_c = \dfrac{[\ce{CH3COO-}][\ce{H3O+}]}{[\ce{CH3COOH}]}$. Water (pure liquid, in vast excess) is omitted. Δn = 2 − 1 = 1. Units: mol dm⁻³.

⚠️ Common wrong approach: Including H₂O(l) in the $K_c$ expression for aqueous equilibria. The concentration of water (~55.5 mol dm⁻³) is essentially constant and is absorbed into $K_c$. For acid dissociation: $K_a = [\ce{H3O+}][\ce{A-}]/[\ce{HA}]$, with no [H₂O].
Example 2 — Using $Q$ to Predict Direction:

For $\ce{CO(g) + H2O(g) <=> CO2(g) + H2(g)}$, $K_c = 4.06$ at 500°C. A 2.0 dm³ flask contains: 0.40 mol CO, 0.60 mol H₂O, 0.80 mol CO₂, and 0.20 mol H₂. Predict the direction of net reaction.

Solution: First, calculate concentrations: [CO] = 0.20, [H₂O] = 0.30, [CO₂] = 0.40, [H₂] = 0.10 mol dm⁻³.
$Q = ([\ce{CO2}][\ce{H2}]) / ([\ce{CO}][\ce{H2O}]) = (0.40 \times 0.10) / (0.20 \times 0.30) = 0.040/0.060 = 0.667$.
Since $Q (0.667) < K_c (4.06)$, the reaction proceeds forward (→), consuming CO and H₂O, producing CO₂ and H₂.

⚠️ Common wrong approach: Forgetting to divide moles by volume to get concentrations. If you use moles directly, $Q$ = (0.80×0.20)/(0.40×0.60) = 0.667 — here it gives the same answer because Δn = 0 (same number of moles on both sides). But this is only true when Δn = 0! Always convert to concentrations to be safe.
Example 3 — $K_c$ Magnitude and Reaction Feasibility:

At 298 K, $K_c = 2.0 \times 10^{31}$ for $\ce{2H2(g) + O2(g) <=> 2H2O(g)}$. Explain what this value means practically, and why hydrogen and oxygen can coexist at room temperature despite this enormous $K_c$.

Solution: $K_c = 2.0 \times 10^{31} \gg 1$ means the equilibrium lies extremely far to the right — at equilibrium, the reaction mixture would contain almost entirely H₂O with negligible H₂ and O₂. However, at room temperature, the activation energy is very high, so the rate is effectively zero. Thermodynamics ($K_c$) tells you where equilibrium lies; kinetics ($E_a$) tells you how fast you get there. H₂ and O₂ can coexist indefinitely at room temperature because, despite the enormous thermodynamic driving force, the kinetic barrier prevents reaction. A spark provides the activation energy, and the mixture explodes.

⚠️ Common wrong approach: "A large $K_c$ means the reaction is fast." No — $K_c$ is thermodynamic, not kinetic. A reaction can have $K_c \approx 10^{50}$ and still be immeasurably slow if $E_a$ is high. Thermodynamics and kinetics are independent quantities.

⚠️ Common Mistakes to Avoid

  1. "$K_c$ includes all species in the balanced equation." Solids and pure liquids are omitted. Only gases and aqueous species appear. A common trap: including $\ce{H2O(l)}$ in $K_c$ for aqueous acid dissociation.
  2. "$K_c$ has no units." $K_c$ generally has units unless $\Delta n = 0$. At IB, you must state the units of $K_c$ when asked. Derive them from the expression: units of (mol dm⁻³)$^{\Delta n}$.
  3. "A large $K_c$ means the reaction is fast." $K_c$ says nothing about rate. It is purely thermodynamic. A reaction with $K_c = 10^{80}$ could be immeasurably slow if $E_a$ is high.
  4. "$K_c$ changes when you add more reactant." $K_c$ is constant at a given temperature. Adding reactant shifts the position of equilibrium (changing individual concentrations) but does not change the ratio $K_c$. Only temperature changes $K_c$.
  5. "$Q$ and $K_c$ are the same thing." $Q$ is calculated using any concentrations (not necessarily equilibrium). $K_c$ uses equilibrium concentrations only. They have the same algebraic form but different meanings.

Question 3: $K_c = 0.36$ for $\ce{PCl5(g) <=> PCl3(g) + Cl2(g)}$ at 250°C. A 1.0 dm³ flask contains 0.50 mol PCl₅, 0.30 mol PCl₃, and 0.40 mol Cl₂. Determine $Q$ and predict the direction of net reaction.

Question 4: $K_c = 170$ for $\ce{2HI(g) <=> H2(g) + I2(g)}$ at 500 K. Interpret the magnitude of $K_c$. At equilibrium, which species predominate?

Question 5: Explain why $K_c$ for $\ce{CaCO3(s) <=> CaO(s) + CO2(g)}$ does not include [CaCO₃] or [CaO], but does include [CO₂]. What is the practical meaning of this $K_c$?

Question 6 (IB-style): At 700 K, $K_c = 54$ for $\ce{H2(g) + I2(g) <=> 2HI(g)}$. A student mixes 0.80 mol H₂, 0.20 mol I₂, and 1.60 mol HI in a 2.0 dm³ flask.
(a) Calculate $Q$.
(b) State the direction of net reaction with reasoning.
(c) Explain why $K_c$ is dimensionless for this reaction.

Answer 1: B. $K_c = [\ce{C}]^2/([\ce{A}]^2[\ce{B}])$. The exponents are the stoichiometric coefficients. Option D incorrectly places coefficients in the denominator.

Answer 2: (a) $K_c = [\ce{N2O4}]/[\ce{NO2}]^2$. Δn = 1−2 = −1, units: dm³ mol⁻¹. (b) $K_c = [\ce{CO2}]^3/[\ce{CO}]^3$. Solids omitted. Δn = 3−3 = 0, dimensionless. (c) $K_c = [\ce{H2O}][\ce{CO2}]$. Solids omitted. Δn = 2−0 = 2, units: mol² dm⁻⁶.

Answer 3: [PCl₅] = 0.50, [PCl₃] = 0.30, [Cl₂] = 0.40 mol dm⁻³. $Q = (0.30\times0.40)/0.50 = 0.24$. $Q (0.24) < K_c (0.36)$ → net reaction proceeds forward (→), producing more PCl₃ and Cl₂.

Answer 4: $K_c = 170$ is moderately large ($K_c > 1$), meaning products dominate at equilibrium — H₂ and I₂ are favoured. However, $K_c$ is not so large that HI is negligible. At equilibrium, the mixture contains all three species with H₂ and I₂ predominating. The equilibrium lies to the right.

Answer 5: Solids have constant "concentration" (activity = 1) — adding more CaCO₃ does not change its effective concentration, which depends only on density. $K_c = [\ce{CO2}]$ means that at a given temperature, there is a fixed equilibrium pressure of CO₂ above the solids, regardless of how much CaCO₃ or CaO is present. If [CO₂] exceeds this value, CaO reacts to form CaCO₃; if it falls below, CaCO₃ decomposes.

Answer 6: (a) [H₂] = 0.40, [I₂] = 0.10, [HI] = 0.80 mol dm⁻³. $Q = (0.80)^2/(0.40\times0.10) = 0.64/0.04 = 16$. (b) $Q (16) < K_c (54)$ → net reaction forward (→), producing more HI. (c) Δn = 2 − (1+1) = 0. Same number of moles on both sides → units cancel → $K_c$ is dimensionless.

7. Key Terms

TermDefinition
Equilibrium constant ($K_c$)The ratio of product concentrations to reactant concentrations at equilibrium, each raised to its stoichiometric coefficient
$K_c$ expressionThe algebraic form: $K_c = [\ce{C}]^c[\ce{D}]^d / ([\ce{A}]^a[\ce{B}]^b)$ for $\ce{aA + bB <=> cC + dD}$
Homogeneous equilibriumAn equilibrium where all species are in the same phase (all gases, or all aqueous)
Heterogeneous equilibriumAn equilibrium involving species in different phases (e.g., solid + gas)
Reaction quotient ($Q$)The same ratio as $K_c$ but calculated using non-equilibrium concentrations; used to predict direction of shift
ΔnThe change in moles of gas (or aqueous species): (moles products) − (moles reactants); determines units of $K_c$
ActivityThe effective concentration of a species; for pure solids and liquids, activity = 1

8. Exam Tips

Writing $K_c$ Expressions: The most common error is including solids or pure liquids. Remember: only (g) and (aq). Write the expression first, then determine units: if Δn = 0, $K_c$ is dimensionless. If Δn ≠ 0, units are (mol dm⁻³)^Δn. State units explicitly — at least one mark depends on it.
$Q$ vs. $K_c$ Comparison: Always state explicitly: "$Q < K_c$, therefore the reaction proceeds forwards" (or vice versa). Do not just write the numerical comparison — explain the direction. A complete one-mark answer: "$Q = 0.24$, which is less than $K_c = 0.36$, so the net reaction proceeds to the right."
Paper 1 Trap: A question may give two different $K_c$ values at two temperatures and ask what this tells you. If $K_c$ decreases as $T$ increases, the forward reaction is exothermic (ΔH < 0). If $K_c$ increases as $T$ increases, the forward reaction is endothermic (ΔH > 0). This is an application of Le Chatelier's principle — temperature is the only perturbation that changes $K_c$ itself.
