Lesson 12: Metallic Bonding, Giant Covalent, σ/π Bonds & Hybridisation (HL)

Syllabus reference: 4.5/14.1/14.2 — Metallic bonding, giant covalent structures, sigma/pi bonds, hybridisation

Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to:

  • Describe metallic bonding as the electrostatic attraction between a lattice of cations and a sea of delocalised electrons.
  • Explain the properties of metals (conductivity, malleability, melting points) using the metallic bonding model.
  • Compare the structures and properties of diamond, graphite, graphene, and silicon dioxide as giant covalent substances.
  • Distinguish between sigma (σ) and pi (π) bonds in terms of orbital overlap and electron density distribution.
  • Determine the hybridisation state (sp, sp², sp³) of atoms in molecules and relate it to molecular shape.

1. Prior Knowledge Check

Question 1: What is the key difference between metallic, ionic, and covalent bonding?

▸ Show
Metallic: electrostatic attraction between metal cations and a sea of delocalised valence electrons. Ionic: electrostatic attraction between discrete cations and anions (electron transfer). Covalent: electrostatic attraction between a shared pair of electrons and two nuclei (localised electron sharing).

Question 2: What is an allotrope? Give an example.

▸ Show
Allotropes are different structural forms of the same element in the same physical state. Example: carbon exists as diamond (sp³, tetrahedral network), graphite (sp², layered), graphene (single layer of graphite), and fullerenes (e.g., C₆₀). Each has different properties due to different bonding arrangements.

Question 3: What does the term "delocalised electrons" mean?

▸ Show
Delocalised electrons are not confined to a single bond or atom — they are spread over multiple atoms. In metals, valence electrons are delocalised throughout the entire lattice. In graphite and the carbonate ion ($\ce{CO3^{2-}}$), electrons are delocalised across multiple atoms.

2. Metallic Bonding — The Electron Sea Model

2.1 The Model

In a metal, atoms are packed in a regular lattice. The valence electrons detach from individual atoms and form a "sea" of delocalised electrons that moves freely throughout the lattice. The positive metal cations are held together by their electrostatic attraction to this electron sea.

Electrical conductivity: Delocalised e⁻ move freely through the lattice • Malleability/ductility: Layers of cations slide past each other; e⁻ sea re-forms • Thermal conductivity: Delocalised e⁻ transfer kinetic energy rapidly
Figure 1: The metallic bonding model. Positive metal cations (blue) are held in a regular lattice by electrostatic attraction to a "sea" of delocalised valence electrons (orange dots). This simple model explains conductivity, malleability, and ductility.

2.2 Factors Affecting Metallic Bond Strength

The strength of metallic bonding (and thus melting point) depends on:

  1. Number of delocalised valence electrons — more e⁻ per atom = stronger bonding. Group 1 (1 e⁻) → low m.p.; Group 3 (3 e⁻) → higher m.p.
  2. Size of the cation — smaller cations hold the electron sea more tightly (greater charge density).
  3. Charge of the cation — higher charge = stronger attraction to the electron sea.

This explains why Mg (2 delocalised e⁻, m.p. 650°C) has a higher melting point than Na (1 delocalised e⁻, m.p. 98°C) — despite both being Period 3 metals. Similarly, transition metals have high melting points because they contribute d-electrons to the delocalised sea.

Check-in: Explain why aluminium (Group 13, m.p. 660°C) is a stronger metal than sodium (Group 1, m.p. 98°C).

▸ Show
Aluminium contributes 3 delocalised electrons per atom to the electron sea, compared to sodium's 1. This creates a much stronger electrostatic attraction between Al³⁺ cations and the electron sea. Additionally, Al³⁺ has a smaller ionic radius and higher charge density than Na⁺, further strengthening the metallic bonds. The result: Al has a melting point over 550°C higher than Na and is mechanically much stronger.

3. Giant Covalent Structures — Carbon Allotropes and SiO₂

3.1 Diamond — sp³ Tetrahedral Network

Diamond: each carbon atom is sp³ hybridised and bonded tetrahedrally to four other carbon atoms by strong covalent σ bonds. This forms a rigid, three-dimensional network.

  • Extremely hard — every atom is locked in place by strong covalent bonds in all directions.
  • Very high melting point (~3550°C) — must break strong C–C covalent bonds.
  • Non-conductor of electricity — all electrons are localised in σ bonds; no delocalised electrons.
  • Insoluble in any solvent — covalent bonds throughout.

3.2 Graphite — sp² Layered Structure

Graphite: each carbon is sp² hybridised, bonded to three other carbons in a hexagonal planar layer. The fourth valence electron (in a p orbital) forms a delocalised π bond across the entire layer.

Figure 2: Graphite's layered structure. Strong covalent σ bonds within each hexagonal layer, delocalised π electrons above and below (enabling conductivity), and weak London forces between layers (enabling lubricating properties).

3.3 Graphene — A Single Layer of Graphite

Graphene is a single atom-thick sheet of sp² carbon atoms in a hexagonal lattice:

  • 200× stronger than steel — yet incredibly light.
  • Excellent conductor — delocalised π electrons move freely across the sheet.
  • Transparent — absorbs only ~2.3% of visible light.
  • High thermal conductivity.

3.4 Silicon Dioxide ($\ce{SiO2}$)

SiO₂ (quartz) is a giant covalent network with each Si atom bonded tetrahedrally to four O atoms, and each O bridging two Si atoms. It is analogous to diamond but with O bridges. Properties: very hard, very high m.p. (~1710°C), non-conductor. Note: SiO₂ is the empirical formula — there are no discrete SiO₂ molecules.

Check-in: Silicon is in Group 14, directly below carbon. Why doesn't silicon form a graphite-like allotrope?

▸ Show
Silicon's 3p orbitals are more diffuse and overlap less effectively side-to-side than carbon's 2p orbitals. This makes Si=Si π bonds much weaker than C=C π bonds. Consequently, silicon cannot form stable sp² networks with delocalised π bonds — it strongly prefers sp³ tetrahedral bonding (as in the diamond structure). The same reasoning explains why $\ce{CO2}$ is a discrete molecule (O=C=O) while $\ce{SiO2}$ is a giant network — silicon avoids double bonds with oxygen in favour of four single bonds.

4. Sigma (σ) and Pi (π) Bonds (HL)

4.1 Sigma Bonds — Head-on Overlap

A sigma (σ) bond is formed by the head-on (end-on) overlap of atomic orbitals along the internuclear axis. The electron density is concentrated symmetrically about the bond axis. Sigma bonds allow free rotation about the bond.

Types of σ overlap: s–s, s–p, p–p (end-on), and hybrid–hybrid, hybrid–s, hybrid–p.

4.2 Pi Bonds — Sideways Overlap

A pi (π) bond is formed by the sideways overlap of parallel p orbitals above and below the internuclear axis. The electron density is concentrated in two lobes, one above and one below the bond axis. Pi bonds restrict rotation — rotating would break the sideways overlap.

Figure 3: Sigma bonds (left) form by head-on orbital overlap with electron density concentrated along the bond axis. Pi bonds (right) form by sideways overlap of parallel p orbitals, with electron density concentrated above and below the bond axis. A double bond = 1 σ + 1 π. A triple bond = 1 σ + 2 π.

4.3 Counting σ and π Bonds

  • Single bond: 1 σ bond.
  • Double bond: 1 σ + 1 π bond.
  • Triple bond: 1 σ + 2 π bonds.

Check-in: How many σ and π bonds are in: (a) $\ce{H2C=CH2}$ (ethene), (b) $\ce{HC#CH}$ (ethyne), (c) $\ce{CO2}$?

▸ Show
(a) Ethene: 5 σ bonds (4 C–H + 1 C–C σ) + 1 π bond (C=C). Total = 5σ + 1π. (b) Ethyne: 3 σ bonds (2 C–H + 1 C–C σ) + 2 π bonds (C≡C). Total = 3σ + 2π. (c) CO₂: 2 σ bonds (one for each C=O) + 2 π bonds (one for each C=O). Total = 2σ + 2π.

5. Hybridisation (HL)

5.1 Why Hybridise?

Atomic orbitals (s, p, d) do not directly explain the shapes predicted by VSEPR. For example, carbon's ground state is $2s^2 2p^2$, which suggests it should form two bonds at 90° — but methane is tetrahedral (109.5°) with four identical bonds. Hybridisation resolves this: atomic orbitals mix (combine) to form new, equivalent hybrid orbitals that point in the directions predicted by VSEPR.

How to determine hybridisation: Count the electron domains (bonding + lone pairs) around the atom. The number of domains = the number of hybrid orbitals needed.

2 domains → sp   (e.g., Be in BeCl₂, C in CO₂, C in HC≡CH)
3 domains → sp²  (e.g., B in BF₃, C in H₂C=CH₂, N in NO₃⁻)
4 domains → sp³  (e.g., C in CH₄, N in NH₃, O in H₂O)

Check-in: Determine the hybridisation of: (a) the carbon in $\ce{CH2O}$ (methanal), (b) the nitrogen in $\ce{NH3}$, (c) the central carbon in $\ce{CH3COOH}$.

▸ Show
(a) In CH₂O, C is bonded to two H atoms and one O (double bond). That's 3 electron domains → sp². Trigonal planar, 120°. (b) N in NH₃: 3 bonding + 1 lone pair = 4 domains → sp³. (c) CH₃COOH has two carbon atoms: the CH₃ carbon has 4 σ bonds (3 C–H + 1 C–C) → sp³. The COOH carbon has 3 domains (C=O double bond + C–O single bond + C–C single bond) → sp².

5.3 Hybridisation and Bonding in Ethene and Ethyne

Ethene ($\ce{H2C=CH2}$): Each C is sp² hybridised. The three sp² hybrids form three σ bonds (two C–H and one C–C). The remaining unhybridised p orbital on each C overlaps sideways to form the π bond. The molecule is planar with 120° bond angles.

Ethyne ($\ce{HC#CH}$): Each C is sp hybridised. The two sp hybrids form two σ bonds (one C–H and one C–C). The two remaining unhybridised p orbitals on each C overlap sideways to form two π bonds (perpendicular to each other). The molecule is linear with 180° bond angles.

Check-in: Why does the C=C double bond in ethene prevent rotation, while the C–C single bond in ethane allows free rotation?

▸ Show
The C=C double bond consists of one σ bond and one π bond. The π bond is formed by sideways overlap of parallel p orbitals. If one CH₂ group rotates relative to the other, the p orbitals would no longer be parallel and the π bond would break. Breaking a π bond requires significant energy (~264 kJ mol⁻¹), so rotation is restricted at room temperature. The C–C single bond in ethane is a pure σ bond — head-on overlap allows rotation without breaking the bond.

6. Worked Examples

Example 1 — Explaining Metallic Properties:

Explain why sodium is malleable (can be hammered into sheets) but sodium chloride shatters when struck.

In sodium metal, the Na⁺ cations are embedded in a delocalised sea of electrons. When the metal is deformed, layers of cations slide past each other, but the electron sea immediately re-forms around the new arrangement — the metallic bonds are non-directional. In NaCl, the Na⁺ and Cl⁻ ions are locked in specific positions. When the crystal is struck, a layer shifts and like charges (Na⁺ next to Na⁺, Cl⁻ next to Cl⁻) align, causing strong repulsion that shatters the crystal. Ionic bonds are directional in the sense that the ordered lattice must be maintained.
Example 2 — σ/π Bond Counting in Organic Molecules:

For $\ce{CH2=CH-CH=CH2}$ (buta-1,3-diene), determine: (a) the total number of σ bonds, (b) the total number of π bonds, (c) the hybridisation of each carbon atom.

Structure: $\ce{H2C=CH-CH=CH2}$. Carbons numbered C1–C4.
(a) σ bonds: Each C–H = 1 σ (6 total). C1=C2: 1 σ. C2–C3: 1 σ. C3=C4: 1 σ. Total = 6 + 3 = 9 σ bonds.
(b) π bonds: C1=C2: 1 π. C3=C4: 1 π. Total = 2 π bonds.
(c) Hybridisation: C1: 3 domains (2 C–H σ + 1 C=C σ) → sp². C2: 3 domains (1 C–H σ + C1=C2 σ + C2–C3 σ) → sp². C3: 3 domains → sp². C4: 3 domains → sp². All carbons are sp² hybridised. The molecule is fully conjugated — the π system is delocalised across all four carbons.

7. Common Misconceptions

⚠️ Common Mistakes to Avoid

  1. Thinking metallic bonding is just "ions in a sea." Be precise: it's the electrostatic attraction between positive cations and delocalised valence electrons. The electrons are not fixed to any one atom.
  2. Confusing σ and π bonds. A double bond is NOT two identical bonds — it's 1 σ + 1 π. The σ bond forms first and is stronger. The π bond is weaker and more reactive. All single bonds are σ. The first bond of any multiple bond is always σ.
  3. Thinking graphite conducts electricity because of free ions. There are no ions in graphite. It conducts because of delocalised π electrons that can move freely along the layers.
  4. Applying hybridisation to isolated atoms. Hybridisation is a bonding model — atoms hybridise only when forming bonds. An isolated carbon atom is not sp³ hybridised; it becomes sp³ hybridised as it forms four bonds in methane.
  5. Forgetting that lone pairs count as electron domains for hybridisation. In NH₃, N has 3 bonding pairs + 1 lone pair = 4 domains → sp³ hybridised. The lone pair occupies an sp³ hybrid orbital. Similarly, H₂O: O is sp³ hybridised with 2 bonding + 2 lone pairs.

8. End-of-Lesson Practice

Question 1: Explain why magnesium is a better electrical conductor than sodium, using the metallic bonding model.

Question 2: Silicon dioxide ($\ce{SiO2}$) has a very high melting point. Explain in terms of its structure and bonding, and contrast with $\ce{CO2}$.

Question 3: State the number of σ and π bonds in: (a) $\ce{N2}$ (nitrogen gas), (b) $\ce{H2CO}$ (methanal), (c) $\ce{C2H4}$ (ethene).

Question 4: Determine the hybridisation of the underlined atom: (a) $\ce{\underline{C}H4}$, (b) $\ce{\underline{B}F3}$, (c) the carbonyl carbon in $\ce{CH3\underline{C}OCH3}$.

Question 5: Graphene is described as the "strongest material ever measured." Explain this property in terms of its bonding and structure.

Question 6 (HL synthesis): For the molecule $\ce{H2C=C=CH2}$ (allene): (a) draw the Lewis structure, (b) determine the hybridisation of each carbon, (c) state the total number of σ and π bonds, (d) explain why all four H atoms are NOT in the same plane.

Answer 1: Magnesium contributes 2 delocalised electrons per atom to the electron sea, compared to sodium's 1. This gives a higher electron density in the conduction band. Additionally, Mg²⁺ cations have a higher charge density (+2 charge, smaller radius) than Na⁺, creating a stronger electrostatic attraction that holds the lattice more tightly — but the delocalised electrons are still mobile enough for good conductivity. Magnesium has both higher melting point and good conductivity.

Answer 2: $\ce{SiO2}$ is a giant covalent network: each Si is tetrahedrally bonded to four O atoms, and each O bridges two Si atoms. Melting requires breaking strong Si–O covalent bonds throughout the lattice (bond energy ~466 kJ mol⁻¹), giving a m.p. of ~1710°C. $\ce{CO2}$ is a discrete molecular substance (O=C=O) — molecules are held together only by weak London forces, so it sublimes at −78°C. The difference arises because carbon forms stable π bonds (C=O double bonds), while silicon avoids π bonding, preferring four single bonds in a network.

Answer 3: (a) $\ce{N2}$: N≡N → 1 σ + 2 π bonds. (b) $\ce{H2CO}$: 3 σ bonds (2 C–H + 1 C–O σ) + 1 π bond (C=O). Total = 3σ + 1π. (c) $\ce{C2H4}$ (ethene): 5 σ bonds (4 C–H + 1 C–C σ) + 1 π bond (C=C). Total = 5σ + 1π.

Answer 4: (a) C in CH₄: 4 domains → sp³. (b) B in BF₃: 3 domains → sp². (c) The carbonyl carbon in CH₃COCH₃ (propanone/acetone): 3 domains (C=O double bond + 2 C–C single bonds) → sp². Note: the CH₃ carbons are sp³.

Answer 5: Graphene is a single layer of sp²-hybridised carbon atoms in a hexagonal lattice. Each C–C bond is a strong covalent σ bond. The absence of defects in a perfect sheet means any applied force is distributed across an enormous number of these strong bonds. Additionally, the delocalised π electron system (one π electron per carbon atom, delocalised across the entire sheet) provides extra stabilisation. The 2D nature means there are no weak interlayer forces to shear — unlike graphite, graphene cannot fail by layer slippage.

Answer 6: (a) $\ce{H2C=C=CH2}$: central C has two double bonds. Total valence electrons: 3×C(4) + 4×H(1) = 16. (b) C1 (left): 3 domains (2 C–H + 1 C=C) → sp². C2 (central): 2 domains (two double bonds) → sp. C3 (right): 3 domains → sp². (c) σ bonds: 4 C–H + 2 C–C σ = 6. π bonds: 2 (one for each C=C). Total = 6σ + 2π. (d) The central sp carbon has two π bonds that are perpendicular to each other (using the two unhybridised p orbitals: p_y and p_z). The C1 end uses one of these p orbitals for its π bond, and the C3 end uses the other. This forces the two CH₂ planes to be orthogonal (90° to each other) — the molecule is non-planar overall. This is a classic stereochemical feature of allenes.

10. Exam Tips

Paper 1 — σ/π Bond Counting: The fastest method: every single bond = 1 σ. Every additional bond in a multiple bond = 1 π. So a double bond = 1 σ + 1 π; a triple bond = 1 σ + 2 π. Count single bonds first, then add π from multiples. For ethene ($\ce{C2H4}$): 5 σ bonds + 1 π bond. For ethyne ($\ce{C2H2}$): 3 σ bonds + 2 π bonds.
Paper 2 — Hybridisation Questions: Always link hybridisation to electron domain count. State: "X has N electron domains (M bonding pairs + L lone pairs), therefore it is spᴺ⁻¹ hybridised." For example: "The oxygen in H₂O has 4 electron domains (2 bonding + 2 lone pairs), therefore it is sp³ hybridised." The IB marking scheme rewards explicit connection between domain count and hybridisation.
Giant Covalent vs. Molecular — The CO₂/SiO₂ Trap: This is one of the most tested comparisons in IB Chemistry. CO₂ is a linear molecular gas (sublimes at −78°C); SiO₂ is a giant covalent solid (m.p. 1710°C). The key reason: carbon forms stable π bonds (favouring C=O double bonds), while silicon's larger size and more diffuse p orbitals make Si=O π bonds weak, so it forms four Si–O single bonds in a network instead. NEVER write SiO₂ as O=Si=O — it doesn't exist as discrete molecules.
