# Lesson 32: Oxidation Numbers and Identifying Redox Reactions

## What You'll Learn

In this lesson, you will learn what a redox reaction is — one of the most important ideas in chemistry. You will learn how to assign oxidation numbers to atoms in any molecule or ion. You will learn how to use those oxidation numbers to tell whether a reaction is a redox reaction. You will also learn to identify which substance is being oxidised, which is being reduced, and what the words "oxidising agent" and "reducing agent" mean.

---

## 1. What Are Oxidation and Reduction? The Electron Definition

This concept tells us what happens to electrons when chemical reactions occur. Some atoms lose electrons, and some atoms gain electrons. These two processes always happen together — you can never have one without the other. The combined process is called **redox**, which is short for **reduction and oxidation**.

Let us define each process carefully.

**Oxidation** is the process in which a chemical species (an atom, an ion, or a molecule) loses one or more electrons. A helpful memory phrase is **OIL**: Oxidation Is Loss of electrons.

**Reduction** is the process in which a chemical species gains one or more electrons. The companion memory phrase is **RIG**: Reduction Is Gain of electrons.

Together: **OIL RIG** — Oxidation Is Loss, Reduction Is Gain.

It is important to understand: if one species loses electrons, some other species must gain those electrons. Electrons do not simply disappear. Every oxidation is matched by a reduction. That is why we use the word "redox" — the two halves are inseparable.

Let us look at a concrete example. Imagine we take a piece of zinc metal and place it into a solution that contains dissolved copper(II) ions. (Copper(II) ions are written as Cu²⁺; the "2+" means the copper atom has lost two electrons and therefore has a positive charge of 2.) A reaction occurs:

$$\ce{Zn(s) + Cu^{2+}(aq) -> Zn^{2+}(aq) + Cu(s)}$$

In this reaction, the zinc atom (Zn) starts as a neutral metal with no charge. It ends up as Zn²⁺, meaning it has lost two electrons. Therefore the zinc has been **oxidised**. At the same time, each copper(II) ion (Cu²⁺) starts with a charge of 2+ and ends up as a neutral copper atom (Cu). To go from 2+ to 0, the copper must gain two electrons. Therefore the copper has been **reduced**.

**Common misconception:** Students sometimes think oxidation means "adding oxygen." That is true in some specific contexts (for example, when magnesium burns in air to form magnesium oxide), but the deeper, more general definition is about losing electrons. The electron definition covers all redox reactions, including those that have nothing to do with oxygen.

## 2. Oxidation Numbers — A Bookkeeping System for Electrons

An **oxidation number** (also called an **oxidation state**) is a number assigned to each atom in a compound or ion. It tells us, in a bookkeeping sense, how many electrons that atom has gained or lost compared to its state as a neutral, uncombined element. Oxidation numbers are not real charges in most cases — they are a tool to track where electrons go during a reaction.

Think of oxidation numbers like a scorecard. Before the reaction, every atom has a certain number. After the reaction, we check the numbers again. If the number goes up, that atom lost electrons (oxidation). If the number goes down, that atom gained electrons (reduction).

### The Rules for Assigning Oxidation Numbers

These rules must be applied **in the order given**. When you are assigning oxidation numbers to the atoms in a compound, start with Rule 1. If Rule 1 does not tell you the answer for a particular atom, move to Rule 2, then Rule 3, and so on. The last rule (Rule 6) is used to find an unknown oxidation number after all the known ones have been assigned.

**Rule 1: Any atom in its elemental form has oxidation number 0.**

An "elemental form" means the pure element, not combined with any other element. This includes metals (like zinc, written as Zn), non-metal molecules (like oxygen gas, written as O₂), and any other form where the atom is alone. Examples: every atom in Na, Fe, O₂, Cl₂, S₈, and P₄ has oxidation number 0.

**Rule 2: For a simple monatomic ion, the oxidation number equals the charge on the ion.**

A "monatomic ion" means an ion made from a single atom. Examples: Na⁺ has oxidation number +1. Cl⁻ has oxidation number −1. Mg²⁺ has oxidation number +2. O²⁻ has oxidation number −2.

**Rule 3: In most compounds, hydrogen has oxidation number +1.**

The exception is when hydrogen is bonded to a metal. In those cases (called metal hydrides, such as NaH), hydrogen has oxidation number −1.

**Rule 4: In most compounds, oxygen has oxidation number −2.**

There are two important exceptions. In peroxides (compounds containing an O–O single bond, such as hydrogen peroxide, H₂O₂), oxygen has oxidation number −1. In the compound OF₂ (oxygen difluoride), oxygen has oxidation number +2 because fluorine is more electronegative.

**Rule 5: Fluorine always has oxidation number −1 in all its compounds.**

Fluorine is the most electronegative element, meaning it always pulls electrons toward itself. No exceptions.

**Rule 6: The sum of all oxidation numbers in a species equals the overall charge of that species.**

For a neutral molecule, the sum must be 0. For a polyatomic ion (an ion made of several atoms bonded together), the sum must equal the charge on the ion. For example, in the sulfate ion SO₄²⁻, the sum of oxidation numbers (S + four O atoms) must equal −2.

## 3. How to Assign Oxidation Numbers — Step-by-Step Examples

Each worked example below follows the rules in order. Read each step slowly and make sure you understand why each number is assigned before moving to the next step.

<div class="worked">
<strong>Worked Example 1: Find the oxidation number of manganese in KMnO₄.</strong>

The compound KMnO₄ is potassium permanganate. It is a neutral compound, so the sum of oxidation numbers must be 0.

**Step 1:** Look at potassium (K). Potassium is in Group 1 of the periodic table. In compounds, Group 1 metals always have oxidation number +1. This follows from Rule 2 (K forms K⁺ ions). So K = +1.

**Step 2:** Look at oxygen (O). KMnO₄ is not a peroxide, and it does not contain fluorine bonded to oxygen. So we use Rule 4: oxygen = −2. There are four oxygen atoms, each at −2.

**Step 3:** The oxidation number of manganese (Mn) is unknown. Let us call it x. Because the compound is neutral (Rule 6), the sum must be zero:

$$(+1) + x + 4(-2) = 0$$
$$+1 + x - 8 = 0$$
$$x - 7 = 0$$
$$x = +7$$

**Answer:** The oxidation number of Mn in KMnO₄ is +7.

**Why this makes sense:** Potassium is +1, each oxygen is −2 (total −8 from oxygen), so manganese must be +7 to make the overall charge zero: +1 + 7 + (−8) = 0. This is a high positive oxidation number, which tells you manganese has lost a lot of electron density in this compound.
</div>

<div class="worked">
<strong>Worked Example 2: Find the oxidation number of chromium in Cr₂O₇²⁻.</strong>

This is the dichromate(VI) ion. It is a polyatomic ion with an overall charge of −2. This means the sum of all oxidation numbers must equal −2 (Rule 6).

**Step 1:** Oxygen is not a peroxide here, so Rule 4 applies: each O = −2. There are seven oxygen atoms.

**Step 2:** Chromium (Cr) is the unknown. Let us call the oxidation number of each Cr atom x. There are two chromium atoms.

**Step 3:** Apply Rule 6:

$$2x + 7(-2) = -2$$
$$2x - 14 = -2$$
$$2x = 12$$
$$x = +6$$

**Answer:** Each chromium atom in Cr₂O₇²⁻ has oxidation number +6.

**Why this makes sense:** The seven oxygens contribute −14. To reach the overall charge of −2, the two chromiums together must contribute +12, so each is +6.
</div>

<div class="worked">
<strong>Worked Example 3: Find the oxidation number of sulfur in Na₂S₂O₃.</strong>

This compound is sodium thiosulfate. It is neutral, so the sum of oxidation numbers must be 0.

**Step 1:** Sodium (Na) is a Group 1 metal. By Rule 2, Na = +1. There are two sodium atoms, contributing +2 total.

**Step 2:** Oxygen follows Rule 4: O = −2. There are three oxygen atoms, contributing −6 total.

**Step 3:** Let each sulfur atom have oxidation number x. There are two sulfur atoms.

$$2(+1) + 2x + 3(-2) = 0$$
$$2 + 2x - 6 = 0$$
$$2x = 4$$
$$x = +2$$

**Answer:** The average oxidation number of sulfur in Na₂S₂O₃ is +2.

**Important note:** In this compound, the two sulfur atoms are actually in different chemical environments — one is at +6 and one is at −2. The calculation method gives us the average. For the purposes of identifying redox reactions, the average is usually what we need.
</div>

<div class="worked">
<strong>Worked Example 4: Find the oxidation number of nitrogen in NH₃ and sulfur in H₂SO₄.</strong>

**For NH₃ (ammonia):** Hydrogen follows Rule 3: H = +1. There are three hydrogens. The molecule is neutral.

$$x + 3(+1) = 0$$
$$x + 3 = 0$$
$$x = -3$$

Nitrogen in NH₃ is −3. This is a negative oxidation number, meaning nitrogen has gained electron density compared to its elemental state.

**For H₂SO₄ (sulfuric acid):** H = +1 (Rule 3). O = −2 (Rule 4). The molecule is neutral.

$$2(+1) + x + 4(-2) = 0$$
$$2 + x - 8 = 0$$
$$x = +6$$

Sulfur in H₂SO₄ is +6.
</div>

## 4. How to Tell If a Reaction Is a Redox Reaction

A chemical reaction is a **redox reaction** if, and only if, **at least one atom changes its oxidation number** from the reactant side to the product side. If every atom keeps the same oxidation number, the reaction is not a redox reaction (it might be an acid-base reaction, a precipitation reaction, or some other type).

To check, follow this procedure:
1. Assign oxidation numbers to every atom on the reactant side.
2. Assign oxidation numbers to every atom on the product side.
3. Compare. If any number changes, it is a redox reaction.

**Why oxidation number changes indicate redox:** When an oxidation number increases, that atom has lost electrons — it has been oxidised. When an oxidation number decreases, that atom has gained electrons — it has been reduced. If both happen (which they must; electrons cannot vanish), the reaction is redox.

<div class="worked">
<strong>Worked Example 5: Is the following reaction a redox reaction?</strong>

$$\ce{Zn(s) + 2HCl(aq) -> ZnCl2(aq) + H2(g)}$$

**Step 1: Assign oxidation numbers to reactants.**
- Zn is an element (Rule 1): Zn = 0.
- In HCl: H = +1 (Rule 3), Cl = −1 (Rule 2, it is Cl⁻).

**Step 2: Assign oxidation numbers to products.**
- In ZnCl₂: Zn = +2 (Rule 2, Zn²⁺), Cl = −1.
- In H₂: H is an element (Rule 1), so H = 0.

**Step 3: Compare.**
- Zn: goes from 0 to +2. This is an **increase** — Zn has been **oxidised**.
- H: goes from +1 to 0. This is a **decrease** — H has been **reduced**.
- Cl: stays at −1 throughout — no change.

At least one oxidation number changed (in fact, two changed). **This IS a redox reaction.**
</div>

<div class="worked">
<strong>Worked Example 6: Is the following reaction a redox reaction?</strong>

$$\ce{NaOH(aq) + HCl(aq) -> NaCl(aq) + H2O(l)}$$

**Step 1: Assign oxidation numbers to reactants.**
- NaOH: Na = +1 (Rule 2, Group 1), O = −2 (Rule 4), H = +1 (Rule 3).
- HCl: H = +1, Cl = −1.

**Step 2: Assign oxidation numbers to products.**
- NaCl: Na = +1, Cl = −1.
- H₂O: H = +1, O = −2.

**Step 3: Compare.**
- Na: +1 → +1. No change.
- O: −2 → −2. No change.
- H: +1 → +1. No change.
- Cl: −1 → −1. No change.

No oxidation number changes at all. **This is NOT a redox reaction.** It is an acid-base neutralisation.
</div>

## 5. Oxidising Agents and Reducing Agents

In a redox reaction, we give special names to the substances that make oxidation and reduction happen.

The **oxidising agent** is the substance that *causes* another substance to be oxidised. How does it do this? By accepting (gaining) electrons from that other substance. Because the oxidising agent gains electrons, the oxidising agent itself is **reduced**. Every oxidising agent gets reduced in the process.

The **reducing agent** is the substance that *causes* another substance to be reduced. How? By donating (losing) electrons to that other substance. Because the reducing agent loses electrons, the reducing agent itself is **oxidised**. Every reducing agent gets oxidised in the process.

**Memory aid:** The oxidising agent is the one that gets reduced. The reducing agent is the one that gets oxidised. This sounds backwards at first, but it makes sense: the agent does something *to* the other substance, while the opposite happens *to* the agent itself.

In the reaction $$\ce{Zn(s) + Cu^{2+}(aq) -> Zn^{2+}(aq) + Cu(s)}$$:
- Cu²⁺ causes Zn to be oxidised by accepting electrons from Zn. Cu²⁺ is the **oxidising agent**. Cu²⁺ itself gets reduced (Cu²⁺ becomes Cu).
- Zn causes Cu²⁺ to be reduced by donating electrons to Cu²⁺. Zn is the **reducing agent**. Zn itself gets oxidised (Zn becomes Zn²⁺).

## 6. Disproportionation — When One Substance Does Both

**Disproportionation** is a special type of redox reaction. In a disproportionation reaction, the **same chemical species** is both oxidised and reduced at the same time. A single element in a single reactant ends up with both a higher oxidation number and a lower oxidation number in the products.

This happens when an element is in an intermediate oxidation state — not as high as it could be, and not as low as it could be. From that middle position, some atoms of that element go up (oxidation) and some go down (reduction).

<div class="worked">
<strong>Worked Example 7: Disproportionation of chlorine in water.</strong>

Consider the reaction of chlorine gas with water:

$$\ce{Cl2(g) + H2O(l) -> HOCl(aq) + HCl(aq)}$$

**Assign oxidation numbers:**
- In Cl₂: Cl is an element (Rule 1), so Cl = 0.
- In HOCl (hypochlorous acid): H = +1, O = −2. Let Cl = x. The molecule is neutral: (+1) + x + (−2) = 0, so x = +1. Cl is now +1.
- In HCl: H = +1, so Cl = −1.

**Analysis:** Chlorine started at oxidation number 0. In the products, some chlorine atoms are at +1 (in HOCl) and some are at −1 (in HCl). Going from 0 to +1 is oxidation (loss of electrons). Going from 0 to −1 is reduction (gain of electrons). The same element, chlorine, has been both oxidised and reduced. **This is a disproportionation reaction.**
</div>

<div class="worked">
<strong>Worked Example 8: Disproportionation of hydrogen peroxide.</strong>

Consider the decomposition of hydrogen peroxide:

$$\ce{2H2O2(aq) -> 2H2O(l) + O2(g)}$$

**Assign oxidation numbers:**
- In H₂O₂: This is a peroxide. Rule 4 tells us oxygen in peroxides has oxidation number −1. (H = +1.)
- In H₂O: O = −2 (Rule 4, normal oxide).
- In O₂: O = 0 (Rule 1, elemental form).

**Analysis:** Oxygen starts at −1. In the products, some oxygen atoms go to −2 (in H₂O) — this is reduction. Other oxygen atoms go to 0 (in O₂) — this is oxidation. The same element, oxygen, has been both oxidised and reduced. **This is a disproportionation reaction.**
</div>

## Answers

**Answer 1.**

(a) In SO₂: Oxygen follows Rule 4, so O = −2 each. The molecule is neutral (Rule 6). Let S = x:
$$x + 2(-2) = 0$$
$$x - 4 = 0$$
$$x = +4$$
Therefore S = +4, O = −2.

(b) In HNO₃: H = +1 (Rule 3). O = −2 (Rule 4). The molecule is neutral. Let N = x:
$$+1 + x + 3(-2) = 0$$
$$1 + x - 6 = 0$$
$$x = +5$$
Therefore H = +1, N = +5, O = −2.

(c) In MnO₂: O = −2 (Rule 4). The molecule is neutral. Let Mn = x:
$$x + 2(-2) = 0$$
$$x - 4 = 0$$
$$x = +4$$
Therefore Mn = +4, O = −2.

(d) In Fe₂O₃: O = −2 (Rule 4). The molecule is neutral. There are two iron atoms. Let each Fe = x:
$$2x + 3(-2) = 0$$
$$2x - 6 = 0$$
$$2x = 6$$
$$x = +3$$
Therefore Fe = +3, O = −2.

**Answer 2.**

(a) P in H₃PO₄: H = +1 (Rule 3), O = −2 (Rule 4). Neutral molecule. Let P = x:
$$3(+1) + x + 4(-2) = 0$$
$$3 + x - 8 = 0$$
$$x = +5$$
The oxidation number of phosphorus is +5.

(b) Cl in ClO₃⁻: O = −2 (Rule 4). The ion has charge −1 (Rule 6). Let Cl = x:
$$x + 3(-2) = -1$$
$$x - 6 = -1$$
$$x = +5$$
The oxidation number of chlorine is +5.

(c) N in NO₂⁻: O = −2 (Rule 4). The ion has charge −1 (Rule 6). Let N = x:
$$x + 2(-2) = -1$$
$$x - 4 = -1$$
$$x = +3$$
The oxidation number of nitrogen is +3.

(d) C in C₂O₄²⁻: O = −2 (Rule 4). The ion has charge −2 (Rule 6). There are two carbon atoms. Let each C = x:
$$2x + 4(-2) = -2$$
$$2x - 8 = -2$$
$$2x = 6$$
$$x = +3$$
The oxidation number of each carbon is +3.

**Answer 3.**

(a) On the reactant side: Each Fe³⁺ has oxidation number +3 (Rule 2: monatomic ion equals its charge). Each I⁻ has oxidation number −1 (Rule 2). On the product side: Each Fe²⁺ has oxidation number +2. Each I atom in I₂ has oxidation number 0 (Rule 1, elemental form).

(b) Iodide ions (I⁻) are oxidised. Each I⁻ goes from oxidation number −1 to 0 in I₂. This is an increase in oxidation number, meaning each I⁻ has lost one electron. The loss of electrons is oxidation.

(c) Iron(III) ions (Fe³⁺) are reduced. Each Fe³⁺ goes from oxidation number +3 to +2 in Fe²⁺. This is a decrease in oxidation number, meaning each Fe³⁺ has gained one electron. The gain of electrons is reduction.

(d) The oxidising agent is Fe³⁺ because it accepts electrons and is itself reduced. The reducing agent is I⁻ because it donates electrons and is itself oxidised. Remember: the agent does something to the other species while the opposite happens to itself.

**Answer 4.**

(a) In CaCO₃ → CaO + CO₂: Let us assign all oxidation numbers. CaCO₃: Ca = +2 (Group 2), C must be calculated. O = −2 each, three oxygens. CaCO₃ is neutral: (+2) + x + 3(−2) = 0, so x = +4. C = +4. Now CaO: Ca = +2, O = −2. CO₂: C = +4, O = −2 each. Every atom keeps the same oxidation number (Ca at +2, C at +4, O at −2). No oxidation number changes. **This is NOT a redox reaction.** It is a thermal decomposition.

(b) In 2Al + Fe₂O₃ → Al₂O₃ + 2Fe: Al starts as elemental Al (oxidation number 0) and becomes Al³⁺ in Al₂O₃ (oxidation number +3). Iron starts as Fe³⁺ in Fe₂O₃ (oxidation number +3) and becomes elemental Fe (oxidation number 0). Aluminium is oxidised (0 → +3). Iron is reduced (+3 → 0). **This IS a redox reaction.** This is the well-known thermite reaction, which produces molten iron.

(c) In AgNO₃ + NaCl → AgCl + NaNO₃: Ag = +1 throughout, N = +5 throughout, O = −2 throughout, Na = +1 throughout, Cl = −1 throughout. No oxidation number changes. **This is NOT a redox reaction.** It is a precipitation reaction (solid AgCl forms from dissolved ions).

**Answer 5.**

(a) The mass of oxygen in the sample = 2.00 g (total) − 1.26 g (manganese) = 0.74 g.

Amount of Mn = mass / molar mass = 1.26 g / 54.94 g mol⁻¹ = 0.0229 mol.
Amount of O = 0.74 g / 16.00 g mol⁻¹ = 0.0463 mol.

To find the simplest whole-number ratio, divide both amounts by the smaller (0.0229 mol):
Mn: 0.0229 / 0.0229 = 1.00
O: 0.0463 / 0.0229 = 2.02, which rounds to 2.

The empirical formula of X is MnO₂.

(b) In MnO₂, O = −2 each (Rule 4). The formula is neutral. Let Mn = x:
$$x + 2(-2) = 0$$
$$x - 4 = 0$$
$$x = +4$$
The oxidation number of manganese in X is +4.

(c) The balanced equation for the decomposition is:
$$\ce{3MnO2(s) -> Mn3O4(s) + O2(g)}$$

Check: 3 Mn on each side, 6 O on each side (3 × 2 = 6 on the left; 4 + 2 = 6 on the right).

Now assign oxidation numbers. In MnO₂ (reactant): Mn = +4, O = −2. In Mn₃O₄ (product): we are told the average Mn oxidation number is +⁸⁄₃. (This is because Mn₃O₄ is a mixed oxide containing Mn²⁺ and Mn³⁺: one Mn²⁺ and two Mn³⁺ per formula unit.) In O₂ (product): O = 0 (elemental form).

Manganese: goes from +4 to an average of +⁸⁄₃, which is a decrease. Some Mn atoms are reduced. Oxygen: goes from −2 to 0, which is an increase. Some O atoms are oxidised. The same compound, MnO₂, contains the element (Mn) that is reduced and the element (O) that is oxidised. Additionally, Mn itself undergoes disproportionation because some Mn atoms go to +2 and others to +3 (both lower than +4), while O is oxidised. This complex behaviour — where the same reactant supplies atoms that follow different redox paths — makes this a disproportionation reaction.
