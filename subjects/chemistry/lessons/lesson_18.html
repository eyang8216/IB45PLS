1. Prior Knowledge Check

Question 1: What does "spontaneous" mean in chemistry? Does it mean "fast"?

â–¸ Show
In chemistry, spontaneous (or feasible) means a reaction is thermodynamically favoured â€” $\Delta G < 0$. It does not mean fast. â–¸ Show

2. Entropy â€” The Measure of Disorder

2.1 What Is Entropy?

Entropy ($S$) is a thermodynamic quantity that measures the degree of disorder or the number of ways energy can be distributed among particles in a system. The more disordered a system, the higher its entropy.

Entropy change: $\Delta S = S_{\text{products}} - S_{\text{reactants}}$. Units: J Kâپ»آ¹ molâپ»آ¹. $\Delta S > 0$ means disorder increases; $\Delta S < 0$ means disorder decreases. The Second Law of Thermodynamics: the total entropy of the universe always increases for a spontaneous process.

3. Gibbs Free Energy â€” Combining Enthalpy and Entropy

3.1 The Fundamental Equation

Gibbs free energy equation: $$\Delta G = \Delta H - T\Delta S$$ $\Delta G$ = Gibbs free energy change (kJ molâپ»آ¹). $\Delta H$ = enthalpy change (kJ molâپ»آ¹). $T$ = absolute temperature (K). $\Delta S$ = entropy change (J Kâپ»آ¹ molâپ»آ¹ â€” remember to convert to kJ!).

A reaction is spontaneous (thermodynamically feasible) when $\Delta G < 0$. When $\Delta G = 0$, the system is at equilibrium. When $\Delta G > 0$, the reaction is not spontaneous in the forward direction (but the reverse reaction is).

Check-in: A reaction has $\Delta H = +178$ kJ molâپ»آ¹ and $\Delta S = +160$ J Kâپ»آ¹ molâپ»آ¹. At what temperature does it become spontaneous?

â–¸ Show
Set $\Delta G = 0$: $0 = 178 - T(0.160)$. Convert $\Delta S$ to kJ: $160\text{ J K}^{-1} = 0.160\text{ kJ K}^{-1}$. $T = 178 / 0.160 = \mathbf{1112.5\text{ K}}$ (â‰ˆ839آ°C). 3.3 Standard Gibbs Free Energy Change

The standard Gibbs free energy change ($\Delta G^\ominus$) can also be calculated from standard Gibbs free energies of formation, exactly like Hess's Law:

$$\Delta G^\ominus = \sum \Delta G_f^\ominus(\text{products}) - \sum \Delta G_f^\ominus(\text{reactants})$$

By definition, $\Delta G_f^\ominus$ of any element in its standard state is zero â€” same convention as $\Delta H_f^\ominus$.

Check-in: Calculate $\Delta G^\ominus$ for $\ce{2SO2(g) + O2(g) -> 2SO3(g)}$ using $\Delta G_f^\ominus$ values (kJ molâپ»آ¹): $\ce{SO2(g)} = -300$, $\ce{SO3(g)} = -371$.

â–¸ Show
$\Delta G^\ominus = 2(-371) - [2(-300) + 0] = -742 - (-600) = \mathbf{-142\text{ kJ mol}^{-1}}$. $\Delta G^\ominus < 0$, so the reaction is spontaneous under standard conditions (298 K). This is the Contact Process for sulfuric acid manufacture.

4. Worked Examples

Example 1 â€” Predicting Spontaneity Temperature:

For the reaction $\ce{NH4Cl(s) -> NH3(g) + HCl(g)}$, $\Delta H^\ominus = +176$ kJ molâپ»آ¹ and $\Delta S^\ominus = +285$ J Kâپ»آ¹ molâپ»آ¹. (a) Is the reaction spontaneous at 298 K? (b) At what temperature does it become spontaneous?

Step 2: Set $\Delta G = 0$: $T = 176 / 0.285 = \mathbf{618\text{ K}}$ (345آ°C). Above 618 K, $\Delta G < 0$ and decomposition is spontaneous.

Example 2 â€” Which Factor Dominates?:

Step 3: As $T$ increases, the $-T\Delta S$ term becomes more positive (since $\Delta S$ is negative, $-T\Delta S$ is positive), making $\Delta G$ less negative. At very high temperatures, $\Delta G$ could become positive. The enthalpy term dominates at low T; the entropy term grows with T.

âڑ ï¸ڈ Common Mistakes to Avoid

  1. "$\Delta G < 0$ means the reaction is fast." Thermodynamics says nothing about rate. Diamond â†’ graphite has $\Delta G < 0$ but an enormous $E_a$. A reaction can be thermodynamically spontaneous yet kinetically inert. Conversely, a reaction with $\Delta G > 0$ can be driven by coupling to a spontaneous reaction (e.g., ATP hydrolysis driving biosynthesis).
  2. "$\Delta H < 0$ means the reaction is spontaneous." Not true in general. Water freezing at +5آ°C has $\Delta H < 0$ but is not spontaneous because $\Delta S$ is also negative and, at that temperature, $|T\Delta S| < |\Delta H|$ is not satisfied. You must evaluate $\Delta G$, not just $\Delta H$.
  3. "Using J for $\Delta S$ and kJ for $\Delta H$ in the same equation without converting." $\Delta S$ is given in J Kâپ»آ¹ molâپ»آ¹. In $\Delta G = \Delta H - T\Delta S$, if $\Delta H$ is in kJ, $\Delta S$ must be converted to kJ Kâپ»آ¹ molâپ»آ¹ (divide by 1000) â€” or convert $\Delta H$ to J. Mismatched units give answers 1000أ— wrong.
  4. "Forgetting that $\Delta G$ depends on temperature." $\Delta G$ is temperature-dependent through the $-T\Delta S$ term. A value of $\Delta G^\ominus$ calculated at 298 K tells you nothing about spontaneity at 1000 K. Always recalculate at the temperature of interest.
  5. "Predicting $\Delta S$ sign incorrectly when gases dissolve." Dissolving a gas (e.g., $\ce{CO2(g) -> CO2(aq)}$) decreases entropy ($\Delta S < 0$) â€” gas molecules lose their freedom of movement. Dissolving a solid usually increases entropy ($\Delta S > 0$). Do not assume all dissolution increases disorder.

6. Key Terms

TermDefinition
Entropy ($S$)A measure of the disorder of a system; the number of ways energy can be distributed among particles (units: J Kâپ»آ¹ molâپ»آ¹)
Standard molar entropy ($S^\ominus$)The absolute entropy of one mole of a substance under standard conditions; unlike $\Delta H_f^\ominus$, $S^\ominus$ for elements is NOT zero
Second Law of ThermodynamicsThe total entropy of the universe always increases for any spontaneous process: $\Delta S_{\text{universe}} > 0$
Gibbs free energy ($G$)A thermodynamic potential combining enthalpy and entropy: $G = H - TS$; $\Delta G$ determines spontaneity at constant T and P
Spontaneous processA process that is thermodynamically favoured: $\Delta G < 0$; does NOT imply the reaction is fast
Equilibrium (from $\Delta G$)The condition where $\Delta G = 0$; no net change occurs; the system is at its minimum free energy
$\Delta G_f^\ominus$Standard Gibbs free energy of formation; $\Delta G_f^\ominus = 0$ for elements in standard states
Crossover temperatureThe temperature at which $\Delta G = 0$; $T = \Delta H / \Delta S$ (when both have the same sign)
