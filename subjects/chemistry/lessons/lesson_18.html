1. What Is Entropy?

A New Way to Think About Chemical Change

In Lessons 14 through 17, we focused almost entirely on enthalpy ‚ÄĒ the heat absorbed or released by a reaction. But enthalpy alone cannot explain why some reactions happen and others do not. There is a second factor that determines whether a chemical process will occur: entropy.

Entropy (symbol: S) is a measure of the disorder of a system. More precisely, entropy measures the number of ways that energy can be distributed among the particles in a system. A system with high entropy has its energy spread out among many different arrangements; a system with low entropy has its energy concentrated in fewer arrangements.

An Analogy: A Deck of Cards

Imagine a brand-new deck of playing cards, perfectly sorted by suit and rank. This ordered arrangement has low entropy ‚ÄĒ there is essentially only one way to arrange the cards in this order. Now shuffle the deck thoroughly. The cards are now in a random, disordered arrangement. There are an enormous number of possible random arrangements. The shuffled deck has high entropy.

In chemistry, nature tends to favor states with higher entropy ‚ÄĒ just as a shuffled deck is far more probable than a perfectly sorted one, a disordered distribution of molecules is far more probable than an ordered one.

Key Facts About Entropy

Fact 1: Solids have the lowest entropy. In a solid, particles are locked into fixed positions in a crystal lattice. They can vibrate, but they cannot move from place to place. The arrangement is highly ordered, so the number of ways to distribute energy is relatively small. Entropy values for solids are low.

Fact 2: Liquids have higher entropy than solids. When a solid melts, the particles gain the ability to move past one another. The arrangement becomes less ordered, and the number of ways to distribute energy increases. The entropy of a liquid is always greater than the entropy of the same substance as a solid at the same temperature.

Fact 3: Gases have much higher entropy than liquids. In a gas, particles move freely in all directions and occupy the entire volume of their container. There are vastly more possible arrangements. The entropy of a gas is much larger than the entropy of the same substance as a liquid.

Fact 4: More particles mean more entropy. If a reaction produces more gas molecules than it consumes, the entropy increases (őĒS is positive). More particles mean more possible arrangements and more ways to distribute energy.

Fact 5: Dissolving usually increases entropy. When a solid dissolves, its ions or molecules become dispersed throughout the solution. This spread-out arrangement has much higher entropy than the orderly crystal lattice.

A Critical Difference Between Entropy and Enthalpy

There is a very important difference between standard entropy values (S‚¶Ķ) and standard enthalpies of formation (őĒH_f‚¶Ķ) that many students miss:

  • őĒH_f‚¶Ķ of an element in its standard state is zero by definition.
  • S‚¶Ķ of an element in its standard state is NOT zero. Every substance, including elements, has a positive entropy at temperatures above absolute zero.

For example, at 298 K:

  • S‚¶Ķ[H‚āā(g)] = 131 J K‚ĀĽ¬Ļ mol‚ĀĽ¬Ļ
  • S‚¶Ķ[O‚āā(g)] = 205 J K‚ĀĽ¬Ļ mol‚ĀĽ¬Ļ
  • S‚¶Ķ[C(s, graphite)] = 5.7 J K‚ĀĽ¬Ļ mol‚ĀĽ¬Ļ
  • S‚¶Ķ[Fe(s)] = 27 J K‚ĀĽ¬Ļ mol‚ĀĽ¬Ļ

Notice that even graphite, a highly ordered solid, has a small positive entropy. Only at absolute zero (0 K) would a perfectly ordered crystal have S = 0. This is the Third Law of Thermodynamics.

Units of Entropy

Entropy is measured in J K‚ĀĽ¬Ļ mol‚ĀĽ¬Ļ (joules per kelvin per mole). Note the unit: joules, not kilojoules. This is the source of one of the most common calculation errors in this topic ‚ÄĒ forgetting to convert between joules and kilojoules when combining őĒH (usually in kJ) and őĒS (always in J K‚ĀĽ¬Ļ). We will address this carefully in Section 4.

2. Predicting the Sign of őĒS

What to Look For

You can predict whether the entropy change (őĒS) for a reaction is positive or negative by looking at the balanced chemical equation and asking two questions:

Question 1: Is there a change of state?

If a reaction produces gases from solids or liquids, őĒS is strongly positive. If a reaction consumes gases to make solids or liquids, őĒS is strongly negative. Changes of state almost always dominate over other considerations.

Question 2: Does the number of gas molecules change?

If a reaction has more gas molecules on the product side than on the reactant side, őĒS is positive (more disorder). If there are fewer gas molecules on the product side, őĒS is negative (more order).

Worked Example 1: Predicting őĒS Signs

Problem: Predict the sign of őĒS (positive or negative) for each of the following reactions. Give a brief reason for each prediction.

(a) CaCO‚āÉ(s) ‚Üí CaO(s) + CO‚āā(g)

(b) N‚āā(g) + 3H‚āā(g) ‚Üí 2NH‚āÉ(g)

(c) H‚āāO(l) ‚Üí H‚āāO(s)

(d) NH‚āĄNO‚āÉ(s) ‚Üí NH‚āĄ‚Āļ(aq) + NO‚āÉ‚ĀĽ(aq)

Answers:

(a) őĒS is positive. On the reactant side, there are zero gas molecules (only a solid). On the product side, one molecule of CO‚āā gas is produced. Producing a gas from a solid represents a huge increase in disorder.

(b) őĒS is negative. Count the gas molecules: on the reactant side, there are 1 + 3 = 4 moles of gas. On the product side, there are only 2 moles of gas. Fewer gas molecules means fewer possible arrangements, so entropy decreases.

(c) őĒS is negative. Liquid water freezing to solid ice represents a transition from a disordered state (liquid, where molecules move freely) to an ordered state (solid, where molecules are locked in a crystal lattice). Freezing always decreases entropy.

(d) őĒS is positive. A solid ionic compound dissolves, releasing ions that become dispersed throughout the aqueous solution. The ions in solution have far more possible arrangements than they did in the ordered crystal lattice. Dissolving a solid almost always increases entropy.

Common Misconception

Students sometimes think that if the number of molecules stays the same, őĒS must be zero. This is not correct. The entropy change depends on many factors, including the complexity of the molecules and the states involved. For example, the reaction H‚āā(g) + I‚āā(g) ‚Üí 2HI(g) has the same number of gas molecules on both sides (2 ‚Üí 2), but the actual őĒS is small but not zero.

3. Calculating őĒS‚¶Ķ Using Standard Entropy Values

The Formula

The standard entropy change for a reaction is calculated in the same way we calculated őĒH from enthalpies of formation:

őĒS‚¶Ķ = ő£S‚¶Ķ(products) ‚ąí ő£S‚¶Ķ(reactants)

You take each product's standard entropy, multiply it by the coefficient in the balanced equation, add them all up, then subtract the sum for the reactants (each multiplied by its coefficient).

Worked Example 2: Calculating őĒS‚¶Ķ

Problem: Calculate the standard entropy change for the reaction: 2H‚āā(g) + O‚āā(g) ‚Üí 2H‚āāO(l). The standard entropy values are:

  • S‚¶Ķ[H‚āā(g)] = 131 J K‚ĀĽ¬Ļ mol‚ĀĽ¬Ļ
  • S‚¶Ķ[O‚āā(g)] = 205 J K‚ĀĽ¬Ļ mol‚ĀĽ¬Ļ
  • S‚¶Ķ[H‚āāO(l)] = 70 J K‚ĀĽ¬Ļ mol‚ĀĽ¬Ļ

Strategy: Apply the formula. Multiply each S‚¶Ķ by its coefficient.

Step 1 ‚ÄĒ Sum of S‚¶Ķ for products:

ő£S‚¶Ķ(products) = 2 √ó 70 = 140 J K‚ĀĽ¬Ļ.

Step 2 ‚ÄĒ Sum of S‚¶Ķ for reactants:

ő£S‚¶Ķ(reactants) = 2 √ó 131 + 1 √ó 205 = 262 + 205 = 467 J K‚ĀĽ¬Ļ.

Step 3 ‚ÄĒ Calculate őĒS‚¶Ķ:

őĒS‚¶Ķ = 140 ‚ąí 467 = ‚ąí327 J K‚ĀĽ¬Ļ mol‚ĀĽ¬Ļ.

Why this makes sense: The reaction converts 3 moles of gas (2H‚āā + O‚āā) into 2 moles of liquid water. Gases have much higher entropy than liquids, so a large decrease in entropy is expected. The negative sign is correct.

Worked Example 3: Formation of Ammonia

Problem: Calculate őĒS‚¶Ķ for: N‚āā(g) + 3H‚āā(g) ‚Üí 2NH‚āÉ(g). Data:

  • S‚¶Ķ[N‚āā(g)] = 192 J K‚ĀĽ¬Ļ mol‚ĀĽ¬Ļ

  • S‚¶Ķ[NH‚āÉ(g)] = 193 J K‚ĀĽ¬Ļ mol‚ĀĽ¬Ļ

Calculation:

ő£S‚¶Ķ(products) = 2 √ó 193 = 386 J K‚ĀĽ¬Ļ.

ő£S‚¶Ķ(reactants) = 1 √ó 192 + 3 √ó 131 = 192 + 393 = 585 J K‚ĀĽ¬Ļ.

őĒS‚¶Ķ = 386 ‚ąí 585 = ‚ąí199 J K‚ĀĽ¬Ļ mol‚ĀĽ¬Ļ.

Why this makes sense: Four moles of gas become two moles of gas. Fewer gas molecules means lower entropy, so őĒS is negative.

4. Gibbs Free Energy: Combining Enthalpy and Entropy

The Problem That Gibbs Free Energy Solves

We now have two quantities that describe a reaction: őĒH (the enthalpy change, which tells us about heat) and őĒS (the entropy change, which tells us about disorder). But how do we decide whether a reaction will actually happen? Some reactions are exothermic but do not occur (like the conversion of diamond to graphite at room temperature ‚ÄĒ it is exothermic but incredibly slow). Some reactions are endothermic but occur spontaneously (like the dissolving of ammonium nitrate in water, which gets cold but happens readily).

The answer is that both őĒH and őĒS matter. The quantity that combines them is called the Gibbs free energy change, symbol őĒG.

The Definition of Gibbs Free Energy

őĒG‚¶Ķ = őĒH‚¶Ķ ‚ąí TőĒS‚¶Ķ

Where:

  • őĒG‚¶Ķ is the standard Gibbs free energy change (in J mol‚ĀĽ¬Ļ or kJ mol‚ĀĽ¬Ļ)
  • őĒH‚¶Ķ is the standard enthalpy change (typically in kJ mol‚ĀĽ¬Ļ)
  • T is the absolute temperature in kelvin (K)
  • őĒS‚¶Ķ is the standard entropy change (in J K‚ĀĽ¬Ļ mol‚ĀĽ¬Ļ)

What őĒG Tells Us

The sign of őĒG tells us whether a reaction is spontaneous (thermodynamically feasible) under the given conditions:

  • If őĒG < 0 (negative): The reaction is spontaneous in the forward direction. It can occur without an external input of energy.
  • If őĒG > 0 (positive): The reaction is non-spontaneous in the forward direction. It will not occur unless energy is supplied. (The reverse reaction would be spontaneous.)
  • If őĒG = 0: The reaction is at equilibrium. The forward and reverse rates are equal.

The Meaning of "Spontaneous"

In thermodynamics, "spontaneous" does not mean "fast." A spontaneous reaction can be very slow. For example, the combustion of diamond in oxygen has a negative őĒG, meaning it is thermodynamically spontaneous, but a diamond ring does not burst into flames when you wear it because the activation energy is very high. "Spontaneous" means that the reaction is energetically and entropically favored ‚ÄĒ it will eventually happen if given a suitable pathway (or an eternity).

The Critical Unit Trap

This is where many students lose marks. Look at the equation: őĒG = őĒH ‚ąí TőĒS. The term TőĒS must have the same units as őĒH. őĒH is typically given in kilojoules per mole (kJ mol‚ĀĽ¬Ļ). őĒS is always given in joules per kelvin per mole (J K‚ĀĽ¬Ļ mol‚ĀĽ¬Ļ). When you multiply T (in K) by őĒS (in J K‚ĀĽ¬Ļ mol‚ĀĽ¬Ļ), you get an answer in J mol‚ĀĽ¬Ļ.

Therefore, before you can subtract TőĒS from őĒH, you must either:

  • Convert őĒH from kJ to J (multiply by 1000), do the calculation in joules, and then convert back to kJ if needed, OR
  • Convert TőĒS from J to kJ (divide by 1000) and then subtract from őĒH in kJ.

I strongly recommend the first method: convert everything to joules, calculate, then convert back. This reduces the chance of a factor-of-1000 error.

Worked Example 4: Calculating őĒG

Problem: For the decomposition of calcium carbonate: CaCO‚āÉ(s) ‚Üí CaO(s) + CO‚āā(g), the following data are given: őĒH‚¶Ķ = +178 kJ mol‚ĀĽ¬Ļ, őĒS‚¶Ķ = +161 J K‚ĀĽ¬Ļ mol‚ĀĽ¬Ļ. Calculate őĒG‚¶Ķ at 298 K and determine whether the reaction is spontaneous at this temperature.

Strategy: Convert őĒH to J, compute TőĒS in J, subtract, then convert back to kJ if desired.

Calculation:

őĒH‚¶Ķ = +178 kJ mol‚ĀĽ¬Ļ = +178,000 J mol‚ĀĽ¬Ļ.

TőĒS‚¶Ķ = 298 K √ó 161 J K‚ĀĽ¬Ļ mol‚ĀĽ¬Ļ = 47,978 J mol‚ĀĽ¬Ļ.

őĒG‚¶Ķ = 178,000 ‚ąí 47,978 = +130,022 J mol‚ĀĽ¬Ļ = +130 kJ mol‚ĀĽ¬Ļ.

őĒG‚¶Ķ is positive, so the reaction is not spontaneous at 298 K. This makes sense: calcium carbonate (limestone, chalk, marble) does not spontaneously decompose to calcium oxide and carbon dioxide at room temperature ‚ÄĒ it is stable.

5. The Four Cases: How őĒH and őĒS Interact

Understanding Temperature Dependence

The TőĒS term in the Gibbs equation means that temperature plays a crucial role in determining spontaneity. As temperature increases, the TőĒS term becomes larger in magnitude. This means that őĒS becomes more important at high temperatures, while őĒH dominates at low temperatures.

There are four possible combinations of őĒH and őĒS:

Case 1: őĒH negative, őĒS positive (the dream scenario)

őĒG = (negative) ‚ąí T(positive) = always negative, at all temperatures.

This reaction is spontaneous at all temperatures.

Example: The combustion of fuels. Burning methane is exothermic (őĒH negative) and produces more gas molecules than it consumes (őĒS positive). These reactions happen readily once ignited.

Case 2: őĒH positive, őĒS negative (the nightmare scenario)

őĒG = (positive) ‚ąí T(negative) = positive + (positive number) = always positive, at all temperatures.

This reaction is never spontaneous.

Example: The reverse of combustion ‚ÄĒ trying to turn CO‚āā and H‚āāO back into methane and oxygen without an input of energy. This will not happen spontaneously at any temperature.

Case 3: őĒH negative, őĒS negative (spontaneous only at low T)

őĒG = (negative) ‚ąí T(negative) = negative + T|őĒS|.

At low temperatures, the T|őĒS| term is small, so the negative őĒH dominates, and őĒG is negative (spontaneous). At high temperatures, T|őĒS| becomes larger than |őĒH|, and őĒG becomes positive (non-spontaneous).

Example: The Haber process: N‚āā(g) + 3H‚āā(g) ‚Üí 2NH‚āÉ(g). őĒH = ‚ąí92 kJ mol‚ĀĽ¬Ļ, őĒS = ‚ąí199 J K‚ĀĽ¬Ļ mol‚ĀĽ¬Ļ. This reaction is spontaneous at low temperatures but becomes non-spontaneous above a certain threshold.

Case 4: őĒH positive, őĒS positive (spontaneous only at high T)

őĒG = (positive) ‚ąí T(positive).

At low temperatures, the positive őĒH dominates, so őĒG is positive (non-spontaneous). At high temperatures, TőĒS exceeds őĒH, making őĒG negative (spontaneous).

Example: The thermal decomposition of CaCO‚āÉ(s) ‚Üí CaO(s) + CO‚āā(g). őĒH = +178 kJ mol‚ĀĽ¬Ļ, őĒS = +161 J K‚ĀĽ¬Ļ mol‚ĀĽ¬Ļ. This reaction requires high temperature to become spontaneous ‚ÄĒ which is why limestone is heated in a kiln to make quicklime.

Finding the Threshold Temperature

For Cases 3 and 4, there is a temperature at which the reaction switches from spontaneous to non-spontaneous (or vice versa). At this threshold, őĒG = 0.

Setting őĒG = 0 in the Gibbs equation:

0 = őĒH ‚ąí TőĒS

T = őĒH / őĒS

This formula gives the temperature (in kelvin) at which őĒG changes sign. You must use consistent units ‚ÄĒ both őĒH and őĒS must be in joules, or both in kilojoules.

Worked Example 5: Finding the Threshold Temperature

Problem: For the Haber process, N‚āā(g) + 3H‚āā(g) ‚Üí 2NH‚āÉ(g), őĒH = ‚ąí92 kJ mol‚ĀĽ¬Ļ and őĒS = ‚ąí199 J K‚ĀĽ¬Ļ mol‚ĀĽ¬Ļ. Above what temperature does this reaction become non-spontaneous?

Strategy: Both őĒH and őĒS are negative, so this is Case 3 (spontaneous at low T). The threshold is found from T = őĒH/őĒS. We must use consistent units.

Calculation:

Convert őĒH to J: ‚ąí92 kJ mol‚ĀĽ¬Ļ = ‚ąí92,000 J mol‚ĀĽ¬Ļ.

T = őĒH/őĒS = ‚ąí92,000 / (‚ąí199) = 462 K.

Below 462 K (approximately 189¬įC), őĒG is negative and the reaction is spontaneous. Above 462 K, őĒG becomes positive and the reaction is no longer spontaneous.

Why this makes sense: The Haber process is actually run at about 700 K in industry. At 700 K, őĒG is positive, which seems contradictory. The reason is that the reaction is run at high temperature not because it is thermodynamically ideal, but because the rate is far too slow at low temperatures. A catalyst is used, and the high temperature is a compromise: it sacrifices some thermodynamic favorability to gain a practical reaction rate. The ammonia is continuously removed, which shifts the equilibrium and helps compensate.

Worked Example 6: Decomposition Temperature

Problem: Sodium hydrogen carbonate (baking soda) decomposes when heated: 2NaHCO‚āÉ(s) ‚Üí Na‚āāCO‚āÉ(s) + H‚āāO(g) + CO‚āā(g). For this reaction, őĒH‚¶Ķ = +135 kJ mol‚ĀĽ¬Ļ and őĒS‚¶Ķ = +335 J K‚ĀĽ¬Ļ mol‚ĀĽ¬Ļ. Calculate the minimum temperature at which this reaction becomes spontaneous.

At oven temperature, the decomposition becomes spontaneous, releasing CO‚āā gas that creates the bubbles that make the cake light and fluffy.
