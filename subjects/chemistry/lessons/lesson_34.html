1. Prior Knowledge Check

Question 1: In a redox reaction, where do electrons flow ÔÇö from the reducing agent to the oxidising agent, or vice versa?

ÔľŞ Show
Electrons flow from the reducing agent (which loses electrons / is oxidised) to the oxidising agent (which gains electrons / is reduced).

Question 2: What does a positive $\Delta G$ tell you about the spontaneity of a reaction?

ÔľŞ Show
A positive $\Delta G$ means the reaction is non-spontaneous under the given conditions. A negative $\Delta G$ means spontaneous. $\Delta G = 0$ means the system is at equilibrium.

2. The Voltaic (Galvanic) Cell

A voltaic cell converts chemical energy into electrical energy by physically separating the oxidation and reduction half-reactions. Electrons are forced to travel through an external wire, producing an electric current.

Figure 1: The Daniell cell. Zn is oxidised at the anode (Ôłĺ); Cu┬▓Ôü║ is reduced at the cathode (+). The salt bridge completes the circuit by allowing ion migration.

2.1 Key Components

ComponentRole
AnodeElectrode where oxidation occurs. In a voltaic cell, it is the negative electrode (electrons are produced here).
CathodeElectrode where reduction occurs. In a voltaic cell, it is the positive electrode (electrons are consumed here).
Salt bridgeA tube containing an inert electrolyte (e.g., $\ce{KNO3}$) that allows ions to migrate, maintaining electrical neutrality. Without it, charge builds up and the cell stops.
External wireConducts electrons from anode to cathode. This is where we extract useful electrical work.

Check-in: In the Daniell cell, why does the zinc electrode lose mass while the copper electrode gains mass?

ÔľŞ Show
At the anode: $\ce{Zn(s) -> Zn^{2+}(aq) + 2e-}$. Solid zinc dissolves into solution as Zn┬▓Ôü║ ions ÔÇö the electrode loses mass. At the cathode: $\ce{Cu^{2+}(aq) + 2e- -> Cu(s)}$. Cu┬▓Ôü║ ions from solution are deposited as solid copper on the electrode ÔÇö it gains mass.

3. Standard Electrode Potentials ($E^{\ominus}$)

The standard electrode potential ($E^{\ominus}$) measures the tendency of a half-cell to undergo reduction under standard conditions (298 K, 1 mol dmÔü╗┬│, 100 kPa). All values are measured relative to the standard hydrogen electrode (SHE), which is assigned $E^{\ominus} = 0.00\text{ V}$.

Key principles of $E^{\ominus}$:
ÔÇó More positive $E^{\ominus}$ = stronger oxidising agent (greater tendency to be reduced).
ÔÇó More negative $E^{\ominus}$ = stronger reducing agent (greater tendency to be oxidised).
ÔÇó $E^{\ominus}$ is an intensive property ÔÇö it does NOT depend on the amount of substance.
ÔÇó $E^{\ominus}$ values are NOT multiplied when you multiply a half-equation.
Figure 2: The electrochemical series. Species with more negative EÔŽÁ are stronger reducing agents (easily oxidised). Species with more positive EÔŽÁ are stronger oxidising agents (easily reduced).

3.1 Calculating Cell Potential

$$E^{\ominus}_{\text{cell}} = E^{\ominus}_{\text{cathode}} - E^{\ominus}_{\text{anode}}$$

Or equivalently: $E^{\ominus}_{\text{cell}} = E^{\ominus}_{\text{reduction}} + E^{\ominus}_{\text{oxidation}}$, where $E^{\ominus}_{\text{oxidation}} = -E^{\ominus}_{\text{reduction}}$.

For a spontaneous reaction: $E^{\ominus}_{\text{cell}} > 0$. If $E^{\ominus}_{\text{cell}} < 0$, the reaction is non-spontaneous in that direction.

Check-in: Calculate $E^{\ominus}_{\text{cell}}$ for $\ce{Fe^{2+} + Zn -> Fe + Zn^{2+}}$. ($E^{\ominus}$: $\ce{Fe^{2+}/Fe}$ = Ôłĺ0.45 V, $\ce{Zn^{2+}/Zn}$ = Ôłĺ0.76 V). Is it spontaneous?

ÔľŞ Show
Fe┬▓Ôü║ is reduced (cathode, $E^{\ominus}$ = Ôłĺ0.45 V). Zn is oxidised (anode, $E^{\ominus}$ = Ôłĺ0.76 V). $E^{\ominus}_{\text{cell}} = Ôłĺ0.45 Ôłĺ (Ôłĺ0.76) = \mathbf{+0.31\text{ V}}$. Since $E^{\ominus}_{\text{cell}} > 0$, the reaction is spontaneous.

4. Relationship Between $E^{\ominus}_{\text{cell}}$ and $\Delta G^{\ominus}$

The cell potential is directly related to the maximum electrical work the cell can perform:

Key formula: $$\Delta G^{\ominus} = -nFE^{\ominus}_{\text{cell}}$$ $n$ = number of moles of electrons transferred, $F$ = Faraday constant = $9.65 \times 10^4\text{ C mol}^{-1}$, $E^{\ominus}_{\text{cell}}$ in volts.

Implications: $E^{\ominus}_{\text{cell}} > 0 \Rightarrow \Delta G^{\ominus} < 0$ (spontaneous). $E^{\ominus}_{\text{cell}} < 0 \Rightarrow \Delta G^{\ominus} > 0$ (non-spontaneous).

Check-in: For the Daniell cell (Zn/Cu┬▓Ôü║), $E^{\ominus}_{\text{cell}} = 1.10\text{ V}$ and $n = 2$. Calculate $\Delta G^{\ominus}$.

ÔľŞ Show
$\Delta G^{\ominus} = -nFE^{\ominus}_{\text{cell}} = -(2)(96500)(1.10) = \mathbf{-212\text{ kJ mol}^{-1}}$. The negative value confirms the reaction is thermodynamically spontaneous.

5. Worked Examples

Example 1 ÔÇö Predicting Spontaneity:

Will $\ce{Cu}$ displace $\ce{Zn^{2+}}$ from solution? $E^{\ominus}$: $\ce{Cu^{2+}/Cu}$ = +0.34 V, $\ce{Zn^{2+}/Zn}$ = Ôłĺ0.76 V.

Proposed reaction: $\ce{Cu + Zn^{2+} -> Cu^{2+} + Zn}$. Cu is oxidised (anode, $E^{\ominus}$ = +0.34 V). Zn┬▓Ôü║ is reduced (cathode, $E^{\ominus}$ = Ôłĺ0.76 V).
$E^{\ominus}_{\text{cell}} = E^{\ominus}_{\text{cathode}} - E^{\ominus}_{\text{anode}} = -0.76 - 0.34 = \mathbf{-1.10\text{ V}}$. Not spontaneous. This is the reverse of the Daniell cell. Copper cannot displace zinc from solution.

ÔÜá´ŞĆ Common wrong approach: Adding the potentials instead of subtracting: Ôłĺ0.76 + 0.34 = Ôłĺ0.42 V ÔÇö still negative but numerically wrong. Always use cathode Ôłĺ anode.
Example 2 ÔÇö Identifying Oxidising/Reducing Strength:

Given: $\ce{Fe^{3+}/Fe^{2+}}$ = +0.77 V, $\ce{I2/I-}$ = +0.54 V, $\ce{Br2/Br-}$ = +1.07 V. (a) Which is the strongest oxidising agent? (b) Will $\ce{Fe^{3+}}$ oxidise $\ce{I-}$?

(a) Strongest oxidising agent = most positive $E^{\ominus}$ = $\ce{Br2}$ (+1.07 V).
(b) $\ce{2Fe^{3+} + 2I- -> 2Fe^{2+} + I2}$. Cathode: $\ce{Fe^{3+}/Fe^{2+}}$ (+0.77 V). Anode: $\ce{I2/I-}$ (+0.54 V). $E^{\ominus}_{\text{cell}} = 0.77 - 0.54 = +0.23\text{ V} > 0$ Ôćĺ Yes, spontaneous.

ÔÜá´ŞĆ Common wrong approach: Comparing $E^{\ominus}$ values without considering which is cathode and which is anode. For a spontaneous reaction, the species with the higher (more positive) $E^{\ominus}$ must be reduced.
Example 3 ÔÇö Cell Diagram Notation:

Write the cell diagram for a cell where $\ce{Mg}$ is oxidised to $\ce{Mg^{2+}}$ and $\ce{Ag+}$ is reduced to $\ce{Ag}$.

Convention: $\ce{anode | anode\ solution || cathode\ solution | cathode}$
Anode (oxidation): $\ce{Mg(s) | Mg^{2+}(aq)}$
Cathode (reduction): $\ce{Ag+(aq) | Ag(s)}$
Cell diagram: $\ce{Mg(s) | Mg^{2+}(aq) || Ag+(aq) | Ag(s)}$.
$E^{\ominus}_{\text{cell}} = E^{\ominus}(\ce{Ag+/Ag}) - E^{\ominus}(\ce{Mg^{2+}/Mg}) = +0.80 - (-2.37) = +3.17\text{ V}$.

ÔÜá´ŞĆ Common wrong approach: Reversing the order ÔÇö putting the cathode on the left. The convention is always anode (oxidation) on the left, cathode (reduction) on the right.

ÔÜá´ŞĆ Common Mistakes to Avoid

  1. Multiplying $E^{\ominus}$ by the stoichiometric coefficient. $E^{\ominus}$ is an intensive property. Even if you multiply a half-equation by 2, the $E^{\ominus}$ stays the same. Do NOT multiply potentials.
  2. Confusing anode/cathode signs. In a voltaic cell: anode = (Ôłĺ), cathode = (+). In an electrolytic cell, they are reversed. Know which type you're dealing with.
  3. Using $E^{\ominus}$ values under non-standard conditions. $E^{\ominus}$ applies at 298 K, 1 mol dmÔü╗┬│, 100 kPa. Under non-standard conditions, use the Nernst equation (HL only): $E = E^{\ominus} - (RT/nF)\ln Q$.
  4. Forgetting the salt bridge. A cell without a salt bridge will not work ÔÇö charge builds up immediately and the reaction stops. The salt bridge completes the circuit.
