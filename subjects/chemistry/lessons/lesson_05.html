Lesson 5: Ionisation Energy I — First IE, Periodic Trends

What You'll Learn

  • Define first ionisation energy (IE₁) and write the balanced chemical equation, with state symbols, that represents this process
  • Identify and explain the three factors that determine ionisation energy: nuclear charge, distance from the nucleus, and shielding by inner electrons
  • Explain why first ionisation energy generally increases from left to right across a period, using nuclear charge and shielding
  • Explain the two anomalies in the Period 2 and Period 3 IE₁ trends: the drop from beryllium to boron and the drop from nitrogen to oxygen
  • Explain why first ionisation energy decreases down a group, using distance and shielding
  • Use an element's position in the periodic table to predict and compare ionisation energies

1. What Is First Ionisation Energy?

The definition and why it matters

The first ionisation energy (abbreviated IE₁) of an element is the minimum amount of energy required to remove one mole of electrons from one mole of isolated, gaseous atoms, producing one mole of singly charged gaseous cations. This process is always endothermic — energy must be supplied to overcome the electrostatic attraction between the negatively charged electron and the positively charged nucleus.

The chemical equation for the first ionisation energy of any element X is:

\[ \ce{X(g) -> X^+(g) + e^-} \]

Let us examine each part of this definition carefully.

"One mole of electrons from one mole of gaseous atoms" means we are measuring the energy per mole, in units of kilojoules per mole (kJ mol⁻¹). This makes ionisation energies comparable across all elements.

"Gaseous atoms" is essential. The atoms must be isolated in the gas phase so that there are no intermolecular forces between neighbouring atoms complicating the measurement. If the atoms were in a solid or liquid, extra energy would be needed to overcome attractive forces between atoms, and the measurement would not reflect only the attraction between the nucleus and the electron being removed.

"Minimum energy" means we are measuring just enough energy to remove the electron, with no excess kinetic energy imparted to either the resulting ion or the ejected electron.

The first ionisation energy tells us how tightly an atom holds onto its outermost electron. A high IE₁ means the electron is held very tightly and is difficult to remove. A low IE₁ means the electron is held loosely and is easy to remove. Ionisation energy is one of the most important periodic properties because it reveals how atomic structure changes across the periodic table.

Worked Example 1: Writing an Ionisation Energy Equation

Problem: Write the balanced chemical equation, including state symbols, that represents the first ionisation energy of sodium.

Approach: Start with a gaseous sodium atom. Remove one electron. The product is a gaseous sodium ion with a 1+ charge and a free electron.

Solution:

\[ \ce{Na(g) -> Na^+(g) + e^-} \]

Why this makes sense: Sodium has the electron configuration [Ne] 3s¹. The single 3s electron is the outermost electron and is the one removed in the first ionisation. The resulting Na⁺ ion has the stable electron configuration of neon. State symbols (g) are essential in this equation because the definition of ionisation energy specifies gaseous species.

2. The Three Factors That Control Ionisation Energy

What determines how tightly an electron is held

Three factors work together to determine how much energy is needed to remove an electron from an atom. Every trend and anomaly in ionisation energy across the periodic table can be explained by the interplay of these three factors.

Factor 1: Nuclear Charge

The nuclear charge is the number of protons in the nucleus, which is equal to the atomic number Z. A larger nuclear charge means a stronger positive charge in the nucleus, which attracts the negatively charged electrons more strongly. All else being equal, an atom with more protons will hold its electrons more tightly, giving it a higher ionisation energy.

Factor 2: Distance from the Nucleus

Electrons that are further from the nucleus experience a weaker electrostatic attraction. This is because the electrostatic force between two charged particles decreases with the square of the distance between them. An electron in the n = 3 shell (the third energy level) is, on average, further from the nucleus than an electron in the n = 2 shell. Consequently, an n = 3 electron is easier to remove than an n = 2 electron, assuming the same nuclear charge and shielding.

Factor 3: Shielding by Inner Electrons

Electrons in inner shells (those closer to the nucleus) partially block, or shield, the outer electrons from the full attraction of the nucleus. The inner electrons repel the outer electrons, and because they lie between the nucleus and the outer electrons, they reduce the pull of the nucleus on those outer electrons. The net positive charge that an outer electron actually experiences is called the effective nuclear charge. It is less than the full nuclear charge Z.

Effective nuclear charge = Z − (approximate shielding effect of inner electrons).

Electrons in the same shell are poor at shielding each other because they are at roughly the same distance from the nucleus and do not lie between each other and the nucleus. Electrons in inner shells (with smaller n) are very effective at shielding because they spend most of their time closer to the nucleus than the outer electrons.

A helpful analogy: imagine standing in a crowd, trying to see a performer on stage. The people directly between you and the stage block your view — they shield you. The people standing beside you (in your same "shell") do not block your view at all. Similarly, inner-shell electrons shield outer electrons, but electrons in the same shell provide very little shielding of each other.

How the three factors interact

When comparing ionisation energies of two different elements, you must consider all three factors. Sometimes they work together (for example, going down a group, nuclear charge increases but distance and shielding also increase — and the distance and shielding win, so IE decreases). Sometimes they work against each other (for example, going across a period, nuclear charge increases while distance and shielding stay roughly the same, so IE increases).

3. The Trend Across a Period — Why IE₁ Generally Increases

The pattern in Period 3

The table below shows the measured first ionisation energies (in kJ mol⁻¹) for the elements of Period 3, from sodium (Na, Z = 11) to argon (Ar, Z = 18):

Element Na Mg Al Si P S Cl Ar
--------- ----- ----- ----- ----- ----- ----- ----- ------
IE₁ (kJ mol⁻¹) 496 738 578 786 1012 1000 1251 1521

The general trend: an overall increase

Looking at the sequence from sodium (496) to argon (1521), the first ionisation energy clearly increases overall. The value for argon is more than three times that of sodium.

The explanation for this general increase has two parts:

  1. Nuclear charge increases. As you go from sodium (11 protons) to argon (18 protons), the charge on the nucleus steadily rises. With each step to the right, one more proton is added to the nucleus.
  1. Shielding does not increase. All the electrons being added across Period 3 are going into the same shell (n = 3). Electrons in the same shell are poor at shielding one another. The inner-shell shielding — from the 1s², 2s², and 2p⁶ electrons — stays constant at 10 inner electrons for every element from sodium to argon.

Therefore, the effective nuclear charge experienced by the outermost electrons steadily increases across the period, while the distance of those electrons from the nucleus stays roughly the same (they are all in the n = 3 shell). The result is that electrons are held progressively more tightly, and more energy is required to remove them.

Worked Example 2: Explaining a Trend Within a Period

Problem: Explain why chlorine (IE₁ = 1251 kJ mol⁻¹) has a much higher first ionisation energy than sodium (IE₁ = 496 kJ mol⁻¹).

Approach: Compare the three factors for each element.

Solution: Chlorine has a nuclear charge of +17, whereas sodium has a nuclear charge of +11 — chlorine has six more protons. Both elements have their outermost electrons in the n = 3 shell, so the distance from the nucleus is similar. The shielding by inner electrons is identical for both: each has the neon core (1s² 2s² 2p⁶), providing shielding from 10 inner electrons. Since distance and shielding are essentially the same but chlorine has a significantly larger nuclear charge, chlorine's outermost electrons experience a much greater effective nuclear charge and are held far more tightly. This requires more energy to remove, giving chlorine the higher ionisation energy.

Why this makes sense: The ratio of IE₁ values (1251 / 496 ≈ 2.5) is large but plausible given that chlorine has 55% more protons than sodium, all pulling on electrons at roughly the same distance with the same shielding.

4. The Two Anomalies in the Period Trend

Not everything increases smoothly

If you look closely at the Period 3 values, you will notice two places where the IE₁ drops rather than increases: from magnesium (738) to aluminium (578), and from phosphorus (1012) to sulfur (1000). These are not mistakes in the data — they are real effects caused by the details of electron configuration. The same anomalies appear in Period 2 (from beryllium to boron and from nitrogen to oxygen).

Anomaly 1: The drop from magnesium to aluminium (and from beryllium to boron)

Magnesium has the electron configuration [Ne] 3s². The electron being removed in the first ionisation is a 3s electron. Aluminium has the configuration [Ne] 3s² 3p¹. The electron being removed is a 3p electron.

The 3p orbital is slightly higher in energy than the 3s orbital. It is also, on average, slightly further from the nucleus because the p orbital shape extends further out than the spherical s orbital. Even though aluminium has one more proton than magnesium (Z = 13 vs Z = 12), the 3p electron is simply easier to remove than a 3s electron because it starts from a higher-energy, more distant orbital. The orbital energy effect outweighs the small increase in nuclear charge.

The same explanation applies to the Period 2 anomaly from beryllium (2s²) to boron (2s² 2p¹): the 2p electron in boron is easier to remove than the 2s electron in beryllium.

Anomaly 2: The drop from phosphorus to sulfur (and from nitrogen to oxygen)

Phosphorus has the configuration [Ne] 3s² 3p³. In the 3p sub-level, the three electrons each occupy a separate p orbital with parallel spins (Hund's rule). This half-filled sub-level arrangement is especially stable because of exchange energy — a quantum mechanical stabilisation that occurs when electrons with parallel spins occupy different orbitals in the same sub-level.

Sulfur has the configuration [Ne] 3s² 3p⁴. The fourth electron in the 3p sub-level must pair up with one of the existing electrons in an already half-filled orbital. These two paired electrons repel each other because they occupy the same region of space. This electron-electron repulsion makes one of them slightly easier to remove than an unpaired electron in phosphorus. Even though sulfur has one more proton (Z = 16 vs Z = 15), the repulsion between the paired electrons outweighs the increased nuclear attraction, resulting in a slightly lower IE₁.

The same explanation applies to the Period 2 anomaly from nitrogen (2p³, half-filled) to oxygen (2p⁴, one paired).

Many students mistakenly think that sulfur should have a higher IE₁ than phosphorus because sulfur has more protons. This is a natural assumption, but it ignores the important effect of electron pairing. The takeaway is that electron configuration details sometimes override the simple nuclear charge argument.

Worked Example 3: Explaining Both Anomalies in One Answer

Problem: The first ionisation energies (in kJ mol⁻¹) of the Period 2 elements are: Li (520), Be (900), B (801), C (1086), N (1402), O (1314), F (1681), Ne (2081). Identify the two anomalies in this series and explain the cause of each one.

Approach: Look for places where IE₁ decreases rather than increases. Then analyse the electron configurations of the elements involved.

Solution: The first anomaly is the drop from beryllium (900) to boron (801). Beryllium has the configuration 1s² 2s², so the electron removed is a 2s electron. Boron is 1s² 2s² 2p¹, so the electron removed is a 2p electron. The 2p orbital is higher in energy than the 2s orbital, making the electron easier to remove despite boron's higher nuclear charge.

The second anomaly is the drop from nitrogen (1402) to oxygen (1314). Nitrogen has the configuration 1s² 2s² 2p³, with a half-filled 2p sub-level in which each p orbital holds one electron with parallel spin. This half-filled arrangement is particularly stable. Oxygen has 1s² 2s² 2p⁴, in which one p orbital contains a pair of electrons. The repulsion between the paired electrons makes one of them easier to remove than an unpaired electron from nitrogen's half-filled sub-level.

5. The Trend Down a Group — Why IE₁ Decreases

The pattern in Group 1

The table below shows the first ionisation energies for the Group 1 elements (the alkali metals):

Element Li Na K Rb Cs
--------- ----- ----- ----- ----- -----
IE₁ (kJ mol⁻¹) 520 496 419 403 376

The trend is clear: first ionisation energy decreases steadily as you go down the group.

Explaining the decrease

Three factors change as you go down Group 1:

  1. Nuclear charge increases. Lithium has 3 protons, sodium has 11, potassium has 19, rubidium has 37, and caesium has 55. If nuclear charge were the only factor, IE₁ would increase down the group. But it does not.
  1. Distance increases. The outermost electron is in a higher shell for each element down the group: lithium (n = 2), sodium (n = 3), potassium (n = 4), rubidium (n = 5), caesium (n = 6). An electron further from the nucleus is less tightly held.
  1. Shielding increases. Each row down adds a full shell of inner electrons. Lithium has only the 1s² shell shielding its outer electron. Sodium has the 1s² 2s² 2p⁶ shells (10 inner electrons) shielding its outer electron. Caesium has 54 inner electrons shielding its outer electron. More inner electrons mean more shielding, which further reduces the effective nuclear charge felt by the outermost electron.

The increase in distance and shielding together outweigh the increase in nuclear charge, and the net effect is that the outermost electron becomes progressively easier to remove. This is why IE₁ decreases down a group.

Worked Example 4: Comparing Ionisation Energies Down a Group

Problem: Without looking up any values, predict which element has the higher first ionisation energy: lithium (Li, Z = 3) or potassium (K, Z = 19). Justify your prediction.

Approach: Both are in Group 1, so we apply the down-a-group trend.

Solution: Lithium has the higher first ionisation energy. Although potassium has a much larger nuclear charge (19 protons vs 3), potassium's outermost electron is in the n = 4 shell, whereas lithium's is in the n = 2 shell — much closer to the nucleus. Potassium also has significantly more shielding from its inner electrons (18 inner electrons for K vs only 2 for Li). The combined effects of greater distance and greater shielding mean that potassium's outer electron is held less tightly and is easier to remove than lithium's outer electron.

Why this makes sense: The measured values confirm this: Li IE₁ = 520 kJ mol⁻¹ and K IE₁ = 419 kJ mol⁻¹. Lithium, despite having far fewer protons, holds its electron about 25% more tightly because that electron is so much closer to the nucleus.

Practice Problems

Problem 1: Write the balanced chemical equation, including state symbols, for the first ionisation energy of calcium (Ca, Z = 20).

Problem 2: Explain why neon (Ne, Z = 10) has a higher first ionisation energy than fluorine (F, Z = 9). Your explanation should refer to nuclear charge, distance from the nucleus, and shielding.

Problem 3: State the general trend in first ionisation energy across Period 2 from lithium to neon. Explain this trend in terms of the three factors that control ionisation energy.

Problem 4: The first ionisation energy of sodium is 496 kJ mol⁻¹ and the first ionisation energy of potassium is 419 kJ mol⁻¹. (a) State which of these two elements has the larger nuclear charge. (b) Explain why potassium, despite having the larger nuclear charge, has the lower first ionisation energy.

Problem 5 (IB-Style): The first ionisation energies of five consecutive elements in Period 3 are shown in the table below. The elements are labelled A, B, C, D, and E.

Element A B C D E
--------- ----- ----- ----- ----- ------
IE₁ (kJ mol⁻¹) 496 738 578 786 1012

(a) Identify elements A through E by giving their chemical symbols. (b) Explain why element C has a lower first ionisation energy than element B, even though C has a larger atomic number. Your answer should refer to electron configurations. (c) Explain why element E has a higher first ionisation energy than element D. (d) The next element in the period, element F (after E), has a first ionisation energy of 1000 kJ mol⁻¹. Explain why this value is slightly lower than that of element E.

Answers

Answer 1: The equation for the first ionisation energy of calcium is:

\[ \ce{Ca(g) -> Ca^+(g) + e^-} \]

Calcium (Z = 20) has the electron configuration [Ar] 4s². The electron removed is one of the two 4s electrons.

Answer 2: Neon (Z = 10) and fluorine (Z = 9) both have their outermost electrons in the n = 2 shell, so the distance from the nucleus is approximately the same. Both have the same inner-shell shielding from the 1s² electrons (two inner electrons). However, neon has one more proton than fluorine (10 vs 9), giving it a larger nuclear charge. With the same distance and the same shielding but a greater nuclear charge, neon's outermost electrons experience a stronger effective nuclear charge. They are held more tightly, so neon requires more energy to ionise. The measured values are: F IE₁ = 1681 kJ mol⁻¹ and Ne IE₁ = 2081 kJ mol⁻¹.

Answer 3: Across Period 2, from lithium (Z = 3) to neon (Z = 10), the first ionisation energy generally increases. The nuclear charge increases from +3 to +10 over these eight elements, so the nucleus exerts a progressively stronger attraction on the electrons. All the electrons being added across the period go into the n = 2 shell, so the outermost electrons are at roughly the same distance from the nucleus. The shielding from the inner 1s² shell remains constant at two inner electrons for all Period 2 elements. Because nuclear charge increases while distance and shielding remain approximately constant, the effective nuclear charge experienced by the outer electrons steadily increases. The electrons are held more and more tightly, and the ionisation energy rises accordingly, except at the two anomalies (beryllium to boron and nitrogen to oxygen).

Answer 4: (a) Potassium has the larger nuclear charge. Potassium has Z = 19 (19 protons), while sodium has Z = 11 (11 protons).

(b) Despite having more protons, potassium has a lower ionisation energy because its outermost electron is in the n = 4 shell (the 4s orbital), whereas sodium's outermost electron is in the n = 3 shell (the 3s orbital). The greater distance means the electrostatic attraction between the nucleus and the outer electron is weaker. In addition, potassium has 18 inner electrons shielding its outer electron, compared to only 10 inner electrons for sodium. The increased distance and increased shielding together outweigh the increase in nuclear charge, making potassium's outer electron easier to remove.

Answer 5: (a) A = Na (sodium, 496), B = Mg (magnesium, 738), C = Al (aluminium, 578), D = Si (silicon, 786), E = P (phosphorus, 1012).

(b) Element C (aluminium, Z = 13) has a lower IE₁ than element B (magnesium, Z = 12) because of the difference in the orbital from which the electron is removed. Magnesium loses a 3s electron, while aluminium loses a 3p electron. The 3p orbital is higher in energy and, on average, further from the nucleus than the 3s orbital. Even though aluminium has one more proton, the 3p electron is easier to remove than the 3s electron, so aluminium has a lower IE₁.

(c) Element E (phosphorus, Z = 15) has a higher IE₁ than element D (silicon, Z = 14) because phosphorus has one more proton. Both elements have their outermost electrons in the n = 3 shell, and the shielding is the same. The increased nuclear charge, with no change in distance or shielding, means phosphorus's outer electrons are held more tightly.

(d) Element F is sulfur (Z = 16). Although sulfur has one more proton than phosphorus, its IE₁ (1000 kJ mol⁻¹) is slightly lower than phosphorus's (1012 kJ mol⁻¹). This is because sulfur has the electron configuration [Ne] 3s² 3p⁴, which means one of its 3p orbitals contains a pair of electrons. The repulsion between these paired electrons makes one of them slightly easier to remove than an unpaired electron from phosphorus's half-filled 3p³ configuration. The electron-electron repulsion in the doubly occupied orbital outweighs the small increase in nuclear charge from phosphorus to sulfur.
