Lesson 5: Ionisation Energy I — First IE and Periodic Trends

Syllabus reference: 3.1 (The Periodic Table — Periodic Trends), 12.1 (Electrons in Atoms — Ionisation Energy)

Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to:

  • Define first ionisation energy and write balanced chemical equations for the process.
  • Identify the three key factors that determine ionisation energy: nuclear charge, distance/shielding, and electron–electron repulsion.
  • Describe and explain the general trend in first ionisation energy across a period and down a group.
  • Explain the anomalous dips in first ionisation energy at Group 13 (e.g., Be → B) and Group 16 (e.g., N → O).
  • Compare the first ionisation energies of given elements using electron configuration to justify your reasoning.

1. Prior Knowledge Check

Question 1: Write the full electron configuration of magnesium ($Z = 12$) using $1s$, $2s$, $2p$, $3s$ notation.

▸ Show
$\ce{Mg}$: $\mathrm{1s^2\ 2s^2\ 2p^6\ 3s^2}$. Notice that the outermost electron is in the $3s$ subshell — this is the electron removed in the first ionisation.

Question 2: What is meant by "shielding" in the context of atomic structure?

▸ Show
Shielding is the repulsion experienced by outer-shell electrons from inner-shell (core) electrons. Inner electrons lie between the nucleus and the outer electrons, reducing the full attractive pull of the nucleus. The effective nuclear charge, $Z_{\text{eff}}$, is the net positive charge actually felt by an outer electron after shielding is accounted for.

Question 3: What is the relationship between atomic radius and the attraction between the nucleus and outer electrons?

▸ Show
Electrostatic attraction follows Coulomb's law: $F \propto \frac{q_1 q_2}{r^2}$. The greater the distance ($r$) between the nucleus and the outer electron, the weaker the attraction — so less energy is required to remove the electron.

2. What Is First Ionisation Energy?

The first ionisation energy (IE₁) of an element is the minimum energy required to remove one mole of electrons from one mole of gaseous atoms to form one mole of gaseous singly charged cations:

General equation: $$\ce{X(g) -> X+(g) + e-}\qquad \Delta H = \text{IE}_1$$

Ionisation energy is always endothermic ($\Delta H > 0$) because energy must be supplied to overcome the electrostatic attraction between the nucleus and the electron.

Three things to note about this definition — all three must appear in an IB exam answer:

  1. Gaseous state — the atom must be isolated in the gas phase so that no intermolecular forces interfere.
  2. One mole — IE is quoted in kJ mol⁻¹, i.e., per mole of atoms.
  3. Removal of one electron — first IE removes the outermost electron only.

Check-in: Write the equation for the first ionisation energy of aluminium.

▸ Show
$\ce{Al(g) -> Al+(g) + e-}$. The state symbol (g) is essential — omitting it loses the mark. The electron configuration of Al is $\mathrm{[Ne]\ 3s^2\ 3p^1}$, so the $3p$ electron is the one removed.

3. Three Factors That Determine Ionisation Energy

The energy required to remove an electron depends on how strongly the electron is held by the nucleus. Three factors compete:

FactorEffect on IEReason
1. Nuclear charge ($Z$)↑ $Z$ → ↑ IEMore protons in the nucleus → stronger electrostatic attraction on all electrons.
2. Distance / shielding↑ distance → ↓ IEOuter electrons in higher energy levels are further from the nucleus and are shielded by inner electrons, reducing $Z_{\text{eff}}$.
3. Electron–electron repulsion↑ repulsion → ↓ IEElectrons in the same subshell repel each other. Pairing electrons in an orbital raises energy, making one of them easier to remove.
Figure 1: The three factors determining ionisation energy. The nucleus (red, charge +Z) attracts the outermost electron (orange). Core electrons (brown) shield the outer electron, reducing the effective nuclear charge. The greater the distance r, the weaker the attraction — so less energy is needed to remove the electron.

Check-in: Sodium ($Z = 11$) has a lower first IE than neon ($Z = 10$). Using the three factors, explain why.

▸ Show
Neon: $\mathrm{1s^2\ 2s^2\ 2p^6}$ — the outermost electron is in $n = 2$. Sodium: $\mathrm{[Ne]\ 3s^1}$ — the outermost electron is in $n = 3$. Although Na has a larger nuclear charge (+11 vs. +10), the $3s$ electron is further from the nucleus and shielded by all 10 core electrons ($\mathrm{1s^2\ 2s^2\ 2p^6}$). The increased distance and shielding outweigh the increase in nuclear charge, so Na has a much lower IE₁ (496 vs. 2081 kJ mol⁻¹).

4. Periodic Trends in First Ionisation Energy

4.1 Across a Period (→): General Increase

Moving from left to right across a period, first ionisation energy generally increases. This is because:

  • Nuclear charge ($Z$) increases — each step adds one proton.
  • Shielding stays approximately constant — electrons are added to the same outer shell, so the number of inner (shielding) electrons does not change.
  • Atomic radius decreases — the increased nuclear charge pulls the same shell inward.

The result: $Z_{\text{eff}}$ increases across the period, outer electrons are held more tightly, and IE₁ rises.

4.2 Down a Group (↓): General Decrease

Moving down a group, first ionisation energy decreases. This is because:

  • The outer electron occupies a higher principal energy level ($n$ increases).
  • The outer electron is further from the nucleus → weaker attraction.
  • There are more inner shells of electrons → increased shielding.

Although nuclear charge also increases down a group, the effects of increased distance and increased shielding dominate, so IE₁ falls.

4.3 The Anomalies — Where the Trend "Breaks"

The general increase across a period is not smooth — there are two notable dips in each period (from Period 2 onwards). These are exam favourites.

Anomaly 1: Group 2 → Group 13 (e.g., Be → B, Mg → Al)

IE₁ drops from Group 2 to Group 13 despite the increase in nuclear charge. This happens because the electron being removed comes from a new, higher-energy subshell:

ElementElectron removed fromIE₁ / kJ mol⁻¹
Be ($Z = 4$)$2s$ subshell900
B ($Z = 5$)$2p$ subshell801

The $2p$ subshell is at a higher energy (further from the nucleus on average) than the $2s$ subshell. Removing an electron from $2p$ therefore requires less energy than from $2s$, despite the higher nuclear charge of boron. The same logic applies to Mg → Al ($3s$ → $3p$).

Anomaly 2: Group 15 → Group 16 (e.g., N → O, P → S)

IE₁ drops from Group 15 to Group 16. This is due to electron–electron repulsion within a doubly occupied orbital:

Element$p$-subshell configurationIE₁ / kJ mol⁻¹
N ($Z = 7$)$2p_x^1\ 2p_y^1\ 2p_z^1$ (all half-filled)1402
O ($Z = 8$)$2p_x^2\ 2p_y^1\ 2p_z^1$ (one orbital doubly occupied)1314

In nitrogen, each $2p$ orbital has exactly one electron — a stable half-filled arrangement. In oxygen, one $2p$ orbital contains two paired electrons. The repulsion between these paired electrons makes one of them easier to remove, so IE₁ drops despite the higher nuclear charge. The same logic applies to P → S ($3p$).

Figure 2: First ionisation energies across Periods 2 and 3. Notice the general upward trend, interrupted by dips at Group 13 (B, Al — new subshell) and Group 16 (O, S — paired electron repulsion). Noble gases (Ne, Ar) sit at the peak due to their stable full-shell configuration.

Check-in: Phosphorus ($Z = 15$) has a higher first IE than sulfur ($Z = 16$). Explain this anomaly using electron configurations.

▸ Show
Phosphorus: $\mathrm{[Ne]\ 3s^2\ 3p_x^1\ 3p_y^1\ 3p_z^1}$ — each $3p$ orbital has one electron (half-filled, stable arrangement). Sulfur: $\mathrm{[Ne]\ 3s^2\ 3p_x^2\ 3p_y^1\ 3p_z^1}$ — one $3p$ orbital contains two paired electrons. The electron–electron repulsion between the paired electrons in sulfur's $3p$ orbital makes one of them easier to remove, so IE₁ drops despite sulfur's higher nuclear charge.

5. Worked Examples

Example 1 — Writing IE Equations:

Write the equation for the first ionisation energy of potassium and state why the value (419 kJ mol⁻¹) is much lower than that of sodium (496 kJ mol⁻¹).

Equation: $\ce{K(g) -> K+(g) + e-}$

Explanation: Potassium ($Z = 19$) has electron configuration $\mathrm{[Ar]\ 4s^1}$. The electron removed is from the $4s$ subshell. Sodium ($Z = 11$) has configuration $\mathrm{[Ne]\ 3s^1}$ — the electron removed is from the $3s$ subshell.

Although K has a larger nuclear charge (+19 vs. +11), the $4s$ electron is further from the nucleus (in $n = 4$ vs. $n = 3$) and is shielded by more core electrons (18 core electrons in K vs. 10 in Na). The increased distance and shielding outweigh the increased nuclear charge, so K has a lower IE₁.

💡 Key exam phrase: "The increased distance and shielding outweigh the increase in nuclear charge" — this exact wording scores the mark for explaining a trend down a group.
Example 2 — Explaining the Be → B Anomaly:

Beryllium has IE₁ = 900 kJ mol⁻¹, while boron has IE₁ = 801 kJ mol⁻¹. Despite boron having the larger nuclear charge, its IE₁ is lower. Explain.

Electron configurations:
Be: $\mathrm{1s^2\ 2s^2}$ — the electron to be removed is from the $2s$ subshell.
B: $\mathrm{1s^2\ 2s^2\ 2p^1}$ — the electron to be removed is from the $2p$ subshell.

Explanation: The $2p$ subshell is at a higher energy level than the $2s$ subshell within the same $n = 2$ shell. An electron in $2p$ is, on average, further from the nucleus and therefore easier to remove. This effect dominates over the small increase in nuclear charge (+4 → +5), causing the IE₁ to drop.

💡 The same logic applies to Mg → Al ($3s$ → $3p$), another common exam question.
Example 3 — Explaining the N → O Anomaly:

Nitrogen has IE₁ = 1402 kJ mol⁻¹, while oxygen has IE₁ = 1314 kJ mol⁻¹. Explain why oxygen's IE₁ is lower despite having a higher nuclear charge.

Electron configurations (using orbital box notation for the $2p$ subshell):
N ($Z = 7$): $2p$: $\uparrow\ \ \ \uparrow\ \ \ \uparrow$   (three unpaired electrons; half-filled, all orbitals singly occupied)
O ($Z = 8$): $2p$: $\uparrow\downarrow\ \ \ \uparrow\ \ \ \uparrow$   (one orbital doubly occupied, two singly occupied)

Explanation: In nitrogen, each $2p$ orbital has exactly one electron — a symmetrical, half-filled configuration that is particularly stable. In oxygen, one $2p$ orbital contains two paired electrons. The electrostatic repulsion between these paired electrons raises their energy, making one of them easier to remove. This pairing energy penalty outweighs the small increase in nuclear charge (+7 → +8), so IE₁ drops.

💡 Exam note: You must mention "paired electrons" and "repulsion" explicitly. Simply saying "O has a lower IE because it has more electrons" is insufficient and will not earn marks.

⚠️ Common Mistakes to Avoid

  1. Confusing "nuclear charge" with "effective nuclear charge." Nuclear charge ($Z$) is simply the number of protons. Effective nuclear charge ($Z_{\text{eff}}$) is what an outer electron actually experiences after shielding is taken into account. When explaining trends, always talk about $Z_{\text{eff}}$, not just $Z$. Across a period, $Z_{\text{eff}}$ increases because $Z$ increases but shielding stays the same. Down a group, $Z_{\text{eff}}$ does not increase significantly because the added protons are offset by added shielding.
  2. Thinking that more protons always means higher IE. This is the most common error. Sodium has 11 protons and neon has 10, yet Na has a much lower IE₁. Distance and shielding matter just as much as nuclear charge. You must consider all three factors together.
  3. Forgetting state symbols in IE equations. The IB definition specifies gaseous atoms. Writing $\ce{Na(s) -> Na+(s) + e-}$ instead of $\ce{Na(g) -> Na+(g) + e-}$ will lose the mark. All species must be (g).
  4. Explaining the N → O anomaly as "N has a half-filled shell so it's more stable." While this captures part of the idea, the IB mark scheme requires you to mention electron–electron repulsion in the doubly occupied orbital of oxygen. The stability of nitrogen's configuration is only half the story — the repulsion in oxygen is the active cause of the drop.
  5. Confusing first IE with electronegativity. Both increase across a period and decrease down a group, and both relate to how strongly an atom attracts electrons. But IE measures the energy to remove an electron from an isolated gaseous atom, while electronegativity describes how strongly an atom attracts a shared electron pair in a bond. They are different concepts measured in different ways.

Question 5 (IB-style multi-part): The first five ionisation energies of element X are (in kJ mol⁻¹):
IE₁ = 578   IE₂ = 1817   IE₃ = 2745   IE₄ = 11578   IE₅ = 14831
(a) Identify the group of element X in the periodic table. Justify your answer by referring to the data.
(b) Write the electron configuration of element X and identify the element.
(c) Explain why there is a very large jump between IE₃ and IE₄.

Answer 1: C. Mg: $\mathrm{[Ne]\ 3s^2}$ — the electron removed is from $3s$. Al: $\mathrm{[Ne]\ 3s^2\ 3p^1}$ — the electron removed is from $3p$. The $3p$ subshell is higher in energy than $3s$, so less energy is required. Exam trap: Option D is tempting but wrong — Al and Mg have the same number of inner shielding electrons ($\mathrm{1s^2\ 2s^2\ 2p^6}$); the extra electron in Al is in the outer shell and does not contribute to shielding.

Answer 2: Equation: $\ce{Cl(g) -> Cl+(g) + e-}$. The process is endothermic ($\Delta H > 0$). Energy must be supplied to overcome the electrostatic attraction between the positively charged nucleus and the negatively charged electron. Ionisation is always endothermic — energy goes into the system.

Answer 3:
(a) Across Period 3, nuclear charge increases (+11 to +18) while shielding stays approximately constant (electrons are added to the same $n = 3$ shell). This increases $Z_{\text{eff}}$, pulling the outer electrons in more tightly, so IE₁ generally rises.
(b) Mg → Al: The electron removed from Mg is in a $3s$ orbital; the electron removed from Al is in a $3p$ orbital. The $3p$ subshell is higher in energy than $3s$, so the electron is easier to remove despite Al's higher nuclear charge.
(c) P → S: In P, all three $3p$ orbitals are singly occupied (half-filled). In S, one $3p$ orbital contains a pair of electrons. The repulsion between these paired electrons makes one easier to remove — so IE₁ drops.

Answer 4: Going down Group 1, the outer electron occupies progressively higher principal energy levels ($2s$ → $3s$ → $4s$ → $5s$ → $6s$). The outer electron is increasingly further from the nucleus and shielded by more inner shells. Although nuclear charge also increases, the effects of increased distance and increased shielding dominate, so $Z_{\text{eff}}$ experienced by the outermost electron decreases down the group, and IE₁ falls.

Answer 5:
(a) The very large jump occurs between IE₃ (2745) and IE₄ (11578) — a factor of ~4.2. This indicates that the fourth electron is being removed from an inner shell (closer to the nucleus, less shielded). Element X therefore has 3 valence electrons and belongs to Group 13.
(b) Configuration: $\mathrm{1s^2\ 2s^2\ 2p^6\ 3s^2\ 3p^1}$. This is aluminium (Al, $Z = 13$).
(c) The first three electrons come from the $n = 3$ shell ($3s^2\ 3p^1$). The fourth electron must come from the $n = 2$ shell ($2p$). This electron is much closer to the nucleus, experiences far less shielding, and thus requires significantly more energy to remove — hence the large jump.

7. Key Terms

TermDefinition
First ionisation energy (IE₁)The minimum energy required to remove one mole of electrons from one mole of gaseous atoms to form one mole of gaseous singly charged cations: $\ce{X(g) -> X+(g) + e-}$
Effective nuclear charge ($Z_{\text{eff}}$)The net positive charge experienced by an electron after accounting for the shielding effect of inner (core) electrons
ShieldingThe reduction in nuclear attraction felt by outer electrons due to the repulsive effect of inner-shell electrons lying between them and the nucleus
Electron–electron repulsionThe electrostatic repulsion between two electrons occupying the same orbital; this raises their energy and makes one of them easier to remove
$s$-block elementAn element whose outermost electron occupies an $s$ subshell (Groups 1 and 2)
$p$-block elementAn element whose outermost electron occupies a $p$ subshell (Groups 13–18)
Half-filled subshell stabilityA configuration in which each orbital in a subshell is singly occupied (e.g., $\mathrm{N}\ 2p^3$ or $\mathrm{Cr}\ 3d^5$), conferring extra stability due to symmetry and reduced electron–electron repulsion
Successive ionisation energiesThe energies required to remove the 1st, 2nd, 3rd, … electron from a gaseous species; large jumps indicate a change to an inner shell, revealing the element's group

8. Exam Tips

Paper 1 (Multiple Choice): Questions on IE anomalies are extremely common — expect at least one on Be → B or N → O. Remember: the Group 2 → 13 dip is about subshell energy ($s$ vs. $p$), while the Group 15 → 16 dip is about electron pairing and repulsion. If you mix these up, you will pick the wrong answer. Also watch for questions that give you a table of successive IEs and ask you to identify the group — look for the largest jump.
Paper 2 (Structured): "Explain" questions on IE trends typically carry 3 marks. The mark scheme expects you to mention all three factors: (1) nuclear charge, (2) distance/shielding, and (3) which one dominates. For across a period: "shielding stays constant, nuclear charge increases, so $Z_{\text{eff}}$ increases." For down a group: "increased distance and shielding outweigh the increased nuclear charge." Use these exact phrases.
State symbols matter. If a question asks you to "write the equation for the first ionisation energy of …", you MUST include (g) on all species. The IB mark scheme specifically awards a mark for correct state symbols. Without them, the equation is simply wrong by definition — ionisation energy is defined for gaseous species only.
