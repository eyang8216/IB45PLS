Lesson 16: Bond Enthalpies & Energy Profiles

Syllabus reference: 5.3 — Bond Enthalpies

Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to:

  • Distinguish between bond-breaking (endothermic) and bond-making (exothermic) and explain why all chemical reactions involve both.
  • Define average bond enthalpy and explain why it differs from the bond enthalpy in a specific molecule.
  • Calculate $\Delta H$ for a reaction using average bond enthalpies: $\Delta H = \sum E(\text{bonds broken}) - \sum E(\text{bonds formed})$.
  • Draw and interpret energy profile diagrams showing reactants, products, transition state, and activation energy ($E_a$).
  • Identify the activation energy and $\Delta H$ on an energy profile for both exothermic and endothermic reactions.

1. Prior Knowledge Check

Question 1: Is breaking a chemical bond endothermic or exothermic? Why?

▸ Show
Endothermic. Breaking a bond requires you to overcome the attractive forces holding the atoms together — you must put energy in. Conversely, making a bond releases energy (exothermic). This is why all reactions involve both: you break bonds in reactants (endothermic) and make bonds in products (exothermic). The net $\Delta H$ is the balance.

Question 2: In an exothermic reaction, which is larger — the energy released by bond-making or the energy absorbed by bond-breaking?

▸ Show
In an exothermic reaction, the energy released by making new bonds (in products) is larger than the energy absorbed when breaking old bonds (in reactants). The net release of energy means $\Delta H < 0$. In endothermic reactions, the opposite is true.

Question 3: What does "activation energy" ($E_a$) mean?

▸ Show
Activation energy ($E_a$) is the minimum energy that colliding particles must possess for a reaction to occur. It is the energy barrier between reactants and products — the energy required to reach the transition state (activated complex) where bonds are partially broken and partially formed.

2. Bond Enthalpy — The Energy of a Chemical Bond

2.1 Definition and Significance

Bond enthalpy is the energy required to break one mole of a particular covalent bond in the gaseous state. For example, breaking one mole of $\ce{H-H}$ bonds requires 436 kJ:

$$\ce{H2(g) -> 2H(g)}, \quad \Delta H = +436\text{ kJ mol}^{-1}$$

Since bond-breaking is always endothermic, bond enthalpies are always reported as positive values. Bond-making releases the exact same amount of energy (just with a negative sign).

Average bond enthalpy: The energy required to break one mole of a given bond, averaged across many different compounds containing that bond. For example, the $\ce{C-H}$ bond enthalpy (414 kJ mol⁻¹) is the average of $\ce{C-H}$ bonds in $\ce{CH4}$, $\ce{C2H6}$, $\ce{C2H4}$, etc. — each of which has a slightly different value.

2.2 Why "Average"?

The $\ce{C-H}$ bond in methane ($\ce{CH4}$) is not exactly the same as a $\ce{C-H}$ bond in ethene ($\ce{C2H4}$) or chloroform ($\ce{CHCl3}$). The surrounding atoms affect the bond strength. The IB Data Booklet gives average bond enthalpies — these are useful for estimating $\Delta H$, but calculations using them are approximate.

Check-in: The bond enthalpy of $\ce{H-Cl}$ is +431 kJ mol⁻¹. What is the enthalpy change for $\ce{H(g) + Cl(g) -> HCl(g)}$?

▸ Show
Making a bond is the reverse of breaking it, so $\Delta H = \mathbf{-431\text{ kJ mol}^{-1}}$. This is bond formation, which is always exothermic. The magnitude is the same as the bond enthalpy; only the sign changes.

3. Calculating ΔH from Bond Enthalpies

The fundamental principle: in any reaction, you break all the bonds in the reactants and form all the bonds in the products. The net enthalpy change is the difference:

ΔH from bond enthalpies: $$\Delta H = \sum E(\text{bonds broken}) - \sum E(\text{bonds formed})$$ $E$ = bond enthalpy (always positive). Bonds broken: endothermic (+). Bonds formed: exothermic (−). The net is their difference.
Figure 1: The bond enthalpy cycle. All bonds are broken to produce free gaseous atoms (up), then new bonds form to give products (down). $\Delta H$ is the algebraic sum.

Check-in: Calculate $\Delta H$ for $\ce{H2(g) + Cl2(g) -> 2HCl(g)}$ using bond enthalpies: $E(\ce{H-H}) = 436$, $E(\ce{Cl-Cl}) = 243$, $E(\ce{H-Cl}) = 431$ kJ mol⁻¹.

▸ Show
Bonds broken: 1 × $\ce{H-H}$ (+436) + 1 × $\ce{Cl-Cl}$ (+243) = +679 kJ. Bonds formed: 2 × $\ce{H-Cl}$ = 2 × (−431) = −862 kJ. $\Delta H = +679 + (-862) = \mathbf{-183\text{ kJ mol}^{-1}}$. The reaction is exothermic because stronger $\ce{H-Cl}$ bonds replace weaker $\ce{H-H}$ and $\ce{Cl-Cl}$ bonds.

3.1 O₂ and N₂ — Special Cases (HL)

When using bond enthalpies for reactions involving $\ce{O2}$ and $\ce{N2}$, you must use the correct bond order. $\ce{O2}$ has an $\ce{O=O}$ double bond ($E = 498$ kJ mol⁻¹), and $\ce{N2}$ has an $\ce{N#N}$ triple bond ($E = 945$ kJ mol⁻¹). The IB Data Booklet gives these values.

Check-in: Calculate $\Delta H$ for $\ce{N2(g) + 3H2(g) -> 2NH3(g)}$ using: $E(\ce{N#N}) = 945$, $E(\ce{H-H}) = 436$, $E(\ce{N-H}) = 391$ kJ mol⁻¹.

▸ Show
Bonds broken: 1 × $\ce{N#N}$ (945) + 3 × $\ce{H-H}$ (3 × 436 = 1308) = +2253 kJ. Bonds formed: in 2 $\ce{NH3}$, there are 2 × 3 = 6 $\ce{N-H}$ bonds. 6 × 391 = 2346 kJ formed = −2346 kJ. $\Delta H = 2253 - 2346 = \mathbf{-93\text{ kJ mol}^{-1}}$.

4. Energy Profiles — Visualising Reaction Energetics

4.1 The Energy Profile Diagram

An energy profile (also called a reaction coordinate diagram) plots enthalpy against the "progress of reaction". It shows:

  • Reactants: starting enthalpy level (left).
  • Products: ending enthalpy level (right).
  • Transition state: the highest-energy point — the "activated complex" where bonds are half-broken, half-formed.
  • Activation energy ($E_a$): the energy difference between reactants and the transition state.
  • $\Delta H$: the energy difference between reactants and products.
Figure 2: Energy profiles for exothermic (left) and endothermic (right) reactions. Note: for endothermic reactions, $E_a$ is always larger than $\Delta H$ because you must climb over the transition state before reaching the higher product level.

Check-in: On an energy profile for an exothermic reaction, the activation energy is 50 kJ mol⁻¹ and $\Delta H = -150$ kJ mol⁻¹. What is the activation energy of the reverse reaction?

▸ Show
The reverse $E_a = E_a(\text{forward}) + |\Delta H| = 50 + 150 = \mathbf{200\text{ kJ mol}^{-1}}$. The reverse reaction must climb from the product level back up to the transition state, which means crossing both the forward $E_a$ AND the $\Delta H$ gap.

5. Worked Examples

Example 1 — Combustion of Methane Using Bond Enthalpies:

Calculate $\Delta H$ for $\ce{CH4(g) + 2O2(g) -> CO2(g) + 2H2O(g)}$ using bond enthalpies (kJ mol⁻¹): $\ce{C-H} = 414$, $\ce{O=O} = 498$, $\ce{C=O} = 804$, $\ce{O-H} = 463$.

Step 1: Draw Lewis structures. $\ce{CH4}$: 4 $\ce{C-H}$ bonds. $\ce{O2}$: $\ce{O=O}$. $\ce{CO2}$: $\ce{O=C=O}$ (2 $\ce{C=O}$ bonds). $\ce{H2O}$: $\ce{H-O-H}$ (2 $\ce{O-H}$ bonds per molecule).
Step 2: Bonds broken. 4 × $\ce{C-H}$ = 4(414) = 1656. 2 × $\ce{O=O}$ = 2(498) = 996. Total = +2652 kJ.
Step 3: Bonds formed. 4 × $\ce{C=O}$ (2 per $\ce{CO2}$ but wait — $\ce{CO2}$ has 2 $\ce{C=O}$ bonds) = 2(804) = 1608. 4 × $\ce{O-H}$ (2 per $\ce{H2O}$, 2 molecules) = 4(463) = 1852. Total formed = 3460 kJ → −3460.
Step 4: $\Delta H = 2652 - 3460 = \mathbf{-808\text{ kJ mol}^{-1}}$.
Literature is −891; the difference is because average bond enthalpies are approximations and here we used $\ce{H2O(g)}$ rather than $\ce{H2O(l)}$.

⚠️ Common wrong approach: Miscounting the bonds in $\ce{CO2}$. Students sometimes count 2 $\ce{C=O}$ bonds as one "double bond enclosure" and forget there are 2 separate $\ce{C=O}$ bonds, each needing 804 kJ.
Example 2 — Reverse Activation Energy:

The forward reaction $\ce{A -> B}$ has $E_a = 75$ kJ mol⁻¹ and $\Delta H = -120$ kJ mol⁻¹. Calculate $E_a$ for the reverse reaction and sketch the energy profile.

Step 1: $\Delta H = -120$ means products are 120 kJ lower than reactants.
Step 2: Reverse $E_a$ = forward $E_a$ + $|\Delta H|$ = 75 + 120 = $\mathbf{195\text{ kJ mol}^{-1}}$.
Step 3 (sketch): Reactants at some reference (0). TS at +75. Products at −120. Reverse must climb from −120 up to +75 = 195 kJ.

⚠️ Common wrong approach: Thinking reverse $E_a$ = forward $E_a - |\Delta H|$. That would give 75 − 120 = −45, which is impossible (activation energies are always positive).
Example 3 — Ozone Decomposition:

Calculate $\Delta H$ for $\ce{2O3(g) -> 3O2(g)}$ using bond enthalpies. In $\ce{O3}$, there are 2 $\ce{O-O}$ bonds (one single, one with partial double-bond character — but use the average single-bond value of 146 kJ mol⁻¹). $\ce{O=O} = 498$.

Step 1: Bonds broken in 2 $\ce{O3}$: each $\ce{O3}$ has 2 $\ce{O-O}$ bonds → 4 × 146 = +584 kJ.
Step 2: Bonds formed in 3 $\ce{O2}$: each $\ce{O2}$ has 1 $\ce{O=O}$ → 3 × 498 = 1494 kJ formed = −1494 kJ.
Step 3: $\Delta H = 584 - 1494 = \mathbf{-910\text{ kJ mol}^{-1}}$ for 2 $\ce{O3}$ decomposing. Per mole of $\ce{O3}$: −455 kJ mol⁻¹. The reaction is highly exothermic — ozone is thermodynamically unstable relative to $\ce{O2}$.

⚠️ Common wrong approach: Using the wrong bond for $\ce{O3}$. Ozone has resonance — the actual bond order is 1.5, but IB uses the average $\ce{O-O}$ single bond enthalpy. Also, students miscount: 2 $\ce{O3}$ molecules means 4 $\ce{O-O}$ bonds broken, not 2.

⚠️ Common Mistakes to Avoid

  1. "Thinking bond-breaking releases energy." Breaking bonds ALWAYS requires energy (endothermic). Making bonds ALWAYS releases energy (exothermic). Many students confuse this because they associate combustion (burning) with "breaking bonds in the fuel" and assume breaking releases energy. The energy comes from the new bonds formed, not the old bonds broken.
  2. "Forgetting that bond enthalpy calculations require all species to be gaseous." Bond enthalpies are defined for the gaseous state. If products include $\ce{H2O(l)}$, you need to account for condensation energy. Using average bond enthalpies for reactions producing liquids introduces significant error.
  3. "Misidentifying the activation energy on a diagram." $E_a$ is always measured from the reactants to the transition state — not from some arbitrary baseline. For an endothermic reaction, $E_a$ is larger than $\Delta H$; for an exothermic reaction, $E_a$ may be smaller or larger than $|\Delta H|$.
  4. "Confusing bond enthalpy with bond polarity strength." A polar bond (e.g., $\ce{H-F}$) is not necessarily stronger because it's polar — but $\ce{H-F}$ (567 kJ mol⁻¹) is indeed stronger than $\ce{H-H}$ (436) because of the electronegativity difference adding ionic character to the covalent bond.

6. End-of-Lesson Practice

Question 1 (Paper 1 style): Which statement about bond enthalpies is correct?
A. Bond enthalpies are exact values for each specific molecule.
B. Bond-breaking is always exothermic.
C. Average bond enthalpies are used because bond strength varies slightly between different molecules.
D. Bond enthalpies can be used to calculate $\Delta H$ exactly, with no error.

Question 2: Calculate $\Delta H$ for $\ce{C2H4(g) + H2(g) -> C2H6(g)}$ using bond enthalpies (kJ mol⁻¹): $\ce{C=C} = 614$, $\ce{C-H} = 414$, $\ce{H-H} = 436$, $\ce{C-C} = 346$. (Note: $\ce{C2H4}$ has 1 $\ce{C=C}$ and 4 $\ce{C-H}$. $\ce{C2H6}$ has 1 $\ce{C-C}$ and 6 $\ce{C-H}$.)

Question 3: Calculate $\Delta H$ for $\ce{CH3OH(g) + 1.5 O2(g) -> CO2(g) + 2H2O(g)}$ using bond enthalpies. $\ce{CH3OH}$: 3 $\ce{C-H}$, 1 $\ce{C-O}$, 1 $\ce{O-H}$. Data: $\ce{C-H} = 414$, $\ce{C-O} = 358$, $\ce{O-H} = 463$, $\ce{O=O} = 498$, $\ce{C=O} = 804$.

Question 4: An exothermic reaction has $E_a(\text{forward}) = 85$ kJ mol⁻¹ and $\Delta H = -210$ kJ mol⁻¹. Calculate $E_a$ for the reverse reaction and state whether the reverse reaction is faster or slower than the forward (at the same temperature).

Question 5: Hydrazine is used as rocket fuel: $\ce{N2H4(g) + O2(g) -> N2(g) + 2H2O(g)}$. $\ce{N2H4}$ has 1 $\ce{N-N}$ single bond and 4 $\ce{N-H}$ bonds. $\ce{O2}$ has $\ce{O=O}$. $\ce{N2}$ has $\ce{N#N}$. $\ce{H2O}$ has 2 $\ce{O-H}$. Data (kJ mol⁻¹): $\ce{N-N} = 163$, $\ce{N-H} = 391$, $\ce{O=O} = 498$, $\ce{N#N} = 945$, $\ce{O-H} = 463$. Calculate $\Delta H$.

Question 6 (IB-style): The reaction $\ce{2HI(g) -> H2(g) + I2(g)}$ has $\Delta H = -9\text{ kJ mol}^{-1}$ (experimental).
(a) Calculate $\Delta H$ using bond enthalpies: $E(\ce{H-I}) = 299$, $E(\ce{H-H}) = 436$, $E(\ce{I-I}) = 151$ kJ mol⁻¹.
(b) Explain any difference between your calculated and the experimental value.
(c) Explain why this reaction has a very small $|\Delta H|$.

Answer 1: C. Bond enthalpies are averages across many compounds; they vary with molecular environment. A is wrong (they are average, not exact). B is wrong (breaking is endothermic). D is wrong (bond enthalpy calculations are approximate).

Answer 2: Bonds broken: 1 $\ce{C=C}$ (614) + 4 $\ce{C-H}$ (4×414 = 1656) + 1 $\ce{H-H}$ (436) = +2706 kJ. Bonds formed: 1 $\ce{C-C}$ (346) + 6 $\ce{C-H}$ (6×414 = 2484) = 2830 kJ → −2830 kJ. $\Delta H = 2706 - 2830 = \mathbf{-124\text{ kJ mol}^{-1}}$.

Answer 3: Bonds broken: 3 $\ce{C-H}$ (1242) + 1 $\ce{C-O}$ (358) + 1 $\ce{O-H}$ (463) + 1.5 $\ce{O=O}$ (747) = +2810 kJ. Bonds formed: 2 $\ce{C=O}$ in $\ce{CO2}$ (2×804 = 1608) + 4 $\ce{O-H}$ in 2 $\ce{H2O}$ (4×463 = 1852) = 3460 → −3460 kJ. $\Delta H = 2810 - 3460 = \mathbf{-650\text{ kJ mol}^{-1}}$.

Answer 4: Reverse $E_a = 85 + 210 = \mathbf{295\text{ kJ mol}^{-1}}$. The reverse reaction is much slower because it has a much higher activation energy — fewer molecules have enough energy to overcome this larger barrier at any given temperature.

Answer 5: Bonds broken: 1 $\ce{N-N}$ (163) + 4 $\ce{N-H}$ (1564) + 1 $\ce{O=O}$ (498) = +2225 kJ. Bonds formed: 1 $\ce{N#N}$ (945) + 4 $\ce{O-H}$ (1852) = 2797 → −2797 kJ. $\Delta H = 2225 - 2797 = \mathbf{-572\text{ kJ mol}^{-1}}$. The very strong $\ce{N#N}$ triple bond makes the products much more stable.

Answer 6: (a) Bonds broken: 2 $\ce{H-I}$ = 2(299) = +598 kJ. Bonds formed: 1 $\ce{H-H}$ (436) + 1 $\ce{I-I}$ (151) = 587 → −587 kJ. $\Delta H = 598 - 587 = +11\text{ kJ mol}^{-1}$. (b) The experimental value is −9, the calculated is +11. Difference is due to using average bond enthalpies — the actual $\ce{H-I}$ bond strength differs slightly. (c) $|\Delta H|$ is small because the bonds broken (2 H–I, total 598) and bonds formed (H–H + I–I, total 587) are very similar in energy — the reaction is nearly thermoneutral.

7. Key Terms

TermDefinition
Bond enthalpy ($E$)The energy required to break one mole of a covalent bond in the gaseous state; always positive
Average bond enthalpyThe average bond-breaking energy for a specific bond type, averaged over many different compounds
Bond-breakingAlways endothermic (+); requires energy input to overcome attractive forces between atoms
Bond-makingAlways exothermic (−); energy is released when new bonds form
Energy profileA diagram showing enthalpy change along the reaction coordinate; displays reactants, products, transition state, $E_a$, and $\Delta H$
Transition stateThe highest-energy arrangement of atoms along the reaction path; bonds are partially broken and partially formed (the activated complex)
Activation energy ($E_a$)The minimum energy required for reactants to reach the transition state and form products
Reverse activation energy$E_a(\text{reverse}) = E_a(\text{forward}) + |\Delta H|$ for exothermic; $E_a(\text{reverse}) = E_a(\text{forward}) - \Delta H$ for endothermic

8. Exam Tips

Paper 1 (Multiple Choice): Energy profile questions almost always appear. You must be able to look at a diagram and identify $E_a$, $\Delta H$, and whether the reaction is exothermic or endothermic. Also, be prepared for the "reverse $E_a$" calculation — a favourite one-mark question: "The forward $E_a$ is X and $\Delta H$ is Y. What is the reverse $E_a$?"
Paper 2 — Bond Enthalpy Calculations: Always draw the Lewis structure (or at least note bond counts) before plugging numbers into the formula. The most common loss of marks comes from miscounting bonds — especially in $\ce{CO2}$ (2 $\ce{C=O}$ bonds), $\ce{H2O}$ (2 $\ce{O-H}$ bonds), and $\ce{N2}$ (triple bond). Also, explicitly state "bonds broken" and "bonds formed" on separate lines — this earns method marks even if the arithmetic is wrong.
Paper 2 — Justifying Stability: When asked why a reaction is exothermic, link it to bond enthalpies: "The products have stronger bonds than the reactants, so more energy is released when product bonds form than is absorbed when reactant bonds break." Use specific bond enthalpy values to support your answer.
