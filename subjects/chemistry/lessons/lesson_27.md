## 1. Brønsted-Lowry Theory — A Way to Define Acids and Bases

### What is an acid? What is a base?

The first thing you need to know about acids and bases is how to recognise them. In 1923, two chemists named Johannes Brønsted and Thomas Lowry independently proposed the same idea. They said: when you see a chemical reaction, look for which species gives away a hydrogen ion and which species receives it.

A **hydrogen ion** is a single proton. Its chemical symbol is H⁺. (Sometimes you will see it written as H₃O⁺, which is a hydrogen ion attached to a water molecule. For most of our calculations, H⁺ and H₃O⁺ mean the same thing.)

A **Brønsted-Lowry acid** is any chemical species (an atom, a molecule, or an ion) that **gives away** a hydrogen ion. Think of it as a proton donor. The word "donor" means it hands over a proton to something else.

A **Brønsted-Lowry base** is any chemical species that **receives** a hydrogen ion. Think of it as a proton acceptor. The word "acceptor" means it takes a proton from something else.

This idea is more useful than older definitions because it does not require water to be present. It works for reactions in any solvent, and even for reactions in the gas phase.

### A concrete example

Let us look at what happens when hydrogen chloride gas dissolves in water. The chemical equation with state symbols is:

$$\ce{HCl(g) + H2O(l) -> H3O+(aq) + Cl-(aq)}$$

The symbol (g) means gas, (l) means liquid, and (aq) means aqueous — dissolved in water.

In this reaction, the hydrogen chloride molecule, HCl, gives away its hydrogen ion to the water molecule. Because HCl donates a proton, HCl is acting as the Brønsted-Lowry acid. The water molecule, H₂O, receives that proton. Because H₂O accepts a proton, H₂O is acting as the Brønsted-Lowry base.

Many students think that acids are always liquids that burn your skin, and bases are always slippery solids. But actually, according to Brønsted-Lowry theory, being an acid or a base is a **behaviour** — it is something a species does in a specific reaction, not something it always is. Water, for example, can act as a base (as it does here) or as an acid in other reactions.

### What the "p" in pH means

The letter "p" in chemistry notation is an operator that means "take the negative logarithm of whatever comes after it." So when you see "pH," it means the negative logarithm (base 10) of the hydrogen ion concentration. This is a mathematical tool for making very small numbers easier to work with. Instead of writing the hydrogen ion concentration as 0.0000001, we can write the pH as 7.

## 2. Conjugate Acid-Base Pairs — The Other Side of Every Reaction

### What "conjugate" means

When a Brønsted-Lowry acid gives away its proton, whatever is left behind is called its **conjugate base**. The word "conjugate" here means "linked together" or "paired." A conjugate base is simply the acid after it has lost a proton.

When a Brønsted-Lowry base receives a proton, the new species that forms is called its **conjugate acid**. It is the base after it has gained a proton.

A **conjugate acid-base pair** is two species that differ from each other by exactly one hydrogen ion. For example, HCl and Cl⁻ are a conjugate acid-base pair. HCl has one more H⁺ than Cl⁻ does. Every acid has a conjugate base, and every base has a conjugate acid.

### How to find conjugate pairs in a reaction

You can practise finding conjugate pairs by looking at a reaction and asking yourself two questions: Who lost a proton? Who gained a proton?

Here is a table showing some common conjugate pairs:

| Acid | Its Conjugate Base | Base | Its Conjugate Acid |
|------|---------------------|------|---------------------|
| HCl | Cl⁻ | NH₃ | NH₄⁺ |
| H₂SO₄ | HSO₄⁻ | OH⁻ | H₂O |
| CH₃COOH | CH₃COO⁻ | CO₃²⁻ | HCO₃⁻ |
| H₃O⁺ | H₂O | H₂O | H₃O⁺ |

Look carefully at the last row of the table. Water appears on both sides. It is listed as an acid (paired with OH⁻ as its conjugate base) AND as a base (paired with H₃O⁺ as its conjugate acid). This means water can behave as either an acid or a base depending on what it reacts with.

### Amphiprotic species

A species that can act as either a Brønsted-Lowry acid or a Brønsted-Lowry base is called **amphiprotic**. The word comes from "amphi" meaning "both" and "protic" referring to protons.

Water (H₂O) is amphiprotic. So are the hydrogen carbonate ion (HCO₃⁻), the hydrogen sulfate ion (HSO₄⁻), the dihydrogen phosphate ion (H₂PO₄⁻), and the hydrogen phosphate ion (HPO₄²⁻). You can recognise amphiprotic species because they contain at least one hydrogen atom that they could give away (acting as an acid) AND they carry a negative charge or a lone pair that could accept a proton (acting as a base).

<div class="worked">
<p><strong>Worked Example 1:</strong> A reaction occurs between ammonia and water: NH₃ + H₂O ⇌ NH₄⁺ + OH⁻. Identify the two conjugate acid-base pairs in this reaction.</p>

<p><strong>Strategy for solving this problem:</strong> We need to find who lost a proton and who gained a proton. Start by comparing the left side (reactants) and the right side (products) of the equation.</p>

<p>Look at NH₃ on the left. On the right side, NH₃ has become NH₄⁺. An NH₄⁺ ion has one more hydrogen than an NH₃ molecule. So NH₃ must have accepted a proton from somewhere. Since NH₃ accepted a proton, NH₃ is acting as a Brønsted-Lowry base. Its conjugate acid is NH₄⁺. Thus the first conjugate pair is NH₄⁺/NH₃.</p>

<p>Now look at H₂O on the left. On the right, H₂O has become OH⁻. An OH⁻ ion has one fewer hydrogen than an H₂O molecule. So H₂O must have donated a proton. Since H₂O donated a proton, H₂O is acting as a Brønsted-Lowry acid. Its conjugate base is OH⁻. Thus the second conjugate pair is H₂O/OH⁻.</p>

<p><strong>Why this makes sense:</strong> In any Brønsted-Lowry reaction, two conjugate pairs must exist. One species donates a proton (acid becoming conjugate base) and another species accepts that same proton (base becoming conjugate acid).</p>
</div>

## 3. pH and the Ionic Product of Water, Kw

### What pH actually measures

The **pH** of a solution is a number that tells you how acidic or basic (alkaline) that solution is. The pH scale usually runs from 0 to 14, although values outside this range are possible for very concentrated acids or bases.

The mathematical definition of pH is:

$$\text{pH} = -\log_{10}[\ce{H+}]$$

In this equation, [H⁺] means "the concentration of hydrogen ions" measured in moles per cubic decimetre (mol dm⁻³). The square brackets are a standard notation in chemistry meaning "concentration of." The function "log₁₀" is the base-10 logarithm. The negative sign means that a higher concentration of H⁺ gives a lower pH.

The reverse calculation is equally important. If you know the pH, you can find the hydrogen ion concentration:

$$[\ce{H+}] = 10^{-\text{pH}} \ \text{mol dm}^{-3}$$

### What the numbers mean

Here is how pH values relate to hydrogen ion concentration:

| pH value | [H⁺] in mol dm⁻³ | What this means |
|----------|-------------------|-----------------|
| 0 | 1.0 | Very strongly acidic |
| 1 | 0.1 | Strongly acidic |
| 3 | 0.001 | Acidic |
| 5 | 1.0 × 10⁻⁵ | Weakly acidic |
| 7 | 1.0 × 10⁻⁷ | Neutral |
| 9 | 1.0 × 10⁻⁹ | Weakly basic |
| 11 | 1.0 × 10⁻¹¹ | Basic |
| 14 | 1.0 × 10⁻¹⁴ | Very strongly basic |

Notice the pattern: every time the pH goes down by 1 unit, the hydrogen ion concentration becomes 10 times larger. A solution with pH 3 has ten times more H⁺ than a solution with pH 4.

### The ionic product of water, Kw

Even the purest water you can find contains a tiny number of hydrogen ions and hydroxide ions. This is because water molecules can transfer a proton from one water molecule to another. This process is called the **self-ionisation of water**:

$$\ce{2H2O(l) <=> H3O+(aq) + OH-(aq)}$$

The double arrow (⇌) means this is an equilibrium — the forward reaction and the reverse reaction happen at the same time.

For this equilibrium, we define a constant called **Kw**, the ionic product of water. At a temperature of 298 K (which is 25 degrees Celsius), Kw has a fixed value:

$$K_w = [\ce{H+}][\ce{OH-}] = 1.0 \times 10^{-14} \ \text{mol}^2\text{dm}^{-6}$$

This equation tells us something very important: the product of [H⁺] and [OH⁻] in ANY aqueous solution at 298 K must always equal 1.0 × 10⁻¹⁴. If you know the concentration of H⁺, you can calculate the concentration of OH⁻, and the other way around.

In pure water at 298 K, the concentrations of H⁺ and OH⁻ must be equal (because the self-ionisation equation produces one of each). Therefore:

[H⁺] = [OH⁻] = √(1.0 × 10⁻¹⁴) = 1.0 × 10⁻⁷ mol dm⁻³

And pH = −log(1.0 × 10⁻⁷) = 7. This is why pH 7 is called "neutral."

A common misconception is that any solution with pH 7 is neutral at all temperatures. This is not true. The value of Kw changes with temperature. At 298 K, pH 7 is neutral, but at higher temperatures, neutrality occurs at a pH lower than 7 because Kw becomes larger.

<div class="worked">
<p><strong>Worked Example 2:</strong> A solution at 298 K has a hydrogen ion concentration of 2.0 × 10⁻⁵ mol dm⁻³. Calculate (a) the pH of the solution and (b) the hydroxide ion concentration.</p>

<p><strong>Strategy for part (a):</strong> Use pH = −log[H⁺]. Since we have a number in scientific notation (2.0 × 10⁻⁵), we can use the logarithm rule: log(A × B) = log(A) + log(B).</p>

<p>pH = −log(2.0 × 10⁻⁵)</p>
<p>pH = −[log(2.0) + log(10⁻⁵)]</p>
<p>pH = −[0.30 + (−5.0)]</p>
<p>pH = −[−4.70]</p>
<p>pH = 4.70</p>

<p><strong>Strategy for part (b):</strong> Use Kw = [H⁺][OH⁻] = 1.0 × 10⁻¹⁴. Rearranging gives [OH⁻] = Kw ÷ [H⁺].</p>

<p>[OH⁻] = (1.0 × 10⁻¹⁴) ÷ (2.0 × 10⁻⁵)</p>
<p>[OH⁻] = (1.0 ÷ 2.0) × (10⁻¹⁴ ÷ 10⁻⁵)</p>
<p>[OH⁻] = 0.50 × 10⁻⁹</p>
<p>[OH⁻] = 5.0 × 10⁻¹⁰ mol dm⁻³</p>

<p><strong>Why this makes sense:</strong> A pH of 4.70 is acidic, so we expect [H⁺] to be much larger than [OH⁻]. Indeed, 2.0 × 10⁻⁵ is 100,000 times larger than 5.0 × 10⁻¹⁰. Their product (2.0 × 10⁻⁵) × (5.0 × 10⁻¹⁰) = 10 × 10⁻¹⁵ = 1.0 × 10⁻¹⁴, which is exactly Kw.</p>
</div>

## 4. Strong Acids — Complete Dissociation

### What "strong" means for an acid

The word "strong" in chemistry has a very specific meaning that is different from everyday language. A **strong acid** is an acid that dissociates completely when it dissolves in water. "Dissociate" means the acid molecules split apart into ions. "Completely" means that every single acid molecule breaks apart — there are no undissociated acid molecules left in the solution.

This is different from "concentrated," which tells you how much acid was dissolved. You can have a dilute strong acid (a small amount of HCl dissolved in lots of water) or a concentrated weak acid (a large amount of ethanoic acid dissolved in water, where most molecules stay intact).

### The common strong acids

For IB Chemistry HL, you need to know these strong acids:

Hydrochloric acid: HCl (aq) → H⁺ (aq) + Cl⁻ (aq)

Nitric acid: HNO₃ (aq) → H⁺ (aq) + NO₃⁻ (aq)

Sulfuric acid (first dissociation): H₂SO₄ (aq) → H⁺ (aq) + HSO₄⁻ (aq)

Notice that I wrote a single arrow (→) for these reactions rather than a double arrow (⇌). The single arrow means the reaction goes to completion — it does not establish an equilibrium because there is nothing to establish an equilibrium with. All of the acid dissociates.

Sulfuric acid, H₂SO₄, is a diprotic acid, meaning it has two hydrogen ions it can give away. The first hydrogen ion comes off completely (strong acid behaviour). The second hydrogen ion comes off the HSO₄⁻ ion and this dissociation is only partial — it behaves as a weak acid. For IB HL, when asked about H₂SO₄ pH calculations, the syllabus typically expects you to treat both protons as fully dissociated unless told otherwise.

### Calculating pH of a strong acid

Because a strong monoprotic acid (an acid with one H⁺ to give away) dissociates completely, the concentration of H⁺ in the solution equals the original concentration of the acid.

<div class="worked">
<p><strong>Worked Example 3:</strong> A student prepares a solution by dissolving hydrogen chloride gas in water to make a solution with a concentration of 0.010 mol dm⁻³. Calculate the pH of this solution.</p>

<p><strong>Strategy:</strong> HCl is a strong monoprotic acid. Every HCl molecule produces exactly one H⁺ ion. Therefore [H⁺] equals the original concentration of HCl.</p>

<p>[H⁺] = 0.010 mol dm⁻³</p>
<p>pH = −log(0.010)</p>
<p>Since 0.010 = 1.0 × 10⁻², this becomes:</p>
<p>pH = −log(1.0 × 10⁻²) = −(−2.00) = 2.00</p>

<p><strong>Why this makes sense:</strong> A concentration of 0.010 mol dm⁻³ is exactly 10⁻² mol dm⁻³, so pH should be exactly 2. The calculation confirms this.</p>
</div>

<div class="worked">
<p><strong>Worked Example 4:</strong> Calculate the pH of a solution of sulfuric acid, H₂SO₄, with a concentration of 0.050 mol dm⁻³. Assume both protons dissociate completely for this calculation.</p>

<p><strong>Strategy:</strong> H₂SO₄ is a diprotic acid. Each molecule can give away two H⁺ ions. So [H⁺] is twice the concentration of the acid.</p>

<p>[H⁺] = 2 × 0.050 = 0.10 mol dm⁻³</p>
<p>pH = −log(0.10)</p>
<p>Since 0.10 = 1.0 × 10⁻¹:</p>
<p>pH = −log(1.0 × 10⁻¹) = −(−1.00) = 1.00</p>

<p><strong>Why this makes sense:</strong> Doubling the H⁺ concentration from 0.050 to 0.10 mol dm⁻³ causes only a small change in pH (from 1.30 to 1.00) because pH is logarithmic. This is an important pattern: a factor of 10 in concentration gives a change of 1 in pH.</p>
</div>

## 5. Strong Bases — Complete Dissociation

### What "strong" means for a base

A **strong base** is a base that dissociates completely when it dissolves in water, releasing hydroxide ions, OH⁻. Just as with strong acids, every formula unit of the base separates into its ions.

The strong bases you need to know for IB Chemistry HL are:

Sodium hydroxide: NaOH (s) → Na⁺ (aq) + OH⁻ (aq)

Potassium hydroxide: KOH (s) → K⁺ (aq) + OH⁻ (aq)

Barium hydroxide: Ba(OH)₂ (s) → Ba²⁺ (aq) + 2OH⁻ (aq)

Notice a crucial detail: sodium hydroxide and potassium hydroxide each produce one OH⁻ ion per formula unit. Barium hydroxide produces two OH⁻ ions per formula unit. When calculating [OH⁻], you must multiply the concentration of Ba(OH)₂ by 2.

### Calculating pH of a strong base

For a strong base, you do not calculate pH directly because pH uses [H⁺]. Instead, you follow a three-step process: (1) Find [OH⁻] from the base concentration, remembering to account for the number of OH⁻ ions per formula unit. (2) Use Kw to find [H⁺]. (3) Convert [H⁺] to pH.

<div class="worked">
<p><strong>Worked Example 5:</strong> Calculate the pH of a sodium hydroxide solution with a concentration of 0.0010 mol dm⁻³ at 298 K.</p>

<p><strong>Strategy:</strong> NaOH is a strong base that produces one OH⁻ per formula unit.</p>

<p>Step 1 — Find [OH⁻]:</p>
<p>[OH⁻] = concentration of NaOH = 0.0010 = 1.0 × 10⁻³ mol dm⁻³</p>

<p>Step 2 — Use Kw to find [H⁺]:</p>
<p>[H⁺] = Kw ÷ [OH⁻] = (1.0 × 10⁻¹⁴) ÷ (1.0 × 10⁻³)</p>
<p>[H⁺] = 1.0 × 10⁻(¹⁴⁻³) = 1.0 × 10⁻¹¹ mol dm⁻³</p>

<p>Step 3 — Convert to pH:</p>
<p>pH = −log(1.0 × 10⁻¹¹) = 11.00</p>

<p><strong>Why this makes sense:</strong> A base with [OH⁻] = 10⁻³ should produce a pH well above 7. Since [H⁺] = 10⁻¹¹, the pH is 11, which fits the pattern: 14 − pOH = 14 − 3 = 11, where pOH = −log[OH⁻].</p>
</div>

<div class="worked">
<p><strong>Worked Example 6:</strong> Calculate the pH of a barium hydroxide solution with a concentration of 0.020 mol dm⁻³ at 298 K.</p>

<p><strong>Strategy:</strong> Ba(OH)₂ produces two OH⁻ ions per formula unit. So [OH⁻] is twice the given concentration.</p>

<p>Step 1 — Find [OH⁻]:</p>
<p>[OH⁻] = 2 × 0.020 = 0.040 mol dm⁻³</p>

<p>Step 2 — Find [H⁺]:</p>
<p>[H⁺] = (1.0 × 10⁻¹⁴) ÷ 0.040</p>
<p>[H⁺] = (1.0 × 10⁻¹⁴) ÷ (4.0 × 10⁻²)</p>
<p>[H⁺] = (1.0 ÷ 4.0) × (10⁻¹⁴ ÷ 10⁻²)</p>
<p>[H⁺] = 0.25 × 10⁻¹²</p>
<p>[H⁺] = 2.5 × 10⁻¹³ mol dm⁻³</p>

<p>Step 3 — Convert to pH:</p>
<p>pH = −log(2.5 × 10⁻¹³) = −[log(2.5) + log(10⁻¹³)]</p>
<p>pH = −[0.40 + (−13.0)] = −(−12.60) = 12.60</p>

<p><strong>Why this makes sense:</strong> This base is more concentrated than the NaOH in Worked Example 5, and it produces twice as many OH⁻ ions. We expect a pH higher than 11.00, and 12.60 is indeed higher.</p>
</div>

### Dilution — What happens when you add water

When you add water to an acid or base solution, you are diluting it. The amount (number of moles) of acid or base stays the same, but the volume increases, so the concentration decreases. To calculate the new concentration after dilution, use the formula:

C₁V₁ = C₂V₂

In this formula, C₁ is the original concentration, V₁ is the original volume, C₂ is the new concentration after dilution, and V₂ is the new total volume. The units for volume must be the same on both sides (both in cm³ or both in dm³).

<div class="worked">
<p><strong>Worked Example 7:</strong> A student takes 25.0 cm³ of hydrochloric acid with a concentration of 0.10 mol dm⁻³ and adds enough distilled water to make the total volume 250 cm³. Calculate the pH of the diluted solution.</p>

<p><strong>Strategy:</strong> First find the new concentration using C₁V₁ = C₂V₂. Then calculate pH.</p>

<p>C₂ = (C₁ × V₁) ÷ V₂</p>
<p>C₂ = (0.10 × 25.0) ÷ 250</p>
<p>C₂ = 2.5 ÷ 250</p>
<p>C₂ = 0.010 mol dm⁻³</p>

<p>Now [H⁺] = 0.010 mol dm⁻³ (since HCl is a strong monoprotic acid).</p>
<p>pH = −log(0.010) = 2.00</p>

<p><strong>Why this makes sense:</strong> The volume increased by a factor of 10 (from 25 cm³ to 250 cm³), so the concentration decreased by a factor of 10 (from 0.10 to 0.010 mol dm⁻³). Therefore the pH increased by exactly 1 unit (from 1.00 to 2.00).</p>
</div>

## Answers

### Answer 1

In the reaction NH₃ (aq) + H₂O (l) ⇌ NH₄⁺ (aq) + OH⁻ (aq), we need to identify who donates a proton and who accepts a proton.

First conjugate pair: The NH₃ molecule accepts a proton and becomes NH₄⁺. Since NH₃ accepts a proton, NH₃ is acting as a Brønsted-Lowry base. NH₄⁺ is the conjugate acid of NH₃. The conjugate pair is NH₄⁺ (acid) and NH₃ (conjugate base). Note that in a conjugate pair, the acid always has one more H⁺ than its conjugate base.

Second conjugate pair: The H₂O molecule donates a proton and becomes OH⁻. Since H₂O donates a proton, H₂O is acting as a Brønsted-Lowry acid. OH⁻ is the conjugate base of H₂O. The conjugate pair is H₂O (acid) and OH⁻ (conjugate base).

A common mistake is to say that NH₃ is the acid because it contains hydrogen atoms. But what matters is not how many hydrogens a species starts with — what matters is whether it gains or loses a proton in the reaction. NH₃ gains a proton (from three H to four H), so it is the base, not the acid.

### Answer 2

**(a)** For Solution X with pH 3.50, we use [H⁺] = 10⁻ᵖᴴ = 10⁻³·⁵⁰.

[H⁺] = 10⁻³·⁵⁰. We split this: 10⁻³·⁵⁰ = 10⁻⁰·⁵⁰ × 10⁻³.

Since 10⁻⁰·⁵⁰ = 1 ÷ 10⁰·⁵⁰ = 1 ÷ √10 ≈ 1 ÷ 3.16 ≈ 0.316:

[H⁺] = 0.316 × 10⁻³ = 3.16 × 10⁻⁴ mol dm⁻³.

**(b)** For Solution Z with pH 10.20, we use [H⁺] = 10⁻¹⁰·²⁰.

[H⁺] = 10⁻¹⁰·²⁰ = 10⁻⁰·²⁰ × 10⁻¹⁰.

Since 10⁻⁰·²⁰ ≈ 0.63:

[H⁺] = 0.63 × 10⁻¹⁰ = 6.3 × 10⁻¹¹ mol dm⁻³.

**(c)** For Solution X, using Kw = [H⁺][OH⁻] = 1.0 × 10⁻¹⁴:

[OH⁻] = Kw ÷ [H⁺] = (1.0 × 10⁻¹⁴) ÷ (3.16 × 10⁻⁴) = 3.16 × 10⁻¹¹ mol dm⁻³.

**(d)** Solution X is acidic because [H⁺] (3.16 × 10⁻⁴ mol dm⁻³) is much larger than [OH⁻] (3.16 × 10⁻¹¹ mol dm⁻³). In an acidic solution, the hydrogen ion concentration is greater than the hydroxide ion concentration. In a basic solution, [OH⁻] would be greater than [H⁺], and in a neutral solution they would be equal at 1.0 × 10⁻⁷ mol dm⁻³ each.

### Answer 3

**(a)** Nitric acid, HNO₃, is a strong monoprotic acid. It dissociates completely: HNO₃ (aq) → H⁺ (aq) + NO₃⁻ (aq).

[H⁺] = 0.0050 = 5.0 × 10⁻³ mol dm⁻³.

pH = −log(5.0 × 10⁻³) = −[log(5.0) + log(10⁻³)] = −[0.70 + (−3.00)] = −(−2.30) = 2.30.

**(b)** HCl is a strong monoprotic acid. [H⁺] = 0.10 mol dm⁻³.

pH = −log(0.10) = −log(1.0 × 10⁻¹) = 1.00.

**(c)** NaOH is a strong base. [OH⁻] = 0.040 mol dm⁻³.

[H⁺] = (1.0 × 10⁻¹⁴) ÷ 0.040 = 2.5 × 10⁻¹³ mol dm⁻³.

pH = −log(2.5 × 10⁻¹³) = −[log(2.5) + log(10⁻¹³)] = −[0.40 + (−13.00)] = −(−12.60) = 12.60.

**(d)** Ba(OH)₂ produces two OH⁻ ions per formula unit. [OH⁻] = 2 × 0.010 = 0.020 mol dm⁻³.

[H⁺] = (1.0 × 10⁻¹⁴) ÷ 0.020 = 5.0 × 10⁻¹³ mol dm⁻³.

pH = −log(5.0 × 10⁻¹³) = −[log(5.0) + log(10⁻¹³)] = −[0.70 + (−13.00)] = −(−12.30) = 12.30.

### Answer 4

First, calculate the number of moles of each reactant before mixing.

Moles of HCl = concentration × volume = 0.20 mol dm⁻³ × (50.0 ÷ 1000) dm³ = 0.20 × 0.0500 = 0.010 mol.

Moles of NaOH = 0.10 mol dm⁻³ × (50.0 ÷ 1000) dm³ = 0.10 × 0.0500 = 0.0050 mol.

The neutralisation reaction HCl (aq) + NaOH (aq) → NaCl (aq) + H₂O (l) has a 1:1 stoichiometric ratio. This means 1 mole of HCl reacts with exactly 1 mole of NaOH.

Since we have 0.010 mol of HCl and only 0.0050 mol of NaOH, the NaOH is the limiting reactant. All of the NaOH reacts, and we have leftover HCl.

Moles of HCl remaining = 0.010 − 0.0050 = 0.0050 mol.

The total volume after mixing = 50.0 + 50.0 = 100.0 cm³ = 0.100 dm³.

[H⁺] = moles of leftover HCl ÷ total volume = 0.0050 ÷ 0.100 = 0.050 mol dm⁻³.

pH = −log(0.050) = −log(5.0 × 10⁻²) = −[0.70 + (−2.00)] = −(−1.30) = 1.30.

A common mistake here is forgetting to convert the volume from cm³ to dm³ when calculating moles. Remember: 1 dm³ = 1000 cm³, so to convert cm³ to dm³, divide by 1000.

### Answer 5

**(a)** A Brønsted-Lowry base is any chemical species that accepts a hydrogen ion (H⁺, also called a proton) from another species. An example of a Brønsted-Lowry base is the ammonia molecule, NH₃. Another valid example is the hydroxide ion, OH⁻, or the carbonate ion, CO₃²⁻.

**(b)** The conjugate acid of HPO₄²⁻ is H₂PO₄⁻. This species is formed when HPO₄²⁻ accepts one proton (H⁺). The formula H₂PO₄⁻ has one more H and one less negative charge than HPO₄²⁻, reflecting the addition of H⁺ (which carries a +1 charge).

The conjugate base of HPO₄²⁻ is PO₄³⁻. This species is formed when HPO₄²⁻ donates one proton. The formula PO₄³⁻ has one fewer H and one more negative charge than HPO₄²⁻, reflecting the loss of H⁺.

**(c)** Given [OH⁻] = 2.5 × 10⁻³ mol dm⁻³ and Kw = 1.0 × 10⁻¹⁴ at 298 K:

[H⁺] = Kw ÷ [OH⁻] = (1.0 × 10⁻¹⁴) ÷ (2.5 × 10⁻³) = 4.0 × 10⁻¹² mol dm⁻³.

pH = −log(4.0 × 10⁻¹²) = −[log(4.0) + log(10⁻¹²)] = −[0.60 + (−12.00)] = −(−11.40) = 11.40.

**(d)** The student's statement that water is a neutral substance because it has pH 7 is only partially correct. It is true that pure water at 298 K has pH 7. However, describing water simply as "neutral" misses a deeper chemical truth: water is amphiprotic. An amphiprotic species is one that can behave as either a Brønsted-Lowry acid or a Brønsted-Lowry base, depending on what it reacts with.

When water reacts with a base such as ammonia, water acts as an acid by donating a proton:

H₂O (l) + NH₃ (aq) ⇌ OH⁻ (aq) + NH₄⁺ (aq)

When water reacts with an acid such as hydrogen chloride, water acts as a base by accepting a proton:

H₂O (l) + HCl (g) → H₃O⁺ (aq) + Cl⁻ (aq)

In the first equation, water gives away H⁺ and becomes OH⁻ — it is an acid. In the second equation, water receives H⁺ and becomes H₃O⁺ — it is a base. This dual behaviour is what "amphiprotic" means, and it is a fundamental property of water that makes it an excellent solvent for acid-base reactions.
