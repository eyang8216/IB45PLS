Lesson 39: Aldehydes, Ketones, Carboxylic Acids & Esters

Syllabus reference: 10.2, 20.2 (Carbonyl Compounds, Carboxylic Acids and Derivatives)

Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to:

  • Distinguish aldehydes from ketones using Tollens' reagent, Fehling's solution, and acidified dichromate(VI).
  • Draw the nucleophilic addition mechanism for carbonyl compounds with $\ce{HCN}$ and $\ce{NaBH4}$.
  • Explain the acidity of carboxylic acids and the effect of electronegative substituents on $pK_a$.
  • Describe esterification (Fischer) and ester hydrolysis under acidic and basic conditions.
  • Predict the products of condensation reactions: esterification and amide formation.

1. Prior Knowledge Check

Question 1: What is the structural difference between an aldehyde and a ketone?

▸ Show
Both contain the carbonyl group $\ce{>C=O}$. In an aldehyde, the carbonyl carbon is bonded to at least one H atom and the group is always terminal ($\ce{–CHO}$). In a ketone, the carbonyl carbon is bonded to two carbon atoms — it is never terminal. This structural difference is why aldehydes can be oxidised and ketones cannot (under normal conditions).

Question 2: What does it mean when a compound is described as a "weak acid," and what is the approximate $pK_a$ of a carboxylic acid?

▸ Show
A weak acid partially dissociates in water: $\ce{RCOOH + H2O <=> RCOO- + H3O+}$. Carboxylic acids have $pK_a$ ≈ 4.8 (ethanoic acid). This is much stronger than alcohols ($pK_a$ ≈ 16) due to resonance stabilisation of the carboxylate anion $\ce{RCOO-}$ — the negative charge is delocalised over two oxygen atoms, making the conjugate base more stable.

Question 3: What type of reaction is esterification, and what is the role of the acid catalyst?

▸ Show
Esterification is a condensation reaction (specifically a nucleophilic acyl substitution): $\ce{RCOOH + R'OH <=> RCOOR' + H2O}$. The acid catalyst ($\ce{H2SO4}$ or $\ce{HCl}$) protonates the carbonyl oxygen, making the carbonyl carbon more electrophilic and susceptible to nucleophilic attack by the alcohol. The reaction is reversible — the same catalyst also catalyses the reverse (hydrolysis).

2. The Carbonyl Group — Structure and Reactivity

The carbonyl group $\ce{>C=O}$ is the most important functional group in organic chemistry. It is polar: oxygen is more electronegative than carbon, creating a significant dipole. The carbon is electron-deficient ($\delta+$) and is the site of nucleophilic attack.

Figure 1: The carbonyl group. The polarised $\ce{C=O}$ bond makes the carbonyl carbon susceptible to nucleophilic attack — the defining reaction of aldehydes and ketones.

2.1 Distinguishing Aldehydes from Ketones

The key difference: aldehydes are reducing agents; ketones are not (under normal conditions). This is the basis of all distinguishing tests.

Check-in: Why does Tollens' reagent give a positive test for aldehydes but not ketones, even though both contain a carbonyl group?

▸ Show
Aldehydes have a hydrogen atom on the carbonyl carbon ($\ce{–CHO}$). This C–H bond can be cleaved during oxidation — the aldehyde loses two electrons (oxidation: +1 → +3 for the carbonyl carbon). Ketones have no such C–H bond — the carbonyl carbon is bonded to two other carbons. Oxidising a ketone would require breaking a C–C bond, which Tollens' reagent is not strong enough to do. The test is therefore specific for aldehydes.

2.2 Nucleophilic Addition to Carbonyl Compounds

The carbonyl carbon is $sp^2$ hybridised, trigonal planar, with an empty $\pi^*$ antibonding orbital that accepts electron density from nucleophiles. This is the nucleophilic addition mechanism — fundamentally different from nucleophilic substitution (which occurs at $sp^3$ centres).

Figure 2: Nucleophilic addition to the carbonyl group. The first step (rate-determining) involves attack by the nucleophile on the trigonal planar carbonyl carbon, forming a tetrahedral alkoxide intermediate. The second step (protonation) is fast.

Check-in: Why are aldehydes more reactive than ketones towards nucleophilic addition?

▸ Show
Two reasons: (1) Steric: aldehydes have one alkyl group and one small H atom flanking the carbonyl; ketones have two alkyl groups, making the transition state more crowded for the incoming nucleophile. (2) Electronic: alkyl groups are electron-donating (+I effect), which reduces the $\delta+$ charge on the carbonyl carbon. Ketones, having two electron-donating groups, have a less electrophilic carbonyl carbon than aldehydes (one alkyl + one H, which has no +I effect).

3. Carboxylic Acids — Acidity and Derivatives

Carboxylic acids contain the carboxyl group $\ce{–COOH}$ — a carbonyl with a hydroxyl on the same carbon. This combination gives carboxylic acids their characteristic acidity.

3.1 Why Are Carboxylic Acids Acidic?

The $\ce{O–H}$ bond in a carboxylic acid is much more polarised than in an alcohol because the conjugate base ($\ce{RCOO-}$, the carboxylate ion) is stabilised by resonance. The negative charge is delocalised equally over both oxygen atoms — this makes the conjugate base more stable, shifting the equilibrium towards dissociation.

Figure 3: Resonance in the carboxylate ion. The negative charge is delocalised over both oxygen atoms, giving two equivalent C–O bonds (each 1.5 bond order). This delocalisation energy (~120 kJ mol⁻¹) is why carboxylic acids are ~10¹¹ times more acidic than alcohols.

4. Esters — Formation and Hydrolysis

4.1 Fischer Esterification

Esters are formed by the condensation reaction between a carboxylic acid and an alcohol, catalysed by a strong acid:

$$\ce{RCOOH + R'OH <=>[H+][\Delta] RCOOR' + H2O}$$
Figure 4: Fischer esterification. The –OH comes from the acid; the –H of the alcohol's –OH is lost as water. The acid catalyst protonates the carbonyl oxygen, making the carbonyl carbon more electrophilic.

4.2 Ester Hydrolysis — Acidic vs. Basic

Acidic hydrolysis: reverse of esterification. Heat ester with dilute $\ce{HCl}$ or $\ce{H2SO4}$ → carboxylic acid + alcohol. Reversible.

Basic hydrolysis (saponification): heat ester with $\ce{NaOH(aq)}$ → carboxylate salt + alcohol. Irreversible because the carboxylate ion is resonance-stabilised and does not react back with the alcohol.

$$\ce{RCOOR' + NaOH -> RCOO- Na+ + R'OH}$$

Check-in: Why is base-catalysed ester hydrolysis irreversible, while acid-catalysed is reversible?

▸ Show
In basic hydrolysis, the product is a carboxylate ion ($\ce{RCOO-}$), not the carboxylic acid. The carboxylate is resonance-stabilised — the negative charge is delocalised, making it a very poor electrophile. It does not react with the alcohol (a weak nucleophile) to re-form the ester. In acidic hydrolysis, the product is the protonated carboxylic acid, which can be attacked again by the alcohol — the reaction reaches equilibrium. Basic hydrolysis "traps" the product as an unreactive ion.

5. Worked Examples

Example 1 — Distinguishing carbonyl compounds:

You are given three unlabelled bottles: propanal, propanone, and propanoic acid. Describe a series of tests to identify each.

Test 1: Add $\ce{NaHCO3(aq)}$. Propanoic acid effervesces ($\ce{CO2}$ gas). Propanal and propanone: no reaction.
Test 2: For the remaining two, add Tollens' reagent and warm. Propanal gives a silver mirror. Propanone gives no reaction.
Alternative: $\ce{K2Cr2O7/H+}$ — propanoic acid (already oxidised, no change), propanal (orange → green), propanone (no change). Then Tollens' to confirm propanal vs. propanone.

⚠️ Common wrong approach: Trying to distinguish propanal and propanone with $\ce{NaHCO3}$. Neither is acidic enough to react. Use the specific aldehyde test (Tollens' or Fehling's).
Example 2 — Nucleophilic addition mechanism (Paper 2 style):

Draw the mechanism for the reduction of propanone with $\ce{NaBH4}$ followed by acid hydrolysis.

Step 1: $\ce{BH4-}$ acts as a source of $\ce{H-}$ (hydride, a strong nucleophile). $\ce{H-}$ attacks the carbonyl carbon of propanone. Curly arrow: $\ce{H-}$ lone pair → C. Simultaneously, curly arrow: $\ce{C=O}$ $\pi$ bond → O (making $\ce{O-}$). The alkoxide $\ce{(CH3)2CH–O-}$ forms.
Step 2 (hydrolysis): Add dilute acid (or water). $\ce{H+}$ protonates $\ce{O-}$ → propan-2-ol $\ce{(CH3)2CHOH}$.
Stereochemistry: Propanone has two identical methyl groups — the carbonyl carbon is not prochiral, so no stereoisomerism results. But for unsymmetrical ketones like butanone, $\ce{H-}$ can attack from either face → racemic mixture.

⚠️ Common wrong approach: Writing $\ce{NaBH4}$ as $\ce{Na+ BH4-}$ and having $\ce{Na+}$ participate in the mechanism. $\ce{Na+}$ is a spectator ion. Also, confusing $\ce{NaBH4}$ (source of $\ce{H-}$) with $\ce{H2/Ni}$ (catalytic hydrogenation) — they are different reagents with different mechanisms.
Example 3 — Ester hydrolysis product prediction:

Methyl propanoate ($\ce{CH3CH2COOCH3}$) is heated under reflux with excess $\ce{NaOH(aq)}$. The resulting solution is then acidified with $\ce{HCl(aq)}$.
(a) What is the initial product after basic hydrolysis?
(b) What is the final product after acidification?

(a) Basic hydrolysis (saponification): $\ce{CH3CH2COOCH3 + NaOH -> CH3CH2COO- Na+ + CH3OH}$. Products: sodium propanoate and methanol.
(b) Adding $\ce{HCl}$ protonates the carboxylate: $\ce{CH3CH2COO- + H+ -> CH3CH2COOH}$. Final products: propanoic acid and methanol.

⚠️ Common wrong approach: Writing propanoic acid directly from the basic hydrolysis. In basic conditions, the product is the salt (carboxylate), not the acid. The acid is obtained only after acidification.

⚠️ Common Mistakes to Avoid

  1. Tollens' reagent oxidises ONLY aldehydes, not all carbonyls. It specifically tests for the aldehyde functional group ($\ce{–CHO}$). It will NOT react with ketones, carboxylic acids, or esters. A common error: "Tollens' tests for carbonyls" — wrong; it tests specifically for aldehydes.
  2. Nucleophilic addition vs. nucleophilic substitution. Addition occurs at $\ce{C=O}$ ($sp^2$); substitution occurs at $\ce{C–X}$ ($sp^3$). The mechanisms are different. Don't draw a leaving group departing from a carbonyl — the $\pi$ bond breaks instead, forming a tetrahedral intermediate.
  3. Acid-catalysed esterification is reversible. The same catalyst catalyses BOTH forward and reverse reactions. In the exam, if asked how to maximise ester yield, answer: use excess alcohol (cheaper reactant) or remove water by distillation (Le Chatelier). Don't say "use more catalyst."
  4. $\ce{NaBH4}$ reduces aldehydes and ketones but NOT carboxylic acids or esters. $\ce{NaBH4}$ is a mild reducing agent. To reduce carboxylic acids or esters to alcohols, you need $\ce{LiAlH4}$ (stronger, in dry ether). Know the selectivity.
  5. Carboxylic acid $pK_a$ and substituent effects. Electron-withdrawing groups near the –COOH increase acidity (lower $pK_a$). $\ce{Cl3CCOOH}$ ($pK_a \approx 0.7$) is much stronger than $\ce{CH3COOH}$ ($pK_a = 4.76$) due to the –I effect of three chlorine atoms stabilising the anion.

6. End-of-Lesson Practice

Question 1 (Paper 1 style): Which reagent can be used to distinguish butanal from butanone?
A. $\ce{NaHCO3(aq)}$   B. $\ce{Br2(aq)}$   C. Tollens' reagent   D. $\ce{PCl5}$

Question 2: Draw the full mechanism (curly arrows) for the nucleophilic addition of $\ce{HCN}$ to propanone. Name the product.

Question 3: Arrange in order of increasing acidity: ethanol, ethanoic acid, 2-chloroethanoic acid, trichloroethanoic acid. Explain the trend.

Question 4: Ethyl ethanoate is heated with excess $\ce{NaOH(aq)}$. Write the equation and name both products. Explain why this reaction is irreversible.

Question 5: Propose a two-step synthesis of propyl propanoate from propan-1-ol, showing all reagents and conditions.

Question 6 (Paper 2 style): Describe how you would experimentally distinguish between four colourless liquids: ethanoic acid, ethanol, ethanal, and ethyl ethanoate. Include observations and equations where relevant.

Answer 1: C. Tollens' reagent (ammoniacal silver nitrate) oxidises aldehydes to carboxylic acids, depositing a silver mirror. Butanal (aldehyde) gives a positive test; butanone (ketone) does not react. $\ce{Br2(aq)}$ (B) reacts with both if enolisation occurs under acidic conditions. $\ce{PCl5}$ (D) reacts with –OH groups only. $\ce{NaHCO3}$ (A) tests for acids only.

Answer 2: $\ce{CN-}$ attacks the carbonyl carbon of propanone $\ce{CH3COCH3}$. Curly arrow from $\ce{CN-}$ lone pair → C; curly arrow from $\ce{C=O}$ $\pi$ → O. Tetrahedral intermediate: $\ce{(CH3)2C(CN)O-}$. Protonation by $\ce{H+}$ (from HCN or added acid) → $\ce{(CH3)2C(OH)CN}$. Product: 2-hydroxy-2-methylpropanenitrile (a cyanohydrin).

Answer 3: Ethanol ($pK_a \approx 16$) < ethanoic acid ($pK_a \approx 4.76$) < 2-chloroethanoic acid ($pK_a \approx 2.86$) < trichloroethanoic acid ($pK_a \approx 0.7$). The –I effect of electronegative Cl atoms withdraws electron density, stabilising the carboxylate anion → stronger acid (lower $pK_a$). More Cl atoms = stronger –I effect = more acidic.

Answer 4: $\ce{CH3COOC2H5 + NaOH -> CH3COO- Na+ + C2H5OH}$. Products: sodium ethanoate and ethanol. Irreversible because the carboxylate ion $\ce{CH3COO-}$ is resonance-stabilised and does not undergo nucleophilic attack by ethanol (poor electrophile). The equilibrium lies completely to the right.

Answer 5: Step 1: Oxidise propan-1-ol to propanoic acid using $\ce{K2Cr2O7/H+}$, heat under reflux (excess oxidant to go all the way to acid). Step 2: Esterify propanoic acid with propan-1-ol using concentrated $\ce{H2SO4}$ catalyst, heat under reflux → propyl propanoate + water.

Answer 6: Test 1: Add $\ce{NaHCO3(aq)}$ → ethanoic acid effervesces ($\ce{CO2}$). Test 2: Add Tollens' reagent to remaining three and warm → ethanal gives silver mirror. Test 3: Add $\ce{K2Cr2O7/H+}$ to the last two → ethanol gives orange → green; ethyl ethanoate gives no colour change. Alternatively, use $\ce{PCl5}$ — ethanol gives steamy fumes of HCl; ethyl ethanoate does not react.

Carboxyl group$\ce{–COOH}$: a carbonyl + hydroxyl on the same carbon; the –OH proton is acidic ($pK_a$ ~ 5)
Resonance stabilisationDelocalisation of electrons over multiple atoms; carboxylate ion $\ce{RCOO-}$ has two equivalent resonance structures → extra stability
EsterificationCondensation reaction: carboxylic acid + alcohol ⇌ ester + water, catalysed by $\ce{H+}$; also called Fischer esterification
SaponificationBase-catalysed ester hydrolysis: ester + $\ce{OH-}$ → carboxylate salt + alcohol; irreversible

8. Exam Tips

Paper 1 (Multiple Choice): Questions about distinguishing tests are high-frequency. Know: Tollens' → aldehyde only; Fehling's → aldehyde only; $\ce{NaHCO3}$ → carboxylic acid only ($\ce{CO2}$ gas); $\ce{PCl5}$ → alcohols and acids ($\ce{HCl}$ fumes); $\ce{Na}$ metal → alcohols (more vigorous) and acids (less controllable). Learn the specific observation for each.
Paper 2 — Nucleophilic Addition Mechanism (4–5 marks): Two curly arrows in the rate-determining step: Nu⁻ → C AND C=O → O⁻. The intermediate is tetrahedral ($sp^3$). The second step is protonation (fast). Show the product clearly with the Nu now bonded to carbon and O as –OH. If the carbonyl is prochiral (different groups on each side), mention that the product is a racemic mixture (Nu attacks from either face).
Ester hydrolysis conditions matter. "Heat with dilute $\ce{HCl}$" = acidic hydrolysis, reversible → carboxylic acid + alcohol. "Heat with $\ce{NaOH(aq)}$" = basic hydrolysis (saponification), irreversible → carboxylate salt + alcohol. If the question then says "acidify," the salt becomes the free acid. Read the full question — it is often a two-part sequence: basic hydrolysis then acidification.
