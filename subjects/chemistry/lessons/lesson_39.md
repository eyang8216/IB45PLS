# Lesson 39: Aldehydes, Ketones, Carboxylic Acids, and Esters

## What You'll Learn

In this lesson, you will study four families of organic compounds that all contain the carbonyl group (C=O). You will learn the difference between an aldehyde and a ketone. You will learn chemical tests that distinguish between them. You will learn how carboxylic acids behave as weak acids and form esters. You will learn about nucleophilic addition to the carbonyl group using the reducing agent sodium borohydride. This lesson connects oxidation reactions you have already studied with new reactions of the carbonyl group.

---

## 1. The Carbonyl Group — C=O

The **carbonyl group** is a carbon atom double-bonded to an oxygen atom: **C=O**. This group is present in several important families of organic compounds, including aldehydes, ketones, carboxylic acids, esters, and amides. The chemistry of all these families is dominated by the carbonyl group.

The C=O bond is **polar**. Oxygen is more electronegative than carbon (3.5 versus 2.5 on the Pauling scale), so the oxygen pulls electron density toward itself. The oxygen end of the bond is partially negative (δ−), and the carbon end is partially positive (δ+). This polarity has two important consequences:
1. The δ+ carbon is susceptible to attack by **nucleophiles** — species with lone pairs that are attracted to positive centres.
2. The C=O group can participate in hydrogen bonding (as the acceptor) and dipole-dipole interactions, affecting physical properties.

---

## 2. Aldehydes versus Ketones

Both aldehydes and ketones contain the carbonyl group. The difference is where the carbonyl group is located on the carbon chain.

An **aldehyde** has the carbonyl group at the **end** of the carbon chain. The carbonyl carbon is bonded to at least one hydrogen atom. The functional group of an aldehyde is **−CHO** (the carbonyl carbon, an H, and the rest of the chain). The suffix for aldehydes is **−al**. General formula (saturated, one aldehyde group, straight chain): CₙH₂ₙO.

A **ketone** has the carbonyl group **not at the end** — the carbonyl carbon is bonded to two other carbon atoms (one on each side). The functional group is written as **>C=O**. The suffix for ketones is **−one**. General formula (saturated, one ketone group, straight chain): also CₙH₂ₙO (same as aldehydes — they are functional group isomers).

**Naming note:** For aldehydes, the −CHO carbon is always carbon number 1, so you do not need to write "1" in the name. For ketones, you must include the number of the carbonyl carbon (unless there is only one possible position). For example: CH₃CH₂CHO is **propanal** (not propan-1-al — the −CHO is always at the end). CH₃COCH₂CH₃ is **butan-2-one** (the number is needed because the ketone could be at C-2 or C-3 in a 4-carbon chain, though both give the same molecule in this case).

---

## 3. Preparation of Aldehydes and Ketones from Alcohols

The controlled oxidation of alcohols (which you learned about in Lesson 38) is the main way to prepare aldehydes and ketones.

- **Primary alcohol → Aldehyde:** Use acidified potassium dichromate(VI) with **distillation**. The aldehyde, once formed, has a lower boiling point than the alcohol (no hydrogen bonding between aldehyde molecules) and distils off, preventing further oxidation to the carboxylic acid.
- **Primary alcohol → Carboxylic acid:** Use the same oxidising agent but with **reflux** (heating with a condenser so nothing escapes). The aldehyde stays in the mixture and is oxidised all the way to the acid.
- **Secondary alcohol → Ketone:** Use acidified dichromate, usually with reflux. The ketone cannot be oxidised further.

---

## 4. Distinguishing Aldehydes from Ketones — Chemical Tests

Aldehydes can be oxidised further (to carboxylic acids); ketones cannot. This chemical difference is the basis of two important tests:

### Tollens' Reagent (The Silver Mirror Test)

Tollens' reagent is made by adding sodium hydroxide to silver nitrate solution, then adding ammonia until the precipitate dissolves. The active species is the **diamminesilver(I) ion**, [Ag(NH₃)₂]⁺, which is a mild oxidising agent.

When heated with an **aldehyde**, the Ag⁺ in Tollens' reagent is reduced to metallic silver (Ag), which deposits as a shiny **silver mirror** on the inside of the test tube. The aldehyde is oxidised to a carboxylate ion (in the alkaline solution).

$$\ce{RCHO + 2[Ag(NH3)2]+ + 2OH- -> RCOO- + 2Ag(s) + 4NH3 + H2O}$$

When heated with a **ketone**, no reaction occurs — no silver mirror forms.

### Fehling's Solution or Benedict's Solution

Both contain Cu²⁺ ions complexed with ligands, giving a deep blue colour. When heated with an **aldehyde**, the Cu²⁺ is reduced to Cu⁺, which precipitates as copper(I) oxide, Cu₂O — a **brick-red solid**. Aldehydes give a positive test; ketones do not.

These tests work because aldehydes have a hydrogen atom attached to the carbonyl carbon that can be removed during oxidation. Ketones have no such hydrogen (the carbonyl carbon is bonded to two carbon chains), so they resist mild oxidation.

## 5. Nucleophilic Addition to the Carbonyl Group — Reduction with NaBH₄

The most important reaction of aldehydes and ketones is **nucleophilic addition**. A nucleophile attacks the δ+ carbonyl carbon. The π bond of the C=O breaks, and both electrons go to the oxygen, giving it a negative charge (forming an alkoxide intermediate). This intermediate is then protonated (gains an H⁺) to give the final product.

### Reduction with Sodium Borohydride (NaBH₄)

Sodium borohydride, NaBH₄, is a reducing agent. It provides **hydride ions (H⁻)**, which are powerful nucleophiles. A hydride ion is a hydrogen atom with two electrons and a negative charge. The hydride attacks the carbonyl carbon:

**Step 1 (nucleophilic attack):** The hydride ion (H⁻) from NaBH₄ attacks the δ+ carbonyl carbon. The C=O π bond breaks and both electrons move onto the oxygen, forming an alkoxide ion:
$$\ce{R2C=O + H- -> R2CH-O-}$$

**Step 2 (protonation):** The alkoxide ion is protonated by water or by added acid, forming the alcohol:
$$\ce{R2CH-O- + H2O -> R2CH-OH + OH-}$$

**Products:**
- An **aldehyde** reduced by NaBH₄ gives a **primary (1°) alcohol**. The aldehyde carbon (R−CHO) becomes R−CH₂OH.
- A **ketone** reduced by NaBH₄ gives a **secondary (2°) alcohol**. The ketone carbon (R−CO−R') becomes R−CH(OH)−R'.

A stronger reducing agent, **lithium aluminium hydride (LiAlH₄)**, can also reduce carboxylic acids and esters, but NaBH₄ is more selective: it reduces only aldehydes and ketones under normal conditions. NaBH₄ is also safer to use because it can be used in water or alcohol solvents, whereas LiAlH₄ reacts violently with water.

<div class="worked">
<strong>Worked Example 1: Predict the product when propanal is reduced with sodium borohydride, NaBH₄, followed by the addition of water.</strong>

Propanal is CH₃CH₂CHO — an aldehyde.

**To solve this, we need to:** identify that the aldehyde is reduced to a primary alcohol. The hydride (H⁻) adds to the carbonyl carbon, and the oxygen is protonated.

The carbonyl carbon (the −CHO carbon) gains an H from the hydride, converting the C=O to CH−OH. The carbon that was the carbonyl carbon becomes a −CH₂OH group.

Product: CH₃CH₂CH₂OH, which is **propan-1-ol** (a primary alcohol).

**Why this makes sense:** Reduction adds two hydrogen atoms across the C=O bond — one from NaBH₄ (as H⁻) and one from water (as H⁺). This is the reverse of the oxidation of a primary alcohol to an aldehyde.
</div>

## 6. Carboxylic Acids

A **carboxylic acid** has the functional group **−COOH**, which is called the **carboxyl group**. The carboxyl group consists of a carbonyl group (C=O) with a hydroxyl group (−OH) attached to the same carbon. The general formula for a carboxylic acid is **RCOOH**. The suffix is **−oic acid**.

### Properties of Carboxylic Acids

**Acidity:** Carboxylic acids are **weak acids**. In water, they partially dissociate — only some of the molecules lose a proton:
$$\ce{RCOOH(aq) <=> RCOO-(aq) + H+(aq)}$$
The equilibrium lies mostly on the left. The carboxylate ion (RCOO⁻) is stabilised by resonance — the negative charge is shared equally between the two oxygen atoms, making the ion more stable and the acid more willing to lose the proton.

**Reactions as acids:**
- With reactive metals: $$\ce{2CH3COOH(aq) + Mg(s) -> (CH3COO)2Mg(aq) + H2(g)}$$
- With bases: $$\ce{CH3COOH(aq) + NaOH(aq) -> CH3COONa(aq) + H2O(l)}$$
- With carbonates: $$\ce{2CH3COOH(aq) + Na2CO3(aq) -> 2CH3COONa(aq) + H2O(l) + CO2(g)}$$
The reaction with carbonates produces carbon dioxide gas (effervescence), which is a test for carboxylic acids.

**Hydrogen bonding:** Carboxylic acids form very strong hydrogen bonds. In the pure liquid and even in the gas phase, two carboxylic acid molecules can pair up as a **dimer** — two molecules held together by two hydrogen bonds between their −COOH groups. This strong intermolecular force gives carboxylic acids unusually high boiling points compared to alcohols of similar molecular mass.

**Solubility:** Short-chain carboxylic acids (up to about 4 carbons) are fully soluble in water because they can form hydrogen bonds with water molecules. As the hydrocarbon chain gets longer, solubility decreases because the non-polar chain dominates.

## 7. Esters — Formation and Hydrolysis

An **ester** has the functional group **−COO−**. It can be thought of as a carboxylic acid where the −OH has been replaced by an −OR' group (R' is another alkyl chain). The general formula for an ester is **RCOOR'**.

### Esterification

Esters are formed by the reaction of a carboxylic acid with an alcohol, catalysed by a strong acid (usually concentrated sulfuric acid, H₂SO₄) and heated under reflux:

$$\ce{RCOOH + R'OH <=>[H2SO4] RCOOR' + H2O}$$

This is a **condensation reaction** — two molecules join together with the elimination of a small molecule (water). It is also a reversible reaction (the double arrow ⇌ indicates an equilibrium).

For example, ethanoic acid and ethanol:
$$\ce{CH3COOH + CH3CH2OH <=>[H2SO4] CH3COOCH2CH3 + H2O}$$
The ester is **ethyl ethanoate**, which has a characteristic fruity smell (like pear drops).

### Naming Esters

An ester's name has two parts: the alkyl group from the alcohol (first) and the acid-derived part with the suffix **−oate** (second). For example:
- Methyl ethanoate: from methanol + ethanoic acid, CH₃COOCH₃.
- Ethyl propanoate: from ethanol + propanoic acid, CH₃CH₂COOCH₂CH₃.

### Properties of Esters

Esters have pleasant, **fruity smells** and are used as flavourings and in perfumes. They have **lower boiling points** than the corresponding carboxylic acids because ester molecules cannot form hydrogen bonds with each other — they have no −OH group. However, esters can accept hydrogen bonds from water at their C=O oxygen, so small esters are slightly soluble in water.

### Hydrolysis of Esters

**Hydrolysis** means "breaking with water." Esters can be broken back into the parent carboxylic acid and alcohol by hydrolysis.

**Acid hydrolysis** is the reverse of esterification. Water is added, and acid catalyses the reaction:
$$\ce{RCOOR' + H2O <=>[H+] RCOOH + R'OH}$$

**Base hydrolysis (saponification)** uses a strong base such as NaOH. Unlike acid hydrolysis, this goes to completion (it is not reversible):
$$\ce{RCOOR' + NaOH -> RCOO- Na+ + R'OH}$$
The products are the **carboxylate salt** (not the free acid) and the alcohol. The carboxylate ion is resonance-stabilised and does not react further, which drives the reaction to completion. If you want the free carboxylic acid, you add a stronger acid afterwards:
$$\ce{RCOO- Na+ + HCl -> RCOOH + NaCl}$$

"Saponification" is the word for making soap — natural fats and oils are esters of long-chain carboxylic acids, and boiling them with NaOH produces the sodium salts of these acids, which are soaps.

<div class="worked">
<strong>Worked Example 2: Name the ester formed when propanoic acid reacts with methanol, and write the equation for its base-catalysed hydrolysis.</strong>

The acid is propanoic acid (CH₃CH₂COOH). The alcohol is methanol (CH₃OH). The ester is **methyl propanoate**, CH₃CH₂COOCH₃.

Base hydrolysis with NaOH:
$$\ce{CH3CH2COOCH3 + NaOH -> CH3CH2COO- Na+ + CH3OH}$$
The products are **sodium propanoate** and **methanol**. Acidification of the product mixture would liberate propanoic acid.

## Answers

**Answer 1.**

(a) CH₃CH₂CHO: 3 carbon chain, aldehyde (−CHO at the end). Name: **propanal**. (The −CHO carbon is always C-1; no number needed.)

(b) CH₃COCH₂CH₃: 4 carbon chain, ketone (C=O in the middle). Number from the end closer to the C=O: C=O is at C-2. Name: **butan-2-one** (commonly called butanone).

(c) CH₃CH₂CH₂COOH: 4 carbon chain including the −COOH carbon. Carboxylic acid. Name: **butanoic acid**. (The −COOH carbon is always C-1.)

**Answer 2.**

Two tests can be used: Tollens' reagent and Benedict's/Fehling's solution.

With **Tollens' reagent**: Propanal (an aldehyde) will produce a silver mirror on the inside of the test tube when warmed. Propanone (a ketone) will show no visible change. The aldehyde reduces Ag⁺ to Ag(s) because it can be oxidised further; the ketone cannot be oxidised.

With **Benedict's solution**: Propanal will produce a brick-red precipitate of Cu₂O when warmed. Propanone will show no change (the solution stays blue). Again, the aldehyde reduces Cu²⁺ to Cu⁺; the ketone cannot.

The chemical reason: Aldehydes have a hydrogen atom attached to the carbonyl carbon. This C−H bond can be broken during oxidation. Ketones have no such hydrogen (the carbonyl carbon is bonded to two alkyl groups), so they resist oxidation under these mild conditions.

**Answer 3.**

(a) Oxidation of ethanol to ethanoic acid (reflux conditions needed for full oxidation):
$$\ce{CH3CH2OH + 2[O] -> CH3COOH + H2O}$$
The [O] represents the oxygen provided by the dichromate oxidising agent.

(b) Reduction of butanone: CH₃CH₂COCH₃ + 2[H] → CH₃CH₂CH(OH)CH₃.
The product is **butan-2-ol**, a secondary alcohol.

(c) Esterification: HCOOH + CH₃CH₂CH₂OH ⇌ HCOOCH₂CH₂CH₃ + H₂O.
The ester is **propyl methanoate**. (The alkyl group "propyl" from propan-1-ol; "methanoate" from methanoic acid.)

**Answer 4.**

(a) The product that reacts with Na₂CO₃ releasing CO₂ (limewater turns milky) must be a **carboxylic acid**. Carboxylic acids are acidic enough to react with carbonates. The product that gives a positive iodoform test (yellow precipitate of CHI₃) must contain a CH₃CH(OH)− group or a CH₃CO− group. Since it is a product of ester hydrolysis, it is an alcohol with a methyl group next to the −OH carbon. The only alcohol from a C₄ ester that can give a positive iodoform test is **ethanol**, CH₃CH₂OH.

If the alcohol is ethanol, the acid part must have 2 carbons (because the ester has 4 carbons total, and ethanol has 2). The acid is **ethanoic acid**, CH₃COOH.

(b) The ester is **ethyl ethanoate**: CH₃COOCH₂CH₃.

(c) Base hydrolysis:
$$\ce{CH3COOCH2CH3 + NaOH -> CH3COO- Na+ + CH3CH2OH}$$
Acidification:
$$\ce{CH3COO- Na+ + HCl -> CH3COOH + NaCl}$$
(Or with any strong acid; the key is that the carboxylate salt is converted to the free carboxylic acid.)

**Answer 5.**

(a) A gives a positive Benedict's test (brick-red precipitate), which indicates an **aldehyde**. C₃H₆O that is an aldehyde must be **propanal**: CH₃CH₂CHO.

(b) Reduction of propanal with NaBH₄ adds two H atoms across the C=O bond, converting the aldehyde to a primary alcohol. B is **propan-1-ol**: CH₃CH₂CH₂OH. It is a **primary (1°) alcohol** because the −OH carbon is bonded to only one other carbon.

(c) Mechanism for NaBH₄ reduction:

The hydride ion H⁻ (from NaBH₄) acts as a nucleophile. A curly arrow is drawn from the lone pair of H⁻ to the δ+ carbonyl carbon. Simultaneously, a curly arrow is drawn from the C=O π bond to the oxygen atom, showing the π electrons moving onto oxygen. This forms an alkoxide intermediate: CH₃CH₂CH₂O⁻.

In the second step, the alkoxide oxygen uses one of its lone pairs to attack a hydrogen from a water molecule (H₂O). A curly arrow goes from the O⁻ lone pair to the H of water, and the O−H bond of water breaks, with the electrons going to the oxygen of the hydroxide ion. The product is propan-1-ol (CH₃CH₂CH₂OH) and OH⁻.

(d) B (propan-1-ol) is a **primary alcohol**. With acidified dichromate under distillation conditions, primary alcohols are oxidised to aldehydes — the oxidation removes two H atoms, one from the −OH and one from the C−H bond of the same carbon, producing the C=O at the end of the chain. This gives propanal (compound A).

To oxidise further to the carboxylic acid (propanoic acid), the aldehyde must remain in contact with the oxidising agent — this requires **reflux** conditions. Under distillation, the aldehyde (which has a lower boiling point than the alcohol or acid) evaporates and is collected before it can be oxidised further. This is why distillation gives the aldehyde while reflux gives the carboxylic acid.

The fact that B is primary means it has the C−H bond needed for oxidation. If B were secondary, it would give a ketone that could not be further oxidised. If B were tertiary, no oxidation would occur at all.
