Lesson 14: Enthalpy Changes — Definitions, Calorimetry, and q = mcΔT

Syllabus reference: 5.1 — Measuring Energy Changes

Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to:

  • Define enthalpy change $\Delta H$, and distinguish between exothermic ($\Delta H < 0$) and endothermic ($\Delta H > 0$) reactions.
  • Define standard enthalpy changes: $\Delta H^\ominus$ (standard conditions), $\Delta H_c^\ominus$ (combustion), $\Delta H_f^\ominus$ (formation), $\Delta H_{\text{neut}}^\ominus$ (neutralisation).
  • Apply the calorimetry equation $q = mc\Delta T$ to calculate heat transferred in a reaction.
  • Calculate $\Delta H$ from experimental data, including the effect of heat capacity of the calorimeter.

1. Prior Knowledge Check

Question 1: What is the difference between temperature and heat?

▸ Show
Temperature is a measure of the average kinetic energy of particles (units: K or °C). Heat is the total thermal energy transferred between a system and its surroundings (units: J). A cup of water at 80°C and a swimming pool at 80°C have the same temperature but very different heat contents.

Question 2: What does the word "system" mean in thermodynamics, and what are the "surroundings"?

▸ Show
The system is the chemical reaction or physical process you are studying — the reactants and products. The surroundings are everything else (the container, the solvent, the air, the thermometer). In calorimetry, the surroundings usually include the water that absorbs the heat released by the reaction.

Question 3: What does "standard conditions" mean in chemistry?

▸ Show
Standard conditions: pressure = 100 kPa (1 bar), temperature = 298 K (25°C), and all solutions at 1 mol dm⁻³. Any substance must be in its standard state (most stable form) at that temperature and pressure. The standard enthalpy change is denoted by the plimsoll symbol: $\Delta H^\ominus$.

2. What Is Enthalpy?

Enthalpy ($H$) is a measure of the total heat content of a system. You cannot measure absolute enthalpy — only changes in enthalpy ($\Delta H$) when a reaction occurs at constant pressure. This is what calorimetry measures.

Enthalpy change: $\Delta H = H_{\text{products}} - H_{\text{reactants}}$. If $\Delta H < 0$, the reaction is exothermic (heat released to surroundings). If $\Delta H > 0$, the reaction is endothermic (heat absorbed from surroundings).

2.1 Exothermic vs. Endothermic — Energy Flow

In an exothermic reaction, the products are lower in enthalpy than the reactants. The "lost" enthalpy is transferred to the surroundings as heat — the surroundings get warmer. Combustion of methane: $\ce{CH4 + 2O2 -> CO2 + 2H2O}$ has $\Delta H = -890\text{ kJ mol}^{-1}$.

In an endothermic reaction, the products are higher in enthalpy. The system absorbs heat from the surroundings — the surroundings get cooler. Photosynthesis: $\ce{6CO2 + 6H2O -> C6H12O6 + 6O2}$ has $\Delta H = +2800\text{ kJ mol}^{-1}$.

Figure 1: Exothermic reactions release energy — products sit lower than reactants on the enthalpy ladder. Endothermic reactions absorb energy — products sit higher.

Check-in: When you dissolve $\ce{NH4NO3}$ in water, the beaker feels cold. Is this exothermic or endothermic?

▸ Show
Endothermic. The beaker feels cold because the dissolving process absorbs heat from the surroundings (the water, the beaker, your hand). The system ($\ce{NH4NO3}$ dissolving) has taken energy in, so $\Delta H > 0$.

2.2 Types of Standard Enthalpy Changes

TypeSymbolDefinition
Standard enthalpy of combustion$\Delta H_c^\ominus$Enthalpy change when one mole of a substance is completely burned in excess oxygen under standard conditions. Always exothermic.
Standard enthalpy of formation$\Delta H_f^\ominus$Enthalpy change when one mole of a compound is formed from its elements in their standard states. Can be exothermic or endothermic. $\Delta H_f^\ominus$ for any element in its standard state = 0 by definition.
Standard enthalpy of neutralisation$\Delta H_{\text{neut}}^\ominus$Enthalpy change when one mole of water is formed from the reaction of an acid and a base under standard conditions. For strong acid + strong base: $\approx -57\text{ kJ mol}^{-1}$.

Check-in: Why is $\Delta H_f^\ominus$ of $\ce{O2(g)}$ equal to zero, but $\Delta H_f^\ominus$ of $\ce{O3(g)}$ is not?

▸ Show
By definition, the standard enthalpy of formation of any element in its standard state is zero. $\ce{O2(g)}$ is the most stable form of oxygen at 298 K and 100 kPa — it's the standard state. $\ce{O3(g)}$ (ozone) is less stable, so forming it from $\ce{O2}$ requires energy: $\Delta H_f^\ominus(\ce{O3}) = +142.7\text{ kJ mol}^{-1}$.

3. Calorimetry — Measuring Enthalpy Changes

3.1 The Basic Equation: q = mcΔT

When a reaction transfers heat to (or from) a known mass of water, the water's temperature changes. The heat transferred is:

Calorimetry equation: $$q = mc\Delta T$$ $q$ = heat transferred (J), $m$ = mass of water/solution (g), $c$ = specific heat capacity (J g⁻¹ K⁻¹), $\Delta T$ = temperature change (K or °C). For water: $c = 4.18\text{ J g}^{-1}\text{ K}^{-1}$.

To find $\Delta H$ (in kJ mol⁻¹), you also need the number of moles that reacted:

$$\Delta H = -\frac{q}{n} \quad \text{(convert J to kJ: divide by 1000)}$$

The negative sign is crucial: if the water gains heat ($\Delta T > 0$), the reaction is giving energy away — it is exothermic, so $\Delta H$ must be negative. If the water loses heat, the reaction is endothermic.

Figure 2: The three-step calorimetry calculation. Measure → calculate heat transferred (q) → convert to molar enthalpy change (ΔH). Don't forget the negative sign!

Check-in: 0.500 g of $\ce{Mg}$ is added to 100 cm³ of excess $\ce{HCl}$ in a polystyrene cup. The temperature rises from 21.0°C to 42.0°C. Calculate $\Delta H$ for $\ce{Mg + 2HCl -> MgCl2 + H2}$. ($\ce{Mg}$ = 24.3, assume density of solution = 1.00 g cm⁻³, $c = 4.18$ J g⁻¹ K⁻¹)

▸ Show
$m = 100$ g (100 cm³ × 1.00 g cm⁻³), $\Delta T = 42.0 - 21.0 = 21.0$ K. $q = 100 \times 4.18 \times 21.0 = 8778$ J = 8.778 kJ. $n(\ce{Mg}) = 0.500 / 24.3 = 0.02058$ mol. $\Delta H = -8.778 / 0.02058 = \mathbf{-427\text{ kJ mol}^{-1}}$. Note: the literature value is about $-467$ kJ mol⁻¹; the difference is due to heat loss in the simple calorimeter.

3.2 Extrapolation — Correcting for Heat Loss

In a real experiment, temperature does not rise (or fall) instantly. You must plot temperature vs. time, draw a cooling curve after the reaction, and extrapolate back to the mixing time ($t = 0$ for the reaction start). The extrapolated $\Delta T$ is used, not the raw maximum.

Figure 3: Extrapolation in calorimetry. The dashed red line extends the cooling portion back to the mixing time to estimate the temperature change that would have occurred with zero heat loss.

4. Worked Examples

Example 1 — Combustion Calorimetry:

0.320 g of ethanol ($\ce{C2H5OH}$) is burned in a spirit burner to heat 200 g of water from 22.0°C to 45.5°C. Calculate $\Delta H_c$ of ethanol in kJ mol⁻¹.

Step 1: Calculate heat absorbed by water. $m = 200$ g, $\Delta T = 45.5 - 22.0 = 23.5$ K. $q = 200 \times 4.18 \times 23.5 = 19646$ J.
Step 2: Convert to kJ. $q = 19.646$ kJ.
Step 3: Moles of ethanol. $M(\ce{C2H5OH}) = 46.0$ g mol⁻¹. $n = 0.320 / 46.0 = 0.006957$ mol.
Step 4: Calculate $\Delta H_c$. $\Delta H_c = -19.646 / 0.006957 = \mathbf{-2820\text{ kJ mol}^{-1}}$ (approx; literature is $-1367$ due to incomplete combustion and heat loss).

⚠️ Common wrong approach: Forgetting that combustion is always exothermic — writing $\Delta H_c$ as positive. Also, students often forget to convert J to kJ before dividing by moles, giving an answer 1000× too large.
Example 2 — Neutralisation:

50.0 cm³ of 2.00 mol dm⁻³ $\ce{HCl}$ is added to 50.0 cm³ of 2.00 mol dm⁻³ $\ce{NaOH}$ in a polystyrene cup. Temperature rises from 20.0°C to 33.4°C. Calculate $\Delta H_{\text{neut}}$. (Density = 1.00 g cm⁻³, $c = 4.18$ J g⁻¹ K⁻¹)

Step 1: Total volume = 100 cm³, so $m = 100$ g. $\Delta T = 33.4 - 20.0 = 13.4$ K.
Step 2: $q = 100 \times 4.18 \times 13.4 = 5601$ J = 5.601 kJ.
Step 3: Moles of water formed. $n(\ce{HCl}) = 2.00 \times 0.0500 = 0.100$ mol, $n(\ce{NaOH}) = 0.100$ mol. They react 1:1, so $n(\ce{H2O}) = 0.100$ mol.
Step 4: $\Delta H_{\text{neut}} = -5.601 / 0.100 = \mathbf{-56.0\text{ kJ mol}^{-1}}$.

⚠️ Common wrong approach: Using only the mass of HCl solution (50 g) instead of the total solution mass (100 g). The heat warms ALL of the solution, not just one reactant.
Example 3 — Displacement with Heat Loss Correction:

0.650 g of Zn powder is added to 50.0 cm³ of 1.00 mol dm⁻³ $\ce{CuSO4}$ in a calorimeter of heat capacity 45.0 J K⁻¹. Temperature rises by 12.5 K. Calculate $\Delta H$ for $\ce{Zn + CuSO4 -> ZnSO4 + Cu}$. ($\ce{Zn} = 65.4$, $c$ of solution = 4.18 J g⁻¹ K⁻¹, density = 1.00 g cm⁻³)

Step 1: Heat absorbed by solution: $q_{\text{soln}} = 50.0 \times 4.18 \times 12.5 = 2613$ J.
Step 2: Heat absorbed by calorimeter: $q_{\text{cal}} = 45.0 \times 12.5 = 562.5$ J.
Step 3: Total heat released by reaction: $q_{\text{total}} = 2613 + 562.5 = 3175.5$ J = 3.176 kJ.
Step 4: Moles of Zn: $n = 0.650 / 65.4 = 0.009939$ mol. CuSO₄ moles: $1.00 \times 0.0500 = 0.0500$ mol, so Zn is limiting.
Step 5: $\Delta H = -3.176 / 0.009939 = \mathbf{-320\text{ kJ mol}^{-1}}$.

⚠️ Common wrong approach: Ignoring the heat capacity of the calorimeter altogether. The calorimeter itself absorbs some heat — if you only use $q_{\text{soln}}$, you underestimate $\Delta H$.

⚠️ Common Mistakes to Avoid

  1. "$\Delta H = q$ without the minus sign." If the water warms up, the reaction is exothermic. $\Delta H$ must be negative. The sign is physical, not optional — losing it costs marks and shows you do not understand the direction of energy flow.
  2. "Using the mass of the reactant instead of the mass of the solution." In $q = mc\Delta T$, $m$ is the mass of what is being heated — almost always the water or solution, not the solid reactant. A 0.5 g piece of Mg is not what absorbs 8 kJ of heat.
  3. "Forgetting to convert J to kJ." $q = mc\Delta T$ gives joules. $\Delta H$ is reported in kJ mol⁻¹. Divide by 1000 before dividing by moles, or your answer will be 1000× too large.
  4. "Confusing $\Delta H$ with the heat capacity of the calorimeter." Some IB questions give the calorimeter's heat capacity in J K⁻¹ (no mass). The heat absorbed by the calorimeter is $q = C_{\text{cal}} \times \Delta T$, not $mc\Delta T$. Add this to $q_{\text{soln}}$.
  5. "Using °C instead of K in $q = mc\Delta T$." Since $\Delta T$ is a difference, a change of 10°C equals a change of 10 K — so this is actually fine numerically. But some students try to add 273 to each temperature before subtracting, which gives the same result but wastes time.

5. End-of-Lesson Practice

Question 1 (Paper 1 style): Which statement about an exothermic reaction is correct?
A. The products have more enthalpy than the reactants.
B. Heat is absorbed from the surroundings.
C. $\Delta H$ has a negative value.
D. The temperature of the surroundings decreases.

Question 2: 100 cm³ of 1.00 mol dm⁻³ $\ce{HCl}$ is added to 100 cm³ of 1.00 mol dm⁻³ $\ce{KOH}$. Temperature rises from 20.0°C to 26.8°C. Calculate $\Delta H_{\text{neut}}$. (Density = 1.00 g cm⁻³, $c = 4.18$ J g⁻¹ K⁻¹)

Question 3: 1.20 g of $\ce{CaO}$ is added to 200 cm³ of water in a calorimeter. The temperature rises from 18.5°C to 31.0°C. Calculate $\Delta H$ for $\ce{CaO(s) + H2O(l) -> Ca(OH)2(aq)}$ per mole of $\ce{CaO}$. ($\ce{Ca}$ = 40.1, $\ce{O}$ = 16.0, $c = 4.18$ J g⁻¹ K⁻¹, density = 1.00 g cm⁻³)

Question 4: 0.460 g of ethanol ($\ce{C2H5OH}$, $M = 46.0$) is burned to heat 150 g of water. The water temperature rises by 27.5°C. Calculate the experimental $\Delta H_c$ and suggest two reasons why it differs from the literature value of $-1367$ kJ mol⁻¹.

Question 5: A calorimeter has a heat capacity of 80.0 J K⁻¹. When 0.540 g of Mg ($A_r = 24.3$) reacts with excess HCl in 80.0 cm³ of solution (density 1.00 g cm⁻³, $c = 4.18$ J g⁻¹ K⁻¹), the temperature rises by 24.0 K. Calculate $\Delta H$ for the reaction.

Question 6 (IB-style): A student measures $\Delta H_{\text{neut}}$ for $\ce{HCl + NaOH}$. She uses a polystyrene cup, a thermometer reading to ±0.5°C, and a measuring cylinder. Her result is −52 kJ mol⁻¹, but the accepted value is −57 kJ mol⁻¹.
(a) Calculate the percentage error.
(b) Suggest three improvements to her experimental procedure to obtain a more accurate result.
(c) Explain why a polystyrene cup is used rather than a glass beaker.

Answer 1: C. In an exothermic reaction, $\Delta H < 0$. Products are lower in enthalpy than reactants (A is wrong), heat is released to surroundings (B is wrong), and the surroundings get warmer (D is wrong).

Answer 2: Total $V = 200$ cm³, $m = 200$ g. $\Delta T = 6.8$ K. $q = 200 \times 4.18 \times 6.8 = 5685$ J = 5.685 kJ. $n(\ce{H2O}) = 0.100$ mol (both acid and base are 0.100 mol). $\Delta H_{\text{neut}} = -5.685 / 0.100 = \mathbf{-56.9\text{ kJ mol}^{-1}}$. Close to the accepted −57 kJ mol⁻¹.

Answer 3: $m = 200$ g, $\Delta T = 31.0 - 18.5 = 12.5$ K. $q = 200 \times 4.18 \times 12.5 = 10450$ J = 10.45 kJ. $M(\ce{CaO}) = 56.1$ g mol⁻¹. $n(\ce{CaO}) = 1.20 / 56.1 = 0.02139$ mol. $\Delta H = -10.45 / 0.02139 = \mathbf{-489\text{ kJ mol}^{-1}}$.

Answer 4: $q = 150 \times 4.18 \times 27.5 = 17243$ J = 17.24 kJ. $n = 0.460/46.0 = 0.0100$ mol. $\Delta H_c = -17.24/0.0100 = \mathbf{-1724\text{ kJ mol}^{-1}}$. Reasons for difference: (1) Incomplete combustion — some ethanol may not burn fully to $\ce{CO2}$. (2) Heat loss to surroundings — not all heat is transferred to the water. (3) The spirit burner, the can, and the air all absorb some heat.

Answer 5: $q_{\text{soln}} = 80.0 \times 4.18 \times 24.0 = 8026$ J. $q_{\text{cal}} = 80.0 \times 24.0 = 1920$ J. $q_{\text{total}} = 9946$ J = 9.946 kJ. $n(\ce{Mg}) = 0.540/24.3 = 0.02222$ mol. $\Delta H = -9.946/0.02222 = \mathbf{-448\text{ kJ mol}^{-1}}$.

Answer 6: (a) % error = $|(-52) - (-57)| / 57 \times 100 = 8.8\%$. (b) Improvements: (i) Use a digital thermometer (±0.1°C) instead of one reading to ±0.5°C. (ii) Use a lid/insulation on the polystyrene cup to reduce heat loss. (iii) Stir the solution continuously for uniform temperature. (iv) Extrapolate the temperature-time graph to correct for cooling. (c) Polystyrene is a good thermal insulator — it minimises heat exchange with the surroundings. Glass conducts heat much more readily, leading to greater heat loss and a less accurate result.

6. Key Terms

TermDefinition
Enthalpy ($H$)The total heat content of a system at constant pressure; only changes ($\Delta H$) can be measured
ExothermicA reaction that releases heat to the surroundings; $\Delta H < 0$; products have lower enthalpy than reactants
EndothermicA reaction that absorbs heat from the surroundings; $\Delta H > 0$; products have higher enthalpy than reactants
Standard conditionsPressure = 100 kPa, temperature = 298 K, all solutions at 1 mol dm⁻³; denoted by $\ominus$
$\Delta H_c^\ominus$Standard enthalpy of combustion: enthalpy change when one mole of a substance is completely burned in excess oxygen
$\Delta H_f^\ominus$Standard enthalpy of formation: enthalpy change when one mole of a compound is formed from its elements in their standard states
$\Delta H_{\text{neut}}^\ominus$Standard enthalpy of neutralisation: enthalpy change when one mole of water is formed from an acid–base reaction
Specific heat capacity ($c$)The energy required to raise the temperature of 1 g of a substance by 1 K; for water = 4.18 J g⁻¹ K⁻¹
CalorimetryThe experimental measurement of heat changes in chemical reactions
Heat capacity of calorimeterThe energy required to raise the temperature of the calorimeter itself by 1 K (units: J K⁻¹)
ExtrapolationExtending a graph's line back to the mixing time to estimate the temperature change corrected for heat loss

7. Exam Tips

Paper 1 (Multiple Choice): Look for the "sign" trap. Questions often list four reactions and ask which one is endothermic. Exothermic = combustion, neutralisation, respiration. Endothermic = photosynthesis, thermal decomposition (e.g., $\ce{CaCO3 -> CaO + CO2}$), dissolving ammonium salts. Also, watch for questions that test whether $\Delta H_f^\ominus$ of elements is zero — only for the standard state form.
Paper 2 — Calorimetry Calculations: You must show: (1) $q = mc\Delta T$ with correct mass, (2) conversion of J to kJ, (3) calculation of moles of the limiting reagent, (4) $\Delta H = -q/n$. Each step earns a mark. If you combine them into one line and make a mistake, you lose all marks.
Paper 3 — Calorimetry Experiment: Be ready to describe the procedure for determining $\Delta H_c$ of a liquid fuel using a spirit burner and a metal calorimeter. Key marking points: stir the water, use a draught shield, measure the mass of the burner before and after, record the temperature at regular intervals, plot a graph and extrapolate. The draught shield and extrapolation are the two most commonly missed improvements.
