1. Prior Knowledge Check

Q1: A mass spectrum of $\ce{Br2}$ shows three peaks at m/z 158, 160, and 162. Why three peaks for a diatomic molecule with only two isotopes?

▸ Show
Each $\ce{Br2}$ molecule contains two Br atoms. With two isotopes (⁷⁹Br and ⁸¹Br), there are three possible combinations: ⁷⁹-⁷⁹ (158), ⁷⁹-⁸¹ (160), ⁸¹-⁸¹ (162). The ⁷⁹-⁸¹ combination can form in two ways, making the 160 peak twice as intense.

Q2: An element has the successive IEs: 738, 1451, 7733 kJ mol⁻¹. In which group is it?

▸ Show
Jump between IE₂ (1451) and IE₃ (7733) — 5.3×. 2 valence electrons → Group 2. Magnesium.

2. Integrated Practice — IB Exam-Style Problems

These 7 problems span mass spectrometry, electron configuration, emission spectra, and ionisation energy. Treat this as a full review test for Topics 2 and 12.

Q1 (Paper 1): An element X has the following mass spectrum: m/z 50 (4%), m/z 52 (84%), m/z 53 (10%), m/z 54 (2%). What is $A_r$?

Q2 (Paper 1): Which electron configuration is a transition metal in its ground state?
A. $\ce{[Ar] 4s^2 3d^{10}}$   B. $\ce{[Ar] 4s^2 3d^5}$   C. $\ce{[Ar] 3d^{10}}$   D. $\ce{[Ar] 4s^1 3d^{10}}$

Q3: The mass spectrum of $\ce{Cl2}$ shows peaks at m/z 70, 72, 74 with ratio ~9:6:1. (a) Identify the combination giving each peak. (b) Verify the ratio given that ³⁵Cl = 75% and ³⁷Cl = 25%.

Q4: The convergence limit of the Lyman series is at 91.2 nm. (a) Calculate the ionisation energy of hydrogen in kJ mol⁻¹. (b) Explain what the convergence limit represents. ($h=6.63\times10^{-34}$ J s, $c=3.00\times10^8$ m s⁻¹, $N_A=6.02\times10^{23}$)

Q5: Element Z has successive IEs (kJ mol⁻¹): 1012, 1907, 2914, 4964, 6274, 21267, 25431. (a) Identify the group. (b) Write its condensed electron configuration. (c) Predict the formula of its oxide.

Q6: The $\ce{Fe^{3+}}$ ion is more stable than $\ce{Fe^{2+}}$. Explain this observation using electron configurations.

Q7 (IB-style): The first seven IEs of element Q (kJ mol⁻¹): 578, 1817, 2745, 11578, 14831, 18378, 23296. (a) Determine the group of Q. (b) Q forms an ion $\ce{Q^{3+}}$. Write its electron configuration. (c) The third IE (2745) is less than twice the second IE (1817), but the fourth IE (11578) is over four times the third. Explain this difference in detail, referring to electron shells.

A1: $A_r = [(50\times4)+(52\times84)+(53\times10)+(54\times2)]/100 = (200+4368+530+108)/100 = 52.06$. This is chromium (Cr).

A2: B — $\ce{[Ar] 4s^2 3d^5}$ has a partially filled d-subshell. A and D have full d¹⁰; C has no 4s electrons.

A3: (a) m/z 70: ³⁵Cl–³⁵Cl; m/z 72: ³⁵Cl–³⁷Cl + ³⁷Cl–³⁵Cl; m/z 74: ³⁷Cl–³⁷Cl. (b) P(70)=0.75²=0.5625; P(72)=2×0.75×0.25=0.375; P(74)=0.25²=0.0625. Ratio = 0.5625:0.375:0.0625 = 9:6:1. ✓

A4: (a) $\lambda = 9.12\times10^{-8}$ m. $\Delta E = hc/\lambda = (6.63\times10^{-34}\times3.00\times10^8)/(9.12\times10^{-8}) = 2.18\times10^{-18}$ J. kJ mol⁻¹: $(2.18\times10^{-18}\times6.02\times10^{23})/1000 = 1312$ kJ mol⁻¹. (b) The convergence limit represents the ionisation energy — the energy needed to completely remove the electron from n=1 to n=∞. At the convergence limit, the electron is no longer bound to the nucleus.

A5: (a) Jump IE₅ (6274) → IE₆ (21267) = 3.4×. 5 valence → Group 15. Element is phosphorus (P). (b) $\ce{[Ne] 3s^2 3p^3}$. (c) 5 valence electrons → $\ce{P2O5}$ or $\ce{P4O10}$.

A6: Fe: $\ce{[Ar] 4s^2 3d^6}$. Fe²⁺: $\ce{[Ar] 3d^6}$. Fe³⁺: $\ce{[Ar] 3d^5}$. The 3d⁵ configuration in Fe³⁺ is half-filled, which has extra stability from exchange energy — the same reason Cr adopts $\ce{[Ar] 4s^1 3d^5}$. Half-filled and fully-filled d subshells are unusually stable.

A7: (a) Jump IE₃ (2745) → IE₄ (11578) = 4.2×. 3 valence → Group 13. Q is aluminium (Al). (b) $\ce{Al^{3+}}$: $\ce{1s^2 2s^2 2p^6}$ or $\ce{[Ne]}$. (c) IE₁, IE₂, and IE₃ all remove electrons from the n=3 shell (Al: 3s²3p¹). Within the same shell, each successive removal requires more energy because the ion becomes more positively charged (greater $Z_{\text{eff}}$), but the increase is moderate (1.5-2×). IE₄ must remove an electron from the n=2 shell (the [Ne] core). This electron is in a lower principal quantum number, much closer to the nucleus, with far less shielding from inner electrons. The dramatic jump — over 4× larger than IE₃ — signals the transition from the valence shell to the inner noble gas core.

3. Key Terms

TermDefinition
Convergence limitThe frequency/wavelength where spectral lines merge into a continuum; energy equals the ionisation energy from that level
Ionisation energyEnergy to remove 1 mol of electrons from 1 mol of gaseous atoms/ions
Exchange energyQuantum mechanical stabilisation from parallel-spin electrons in separate degenerate orbitals

4. Exam Tips

Paper 1: Mass spectrum questions with peaks at m/z values that don't match expected isotopes often test whether you recognize that a 2+ ion appears at half its mass. If you see a peak at an unexpected m/z, consider multiply charged ions.
Paper 2 (data-based): When given a table of successive IEs, the marking scheme expects: (1) identify the jump, (2) state number of valence electrons, (3) name the group, (4) use electron configuration to explain the jump in terms of shell change. All four points for full marks.
