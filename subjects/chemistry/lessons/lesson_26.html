Lesson 26: $K_p$ & Advanced Equilibrium (HL)

Syllabus reference: 17.1 — The Equilibrium Law (HL): $K_p$, mole fractions, partial pressures

Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to:

  • Define partial pressure and mole fraction; calculate partial pressure as $p_i = x_i \times P_{\text{total}}$.
  • Write the $K_p$ expression for any gaseous equilibrium and determine its units.
  • Calculate $K_p$ from equilibrium amounts and total pressure using ICE tables adapted for pressures.
  • Derive the relationship $K_p = K_c(RT)^{\Delta n}$ and use it to interconvert $K_p$ and $K_c$.
  • Explain how $K_p$ (like $K_c$) depends only on temperature and is independent of pressure or catalyst.

1. Prior Knowledge Check

Question 1: For a mixture of gases, what is Dalton's Law of partial pressures?

▸ Show
Dalton's Law: The total pressure of a gas mixture is the sum of the partial pressures of each component: $P_{\text{total}} = p_A + p_B + p_C + \dots$. Each gas behaves as if it alone occupied the entire volume. The partial pressure of component $i$ is $p_i = x_i \times P_{\text{total}}$, where $x_i$ is the mole fraction.

Question 2: How is the mole fraction of a gas in a mixture calculated?

▸ Show
Mole fraction $x_i = n_i / n_{\text{total}}$, where $n_i$ is the number of moles of component $i$ and $n_{\text{total}}$ is the total moles of all gases present. The sum of all mole fractions in a mixture equals 1.

2. Partial Pressure and Mole Fraction

When dealing with gaseous equilibria, it is often more convenient to use partial pressures instead of concentrations. The equilibrium constant expressed in terms of partial pressures is $K_p$.

Partial pressure: $p_i = x_i \times P_{\text{total}}$, where $x_i = n_i / n_{\text{total}}$ (mole fraction).
Figure 1: Calculating partial pressures from mole fractions. Each gas contributes to the total pressure in proportion to its mole fraction. $p_i = x_i \times P_{\text{total}}$.

3. The $K_p$ Expression

For a general gaseous equilibrium: $\ce{aA(g) + bB(g) <=> cC(g) + dD(g)}$ $$K_p = \frac{(p_{\ce{C}})^c (p_{\ce{D}})^d}{(p_{\ce{A}})^a (p_{\ce{B}})^b}$$ All partial pressures are equilibrium values. $K_p$ has units of (pressure)$^{\Delta n}$, where $\Delta n$ counts gaseous species only.

The rules for $K_p$ mirror those for $K_c$: solids and pure liquids are omitted, exponents are stoichiometric coefficients, and $K_p$ is constant at a given temperature. The standard pressure unit at IB is typically kPa or atm — be consistent.

Check-in: Write $K_p$ for $\ce{N2(g) + 3H2(g) <=> 2NH3(g)}$. What are its units if pressures are in kPa?

▸ Show
$K_p = (p_{\ce{NH3}})^2 / (p_{\ce{N2}} \times (p_{\ce{H2}})^3)$. Δn = 2 − (1+3) = −2. Units: kPa⁻². The negative exponent means $K_p$ has units of 1/(pressure)² — a larger $K_p$ numerical value at higher pressure units. Always state the units.

4. $K_p$ Calculations Using ICE Tables for Pressure

The ICE method adapts naturally to $K_p$. Instead of tracking concentrations, you track moles (or mole fractions) and then convert to partial pressures using the total pressure.

Figure 2: ICE table for $K_p$. Track moles first, then convert to mole fractions and partial pressures using $x_i = n_i/n_{\text{total}}$ and $p_i = x_i \times P_{\text{total}}$.

Check-in: For $\ce{2SO2(g) + O2(g) <=> 2SO3(g)}$, the total pressure at equilibrium is 500 kPa. The mole fractions are: $x_{\ce{SO2}} = 0.10$, $x_{\ce{O2}} = 0.20$, $x_{\ce{SO3}} = 0.70$. Calculate $K_p$.

▸ Show
$p_{\ce{SO2}} = 0.10 \times 500 = 50$ kPa, $p_{\ce{O2}} = 0.20 \times 500 = 100$ kPa, $p_{\ce{SO3}} = 0.70 \times 500 = 350$ kPa. $K_p = (350)^2 / ((50)^2 \times 100) = 122500 / (2500 \times 100) = 122500 / 250000 = 0.49$. Units: kPa⁻¹. (Δn = 2−3 = −1.)

5. The Relationship: $K_p = K_c(RT)^{\Delta n}$

For a gaseous equilibrium, $K_p$ and $K_c$ are related through the ideal gas law. Starting from $p_i = (n_i/V)RT = [i]RT$:

$$K_p = K_c(RT)^{\Delta n}$$ where $\Delta n$ = (moles of gaseous products) − (moles of gaseous reactants), $R = 8.31$ J K⁻¹ mol⁻¹ (if using Pa), and $T$ is in kelvin.

Important: If you use $R = 8.31$ (J K⁻¹ mol⁻¹ = Pa m³ K⁻¹ mol⁻¹), partial pressures must be in Pa and $K_c$ must use concentrations in mol m⁻³. In practice, IB often gives pressures in kPa — be careful with unit conversions, or use $R = 8.31 \times 10^{-3}$ kPa m³ K⁻¹ mol⁻¹ when $p$ is in kPa and concentration is in mol dm⁻³: the effective constant becomes $RT$ in appropriate units.

Check-in: For $\ce{N2O4(g) <=> 2NO2(g)}$ at 298 K, $K_c = 0.0058$ mol dm⁻³. Calculate $K_p$ in kPa. ($R = 8.31$ J K⁻¹ mol⁻¹)

▸ Show
Δn = 2−1 = 1. $RT = 8.31 \times 298 = 2476$ J mol⁻¹. But $K_c$ is in mol dm⁻³ and $K_p$ should be in kPa. We need: $K_p = K_c \times (RT)^{\Delta n}$, with care on units. Using $R = 0.008314$ kPa m³ K⁻¹ mol⁻¹ and noting 1 m³ = 1000 dm³, $R$ (adjusted) = 8.314 kPa dm³ K⁻¹ mol⁻¹. $RT = 8.314 \times 298 = 2478$ kPa dm³ mol⁻¹. $K_p = 0.0058 \times 2478 = 14.4$ kPa. Units: kPa¹.

6. Worked Examples

Example 1 — Calculating $K_p$ from Initial Amounts and Total Pressure:

0.200 mol of $\ce{PCl5}$ is heated to 500 K in a container. At equilibrium, the total pressure is 300 kPa and the mixture contains 0.120 mol of $\ce{PCl5}$: $\ce{PCl5(g) <=> PCl3(g) + Cl2(g)}$. Find $K_p$.

Solution: ICE in moles: I = [0.200, 0, 0]; C = [−x, +x, +x]; E = [0.200−x, x, x]. Given 0.200−x = 0.120 → $x = 0.080$. E: n(PCl₅) = 0.120, n(PCl₃) = 0.080, n(Cl₂) = 0.080. $n_{\text{total}} = 0.280$ mol.
Mole fractions: $x_{\ce{PCl5}} = 0.120/0.280 = 0.4286$, $x_{\ce{PCl3}} = x_{\ce{Cl2}} = 0.080/0.280 = 0.2857$.
Partial pressures: $p_{\ce{PCl5}} = 0.4286 \times 300 = 128.6$ kPa, $p_{\ce{PCl3}} = p_{\ce{Cl2}} = 85.7$ kPa.
$K_p = (85.7 \times 85.7) / 128.6 = 7344 / 128.6 = \mathbf{57.1\text{ kPa}}$ (units: kPa¹).

⚠️ Common wrong approach: Using initial moles instead of equilibrium moles to calculate mole fractions. The total moles change during the reaction because Δn ≠ 0 — $n_{\text{total}}$ at equilibrium must be calculated from the E row, not the I row.
Example 2 — $K_p$ to $K_c$ Conversion:

For $\ce{2HI(g) <=> H2(g) + I2(g)}$ at 700 K, $K_p = 0.020$. Calculate $K_c$.

Solution: Δn = (1+1) − 2 = 0. Since Δn = 0, $K_p = K_c(RT)^0 = K_c$. Therefore, $K_c = \mathbf{0.020}$ (dimensionless). When Δn = 0, $K_p$ and $K_c$ are numerically equal and both dimensionless. This is a time-saving insight — always check Δn first.

⚠️ Common wrong approach: Assuming $K_p$ and $K_c$ always differ. They are equal whenever the number of moles of gas is the same on both sides. For $\ce{H2 + I2 <=> 2HI}$, both sides have 2 moles → $K_p = K_c$ at all temperatures.
Example 3 — $K_p$ from $K_c$ with Unit Conversion:

For $\ce{N2(g) + 3H2(g) <=> 2NH3(g)}$ at 673 K, $K_c = 0.50$ dm⁶ mol⁻². Calculate $K_p$ in atm. ($R = 0.0821$ dm³ atm K⁻¹ mol⁻¹)

Solution: Δn = 2 − 4 = −2. $RT = 0.0821 \times 673 = 55.25$ dm³ atm mol⁻¹.
$K_p = K_c \times (RT)^{-2} = 0.50 / (55.25)^2 = 0.50 / 3052 = \mathbf{1.64 \times 10^{-4}\text{ atm}^{-2}}$.

Note how the large $RT$ value (55.25) squared makes $K_p$ much smaller than $K_c$ when Δn is negative. The conversion is highly sensitive to $T$ and Δn.

⚠️ Common Mistakes to Avoid

  1. "Mole fraction is moles of that gas divided by volume." No. Mole fraction is $n_i / n_{\text{total}}$ — it is dimensionless, based on moles, not concentration. It does not depend on volume.
  2. "$K_p$ has the same numerical value as $K_c$." Only when Δn = 0. When Δn ≠ 0, they are related by $(RT)^{\Delta n}$, which can differ by many orders of magnitude.
  3. "Adding an inert gas at constant volume changes partial pressures and shifts equilibrium." Adding an inert gas at constant volume increases total pressure but does not change the partial pressures of reactants or products (their mole fractions decrease proportionally, but their partial pressures remain unchanged because $p_i = (n_i/V)RT$ and $n_i/V$ is unchanged). Therefore, $Q_p$ is unchanged and equilibrium does not shift.
  4. "The total pressure at equilibrium equals the initial pressure." Only if Δn = 0. If the number of moles of gas changes during reaction, the total pressure changes even at constant volume and temperature, because $P \propto n_{\text{total}}$.
  5. "$K_p$ changes when the total pressure changes." No. Like $K_c$, $K_p$ depends only on temperature. Changing the total pressure (by changing volume) shifts the position of equilibrium (by Le Chatelier) but does not change $K_p$.

Question 2: At equilibrium for $\ce{2NO2(g) <=> N2O4(g)}$, $x_{\ce{NO2}} = 0.30$, $x_{\ce{N2O4}} = 0.70$, and $P_{\text{total}} = 150$ kPa. Calculate $K_p$ with units.

Question 3: 0.500 mol $\ce{SO2Cl2}$ is heated at 375 K: $\ce{SO2Cl2(g) <=> SO2(g) + Cl2(g)}$. At equilibrium, $n_{\text{total}} = 0.700$ mol and $P_{\text{total}} = 250$ kPa. Calculate $K_p$.

Question 4: For $\ce{2NH3(g) <=> N2(g) + 3H2(g)}$ at 298 K, $K_c = 2.5 \times 10^{-8}$ mol² dm⁻⁶. Calculate $K_p$ in kPa. ($R = 8.31$ J K⁻¹ mol⁻¹)

Question 5: $\ce{COCl2(g) <=> CO(g) + Cl2(g)}$. At 600 K, $K_p = 12.5$ kPa. Initially, $p_{\ce{COCl2}} = 80$ kPa with no CO or Cl₂. Calculate the equilibrium partial pressures of all species.

Question 6 (IB multi-step): 1.00 mol of $\ce{N2O4}$ is placed in a sealed container at 350 K. At equilibrium, 20% has dissociated: $\ce{N2O4(g) <=> 2NO2(g)}$. The total pressure is 180 kPa.
(a) Calculate the equilibrium moles of each species.
(b) Calculate mole fractions and partial pressures.
(c) Calculate $K_p$ with units.
(d) Using $R = 8.31$, calculate $K_c$.

Answer 1: C. $\ce{H2 + I2 <=> 2HI}$ has 2 moles on both sides → Δn = 0 → $K_p = K_c$. All others have Δn ≠ 0.

Answer 2: $p_{\ce{NO2}} = 0.30 \times 150 = 45$ kPa, $p_{\ce{N2O4}} = 0.70 \times 150 = 105$ kPa. $K_p = 105/(45)^2 = 105/2025 = \mathbf{0.0519\text{ kPa}^{-1}}$.

Answer 3: ICE: n₀(SO₂Cl₂) = 0.500. At equilibrium: 0.500−x + x + x = 0.700 → $x = 0.200$. n(SO₂Cl₂) = 0.300, n(SO₂) = n(Cl₂) = 0.200. $x_{\ce{SO2Cl2}} = 0.300/0.700 = 0.4286$, $x_{\ce{SO2}} = x_{\ce{Cl2}} = 0.200/0.700 = 0.2857$. $p_{\ce{SO2Cl2}} = 107.2$, $p_{\ce{SO2}} = p_{\ce{Cl2}} = 71.4$ kPa. $K_p = (71.4\times71.4)/107.2 = \mathbf{47.6\text{ kPa}}$.

Answer 4: Δn = (1+3) − 2 = +2. $RT = 8.31 \times 298 = 2476$ J mol⁻¹. But careful: $K_c$ in mol² dm⁻⁶ → need consistent units. $R = 8.314$ kPa dm³ K⁻¹ mol⁻¹. $RT = 8.314 \times 298 = 2478$ kPa dm³ mol⁻¹. $K_p = K_c(RT)^2 = 2.5\times10^{-8} \times (2478)^2 = 2.5\times10^{-8} \times 6.14\times10^6 = \mathbf{0.154\text{ kPa}^2}$.

Answer 5: ICE in pressures: I = [80, 0, 0]; C = [−p, +p, +p]; E = [80−p, p, p]. $K_p = p^2/(80-p) = 12.5$. $p^2 = 1000 - 12.5p$ → $p^2 + 12.5p - 1000 = 0$. $p = (-12.5 + \sqrt{156.25 + 4000})/2 = (-12.5 + 64.5)/2 = 26.0$ kPa. Equil: $p_{\ce{COCl2}} = 54.0$ kPa, $p_{\ce{CO}} = p_{\ce{Cl2}} = 26.0$ kPa. Check: $(26)^2/54 = 676/54 = 12.5$ ✓.

Answer 6: (a) 20% dissociated → 0.20 mol N₂O₄ reacted. n(N₂O₄)eq = 0.80, n(NO₂)eq = 2×0.20 = 0.40. $n_{\text{total}} = 1.20$. (b) $x_{\ce{N2O4}} = 0.80/1.20 = 0.667$, $x_{\ce{NO2}} = 0.40/1.20 = 0.333$. $p_{\ce{N2O4}} = 0.667\times180 = 120$ kPa, $p_{\ce{NO2}} = 0.333\times180 = 60$ kPa. (c) $K_p = (60)^2/120 = 3600/120 = 30$ kPa. (d) Δn = 1. $K_p = K_c(RT)$. Need RT in kPa dm³ mol⁻¹: $RT = 8.314\times350 = 2910$ kPa dm³ mol⁻¹. $K_c = 30/2910 = \mathbf{0.0103\text{ mol dm}^{-3}}$.

7. Key Terms

TermDefinition
Partial pressure ($p_i$)The pressure a gas would exert if it alone occupied the container: $p_i = x_i \times P_{\text{total}}$
Mole fraction ($x_i$)The fraction of total moles contributed by component $i$: $x_i = n_i / n_{\text{total}}$
$K_p$Equilibrium constant expressed in terms of partial pressures of gaseous species
Dalton's Law$P_{\text{total}} = \sum p_i$ — the total pressure equals the sum of partial pressures
$K_p = K_c(RT)^{\Delta n}$Relationship linking the two equilibrium constants; depends on Δn and temperature
Δn (for $K_p$)Change in moles of gas only: (gaseous products) − (gaseous reactants)

8. Exam Tips

Paper 2 (Structured): $K_p$ calculations follow the same ICE logic as $K_c$, but track moles in the table, then convert to partial pressures. The most commonly missed step: recalculating $n_{\text{total}}$ at equilibrium (it changes when Δn ≠ 0). Always write: "$n_{\text{total}} = \text{sum of E row}$" before calculating mole fractions.
Unit Conversion for $K_p \leftrightarrow K_c$: When using $R = 8.31$ J K⁻¹ mol⁻¹, remember this is Pa m³ K⁻¹ mol⁻¹. If $K_c$ is in mol dm⁻³, convert: 1 mol dm⁻³ = 1000 mol m⁻³. Alternatively, use $R = 8.314$ kPa dm³ K⁻¹ mol⁻¹ if pressures are in kPa — this avoids cubic-metre conversions and is safer for IB.
Paper 1 Trap: Adding inert gas questions are common. At constant volume: adding inert gas increases $P_{\text{total}}$ but does NOT change partial pressures of reacting gases → $Q_p = K_p$ → no shift. At constant pressure: adding inert gas increases volume, decreasing all partial pressures proportionally, which may shift equilibrium if Δn ≠ 0 (like a pressure decrease). Know the distinction.
