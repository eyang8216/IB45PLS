Lesson 41: Stereoisomerism & Synthetic Routes (HL)

Syllabus reference: 20.3 (Stereoisomerism), 20.2 (Synthetic Routes — HL)

Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to:

  • Identify and name E/Z (cis–trans) isomers using Cahn–Ingold–Prelog (CIP) priority rules.
  • Determine whether a molecule is chiral and assign R/S absolute configuration at stereogenic centres.
  • Explain the physical and chemical basis of optical activity, racemic mixtures, and enantiomeric excess.
  • Design multi-step synthetic routes between organic functional groups, selecting appropriate reagents and conditions.
  • Apply retrosynthetic analysis to plan the synthesis of target molecules from simple starting materials.

1. Prior Knowledge Check

Question 1: What is the difference between a structural (constitutional) isomer and a stereoisomer?

▸ Show
Structural isomers have the same molecular formula but different connectivity — atoms are bonded in a different order. Example: propanal $\ce{CH3CH2CHO}$ and propanone $\ce{CH3COCH3}$ (both $\ce{C3H6O}$). Stereoisomers have the same molecular formula AND the same connectivity — they differ only in the spatial arrangement of atoms. Example: (E)-but-2-ene and (Z)-but-2-ene (both $\ce{CH3CH=CHCH3}$).

Question 2: What makes a carbon atom a "chiral centre" (stereogenic centre)?

▸ Show
A carbon atom is a chiral centre (stereogenic centre) if it is $sp^3$ hybridised and bonded to four different groups. The four groups must all be distinct. Example: the C2 of butan-2-ol $\ce{CH3CH(OH)CH2CH3}$ — the groups are $\ce{–H}$, $\ce{–OH}$, $\ce{–CH3}$, and $\ce{–CH2CH3}$ (all different). A molecule with a chiral centre lacks an internal plane of symmetry and is chiral (non-superimposable on its mirror image).

Question 3: What is a "synthetic route" in organic chemistry?

▸ Show
A synthetic route is a sequence of chemical reactions that converts a starting material into a target molecule. Each step specifies reagents, conditions, and the intermediate product. IB expects you to know interconversions between alkanes, alkenes, halogenoalkanes, alcohols, aldehydes, ketones, carboxylic acids, esters, amines, amides, and nitriles — using the reactions learned throughout the organic chemistry unit.

2. E/Z Isomerism (Cis–Trans Stereoisomerism)

E/Z isomerism occurs in alkenes when both carbons of the $\ce{C=C}$ double bond have two different groups attached. The $\pi$ bond prevents rotation, locking the groups in place.

Rule 1: Higher atomic number = higher priority. (Br > Cl > O > N > C > H) Rule 2: If atoms directly attached are the same, move to the next atom. (−CH₂CH₃ > −CH₃ because C,(C,H,H) > C,(H,H,H)) Rule 3: Multiple bonds are treated as multiple single bonds to that atom. (>C=O is treated as C bonded to two oxygens) Assignment: If high-priority groups are on opposite sides → E. If on the same side → Z.
Figure 1: E/Z isomerism in but-2-ene. At each carbon of the C=C, the two groups are ranked by CIP priority. If the two high-priority groups are on opposite sides → E (German entgegen); same side → Z (zusammen).

2.1 Requirement for E/Z Isomerism

A molecule can exhibit E/Z isomerism only if each carbon of the double bond has two different groups. If either carbon has two identical groups (e.g., $\ce{CH2=CH2}$ or $\ce{CH2=CHCH3}$), E/Z isomerism is impossible.

Check-in: Does $\ce{CH3CH=CHCl}$ exhibit E/Z isomerism? If so, draw and label the two isomers.

▸ Show
Yes! C1 of the double bond: groups = H and $\ce{CH3}$ (different). C2: groups = H and Cl (different). Both carbons pass the test. CIP priorities: C1: $\ce{–CH3}$ (C,H,H,H) > $\ce{–H}$ (H). C2: $\ce{–Cl}$ (Cl) > $\ce{–H}$ (H). (E)-isomer: $\ce{CH3}$ and $\ce{Cl}$ on opposite sides. (Z)-isomer: $\ce{CH3}$ and $\ce{Cl}$ on the same side. The (E)-isomer is the more stable (less steric clash).

3. Optical Isomerism — Chirality and Enantiomers

A molecule is chiral if it is not superimposable on its mirror image. The most common source of chirality in organic molecules is a carbon atom bonded to four different groups — a stereogenic centre (chiral centre).

R (S)-butan-2-ol C* CH₃ C₂H₅ H OH CIP order: –OH (1) > –C₂H₅ (2) > –CH₃ (3) > –H (4) With H pointing away: OH→C₂H₅→CH₃ is anticlockwise → S Enantiomers have identical physical properties (bp, mp, solubility) EXCEPT: they rotate plane-polarised light in opposite directions. A 50:50 mixture → racemic mixture (racemate): optically inactive (no net rotation).
Figure 2: Enantiomers of butan-2-ol. The two mirror-image forms are non-superimposable. R/S assignment uses CIP priorities: assign priorities 1–4, place the lowest priority group (4, usually H) pointing away, then trace 1→2→3. Clockwise = R (Latin rectus); anticlockwise = S (sinister).

3.1 Optical Activity

Chiral molecules rotate the plane of plane-polarised light. One enantiomer rotates it clockwise (+ or d); the other rotates it anticlockwise (− or l). The magnitude of rotation depends on concentration, path length, wavelength, and temperature.

Specific rotation: $$[\alpha]_\lambda^T = \frac{\alpha}{c \times l}$$ where $\alpha$ = observed rotation (°), $c$ = concentration (g cm⁻³), $l$ = path length (dm). A racemic mixture has $[\alpha] = 0$. Enantiomeric excess: $\text{ee} = \frac{[\alpha]_\text{obs}}{[\alpha]_\text{pure}} \times 100\%$.

Check-in: A sample of 2-bromobutane has a specific rotation of +11.5°. Pure (R)-2-bromobutane has $[\alpha] = +23.0°$. What is the enantiomeric composition?

▸ Show
Enantiomeric excess = (11.5 / 23.0) × 100% = 50% ee. This means 50% of the sample is the excess (R)-enantiomer, and the remaining 50% is racemic (25% R + 25% S). Total composition: 75% R, 25% S. Check: rotation = (0.75 × +23.0) + (0.25 × −23.0) = +17.25 − 5.75 = +11.5°. ✓

3.2 Chirality Beyond Tetrahedral Carbon

While $sp^3$ carbons with four different groups are the most common chiral centres, other sources exist: axial chirality (e.g., substituted biphenyls where rotation is restricted) and helical chirality (e.g., hexahelicene). For IB, focus on tetrahedral stereogenic centres.

4. Synthetic Routes — The Organic Chemistry "Roadmap"

IB HL expects you to design multi-step syntheses connecting all the functional groups studied. The key is knowing which reagents convert one functional group to another.

Figure 3: The organic synthesis roadmap. Learn the reagents and conditions for each arrow — this is the core of Paper 2 synthesis questions. Arrows are colour-coded by reaction type.

4.1 Retrosynthetic Analysis

Retrosynthesis works backwards: start from the target molecule and "disconnect" bonds to simpler precursors. The symbol ⇒ indicates a retrosynthetic step (the opposite of →).

Check-in: Propose a two-step synthesis of propylamine $\ce{CH3CH2CH2NH2}$ from propan-1-ol.

▸ Show
Route 1: (1) $\ce{CH3CH2CH2OH + SOCl2 -> CH3CH2CH2Cl}$ (or $\ce{PCl5}$). (2) $\ce{CH3CH2CH2Cl + excess NH3}$ (in ethanol, sealed tube, heat) → $\ce{CH3CH2CH2NH2}$ (SN2). Route 2: (1) $\ce{CH3CH2CH2OH + K2Cr2O7/H+}$ reflux → $\ce{CH3CH2COOH}$. (2) $\ce{CH3CH2COOH + SOCl2 -> CH3CH2COCl}$. (3) $\ce{CH3CH2COCl + NH3 -> CH3CH2CONH2}$. (4) $\ce{CH3CH2CONH2 + LiAlH4}$ → $\ce{CH3CH2CH2NH2}$. Route 1 is shorter (2 steps vs 4) and more efficient. IB rewards the shortest reasonable route.

5. Worked Examples

Example 1 — R/S Assignment:

Assign R/S configuration to the chiral centre in (S)-2-chlorobutane $\ce{CH3CHClCH2CH3}$ and verify the label.

Step 1: Identify the four groups on C2: $\ce{–Cl}$, $\ce{–CH3}$, $\ce{–CH2CH3}$, $\ce{–H}$.
Step 2: CIP priority by atomic number: Cl (17) > C (6) > C (6) > H (1). So: Cl = priority 1, H = priority 4.
Step 3: Tie between $\ce{–CH3}$ and $\ce{–CH2CH3}$ — both start with C. Move to next atoms: $\ce{–CH3}$: C,(H,H,H). $\ce{–CH2CH3}$: C,(C,H,H). The ethyl group "meets" a carbon sooner → $\ce{–CH2CH3}$ = priority 2, $\ce{–CH3}$ = priority 3.
Step 4: Orient with H (priority 4) pointing away. Trace 1→2→3 ($\ce{Cl -> CH2CH3 -> CH3}$). In the S enantiomer, this is anticlockwise → S. ✓

⚠️ Common wrong approach: Assigning $\ce{–CH3}$ higher priority than $\ce{–CH2CH3}$ because "methyl comes before ethyl alphabetically." CIP priority uses atomic number, NOT alphabetical order.
Example 2 — Multi-step Synthesis Design:

Design a synthesis of ethyl ethanoate starting from ethene as the ONLY organic starting material. All other reagents (inorganic) may be used.

Retrosynthesis: Ethyl ethanoate $\ce{CH3COOC2H5}$ ⇒ ethanoic acid + ethanol ⇒ ethene (common origin).
Forward route:
Path A — Ethanol: $\ce{CH2=CH2 + H2O ->[H3PO4][300°C, 60 atm] CH3CH2OH}$ (hydration).
Path B — Ethanoic acid: $\ce{CH2=CH2 + H2O -> CH3CH2OH}$ (as above). Then: $\ce{CH3CH2OH + K2Cr2O7/H+}$ (reflux, excess oxidant) → $\ce{CH3COOH}$ (via acetaldehyde).
Esterification: $\ce{CH3COOH + CH3CH2OH ->[H2SO4][\Delta] CH3COOCH2CH3 + H2O}$.
Overall: 3 steps from ethene. All carbon atoms in the product come from ethene.

⚠️ Common wrong approach: Starting from ethanol and ethanoic acid as if they are "available" starting materials — the question specifies ethene as the only organic starting material.
Example 3 — Isomer Enumeration with Stereoisomerism:

Draw and name ALL isomers of $\ce{C4H8}$ (including stereoisomers).

Alkenes ($\ce{C4H8}$): (1) but-1-ene $\ce{CH2=CHCH2CH3}$, (2) (E)-but-2-ene, (3) (Z)-but-2-ene, (4) 2-methylpropene $\ce{(CH3)2C=CH2}$.
Cycloalkanes ($\ce{C4H8}$): (5) cyclobutane, (6) methylcyclopropane.
Total: 6 isomers. But-1-ene has no E/Z (C1 has two identical H atoms). 2-methylpropene also has no E/Z. But-2-ene gives E and Z stereoisomers.

⚠️ Common wrong approach: Forgetting cycloalkanes. $\ce{C_nH_{2n}}$ can be either an alkene OR a cycloalkane — they are structural isomers (different functional groups/connectivity). Also, counting but-2-ene as only one isomer and missing E/Z.

⚠️ Common Mistakes to Avoid

  1. CIP priority is NOT alphabetical. It is based on atomic number at the first point of difference. $\ce{–Br > –Cl > –OCH3 > –OH > –NH2 > –COOH > –CHO > –CH2OH > –C6H5 > –CH3 > –H}$. Memorise the common priority groups.
  2. E/Z and cis–trans are NOT always equivalent. Cis–trans works only when each carbon of the C=C has one H and one other group. E/Z is the general system using CIP rules. For but-2-ene, (E) = trans and (Z) = cis. For $\ce{CH3CH=CHCl}$, (E) ≠ simple cis/trans terminology — use E/Z.
  3. A molecule with a chiral centre is not always chiral. Meso compounds have chiral centres but an internal plane of symmetry, making the molecule achiral overall. Example: tartaric acid — the (R,S) form is meso (optically inactive despite having two chiral centres).
  4. Optical rotation (+/−) does NOT correlate with R/S. R/S is a naming convention based on CIP rules. (+) and (−) are experimental measurements. An (R)-enantiomer can be (+) or (−) — there is no relationship. Do not assume R = +.
  5. Every synthetic step must have correct reagents AND conditions. Writing "oxidise" without specifying $\ce{K2Cr2O7/H+}$ (or $\ce{KMnO4/H+}$) AND whether to distil or reflux loses marks. Reagents + conditions = full marks.

6. End-of-Lesson Practice

Question 1 (Paper 1 style): Which molecule is chiral?
A. $\ce{CH3CH2COOH}$   B. $\ce{CH3CH(OH)CH3}$   C. $\ce{CH3CHClCH2CH3}$   D. $\ce{CH3COCH3}$

Question 2: Assign E/Z configuration to each of the following: (a) $\ce{CH3CH=CHBr}$, (b) $\ce{(CH3)2C=CHCH3}$, (c) $\ce{ClCH=CHCOOH}$.

Question 3: (R)-2-bromobutane reacts with $\ce{NaOH(aq)}$ via SN2. What is the stereochemistry of the product? Explain using the mechanism.

Question 4: Propose a three-step synthesis of propanoic acid from propan-1-ol, showing all reagents and conditions.

Question 5: Draw all stereoisomers of 2,3-dichlorobutane $\ce{CH3CHClCHClCH3}$. Identify which are enantiomers and which are diastereomers. Is there a meso compound?

Question 6 (Paper 2 style): Starting from benzene, propose a synthesis of (a) 4-nitrobenzoic acid and (b) 1,4-diaminobenzene. Show all reagents, conditions, and intermediates. Justify the order of reactions.

Answer 1: C. $\ce{CH3CHClCH2CH3}$ (2-chlorobutane) — C2 is bonded to $\ce{–H}$, $\ce{–Cl}$, $\ce{–CH3}$, and $\ce{–CH2CH3}$ (all different) → chiral. A: $\ce{CH3CH2COOH}$ — C2 has two H atoms (not chiral). B: $\ce{CH3CH(OH)CH3}$ — C2 bonded to $\ce{–H}$, $\ce{–OH}$, $\ce{–CH3}$, and $\ce{–CH3}$ (two identical groups → achiral). D: $\ce{CH3COCH3}$ — carbonyl C is $sp^2$, not a stereogenic centre.

Answer 2: (a) C1: $\ce{–CH3}$ > $\ce{–H}$. C2: $\ce{–Br}$ > $\ce{–H}$. If $\ce{CH3}$ and $\ce{Br}$ are on opposite sides → E; same side → Z. (b) C1: $\ce{–CH3}$ > $\ce{–CH3}$ (same!) → C1 has two identical groups → no E/Z isomerism possible. (c) C1: $\ce{–Cl}$ > $\ce{–H}$. C2: $\ce{–COOH}$ (C,(O,O,O)) > $\ce{–H}$. E or Z depending on relative positions.

Answer 3: SN2 proceeds with Walden inversion. $\ce{OH-}$ attacks from the back side (opposite to Br). The (R)-2-bromobutane becomes (S)-butan-2-ol. The CIP priority at C2 changes: OH (priority 1) replaces Br (priority 1 in starting material). The absolute configuration flips from R to S.

Answer 4: Step 1: Propan-1-ol + $\ce{K2Cr2O7/H+}$, distil → propanal $\ce{CH3CH2CHO}$. Step 2: Propanal + $\ce{K2Cr2O7/H+}$, reflux → propanoic acid $\ce{CH3CH2COOH}$. Or directly: propan-1-ol + excess $\ce{K2Cr2O7/H+}$, reflux → propanoic acid (one-pot, 1 step — but two oxidation events). IB may accept either the one-pot or two-step approach. Alternative: $\ce{SOCl2}$ then $\ce{KCN}$ then $\ce{H+/H2O}$ hydrolysis — longer but valid.

Answer 5: $\ce{CH3CHClCHClCH3}$ has two chiral centres (C2 and C3). Four possible stereoisomers (2² = 4): (2R,3R), (2S,3S), (2R,3S), and (2S,3R). The (2R,3S) and (2S,3R) forms are identical — this is the meso compound (internal plane of symmetry). So only 3 stereoisomers exist: the (R,R)/(S,S) enantiomeric pair and the meso form. The meso compound is optically inactive. (R,R) and meso are diastereomers (not mirror images).

Answer 6: (a) Step 1 — FC alkylation: $\ce{C6H6 + CH3Cl ->[AlCl3] C6H5CH3}$ (toluene). Methyl is o/p-directing. Step 2 — Oxidation: $\ce{C6H5CH3 + KMnO4/H+}$, reflux → $\ce{C6H5COOH}$ (benzoic acid). Step 3 — Nitration: $\ce{C6H5COOH + HNO3/H2SO4}$ → 3-nitrobenzoic acid (meta, because –COOH is m-directing). Hmm — the target is 4-nitro. Better route: Nitrate toluene first (methyl = o/p-directing): $\ce{C6H5CH3 + HNO3/H2SO4}$ → mixture of 2-nitrotoluene and 4-nitrotoluene. Separate or use low temperature to favour para. Then oxidise: 4-nitrotoluene + $\ce{KMnO4/H+}$ → 4-nitrobenzoic acid. Order matters: –CH₃ is o/p-directing; –COOH is m-directing. Introducing –NO₂ before oxidation gives the para product. (b) Nitrate benzene → nitrobenzene. Reduce: $\ce{C6H5NO2 + Sn/HCl}$ or $\ce{H2/Ni}$ → $\ce{C6H5NH2}$ (aniline). Then nitrate aniline — but $\ce{–NH2}$ is strongly activating and o/p-directing. Protect or use a different route.

7. Key Terms

TermDefinition
StereoisomerCompounds with the same molecular formula and same connectivity, but different spatial arrangement of atoms
E/Z isomerismStereoisomerism about a double bond; E = higher priority groups on opposite sides; Z = same side (CIP rules)
Cahn–Ingold–Prelog (CIP) rulesSystem for assigning priority: higher atomic number = higher priority; if tied, compare next atoms; treat multiple bonds as multiple single bonds
ChiralityA molecule is chiral if it is non-superimposable on its mirror image; usually due to an $sp^3$ carbon with four different groups
EnantiomerOne of a pair of non-superimposable mirror-image stereoisomers; identical physical properties except optical rotation
Racemic mixture (racemate)A 50:50 mixture of two enantiomers; optically inactive (equal and opposite rotations cancel)
DiastereomerStereoisomers that are NOT mirror images; different physical properties (mp, bp, solubility)
Meso compoundA molecule with chiral centres but an internal plane of symmetry; achiral overall and optically inactive
Optical activityThe ability of a chiral molecule to rotate the plane of plane-polarised light; measured as specific rotation $[\alpha]$
Enantiomeric excess (ee)The percentage excess of one enantiomer over the other in a mixture: ee = (|R−S|/(R+S)) × 100%
Synthetic routeA planned sequence of chemical reactions converting a starting material to a target molecule, specifying reagents and conditions at each step
RetrosynthesisWorking backwards from a target molecule to simpler precursors by "disconnecting" bonds; denoted by ⇒

9. Exam Tips

Paper 1 — R/S and E/Z: Questions often show a molecule and ask for its configuration. Practice rotating molecules in your head so the lowest priority group (H or CH₃) is pointing away. Remember: if H is NOT pointing away, the clockwise/anticlockwise assignment is reversed. Use the "swap and invert" trick: swap the group at the back with the lowest priority, determine R/S, then invert the answer.
Paper 2 — Synthesis Design (5–8 marks): Write each step as a separate equation with reagents OVER the arrow, conditions BELOW. Show all intermediates. If asked for the "shortest route," count steps — fewer steps = better marks. Justify your route: "I introduced group X before group Y because X is ortho/para-directing, giving the desired substitution pattern."
Stereochemistry in mechanism questions: When an SN2 reaction occurs at a chiral centre, you MUST show inversion in your product drawing. Draw the starting material with clear wedges/dashes, the transition state with the nucleophile approaching from the back, and the product with inverted configuration. Missing the stereochemistry loses 1–2 marks even if the rest of the mechanism is correct.
