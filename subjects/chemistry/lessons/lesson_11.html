1. Prior Knowledge Check

Question 1: What makes a bond polar? Give an example of a polar bond and a non-polar bond.

‚Ėł Show
A bond is polar when the two atoms have different electronegativities, causing unequal sharing of electrons and creating a bond dipole. Polar: $\ce{H-Cl}$ (EN difference ‚Čą 1.0). Non-polar: $\ce{Cl-Cl}$ (EN difference = 0). The greater the EN difference, the more polar the bond.

Question 2: Why is $\ce{CO2}$ non-polar even though the C=O bonds are polar?

‚Ėł Show
$\ce{CO2}$ is linear (O=C=O). The two C=O bond dipoles are equal in magnitude but point in opposite directions (180¬į apart). They cancel vectorially, giving no net molecular dipole. Symmetry is the key ‚ÄĒ a molecule with polar bonds can still be non-polar if the shape makes the dipoles cancel.

Question 3: Name the strongest type of intermolecular force. Between which atoms does it occur?

‚Ėł Show
Hydrogen bonding is the strongest intermolecular force. It occurs when H is covalently bonded to a highly electronegative atom (N, O, or F) and is attracted to a lone pair on N, O, or F on a neighbouring molecule. Example: between $\ce{H2O}$ molecules.

2. Molecular Polarity ‚ÄĒ Shape Matters

2.1 From Bond Polarity to Molecular Polarity

A molecule is polar if it has a net dipole moment ‚ÄĒ an overall separation of charge. This requires:

  1. Polar bonds (atoms with different electronegativities).
  2. Asymmetric shape ‚ÄĒ the bond dipoles must NOT cancel vectorially.
Figure 1: CO‚āā has polar bonds but is non-polar ‚ÄĒ the linear shape cancels dipoles. H‚āāO has polar bonds AND is polar ‚ÄĒ the bent shape prevents cancellation. CCl‚āĄ has four polar bonds but tetrahedral symmetry makes it non-polar.

Check-in: $\ce{BF3}$ and $\ce{NF3}$ both have polar bonds. Which is polar and which is non-polar? Explain.

‚Ėł Show
$\ce{BF3}$ is trigonal planar (3 bonding, 0 lone pairs). The three B‚ÄďF bond dipoles are arranged symmetrically at 120¬į and cancel ‚Üí non-polar. $\ce{NF3}$ is trigonal pyramidal (3 bonding, 1 lone pair). The three N‚ÄďF bond dipoles do NOT cancel because the molecule is asymmetric ‚ÄĒ the net dipole points toward the fluorine atoms (downward) ‚Üí polar.

3. The Hierarchy of Intermolecular Forces

3.1 London (Dispersion) Forces ‚ÄĒ Present in ALL Molecules

London forces (also called instantaneous dipole‚Äďinduced dipole forces or van der Waals' forces) arise from temporary fluctuations in electron distribution. At any instant, electrons may be unevenly distributed, creating a temporary dipole that induces a dipole in a neighbouring molecule.

London forces increase with:

  • More electrons ‚ÄĒ larger electron cloud = more polarisable = stronger temporary dipoles.
  • Greater surface area ‚ÄĒ more contact between molecules.
  • Larger molecular mass (as a proxy for electron count).

3.2 Dipole‚ÄďDipole Forces ‚ÄĒ Polar Molecules Only

Permanent dipoles in polar molecules attract each other. The positive end ($\delta^+$) of one molecule is attracted to the negative end ($\delta^-$) of another. These are stronger than London forces for molecules of comparable size.

3.3 Hydrogen Bonding ‚ÄĒ The Strongest IMF

A hydrogen bond is a special, strong dipole‚Äďdipole interaction that occurs when:

  • H is covalently bonded to a very electronegative atom: N, O, or F.
  • This H is attracted to a lone pair on N, O, or F of a neighbouring molecule.
Figure 2: Hydrogen bonding between two water molecules. The H-bond (purple dashed line) is much weaker than a covalent bond but strong enough to dramatically affect physical properties.

Check-in: Which of these can form hydrogen bonds: $\ce{CH4}$, $\ce{NH3}$, $\ce{HF}$, $\ce{CH3F}$, $\ce{CH3OH}$? Explain each.

‚Ėł Show
$\ce{CH4}$: No ‚ÄĒ H is bonded to C (not N/O/F). $\ce{NH3}$: Yes ‚ÄĒ H bonded to N, and N has a lone pair. $\ce{HF}$: Yes ‚ÄĒ H bonded to F, and F has lone pairs. $\ce{CH3F}$: No ‚ÄĒ although F is present, the H atoms are bonded to C, not F. F has lone pairs but no H‚ÄďF bond exists. $\ce{CH3OH}$: Yes ‚ÄĒ the O‚ÄďH group can donate H-bonds, and O has lone pairs to accept them.

4. Physical Properties and IMFs

4.1 Boiling Points ‚ÄĒ The Ultimate IMF Test

Boiling point comparisons are the most common IB exam question. The general trend:

$$\text{Hydrogen bonding} > \text{Dipole‚Äďdipole} > \text{London forces only}$$

Check-in: Rank $\ce{C2H6}$, $\ce{CH3F}$, $\ce{CH3OH}$ in order of increasing boiling point. All have similar molecular mass.

‚Ėł Show
$\ce{C2H6}$ (lowest) ‚ÄĒ only London forces (non-polar). $\ce{CH3F}$ ‚ÄĒ polar (dipole‚Äďdipole), stronger than London alone. $\ce{CH3OH}$ (highest) ‚ÄĒ hydrogen bonding (strongest IMF). Boiling points: C‚āāH‚āÜ = ‚ąí89¬įC, CH‚āÉF = ‚ąí78¬įC, CH‚āÉOH = 65¬įC. The jump from CH‚āÉF to CH‚āÉOH is dramatic because H-bonding is much stronger than dipole‚Äďdipole.

4.2 The Anomalous Properties of Water

Water's hydrogen bonding network explains its unique properties:

  • High boiling point (100¬įC) ‚ÄĒ for such a light molecule (M = 18), this is extraordinary. Compare $\ce{H2S}$ (M = 34, b.p. = ‚ąí60¬įC) and $\ce{H2Se}$ (M = 81, b.p. = ‚ąí41¬įC). Water's H-bonds require extra energy to break.
  • Density maximum at 4¬įC ‚ÄĒ ice is less dense than liquid water because H-bonds hold molecules in an open hexagonal lattice. This is why ice floats ‚ÄĒ crucial for aquatic life.
  • High surface tension ‚ÄĒ the strong cohesive forces from H-bonding allow water striders to walk on water and form droplets.
  • High specific heat capacity ‚ÄĒ H-bonds absorb a lot of energy before breaking, making water an excellent temperature buffer.

5. Worked Examples

Example 1 ‚ÄĒ Boiling Point Trend in Group 16 Hydrides:

The boiling points of Group 16 hydrides are: $\ce{H2O}$ (100¬įC), $\ce{H2S}$ (‚ąí60¬įC), $\ce{H2Se}$ (‚ąí41¬įC), $\ce{H2Te}$ (‚ąí2¬įC). Explain the trend.

If only London forces mattered, boiling point would increase smoothly down the group (more electrons = stronger London forces). This is observed from $\ce{H2S}$ ‚Üí $\ce{H2Se}$ ‚Üí $\ce{H2Te}$. However, $\ce{H2O}$ is dramatically higher ‚ÄĒ an anomaly caused by hydrogen bonding. Water molecules form an extensive H-bond network (each H‚āāO can form up to 4 H-bonds), which requires much more energy to disrupt than London forces alone. $\ce{H2S}$ cannot form H-bonds because S is not electronegative enough (EN = 2.5 vs. O = 3.5).
Example 2 ‚ÄĒ Isomers and Boiling Point:

Pentane ($\ce{CH3CH2CH2CH2CH3}$, b.p. 36¬įC) and 2,2-dimethylpropane ($\ce{C(CH3)4}$, b.p. 9.5¬įC) are isomers ($\ce{C5H12}$). Explain the difference in boiling point despite identical molecular mass.

Both are non-polar, so only London forces operate. However, pentane is a linear chain with a large surface area ‚ÄĒ neighbouring molecules can pack closely and interact along the entire length. 2,2-dimethylpropane is nearly spherical ‚ÄĒ molecules have less surface contact, so the instantaneous dipoles induce less effectively. More branching ‚Üí more spherical ‚Üí smaller surface area ‚Üí weaker London forces ‚Üí lower boiling point.
Example 3 ‚ÄĒ Solubility: "Like Dissolves Like":

Predict whether each substance is soluble in water (polar, H-bonding) or hexane (non-polar): (a) $\ce{NaCl}$, (b) $\ce{I2}$, (c) $\ce{CH3CH2OH}$ (ethanol), (d) $\ce{CH3(CH2)4CH3}$ (hexane itself).

(a) NaCl: ionic ‚ÄĒ soluble in water (ion‚Äďdipole interactions with H‚āāO), insoluble in hexane.
(b) I‚āā: non-polar covalent ‚ÄĒ soluble in hexane (London forces match), very slightly soluble in water.
(c) CH‚āÉCH‚āāOH: has polar O‚ÄďH group (H-bonding) and non-polar C‚āāH‚āÖ group ‚ÄĒ miscible with water (H-bonding) AND soluble in hexane (London forces with alkyl chain). The alkyl chain makes it partly soluble in non-polar solvents.
(d) Hexane: non-polar ‚ÄĒ miscible with hexane, immiscible with water.

6. Common Misconceptions

‚ö†ÔłŹ Common Mistakes to Avoid

  1. Calling all intermolecular forces "van der Waals." The IB distinguishes: London (dispersion) forces, dipole‚Äďdipole forces, and hydrogen bonding are all types of van der Waals' forces. But in IB exams, "van der Waals' forces" usually refers specifically to London/dispersion forces. Be precise.
  2. Thinking hydrogen bonds are covalent bonds. A hydrogen bond is an intermolecular force (‚Čą10‚Äď40 kJ mol‚ĀĽ¬Ļ), much weaker than a covalent O‚ÄďH bond (‚Čą463 kJ mol‚ĀĽ¬Ļ). The H is covalently bonded to one O and H-bonded to a different O.
  3. Saying "H-bonding occurs whenever H is present." The H must be bonded to N, O, or F. $\ce{CH4}$ has H atoms but no H-bonding. Even $\ce{CH3F}$ has no H-bonding because the H atoms are bonded to C, not F.
  4. Ignoring molecular shape when assessing polarity. $\ce{CCl4}$ has four polar C‚ÄďCl bonds but is non-polar because the tetrahedral shape is symmetric. Always draw the shape before deciding polarity.
  5. Assuming larger molecules always have higher boiling points due to stronger London forces ‚ÄĒ without checking for H-bonding. $\ce{H2O}$ (M = 18) has a much higher boiling point than $\ce{C3H8}$ (M = 44) because H-bonding trumps London forces.

Question 5: The boiling points of the noble gases are: He (‚ąí269¬įC), Ne (‚ąí246¬įC), Ar (‚ąí186¬įC), Kr (‚ąí152¬įC), Xe (‚ąí107¬įC). Explain the trend in terms of intermolecular forces.

Question 6 (HL-data analysis): The graph below (described) shows boiling points of Period 2 hydrides: $\ce{CH4}$ (‚ąí164¬įC), $\ce{NH3}$ (‚ąí33¬įC), $\ce{H2O}$ (100¬įC), $\ce{HF}$ (20¬įC). Account for the shape of the graph, explaining each data point in terms of intermolecular forces.

Answer 1: (a) $\ce{CH4}$: tetrahedral, symmetric ‚Üí non-polar. (b) $\ce{CH3Cl}$: tetrahedral but asymmetric (one Cl, three H) ‚Üí polar. (c) $\ce{SF6}$: octahedral, symmetric ‚Üí non-polar. (d) $\ce{SO2}$: bent (one lone pair on S) ‚Üí polar.

Answer 2: (a) $\ce{HCl}$: London forces + dipole‚Äďdipole (polar molecule). No H-bonding (Cl is not N/O/F). (b) $\ce{CH3OCH3}$: London forces + dipole‚Äďdipole (bent around O, polar). The O has lone pairs but no O‚ÄďH bond, so no H-bonding. (c) $\ce{CH3COOH}$: London forces + dipole‚Äďdipole + hydrogen bonding (O‚ÄďH group). Acetic acid actually forms dimers via two H-bonds between carboxyl groups.

Answer 3: HF has hydrogen bonding (H bonded to F, the most electronegative element). Each HF molecule forms strong H-bonds with neighbours. HCl only has dipole‚Äďdipole forces and London forces ‚ÄĒ no H-bonding because Cl is not electronegative enough and is too large for effective H-bonding. Despite HCl having more electrons (stronger London forces), H-bonding in HF dominates, giving a boiling point 105¬įC higher.

Answer 4: Propan-1-ol has an ‚ÄďOH group ‚Üí can form hydrogen bonds. Methoxyethane is an ether ‚ÄĒ it has a polar C‚ÄďO‚ÄďC group and can participate in dipole‚Äďdipole forces but has no H-bond donor (no O‚ÄďH bond). The 89¬įC difference illustrates the dramatic effect of H-bonding vs. dipole‚Äďdipole on boiling point.

Answer 5: Noble gases are monatomic and non-polar ‚ÄĒ only London (dispersion) forces are present. Down the group, the number of electrons increases (He: 2, Ne: 10, Ar: 18, Kr: 36, Xe: 54), so the electron cloud becomes larger and more polarisable. Stronger temporary dipoles ‚Üí stronger London forces ‚Üí higher boiling point. The trend is smooth with no anomalies because no other IMFs are present.

Answer 6: The general trend expected for Period 2 hydrides (based on electron count) would be a smooth increase from CH‚āĄ to HF. Instead, the graph shows three anomalies: NH‚āÉ, H‚āāO, and HF all have elevated boiling points. CH‚āĄ: only London forces (non-polar, tetrahedral) ‚Üí lowest b.p. NH‚āÉ: H-bonding (H‚ÄďN bonds, lone pair on N) ‚Üí elevated. H‚āāO: strongest H-bonding (each H‚āāO can form up to 4 H-bonds, two as donor via O‚ÄďH, two as acceptor via lone pairs) ‚Üí highest b.p. HF: H-bonding (H‚ÄďF bond) but each HF can only form 2 H-bonds on average (one donor, one acceptor) ‚Üí elevated but slightly lower than H‚āāO. The anomaly is caused by H-bonding; without it, the boiling points would follow the electron-count trend like Group 14 hydrides.

8. Key Terms

TermDefinition
ElectronegativityThe ability of an atom to attract a bonding pair of electrons (Pauling scale)
Bond dipoleA separation of charge within a polar covalent bond (őī‚Āļ and őī‚ĀĽ)
Molecular dipole (őľ)The net dipole moment of the entire molecule; the vector sum of all bond dipoles
London (dispersion) forcesWeak IMFs caused by temporary, instantaneous dipoles that induce dipoles in neighbours; present in ALL molecules
Dipole‚Äďdipole forcesAttractions between permanent dipoles of polar molecules
Hydrogen bondA strong dipole‚Äďdipole interaction where H (bonded to N/O/F) is attracted to a lone pair on N/O/F of another molecule
PolarisabilityThe ease with which an electron cloud can be distorted; increases with more electrons and larger atomic radius
Surface tensionThe tendency of a liquid surface to resist rupture; caused by cohesive IMFs
ViscosityA liquid's resistance to flow; stronger IMFs ‚Üí higher viscosity
MiscibilityThe ability of two liquids to mix in all proportions; governed by "like dissolves like"

9. Exam Tips

Paper 1 ‚ÄĒ Boiling Point Comparisons: When asked to rank boiling points, check in this order: (1) H-bonding? ‚Üí highest. (2) Dipole‚Äďdipole? ‚Üí intermediate. (3) Only London forces? ‚Üí lowest for similar mass. Within the same IMF type, more electrons (larger M) ‚Üí higher b.p. For isomers, less branching ‚Üí more linear ‚Üí higher b.p. Memorise: H‚āāO > HF > NH‚āÉ for Period 2 hydrides.
Paper 2 ‚ÄĒ "Explain why..." questions: Always state: (1) the types of IMFs present in each substance, (2) their relative strengths, (3) the consequence for the physical property. Example: "Ethanol has a higher boiling point than dimethyl ether because ethanol can form hydrogen bonds (due to its O‚ÄďH group), whereas dimethyl ether can only form dipole‚Äďdipole forces. Hydrogen bonds are stronger, so more energy is required to separate ethanol molecules."
Common Data Booklet Values: The Data Booklet provides electronegativity values (Pauling scale). Use these to calculate őĒEN and determine bond polarity. General rule: őĒEN > 1.7 ‚Üí ionic; 0.3‚Äď1.7 ‚Üí polar covalent; < 0.3 ‚Üí non-polar covalent. But polarity is a continuum and these are approximate boundaries.
