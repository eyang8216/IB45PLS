Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to:

  • Distinguish between elementary steps and the overall reaction; define molecularity (unimolecular, bimolecular).
  • Identify the rate-determining step (RDS) in a proposed mechanism and predict the resulting rate equation.
  • Explain why the rate equation reflects the molecularity of the RDS, not the overall stoichiometry.
  • Apply the Arrhenius equation: $k = Ae^{-E_a/RT}$ and its logarithmic form $\ln k = \ln A - E_a/RT$.
  • Calculate $E_a$ from rate constant data at two temperatures or from the slope of an Arrhenius plot.

1. Prior Knowledge Check

Question 1: Define the rate-determining step of a multi-step reaction.

‚Ėł Show
The rate-determining step (RDS) is the slowest elementary step in a reaction mechanism. It acts as a bottleneck ‚ÄĒ the overall rate cannot exceed the rate of this step. All steps after the RDS are kinetically invisible because they occur faster than the RDS can feed them intermediates.

Question 2: Why does raising the temperature increase the rate constant $k$?

‚Ėł Show
Higher temperature increases the average kinetic energy of particles, shifting the Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution so that a much larger fraction of collisions have energy $\ge E_a$. This exponentially increases the proportion of effective collisions, which is reflected in a larger $k$. The Arrhenius equation quantifies this: $k = Ae^{-E_a/RT}$.

2. Reaction Mechanisms ‚ÄĒ Elementary Steps

Most chemical reactions do not occur in a single collision. Instead, they proceed through a series of elementary steps, each involving a small number of particles colliding or a single particle dissociating. The sequence of elementary steps is the reaction mechanism.

2.1 Molecularity

  • Unimolecular: One particle rearranges or dissociates (e.g., $\ce{A -> B + C}$). Rate = $k[\ce{A}]$.
  • Bimolecular: Two particles collide (e.g., $\ce{A + B -> C}$ or $\ce{2A -> C}$). Rate = $k[\ce{A}][\ce{B}]$ or $k[\ce{A}]^2$.
  • Termolecular: Three particles collide simultaneously ‚ÄĒ extremely rare in practice.
Key principle: For any elementary step, the rate law follows directly from the molecularity. The order with respect to each reactant equals its coefficient in that step.

2.2 The Rate-Determining Step (RDS)

In a multi-step mechanism, the overall rate is governed by the slowest step ‚ÄĒ the RDS. Steps before the RDS establish a pre-equilibrium that feeds the RDS. Steps after the RDS consume intermediates rapidly and do not affect the rate law.

Figure 1: A three-step mechanism. Step 2 is the RDS (slowest). Only the species in Step 2 appear in the rate law. Steps before the RDS may create intermediates that appear in the rate law; steps after the RDS are kinetically invisible.

Check-in: A proposed mechanism for $\ce{2A + B -> C + D}$ is: (1) $\ce{A + B -> X}$ (slow), (2) $\ce{X + A -> C + D}$ (fast). Predict the rate equation.

‚Ėł Show
Step 1 is rate-determining and bimolecular (A + B colliding). The rate = $k[\ce{A}][\ce{B}]$. Overall order = 2. Note: even though the overall equation has 2A + B, the rate law is $k[\ce{A}][\ce{B}]$, not $k[\ce{A}]^2[\ce{B}]$. The stoichiometric coefficient of A (2) is irrelevant ‚ÄĒ only what happens in the RDS matters.

3. The Arrhenius Equation

The Arrhenius equation quantifies how the rate constant $k$ depends on temperature and activation energy:

Arrhenius equation: $$k = A e^{-E_a/RT}$$ $k$ = rate constant; $A$ = pre-exponential (frequency) factor; $E_a$ = activation energy (J mol‚ĀĽ¬Ļ); $R = 8.31$ J K‚ĀĽ¬Ļ mol‚ĀĽ¬Ļ; $T$ = temperature (K).

The exponential term $e^{-E_a/RT}$ represents the fraction of collisions with energy $\ge E_a$. As $T$ increases or $E_a$ decreases, this fraction grows. The pre-exponential factor $A$ accounts for collision frequency and orientation probability.

3.1 Logarithmic Form

Taking natural logs of both sides gives a linear equation:

$$\ln k = \ln A - \frac{E_a}{R}\left(\frac{1}{T}\right)$$

This has the form $y = c + mx$ where $y = \ln k$, $x = 1/T$, $m = -E_a/R$, and $c = \ln A$.

3.2 Two-Point Form

If you know $k$ at two temperatures:

$$\ln\left(\frac{k_2}{k_1}\right) = \frac{E_a}{R}\left(\frac{1}{T_1} - \frac{1}{T_2}\right)$$
Figure 2: An Arrhenius plot: $\ln k$ against $1/T$. The negative slope = $-E_a/R$. The $y$-intercept at $1/T=0$ (infinite temperature) gives $\ln A$. A steeper slope means a higher $E_a$ ‚ÄĒ the reaction is more temperature-sensitive.

Check-in: The rate constant doubles when the temperature is raised from 298 K to 308 K. Estimate $E_a$. ($R = 8.31$ J K‚ĀĽ¬Ļ mol‚ĀĽ¬Ļ)

‚Ėł Show
$\ln(k_2/k_1) = \ln 2 = 0.693$. $1/T_1 - 1/T_2 = 1/298 - 1/308 = 1.09 \times 10^{-4}$. $E_a = R \times \ln 2 / (1/T_1 - 1/T_2) = 8.31 \times 0.693 / (1.09\times10^{-4}) = \mathbf{5.28 \times 10^4\text{ J mol}^{-1} = 52.8\text{ kJ mol}^{-1}}$. This is a typical $E_a$ for many organic reactions. Note that J mol‚ĀĽ¬Ļ is the IB-preferred unit for Arrhenius calculations.

4. Connecting Mechanism to Rate Law

When the RDS involves an intermediate formed in a prior fast equilibrium step, you must express the intermediate's concentration in terms of reactants using the equilibrium constant.

Check-in: Mechanism: $\ce{A <=>[fast] B}$; $\ce{B + C -> D}$ (slow). Derive the rate law.

‚Ėł Show
RDS: rate = $k_2[\ce{B}][\ce{C}]$. But B is an intermediate. From the fast equilibrium: $K = [\ce{B}]/[\ce{A}]$, so $[\ce{B}] = K[\ce{A}]$. Substituting: rate = $k_2 K [\ce{A}][\ce{C}] = k_{\text{obs}}[\ce{A}][\ce{C}]$, where $k_{\text{obs}} = k_2 K$. The overall rate law is first order in A and first order in C, even though C does not appear in the equilibrium step.

5. Worked Examples

Example 1 ‚ÄĒ Predicting Rate Law from Mechanism:

The reaction $\ce{2NO(g) + O2(g) -> 2NO2(g)}$ has the experimental rate law: rate = $k[\ce{NO}]^2[\ce{O2}]$. A proposed mechanism is:
(1) $\ce{NO + O2 <=>[\text{fast}] NO3}$
(2) $\ce{NO3 + NO ->[\text{slow}] 2NO2}$
Is the mechanism consistent with the rate law?

Solution: Step 2 is RDS: rate = $k_2[\ce{NO3}][\ce{NO}]$. $\ce{NO3}$ is an intermediate. From step 1 equilibrium: $K = [\ce{NO3}]/([\ce{NO}][\ce{O2}])$, so $[\ce{NO3}] = K[\ce{NO}][\ce{O2}]$. Substituting: rate = $k_2 K [\ce{NO}][\ce{O2}][\ce{NO}] = k[\ce{NO}]^2[\ce{O2}]$, where $k = k_2 K$. This matches the experimental rate law ‚ÄĒ the mechanism is consistent.

‚ö†ÔłŹ Common wrong approach: Claiming "the mechanism is correct." Consistency does not prove the mechanism ‚ÄĒ it only shows the mechanism is not ruled out. Other mechanisms might also be consistent. Kinetics can disprove but never prove a mechanism.
Example 2 ‚ÄĒ Arrhenius from Two Temperatures:

For $\ce{2HI -> H2 + I2}$, $k = 3.5 \times 10^{-7}$ dm¬≥ mol‚ĀĽ¬Ļ s‚ĀĽ¬Ļ at 556 K and $k = 1.4 \times 10^{-5}$ dm¬≥ mol‚ĀĽ¬Ļ s‚ĀĽ¬Ļ at 629 K. Calculate $E_a$ and $A$.

Solution: $k_2/k_1 = 1.4\times10^{-5} / 3.5\times10^{-7} = 40$. $\ln(40) = 3.69$.
$1/T_1 - 1/T_2 = 1/556 - 1/629 = 1.799\times10^{-3} - 1.590\times10^{-3} = 2.09\times10^{-4}$.
$E_a = R \times \ln(40) / (2.09\times10^{-4}) = 8.31 \times 3.69 / 2.09\times10^{-4} = \mathbf{1.47 \times 10^5\text{ J mol}^{-1} = 147\text{ kJ mol}^{-1}}$.
For $A$: $A = k / e^{-E_a/RT}$. Using $T = 556$ K: $E_a/RT = 147000/(8.31\times556) = 31.8$. $e^{-31.8} = 1.56\times10^{-14}$. $A = 3.5\times10^{-7} / 1.56\times10^{-14} = \mathbf{2.2 \times 10^7\text{ dm}^3\text{ mol}^{-1}\text{ s}^{-1}}$.
Example 3 ‚ÄĒ Arrhenius Plot:

An Arrhenius plot of $\ln k$ vs. $1/T$ gives a straight line with slope = $-1.24 \times 10^4$ K and $y$-intercept = 26.8. Calculate $E_a$ and $A$.

Solution: Slope = $-E_a/R$, so $E_a = -R \times \text{slope} = -8.31 \times (-1.24\times10^4) = \mathbf{1.03 \times 10^5\text{ J mol}^{-1} = 103\text{ kJ mol}^{-1}}$.
$y$-intercept = $\ln A$, so $A = e^{26.8} = \mathbf{4.4 \times 10^{11}}$ (units match those of $k$).

‚ö†ÔłŹ Common wrong approach: Forgetting the negative sign. The slope is $-E_a/R$, so $E_a = -R \times \text{slope}$. If the slope is negative (as it always is for a normal Arrhenius plot), $E_a$ is positive. Also, using ¬įC instead of K ‚ÄĒ the Arrhenius equation requires absolute temperature.

‚ö†ÔłŹ Common Mistakes to Avoid

  1. "All collisions with $E \ge E_a$ react." No ‚ÄĒ orientation also matters. The pre-exponential factor $A$ accounts for the probability that a collision with sufficient energy also has the correct orientation. $A$ is typically much smaller than the total collision frequency.
  2. "The RDS is always the first step." No. The RDS can be any step. If it is the second or third step, prior steps establish equilibria that feed the RDS. The intermediate concentrations appear in the rate law and must be expressed in terms of reactants.
  3. "A mechanism consistent with the rate law is proven correct." Kinetics can only disprove a mechanism (if the predicted rate law does not match experiment). Many different mechanisms can predict the same rate law, so consistency is necessary but not sufficient.
  4. "$E_a$ is the difference in energy between reactants and products." No. That is $\Delta H$. $E_a$ is the energy barrier ‚ÄĒ the difference between the transition state and reactants. A catalyst lowers this barrier without changing $\Delta H$.
  5. "The Arrhenius equation predicts $k$ increases linearly with $T$." The relationship is exponential: $k \propto e^{-1/T}$. A small increase in $T$ produces a large increase in $k$, especially when $E_a$ is high.

6. End-of-Lesson Practice

Question 1 (Paper 1 style): A reaction has mechanism: Step 1: $\ce{A -> B}$ (slow); Step 2: $\ce{B + C -> D}$ (fast). The rate equation is:
A. rate = $k[\ce{A}][\ce{C}]$   B. rate = $k[\ce{A}]$   C. rate = $k[\ce{B}][\ce{C}]$   D. rate = $k[\ce{A}]^2$

Question 2: The rate constant of a reaction is $2.0 \times 10^{-4}$ s‚ĀĽ¬Ļ at 300 K and $8.0 \times 10^{-4}$ s‚ĀĽ¬Ļ at 310 K. Calculate $E_a$.

Question 3: Explain why most reactions approximately double in rate for every 10¬įC rise, using the Arrhenius equation.

Question 4: A proposed mechanism for $\ce{2A + B -> P}$ is: $\ce{A + B <=>[fast] X}$; $\ce{X + A -> P}$ (slow). Deduce the rate law and explain whether it is consistent with the stoichiometry.

Question 5: An Arrhenius plot gives slope = $-9.50 \times 10^3$ K. Find $E_a$ in kJ mol‚ĀĽ¬Ļ. If $\ln A = 24.0$, calculate $k$ at 350 K.

Question 6: For a reaction with $E_a = 75$ kJ mol‚ĀĽ¬Ļ and $A = 1.0 \times 10^{10}$ s‚ĀĽ¬Ļ, calculate $k$ at 298 K and 308 K. Hence find the factor by which the rate increases for this 10¬įC rise.

Answer 1: B. The RDS is Step 1 (slow), which is unimolecular (A ‚Üí B). The rate law for an elementary unimolecular step is rate = $k[\ce{A}]$. Step 2 is fast and does not affect the rate law.

Answer 2: $\ln(8.0\times10^{-4}/2.0\times10^{-4}) = \ln 4 = 1.386$. $1/T_1 - 1/T_2 = 1/300 - 1/310 = 1.075\times10^{-4}$. $E_a = 8.31 \times 1.386 / 1.075\times10^{-4} = \mathbf{1.07 \times 10^5\text{ J mol}^{-1} = 107\text{ kJ mol}^{-1}}$.

Answer 3: From $k = Ae^{-E_a/RT}$: the factor by which $k$ increases for a 10¬įC rise depends on $E_a$. For a typical $E_a \approx 50$‚Äď$60$ kJ mol‚ĀĽ¬Ļ and $T \approx 300$ K, $e^{-E_a/R} \times (1/T_1 - 1/T_2) \approx e^{0.7} \approx 2$. Thus $k$ roughly doubles. This is an approximation ‚ÄĒ for higher $E_a$, the factor is larger; for lower $E_a$, smaller.

Answer 4: RDS is step 2: rate = $k_2[\ce{X}][\ce{A}]$. From equilibrium: $K = [\ce{X}]/([\ce{A}][\ce{B}])$, so $[\ce{X}] = K[\ce{A}][\ce{B}]$. Substituting: rate = $k_2K[\ce{A}][\ce{B}][\ce{A}] = k[\ce{A}]^2[\ce{B}]$. This matches the stoichiometry (2A + B), but this is coincidental ‚ÄĒ the mechanism could easily give a different rate law. Always check the mechanism, not the stoichiometry.

Answer 5: $E_a = -R \times \text{slope} = -8.31 \times (-9.50\times10^3) = 7.89\times10^4$ J mol‚ĀĽ¬Ļ = 78.9 kJ mol‚ĀĽ¬Ļ. $\ln k = \ln A - E_a/RT = 24.0 - 78900/(8.31\times350) = 24.0 - 27.1 = -3.1$. $k = e^{-3.1} = \mathbf{4.5 \times 10^{-2}\text{ s}^{-1}}$.

Answer 6: At 298 K: $E_a/RT = 75000/(8.31\times298) = 30.3$, $e^{-30.3} = 6.9\times10^{-14}$, $k = 1.0\times10^{10} \times 6.9\times10^{-14} = \mathbf{6.9 \times 10^{-4}\text{ s}^{-1}}$. At 308 K: $E_a/RT = 75000/(8.31\times308) = 29.3$, $e^{-29.3} = 1.9\times10^{-13}$, $k = 1.0\times10^{10} \times 1.9\times10^{-13} = \mathbf{1.9 \times 10^{-3}\text{ s}^{-1}}$. Factor = $1.9\times10^{-3} / 6.9\times10^{-4} = \mathbf{2.75}$. The rate increases by a factor of about 2.8 for a 10¬įC rise ‚ÄĒ close to the "doubling" rule of thumb.

7. Key Terms

TermDefinition
Elementary stepA single molecular event (collision or dissociation) in a reaction mechanism
MolecularityThe number of particles participating in an elementary step (unimolecular = 1, bimolecular = 2)
Rate-determining step (RDS)The slowest elementary step; its rate law determines the overall rate equation
Reaction intermediateA species produced in one elementary step and consumed in a later step; does not appear in the overall equation
Arrhenius equation$k = A e^{-E_a/RT}$ ‚ÄĒ relates the rate constant to temperature and activation energy
Activation energy ($E_a$)The minimum energy required for a collision to result in reaction; the barrier height on a reaction profile
Pre-exponential factor ($A$)The frequency of collisions with correct orientation; the maximum possible $k$ as $T \to \infty$
Arrhenius plotA graph of $\ln k$ vs. $1/T$; slope = $-E_a/R$, $y$-intercept = $\ln A$

8. Exam Tips

Paper 2 (Structured): When asked "Is this mechanism consistent with the rate law?", you must: (1) identify the RDS, (2) write its rate law using intermediates, (3) use fast pre-equilibria to express intermediates in terms of reactants, (4) compare the final expression to the experimental rate law. State clearly: "The mechanism is consistent with the rate law but does not prove it ‚ÄĒ other mechanisms may also be consistent."
Arrhenius Calculations: Always convert $E_a$ to J mol‚ĀĽ¬Ļ (not kJ mol‚ĀĽ¬Ļ) when using $R = 8.31$ J K‚ĀĽ¬Ļ mol‚ĀĽ¬Ļ. If the question gives $E_a$ in kJ mol‚ĀĽ¬Ļ, multiply by 1000. The most common arithmetic error is unit mismatch between $E_a$ and $R$. Also, temperatures must be in kelvin ‚ÄĒ add 273 to Celsius values.
Paper 1 Trap: An Arrhenius question may ask "Which graph gives a straight line?" The answer is $\ln k$ vs. $1/T$ (not $\ln k$ vs. $T$, not $k$ vs. $T$, not $k$ vs. $1/T$). Only the logarithmic form linearises the exponential relationship. The slope is negative ‚ÄĒ as $T$ increases, $1/T$ decreases and $\ln k$ increases.
