Lesson 13: Transition Metals — Complex Ions, Colour, Crystal Field Theory

Syllabus reference: 13.1–13.2 (The Transition Metals, HL only)

Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to:

  • Define a transition metal by its electron configuration — a d-block element that forms at least one ion with an incomplete d-subshell.
  • Identify ligands as Lewis bases and explain how they bind to a central metal ion to form complex ions.
  • Determine the coordination number and shape of a complex from its formula (octahedral, tetrahedral, square planar).
  • Apply Crystal Field Theory to explain why the five d-orbitals split into two energy levels in an octahedral field.
  • Explain the origin of colour in transition metal complexes using d–d electronic transitions and the spectrochemical series.
  • Relate the colour observed to the wavelength absorbed and the complementary colour wheel.

1. Prior Knowledge Check

Question 1: Write the electron configuration of iron, $\ce{Fe}$ ($Z = 26$), using the noble-gas abbreviation.

▸ Show
$\ce{Fe}$: $\ce{[Ar] 4s^2 3d^6}$. Remember that the 4s orbital fills before 3d, but 4s electrons are lost first when forming cations.

Question 2: What is a Lewis base? Give an example of a molecule or ion that can act as a Lewis base.

▸ Show
A Lewis base is an electron-pair donor — it has a lone pair of electrons that it can donate to form a coordinate (dative covalent) bond. Examples: $\ce{NH3}$ (lone pair on N), $\ce{H2O\!:}$ (lone pairs on O), $\ce{Cl-}$ (lone pairs on Cl⁻).

Question 3: Why does an aqueous solution of $\ce{CuSO4}$ appear blue, while solid $\ce{CuSO4}$ (anhydrous) appears white?

▸ Show
Anhydrous $\ce{CuSO4}$ has no ligands bonded to $\ce{Cu^{2+}}$ — the d-orbitals are not split, so no d–d transitions are possible. When water ligands coordinate to form $\ce{[Cu(H2O)6]^{2+}}$, the d-orbitals split, and the complex absorbs orange/red light, transmitting blue — which we see. The colour is a ligand-field effect.

2. What Is a Transition Metal?

The IUPAC definition is precise: a transition metal is a d-block element that forms at least one stable ion with a partially filled d-subshell. This is the key — not simply being in the d-block.

This means zinc ($\ce{[Ar] 3d^{10} 4s^2}$) and scandium ($\ce{[Ar] 3d^1 4s^2}$) are not transition metals. Zinc only forms $\ce{Zn^{2+}}$ ($\ce{[Ar] 3d^{10}}$ — a full d-subshell), and scandium only forms $\ce{Sc^{3+}}$ ($\ce{[Ar]}$ — an empty d-subshell). No partially filled d-orbitals means no d–d transitions, and therefore no colour from d–d transitions — $\ce{Zn^{2+}}$ and $\ce{Sc^{3+}}$ compounds are typically white or colourless.

2.1 Characteristic Properties

Transition metals share a set of properties that arise directly from their partially filled d-orbitals:

PropertyExplanation
Variable oxidation statesSmall energy differences between 3d and 4s allow loss of different numbers of electrons (e.g. $\ce{Fe^{2+}}$, $\ce{Fe^{3+}}$).
Formation of coloured compoundsd–d transitions absorb visible light; the transmitted colour we see is complementary.
Formation of complex ionsEmpty 3d and 4s orbitals accept lone pairs from ligands to form coordinate bonds.
Catalytic activityVariable oxidation states allow transition metals to provide alternative reaction pathways with lower $E_a$ (homogeneous and heterogeneous catalysis).
Magnetic propertiesUnpaired d-electrons make many complexes paramagnetic.

Check-in: Chromium has electron configuration $\ce{[Ar] 3d^5 4s^1}$. Explain why copper, $\ce{[Ar] 3d^{10} 4s^1}$, IS a transition metal but zinc is not.

▸ Show
Copper forms $\ce{Cu^{2+}}$, which has configuration $\ce{[Ar] 3d^9}$ — a partially filled d-subshell. This satisfies the IUPAC definition. Zinc forms only $\ce{Zn^{2+}}$ ($\ce{[Ar] 3d^{10}}$) — a full d-subshell, so it fails the definition. The key is the ion, not the neutral atom.

3. Complex Ions — Ligands, Coordination, and Shapes

3.1 What Is a Complex Ion?

A complex ion consists of a central metal ion bonded to a set of ligands. A ligand is a Lewis base — an ion or molecule with at least one lone pair that it can donate to form a coordinate (dative covalent) bond with the metal ion. The metal ion acts as a Lewis acid (electron-pair acceptor).

$$\ce{Cu^{2+} + 6H2O\!: -> [Cu(H2O)6]^{2+}}$$

Each $\ce{H2O}$ molecule donates one lone pair from oxygen. The resulting complex carries a 2+ charge because the six neutral water molecules do not alter the charge of the central $\ce{Cu^{2+}}$ ion.

3.2 Coordination Number and Denticity

The coordination number (CN) is the number of coordinate bonds from ligands to the central metal ion. It is NOT the same as the number of ligands — a single polydentate ligand can form multiple bonds.

Check-in: Ethylenediamine (en), $\ce{H2NCH2CH2NH2}$, is a bidentate ligand. What is the coordination number of $\ce{Ni^{2+}}$ in $\ce{[Ni(en)3]^{2+}}$? What is the shape?

▸ Show
Each en ligand forms 2 coordinate bonds (one per N atom). With 3 en ligands: CN = 3 × 2 = 6. Shape: octahedral. The formula might look like "3 ligands" but the coordination number is 6.

4. Crystal Field Theory — d-Orbital Splitting

4.1 The Five d-Orbitals

In an isolated transition metal ion, all five 3d orbitals ($d_{xy}$, $d_{xz}$, $d_{yz}$, $d_{x^2-y^2}$, $d_{z^2}$) have the same energy — they are degenerate.

When ligands approach, this changes. Crystal Field Theory (CFT) models ligands as negative point charges (or dipoles) that repel the d-electrons. The key insight: the repulsion is not equal for all five d-orbitals.

4.2 Octahedral Splitting — The Key Diagram

In an octahedral complex, six ligands approach along the x, y, and z axes. Two orbitals — $d_{x^2-y^2}$ and $d_{z^2}$ — point directly along these axes, experiencing strong repulsion. The other three — $d_{xy}$, $d_{xz}$, $d_{yz}$ — point between the axes, experiencing less repulsion.

g dx²−y², dz² t2g dxy, dxz, dyz Δoct barycentre +0.6 Δoct −0.4 Δoct Lobes point ALONG axes → strong repulsion Lobes point BETWEEN axes → less repulsion + ligands
Figure 2: d-orbital splitting in an octahedral field. The five degenerate orbitals split into a lower-energy t2g set (3 orbitals) and a higher-energy eg set (2 orbitals). The splitting energy, Δoct, corresponds to the energy of visible-light photons — the origin of colour.

The energy gap, $\Delta_{\text{oct}}$ (or $10Dq$), is the crystal field splitting energy. Its magnitude depends on:

  1. The metal ion: Higher oxidation state → larger $\Delta$. 3d < 4d < 5d series (Δ increases down a group).
  2. The ligands: Stronger-field ligands produce larger splitting (see spectrochemical series).
  3. The geometry: $\Delta_{\text{tet}} \approx \frac{4}{9}\Delta_{\text{oct}}$ — tetrahedral splitting is smaller.

4.3 The Spectrochemical Series

Ligands can be ranked by the size of $\Delta$ they produce. This is the spectrochemical series:

Spectrochemical Series (increasing Δ):
$\ce{I- < Br- < Cl- < F- < OH- < H2O < NH3 < en < NO2- < CN- < CO}$

Weak-field (small Δ, high-spin)   →   Strong-field (large Δ, low-spin)

Check-in: $\ce{[CoF6]^{3-}}$ is green; $\ce{[Co(NH3)6]^{3+}}$ is yellow-orange. Which has the larger Δ? Use the spectrochemical series to justify your answer.

▸ Show
$\ce{NH3}$ is a stronger-field ligand than $\ce{F-}$ (it's further right in the spectrochemical series), so $\ce{[Co(NH3)6]^{3+}}$ has a larger Δ. A larger Δ means the absorbed photon has higher energy (shorter wavelength). $\ce{[Co(NH3)6]^{3+}}$ absorbs violet/blue (higher energy), transmitting yellow-orange. $\ce{[CoF6]^{3-}}$ absorbs lower-energy red light, transmitting green.

5. Colour of Transition Metal Complexes

5.1 The d–d Transition Mechanism

When white light passes through a complex ion, a photon of energy exactly equal to $\Delta_{\text{oct}}$ can be absorbed, promoting an electron from a $t_{2g}$ orbital to an $e_g$ orbital. This is a d–d transition. The wavelengths not absorbed are transmitted — and that is the colour we see.

$$\Delta E = h\nu = \frac{hc}{\lambda}$$

If $\Delta_{\text{oct}}$ is small (weak-field ligands), low-energy red photons are absorbed, and the complex appears green/blue. If $\Delta_{\text{oct}}$ is large (strong-field ligands), high-energy violet photons are absorbed, and the complex appears yellow/orange/red.

5.2 The Complementary Colour Wheel

Figure 3: The complementary colour wheel. A complex absorbs one colour and transmits the complementary colour directly opposite. If a complex absorbs orange light (~600 nm), it appears blue. The dashed line shows the red–green complementary pair.

5.3 When Is a Complex Colourless?

A complex appears colourless when d–d transitions are impossible. This happens in three cases:

  1. d⁰ configuration: No d-electrons to promote (e.g., $\ce{Sc^{3+}}$, $\ce{Ti^{4+}}$).
  2. d¹⁰ configuration: All d-orbitals are full; there is no empty orbital to receive an electron (e.g., $\ce{Zn^{2+}}$, $\ce{Cu+}$).
  3. Δ falls outside the visible range: If Δ is very small (far-IR absorption) or very large (UV absorption), no visible light is absorbed. This is rare for first-row transition metals with common ligands.

Check-in: $\ce{[Cu(NH3)4(H2O)2]^{2+}}$ appears deep blue. Explain why an aqueous solution of $\ce{ZnSO4}$ is colourless, even though both contain d-block metal ions.

▸ Show
$\ce{Cu^{2+}}$ is $\ce{[Ar] 3d^9}$ — it has a hole in the d-subshell, allowing d–d transitions. $\ce{Zn^{2+}}$ is $\ce{[Ar] 3d^{10}}$ — all five d-orbitals are fully occupied. There is no empty orbital for an electron to be promoted into, so no d–d transition is possible. Any absorbed UV photon would not produce visible colour.

6. Worked Examples

Example 1 — Determining the Formula of a Complex Ion:

When excess aqueous ammonia is added to a solution of $\ce{NiCl2}$, a violet-blue complex forms with the formula $\ce{[Ni(NH3)6]Cl2}$. (a) What is the oxidation state of nickel? (b) What is the coordination number? (c) How many ions are present per formula unit when dissolved in water?

Answer:
(a) The two $\ce{Cl-}$ counter-ions outside the brackets carry a total charge of 2−. For the compound to be neutral, the complex cation must be 2+: $\ce{[Ni(NH3)6]^{2+}}$. Since $\ce{NH3}$ is neutral, Ni must be +2.
(b) Six $\ce{NH3}$ ligands, each monodentate → CN = 6.
(c) In water: $\ce{[Ni(NH3)6]Cl2 -> [Ni(NH3)6]^{2+} + 2Cl-}$. Total ions = 3 (one complex cation + two chloride anions).

⚠ Common trap: Students write $\ce{Ni^{2+}}$ and 6 $\ce{NH3}$ as separate ions. The $\ce{NH3}$ molecules are coordinated to the metal — they do not dissociate. Only the counter-ions outside the brackets dissociate.
Example 2 — Predicting Colour Using the Spectrochemical Series:

$\ce{[Cr(H2O)6]^{3+}}$ appears violet. Predict whether $\ce{[Cr(NH3)6]^{3+}}$ absorbs light of higher or lower energy, and state the colour you expect for this complex. ($\ce{NH3}$ is a stronger-field ligand than $\ce{H2O}$.)

Answer:
$\ce{NH3}$ produces a larger Δ than $\ce{H2O}$. A larger Δ means the absorbed photon has higher energy ($\Delta E = hc/\lambda$), so $\ce{[Cr(NH3)6]^{3+}}$ absorbs shorter-wavelength light (shifted towards violet/UV).

$\ce{[Cr(H2O)6]^{3+}}$ absorbs yellow (~570 nm) → appears violet (complementary).
$\ce{[Cr(NH3)6]^{3+}}$ absorbs violet/blue (~420 nm) → appears yellow-orange (complementary).

This is the reason complexes of the same metal ion with different ligands have different colours. The ligand determines Δ, which determines the absorbed wavelength, which determines the observed colour.
Example 3 — Relating Absorption Wavelength to Δ:

$\ce{[Ti(H2O)6]^{3+}}$ ($d^1$) absorbs light maximally at $\lambda = 510$ nm. Calculate the crystal field splitting energy Δ in kJ mol⁻¹.
($h = 6.63 \times 10^{-34}$ J s, $c = 3.00 \times 10^8$ m s⁻¹, $N_A = 6.02 \times 10^{23}$ mol⁻¹)

Step 1: $\Delta E = hc/\lambda = (6.63\times10^{-34} \times 3.00\times10^8) / (510 \times 10^{-9}) = 3.90 \times 10^{-19}$ J per ion.
Step 2: Per mole: $\Delta E_{\text{mol}} = 3.90\times10^{-19} \times 6.02\times10^{23} = 2.35 \times 10^5$ J mol⁻¹ = 235 kJ mol⁻¹.

This energy is comparable to the strength of a chemical bond — which is why ligand substitution can dramatically alter a complex's properties.

⚠️ Common Mistakes to Avoid

  1. Confusing zinc and copper as transition metals. Zinc is NOT a transition metal ($\ce{Zn^{2+}}$ is d¹⁰); copper IS ($\ce{Cu^{2+}}$ is d⁹). Check the ion, not the neutral atom. IB examiners love this distinction.
  2. Mixing up coordination number and number of ligands. With polydentate ligands like en or EDTA, the CN is larger than the ligand count. $\ce{[Ni(en)3]^{2+}}$ has 3 ligands but CN = 6.
  3. Thinking the complex ion dissociates completely in water. Only the counter-ions outside the coordination sphere dissociate. The ligands inside the brackets stay bound to the metal — they do not become free ions.
  4. Confusing the colour absorbed with the colour observed. The complex transmits the complementary colour of the light absorbed. If red is absorbed (~650 nm), the complex appears green. Always use the colour wheel.
  5. Forgetting that tetrahedral splitting is smaller than octahedral. $\Delta_{\text{tet}} \approx \frac{4}{9}\Delta_{\text{oct}}$ because only 4 ligands approach (vs. 6) and they do not point directly at the d-orbitals. Tetrahedral complexes tend to absorb lower-energy light and often appear more deeply coloured.
  6. Assuming all d-block compounds are coloured. d⁰ and d¹⁰ configurations produce colourless compounds. $\ce{CuSO4}$ is blue while $\ce{ZnSO4}$ is colourless — despite both being d-block sulfates.

Question 1 (Paper 1 style): Which of the following is NOT a transition metal according to the IUPAC definition?
A. $\ce{Fe}$   B. $\ce{Cu}$   C. $\ce{Zn}$   D. $\ce{Mn}$

Question 2: Determine the oxidation state of the metal and the coordination number in each complex:
(a) $\ce{[Fe(CN)6]^{4-}}$   (b) $\ce{[Co(NH3)5Cl]^{2+}}$   (c) $\ce{[Cr(en)3]^{3+}}$

Question 3: $\ce{[Fe(H2O)6]^{2+}}$ is pale green, while $\ce{[Fe(CN)6]^{4-}}$ is deep yellow. Use Crystal Field Theory and the spectrochemical series to explain this difference clearly.

Question 4: The complex $\ce{[Ni(H2O)6]^{2+}}$ is green. It absorbs light at approximately 680 nm (red region). (a) Explain, using CFT, why this absorption occurs. (b) $\ce{[Ni(NH3)6]^{2+}}$ is blue. State and explain whether $\ce{NH3}$ produces a larger or smaller Δ than $\ce{H2O}$.

Question 5: The complex $\ce{[Cu(H2O)6]^{2+}}$ absorbs light at 800 nm. Calculate the crystal field splitting energy Δ in kJ mol⁻¹. ($h = 6.63 \times 10^{-34}$ J s, $c = 3.00 \times 10^8$ m s⁻¹, $L = 6.02 \times 10^{23}$ mol⁻¹)

Question 6 (IB-style multi-step): Chromium(III) forms a complex with water, $\ce{[Cr(H2O)6]^{3+}}$, which is violet.
(a) Write the electron configuration of $\ce{Cr^{3+}}$ using the noble-gas abbreviation.
(b) In an octahedral field, how many electrons occupy the $t_{2g}$ and $e_g$ sets for this ion? Sketch the orbital filling.
(c) The complex absorbs light in the yellow region (570 nm). Calculate the energy of this transition in kJ mol⁻¹.
(d) When $\ce{NH3}$ replaces $\ce{H2O}$, the complex turns yellow-orange. Use the spectrochemical series to explain this colour change.

Answer 1: C. Zinc ($\ce{Zn}$) forms only $\ce{Zn^{2+}}$, which has a full d¹⁰ configuration, so it does not meet the IUPAC definition of a transition metal. Fe, Cu, and Mn all form ions with incomplete d-subshells.

Answer 2:
(a) $\ce{[Fe(CN)6]^{4-}}$: 6 × $\ce{CN-}$ (charge −1 each) = −6 total from ligands. Complex has −4 overall, so Fe must be +2 (since +2 − 6 = −4). CN = 6, Fe = +2.
(b) $\ce{[Co(NH3)5Cl]^{2+}}$: 5 × $\ce{NH3}$ (0) + 1 × $\ce{Cl-}$ (−1) = −1 total from ligands. Overall +2, so Co = +3 (since +3 − 1 = +2). CN = 6 (5 × monodentate $\ce{NH3}$ + 1 × monodentate $\ce{Cl-}$).
(c) $\ce{[Cr(en)3]^{3+}}$: 3 × en (neutral) = 0 from ligands. Overall +3, so Cr = +3. CN = 3 × 2 = 6 (each en is bidentate).

Answer 3: $\ce{CN-}$ is a much stronger-field ligand than $\ce{H2O}$ (farther right on the spectrochemical series). $\ce{[Fe(CN)6]^{4-}}$ has a larger Δ, absorbing higher-energy violet/blue photons → transmits yellow. $\ce{[Fe(H2O)6]^{2+}}$ has a smaller Δ, absorbing lower-energy red photons → transmits green. Additionally, Fe is +2 in both, but the stronger field from $\ce{CN-}$ can lead to low-spin pairing, further affecting the electronic structure. The key is: stronger ligand → larger Δ → higher-energy absorption → shorter-wavelength absorbed.

Answer 4:
(a) $\ce{Ni^{2+}}$ is d⁸. In an octahedral field, the d-orbitals split into $t_{2g}$ (lower) and $e_g$ (higher). A photon of red light (680 nm, ~1.82 eV) has energy equal to Δ, promoting an electron from a $t_{2g}$ to an $e_g$ orbital. Red light is absorbed; the complementary colour green is transmitted.
(b) $\ce{[Ni(NH3)6]^{2+}}$ is blue, meaning it absorbs orange light (~600 nm). Since orange has shorter wavelength (higher energy) than red, $\ce{NH3}$ must produce a larger Δ than $\ce{H2O}$. This matches the spectrochemical series: $\ce{NH3}$ is a stronger-field ligand than $\ce{H2O}$.

Answer 5: $\Delta E = hc/\lambda = (6.63\times10^{-34} \times 3.00\times10^8) / (800 \times 10^{-9}) = 2.49 \times 10^{-19}$ J per ion. Per mole: $2.49\times10^{-19} \times 6.02\times10^{23} = 1.50 \times 10^5$ J mol⁻¹ = 150 kJ mol⁻¹.

Answer 6:
(a) $\ce{Cr^{3+}}$: Cr is $\ce{[Ar] 3d^5 4s^1}$. Remove 3 electrons (4s first, then two from 3d): $\ce{Cr^{3+}}$ = $\ce{[Ar] 3d^3}$.
(b) All three d-electrons occupy the lower-energy $t_{2g}$ set: $t_{2g}^3\ e_g^0$. By Hund's rule, each $t_{2g}$ orbital gets one electron with parallel spins.
(c) $\Delta E = hc/\lambda = (6.63\times10^{-34} \times 3.00\times10^8) / (570 \times 10^{-9}) = 3.49 \times 10^{-19}$ J. Per mole: $3.49\times10^{-19} \times 6.02\times10^{23} = 2.10 \times 10^5$ J mol⁻¹ = 210 kJ mol⁻¹.
(d) $\ce{NH3}$ is a stronger-field ligand than $\ce{H2O}$, so Δ increases. The complex absorbs higher-energy (shorter-wavelength) light — shifting from yellow absorption (~570 nm) towards violet/blue absorption. With violet/blue absorbed, the complementary colour yellow-orange is transmitted. Hence the colour change from violet to yellow-orange.

Coordination number (CN)The number of coordinate bonds from ligands to the central metal ion (NOT simply the number of ligands)

9. Exam Tips

Paper 1 (Multiple Choice): The "zinc is NOT a transition metal" question appears in some form almost every year. Memorise: Zn is d¹⁰ in its only ion ($\ce{Zn^{2+}}$), so it fails the definition. Also, Sc ($\ce{Sc^{3+}}$ is d⁰) is not a transition metal. The definition hinges on the ion having an incomplete d-subshell.
Paper 2 (Structured): When asked to explain the colour of a complex, use the full chain of reasoning: (1) state the d-electron configuration, (2) name the geometry and state that the d-orbitals split, (3) identify which light is absorbed (Δ = photon energy), (4) state the complementary colour transmitted. Four clear sentences earn full marks. A vague "the complex is blue because of d-orbitals" gets almost nothing.
Paper 2 — Oxidation State Calculations: When given a complex like $\ce{[Co(NH3)5Cl]Cl2}$, remember: ligands inside the brackets plus the metal must balance the overall charge of the complex ion. Counter-ions outside the brackets balance the complex ion's charge. Write an equation: $x + 5(0) + (-1) = +2$ → $x = +3$ for cobalt. Always check your answer — the oxidation state should be a common one for that metal (Co is often +2 or +3).
Paper 2/3 — CFT Diagrams: You may be asked to sketch the d-orbital splitting diagram for an octahedral complex. Label the axes (Energy), show the degenerate set splitting into $t_{2g}$ (lower, three orbitals) and $e_g$ (higher, two orbitals), label $\Delta_{\text{oct}}$, and populate the orbitals with electrons for the given dⁿ configuration. Marks are awarded for correct relative energies, correct labels, and correct electron filling (follow Hund's rule).
