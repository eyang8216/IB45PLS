# Lesson 36: Organic Chemistry Fundamentals — Functional Groups and IUPAC Nomenclature

## What You'll Learn

This lesson is your introduction to organic chemistry — the chemistry of carbon compounds. You will learn why carbon is special and can form millions of different compounds. You will learn what a functional group is and how to recognise common ones. Most importantly, you will learn the IUPAC system — the international rules for naming organic molecules. By the end of this lesson, you will be able to look at a molecular formula or a structural diagram and give the molecule its correct systematic name.

---

## 1. What Is Organic Chemistry?

Organic chemistry is the branch of chemistry that studies compounds containing the element carbon. The name "organic" comes from history — scientists once believed that these compounds could only be made by living organisms. We now know that is not true, but the name has stayed. Organic chemistry today is about the structure, properties, reactions, and synthesis of carbon-based molecules, whether they come from nature or are made in a laboratory.

**Why carbon is special.** Carbon atoms have the unique ability to form strong covalent bonds with other carbon atoms. A covalent bond is a bond in which two atoms share a pair of electrons. Carbon can form long chains of carbon atoms linked together — a property called **catenation**. Carbon can also form rings, branched structures, and bonds with many other elements like hydrogen, oxygen, nitrogen, and the halogens (fluorine, chlorine, bromine, iodine).

Carbon has 4 electrons in its outer shell (its valence shell). Because of this, a carbon atom always forms 4 covalent bonds in its stable molecules. It can do this by forming four single bonds, or two single bonds and one double bond, or one single bond and one triple bond, or two double bonds. The ability to form double and triple bonds (where two or three pairs of electrons are shared) adds to the diversity of organic molecules.

**Hydrocarbons** are the simplest organic compounds — they contain only carbon and hydrogen. All other organic compounds can be thought of as hydrocarbons in which one or more hydrogen atoms have been replaced by other atoms or groups of atoms.

## 2. Homologous Series — Families of Organic Compounds

A **homologous series** is a family of organic compounds that share the same general features:
- All members of the series have the **same functional group** (explained below).
- Each member differs from the next by one **CH₂ group** — that is, by one carbon atom and two hydrogen atoms.
- The series has a **general molecular formula** that describes any member.
- All members have **similar chemical properties** because they have the same functional group.
- Physical properties (such as boiling point and melting point) show a **gradual trend** as the chain gets longer.

Think of a homologous series like a ladder. Each rung is one CH₂ unit higher than the previous one. The chemistry is similar at every rung because the same functional group is present.

## 3. Functional Groups — The Reactive Parts of Molecules

A **functional group** is a specific atom or group of atoms within a molecule that gives the molecule its characteristic chemical reactions. The rest of the molecule (the hydrocarbon skeleton) affects physical properties but does not usually participate directly in reactions.

When you look at an organic molecule, the first thing to identify is the functional group because it tells you what kind of reactions the molecule can do. Two molecules with the same functional group but different chain lengths will undergo the same kinds of reactions.

Here is a table of the common functional groups you need to know for IB Chemistry HL. Study each one carefully. The "general formula" column shows the formula for the homologous series if there is only one of that functional group in an otherwise straight-chain molecule.

| Homologous Series | Functional Group | Identifying Feature | Suffix (name ending) | General Formula |
|-------------------|------------------|---------------------|----------------------|-----------------|
| Alkanes | None (C–C and C–H only) | All single bonds | **−ane** | CₙH₂ₙ₊₂ |
| Alkenes | C=C (carbon-carbon double bond) | A double bond between two carbons | **−ene** | CₙH₂ₙ |
| Alkynes | C≡C (carbon-carbon triple bond) | A triple bond between two carbons | **−yne** | CₙH₂ₙ₋₂ |
| Alcohols | −OH (hydroxyl group) | An –OH group bonded to a carbon | **−ol** | CₙH₂ₙ₊₁OH |
| Aldehydes | −CHO (carbonyl group at chain end) | C=O at the end of the chain; the carbonyl carbon is also bonded to H | **−al** | CₙH₂ₙO |
| Ketones | >C=O (carbonyl group not at end) | C=O with the carbon bonded to two other carbons | **−one** | CₙH₂ₙO |
| Carboxylic acids | −COOH (carboxyl group) | C=O and –OH on the same terminal carbon | **−oic acid** | CₙH₂ₙO₂ |
| Esters | −COO− | C=O and –O– linking two carbon chains | **−oate** (alkyl alkanoate) | CₙH₂ₙO₂ |
| Amines | −NH₂ (amino group) | A nitrogen atom bonded to carbon(s) and hydrogen(s) | **−amine** | CₙH₂ₙ₊₁NH₂ |
| Amides | −CONH₂ | C=O bonded to –NH₂ | **−amide** | CₙH₂ₙ₊₁NO |
| Halogenoalkanes | −X (where X = F, Cl, Br, I) | A halogen atom bonded to a carbon | Prefix: **fluoro−, chloro−, bromo−, iodo−** | CₙH₂ₙ₊₁X |
| Nitriles | −C≡N (cyano group) | A carbon-nitrogen triple bond | **−nitrile** | CₙH₂ₙ₋₁N |

## 4. The IUPAC Naming System — Rules for Giving Every Molecule a Unique Name

**IUPAC** stands for the International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry. The IUPAC naming system (also called systematic nomenclature) is a set of rules that assigns a unique name to every possible organic molecule. If you follow the rules correctly, anyone anywhere in the world can draw the molecule from just its name.

Here are the steps, explained one at a time. You must follow them in this order.

**Step 1: Find the longest continuous chain of carbon atoms.** This chain is called the **parent chain**. The number of carbon atoms in the parent chain determines the root of the name:
- 1 carbon: **meth−**
- 2 carbons: **eth−**
- 3 carbons: **prop−**
- 4 carbons: **but−**
- 5 carbons: **pent−**
- 6 carbons: **hex−**
- 7 carbons: **hept−**
- 8 carbons: **oct−**
- 9 carbons: **non−**
- 10 carbons: **dec−**

**Step 2: Identify the principal functional group.** Among all the functional groups present, one is the "principal" one — it determines the suffix (ending) of the name. The priority order (highest priority first) is: carboxylic acid > ester > amide > nitrile > aldehyde > ketone > alcohol > amine > alkene > alkyne > alkane > halogenoalkane. The highest-priority group gets the suffix; lower-priority groups are named as prefixes.

**Step 3: Number the carbon atoms in the parent chain.** Start numbering from the end of the chain that gives the **principal functional group the lowest possible number**. If there is no functional group (alkane), number from the end that gives the first substituent the lowest number.

**Step 4: Identify and name substituents.** A **substituent** is any atom or group attached to the parent chain that is not part of the principal functional group. Common substituents include:
- Alkyl groups (branches from the main chain): methyl (−CH₃), ethyl (−C₂H₅), propyl (−C₃H₇)
- Halogens: fluoro (−F), chloro (−Cl), bromo (−Br), iodo (−I)
- Lower-priority functional groups named as prefixes: hydroxy (−OH when not the principal group), amino (−NH₂ when not principal)

**Step 5: Write the name as one word (with hyphens and commas).** The format is: (substituent positions)-(substituent names)(parent chain)(functional group suffix). If there are multiple identical substituents, use di− (2), tri− (3), tetra− (4). List substituents in alphabetical order (ignoring di−, tri− when alphabetising). Use commas between numbers and hyphens between numbers and words.

## 5. Worked Examples — Naming Molecules Step by Step

<div class="worked">
<strong>Worked Example 1: Name the molecule with the structural formula CH₃CH₂CH₂COOH.</strong>

**To solve this, we need to:** follow the IUPAC steps.

Step 1: Count the longest carbon chain that includes the functional group. The chain is CH₃−CH₂−CH₂−COOH. That is 4 carbon atoms. Root: **but−**.

Step 2: The functional group is −COOH, which is a carboxylic acid. This is the highest priority group. Suffix: **−oic acid**. Replace the "e" of butane: **butanoic acid**.

Step 3: Numbering: The −COOH carbon is always number 1 in carboxylic acids. No need to write "1" in the name.

Answer: **butanoic acid**.

There are no substituents, so the name is simply the root plus the suffix.
</div>

<div class="worked">
<strong>Worked Example 2: Name the molecule CH₃CH(Cl)CH₃.</strong>

**To solve this, we need to:** identify the parent chain and substituents.

Step 1: The longest carbon chain: CH₃−CH−CH₃. That is 3 carbon atoms. Root: **prop−**.

Step 2: There are only single bonds and a chlorine atom. No multiple bonds, no oxygen or nitrogen groups. The molecule is an alkane with a halogen substituent. The principal group is the alkane. Suffix: **−ane**.

Step 3: Numbering: Number from the end that gives the chlorine the lowest number. If we number from the left: carbon 1 is CH₃, carbon 2 is CH(Cl), carbon 3 is CH₃. Chlorine is at position 2. If we number from the right, chlorine is also at position 2. So chlorine is at C-2.

Step 4: The substituent is a chlorine atom. As a prefix: **chloro−**. Its position is 2.

Step 5: Put it together: **2-chloropropane**.

Notice: numbers and words are separated by hyphens. No space between "chloro" and "propane."
</div>

<div class="worked">
<strong>Worked Example 3: Name the molecule CH₃CH=CHCH₃.</strong>

Step 1: The longest chain with the double bond: 4 carbon atoms. Root: **but−**.

Step 2: The functional group is the C=C double bond — an alkene. Suffix: **−ene**.

Step 3: Numbering: We number to give the double bond the lowest possible number. The double bond starts at C-2 (whether we number from left or right). So the double bond is between C-2 and C-3.

Step 4: No substituents.

Step 5: Put it together: **but−2−ene**. More commonly written as **but-2-ene**.

The "2" indicates the position of the double bond — specifically, it starts at carbon 2.
</div>

<div class="worked">
<strong>Worked Example 4: Name the molecule CH₃CH₂COCH₃.</strong>

Step 1: Longest chain including the C=O group: CH₃−CH₂−CO−CH₃. That is 4 carbon atoms. Root: **but−**.

Step 2: The functional group is >C=O (carbonyl) in the middle of the chain — a ketone. Suffix: **−one**.

Step 3: Numbering: Number from the end that gives the C=O the lowest number. From the left: C-1 is CH₃, C-2 is CH₂, C-3 is CO, C-4 is CH₃. C=O is at C-3. From the right: C=O is at C-2. So number from the right, giving C=O position 2.

Step 4: No substituents.

Step 5: Put it together: **butan−2−one**. More commonly: **butan-2-one**.

Note the "an" between "but" and "one" — this comes from the alkane name "butane." The "e" of "ane" is dropped before adding "one": butane → butanone.
</div>

<div class="worked">
<strong>Worked Example 5: Name the molecule CH₃CH(OH)CH₂CH₃.</strong>

Step 1: Longest chain: 4 carbon atoms. Root: **but−**.

Step 2: Functional group: −OH (hydroxyl), which is an alcohol. Suffix: **−ol**.

Step 3: Numbering: Number from the end that gives the −OH the lowest number. From the left: −OH is at C-2. From the right: −OH is at C-3. So number from the left.

Step 4: No other substituents.

Step 5: Put it together: **butan-2-ol**. The "e" is kept before "ol": butane → butanol, and the number is inserted.
</div>

<div class="worked">
<strong>Worked Example 6: Name the molecule CH₃CH(CH₃)CH₂CH₃.</strong>

Step 1: Longest chain: The backbone is CH₃−CH−CH₂−CH₃. That is 4 carbons. Root: **but−**.

Step 2: Only single bonds — alkane. Suffix: **−ane**.

Step 3: Numbering: Number from the end closer to the branch. The branch (methyl group) is on C-2 from the left, or C-3 from the right. So number from the left. The methyl group is at position 2.

Step 4: Substituent: a methyl group (−CH₃) at position 2. Prefix: **methyl−**.

Step 5: **2-methylbutane**.
</div>

<div class="worked">
<strong>Worked Example 7: Name the ester CH₃CH₂COOCH₃.</strong>

An ester has the general structure R−COO−R', where R is the part from the carboxylic acid and R' is the part from the alcohol. To name an ester, we name the alkyl group from the alcohol first, then the acid part with the suffix **−oate**.

The molecule: CH₃CH₂−COO−CH₃.

The alcohol part (the −OR' group): −O−CH₃ comes from methanol. The alkyl group is **methyl**.

The acid part (the RCO− group): CH₃CH₂−CO− comes from propanoic acid (CH₃CH₂COOH). As an ester suffix, propanoic acid becomes **propanoate**.

Put together: **methyl propanoate**.

(Note: in the full name, the two words are separated by a space — this is an exception to the one-word rule.)
</div>

<div class="worked">
<strong>Worked Example 8: Name the molecule CH₃CHBrCH₂CH₃.</strong>

Step 1: Longest chain: 4 carbon atoms. Root: **but−**.

Step 2: Only single bonds — alkane. Suffix: **−ane**.

Step 3: Numbering: Number to give Br the lowest number. From the left: Br at C-2. From the right: Br at C-3. Number from the left.

Step 4: Substituent: bromine at position 2. Prefix: **bromo−**.

Step 5: **2-bromobutane**.
</div>

## Practice Problems

**Problem 1:** Write the general molecular formula for each of the following homologous series:
(a) Alkenes.
(b) Alcohols (with one −OH group, straight chain).
(c) Carboxylic acids (with one −COOH group, straight chain).

**Problem 2:** For each of the following compounds, identify the functional group present and state which homologous series the compound belongs to.
(a) CH₃CH₂CHO
(b) CH₃COCH₂CH₃
(c) CH₃CH₂NH₂
(d) CH₃CN

**Problem 3:** Give the systematic IUPAC name for each of the following compounds. Show your reasoning by stating the length of the parent chain, the principal functional group, the numbering, and any substituents.
(a) CH₃CH₂CH₂CH₃
(b) CH₃CH₂CH₂OH
(c) CH₃CH=CH₂
(d) CH₃CHClCH₂CH₃
(e) CH₃CH₂COOH

**Problem 4:** Draw the structural formula (showing all atoms) for each of the following compounds, as indicated by the IUPAC name:
(a) Pentan-3-one
(b) 2-methylpropan-1-ol
(c) Ethyl ethanoate
(d) 1,2-dibromoethane

**Problem 5 (IB-style):** Two organic compounds, A and B, both have the molecular formula C₄H₁₀O.
- Compound A does not react when heated with acidified potassium dichromate(VI) solution (K₂Cr₂O₇ dissolved in dilute sulfuric acid). The solution remains orange.
- Compound B does react with acidified potassium dichromate(VI): the orange solution turns green, and a new compound C with the formula C₄H₈O is formed. Compound C gives a positive result with Tollens' reagent (a silver mirror forms).
(a) Explain what the reaction (or lack of reaction) with acidified potassium dichromate(VI) tells you about the type of alcohol present in compounds A and B. Classify each as primary (1°), secondary (2°), or tertiary (3°), or state if it is not an alcohol at all.
(b) Compound C gives a positive Tollens' test. What functional group does this indicate is present in compound C? Based on this, what type of alcohol must compound B be?
(c) Suggest one possible structural formula and IUPAC name for compound A, one for compound B, and one for compound C. Explain how you arrived at each answer.
(d) Explain, in terms of bonding, why compound A does not react with acidified dichromate(VI), whereas compound B does.

## Answers

**Answer 1.**

(a) Alkenes (one double bond, straight chain): CₙH₂ₙ. Each double bond reduces the hydrogen count by 2 compared to the alkane with the same number of carbons.

(b) Alcohols (one −OH, straight chain): CₙH₂ₙ₊₁OH. The general formula can also be written as CₙH₂ₙ₊₂O, but CₙH₂ₙ₊₁OH makes the functional group explicit.

(c) Carboxylic acids (one −COOH, straight chain): CₙH₂ₙO₂. For example, methanoic acid is HCOOH (n=1 gives CH₂O₂, which matches: 1 carbon, 2 hydrogens, 2 oxygens).

**Answer 2.**

(a) CH₃CH₂CHO: The group at the end is −CHO (carbonyl with H attached). This is an **aldehyde** group. The compound belongs to the **aldehyde** homologous series.

(b) CH₃COCH₂CH₃: The C=O group has carbon atoms on both sides (CH₃− on one side, −CH₂CH₃ on the other). This is a **ketone** group. The compound belongs to the **ketone** homologous series. Its IUPAC name is butanone.

(c) CH₃CH₂NH₂: The −NH₂ group is an **amine** (amino group). The compound belongs to the **amine** homologous series. Its IUPAC name is ethylamine or aminoethane.

(d) CH₃CN: The −C≡N group is a **nitrile** (cyano group). The compound belongs to the **nitrile** homologous series. Its IUPAC name is ethanenitrile.

**Answer 3.**

(a) CH₃CH₂CH₂CH₃: 4 carbon chain, all single bonds → alkane. Root: **but−**, suffix: **−ane**. Name: **butane**.

(b) CH₃CH₂CH₂OH: 3 carbon chain. Functional group: −OH (alcohol). Root: **prop−**, suffix: **−ol**. Numbering: −OH is at C-1 (numbering from the −OH end). Name: **propan-1-ol**.

(c) CH₃CH=CH₂: 3 carbon chain. Functional group: C=C (alkene). Root: **prop−**, suffix: **−ene**. Numbering: double bond starts at C-1. Name: **propene** (no number needed since the double bond can only be at C-1 in a 3-carbon chain).

(d) CH₃CHClCH₂CH₃: 4 carbon chain. All single bonds → alkane. Root: **but−**, suffix: **−ane**. Numbering: Cl is at C-2 (number from end closest to Cl). Substituent: chloro at C-2. Name: **2-chlorobutane**.

(e) CH₃CH₂COOH: 3 carbon chain including the −COOH carbon. Functional group: −COOH (carboxylic acid). Root: **prop−**, suffix: **−oic acid**. The −COOH carbon is automatically number 1. Name: **propanoic acid**.

**Answer 4.**

(a) Pentan-3-one: "Pent" = 5 carbons. "3-one" = ketone (C=O) at C-3. Structure: CH₃CH₂−CO−CH₂CH₃.

(b) 2-methylpropan-1-ol: "Prop" = 3 carbon parent chain. "1-ol" = −OH at C-1. "2-methyl" = methyl group at C-2. Structure: (CH₃)₂CH−CH₂OH. The central carbon (C-2) is bonded to H, CH₃, CH₃, and CH₂OH.

(c) Ethyl ethanoate: Ester. "Ethyl" = alkyl group from ethanol (CH₃CH₂−). "Ethanoate" = from ethanoic acid (CH₃COO−). Structure: CH₃COOCH₂CH₃.

(d) 1,2-dibromoethane: "Eth" = 2 carbon chain. "1,2-dibromo" = bromine atoms at C-1 and C-2. Structure: CH₂Br−CH₂Br.

**Answer 5.**

(a) Acidified potassium dichromate(VI) is an oxidising agent. In organic chemistry, it can oxidise **primary (1°) alcohols** (first to aldehydes and then to carboxylic acids) and **secondary (2°) alcohols** (to ketones). It cannot oxidise **tertiary (3°) alcohols**. Compound A does not react, which suggests A is a tertiary alcohol. Compound B does react, which suggests B is either a primary or a secondary alcohol.

(b) A positive Tollens' test (silver mirror) indicates the presence of an **aldehyde**. Aldehydes are formed by oxidation of primary alcohols. Therefore, compound B must be a **primary (1°) alcohol**. Compound C is the aldehyde produced by oxidation of B.

(c) Possible answers (there are several isomers of C₄H₁₀O):

Compound A (tertiary alcohol, C₄H₁₀O): **2-methylpropan-2-ol**, also written as (CH₃)₃COH. The carbon bearing the −OH is attached to three other carbon atoms (three methyl groups) and has no hydrogen atom attached to it.

Compound B (primary alcohol, C₄H₁₀O): **butan-1-ol**, CH₃CH₂CH₂CH₂OH. This is a straight-chain primary alcohol.

Compound C (aldehyde, C₄H₈O): **butanal**, CH₃CH₂CH₂CHO. This is formed by oxidation of butan-1-ol at the −CH₂OH end.

(d) Oxidation of alcohols by dichromate(VI) involves the removal of two hydrogen atoms: one from the −OH group and one from the carbon atom that is bonded to the −OH group. For this to happen, that carbon atom must have at least one hydrogen atom attached to it. In a tertiary alcohol such as 2-methylpropan-2-ol, the carbon bearing the −OH group is bonded to three other carbon atoms and has no C−H bond at that position. Since there is no hydrogen atom to remove from that carbon, oxidation cannot occur. The dichromate remains orange because no Cr(VI) is reduced to Cr(III).
