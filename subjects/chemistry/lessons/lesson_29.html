Lesson 29: Buffer Solutions — Mechanism & Calculations

Syllabus reference: 18.2–18.3 (HL) — Buffer Solutions, Henderson–Hasselbalch Equation

Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to:

  • Define a buffer solution and explain how it resists pH changes using equilibrium principles.
  • Identify whether a given mixture will form a buffer (weak acid + conjugate base, or partial neutralisation).
  • Calculate the pH of a buffer solution using the Henderson–Hasselbalch equation.
  • Calculate the pH change when small amounts of strong acid or base are added to a buffer.
  • Describe how to prepare a buffer of a desired pH in the laboratory.

1. Prior Knowledge Check

Question 1: What species must be present in a buffer solution?

▸ Show
A buffer must contain a weak acid and its conjugate base (or a weak base and its conjugate acid) in significant concentrations. Both species must be present — one to neutralise added acid, the other to neutralise added base.

Question 2: Write the $K_a$ expression for a generic weak acid $\ce{HA}$ and rearrange it to solve for $[\ce{H+}]$.

▸ Show
$K_a = [\ce{H+}][\ce{A-}] / [\ce{HA}]$. Rearranging: $[\ce{H+}] = K_a \times [\ce{HA}] / [\ce{A-}]$. Taking $-\log$ of both sides gives the Henderson–Hasselbalch equation.

2. What Is a Buffer Solution?

A buffer solution is an aqueous solution that resists changes in pH when small amounts of strong acid or strong base are added. Buffers are essential in biological systems — your blood is buffered at pH 7.4 by the $\ce{H2CO3/HCO3-}$ system.

A buffer contains a weak acid–conjugate base pair, such as:

  • $\ce{CH3COOH / CH3COO-}$ (ethanoic acid / ethanoate)
  • $\ce{NH4+ / NH3}$ (ammonium / ammonia — this is a weak acid/conjugate base pair!)
  • $\ce{H2CO3 / HCO3-}$ (carbonic acid / hydrogencarbonate)

2.1 How a Buffer Works — The Two-Way Defence

Consider an ethanoic acid / ethanoate buffer: $\ce{CH3COOH}$ (the weak acid, HA) and $\ce{CH3COONa}$ (providing the conjugate base, $\ce{CH3COO-}$).

Figure 1: Buffer action. Adding H⁺ is neutralised by the conjugate base; adding OH⁻ is neutralised by the weak acid. The large reservoirs of both species ensure the pH stays stable.

Check-in: Which of these mixtures forms a buffer? (a) $\ce{HCl + NaCl}$, (b) $\ce{CH3COOH + CH3COONa}$, (c) $\ce{NH3 + NH4Cl}$, (d) $\ce{HNO3 + NaNO3}$.

▸ Show
(a) No — HCl is a strong acid, not a buffer system. (b) Yes — weak acid + its conjugate base salt. (c) Yes — $\ce{NH4+}$ is a weak acid, $\ce{NH3}$ is its conjugate base. (d) No — HNO₃ is a strong acid. Buffers require weak acid/base + conjugate.

3. The Henderson–Hasselbalch Equation

The pH of a buffer is calculated using the Henderson–Hasselbalch equation, derived from the $K_a$ expression:

Henderson–Hasselbalch Equation: $$\mathrm{pH} = \mathrm{p}K_a + \log_{10}\frac{[\ce{A-}]}{[\ce{HA}]}$$ where $[\ce{A-}]$ = concentration of conjugate base, $[\ce{HA}]$ = concentration of weak acid.

Key insight: When $[\ce{A-}] = [\ce{HA}]$, the log term becomes $\log(1) = 0$, and $\mathrm{pH} = \mathrm{p}K_a$. This is the point of maximum buffering capacity — the buffer works best within ±1 pH unit of $\mathrm{p}K_a$.

3.1 Making a Buffer by Partial Neutralisation

You can make a buffer by partially neutralising a weak acid with a strong base. For example, adding 0.5 equivalents of $\ce{NaOH}$ to $\ce{CH3COOH}$ leaves half the acid unreacted and converts the other half to $\ce{CH3COO-}$ — creating an equimolar buffer at $\mathrm{pH} = \mathrm{p}K_a = 4.76$.

Figure 2: Buffering capacity is highest at pH = pKₐ and falls off beyond ±1 pH unit. Outside this range, the buffer is effectively exhausted.

Check-in: A buffer is made from 0.200 mol dm⁻³ $\ce{CH3COOH}$ and 0.150 mol dm⁻³ $\ce{CH3COONa}$ (pKₐ = 4.76). Calculate its pH.

▸ Show
$\mathrm{pH} = 4.76 + \log(0.150/0.200) = 4.76 + \log(0.750) = 4.76 - 0.125 = \mathbf{4.64}$. The pH is slightly below pKₐ because there is more acid than conjugate base.

4. Calculating pH Change After Adding Strong Acid/Base

When a strong acid is added to a buffer, the added $\ce{H+}$ reacts completely with the conjugate base $\ce{A-}$, converting it to $\ce{HA}$. Then recalculate using the new $[\ce{A-}]$ and $[\ce{HA}]$ in the Henderson–Hasselbalch equation.

Check-in: 1.00 dm³ of buffer contains 0.500 mol $\ce{CH3COOH}$ and 0.500 mol $\ce{CH3COONa}$ (pKₐ = 4.76). Calculate the pH after adding 0.050 mol $\ce{HCl}$.

▸ Show
$\ce{H+}$ (0.050 mol) reacts with $\ce{CH3COO-}$: new $n(\ce{CH3COO-}) = 0.500 - 0.050 = 0.450$ mol. New $n(\ce{CH3COOH}) = 0.500 + 0.050 = 0.550$ mol. Volume unchanged (1.00 dm³). $\mathrm{pH} = 4.76 + \log(0.450/0.550) = 4.76 + \log(0.818) = 4.76 - 0.087 = \mathbf{4.67}$. Without the buffer, 0.050 mol HCl in 1 dm³ gives pH = 1.30 — the buffer kept it at 4.67!

5. Worked Examples

Example 1 — Preparing a Buffer at a Desired pH:

You need to prepare a buffer at pH 5.00 using ethanoic acid ($\mathrm{p}K_a = 4.76$) and sodium ethanoate. What must the ratio $[\ce{CH3COO-}]/[\ce{CH3COOH}]$ be?

Step 1: Use Henderson–Hasselbalch: $5.00 = 4.76 + \log([\ce{A-}]/[\ce{HA}])$.
Step 2: $\log([\ce{A-}]/[\ce{HA}]) = 5.00 - 4.76 = 0.24$.
Step 3: $[\ce{A-}]/[\ce{HA}] = 10^{0.24} = \mathbf{1.74}$. So you need 1.74× more ethanoate than ethanoic acid.
Practical: If you want $[\ce{HA}] = 0.100$ mol dm⁻³, then $[\ce{A-}] = 0.174$ mol dm⁻³.

⚠️ Common wrong approach: Assuming pH = pKₐ means you can use any concentrations. pH = pKₐ only when $[\ce{A-}] = [\ce{HA}]$. For any other pH, you need to calculate the ratio.
Example 2 — Buffer from Partial Neutralisation:

25.0 cm³ of 0.100 mol dm⁻³ $\ce{CH3COOH}$ is mixed with 15.0 cm³ of 0.100 mol dm⁻³ $\ce{NaOH}$. Calculate the pH of the resulting buffer. (pKₐ = 4.76)

Step 1: Moles initially. $n(\ce{CH3COOH})_{\text{initial}} = 0.100 \times 0.0250 = 0.00250$ mol. $n(\ce{NaOH}) = 0.100 \times 0.0150 = 0.00150$ mol.
Step 2: Reaction. $\ce{CH3COOH + OH- -> CH3COO- + H2O}$. OH⁻ is limiting. $n(\ce{CH3COOH})_{\text{remaining}} = 0.00250 - 0.00150 = 0.00100$ mol. $n(\ce{CH3COO-})_{\text{formed}} = 0.00150$ mol.
Step 3: Total volume = 40.0 cm³ = 0.0400 dm³. Concentrations: $[\ce{CH3COOH}] = 0.00100/0.0400 = 0.0250$, $[\ce{CH3COO-}] = 0.00150/0.0400 = 0.0375$ mol dm⁻³.
Step 4: $\mathrm{pH} = 4.76 + \log(0.0375/0.0250) = 4.76 + \log(1.50) = 4.76 + 0.176 = \mathbf{4.94}$.

⚠️ Common wrong approach: Forgetting to account for the total volume when converting moles to concentration. Since both species are in the same solution, the volume cancels in the ratio — you can use moles directly: $\mathrm{pH} = 4.76 + \log(0.00150/0.00100) = 4.94$. This shortcut works!
Example 3 — Adding Strong Base to a Buffer:

A buffer is prepared with 0.300 mol $\ce{NH4Cl}$ and 0.200 mol $\ce{NH3}$ in 1.00 dm³. ($\mathrm{p}K_a$ of $\ce{NH4+}$ = 9.25). (a) Calculate the initial pH. (b) Calculate the pH after adding 0.050 mol $\ce{NaOH}$.

(a) $\mathrm{pH} = 9.25 + \log([\ce{NH3}]/[\ce{NH4+}]) = 9.25 + \log(0.200/0.300) = 9.25 - 0.176 = \mathbf{9.07}$.
(b) $\ce{OH-}$ reacts with $\ce{NH4+}$: $\ce{NH4+ + OH- -> NH3 + H2O}$. New $n(\ce{NH4+}) = 0.300 - 0.050 = 0.250$ mol. New $n(\ce{NH3}) = 0.200 + 0.050 = 0.250$ mol.
$\mathrm{pH} = 9.25 + \log(0.250/0.250) = 9.25 + 0 = \mathbf{9.25}$. Now the buffer is exactly at its pKₐ!

⚠️ Common wrong approach: For the $\ce{NH4+/NH3}$ buffer, students sometimes use pKb instead of pKa. Remember: Henderson–Hasselbalch uses the pKa of the acid form — here $\ce{NH4+}$ is the acid, so use its pKa (which is 14 − pKb = 14 − 4.75 = 9.25).

⚠️ Common Mistakes to Avoid

  1. Thinking any mixture of acid and base makes a buffer. A buffer requires a weak acid/base pair. Mixtures of strong acids and strong bases do not create buffers — they simply neutralise each other.
  2. Forgetting that buffers have limited capacity. If you add too much strong acid or base, the buffer will be overwhelmed and the pH will change dramatically. Buffer capacity is proportional to the concentrations of HA and A⁻.
  3. Using pKb instead of pKa for buffers involving weak bases like NH₃. In the Henderson–Hasselbalch equation, always use the pKa of the acid form. For NH₄⁺/NH₃, the acid is NH₄⁺.
  4. Diluting a buffer changes concentrations but not pH significantly. Since both [HA] and [A⁻] are diluted by the same factor, their ratio (and therefore pH) remains approximately constant. This is because the Henderson–Hasselbalch equation uses the ratio, not absolute concentrations.

A. $\ce{HCl}$ and $\ce{NaCl}$   B. $\ce{NaOH}$ and $\ce{NaCl}$   C. $\ce{CH3COOH}$ and $\ce{CH3COONa}$   D. $\ce{HNO3}$ and $\ce{NaNO3}$

Question 2: A buffer contains 0.200 mol dm⁻³ $\ce{HCOOH}$ (pKₐ = 3.75) and 0.300 mol dm⁻³ $\ce{HCOONa}$. Calculate its pH.

Question 3: 40.0 cm³ of 0.200 mol dm⁻³ $\ce{CH3COOH}$ is mixed with 30.0 cm³ of 0.200 mol dm⁻³ $\ce{NaOH}$. (a) Show that the resulting solution is a buffer. (b) Calculate its pH. (pKₐ = 4.76)

Question 4: A buffer is made by dissolving 0.400 mol $\ce{CH3COOH}$ and 0.400 mol $\ce{CH3COONa}$ in water to make 1.00 dm³ of solution. (a) Calculate the pH. (b) What is the new pH after adding 0.100 mol of $\ce{HCl}$?

Question 5: A biochemist needs a buffer at pH 7.40. She has $\ce{NaH2PO4}$ (pKₐ = 7.21 for $\ce{H2PO4-}$). What mass of $\ce{Na2HPO4}$ (molar mass 142 g mol⁻¹) must be added to 500 cm³ of 0.100 mol dm⁻³ $\ce{NaH2PO4}$ to achieve this pH?

Question 6 (IB-style): A student makes a buffer by mixing 50.0 cm³ of 0.500 mol dm⁻³ $\ce{CH3COOH}$ with 50.0 cm³ of 0.500 mol dm⁻³ $\ce{NaOH}$. (a) Explain why this does NOT produce a buffer. (b) What volume of 0.500 mol dm⁻³ $\ce{NaOH}$ should have been used to make a buffer at pH = pKₐ (4.76)?

Answer 1: C. Only a weak acid + its conjugate base salt produces a buffer. HCl, HNO₃ and NaOH are all strong — they fully dissociate/neutralise.

Answer 2: $\mathrm{pH} = 3.75 + \log(0.300/0.200) = 3.75 + \log(1.50) = 3.75 + 0.176 = \mathbf{3.93}$.

Answer 3: (a) $n(\ce{CH3COOH})_{\text{initial}} = 0.200 \times 0.0400 = 0.00800$. $n(\ce{NaOH}) = 0.200 \times 0.0300 = 0.00600$. After reaction: $n(\ce{CH3COOH}) = 0.00200$, $n(\ce{CH3COO-}) = 0.00600$. Both are present → buffer. (b) Ratio = 0.00600/0.00200 = 3.00. $\mathrm{pH} = 4.76 + \log(3.00) = 4.76 + 0.477 = \mathbf{5.24}$.

Answer 4: (a) pH = 4.76 + log(0.400/0.400) = 4.76. (b) After HCl: $n(\ce{CH3COO-}) = 0.400 - 0.100 = 0.300$, $n(\ce{CH3COOH}) = 0.400 + 0.100 = 0.500$. pH = 4.76 + log(0.300/0.500) = 4.76 − 0.222 = 4.54. Change is only −0.22 units.

Answer 5: pH = 7.40, pKₐ = 7.21. $\log([\ce{HPO4^{2-}}]/[\ce{H2PO4-}]) = 7.40 − 7.21 = 0.19$. Ratio = $10^{0.19} = 1.55$. So $[\ce{HPO4^{2-}}] = 1.55 \times 0.100 = 0.155$ mol dm⁻³. Moles in 0.500 dm³ = 0.155 × 0.500 = 0.0775 mol. Mass = 0.0775 × 142 = 11.0 g.

Answer 6: (a) Both have 0.0250 mol. NaOH completely neutralises CH₃COOH, leaving only CH₃COO⁻ — no weak acid remains, so it's not a buffer. (b) For pH = pKₐ, need equal moles of HA and A⁻. So add half as much NaOH: 0.0125 mol → 25.0 cm³ of 0.500 mol dm⁻³ NaOH.

7. Key Terms

TermDefinition
Buffer solutionAn aqueous solution that resists changes in pH upon addition of small amounts of strong acid or base
Henderson–Hasselbalch equation$\mathrm{pH} = \mathrm{p}K_a + \log([\ce{A-}]/[\ce{HA}])$ — used to calculate the pH of a buffer
Buffer capacityThe amount of strong acid or base a buffer can neutralise before the pH changes significantly; maximum when $[\ce{A-}] = [\ce{HA}]$
Conjugate acid–base pairTwo species that differ by one proton; e.g., $\ce{CH3COOH/CH3COO-}$ or $\ce{NH4+/NH3}$
Partial neutralisationA method of making a buffer by adding less than one equivalent of strong base to a weak acid
Effective buffer range$\mathrm{p}K_a \pm 1$; outside this range, the buffer is largely ineffective
Equimolar bufferA buffer where $[\ce{A-}] = [\ce{HA}]$, giving $\mathrm{pH} = \mathrm{p}K_a$

8. Exam Tips

Paper 1 (Multiple Choice): Questions often ask you to identify which mixture is a buffer. Look for a weak acid/base plus its conjugate. If both species are strong (e.g., HCl + NaCl), it is NOT a buffer. Also, if you see equimolar amounts of a weak acid and strong base, check carefully — that produces the conjugate base only, not a buffer.
Paper 2 (Structured): Buffer calculations are typically 3–5 mark questions. You earn marks for: (1) identifying the acid and conjugate base, (2) writing the Henderson–Hasselbalch equation, (3) correctly calculating moles before and after reaction for partial neutralisation questions, (4) correctly substituting the ratio. Show the equation before plugging in numbers.
Explaining buffer action: If asked "how does a buffer work?", use the two-equation model: $\ce{A- + H+ -> HA}$ (for added acid) and $\ce{HA + OH- -> A- + H2O}$ (for added base). Then state that the equilibrium $\ce{HA <=> H+ + A-}$ shifts to counteract the disturbance — this is Le Chatelier's principle in action.
