# Unit 9: Organic Chemistry — Practice Problems

## Problem 1
A student is given an unknown organic compound with the molecular formula \ce{C3H6O}. The compound reacts with 2,4-dinitrophenylhydrazine (2,4-DNPH) to form an orange precipitate, but it gives no reaction with Tollens' reagent or Fehling's solution. The compound can be reduced by sodium borohydride (\ce{NaBH4}) to form a secondary alcohol. Draw the structural formula and give the IUPAC name of the compound. Explain how each piece of evidence leads to your conclusion.

## Problem 2
Give the IUPAC name for each of the following compounds:
(a) \ce{CH3CH2CH(CH3)COOH}
(b) \ce{CH3CH2COOCH2CH3}
(c) \ce{CH3CH2CH2NH2}
(d) \ce{CH3CONHCH3}
(e) \ce{CH3CH(Cl)CH2CH3}

## Problem 3
A compound has the molecular formula \ce{C4H10O}. Its \ce{^1H} NMR spectrum shows three signals: a triplet at δ 1.1 (3H), a sextet at δ 1.6 (2H), a triplet at δ 3.4 (2H), and a singlet at δ 2.1 (1H) that disappears upon addition of \ce{D2O}. The IR spectrum shows a broad absorption at 3350 cm⁻¹ but no absorption in the region 1680–1750 cm⁻¹. Deduce the structure and give the IUPAC name. Explain how you used each piece of spectral data.

## Problem 4
(a) Write the general formula for each of the following homologous series: alkanes, alkenes, alcohols, carboxylic acids, and amines. (b) Explain why cycloalkanes have the same general formula as alkenes but belong to a different homologous series. (c) A compound has the molecular formula \ce{C5H10O2}. Suggest two possible homologous series to which this compound could belong, giving an example structure for each.

## Problem 5
Draw and name ALL the structural isomers of \ce{C4H9Br}. Classify each isomer according to the type of halogenoalkane it represents (primary, secondary, or tertiary). For any two isomers that are positional isomers of each other, explain what makes them positional rather than chain isomers.

## Problem 6
The molecular formula \ce{C3H6O2} corresponds to several structural isomers containing different functional groups. Draw and name one isomer from each of the following functional group classes: a carboxylic acid, an ester, a hydroxyaldehyde, and a hydroxyketone. For each structure, identify the functional group present.

## Problem 7
Methane reacts with chlorine gas in the presence of ultraviolet light to form chloromethane and hydrogen chloride. (a) Write the overall equation for the reaction. (b) Describe the three stages of the free-radical substitution mechanism, giving equations for each step and using curly half-arrows to show electron movement. (c) Explain why a small amount of ethane (\ce{C2H6}) is also formed as a by-product in this reaction. (d) Explain why this reaction is not a good method for preparing a pure sample of chloromethane in the laboratory.

## Problem 8
Propene (\ce{CH3CH=CH2}) reacts with hydrogen bromide (\ce{HBr}) to form two possible products. (a) Draw the two possible products and name them. (b) Identify the major product according to Markovnikov's rule and explain the rule in terms of carbocation stability. (c) Show the full electrophilic addition mechanism for the formation of the major product, using curly arrows. (d) Explain why the reaction of propene with \ce{HBr} in the presence of a peroxide (\ce{ROOR}) gives a different product distribution.

## Problem 9
An alkene with the molecular formula \ce{C4H8} reacts with cold, dilute potassium manganate(VII) solution to form a diol. The original alkene exists as a pair of E/Z stereoisomers. (a) Draw the structural formula of the alkene and explain why it exhibits E/Z isomerism. (b) Assign the E and Z configurations to each isomer using Cahn–Ingold–Prelog priority rules, showing your reasoning. (c) Write the equation for the reaction of the alkene with cold, dilute \ce{KMnO4}, and state the colour change observed.

## Problem 10
Ethene undergoes electrophilic addition with concentrated sulfuric acid at room temperature, followed by hydrolysis with water, to produce ethanol. (a) Write equations for both steps of this two-step process. (b) Name the type of reaction occurring in the first step and explain why ethene is able to act as a nucleophile even though it has no lone pairs of electrons. (c) The same overall transformation (ethene to ethanol) can be achieved by the direct hydration of ethene with steam and a phosphoric acid catalyst. Compare the two methods, noting one advantage of each approach.

## Problem 11
The repeating unit of a common addition polymer is \ce{–[CH2–CHCl]_{n}–}. (a) Draw the structural formula of the monomer used to produce this polymer and give the IUPAC name of the monomer. (b) Give the common name and one major use of this polymer. (c) Write an equation showing the polymerisation of 2.0 mol of the monomer, clearly showing the repeating unit. (d) Draw the repeating unit of the polymer formed from tetrafluoroethene (\ce{CF2=CF2}) and explain why this polymer, PTFE, is chemically unreactive and has a very low coefficient of friction.

## Problem 12
Consider the three alcohols: butan-2-ol, 2-methylpropan-2-ol, and butan-1-ol. (a) Classify each alcohol as primary, secondary, or tertiary. (b) For each alcohol, predict the product(s) formed upon heating with acidified potassium dichromate(VI) solution. If no reaction occurs, explain why. (c) Describe the colour change that would be observed during a positive oxidation test. (d) Write a balanced equation for the complete oxidation of butan-1-ol to its final organic product, using \ce{[O]} to represent the oxidising agent.

## Problem 13
A student heats butan-2-ol with acidified potassium dichromate(VI) and distils the product as it forms. The product gives an orange precipitate with 2,4-DNPH but no reaction with Tollens' reagent. (a) Identify the product and explain the evidence. (b) Explain why the student chose to distil the product rather than reflux the reaction mixture. (c) Draw the structure of the product that would have been obtained if the student had refluxed butan-1-ol with excess acidified dichromate(VI) instead.

## Problem 14
(a) Explain why tertiary alcohols cannot be oxidised by acidified dichromate(VI), whereas primary and secondary alcohols can. Use structural diagrams to support your explanation. (b) Describe a chemical test, other than oxidation with dichromate(VI), that could distinguish between a sample of propan-1-ol and propan-2-ol. State the reagent, the observation for each alcohol, and the chemistry underlying the test.

## Problem 15
A student has three unlabelled bottles containing propanal, propanone, and propanoic acid. (a) Describe a sequence of chemical tests the student could use to identify each compound. For each test, state the reagent, the expected observation for each compound, and the chemical principle behind the test. (b) Propanal can be distinguished from propanone using both Tollens' reagent and Fehling's solution. Explain why propanal gives a positive result with both reagents while propanone does not, and write half-equations for the oxidation of propanal in Tollens' test.

## Problem 16
(a) Classify each of the following halogenoalkanes as primary, secondary, or tertiary: 1-bromobutane, 2-bromobutane, 2-bromo-2-methylpropane. (b) When each halogenoalkane is warmed with aqueous sodium hydroxide, hydrolysis occurs. For each compound, state whether the reaction would proceed predominantly by an \ce{S_N1} or \ce{S_N2} mechanism, and explain your reasoning. (c) Compare the rate equations and stereochemical outcomes of \ce{S_N1} and \ce{S_N2} reactions. (d) Explain why the rate of hydrolysis of 2-bromo-2-methylpropane is independent of the concentration of hydroxide ions.

## Problem 17
The reaction of 1-bromobutane with potassium cyanide (\ce{KCN}) in ethanol is found to follow second-order kinetics: rate = k[\ce{CH3CH2CH2CH2Br}][\ce{CN^-}]. (a) Name the type of reaction mechanism operating here and draw the transition state for the rate-determining step. (b) The product of this reaction is then reduced with hydrogen gas over a nickel catalyst. Draw the structure of the final organic product. (c) If the same reaction of 1-bromobutane were carried out with hot ethanolic potassium hydroxide instead of \ce{KCN}, the major organic product would be different. Name the type of reaction that would occur and draw the structure of the product.

## Problem 18
A chemist compares the rates of hydrolysis of three halogenoalkanes with aqueous silver nitrate in ethanol: \ce{CH3CH2CH2CH2Cl}, \ce{CH3CH2CH2CH2Br}, and \ce{CH3CH2CH2CH2I}. Under identical conditions, the iodide reacts fastest and the chloride slowest. (a) Explain this trend in terms of bond enthalpies and the mechanism of the reaction. (b) Predict the relative rates of hydrolysis of \ce{(CH3)3CBr}, \ce{CH3CH2CHBrCH3}, and \ce{CH3CH2CH2CH2Br}, and explain your reasoning. (c) State the role of the silver nitrate in this test and write an ionic equation for the precipitation reaction that confirms the presence of halide ions.

## Problem 19
When 2-bromobutane is heated with ethanolic potassium hydroxide, two competing reactions take place: nucleophilic substitution and elimination. (a) Draw the structures of all possible organic products from both reaction pathways and name each product. (b) Identify which product(s) exhibit E/Z stereoisomerism and explain why. (c) Explain how a chemist could favour the elimination pathway over the substitution pathway by changing the reaction conditions. (d) If optically pure (R)-2-bromobutane is used as the starting material, describe the optical activity of the substitution product(s) obtained, and explain your answer in terms of the reaction mechanism.

## Problem 20
Propanal and propanone are both reduced by sodium borohydride (\ce{NaBH4}) in aqueous solution. (a) Draw the product formed from each reduction and name the products. (b) Although both reductions follow a nucleophilic addition mechanism, only one of the products contains a chiral carbon atom. Identify which product is chiral, draw the two enantiomers using three-dimensional diagrams, and explain why the other product is achiral. (c) \ce{NaBH4} is the reducing agent in these reactions; it is a source of hydride ions (\ce{H^-}). Using curly arrows, show the mechanism for the reduction of propanal, clearly indicating the role of the hydride ion.

## Problem 21
A solution of ethanoic acid in water has a concentration of 0.10 mol dm⁻³ and a pH of approximately 2.9. (a) Write the equation for the dissociation of ethanoic acid in water and explain why ethanoic acid is described as a weak acid. (b) Calculate the approximate value of the acid dissociation constant, \ce{K_a}, for ethanoic acid. (c) Ethanoic acid reacts with ethanol in the presence of concentrated sulfuric acid to form an ester. Write the equation for this reaction, state the name of the ester, and explain why concentrated sulfuric acid is used rather than dilute acid. (d) The same ester can be prepared more efficiently by reacting ethanol with ethanoyl chloride (\ce{CH3COCl}) instead of ethanoic acid. Write the equation and state one advantage of using ethanoyl chloride.

## Problem 22
(a) Write an equation for the condensation reaction between methanoic acid and methanol, giving the structural formula of the ester formed. (b) The esterification reaction is slow at room temperature and does not go to completion. Explain the role of the sulfuric acid catalyst and why the reaction is an equilibrium process. (c) Suggest a method to increase the yield of ester from this equilibrium reaction. (d) The ester formed in part (a) has a distinctive smell and is found in some fruits. However, the parent carboxylic acid, methanoic acid, has a pungent, irritating odour. Explain, in terms of intermolecular forces, why methanoic acid has a higher boiling point (101 °C) than its methyl ester (32 °C) despite having a lower relative molecular mass.

## Problem 23
Ethyl ethanoate can be hydrolysed under both acidic and basic conditions. (a) Write equations for the hydrolysis of ethyl ethanoate using (i) dilute hydrochloric acid and (ii) aqueous sodium hydroxide, naming the organic products in each case. (b) Explain why the base-catalysed hydrolysis is described as "irreversible" while the acid-catalysed hydrolysis is reversible. (c) In the base-catalysed hydrolysis of ethyl ethanoate, the rate law is: rate = k[\ce{CH3COOC2H5}][\ce{OH^-}]. Propose a two-step mechanism that is consistent with this rate law, showing curly arrows for each step. (d) Soaps are produced by the alkaline hydrolysis of long-chain esters (triglycerides). Explain why this process is called saponification and why the resulting carboxylate salts are effective cleaning agents.

## Problem 24
Benzene (\ce{C6H6}) is an aromatic hydrocarbon with a planar hexagonal structure in which all carbon–carbon bonds are of equal length (140 pm), intermediate between the typical \ce{C–C} single bond (154 pm) and \ce{C=C} double bond (134 pm). (a) Describe the delocalised model of benzene, explaining how the delocalisation of π electrons accounts for the equal bond lengths. (b) The enthalpy change of hydrogenation of cyclohexene is –120 kJ mol⁻¹. Based on this value, the expected enthalpy change of hydrogenation of benzene (if it contained three isolated double bonds) would be –360 kJ mol⁻¹. However, the experimentally measured value is only –208 kJ mol⁻¹. Calculate the stabilisation energy of benzene and explain what this value reveals about the stability of benzene relative to a hypothetical "cyclohexatriene." (c) Benzene undergoes electrophilic substitution rather than electrophilic addition. Explain this observation in terms of the stability of the aromatic system.

## Problem 25
Benzene reacts with a mixture of concentrated nitric acid and concentrated sulfuric acid at 50 °C to form nitrobenzene. (a) Write the overall equation for the nitration of benzene. (b) The electrophile in this reaction is the nitronium ion, \ce{NO2+}. Write equations to show how the nitronium ion is generated from the mixture of nitric and sulfuric acids. (c) Show the full mechanism for the electrophilic substitution of benzene by the nitronium ion, including the formation of the σ-complex (Wheland intermediate) and the regeneration of the aromatic system. Use curly arrows. (d) Explain why the reaction temperature must be carefully controlled at around 50 °C, and describe what would happen if the temperature were significantly higher.

## Problem 26
Friedel–Crafts alkylation allows the attachment of alkyl groups to a benzene ring. (a) Write equations showing how the electrophile is generated when benzene is reacted with chloroethane in the presence of anhydrous aluminium chloride. (b) Show the mechanism for the Friedel–Crafts alkylation of benzene by the electrophile from part (a). (c) The alkylbenzene product is more reactive than benzene itself towards further electrophilic substitution. Explain why this is the case and predict whether the next electrophilic substitution would occur mainly at the ortho/para or meta positions. (d) A limitation of Friedel–Crafts alkylation is that polyalkylation often occurs. Explain why this happens and suggest how the reaction conditions could be adjusted to favour monoalkylation.

## Problem 27
Nitrobenzene and methylbenzene (toluene) both undergo electrophilic substitution, but they react at different rates and with different regioselectivity. (a) Classify the nitro group (\ce{–NO2}) and the methyl group (\ce{–CH3}) as activating or deactivating, and as ortho/para-directing or meta-directing. (b) Explain, using resonance structures where appropriate, why the nitro group directs incoming electrophiles to the meta position. (c) Explain, using the concept of inductive effects and hyperconjugation, why the methyl group activates the ring and directs to the ortho and para positions. (d) Predict the major product when methylbenzene is treated with chlorine gas in the presence of an aluminium chloride catalyst. Write the overall equation.

## Problem 28

(b) Draw the E and Z isomers, applying Cahn–Ingold–Prelog priority rules to assign the configuration. Show your reasoning for the priority assignment at each carbon of the double bond. (c) A student claims that but-1-ene (\ce{CH3CH2CH=CH2}) can also exist as E/Z isomers. Explain why the student is incorrect. (d) State what structural feature is required for a molecule to exhibit E/Z isomerism.

## Problem 29

Explain why this is the case and name the type of mixture formed. (d) In contrast, lactic acid produced by fermentation of sugars in muscle cells is optically active. Explain the difference between the two samples at the molecular level.
