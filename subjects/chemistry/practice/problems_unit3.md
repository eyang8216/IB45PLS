# Unit 3: Periodicity & Bonding — Practice Problems

## Problem 1
Explain why atomic radius decreases across Period 3 from sodium to argon, even though the number of electrons in the atom is increasing. Reference the relevant factors that determine atomic size.

## Problem 2
The ions \ce{O^{2-}}, \ce{F-}, \ce{Na+}, \ce{Mg^{2+}}, and \ce{Al^{3+}} form an isoelectronic series. Arrange these five ions in order of increasing ionic radius and justify your ordering.

## Problem 3
The first ionisation energy of aluminium (578 kJ mol⁻¹) is lower than that of magnesium (738 kJ mol⁻¹), even though the general trend across a period is an increase in ionisation energy. Account for this anomaly with reference to electron configurations.

## Problem 4
Predict which element in each pair has the higher electronegativity and explain your reasoning: (a) nitrogen or phosphorus, (b) chlorine or bromine, (c) oxygen or sulfur.

## Problem 5
Period 3 oxides range from strongly basic to strongly acidic. Classify \ce{Na2O}, \ce{MgO}, \ce{Al2O3}, \ce{SiO2}, \ce{P4O10}, and \ce{SO3} according to their acid–base character (basic, amphoteric, or acidic), and write balanced equations for the reaction of \ce{Na2O} and \ce{SO3} with water.

## Problem 6
Aluminium oxide, \ce{Al2O3}, is described as amphoteric. Write balanced equations to show the reaction of \ce{Al2O3} with (a) aqueous hydrochloric acid and (b) aqueous sodium hydroxide. Explain why this oxide exhibits this dual behaviour.

## Problem 7
The Period 3 chlorides \ce{NaCl}, \ce{MgCl2}, \ce{AlCl3}, \ce{SiCl4}, and \ce{PCl5} show a trend in structure and bonding from ionic to covalent. (a) Describe the structure and bonding in each chloride. (b) \ce{SiCl4} undergoes rapid hydrolysis with water whereas \ce{CCl4} does not. Explain this difference.

## Problem 8
Draw the Lewis structure of \ce{CO2}. State the number of bonding and non-bonding electron pairs around the central atom, and identify the formal charges on each atom.

## Problem 9
Draw the Lewis structure of the sulfate ion, \ce{SO4^{2-}}. Calculate the formal charge on the sulfur atom and on each oxygen atom in your structure. Identify the total number of resonance structures and explain why they are necessary.

## Problem 10
Beryllium chloride, \ce{BeCl2}, and boron trifluoride, \ce{BF3}, are examples of electron-deficient compounds. Draw their Lewis structures and explain why these molecules are exceptions to the octet rule. Explain why \ce{BF3} can act as a Lewis acid.

## Problem 11
Sulfur hexafluoride, \ce{SF6}, and phosphorus pentafluoride, \ce{PF5}, are molecules in which the central atom exceeds the octet. Draw the Lewis structure of each molecule and explain why these elements can expand their valence shell.

## Problem 12
The azide ion, \ce{N3-}, has three nitrogen atoms in a linear arrangement. Draw all significant resonance structures and calculate the formal charge on each nitrogen atom in each structure. Based on formal charges, which resonance structure(s) contribute most to the hybrid?

## Problem 13
Using VSEPR theory, predict the molecular geometry, ideal bond angle, and expected deviation (if any) for \ce{CH4}, \ce{NH3}, and \ce{H2O}. Account for the trend in bond angles across this series in terms of electron-pair repulsion.

## Problem 14
Predict the shape and F–P–F bond angles (both axial–equatorial and equatorial–equatorial) in \ce{PF5}. Explain why the axial bonds in \ce{PF5} are longer than the equatorial bonds.

## Problem 15
Predict the electron-domain geometry, molecular geometry, and approximate bond angles for each of the following species: (a) \ce{SF6}, (b) \ce{BrF5}, (c) \ce{XeF4}, (d) \ce{I3-}. State the number of lone pairs on the central atom in each case.

## Problem 16
Predict whether each of the following molecules is polar or non-polar by considering both the bond polarities and the molecular geometry: (a) \ce{CO2}, (b) \ce{SO2}, (c) \ce{BF3}, (d) \ce{CH2Cl2}, (e) \ce{SF6}. Justify each answer with reference to the vector sum of bond dipoles.

## Problem 17
Consider \ce{NH3} and \ce{NF3}. Both molecules have a trigonal pyramidal shape, yet \ce{NH3} is a polar molecule with a dipole moment of 1.47 D while \ce{NF3} has a much smaller dipole moment of 0.24 D. Explain this difference using bond polarities and the direction of the lone-pair dipole.

## Problem 18
Rank the following substances in order of increasing boiling point and state the dominant intermolecular force in each: \ce{CH4}, \ce{CH3Cl}, \ce{CH3OH}, \ce{NaCl}. Justify the ranking.

## Problem 19
Explain why hydrogen fluoride, \ce{HF}, has an unusually high boiling point (19.5 °C) compared to \ce{HCl} (−85 °C), \ce{HBr} (−67 °C), and \ce{HI} (−35 °C). What type of intermolecular force is responsible for this anomaly?

## Problem 20
Explain, with reference to bonding and structure, the following observations: (a) diamond is extremely hard and does not conduct electricity, while graphite is soft and conducts electricity parallel to its layers; (b) graphene is an excellent conductor of electricity and is stronger than steel.

## Problem 21
Describe metallic bonding using the electron-sea model. Explain why metals are malleable and ductile, and why alloys such as brass (copper–zinc) are typically harder than the pure metals from which they are made.

## Problem 22
For ethene, \ce{C2H4}, and ethyne, \ce{C2H2}, state the hybridisation of each carbon atom and the number of σ and π bonds in each molecule. Explain why the carbon–carbon bond length decreases from ethane (154 pm) to ethene (134 pm) to ethyne (120 pm).

## Problem 23
Determine the hybridisation of the central atom in each of the following species: \ce{BeCl2}, \ce{BH3}, \ce{CH4}, \ce{PCl5}, \ce{SF6}. State the number and type (\sigma or \pi) of bonds formed between the central atom and the surrounding atoms in each case.

## Problem 24
Define a transition metal according to the IUPAC definition. Explain why zinc (atomic number 30) is **not** considered a transition metal even though it is in the d-block of the periodic table.

## Problem 25
The complex ion \ce{[Cu(H2O)6]^{2+}} is pale blue, while \ce{[Cu(NH3)4(H2O)2]^{2+}} is deep blue. Using crystal field theory and the spectrochemical series, explain the difference in colour between these two complex ions. Include the concept of d-orbital splitting in your answer.

## Problem 26
A complex ion has the formula \ce{[CoCl2(NH3)4]+}. (a) Determine the oxidation state of cobalt. (b) State the coordination number. (c) Draw the two possible geometric isomers. (d) Name the complex using IUPAC nomenclature.

## Problem 27
The nitrate ion, \ce{NO3-}, can be represented by three equivalent resonance structures. Draw all three structures, calculate the formal charge on each atom, and explain what the resonance hybrid implies about the three N–O bonds.
