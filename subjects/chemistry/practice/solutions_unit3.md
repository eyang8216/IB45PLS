# Unit 3: Periodicity & Bonding — Solutions

## Problem 1

Atomic radius decreases across Period 3 from \ce{Na} (sodium) to \ce{Ar} (argon) because:

1. **Increasing nuclear charge**: Across a period, each successive element has one additional proton in the nucleus. The nuclear charge increases from +11 for Na to +18 for Ar.

2. **Ineffective shielding within the same shell**: The added electrons all enter the same principal quantum shell (n = 3). Electrons in the same shell do not shield one another effectively from the increasing nuclear charge. Shielding remains roughly constant because the inner-shell electron count (1s² 2s² 2p⁶, i.e. 10 electrons) stays the same throughout Period 3.

3. **Greater effective nuclear charge (Z_eff)** results: The attractive force on the outer electrons increases steadily, pulling the electron cloud closer to the nucleus. Even though the total number of electrons increases, the dominant factor is the stronger nuclear attraction — hence the atomic radius decreases.

**Pitfall**: Students often think "more electrons = bigger atom," but the key is *where* those electrons go and how strongly they are pulled by the nucleus.

## Problem 2

The five ions \ce{O^{2-}}, \ce{F-}, \ce{Na+}, \ce{Mg^{2+}}, and \ce{Al^{3+}} are isoelectronic — they all have the electron configuration 1s² 2s² 2p⁶ (10 electrons total, like neon).

Order of **increasing** ionic radius (smallest → largest):

\ce{Al^{3+}} < \ce{Mg^{2+}} < \ce{Na+} < \ce{F-} < \ce{O^{2-}}

**Justification**: In an isoelectronic series, all ions have the same number of electrons and thus the same electron–electron repulsion. The only variable is the nuclear charge. \ce{Al^{3+}} has the largest nuclear charge (Z = 13), so its electrons are pulled in most tightly, giving it the smallest radius. \ce{O^{2-}} has the smallest nuclear charge (Z = 8), so its electrons experience the weakest attraction and spread out the most — largest radius.

**Pitfall**: Remember that for anions (negative ions), the ion is *larger* than the parent atom; for cations (positive ions), the ion is *smaller*. Here, the trend within the isoelectronic series is governed solely by Z.

## Problem 3

**Electron configurations**:
- \ce{Mg}: 1s² 2s² 2p⁶ **3s²** — the electron to be removed comes from a filled 3s subshell.
- \ce{Al}: 1s² 2s² 2p⁶ **3s² 3p¹** — the electron to be removed comes from a singly occupied 3p orbital.

The 3p electron in aluminium is:
- **Higher in energy** than the 3s electron in magnesium (3p is farther from the nucleus on average).
- **Better shielded** from the nucleus because the filled 3s² subshell provides additional shielding.

Both factors make it *easier* to remove the outermost electron from Al than from Mg, so Al has a lower first ionisation energy despite having a greater nuclear charge. This anomaly illustrates that ionisation energy depends on both nuclear charge and the subshell from which the electron is removed.

## Problem 4

Electronegativity decreases down a group (atomic radius increases, reducing the nucleus's pull on bonding electrons) and increases across a period (Z_eff increases).

(a) **Nitrogen** > phosphorus. N is in Period 2, directly above P. N has a smaller atomic radius, so shared bonding electrons are closer to the nucleus and more strongly attracted.

(b) **Chlorine** > bromine. Cl is in Period 3, above Br in Period 4. Smaller atom, greater attraction for bonding electrons.

(c) **Oxygen** > sulfur. O is in Period 2, above S in Period 3. Same reasoning: oxygen's smaller size gives a stronger pull on bonding electrons.

## Problem 7

(a) **Structures and bonding**:

- **\ce{NaCl}**: Ionic lattice. Na⁺ and Cl⁻ ions held by strong electrostatic forces in a giant ionic structure.
- **\ce{MgCl2}**: Ionic lattice. Mg²⁺ and Cl⁻ ions. Higher charge density of Mg²⁺ gives stronger ionic bonding and more covalent character (polarisation) than NaCl.
- **\ce{AlCl3}**: Exists as a dimer \ce{Al2Cl6} in the solid and liquid phases, with covalent bonding and dative bonds. At higher temperatures it dissociates to \ce{AlCl3} monomers (trigonal planar). The high charge density of \ce{Al^{3+}} polarises the Cl⁻ ions so much that bonding is essentially covalent.
- **\ce{SiCl4}**: Simple molecular (covalent). Discrete tetrahedral \ce{SiCl4} molecules with weak London forces between them.
- **\ce{PCl5}**: Simple molecular (covalent) in the gas phase; exists as \ce{[PCl4]+[PCl6]-} in the solid state.

(b) \ce{SiCl4} undergoes hydrolysis because silicon has vacant 3d orbitals that can accept a lone pair from water, forming an intermediate that decomposes:

\ce{SiCl4(l) + 2H2O(l) -> SiO2(s) + 4HCl(aq)}

In contrast, carbon in \ce{CCl4} has no accessible d orbitals (it is in Period 2), so water cannot attack it. \ce{CCl4} is kinetically inert to hydrolysis.

## Problem 8

**Lewis structure of \ce{CO2}**: O=C=O

Carbon is the central atom. Each oxygen is double-bonded to carbon.

**Electron pairs around carbon**: 4 bonding pairs (two double bonds), 0 non-bonding (lone) pairs.

**Formal charges**:
- Carbon: FC = 4 valence e⁻ − (0 non-bonding + ½ × 8 bonding) = 4 − 4 = **0**
- Each oxygen: FC = 6 valence e⁻ − (4 non-bonding + ½ × 4 bonding) = 6 − 6 = **0**

All formal charges are zero — this is the most stable Lewis structure.

## Problem 9

**Lewis structure of \ce{SO4^{2-}}**: Sulfur is central, surrounded by four oxygen atoms. The ion carries a 2− charge overall.

In the most stable Lewis structure (with expanded octet on S), sulfur forms four S–O single bonds and two S=O double bonds distributed across the four positions by resonance — giving a bond order of 1.5 for each S–O.

**Formal charges** (in any one resonance form with two S=O and two S–O⁻):

- S: FC = 6 valence − (0 non-bonding + ½ × 12 bonding) = 6 − 6 = **0**
- Each doubly-bonded O: FC = 6 − (4 non-bonding + ½ × 4 bonding) = 6 − 6 = **0**
- Each singly-bonded O: FC = 6 − (6 non-bonding + ½ × 2 bonding) = 6 − 7 = **−1**

The two negative charges are delocalised over the four oxygen atoms by resonance.

**Number of resonance structures**: 6 (corresponding to the number of ways you can arrange two double bonds among four positions, i.e. ⁴C₂ = 6). The resonance hybrid gives each S–O bond equal length and strength, with bond order 1.5.

## Problem 10

**Lewis structure of \ce{BeCl2}**: Cl–Be–Cl. Beryllium has only 4 electrons in its valence shell (2 from Be + 1 from each Cl = 4 total). This is an exception to the octet rule: Be is stable with only 4 valence electrons.

**Lewis structure of \ce{BF3}**: Boron is central, with three single bonds to fluorine atoms. Boron has only 6 valence electrons — again an exception to the octet rule.

**Why \ce{BF3} is a Lewis acid**: The boron atom has an empty 2p orbital and only 6 electrons around it. It can accept a lone pair of electrons from a Lewis base (e.g., \ce{NH3} or \ce{F-}) to complete its octet, forming an adduct such as \ce{F3B–NH3}.

## Problem 11

**Lewis structure of \ce{SF6}**: Sulfur is central, with six S–F single bonds. Sulfur has 12 electrons in its valence shell (6 bonding pairs). All six fluorines have a full octet.

**Lewis structure of \ce{PF5}**: Phosphorus is central, with five P–F single bonds. Phosphorus has 10 electrons in its valence shell (5 bonding pairs).

**Why they can exceed the octet**: Sulfur and phosphorus are in Period 3, so they have accessible, low-energy 3d orbitals. These d orbitals can participate in bonding, allowing the central atom to accommodate more than 8 valence electrons. Elements in Period 2 (C, N, O, F) cannot exceed the octet because they lack accessible d orbitals.

## Problem 12

The azide ion \ce{N3-} has 16 valence electrons (3 × 5 + 1 = 16). Three significant resonance structures exist:

**Structure A**: \ce{^{-}N=N^{+}=N}

Formal charges: left N = −1 (5 − (4 + 2) = −1), middle N = +1 (5 − (0 + 4) = +1), right N = 0 (5 − (4 + 2) = −1... wait, let me recalculate properly).

Let's be systematic. Total valence electrons = 3 × 5 + 1 = 16.

**Structure A**: \ce{^{-}N=N^{+}=N}
- Left N: 2 bonding pairs (4 e⁻) + 4 non-bonding = 5 − (4 + 2) = −1
- Middle N: 4 bonding pairs (8 e⁻) + 0 non-bonding = 5 − (0 + 4) = +1
- Right N: 2 bonding pairs (4 e⁻) + 4 non-bonding = 5 − (4 + 2) = −1

Sum = (−1) + (+1) + (−1) = −1 ✓

**Structure B**: \ce{N≡N^{+}–N^{2-}} (similar but triple bond on left)

**Structure C**: mirror of B with triple bond on right.

The most significant contributors are those with the **smallest formal charges**. The structures with N≡N–N (where one terminal N has −2 and the middle has +1) have larger charge separation and contribute less. ## Problem 13

| Molecule | Electron domains | Lone pairs | Molecular geometry | Ideal bond angle | Actual bond angle |
|----------|:---:|:---:|----------|:---:|:---:|
| \ce{CH4} | 4 | 0 | Tetrahedral | 109.5° | ~109.5° |
| \ce{NH3} | 4 | 1 | Trigonal pyramidal | 109.5° | ~107° |
| \ce{H2O} | 4 | 2 | Bent (V-shaped) | 109.5° | ~104.5° |

**Trend explanation**: In VSEPR theory, the repulsion strength follows:
lone pair–lone pair > lone pair–bonding pair > bonding pair–bonding pair

\ce{CH4} has 4 bonding pairs, all equivalent, giving the ideal tetrahedral angle of 109.5°.

\ce{NH3} has 3 bonding pairs and 1 lone pair. The lone pair exerts greater repulsion on the bonding pairs, compressing the H–N–H angle to about 107°.

\ce{H2O} has 2 bonding pairs and 2 lone pairs. The two lone pairs repel each other strongly and compress the bonding pairs even further, reducing the H–O–H angle to about 104.5°.

## Problem 14

\ce{PF5} has 5 bonding pairs and 0 lone pairs on phosphorus → **trigonal bipyramidal** geometry.

**Bond angles**:
- Equatorial–equatorial F–P–F: **120°** (three equatorial fluorines in a plane)
- Axial–equatorial F–P–F: **90°** (each axial fluorine is at 90° to all three equatorial fluorines)
- Axial–axial: **180°** (the two axial fluorines are opposite each other)

**Why axial bonds are longer**: The axial positions experience greater electron-pair repulsion because each axial fluorine has three neighbouring equatorial fluorines at 90° (three 90° interactions), whereas an equatorial fluorine has only two axial neighbours at 90°. Greater repulsion in the axial positions lengthens the axial P–F bonds.

## Problem 16

**Polarity is determined by the vector sum of bond dipoles.**

(a) **\ce{CO2} — Non-polar.** Linear geometry. The two C=O bond dipoles are equal in magnitude and point in opposite directions → they cancel.

(b) **\ce{SO2} — Polar.** Bent geometry (≈119°). The two S=O bond dipoles do NOT cancel because the molecule is not linear. A net dipole exists, pointing along the bisector of the O–S–O angle toward the oxygen side.

(c) **\ce{BF3} — Non-polar.** Trigonal planar geometry. The three B–F bond dipoles are arranged symmetrically at 120°, and their vector sum is zero.

(d) **\ce{CH2Cl2} — Polar.** Tetrahedral geometry. The C–H and C–Cl bond dipoles are different in magnitude (Cl more electronegative than H). The dipoles do not cancel; there is a net dipole moment.

(e) **\ce{SF6} — Non-polar.** Octahedral geometry. All six S–F dipoles are equal and arranged symmetrically → they cancel perfectly.

## Problem 17

Both \ce{NH3} and \ce{NF3} are trigonal pyramidal. The difference in dipole moment arises from the **direction** of the bond dipoles relative to the lone-pair dipole.

In \ce{NH3}:
- N is more electronegative than H, so bond dipoles point **toward N** (N ← H).
- The lone pair on N also contributes a dipole pointing **away from N** (in the same direction as the N ← H bond dipoles).
- The bond dipoles and lone-pair dipole **reinforce** each other → net large dipole (1.47 D).

In \ce{NF3}:
- F is **more** electronegative than N, so bond dipoles point **toward F** (away from N, N → F).
- The lone-pair dipole points **away from N**.
- The bond dipoles and the lone-pair dipole point in **opposite** directions and largely **cancel** → net small dipole (0.24 D).

**Pitfall**: Students often assume both molecules have similar polarity because they have the same shape. The key is comparing the direction of bond polarities.

## Problem 19

\ce{HF} has an anomalously high boiling point because it can form **hydrogen bonds**. Fluorine is the most electronegative element, so the H–F bond is highly polarised. The partially positive H atom on one \ce{HF} molecule is strongly attracted to a lone pair on the F atom of a neighbouring \ce{HF} molecule.

In contrast, \ce{HCl}, \ce{HBr}, and \ce{HI} cannot form hydrogen bonds (Cl, Br, and I are less electronegative and their lone pairs are in larger, more diffuse orbitals that do not form strong H-bonds). Their boiling points increase down the group only because London (dispersion) forces increase with increasing molecular size and polarisability. The anomalously high boiling point of \ce{HF} is a classic piece of evidence for the existence and strength of hydrogen bonding.

## Problem 20

(a) **Diamond**:
- **Hardness**: Each carbon atom is sp³ hybridised and covalently bonded to four other carbon atoms in a rigid, tetrahedral three-dimensional network. All bonds are strong σ bonds. Breaking or deforming diamond requires breaking many covalent bonds, requiring immense energy.
- **Electrical conductivity**: All four valence electrons per carbon are localised in σ bonds. There are no delocalised electrons → insulator.

**Graphite**:
- **Softness**: Carbon atoms are sp² hybridised, arranged in planar hexagonal layers. Within each layer, C atoms are strongly covalently bonded (σ bonds + delocalised π bonds). Between layers, only weak London forces hold the layers together. Layers can slide over each other easily → graphite is soft and used as a lubricant.
- **Conductivity parallel to layers**: The p orbitals perpendicular to each layer overlap to form a delocalised π-electron cloud. These delocalised electrons are mobile within the plane → electrical conductor parallel to layers. Perpendicular to layers, there is no conduction pathway.

(b) **Graphene** (a single layer of graphite):
- **Conductivity**: The delocalised π electrons are free to move across the entire two-dimensional sheet → excellent conductor.
- **Strength**: The σ-bond framework of sp² carbon atoms is exceptionally strong; the C–C bonds in graphene are among the strongest known. Combined with the flawless two-dimensional lattice, graphene has extraordinary tensile strength.

## Problem 21

**Metallic bonding — electron-sea model**:
Metal atoms release their valence electrons into a delocalised "sea" or "gas" of electrons that pervades the entire metal lattice. The positively charged metal cations are arranged in a regular array and are held together by their electrostatic attraction to the sea of delocalised electrons.

**Malleability and ductility**: When a force is applied, the layers of metal cations can slide past one another. Because the electron sea is non-directional and surrounds all cations uniformly, the metallic bonding is maintained even as the ions shift position. The metal deforms rather than shattering (unlike ionic solids, where like-charged ions would repel if layers slide).

**Alloys are harder**: In an alloy such as brass (Cu with Zn atoms), the added atoms have a different size from the host atoms. These "impurity" atoms disrupt the regular layers of the metal lattice, making it more difficult for layers to slide past one another. The distortion of the lattice strengthens the metal — this is called solid-solution strengthening.

## Problem 22

**Ethene, \ce{C2H4}**:
- Each carbon: **sp²** hybridised (three σ bonds: two C–H + one C–C).
- Total σ bonds: 5 (1 C–C σ + 4 C–H σ). Total π bonds: 1 (the C=C π bond).

**Ethyne, \ce{C2H2}**:
- Each carbon: **sp** hybridised (two σ bonds: one C–H + one C–C).
- Total σ bonds: 3 (1 C–C σ + 2 C–H σ). Total π bonds: 2 (the C≡C has two π bonds).

**Bond length trend**: C–C (ethane, 154 pm, sp³) > C=C (ethene, 134 pm, sp²) > C≡C (ethyne, 120 pm, sp)

**Reason**: As the s-character of the hybrid orbital increases (sp³ = 25% s, sp² = 33% s, sp = 50% s), the orbital becomes more compact (higher s-character pulls electrons closer to the nucleus). Shorter, stronger bonds result. Additionally, multiple bonding (π overlap) draws the nuclei closer together.

## Problem 24

**IUPAC definition of a transition metal**: A transition metal is an element that has an atom with a **partially filled d subshell**, or that can form a cation with a partially filled d subshell.

**Zinc (Z = 30)**:
- Electron configuration: [Ar] 3d¹⁰ 4s²
- \ce{Zn^{2+}}: [Ar] 3d¹⁰

In both the atom and its common ion, the d subshell is completely full (d¹⁰). Since there are no partially filled d orbitals, zinc does **not** meet the IUPAC definition of a transition metal, even though it is located in the d-block.

**Contrast**: Scandium (Z = 21, [Ar] 3d¹ 4s²) is also sometimes debated because its only common ion \ce{Sc^{3+}} has [Ar] (no d electrons), but its atom has a partially filled d subshell. Copper (Z = 29) forms \ce{Cu^{2+}} with [Ar] 3d⁹, making it a true transition metal.

## Problem 25

**Crystal field theory explanation**:

In an octahedral complex, the five d orbitals split into two sets:
- **t₂g** (lower energy): d_xy, d_xz, d_yz
- **e_g** (higher energy): d_x²−y², d_z²

The energy gap between these sets is called the **crystal field splitting energy**, Δ (or Δ_oct).

In \ce{[Cu(H2O)6]^{2+}}, water is a weak-field ligand (low on the spectrochemical series), so Δ is relatively small. The complex absorbs light in the red/orange region (lower energy, longer wavelength), transmitting/complementing pale blue.

In \ce{[Cu(NH3)4(H2O)2]^{2+}}, ammonia is a stronger-field ligand (higher on the spectrochemical series: I⁻ < Br⁻ < Cl⁻ < F⁻ < OH⁻ < H₂O < **NH₃** < CN⁻). Ammonia causes a **larger** Δ. The absorbed light shifts to higher energy (shorter wavelength, yellow/orange region), and the transmitted/complementary colour is deep blue.

**Spectrochemical series** (partial): I⁻ < Br⁻ < SCN⁻ < Cl⁻ < NO₃⁻ < F⁻ < OH⁻ < H₂O < NCS⁻ < NH₃ < en < NO₂⁻ < CN⁻ < CO. Ligands to the left are weak-field (small Δ); ligands to the right are strong-field (large Δ).

## Problem 27

The nitrate ion has 24 valence electrons (5 from N + 3 × 6 from O + 1 for the negative charge = 24).

**Three resonance structures**:

Each structure has N as the central atom with one N=O double bond and two N–O⁻ single bonds. The double bond can be on any one of the three oxygen atoms.

**Formal charges** (in one representative structure, say with O(1) double-bonded):
- N: FC = 5 − (0 + ½ × 8) = 5 − 4 = **+1**
- O(1) (double-bonded): FC = 6 − (4 + ½ × 4) = 6 − 6 = **0**
- O(2) (single-bonded): FC = 6 − (6 + ½ × 2) = 6 − 7 = **−1**
- O(3) (single-bonded): FC = 6 − (6 + ½ × 2) = 6 − 7 = **−1**

Sum: (+1) + 0 + (−1) + (−1) = −1 ✓

**Resonance hybrid**: The three structures are equivalent. The hybrid implies that all three N–O bonds are identical — they have equal bond length and bond order of 1⅓ (4/3). The negative charge is delocalised equally over all three oxygen atoms, and the +1 charge on nitrogen is also partially delocalised through π bonding.
