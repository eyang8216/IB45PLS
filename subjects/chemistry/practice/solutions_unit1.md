## Problem 1
**Step 1: Write the formula for amount.** The amount of substance (in moles) is given by n = m / M, where m is mass and M is molar mass.

**Step 2: Calculate the molar mass of NaCl.**
M(NaCl) = 23.0 + 35.5 = 58.5 g mol⁻¹

**Step 3: Substitute and solve.**
n = 5.85 g / 58.5 g mol⁻¹ = **0.100 mol**

*Common pitfall:* Forgetting to add the atomic masses correctly. Always double-check the formula of the compound before calculating M.

## Problem 2
**Step 1: Recall the relationship between moles and number of particles.**
N = n × Nₐ, where Nₐ is Avogadro's number (6.02 × 10²³ mol⁻¹).

**Step 2: Substitute.**
N = 0.500 × Nₐ = **0.500 Nₐ molecules**

*Common pitfall:* Confusing "molecules" with "atoms." CO₂ has 0.500 Nₐ molecules but 3 × 0.500 Nₐ = 1.50 Nₐ atoms. Read the question carefully.

## Problem 3

n = m / M = 4.00 g / 4.00 g mol⁻¹ = 1.00 mol

N = n × L = 1.00 × 6.02 × 10²³ = **6.02 × 10²³ atoms**

## Problem 4
**Step 1: Assume a 100 g sample.** With percentages by mass, the simplest approach is to assume exactly 100 g of the compound. Then the masses are: 40.0 g C, 6.67 g H, 53.33 g O.

**Step 2: Convert each mass to moles.**
n(C) = 40.0 / 12.0 = 3.33 mol
n(H) = 6.67 / 1.00 = 6.67 mol
n(O) = 53.33 / 16.0 = 3.33 mol

**Step 3: Divide by the smallest value (3.33) to find the simplest ratio.**
C : H : O = 3.33/3.33 : 6.67/3.33 : 3.33/3.33 = 1 : 2 : 1

**Step 4: Write the empirical formula.** **CH₂O**

*Common pitfall:* Rounding too early. Keep intermediate values to sufficient precision, then round at the end. A ratio of 1 : 1.99 : 1 should be recognised as 1 : 2 : 1.

## Problem 5

## Problem 6
**Step 1: Calculate the mass of carbon from the mass of CO₂.**
All carbon in the CO₂ came from the hydrocarbon.
Mass fraction of C in CO₂ = 12.0 / 44.0 = 12/44 = 3/11
m(C) = (12.0 / 44.0) × 3.52 g = (3/11) × 3.52 = 0.960 g

**Step 2: Calculate the mass of hydrogen from the mass of H₂O.**
All hydrogen in the H₂O came from the hydrocarbon.
Mass fraction of H in H₂O = 2.00 / 18.0 = 1/9
m(H) = (2.00 / 18.0) × 2.16 g = (1/9) × 2.16 = 0.240 g

**Step 3: Verify the masses sum to the original sample mass.**
0.960 g + 0.240 g = 1.20 g ✓ (This confirms only C and H are present.)

**Step 4: Convert masses to moles.**
n(C) = 0.960 / 12.0 = 0.0800 mol
n(H) = 0.240 / 1.00 = 0.240 mol

**Step 5: Find the simplest ratio.**
C : H = 0.0800 : 0.240 = 1 : 3

**Step 6: Empirical formula = CH₃**

*Note:* CH₃ is the empirical formula of ethane (C₂H₆). The empirical formula shows the simplest ratio, not the actual molecule.

*Common pitfall:* Using the mass of CO₂ as the mass of carbon, or the mass of H₂O as the mass of hydrogen. Only a fraction of each combustion product is the element of interest.

## Problem 7
**Step 1: Determine how much O₂ is needed to react with all the H₂.**
From the equation: 2 mol H₂ reacts with 1 mol O₂.
So 2.50 mol H₂ would require (1/2) × 2.50 = 1.25 mol O₂.
But only 1.00 mol O₂ is available → O₂ is insufficient.

**Step 2: Determine how much H₂ is needed to react with all the O₂.**
1.00 mol O₂ requires 2 × 1.00 = 2.00 mol H₂.
2.50 mol H₂ is available, which exceeds 2.00 mol → H₂ is in excess.

**Step 3: The limiting reagent is O₂** (it runs out first).

**Step 4: Calculate the maximum amount of water.**
From the equation: 1 mol O₂ produces 2 mol H₂O.
n(H₂O) = 2 × 1.00 = **2.00 mol**

**Step 5: Check.** The H₂ consumed = 2.00 mol. Excess H₂ = 2.50 − 2.00 = 0.50 mol remains unreacted.

*Common pitfall:* Assuming the reactant present in the smaller amount (by moles) is always limiting. Here both reactants happen to be the same — but always check using the stoichiometric ratio.

## Problem 8
**Step 1: Calculate the amount of CaCO₃ used.**
M(CaCO₃) = 40.0 + 12.0 + 3(16.0) = 100 g mol⁻¹
n(CaCO₃) = 10.0 / 100 = 0.100 mol

**Step 2: Determine the theoretical amount and mass of CaO.**
From the 1:1 equation, n(CaO) = 0.100 mol.
M(CaO) = 40.0 + 16.0 = 56.0 g mol⁻¹
Theoretical m(CaO) = 0.100 × 56.0 = 5.60 g

**Step 3: Calculate percentage yield.**
% yield = (actual mass / theoretical mass) × 100%
% yield = (4.20 / 5.60) × 100% = 0.750 × 100% = **75.0%**

*Common pitfall:* Using the mass of reactant instead of product in the yield formula. Percentage yield always compares actual product to theoretical product.

## Problem 10
**Step 1: Use the dilution formula c₁V₁ = c₂V₂.**
c₁ = 2.00 mol dm⁻³, V₁ = 25.0 cm³, V₂ = 250 cm³.

**Step 2: Solve for c₂.**
c₂ = c₁V₁ / V₂ = (2.00 × 25.0) / 250 = 50.0 / 250 = **0.200 mol dm⁻³**

**Alternative method:** n = c₁V₁ = 2.00 × 0.0250 = 0.0500 mol. Then c₂ = n / V₂ = 0.0500 / 0.250 = 0.200 mol dm⁻³. Same result.

*Common pitfall:* Forgetting that c₁V₁ = c₂V₂ works regardless of units as long as both volumes use the same units. Here using cm³ throughout is valid.

## Problem 11
**Step 1: Calculate the total amount of HCl added.**
n(HCl total) = c × V = 0.200 × 0.0500 = 0.0100 mol

**Step 2: Calculate the amount of HCl that remained unreacted (excess).**
The excess HCl is neutralised by NaOH: HCl + NaOH → NaCl + H₂O (1:1).
n(NaOH) = 0.100 × 0.0200 = 0.00200 mol
Therefore n(HCl excess) = 0.00200 mol.

**Step 3: Calculate the amount of HCl that reacted with CaCO₃.**
n(HCl reacted) = n(HCl total) − n(HCl excess) = 0.0100 − 0.00200 = 0.00800 mol

**Step 4: Relate HCl to CaCO₃ using the stoichiometric ratio.**
CaCO₃ + 2 HCl → CaCl₂ + H₂O + CO₂
2 mol HCl react with 1 mol CaCO₃.
n(CaCO₃) = n(HCl reacted) / 2 = 0.00800 / 2 = 0.00400 mol

**Step 5: Calculate the mass of CaCO₃.**
M(CaCO₃) = 40.0 + 12.0 + 48.0 = 100 g mol⁻¹
m(CaCO₃) = 0.00400 × 100 = **0.400 g**

*Common pitfall:* Forgetting the 2:1 stoichiometric ratio between HCl and CaCO₃. This is the most frequent error in back-titration problems. Also, confusing "total HCl" with "reacted HCl."

## Problem 13
**Step 1: Recall the ideal gas equation.** PV = nRT

**Step 2: Rearrange to solve for V.** V = nRT / P

**Step 3: Ensure consistent units.**
n = 0.100 mol
R = 8.31 J K⁻¹ mol⁻¹ (which is equivalent to Pa m³ K⁻¹ mol⁻¹)
T = 273 K
P = 100 kPa = 100 000 Pa (must use Pa to match R)

**Step 4: Substitute and calculate V in m³.**
V = (0.100 × 8.31 × 273) / 100 000 = 226.9 / 100 000 = 2.269 × 10⁻³ m³

**Step 5: Convert to dm³.** 1 m³ = 1000 dm³, so V = 2.27 dm³.

**Step 6: Verify.** At STP, molar volume = 22.7 dm³ mol⁻¹. For 0.100 mol, V = 0.100 × 22.7 = 2.27 dm³. This matches. ✓

*Common pitfall:* Using kPa directly in PV = nRT without converting to Pa. R = 8.31 uses SI units, so P must be in Pa and V in m³.

## Problem 14
**Step 1: Calculate the amount of each reactant.**
n(Al) = 2.70 / 27.0 = 0.100 mol
n(HCl) = c × V = 1.00 × 0.100 = 0.100 mol

**Step 2: Determine the limiting reagent using the stoichiometric ratio.**
From the equation: 2 Al + 6 HCl → 2 AlCl₃ + 3 H₂. The ratio Al : HCl = 2 : 6 = 1 : 3.
0.100 mol Al would need 3 × 0.100 = 0.300 mol HCl.
Available HCl is only 0.100 mol → **HCl is the limiting reagent.**

**Step 3: Calculate the amount of H₂ from the limiting reagent.**
From the equation: 6 mol HCl produce 3 mol H₂. Ratio HCl : H₂ = 2 : 1.
n(H₂) = (3/6) × n(HCl) = (1/2) × 0.100 = 0.0500 mol

**Step 4: Calculate the volume of H₂ at STP.**
V(H₂) = n × Vₘ = 0.0500 × 22.7 = **1.14 dm³** (or 1.135 dm³, or approximately 1.1 dm³)

*Common pitfall:* Using the Al amount to calculate H₂ without first identifying the limiting reagent. Had we done that, we would incorrectly get n(H₂) = (3/2) × 0.100 = 0.150 mol and V = 3.41 dm³.

## Problem 16
The **empirical formula** gives the simplest whole-number ratio of atoms of each element in a compound. It is determined experimentally from percentage composition or combustion data. The **molecular formula** gives the actual number of atoms of each element in one molecule of the compound. It is always an integer multiple of the empirical formula: molecular formula = (empirical formula)ₙ, where n = molar mass / empirical formula mass.

**Example:** Ethanoic acid (CH₃COOH, molecular formula C₂H₄O₂) and glucose (C₆H₁₂O₆) both have the same empirical formula CH₂O. Another classic example is ethyne (C₂H₂) and benzene (C₆H₆), which both have the empirical formula CH.

*Common pitfall:* Thinking the empirical formula is always different from the molecular formula. For many compounds (e.g., H₂O, CO₂, NH₃), they are identical because the simplest ratio is already the molecular formula.

## Problem 17
Real gases deviate from ideal gas behaviour at **high pressures** and **low temperatures** because two key assumptions of the kinetic molecular theory break down:

1. **The assumption that gas particles have negligible volume.** At high pressures, gas particles are forced close together. The volume occupied by the particles themselves is no longer negligible compared to the total volume of the container. The "available" volume is less than the container volume, so PV is smaller than predicted by the ideal gas law.

2. **The assumption that there are no intermolecular forces between gas particles.** At low temperatures, particles have less kinetic energy and move more slowly. Attractive intermolecular forces (especially van der Waals forces) become significant. These attractions pull particles together, reducing the frequency and force of collisions with the container walls, so the measured pressure is lower than ideal predictions.

*Common pitfall:* Stating only "particles have volume" without explaining why this matters only at high pressure (when the ratio of particle volume to container volume becomes significant).

---

## Problem 18
A real gas behaves most ideally under conditions of **high temperature** and **low pressure**.

- **High temperature:** Particles have high kinetic energy, so any intermolecular attractive forces are negligible by comparison. The particles overcome weak attractions easily, and the assumption of no intermolecular forces holds.
- **Low pressure:** Particles are far apart on average. The volume occupied by the particles themselves is negligible compared to the total volume, and intermolecular forces are minimal because particles rarely come close enough to interact.

Under these conditions, the gas approaches the behaviour predicted by PV = nRT.

*Common pitfall:* Confusing the direction — high pressure forces particles together (bad for ideality), and low temperature makes attractions significant (also bad). The ideal conditions are the opposite extremes.

## Problem 19
The **limiting reagent** is the reactant that is completely consumed first during a chemical reaction. Once it is used up, the reaction stops and no further product can form, regardless of how much of the other reactants remain. The limiting reagent therefore determines the **maximum theoretical yield** of product.

**Analogy:** Imagine building bicycles. Each bicycle requires 2 wheels and 1 frame. If you have 10 wheels and 8 frames, you can build at most 5 bicycles because wheels run out first (10 wheels ÷ 2 per bicycle = 5). The frames are in excess (8 frames, but only 5 are needed). The wheels are the limiting reagent. Even though you have more wheels than frames by count, the stoichiometric requirement makes wheels limiting.

In chemistry, the same principle applies: you must compare mole ratios, not just masses or initial mole counts. The reactant that produces the least amount of product (when accounting for the stoichiometric coefficient) is the limiting reagent.

## Problem 20
The statement is **correct only for ideal gases**. This is a statement of **Avogadro's law**: equal volumes of all ideal gases, at the same temperature and pressure, contain the same number of particles (and therefore the same number of moles).

**Assumptions required:**
1. The gas particles have negligible volume compared to the container volume.
2. There are no attractive or repulsive forces between gas particles.

Under these ideal assumptions, the volume occupied by a gas depends only on n, T, and P (V = nRT/P), and not on the identity of the gas. At STP, one mole of any ideal gas occupies 22.7 dm³.

Real gases approximate this behaviour at low pressure and high temperature. At very high pressures or low temperatures, different gases deviate by different amounts because their particle volumes and intermolecular forces differ.

## Problem 21
**Part A: Finding the concentration of NaOH**

**Step 1:** Calculate the amount of H₂SO₄ used.
n(H₂SO₄) = c × V = 0.100 × 0.0250 = 0.00250 mol

**Step 2:** Determine the amount of NaOH from the stoichiometric ratio.
2 NaOH + H₂SO₄ → Na₂SO₄ + 2 H₂O. Ratio NaOH : H₂SO₄ = 2 : 1.
n(NaOH) = 2 × 0.00250 = 0.00500 mol

**Step 3:** Calculate the concentration of NaOH.
c(NaOH) = n / V = 0.00500 / 0.0250 = **0.200 mol dm⁻³**

**Part B: Preparing the diluted solution**

**Step 4:** Use the dilution formula c₁V₁ = c₂V₂.
c₁ = 0.200 mol dm⁻³ (original solution)
c₂ = 0.0500 mol dm⁻³ (desired concentration)
V₂ = 100 cm³ (desired final volume)

V₁ = c₂V₂ / c₁ = (0.0500 × 100) / 0.200 = 5.00 / 0.200 = 25.0 cm³

**Step 5: Procedure.** Using a 25.0 cm³ pipette, transfer exactly 25.0 cm³ of the original 0.200 mol dm⁻³ NaOH solution into a 100 cm³ volumetric flask. Add distilled water to bring the volume up to the calibration mark, stopper, and invert several times to mix thoroughly. The resulting solution has a concentration of 0.0500 mol dm⁻³.

*Common pitfall:* Forgetting the 2:1 stoichiometric ratio in the titration. NaOH and H₂SO₄ do NOT react 1:1 — sulfuric acid is diprotic.

## Problem 22
**Step 1: Calculate the mass of carbon in the sample.**
All C ends up in CO₂. Mass fraction of C in CO₂ = 12.0/44.0.
m(C) = (12.0/44.0) × 0.880 g = (3/11) × 0.880 = 0.240 g

**Step 2: Calculate the mass of hydrogen in the sample.**
All H ends up in H₂O. Mass fraction of H in H₂O = 2.00/18.0 = 1/9.
m(H) = (2.00/18.0) × 0.360 g = (1/9) × 0.360 = 0.0400 g

**Step 3: Calculate the mass of oxygen by difference.**
m(O) = total mass − m(C) − m(H) = 0.600 − 0.240 − 0.0400 = 0.320 g

**Step 4: Convert masses to moles.**
n(C) = 0.240 / 12.0 = 0.0200 mol
n(H) = 0.0400 / 1.00 = 0.0400 mol
n(O) = 0.320 / 16.0 = 0.0200 mol

**Step 5: Find the simplest ratio.**
C : H : O = 0.0200 : 0.0400 : 0.0200 = 1 : 2 : 1
**Empirical formula = CH₂O**

**Step 6: Determine the molecular formula.**
M(empirical) = 12.0 + 2.00 + 16.0 = 30.0 g mol⁻¹
n = M(molecular) / M(empirical) = 60.0 / 30.0 = 2
Molecular formula = C₂H₄O₂

*Common pitfall:* Forgetting to calculate oxygen by difference when the compound contains oxygen. The oxygen in the combustion products comes from both the compound and the O₂ used for combustion — you cannot determine oxygen in the compound directly from the products.

## Problem 23
**Step 1: Calculate the amount of each reactant.**
n(BaCl₂) = c × V = 0.400 × 0.0100 = 0.00400 mol
n(Na₂SO₄) = c × V = 0.200 × 0.0200 = 0.00400 mol

**Step 2: Identify the limiting reagent.**
The equation shows a 1:1 ratio: BaCl₂ + Na₂SO₄ → BaSO₄ + 2 NaCl.
Both reactants have 0.00400 mol, so neither is in excess — both are fully consumed. Both are limiting (the mixture is stoichiometric).

**Step 3: Calculate the amount of BaSO₄ formed.**
n(BaSO₄) = n(BaCl₂) = n(Na₂SO₄) = 0.00400 mol

**Step 4: Calculate the molar mass of BaSO₄.**
M(BaSO₄) = 137 + 32.0 + 4(16.0) = 137 + 32.0 + 64.0 = 233 g mol⁻¹

**Step 5: Calculate the mass of precipitate.**
m(BaSO₄) = n × M = 0.00400 × 233 = **0.932 g**

*Common pitfall:* Using the volume units incorrectly. 10.0 cm³ = 0.0100 dm³ (divide by 1000). Also, noting that NaCl remains in solution — it does not contribute to the mass of precipitate.
