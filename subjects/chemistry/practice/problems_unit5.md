# Unit 5: Chemical Kinetics — Practice Problems (HL)

## Problem 1
The reaction between aqueous bromine and methanoic acid is monitored by measuring the volume of carbon dioxide gas evolved over time: \ce{Br2(aq) + HCOOH(aq) -> 2Br-(aq) + 2H+(aq) + CO2(g)}. A student collects 48 cm³ of \ce{CO2} gas in the first 80 seconds of the reaction at room temperature and pressure. Calculate the average rate of reaction in cm³ s⁻¹ over this time interval. Explain why the instantaneous rate at t = 60 s is likely to be lower than this average rate.

## Problem 2
A student investigates the rate of the reaction between marble chips (calcium carbonate) and excess hydrochloric acid: \ce{CaCO3(s) + 2HCl(aq) -> CaCl2(aq) + H2O(l) + CO2(g)}. The student measures the mass of the reaction flask (including its contents) every 30 seconds and records a mass loss of 2.64 g in the first 3.0 minutes. Calculate the average rate of reaction in mol s⁻¹ of \ce{CO2} produced. (Molar mass of \ce{CO2} = 44.0 g mol⁻¹.) Suggest one reason why this method might give less accurate results if the flask is not stoppered with cotton wool.

## Problem 3
For the decomposition of hydrogen peroxide, \ce{2H2O2(aq) -> 2H2O(l) + O2(g)}, the rate of formation of oxygen gas is measured as 0.012 mol dm⁻³ s⁻¹ at a particular instant. Calculate the rate of consumption of \ce{H2O2} at the same instant. Write the rate expression that relates these two rates using the stoichiometric coefficients from the balanced equation.

## Problem 4
The reaction \ce{2HI(g) -> H2(g) + I2(g)} has an activation energy of 183 kJ mol⁻¹. Using collision theory, explain why only a very small fraction of collisions between \ce{HI} molecules result in a reaction at 300 °C. In your answer, state the two conditions that must be satisfied for a collision to be effective.

## Problem 5
Two reactions, A and B, have activation energies of 50 kJ mol⁻¹ and 150 kJ mol⁻¹ respectively. Both occur at the same temperature and have similar collision frequencies. Explain, using collision theory, which reaction will have the larger rate constant. State the additional factor, beyond energy and collision frequency, that determines whether a collision is effective.

## Problem 6
Consider the reaction between zinc granules and dilute sulfuric acid: \ce{Zn(s) + H2SO4(aq) -> ZnSO4(aq) + H2(g)}. Describe and explain the effect on the rate of reaction of: (a) using zinc powder instead of zinc granules of the same mass, and (b) using 2.0 mol dm⁻³ sulfuric acid instead of 1.0 mol dm⁻³ sulfuric acid. In each case, explain your reasoning with reference to collision theory.

## Problem 7
The reaction \ce{2NO(g) + O2(g) -> 2NO2(g)} is carried out in a sealed container. Explain, using collision theory, why increasing the pressure of the system by reducing the volume increases the rate of this reaction. State clearly how the frequency of collisions between \ce{NO} and \ce{O2} molecules is affected.

## Problem 8
A reaction at 25 °C has a rate of 1.0 × 10⁻³ mol dm⁻³ s⁻¹. When the temperature is raised to 35 °C, the rate approximately doubles. With reference to the Maxwell–Boltzmann distribution curve, explain why a relatively small increase in temperature of only 10 °C causes such a large increase in reaction rate. In your explanation, describe what happens to the shape of the curve and the area under the curve beyond the activation energy.

## Problem 9
The decomposition of ammonia on a tungsten surface follows zero-order kinetics: \ce{2NH3(g) -> N2(g) + 3H2(g)}. The half-life of the reaction decreases as the initial concentration of ammonia increases. Explain why this observation is characteristic of a zero-order reaction. Sketch the shape of a concentration–time graph for this decomposition, labelling both axes.

## Problem 10
The reaction \ce{2SO2(g) + O2(g) -> 2SO3(g)} is industrially catalysed by vanadium(V) oxide. On a single set of axes, sketch the Maxwell–Boltzmann distribution curve for the reactant molecules at a given temperature, and superimpose a second curve showing how the distribution changes when a catalyst is present. Explain clearly why the catalyst increases the rate even though it does not change the shape of the Maxwell–Boltzmann distribution curve itself.

## Problem 11
A student is asked to determine the order with respect to \ce{H2O2} for the iodine clock reaction. The student carries out three experiments at the same temperature, varying the initial concentration of \ce{H2O2} while keeping all other concentrations constant. The initial rates are measured. The data are shown in the table below.

| Experiment | [\ce{H2O2}] / mol dm⁻³ | Initial rate / mol dm⁻³ s⁻¹ |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | 0.10 | 2.0 × 10⁻⁴ |
| 2 | 0.20 | 4.0 × 10⁻⁴ |
| 3 | 0.40 | 8.0 × 10⁻⁴ |

Determine the order of reaction with respect to \ce{H2O2}. Write the rate law for the reaction. Hence calculate the value of the rate constant, k, and state its units.

## Problem 12
The reaction \ce{A + B -> C} is studied using the method of initial rates at a fixed temperature. The following data are obtained.

| Experiment | [A] / mol dm⁻³ | [B] / mol dm⁻³ | Initial rate / mol dm⁻³ s⁻¹ |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | 0.10 | 0.10 | 1.0 × 10⁻³ |
| 2 | 0.20 | 0.10 | 4.0 × 10⁻³ |
| 3 | 0.10 | 0.20 | 1.0 × 10⁻³ |

Determine the order of reaction with respect to A and with respect to B. State the overall order of the reaction. Write the rate law and calculate the value of k, including its units.

## Problem 13
A reaction is found to be second order overall. The rate constant k has a value of 0.25 dm³ mol⁻¹ s⁻¹ at a particular temperature. Deduce the units of k for a reaction that is: (a) zero order overall, and (b) first order overall. Explain why the units of k depend on the overall order of the reaction.

## Problem 14
The decomposition of dinitrogen pentoxide in carbon tetrachloride solution follows first-order kinetics: \ce{2N2O5 -> 4NO2 + O2}. The concentration of \ce{N2O5} is measured at regular time intervals.

| Time / min | [\ce{N2O5}] / mol dm⁻³ |
|---|---|
| 0 | 0.200 |
| 10 | 0.100 |
| 20 | 0.050 |
| 30 | 0.025 |

Explain, with reasoning, how these data confirm that the reaction is first order with respect to \ce{N2O5}. What would the concentration–time data look like if the reaction were zero order instead?

## Problem 15
For a certain reaction, a graph of rate (y-axis) against concentration of reactant R (x-axis) produces a horizontal straight line. A graph of concentration of R (y-axis) against time (x-axis) produces a straight line with a negative gradient. Deduce the order of reaction with respect to R. Sketch both graphs clearly, labelling the axes. Explain the reasoning behind your deduction.

## Problem 16
The reaction \ce{2X + Y -> Z} is thought to proceed by the following two-step mechanism.

Step 1: \ce{X + Y -> W} (slow)
Step 2: \ce{W + X -> Z} (fast)

Identify the rate-determining step. State the molecularity of each elementary step. Predict the rate law for the overall reaction from this mechanism, explaining your reasoning.

## Problem 17
The reaction between nitrogen monoxide and hydrogen, \ce{2NO(g) + 2H2(g) -> N2(g) + 2H2O(g)}, has the experimentally determined rate law: rate = k[\ce{NO}]²[\ce{H2}]. A proposed mechanism is:

Step 1: \ce{2NO <=> N2O2} (fast, equilibrium)
Step 2: \ce{N2O2 + H2 -> N2O + H2O} (slow)
Step 3: \ce{N2O + H2 -> N2 + H2O} (fast)

Show, using the rate-determining step and the equilibrium of Step 1, that this mechanism is consistent with the experimental rate law.

## Problem 18
The activation energy for a certain reaction is 75.0 kJ mol⁻¹. At 298 K, the rate constant k₁ is 2.0 × 10⁻³ s⁻¹. Use the Arrhenius equation in its two-point form to calculate the rate constant k₂ at 308 K. (Gas constant R = 8.31 J K⁻¹ mol⁻¹.) Comment on whether your answer is consistent with the general rule that reaction rates approximately double for a 10 °C temperature rise.

## Problem 19
The rate constant for a reaction is measured at four different temperatures. The data are shown below.

| Temperature T / K | k / s⁻¹ | 1/T / K⁻¹ | ln k |
|---|---|---|---|
| 290 | 1.5 × 10⁻⁴ | 3.45 × 10⁻³ | −8.80 |
| 300 | 4.5 × 10⁻⁴ | 3.33 × 10⁻³ | −7.71 |
| 310 | 1.3 × 10⁻³ | 3.23 × 10⁻³ | −6.65 |
| 320 | 3.4 × 10⁻³ | 3.13 × 10⁻³ | −5.68 |

A graph of ln k (y-axis) against 1/T (x-axis) yields a straight line with a gradient of −8.75 × 10³ K. Calculate the activation energy, Ea, of this reaction in kJ mol⁻¹ using the Arrhenius equation in the form ln k = ln A − Ea/RT. (R = 8.31 J K⁻¹ mol⁻¹.)

## Problem 20
The reaction \ce{CH3CHO(g) -> CH4(g) + CO(g)} follows the rate law rate = k[\ce{CH3CHO}]^(3/2). Determine the overall order of this reaction. State the units of the rate constant k for this reaction. Could this reaction be an elementary step? Explain your answer with reference to molecularity.

## Problem 21

Predict the initial rate if the concentration of \ce{P} is doubled to 0.20 mol dm⁻³ and the concentration of \ce{Q} is halved to 0.10 mol dm⁻³, with all other conditions unchanged. Show your reasoning clearly.

## Problem 22

## Problem 23

The following graph shapes are considered: (I) [\ce{HI}] vs time is a curve that becomes less steep over time; (II) ln[\ce{HI}] vs time is a straight line with negative gradient; (III) 1/[\ce{HI}] vs time is a curve. Deduce the order of reaction with respect to \ce{HI}. Explain what each graph tells you and why only one of them being linear is the key to determining the order.

## Problem 24
