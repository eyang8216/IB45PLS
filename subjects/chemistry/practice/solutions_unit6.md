# Unit 6: Chemical Equilibrium — Solutions (HL)

## Problem 1

**Step 1 — Define dynamic equilibrium.**
Dynamic equilibrium is the state in a closed chemical system where the forward reaction and the reverse reaction proceed at exactly the same rate. The concentrations of all reactants and products remain constant over time, but at the molecular level, both reactions continue.

**Step 2 — Two constant macroscopic properties.**
Two properties that remain constant are:
1. The concentration (or partial pressure) of each species.
2. The colour of the mixture (if any species is coloured), or more generally, any intensive property such as density or refractive index.

**Step 3 — Molecular-level explanation.**
At the molecular level, reactant molecules continue to collide and form products, while product molecules simultaneously decompose back into reactants. Because the rates are equal, for every molecule of product formed per unit time, one molecule of product decomposes back. The net change in concentration is zero, so macroscopic properties appear static even though molecular events are ongoing.

*Pitfall: Students often describe equilibrium as "the reaction has stopped." It has not stopped — forward and reverse reactions continue at equal rates.*

## Problem 2

**Step 1 — Write the Kc expression.**
For \ce{2SO2(g) + O2(g) <=> 2SO3(g)}:
Kc = [\ce{SO3}]² / ([\ce{SO2}]²[\ce{O2}]).

**Step 2 — Calculate equilibrium concentrations.**
Volume = 2.0 dm³.
[\ce{SO2}] = 0.40 mol / 2.0 dm³ = **0.20 mol dm⁻³**.
[\ce{O2}] = 0.20 mol / 2.0 dm³ = **0.10 mol dm⁻³**.
[\ce{SO3}] = 0.60 mol / 2.0 dm³ = **0.30 mol dm⁻³**.

**Step 3 — Calculate Kc.**
Kc = (0.30)² / ((0.20)² × 0.10) = 0.090 / (0.040 × 0.10) = 0.090 / 0.0040 = **22.5**.

**Step 4 — Determine units.**
Kc units = (mol dm⁻³)² / ((mol dm⁻³)² × mol dm⁻³) = (mol dm⁻³)² / (mol³ dm⁻⁹) = mol⁻¹ dm³.
So Kc = **22.5 dm³ mol⁻¹**.

## Problem 3

**Step 1 — Write the expression for Q (and Kc).**
Kc = [\ce{HI}]² / ([\ce{H2}][\ce{I2}]).

**Step 2 — Calculate Q.**
Q = (1.50)² / (0.40 × 0.10) = 2.25 / 0.040 = **56.25**.

**Step 3 — Compare Q with Kc.**
Q = 56.25, Kc = 50.0. Since **Q > Kc**, the ratio of products to reactants is currently higher than it would be at equilibrium.

**Step 4 — Predict the direction.**
When Q > Kc, the reaction must shift to reduce the product concentration and increase reactant concentrations to reach equilibrium. The reaction proceeds in the **reverse direction** (right-to-left): \ce{2HI -> H2 + I2}.

*Pitfall: Students sometimes confuse the direction — remember: Q > Kc means "too many products," so shift left; Q < Kc means "too few products," so shift right.*

## Problem 4

**Step 1 — Set up the ICE table.**
Let V be the volume in dm³. Initial concentrations: [CH₃COOH]₀ = 2.0/V, [C₂H₅OH]₀ = 2.0/V, [ester]₀ = 0, [H₂O]₀ = 0.

| | CH₃COOH | C₂H₅OH | CH₃COOC₂H₅ | H₂O |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Initial | 2.0/V | 2.0/V | 0 | 0 |
| Change | −x | −x | +x | +x |
| Equil. | 2.0/V − x | 2.0/V − x | x | x |

**Step 2 — Write Kc and substitute.**
Kc = [ester][H₂O] / ([CH₃COOH][C₂H₅OH]) = x² / ((2.0/V − x)(2.0/V − x)) = 4.0.

**Step 3 — Solve for x.**
x² / (2.0/V − x)² = 4.0.
Take the square root of both sides: x / (2.0/V − x) = 2.0 (positive root only).
x = 2.0 × (2.0/V − x) → x = 4.0/V − 2x → 3x = 4.0/V → **x = 4.0/(3V)**.

**Step 4 — State the ester concentration.**
[\ce{CH3COOC2H5}]_eq = x = **4.0/(3V) mol dm⁻³**.

*Pitfall: Because the stoichiometric coefficients are all 1 and Kc is a perfect square, taking the square root simplifies the algebra — a common IB shortcut.*

## Problem 5

**Step 1 — Set up the ICE table.**
Volume = 2.0 dm³. Initial concentrations: [N₂]₀ = 0.50, [H₂]₀ = 1.50, [NH₃]₀ = 0.

| | N₂ | 3H₂ | 2NH₃ |
|---|---|---|---|
| Initial | 0.50 | 1.50 | 0 |
| Change | −x | −3x | +2x |
| Equil. | 0.50 − x | 1.50 − 3x | 2x |

**Step 2 — Write Kc.**
Kc = [NH₃]² / ([N₂][H₂]³) = (2x)² / ((0.50 − x)(1.50 − 3x)³) = 0.50.

**Step 3 — Simplify using the stoichiometric ratio.**
Because [H₂]₀ = 3[N₂]₀ and the changes maintain this ratio, [H₂] = 3[N₂] at all times. So let y = [N₂] = 0.50 − x.
Then [H₂] = 3y = 1.50 − 3x and [NH₃] = 2x = 2(0.50 − y) = 1.00 − 2y.

Kc = (1.00 − 2y)² / (y × (3y)³) = (1.00 − 2y)² / (27y⁴) = 0.50.
(1.00 − 2y)² = 13.5y⁴.

Taking the positive square root: 1.00 − 2y = 3.674y².
Rearranging: 3.674y² + 2y − 1.00 = 0.
Solving the quadratic: y = 0.316 mol dm⁻³.

**Step 4 — Calculate [NH₃].**
x = 0.50 − y = 0.50 − 0.316 = 0.184.
[\ce{NH3}]_eq = 2x = **0.37 mol dm⁻³** (2 s.f.).

## Problem 6

**Step 1 — Set up the ICE table.**
Volume = 1.0 dm³. Initial: [PCl₅]₀ = 0.50, [PCl₃]₀ = 0, [Cl₂]₀ = 0.

| | PCl₅ | PCl₃ | Cl₂ |
|---|---|---|---|
| Initial | 0.50 | 0 | 0 |
| Change | −x | +x | +x |
| Equil. | 0.50 − x | x | x |

**Step 2 — Write Kc and substitute.**
Kc = [PCl₃][Cl₂] / [PCl₅] = x² / (0.50 − x) = 0.040.

**Step 3 — Apply the small-x approximation.**
Since Kc is small (0.040), x is expected to be small relative to 0.50.
Assume 0.50 − x ≈ 0.50. Then:
x² / 0.50 = 0.040 → x² = 0.020 → x = **0.141 mol dm⁻³**.

**Step 4 — Verify the approximation.**
(0.50 − 0.141) / 0.50 = 0.718, which is a 28% difference — not truly "small" by the usual <5% criterion. The approximation is marginal here, but it gives a reasonable estimate. For a more precise answer, solve x² + 0.040x − 0.020 = 0: x = 0.122 mol dm⁻³. Using the approximation: [PCl₅] ≈ 0.36, [PCl₃] = [Cl₂] = 0.14 mol dm⁻³. Exact solution: [PCl₅] = 0.378, [PCl₃] = [Cl₂] = 0.122 mol dm⁻³.

*Pitfall: Always check the validity of the small-x approximation. If x > 5% of the initial concentration, the quadratic should be solved exactly.*

## Problem 7

**(a) Increasing pressure by reducing volume.**
The forward reaction produces 2 moles of gas from 4 moles of gas (N₂ + 3H₂ → 2NH₃). According to Le Chatelier's principle, increasing pressure favours the side with fewer gas molecules. The equilibrium position shifts **to the right**, producing more NH₃. The value of Kc does **not** change (only temperature changes Kc).

**(b) Raising the temperature.**
The forward reaction is exothermic (ΔH < 0). Increasing temperature adds heat to the system. Le Chatelier's principle states the equilibrium shifts to absorb the added heat, favouring the endothermic direction — the reverse reaction. The equilibrium position shifts **to the left**, decomposing NH₃ into N₂ and H₂. Crucially, Kc **decreases** because the equilibrium now favours reactants.

**(c) Adding more nitrogen gas.**
Adding N₂ increases its concentration. The system responds by consuming the added N₂, shifting the equilibrium position **to the right** to produce more NH₃. Kc remains **unchanged** — after re-equilibration, the ratio [NH₃]²/([N₂][H₂]³) returns to the same numerical value.

**(d) Adding an iron catalyst.**
A catalyst lowers the activation energy for both the forward and reverse reactions equally. It speeds up both directions by the same factor. The equilibrium position is **unchanged**, and Kc is **unchanged**. The system simply reaches equilibrium faster.

## Problem 8

**(a) Temperature increase.**
The forward reaction is exothermic (ΔH = −90 kJ mol⁻¹). Heating the system adds energy, and the equilibrium shifts to favour the endothermic (reverse) direction to absorb this energy. The equilibrium position shifts **left** (towards CO and H₂). Since Kc depends on temperature — increasing for endothermic reactions and decreasing for exothermic ones — the value of Kc **decreases**.

**(b) Adding a catalyst.**
A catalyst provides an alternative pathway with lower activation energy, accelerating both forward and reverse reactions equally. The equilibrium position is **unaffected** and the equilibrium composition remains the same. The value of Kc is **unchanged**. Only the time taken to reach equilibrium is reduced.

*Pitfall: The most common mistake is saying a catalyst "favours the forward reaction." It favours neither direction.*

## Problem 9

**(a) Placed in ice water (cooling).**
The forward reaction 2NO₂ → N₂O₄ is exothermic. Cooling removes heat from the system. By Le Chatelier's principle, the equilibrium shifts to produce heat — favouring the exothermic forward reaction. More brown NO₂ is converted to colourless N₂O₄. The mixture becomes **paler (less brown)**.

**(b) Placed in hot water (heating).**
Heating adds heat. The equilibrium shifts to absorb heat by favouring the endothermic reverse reaction. Colourless N₂O₄ decomposes to brown NO₂. The mixture becomes **darker brown**.

**(c) Sign of ΔH.**
The observations confirm the forward reaction is **exothermic** (ΔH < 0). If the forward reaction were endothermic, heating would shift equilibrium right (darker with heating, paler with cooling) — the opposite of what is described in the problem.

## Problem 10

**(a) Adding inert gas (argon) at constant volume.**
The total pressure increases, but the partial pressures of CO₂ and CO are unchanged because the volume is constant and the amounts of reacting gases are unchanged. Since Kc depends on concentrations (which are unchanged), **Kc is unchanged** and the equilibrium position is unaffected. (Adding inert gas at constant *pressure*, however, would increase volume and shift the equilibrium.)

**(b) Adding more solid carbon.**
The concentration of a pure solid is constant (it depends only on density, not on the amount present) and is incorporated into the equilibrium constant. Adding more C(s) does **not** change [C(s)] and therefore does not affect the equilibrium position. **Kc is unchanged.**

**(c) Increasing the temperature.**
The forward reaction is endothermic (ΔH > 0). Increasing temperature favours the endothermic direction. The equilibrium shifts **right**, producing more CO. Since Kc increases with temperature for endothermic reactions, **Kc increases**.

## Problem 11

**Step 1 — Interpret the magnitude of Kc.**
Kc = 1.0 × 10⁶ is a very large number. Kc = [products]/[reactants], so a large Kc means the equilibrium ratio heavily favours products. At equilibrium, the mixture consists almost entirely of C and D, with only trace amounts of A and B remaining.

**Step 2 — Relationship to rates.**
At equilibrium, the forward rate equals the reverse rate. A large Kc does **not** mean the forward reaction is fast — it means the forward rate constant k_f is much larger than the reverse rate constant k_r (since Kc = k_f/k_r). The reaction could still be very slow (high Ea) but heavily product-favoured at equilibrium.

**Step 3 — Key insight.**
Kc magnitude indicates thermodynamic favourability (position), not kinetic favourability (speed). A reaction with Kc = 10⁶ could take years to reach equilibrium if the activation energy is high.

## Problem 13

**Step 1 — Calculate Q.**
Q = [\ce{HBr}]² / ([\ce{H2}][\ce{Br2}]) = (0.10)² / (0.020 × 0.030) = 0.010 / 0.00060 = **16.7**.

**Step 2 — Compare with Kc.**
Kc = 1.0 × 10⁹. Q (16.7) is vastly smaller than Kc (10⁹). Q < Kc means the product/reactant ratio is too small — the reaction must proceed in the **forward direction** to produce more HBr.

**Step 3 — Why a reaction with large Kc can go in reverse.**
If the initial mixture contains an unusually high concentration of products relative to reactants (e.g., [HBr] is very large and [H₂] and [Br₂] are near zero), Q could exceed Kc even if Kc is enormous. In that scenario, the reaction would proceed in reverse. The direction always depends on Q relative to Kc, not on Kc alone.

## Problem 14

**Step 1 — Set up the ICE table.**
Volume = 1.0 dm³. Initial: [HI]₀ = 0.80, [H₂]₀ = 0, [I₂]₀ = 0.

| | 2HI | H₂ | I₂ |
|---|---|---|---|
| Initial | 0.80 | 0 | 0 |
| Change | −2x | +x | +x |
| Equil. | 0.80 − 2x | x | x |

**Step 2 — Write Kc and substitute.**
Kc = [H₂][I₂] / [HI]² = x² / (0.80 − 2x)² = 0.020.

**Step 3 — Take the square root.**
x / (0.80 − 2x) = √0.020 = 0.1414 (positive root).
x = 0.1414(0.80 − 2x) = 0.1131 − 0.2828x.
1.2828x = 0.1131 → x = **0.0882**.

**Step 4 — Calculate [HI] at equilibrium.**
[\ce{HI}]_eq = 0.80 − 2(0.0882) = 0.80 − 0.1764 = **0.62 mol dm⁻³** (2 s.f.).

*Pitfall: Note the change term is −2x for HI, not −x, because of the stoichiometric coefficient of 2.*

## Problem 15

**Step 1 — Observe the trend.**
Kc at 298 K = 4.0. Kc at 350 K = 2.0. As temperature increases, Kc decreases.

**Step 2 — Apply Le Chatelier's principle.**
Kc = [products]/[reactants]. A decrease in Kc with increasing temperature means that at higher temperature, the equilibrium favours reactants more than at lower temperature. This implies that heating shifts the equilibrium towards reactants (left).

**Step 3 — Deduce ΔH.**
If heating favours the reverse reaction, then the reverse reaction must be endothermic (absorbing the added heat). Therefore the forward reaction is **exothermic** (ΔH < 0).

**Step 4 — General rule.**
For exothermic reactions: Kc decreases as T increases. For endothermic reactions: Kc increases as T increases. This is consistent with the van 't Hoff equation.

## Problem 17

**Step 1 — Recall the relationship.**
Kp = Kc(RT)^Δn, where Δn = moles of gaseous products − moles of gaseous reactants.

**Step 2 — Calculate Δn.**
Δn = 2 (from 2NH₃) − (1 + 3) (from N₂ + 3H₂) = 2 − 4 = **−2**.

**Step 3 — Substitute.**
Kp = 0.50 × (0.0821 × 500)^(−2) = 0.50 × (41.05)^(−2) = 0.50 / (41.05)² = 0.50 / 1685 = **2.97 × 10⁻⁴ atm⁻²**.

**Step 4 — Explain why Kp ≠ Kc.**
Kp and Kc are equal only when Δn = 0 (the number of gas molecules is the same on both sides). Here Δn = −2, so Kp = Kc/(RT)², making Kp much smaller than Kc at this temperature. This is because concentration and partial pressure are related by the ideal gas law — when Δn ≠ 0, the conversion factor (RT)^Δn is not equal to 1.

## Problem 18

**Step 1 — Calculate Δn.**
\ce{H2(g) + I2(g) <=> 2HI(g)}. Δn = 2 − (1 + 1) = **0**.

**Step 2 — Apply Kp = Kc(RT)^Δn.**
Kp = Kc(RT)⁰ = Kc × 1 = **Kc = 50.0**.

**Step 3 — Explain.**
Since (RT)⁰ = 1, Kp and Kc are numerically equal. This occurs whenever the total number of moles of gas is the same on both sides of the equation (Δn = 0). In such cases, the units also cancel, and both Kc and Kp are dimensionless.

*Pitfall: Even when Kp = Kc numerically, they are conceptually different — Kc uses concentration (mol dm⁻³), Kp uses partial pressure (atm). The equality is a consequence of the ideal gas law and Δn = 0.*

## Problem 20

**(a) Compressing the mixture to half the original volume.**
The number of gas molecules on each side is equal: 1 + 1 = 2 on the left, 1 + 1 = 2 on the right. Δn = 0. Compression increases all partial pressures equally, but the ratio remains unchanged. The equilibrium position does **not shift**, and Kc (and Kp) remain **unchanged**. For reactions where Δn ≠ 0, compression would shift the equilibrium towards the side with fewer gas molecules.

**(b) Increasing the temperature by 20 °C.**
ΔH = 0 (thermoneutral reaction). Temperature change does not favour either direction because neither is endothermic or exothermic. The equilibrium position does **not shift**, and Kc remains **unchanged** (unlike for reactions with ΔH ≠ 0, where Kc changes with temperature). This is a special case.

## Problem 22

**Step 1 — How a catalyst works at equilibrium.**
A catalyst provides an alternative pathway with lower activation energy. This lowers Ea for the forward reaction and Ea for the reverse reaction **by exactly the same amount** (the energy profile of the catalysed pathway has the same ΔH as the uncatalysed pathway).

**Step 2 — Why equilibrium position is unchanged.**
The rate constants for both forward and reverse reactions increase by the same factor. Since Kc = k_f/k_r, and both numerator and denominator are multiplied by the same factor, Kc is **unchanged**. The equilibrium composition — the ratio [products]/[reactants] at equilibrium — is therefore identical with or without the catalyst.

**Step 3 — What the catalyst does change.**
The catalyst only increases the **rate** at which equilibrium is attained. It does not change the thermodynamics (ΔG, ΔH, Kc, or equilibrium position). It is a kinetic effect, not a thermodynamic one.

## Problem 23

**Step 1 — Set up the ICE table.**
Volume = 1.0 dm³. Initial: [A]₀ = 1.0, [B]₀ = 0, [C]₀ = 0.

| | 2A | B | C |
|---|---|---|---|
| Initial | 1.0 | 0 | 0 |
| Change | −2x | +x | +x |
| Equil. | 1.0 − 2x | x | x |

**Step 2 — Write Kc and substitute.**
Kc = [B][C] / [A]² = x² / (1.0 − 2x)² = 9.0.

**Step 3 — Take the square root.**
x / (1.0 − 2x) = √9.0 = 3.0.
x = 3.0(1.0 − 2x) = 3.0 − 6.0x.
7.0x = 3.0 → x = 3.0/7.0 = **0.429**.

**Step 4 — Calculate equilibrium concentrations.**
[\ce{A}]_eq = 1.0 − 2(0.429) = 1.0 − 0.857 = **0.14 mol dm⁻³**.
[\ce{B}]_eq = [\ce{C}]_eq = **0.43 mol dm⁻³** (2 s.f.).

*Pitfall: The square root trick works because both products have the same concentration (x) and Kc is a perfect square.*

## Problem 24

**Step 1 — When the statement is correct.**
The statement is correct for gaseous equilibria where Δn ≠ 0. For example, \ce{N2(g) + 3H2(g) <=> 2NH3(g)} (Δn = −2): increasing pressure shifts equilibrium right (fewer gas molecules, 2 vs 4). \ce{PCl5(g) <=> PCl3(g) + Cl2(g)} (Δn = +1): increasing pressure shifts equilibrium left (1 molecule vs 2).

**Step 2 — When the statement is incorrect or misleading.**
- When Δn = 0: \ce{H2(g) + I2(g) <=> 2HI(g)}. Both sides have 2 gas molecules. Increasing pressure compresses the mixture but does not favour either side. No shift occurs.
- When the pressure increase is achieved by adding an inert gas at constant volume: total pressure rises but partial pressures of reacting gases are unchanged, so no shift occurs.
- When solids or liquids are involved: Le Chatelier's principle for pressure changes only applies to gases.

**Step 3 — Conclusion.**
The statement is a useful rule of thumb but must be qualified: pressure changes only shift gaseous equilibria where Δn ≠ 0, and only when the change affects the partial pressures of the reacting gases.

## Problem 25

**Step 1 — Write Kc and Kp expressions.**
Pure solids have constant concentrations (activity = 1) and are omitted:
Kc = [\ce{CO2}].
Kp = p(\ce{CO2}).

**Step 2 — Why solids are omitted.**
The "concentration" of a pure solid is determined by its density divided by its molar mass, which is constant at a given temperature regardless of the amount present. Since it is constant, it is incorporated into the equilibrium constant and does not appear explicitly in the expression.

**Step 3 — Calculate Kp.**
Kp = p(\ce{CO2}) = **0.25 atm**. Units: **atm**.

**Step 4 — Key insight.**
At a given temperature, the equilibrium CO₂ pressure above CaCO₃/CaO is fixed — it does not depend on the amounts of solids present. This is the basis of the thermal decomposition of limestone, where CO₂ must be continually removed to drive the reaction forward.
