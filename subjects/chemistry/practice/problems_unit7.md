# Unit 7: Acids &amp; Bases — Practice Problems

## Problem 1
Identify the two conjugate acid–base pairs in the following equilibrium reaction:

$$\ce{CH3COOH(aq) + H2O(l) <=> CH3COO-(aq) + H3O+(aq)}$$

For one of the pairs you identified, explain what makes the acid and its conjugate base a "conjugate pair" in terms of proton transfer.

## Problem 2
The hydronium ion concentration of a sample of rainwater at 298 K is measured as $3.2 \times 10^{-6}\ \text{mol}\ \text{dm}^{-3}$. Calculate the pH of this rainwater to two decimal places, and then determine the hydroxide ion concentration, $[\ce{OH-}]$, in the sample. State whether the rainwater is acidic, basic, or neutral.

## Problem 3
A solution of hydrochloric acid has a pH of 1.70 at 298 K. Calculate the concentration of $\ce{HCl}$ in the solution, given that hydrochloric acid is a strong monoprotic acid that dissociates completely. Then calculate the pOH of this solution.

## Problem 4
Sodium hydroxide, $\ce{NaOH}$, is a strong base that dissociates completely in water. A student prepares a solution by dissolving 0.40 g of $\ce{NaOH}$ in water and making the volume up to $500\ \text{cm}^3$. Calculate the pH of this solution at 298 K. (Molar mass of $\ce{NaOH} = 40.0\ \text{g}\ \text{mol}^{-1}$.)

## Problem 5
A $0.10\ \text{mol}\ \text{dm}^{-3}$ solution of a weak monoprotic acid, $\ce{HA}$, has a pH of 2.87 at 298 K. Calculate the acid dissociation constant, $K_a$, for $\ce{HA}$, and hence determine its $\text{p}K_a$ value. Show all your working.

## Problem 6
The $\text{p}K_a$ of methanoic acid, $\ce{HCOOH}$, is 3.75 at 298 K. Calculate the pH of a $0.20\ \text{mol}\ \text{dm}^{-3}$ solution of methanoic acid. State any assumption you make and explain why it is valid for this calculation.

## Problem 7
The $K_a$ of ethanoic acid, $\ce{CH3COOH}$, is $1.8 \times 10^{-5}$ at 298 K. Calculate $K_b$ for the ethanoate ion, $\ce{CH3COO-}$, and hence determine the $\text{p}K_b$ of the ethanoate ion. Explain the relationship between $K_a$, $K_b$, and $K_w$ that you used.

## Problem 8
Explain why the $\text{p}K_a$ of chloroethanoic acid, $\ce{ClCH2COOH}$ ($\text{p}K_a = 2.86$), is lower than the $\text{p}K_a$ of ethanoic acid, $\ce{CH3COOH}$ ($\text{p}K_a = 4.76$). Your answer should refer to the inductive effect and the stability of the conjugate base.

## Problem 9
A buffer solution is prepared by dissolving 0.050 mol of ethanoic acid, $\ce{CH3COOH}$, and 0.050 mol of sodium ethanoate, $\ce{CH3COONa}$, in enough water to make $1.0\ \text{dm}^3$ of solution. The $K_a$ of ethanoic acid is $1.8 \times 10^{-5}$ at 298 K. Calculate the pH of this buffer solution. Explain, using equations, how this buffer resists a change in pH when a small amount of hydrochloric acid is added.

## Problem 10
A buffer solution is made by mixing 40.0 cm³ of $0.100\ \text{mol}\ \text{dm}^{-3}$ ethanoic acid with 60.0 cm³ of $0.100\ \text{mol}\ \text{dm}^{-3}$ sodium ethanoate. The $K_a$ of ethanoic acid is $1.8 \times 10^{-5}$. Calculate the pH of this buffer at 298 K.

## Problem 11
A student prepares a buffer by partially neutralising a weak acid. She mixes 50.0 cm³ of $0.200\ \text{mol}\ \text{dm}^{-3}$ propanoic acid ($\ce{C2H5COOH}$, $K_a = 1.3 \times 10^{-5}$) with 25.0 cm³ of $0.200\ \text{mol}\ \text{dm}^{-3}$ sodium hydroxide solution. Calculate the pH of the resulting buffer solution.

## Problem 12
A buffer solution has a total volume of $100\ \text{cm}^3$ and contains 0.010 mol of a weak acid $\ce{HA}$ ($K_a = 2.5 \times 10^{-6}$) and 0.015 mol of its sodium salt $\ce{NaA}$. Calculate the pH of the buffer, and determine whether this buffer is effective at resisting pH changes at pH = 2.0. Justify your answer by referring to the buffer range.

## Problem 13
Describe, with reference to the shape of the curve and the species present, the key features of the titration curve obtained when $25.0\ \text{cm}^3$ of $0.10\ \text{mol}\ \text{dm}^{-3}$ ethanoic acid (a weak acid) is titrated with $0.10\ \text{mol}\ \text{dm}^{-3}$ sodium hydroxide (a strong base). Identify the approximate pH at the equivalence point and explain why it is not 7.00.

## Problem 14
A student titrates $20.0\ \text{cm}^3$ of $0.100\ \text{mol}\ \text{dm}^{-3}$ ammonia solution ($\ce{NH3}$, $K_b = 1.8 \times 10^{-5}$) with $0.100\ \text{mol}\ \text{dm}^{-3}$ hydrochloric acid. Sketch the general shape of the titration curve, and state whether the pH at the equivalence point is greater than, less than, or equal to 7.00. Justify your answer by considering the salt formed at the equivalence point.

## Problem 15
In a titration, a student adds $25.0\ \text{cm}^3$ of $0.10\ \text{mol}\ \text{dm}^{-3}$ $\ce{NaOH}$ to $25.0\ \text{cm}^3$ of $0.10\ \text{mol}\ \text{dm}^{-3}$ $\ce{CH3COOH}$. The $K_a$ of ethanoic acid is $1.8 \times 10^{-5}$. Show that the pH at this point is approximately 8.7, and explain the significance of the term "half-equivalence point" in the context of a weak-acid–strong-base titration.

## Problem 16
A student carries out a titration of a weak acid with a strong base and records the pH at the half-equivalence point as 4.52. Determine the $K_a$ and $\text{p}K_a$ of the weak acid, and explain the theoretical basis for the relationship $\text{pH} = \text{p}K_a$ at the half-equivalence point.

## Problem 17
The indicator phenolphthalein has a $\text{p}K_a$ of approximately 9.3 and changes colour over the pH range 8.2–10.0. Methyl orange has a $\text{p}K_a$ of approximately 3.7 and changes colour over the range 3.1–4.4. For each of the following titrations, state which indicator is appropriate and explain your choice:
(a) Hydrochloric acid titrated with sodium hydroxide.
(b) Ethanoic acid titrated with sodium hydroxide.
(c) Hydrochloric acid titrated with ammonia solution.

## Problem 18
Define a Lewis acid and a Lewis base. For the reaction between boron trifluoride, $\ce{BF3}$, and ammonia, $\ce{NH3}$, to form $\ce{F3B-NH3}$, identify the Lewis acid and the Lewis base, and explain your reasoning in terms of electron pair donation and acceptance.

## Problem 19
Aluminium chloride, $\ce{AlCl3}$, and the hydrated copper(II) ion, $\ce{[Cu(H2O)6]^{2+}}$, both act as Lewis acids. For each species, explain the structural feature that makes it a Lewis acid, and write an equation showing its reaction with a water molecule acting as a Lewis base.

## Problem 20
Phosphoric acid, $\ce{H3PO4}$, is a triprotic acid with successive acid dissociation constants $K_{a1} = 7.1 \times 10^{-3}$, $K_{a2} = 6.3 \times 10^{-8}$, and $K_{a3} = 4.2 \times 10^{-13}$ at 298 K. Explain why each successive $K_a$ value is smaller than the previous one. Calculate the pH of a $0.10\ \text{mol}\ \text{dm}^{-3}$ solution of phosphoric acid, stating clearly any assumption you make about which dissociation step dominates the pH.

## Problem 21
Carbonic acid, $\ce{H2CO3}$, is a diprotic acid with $K_{a1} = 4.3 \times 10^{-7}$ and $K_{a2} = 5.6 \times 10^{-11}$ at 298 K. A student dissolves carbon dioxide in water to make a $0.050\ \text{mol}\ \text{dm}^{-3}$ solution of $\ce{H2CO3}$. Calculate the pH of this solution, the concentration of $\ce{HCO3-}$, and the concentration of $\ce{CO3^{2-}}$ at equilibrium. Justify any approximations you make.

## Problem 22
Sodium ethanoate, $\ce{CH3COONa}$, dissolves in water to give a basic solution. Write an equation for the hydrolysis reaction that occurs when ethanoate ions react with water. Given that $K_a$ for ethanoic acid is $1.8 \times 10^{-5}$, calculate the pH of a $0.10\ \text{mol}\ \text{dm}^{-3}$ solution of sodium ethanoate at 298 K.

## Problem 23
Ammonium chloride, $\ce{NH4Cl}$, is a salt that produces an acidic solution when dissolved in water. Write the hydrolysis equation for the ammonium ion, $\ce{NH4+}$, and, given that $K_b$ for ammonia is $1.8 \times 10^{-5}$, calculate the pH of a $0.20\ \text{mol}\ \text{dm}^{-3}$ solution of ammonium chloride at 298 K.

## Problem 24
Classify aqueous solutions of the following salts as acidic, basic, or neutral at 298 K. For each, write the relevant hydrolysis equation (if any) and justify your classification:
(a) Potassium nitrate, $\ce{KNO3}$
(b) Sodium carbonate, $\ce{Na2CO3}$
(c) Iron(III) chloride, $\ce{FeCl3}$

## Problem 25
A student claims that the pH of a $0.10\ \text{mol}\ \text{dm}^{-3}$ solution of sodium hydrogensulfate, $\ce{NaHSO4}$, is simply determined by $K_{a2}$ of sulfuric acid ($1.2 \times 10^{-2}$). Explain whether the student is correct. The hydrogen sulfate ion, $\ce{HSO4-}$, is amphiprotic. Write equations to show how $\ce{HSO4-}$ can act both as a Brønsted–Lowry acid and as a Brønsted–Lowry base.

## Problem 26
A student prepares three buffer solutions, each with a total volume of $100\ \text{cm}^3$, using a weak acid $\ce{HA}$ ($\text{p}K_a = 4.80$) and its sodium salt $\ce{NaA}$:
- **Buffer X**: 0.050 mol $\ce{HA}$ and 0.050 mol $\ce{NaA}$
- **Buffer Y**: 0.080 mol $\ce{HA}$ and 0.020 mol $\ce{NaA}$
- **Buffer Z**: 0.020 mol $\ce{HA}$ and 0.080 mol $\ce{NaA}$

(a) Calculate the pH of each buffer.
(b) Which buffer has the greatest capacity to resist changes in pH when acid is added? Which has the greatest capacity when base is added? Explain your reasoning.

## Problem 27
Aminoethanoic acid (glycine), $\ce{H2NCH2COOH}$, is an amphiprotic species. In aqueous solution, it can accept a proton to form $\ce{^{+}H3NCH2COOH}$ or donate a proton to form $\ce{H2NCH2COO-}$. Write the two equilibrium equations that describe these processes. Given that $\text{p}K_{a1} = 2.34$ (for the carboxyl group) and $\text{p}K_{a2} = 9.60$ (for the ammonium group), calculate the isoelectric point (the pH at which the zwitterion predominates) of glycine.

## Problem 28
A $0.010\ \text{mol}\ \text{dm}^{-3}$ solution of a weak monoprotic acid has a pH of 3.40 at 298 K. Calculate the percentage ionisation of this acid. Explain why the percentage ionisation of a weak acid increases upon dilution, using Le Chatelier's principle.

## Problem 29
In a laboratory experiment, a student needs to prepare a buffer solution of pH = 5.00 using ethanoic acid ($\ce{CH3COOH}$, $K_a = 1.8 \times 10^{-5}$) and sodium ethanoate ($\ce{CH3COONa}$), both available as $0.10\ \text{mol}\ \text{dm}^{-3}$ solutions. Calculate the volume ratio in which the two solutions must be mixed, and hence state the volumes of each solution needed to prepare $100\ \text{cm}^3$ of the buffer.

## Problem 30
Distinguish between the equivalence point and the endpoint of a titration. Explain why it is important to choose an indicator whose $\text{p}K_a$ is close to the pH at the equivalence point. A student titrates ethanoic acid with sodium hydroxide and uses bromothymol blue (pH range 6.0–7.6) as the indicator: explain why this is a poor choice and identify the systematic error that would result.
