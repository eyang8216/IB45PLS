## Problem 1

**Answer: Propanone (acetone), \ce{CH3COCH3}**

**Step-by-step reasoning:**

1. **Molecular formula \ce{C3H6O}**: This formula has a degree of unsaturation of 1, consistent with either a carbonyl compound (aldehyde or ketone) or an unsaturated alcohol/ether.

2. **Positive 2,4-DNPH test**: The orange precipitate confirms the presence of a carbonyl group (\ce{C=O}). Both aldehydes and ketones give this test. This eliminates alcohols and ethers.

3. **Negative Tollens' and Fehling's tests**: These tests are specific for aldehydes. Tollens' reagent (\ce{[Ag(NH3)2]+}) oxidises aldehydes to carboxylic acids, depositing a silver mirror. Fehling's solution (alkaline \ce{Cu^2+} tartrate) produces a brick-red precipitate of \ce{Cu2O} with aldehydes. Since both tests are negative, the compound is not an aldehyde — it must be a ketone.

4. **Reduction by \ce{NaBH4} to a secondary alcohol**: Sodium borohydride reduces carbonyls. A ketone is reduced to a secondary alcohol; an aldehyde would give a primary alcohol. The product being a secondary alcohol confirms the original compound is a ketone.

5. **Structure**: With \ce{C3H6O} and a ketone functional group, the only possible ketone is propanone: \ce{CH3COCH3} (acetone).

**Common mistake**: Students sometimes confuse the negative Tollens'/Fehling's as meaning no carbonyl is present. The 2,4-DNPH test independently confirms the carbonyl — the negative Tollens'/Fehling's simply eliminates aldehydes.

## Problem 3

**Answer: Butan-1-ol, \ce{CH3CH2CH2CH2OH}**

**Step-by-step analysis:**

1. **Molecular formula \ce{C4H10O}**: Saturated (no unsaturation), consistent with an alcohol or ether.

2. **IR absorption at 3350 cm⁻¹ (broad)**: This is the characteristic \ce{O–H} stretch of an alcohol. The broadness is due to hydrogen bonding. If it were an ether, there would be no \ce{O–H} stretch. The absence of absorption at 1680–1750 cm⁻¹ confirms there is no carbonyl group.

3. **\ce{^1H} NMR analysis**:

   - **Triplet at δ 1.1 (3H)**: A \ce{–CH3} group adjacent to a \ce{–CH2–} group (n+1 rule: 2 neighbours → triplet). This is the terminal methyl of a propyl/butyl chain.
   - **Sextet at δ 1.6 (2H)**: A \ce{–CH2–} group with 5 neighbouring protons (2 from one adjacent \ce{–CH2–} and 3 from the \ce{–CH3}). This is the middle \ce{–CH2–} of a butyl chain.
   - **Triplet at δ 3.4 (2H)**: A \ce{–CH2–} group deshielded by an electronegative atom (oxygen), with 2 neighbouring protons → triplet. This is the \ce{–CH2–OH} group.
   - **Singlet at δ 2.1 (1H), disappears with \ce{D2O}**: This is the \ce{–OH} proton; \ce{D2O} exchanges it for deuterium, making the signal disappear.

4. **Putting it together**: A straight chain of four carbons with the \ce{–OH} on the terminal carbon. Structure: \ce{CH3CH2CH2CH2OH}, **butan-1-ol**.

**Common mistake**: Confusing butan-1-ol with butan-2-ol. In butan-2-ol, the \ce{CH–OH} proton would appear as a multiplet at a different chemical shift and the methyl groups would not be equivalent.

## Problem 4

**(a) General formulas:**

- Alkanes: \ce{C_nH_{2n+2}}
- Alkenes: \ce{C_nH_{2n}} (monounsaturated, one double bond)
- Alcohols: \ce{C_nH_{2n+1}OH} or \ce{C_nH_{2n+2}O} (saturated monoalcohols)
- Carboxylic acids: \ce{C_nH_{2n}O2} (or \ce{C_nH_{2n+1}COOH})
- Amines: \ce{C_nH_{2n+3}N} (primary saturated amines, \ce{C_nH_{2n+1}NH2})

**(b) Cycloalkanes vs. alkenes**: Cycloalkanes have the general formula \ce{C_nH_{2n}}, the same as alkenes. However, they differ in functional group: cycloalkanes contain only \ce{C–C} single bonds in a ring (saturated), while alkenes contain a \ce{C=C} double bond (unsaturated). They belong to different homologous series because the functional group (the characteristic group that defines the series) is different — the ring structure for cycloalkanes, the \ce{C=C} bond for alkenes. Members of the same homologous series share the same functional group and differ by \ce{CH2} units.

**(c) \ce{C5H10O2} — two possible homologous series:**

1. **Carboxylic acid**: Example: pentanoic acid, \ce{CH3CH2CH2CH2COOH}
2. **Ester**: Example: ethyl propanoate, \ce{CH3CH2COOCH2CH3}

Other possibilities include hydroxyaldehydes and hydroxyketones.

## Problem 7

**(a) Overall equation:**

$$\ce{CH4 + Cl2 ->[UV light] CH3Cl + HCl}$$

**(b) Free-radical substitution mechanism:**

**Initiation:** Ultraviolet light provides energy to homolytically cleave the \ce{Cl–Cl} bond, producing two chlorine radicals. Each atom takes one electron from the bond.

$$\ce{Cl2 ->[UV] 2 Cl•}$$

**Propagation (two steps):**

Step 1: A chlorine radical abstracts a hydrogen atom from methane, forming \ce{HCl} and a methyl radical.

$$\ce{CH4 + Cl• -> •CH3 + HCl}$$

Step 2: The methyl radical reacts with a chlorine molecule, forming chloromethane and regenerating a chlorine radical (which can continue the chain).

$$\ce{•CH3 + Cl2 -> CH3Cl + Cl•}$$

**Termination:** Any two radicals combine to terminate the chain. Possible termination steps include:

$$\ce{2 Cl• -> Cl2}$$
$$\ce{2 •CH3 -> C2H6}$$
$$\ce{Cl• + •CH3 -> CH3Cl}$$

**(c) Formation of ethane**: When two methyl radicals (\ce{•CH3}) collide during a termination step, they combine to form ethane, \ce{C2H6}. This is the radical–radical coupling termination step shown above.

**(d) Why this is poor for pure chloromethane**: The reaction is not selective. Once chloromethane is formed, it can undergo further substitution because the chlorine radical can attack \ce{CH3Cl} at any of the remaining \ce{C–H} bonds. This leads to a mixture of products: \ce{CH3Cl}, \ce{CH2Cl2}, \ce{CHCl3}, and \ce{CCl4}, making it difficult to isolate pure chloromethane. The statistical probability of further chlorination increases as more \ce{Cl2} is present.

## Problem 8

**(a) Two possible products:**

- **1-bromopropane**, \ce{CH3CH2CH2Br} (anti-Markovnikov product)
- **2-bromopropane**, \ce{CH3CHBrCH3} (Markovnikov product — major)

**(b) Markovnikov's rule**: The major product is 2-bromopropane. Markovnikov's rule states that when HX adds to an unsymmetrical alkene, the hydrogen atom attaches to the carbon that already has the greater number of hydrogen atoms ("the rich get richer"). This is rationalised by carbocation stability:

- Addition of \ce{H+} to the \ce{C=C} bond can produce either a primary carbocation (\ce{CH3CH2CH2+}) or a secondary carbocation (\ce{CH3CH+CH3}).
- The secondary carbocation is more stable due to the greater inductive effect (+I) of two alkyl groups vs. one.
- The more stable carbocation forms faster, leading to 2-bromopropane as the major product.

**(c) Electrophilic addition mechanism for 2-bromopropane:**

**Step 1:** The π electrons of the \ce{C=C} bond attack the partially positive hydrogen of \ce{HBr}, forming a \ce{C–H} bond. The \ce{H–Br} bond breaks heterolytically, with bromine taking both electrons. The more stable secondary carbocation \ce{CH3CH+CH3} is formed.

$$\ce{CH3CH=CH2 + HBr -> CH3CH+CH3 + Br-}$$

**Step 2:** The bromide ion acts as a nucleophile, attacking the carbocation and forming 2-bromopropane.

$$\ce{CH3CH+CH3 + Br- -> CH3CHBrCH3}$$

**(d) Peroxide effect**: In the presence of peroxides (\ce{ROOR}), the reaction proceeds via a free-radical mechanism rather than an ionic one, giving the anti-Markovnikov product (1-bromopropane). This occurs because the peroxide initiates radical formation, and the bromine radical adds to the less substituted end of the alkene (forming the more stable secondary radical intermediate). This is specific to \ce{HBr}; \ce{HCl} and \ce{HI} do not show this peroxide effect because the \ce{H–Cl} bond is too strong and the \ce{H–I} bond cleavage is endothermic in the radical pathway.

## Problem 9

**(a) The alkene is but-2-ene, \ce{CH3CH=CHCH3}.** It exhibits E/Z isomerism because each carbon of the double bond has two different groups attached: C1 has \ce{–H} and \ce{–CH3}, and C2 has \ce{–H} and \ce{–CH3}. Since both groups on each carbon are different, rotation about the double bond is restricted, giving rise to two non-interconvertible stereoisomers.

**(b) CIP priority assignments:**

- On C1: \ce{–CH3} (atomic number of the directly bonded C is 6) vs. \ce{–H} (atomic number 1). Priority: \ce{–CH3} > \ce{–H}.
- On C2: \ce{–CH3} (C, Z=6) vs. \ce{–H} (Z=1). Priority: \ce{–CH3} > \ce{–H}.

**E isomer**: The two higher-priority groups (\ce{–CH3} on each carbon) are on opposite sides of the double bond. **(E)-but-2-ene**

**Z isomer**: The two higher-priority groups are on the same side. **(Z)-but-2-ene**

**(c) Reaction with cold dilute \ce{KMnO4}:**

$$\ce{CH3CH=CHCH3 + [O] + H2O ->[cold, dilute KMnO4] CH3CH(OH)CH(OH)CH3}$$

The product is **butane-2,3-diol**. The colour change: purple \ce{KMnO4} solution turns to a brown precipitate of manganese(IV) oxide, \ce{MnO2}.

## Problem 10

**(a) Two-step process:**

**Step 1 — electrophilic addition of sulfuric acid:**

$$\ce{CH2=CH2 + H2SO4 -> CH3CH2OSO3H}$$
(ethyl hydrogen sulfate)

**Step 2 — hydrolysis:**

$$\ce{CH3CH2OSO3H + H2O -> CH3CH2OH + H2SO4}$$
(ethanol and regenerated sulfuric acid)

**(b) Type of reaction and nucleophilicity of ethene**: This is electrophilic addition. Ethene acts as a nucleophile because the π bond of \ce{C=C} contains a region of high electron density above and below the plane of the molecule. These π electrons are relatively loosely held and are available to attack an electron-deficient species (electrophile). Ethene does not need lone pairs — the π electrons serve as the nucleophilic centre.

**(c) Comparison:**

| Aspect | Sulfuric acid route (indirect hydration) | Direct hydration |
|--------|------------------------------------------|-------------------|
| **Conditions** | Conc. \ce{H2SO4} at room temp, then water/heat | \ce{H3PO4} catalyst, 300 °C, 60–70 atm steam |
| **Advantage** | Operates at room temperature and pressure | Continuous process, regenerable catalyst, no concentrated acid waste |
| **Disadvantage** | Batch process, concentrated acid handling | High energy cost due to temperature and pressure |

**Advantage of indirect hydration**: Lower energy requirements (room temperature).
**Advantage of direct hydration**: Continuous process without concentrated acid waste; the phosphoric acid catalyst is not consumed.

## Problem 11

**(a) The monomer is chloroethene (vinyl chloride):** \ce{CH2=CHCl}. IUPAC name: **chloroethene**.

**(b) Common name: PVC (polyvinyl chloride).** Major use: pipes, window frames, electrical cable insulation, flooring, or packaging.

**(c) Polymerisation equation:**

$$\ce{2n CH2=CHCl -> –[CH2–CHCl]_{2n}–}$$

Or, more simply for 2.0 mol of monomer:

$$\ce{2 CH2=CHCl -> –[CH2–CHCl]2–}$$

where the two monomer units join to form a dimer of the repeating unit.

**(d) PTFE (polytetrafluoroethene):**

Repeating unit: \ce{–[CF2–CF2]_{n}–}

PTFE is chemically unreactive because of the exceptionally strong \ce{C–F} bonds (bond enthalpy ≈ 485 kJ mol⁻¹, one of the strongest single bonds in organic chemistry). The fluorine atoms are large and form a protective sheath around the carbon backbone, shielding it from chemical attack. The low coefficient of friction arises because the fluorine atoms, being highly electronegative, have tightly held electron clouds with little tendency to engage in intermolecular attractions with other surfaces.

## Problem 13

**(a) Product identification:** The product is **butanone** (\ce{CH3CH2COCH3}).

- **Positive 2,4-DNPH test**: Confirms a carbonyl group — orange precipitate of the hydrazone derivative. This eliminates non-carbonyl compounds.
- **Negative Tollens' test**: Eliminates aldehydes; Tollens' reagent (\ce{[Ag(NH3)2]+}) would produce a silver mirror with an aldehyde. The negative result confirms the product is a ketone, not an aldehyde.
- Starting from a secondary alcohol (butan-2-ol) and obtaining a ketone is consistent with the oxidation pattern: 2° alcohol → ketone.

**(b) Why distil rather than reflux**: Ketones can be further oxidised under forcing conditions. By distilling the product as it forms, the student removes butanone from the reaction mixture, preventing over-oxidation. Butanone has a lower boiling point (80 °C) than butan-2-ol (99 °C) and water, so it distils out first. Refluxing would keep the ketone in contact with hot oxidising agent, potentially leading to cleavage products.

**(c) If butan-1-ol were refluxed with excess acidified dichromate(VI):**

Refluxing a primary alcohol with excess oxidant drives the oxidation all the way to the carboxylic acid. Butan-1-ol → butanal → **butanoic acid**, \ce{CH3CH2CH2COOH}.

## Problem 14

**(a) Why tertiary alcohols cannot be oxidised:**

Oxidation of alcohols by dichromate(VI) involves the removal of a hydrogen atom from the carbon bearing the \ce{–OH} group and the hydrogen from the \ce{–OH} group itself, forming a \ce{C=O} bond. This requires that the carbon bearing the \ce{–OH} has at least one \ce{C–H} bond.

- **Primary alcohol**: The \ce{–OH} carbon has 2 \ce{C–H} bonds → can be oxidised (aldehyde, then carboxylic acid).
- **Secondary alcohol**: The \ce{–OH} carbon has 1 \ce{C–H} bond → can be oxidised (ketone).
- **Tertiary alcohol**: The \ce{–OH} carbon has 0 \ce{C–H} bonds → no hydrogen available for removal, so oxidation cannot occur without breaking \ce{C–C} bonds, which requires much more vigorous conditions.

**(b) Distinguishing propan-1-ol from propan-2-ol:**

**Test: Oxidation followed by Tollens'/Fehling's test, or Lucas test.**

**Oxidation + Tollens' approach:**

1. Gently oxidise both alcohols with acidified dichromate(VI), distilling off the products.
2. Propan-1-ol → propanal (aldehyde); propan-2-ol → propanone (ketone).
3. Test each distillate with Tollens' reagent.
   - The distillate from propan-1-ol: **silver mirror** (aldehyde reduces \ce{[Ag(NH3)2]+} to Ag).
   - The distillate from propan-2-ol: **no silver mirror** (ketone does not react).

**Alternative — Lucas test (\ce{ZnCl2}/conc. \ce{HCl}):**

- Propan-2-ol (secondary): cloudiness appears after 5–10 minutes of warming.
- Propan-1-ol (primary): no reaction at room temperature; cloudiness only after prolonged heating.

## Problem 15

**(a) Sequence of tests:**

**Step 1 — 2,4-DNPH test**: Add a few drops of 2,4-DNPH to a sample of each.

- Propanal, propanone: **orange precipitate** (both contain carbonyl groups).
- Propanoic acid: **no precipitate** (carboxylic acids do contain a carbonyl, but 2,4-DNPH typically does not give a positive result with carboxylic acids under standard conditions because the carbonyl is part of the resonance-stabilised carboxyl group).

**Step 2 — NaHCO₃ test**: To the compound that was negative to 2,4-DNPH, add a few drops of saturated sodium hydrogencarbonate solution.

- Propanoic acid: **effervescence** (\ce{CO2} gas evolved). Carboxylic acids are acidic enough to react with \ce{NaHCO3} to produce \ce{CO2}.
- This confirms propanoic acid.

**Step 3 — Tollens' test**: Warm the two carbonyl compounds with Tollens' reagent.

- Propanal: **silver mirror** forms on the test tube wall (aldehyde is oxidised).
- Propanone: **no silver mirror** (ketones are not oxidised by Tollens' reagent).

**(b) Tollens' and Fehling's specificity:**

Propanal is an aldehyde (\ce{–CHO} group). The aldehyde carbon is at the end of the chain, carrying a hydrogen atom. This hydrogen can be removed during oxidation, converting the aldehyde to a carboxylic acid.

Propanone is a ketone (\ce{>C=O} group). The carbonyl carbon is flanked by two alkyl groups with no hydrogen attached to the carbonyl carbon. Stronger oxidising agents would be needed to break \ce{C–C} bonds.

**Half-equation for Tollens' test (oxidation of propanal):**

$$\ce{CH3CH2CHO + 3 OH- -> CH3CH2COO- + 2 e- + 2 H2O}$$

The silver(I) is reduced:

$$\ce{[Ag(NH3)2]+ + e- -> Ag + 2 NH3}$$

Overall:

$$\ce{CH3CH2CHO + 2 [Ag(NH3)2]+ + 3 OH- -> CH3CH2COO- + 2 Ag + 4 NH3 + 2 H2O}$$

## Problem 16

**(a) Classification:**

- **1-Bromobutane**, \ce{CH3CH2CH2CH2Br}: **primary** (C–Br carbon attached to 1 other carbon)
- **2-Bromobutane**, \ce{CH3CH2CHBrCH3}: **secondary** (C–Br carbon attached to 2 other carbons)
- **2-Bromo-2-methylpropane**, \ce{(CH3)3CBr}: **tertiary** (C–Br carbon attached to 3 other carbons)

**(b) Mechanism preference with aqueous \ce{NaOH}:**

- **1-Bromobutane**: **\ce{S_N2}**. Primary halogenoalkanes have minimal steric hindrance, allowing the hydroxide nucleophile to attack from the back side in a concerted, one-step mechanism.
- **2-Bromobutane**: **\ce{S_N2} predominantly** (with a small contribution from \ce{S_N1}). Secondary halogenoalkanes can react by either mechanism, but in a strong nucleophile like \ce{OH-}, \ce{S_N2} is favoured.
- **2-Bromo-2-methylpropane**: **\ce{S_N1}**. The tertiary carbocation is relatively stable (three alkyl groups donate electron density by induction). The reaction proceeds via a two-step mechanism: slow formation of the carbocation, followed by fast attack of the nucleophile. The \ce{S_N2} pathway is heavily disfavoured because the bulky tert-butyl group blocks backside attack.

**(c) Comparison:**

| Feature | \ce{S_N1} | \ce{S_N2} |
|---------|-----------|-----------|
| **Kinetics** | First-order: rate = k[halogenoalkane] | Second-order: rate = k[halogenoalkane][nucleophile] |
| **Stereochemistry** | Racemisation (planar carbocation intermediate can be attacked from either side) | Inversion of configuration (Walden inversion) |
| **Steps** | Two steps (RDS: carbocation formation) | One concerted step (single transition state) |

**(d) Rate independence of [OH⁻] for 2-bromo-2-methylpropane:**

The hydrolysis follows \ce{S_N1} kinetics: rate = k[\ce{(CH3)3CBr}]. The rate-determining step is the unimolecular cleavage of the \ce{C–Br} bond to form the tert-butyl carbocation. The attack of the nucleophile (hydroxide or water) occurs in a fast second step. Since the nucleophile is not involved in the rate-determining step, its concentration does not affect the overall rate.

## Problem 17

**(a) Mechanism**: Second-order kinetics (rate = k[1-bromobutane][\ce{CN-}]) indicates **\ce{S_N2}** — a bimolecular nucleophilic substitution. The rate-determining step involves both the halogenoalkane and the nucleophile.

**Transition state**: A pentacoordinate carbon with the incoming \ce{CN-} nucleophile approaching from one side and the departing \ce{Br-} leaving from the opposite side. The \ce{C–CN} bond is partially formed, the \ce{C–Br} bond is partially broken, and the three remaining substituents (\ce{C3H7} and two H's) are in a roughly planar arrangement.

````
     H   H
      \ /
   C3H7–C---Br  ← partial bonds
      / \
     H   CN⁻
````

**(b) Product of reaction with \ce{KCN} then reduction:**

**Step 1**: \ce{CH3CH2CH2CH2Br + KCN -> CH3CH2CH2CH2CN + KBr} (nitrile/pentanenitrile)

**Step 2 — catalytic hydrogenation**: \ce{CH3CH2CH2CH2CN + 2 H2 ->[Ni] CH3CH2CH2CH2CH2NH2}

The final product is **pentan-1-amine** (\ce{CH3CH2CH2CH2CH2NH2}), a primary amine. Reduction of nitriles with \ce{H2}/Ni gives primary amines, extending the carbon chain by one.

**(c) With hot ethanolic \ce{KOH}**: Elimination occurs predominantly.

$$\ce{CH3CH2CH2CH2Br + KOH ->[ethanol, heat] CH3CH2CH=CH2 + KBr + H2O}$$

The product is **but-1-ene**. Ethanolic conditions (rather than aqueous) and heat favour elimination over substitution. Minor amounts of but-2-ene (\ce{CH3CH=CHCH3}) may also form if the double bond migrates, along with substitution products.

## Problem 18

**(a) Trend: R–I > R–Br > R–Cl (fastest to slowest)**

The rate-determining step in the hydrolysis of primary halogenoalkanes (under these conditions with \ce{AgNO3}/ethanol) involves breaking the carbon–halogen bond. The \ce{C–I} bond is the weakest (bond enthalpy ≈ 238 kJ mol⁻¹), so it breaks most easily. The \ce{C–Br} bond (≈ 276 kJ mol⁻¹) is intermediate, and the \ce{C–Cl} bond (≈ 338 kJ mol⁻¹) is the strongest, making it the slowest to react. A weaker carbon–halogen bond leads to a lower activation energy and faster hydrolysis.

**(b) Relative rates of isomeric bromobutanes:**

$$\ce{(CH3)3CBr > CH3CH2CHBrCH3 > CH3CH2CH2CH2Br}$$

- **2-Bromo-2-methylpropane (tertiary)**: Fastest. Reacts via \ce{S_N1} mechanism; the tertiary carbocation is highly stable, giving a low activation energy.
- **2-Bromobutane (secondary)**: Intermediate rate. Can react by both \ce{S_N1} and \ce{S_N2} pathways.
- **1-Bromobutane (primary)**: Slowest. Must react via \ce{S_N2}, which has higher activation energy than \ce{S_N1} for tertiary substrates. The primary carbocation is too unstable for \ce{S_N1}.

**(c) Role of silver nitrate:**

Silver ions facilitate the hydrolysis by precipitating halide ions as silver halides, driving the equilibrium towards products and providing a visible indication of reaction.

$$\ce{Ag+ (aq) + X- (aq) -> AgX (s)}$$

The precipitate colour confirms the halide: \ce{AgCl} (white), \ce{AgBr} (cream), \ce{AgI} (yellow).

## Problem 24

**(a) Delocalised model of benzene:**

In benzene, each carbon atom is sp² hybridised, forming three σ bonds (two to adjacent carbons and one to hydrogen) in a planar hexagonal arrangement. The remaining unhybridised p orbital on each carbon (containing one electron) overlaps sideways with p orbitals on both adjacent carbons. Rather than forming three localised π bonds (alternating double bonds), the six p orbitals combine to form a **delocalised π electron cloud** above and below the plane of the ring.

This delocalisation means the π electrons are evenly distributed across all six carbon–carbon bonds, giving each bond a bond order of 1.5 — intermediate between a single and double bond. This explains why all \ce{C–C} bond lengths in benzene are equal (140 pm) and the molecule has perfect hexagonal symmetry.

**(b) Stabilisation energy calculation:**

- Expected ΔH(hydrogenation) for "cyclohexatriene": 3 × (–120) = **–360 kJ mol⁻¹**
- Experimental ΔH(hydrogenation) of benzene: **–208 kJ mol⁻¹**
- **Stabilisation energy** = 360 – 208 = **152 kJ mol⁻¹**

This 152 kJ mol⁻¹ represents the extra energy that must be supplied to overcome the aromatic stabilisation before benzene can be hydrogenated. It shows that benzene is **152 kJ mol⁻¹ more stable** than the hypothetical "cyclohexatriene" with three isolated double bonds. This is direct experimental evidence for the delocalised model — if benzene had isolated double bonds, it would hydrogenate with ΔH ≈ –360 kJ mol⁻¹. The fact that it doesn't confirms that its structure is stabilised by delocalisation.

**(c) Electrophilic substitution vs. addition:**

Alkenes typically undergo electrophilic **addition** because the π bond is localised and relatively easy to break. In benzene, electrophilic addition would destroy the delocalised π system, producing a non-aromatic cyclohexadiene derivative with the loss of the 152 kJ mol⁻¹ stabilisation energy. This is energetically unfavourable.

Instead, benzene undergoes electrophilic **substitution**: an electrophile replaces a hydrogen atom. The reaction proceeds via a high-energy σ-complex (Wheland intermediate), but the final product retains the aromatic sextet — the delocalised π system is regenerated in the last step. The energy "cost" of going through the intermediate is "paid back" by regaining aromaticity in the product.

## Problem 25

**(a) Overall equation:**

$$\ce{C6H6 + HNO3 ->[conc. H2SO4][50 °C] C6H5NO2 + H2O}$$

**(b) Generation of the nitronium ion:**

**Step 1:** Sulfuric acid protonates nitric acid:

$$\ce{HNO3 + H2SO4 ⇌ H2NO3+ + HSO4-}$$

**Step 2:** The protonated nitric acid loses water to form the nitronium ion:

$$\ce{H2NO3+ ⇌ NO2+ + H2O}$$

The sulfuric acid then protonates the water, driving the equilibrium:

$$\ce{H2O + H2SO4 ⇌ H3O+ + HSO4-}$$

Overall: $$\ce{HNO3 + 2 H2SO4 ⇌ NO2+ + 2 HSO4- + H3O+}$$

**Step 1:** The π electrons of benzene attack the nitronium ion (\ce{NO2+}). A \ce{C–N} bond forms, and the aromaticity is broken. ## Problem 27

**(a) Classification:**

- **Nitro group (\ce{–NO2})**: **Deactivating** and **meta-directing**. The nitro group withdraws electron density from the ring through both inductive (–I) and resonance (–M) effects, making the ring less reactive. The greatest electron deficiency is at the ortho and para positions, so the meta position is relatively the most electron-rich, directing electrophiles there.

- **Methyl group (\ce{–CH3})**: **Activating** and **ortho/para-directing**. The methyl group donates electron density through the inductive effect (+I) and hyperconjugation, increasing reactivity, especially at the ortho and para positions.

**(b) Meta-directing effect of \ce{–NO2} (resonance explanation):**

When the electrophile attacks at the ortho or para position in nitrobenzene, one of the resonance contributors of the σ-complex places the positive charge on the carbon bearing the nitro group. This is particularly unfavourable because the nitro group (already electron-withdrawing) is adjacent to a positive charge — a highly destabilising arrangement.

Resonance structure for ortho attack:

```
      NO2                    +NO2
       |                      |
   +===/ \     ←→          / \+
    H  \ /  E              H  \ /  E
```

The positive charge on the carbon adjacent to the strongly electron-withdrawing \ce{–NO2} group makes this intermediate much higher in energy.

When attack occurs at the **meta** position, none of the resonance contributors place the positive charge directly on the carbon bearing the nitro group — the charge always remains on carbons that are not directly attached to \ce{–NO2}. The meta-substituted σ-complex is therefore lower in energy than the ortho- or para-substituted ones, leading to faster reaction at the meta position.

**(c) Activating and ortho/para-directing effect of \ce{–CH3}:**

**Inductive effect (+I):** The methyl group is slightly more electropositive than hydrogen, donating electron density through the σ framework.

**Hyperconjugation:** The \ce{C–H} σ bonds of the methyl group can overlap with the π system of the ring. This is often depicted as:

$$\ce{H–CH2–C6H5 ↔ H+ \ {}^-CH2=C6H5}$$

The donation of electron density increases the π electron density in the ring, making it more reactive. The donation is most effective at the ortho and para positions because these are the positions where the σ-complex intermediate places the positive charge on the carbon directly attached to the methyl group — the methyl group can then stabilise this positive charge through hyperconjugation and +I effects.

**(d) Chlorination of methylbenzene:**

The major products are **2-chloromethylbenzene** (ortho) and **4-chloromethylbenzene** (para), with the para isomer predominating slightly due to steric effects.

## Problem 28

**(a) Why but-2-enoic acid exhibits E/Z isomerism:**

But-2-enoic acid (\ce{CH3CH=CHCOOH}) has a carbon–carbon double bond, and **each carbon of the double bond has two different groups attached**:

- C2 (the carbon bearing \ce{–CH3}): attached to \ce{–CH3} and \ce{–H} — two different groups.
- C3 (the carbon bearing \ce{–COOH}): attached to \ce{–COOH} and \ce{–H} — two different groups.

The restricted rotation about the double bond means these two spatial arrangements cannot interconvert at room temperature, giving rise to E and Z stereoisomers.

**(b) E and Z isomers with CIP reasoning:**

**On C2:** The directly bonded atoms are H (Z=1) and C (Z=6) from the methyl group. **Priority: \ce{–CH3} > \ce{–H}.**

**On C3:** The directly bonded atoms are H (Z=1) and C (Z=6) from the carboxyl group. **Priority: \ce{–COOH} > \ce{–H}.**

To decide between \ce{–COOH} and \ce{–CH3} when comparing priorities across the double bond is not necessary — we only compare the two groups on each carbon separately.

- **(Z)-but-2-enoic acid**: The higher-priority groups (\ce{–CH3} and \ce{–COOH}) are on the **same side** (Z = zusammen = together).
- **(E)-but-2-enoic acid**: The higher-priority groups are on **opposite sides** (E = entgegen = opposite).

**(c) Why but-1-ene cannot exist as E/Z isomers:**

In but-1-ene (\ce{CH3CH2CH=CH2}), C1 of the double bond (\ce{=CH2}) has **two identical groups** (both hydrogen atoms) attached. Since the two groups are the same, there is no distinction — swapping them produces the same molecule. E/Z isomerism requires that each carbon of the double bond has **two different** substituents.

**(d) Structural requirement for E/Z isomerism:**

A molecule must have:
1. A **carbon–carbon double bond** (or another bond with restricted rotation), AND
2. **Each carbon of the double bond must have two different atoms or groups** attached to it.

If either carbon carries two identical groups, E/Z isomerism is not possible.

$$\ce{RNH2 + NaNO2 + 2 HCl -> ROH + N2 ↑ + NaCl + H2O}$$

(For a primary aromatic amine, a diazonium salt would form instead, which can be further tested with alkaline β-naphthol to produce an orange-red azo dye. 2-Phenylethylamine is an aliphatic amine, not aromatic, despite having a phenyl group — the \ce{–NH2} is on the side chain, not directly on the ring.)

Alternative: **Hinsberg test** — reaction with benzenesulfonyl chloride in aqueous \ce{NaOH}. Primary amines give a sulfonamide that dissolves in alkali.
