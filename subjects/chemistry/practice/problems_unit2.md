# Unit 2: Atomic Structure — Practice Problems

## Problem 1
State the number of protons, neutrons, and electrons in a neutral atom of chlorine-35 (³⁵Cl).

## Problem 2
An atom of element X has 26 protons and 30 neutrons. Write the nuclide notation in the form ᴬX for this atom and identify the element by name and symbol.

## Problem 3
Naturally occurring copper consists of two isotopes: copper-63 (relative isotopic mass = 63.0 u, abundance = 70.0%) and copper-65 (relative isotopic mass = 65.0 u, abundance = 30.0%). Calculate the relative atomic mass of copper.

## Problem 4
Describe the five main stages of a mass spectrometer — vaporisation, ionisation, acceleration, deflection, and detection — and explain the purpose of each stage. State what property of an ion determines the degree to which it is deflected in the magnetic field.

## Problem 5
The mass spectrum of an element shows two peaks: one at m/z = 50 with relative intensity 3, and another at m/z = 52 with relative intensity 1. Calculate the relative atomic mass of the element. Suggest which element this might be, referring to the periodic table.

## Problem 6
List the types of information that can be obtained from the mass spectrum of (a) an element, and (b) a compound. Explain how each type of information appears in the spectrum.

## Problem 7
Write the full electron configuration (using the notation 1s² 2s² 2p⁶ ...) for a neutral atom of phosphorus, which has atomic number Z = 15.

## Problem 8
Write the condensed electron configuration (using noble gas notation) for a neutral atom of zinc, which has atomic number Z = 30.

## Problem 9
Draw the orbital diagram (using boxes for orbitals and arrows for electrons) showing all electrons in a neutral oxygen atom (Z = 8). Label each subshell and indicate how your diagram follows Hund's rule and the Pauli exclusion principle.

## Problem 10
Write the full electron configuration of a chromium atom (Z = 24) and explain why this configuration differs from the one predicted by a straightforward application of the Aufbau principle.

## Problem 11
Copper has atomic number Z = 29. Write the electron configuration of the Cu⁺ ion and explain which electrons are removed when copper forms this cation.

## Problem 12
Write the electron configuration of the Fe²⁺ ion. Iron has atomic number Z = 26. Explain the order in which electrons are removed when a transition metal atom forms a cation.

## Problem 13
In neutral atoms, the 4s subshell fills before the 3d subshell. However, when transition metal atoms form cations, the 4s electrons are lost before the 3d electrons. Explain both of these observations.

## Problem 14
The visible emission spectrum of atomic hydrogen consists of several discrete lines that converge at higher energies. These visible lines belong to the Balmer series, in which electrons fall from higher energy levels to the n = 2 level. Explain why the lines in the Balmer series become closer together and eventually converge at the high-energy end of the spectrum.

## Problem 15
A photon emitted by a hydrogen atom has a frequency of 4.57 × 10¹⁴ Hz. Calculate the energy of this photon in joules. (Planck's constant, h = 6.63 × 10⁻³⁴ J s)

## Problem 16
The red line in the hydrogen emission spectrum (Balmer series, n = 3 → n = 2) has a wavelength of 656 nm. Calculate the energy of this photon in joules. (h = 6.63 × 10⁻³⁴ J s, c = 3.00 × 10⁸ m s⁻¹)

## Problem 17
The convergence limit of the Lyman series (transitions to n = 1) in the hydrogen emission spectrum occurs at a wavelength of 91.2 nm. Calculate the ionisation energy of a hydrogen atom from its ground state, expressing your answer in joules per atom and in kilojoules per mole. (h = 6.63 × 10⁻³⁴ J s, c = 3.00 × 10⁸ m s⁻¹, L = 6.02 × 10²³ mol⁻¹)

## Problem 18
Explain the general trend in first ionisation energy across Period 3 (from sodium to argon). Refer to nuclear charge, electron shielding, and atomic radius in your answer.

## Problem 19
Explain the general trend in first ionisation energy down Group 1 (from lithium to caesium). Refer to atomic radius, electron shielding, and the distance of the valence electron from the nucleus in your answer.

## Problem 20
The first ionisation energies of beryllium and boron are 900 kJ mol⁻¹ and 799 kJ mol⁻¹ respectively. Explain why boron has a lower first ionisation energy than beryllium, despite boron having a greater nuclear charge.

## Problem 21
The first ionisation energies of nitrogen and oxygen are 1402 kJ mol⁻¹ and 1314 kJ mol⁻¹ respectively. Explain why oxygen has a lower first ionisation energy than nitrogen, despite the general trend of increasing ionisation energy across a period.

## Problem 22
The successive ionisation energies (in kJ mol⁻¹) of element X are: IE₁ = 496, IE₂ = 4562, IE₃ = 6912, IE₄ = 9544, IE₅ = 13353. Identify the group of the periodic table to which element X belongs and explain your reasoning.

## Problem 23
The first four ionisation energies (in kJ mol⁻¹) of an element are: 578, 1817, 2745, 11578. Identify the group of this element and explain the significance of the large jump in ionisation energy between IE₃ and IE₄.

## Problem 24
The first three ionisation energies (in kJ mol⁻¹) of an unknown main-group element are: IE₁ = 738, IE₂ = 1451, IE₃ = 7733. Identify the group to which this element belongs and predict the formula of its chloride (compound with chlorine).

## Problem 25
Explain, in terms of electron configurations and subshell stability, why chromium (Z = 24) and copper (Z = 29) have anomalous electron configurations. Discuss the concept of "special stability" associated with certain d-subshell configurations.

## Problem 26
Explain why successive ionisation energies of an element always increase (i.e., IE₁ < IE₂ < IE₃ < ...). In your answer, refer to the changing electrostatic forces between the nucleus and the remaining electrons after each ionisation.

## Problem 27
Explain the relationship between the lines observed in an atomic emission spectrum and the electron transitions occurring within the atom. Why is each line at a specific wavelength, and why is the spectrum of each element unique?

## Problem 28
An element is located in Period 3 and Group 16 of the periodic table. Write its full electron configuration. Predict whether its first ionisation energy is higher or lower than that of the Group 17 element in the same period, and justify your prediction.
