## Problem 4
The five stages of a mass spectrometer are:

1. **Vaporisation:** The sample is heated to convert it into a gas, so that individual particles can be separated and analysed. A gaseous sample is necessary for the subsequent stages.

2. **Ionisation:** The gaseous atoms or molecules are bombarded with high-energy electrons, which knock out electrons from the sample particles, creating positive ions (cations). This is essential because only charged particles can be accelerated and deflected by electric and magnetic fields.

3. **Acceleration:** The positive ions pass through an electric field (a potential difference), which accelerates them to a high velocity. All ions with the same charge gain the same kinetic energy, so lighter ions reach higher speeds than heavier ones.

4. **Deflection:** The accelerated ions enter a magnetic field, which exerts a force perpendicular to their direction of motion, causing them to follow a curved path. The radius of curvature depends on the mass-to-charge ratio (m/z): ions with a smaller m/z ratio are deflected more (they follow a tighter curve), while ions with a larger m/z ratio are deflected less.

5. **Detection:** Ions of a particular m/z ratio reach the detector, where they generate an electrical current proportional to their abundance. By varying the magnetic field strength, ions of different m/z values can be directed to the detector sequentially, producing a mass spectrum.

The property that determines the degree of deflection is the **mass-to-charge ratio (m/z)** — lighter ions and ions with a higher charge are deflected more.

## Problem 6
**(a) Mass spectrum of an element:**
- **Number of isotopes:** Each peak at a different m/z value corresponds to a different isotope.
- **Relative isotopic masses:** The m/z value of each peak gives the mass of that isotope (since the charge is typically +1).
- **Relative abundances:** The intensity (height) of each peak relative to the others gives the relative abundance of each isotope. These data allow calculation of the relative atomic mass.

**(b) Mass spectrum of a compound:**
- **Molecular ion peak (M⁺):** The peak at the highest m/z value usually corresponds to the intact molecular ion and gives the relative molecular mass of the compound.
- **Fragment peaks:** Peaks at lower m/z values arise from fragmentation of the molecular ion. The pattern of fragments provides structural information — certain fragments are characteristic of particular functional groups or structural features.
- **Isotopic patterns:** The presence of elements with significant heavy isotopes (e.g., chlorine with ³⁵Cl and ³⁷Cl in a ~3:1 ratio, or bromine with ⁷⁹Br and ⁸¹Br in a ~1:1 ratio) produces characteristic peak patterns that can confirm the presence of those elements.

## Problem 9
**Step 1: Distribute 8 electrons in order of filling.**
1s² 2s² 2p⁴

**Step 2: Draw the orbital diagram using boxes (□) for orbitals and arrows (↑↓) for electrons:**
```
1s:  [↑↓]
2s:  [↑↓]
2p:  [↑↓] [↑ ] [↑ ]
     (2pₓ) (2pᵧ) (2p)
```

**Step 3: Explain the rules.**
- **Pauli exclusion principle:** Each orbital holds at most two electrons, and they must have opposite spins (shown by ↑ and ↓ within the same box).
- **Hund's rule:** The three 2p orbitals are degenerate (same energy). The 4 electrons occupy them so that each orbital receives one electron (all with parallel spins, shown as ↑) before any orbital receives a second electron. Thus: two orbitals are singly occupied, one is doubly occupied.
- **Aufbau principle:** Electrons fill the lowest energy subshells first (1s before 2s, 2s before 2p).

*Common pitfall:* Pairing electrons in one 2p orbital while leaving another 2p orbital empty. Hund's rule requires maximum multiplicity — electrons spread out before pairing.

## Problem 10
**Step 1: State the predicted configuration.** Applying the Aufbau principle straightforwardly: after [Ar], we would expect 4s² 3d⁴, giving: **1s² 2s² 2p⁶ 3s² 3p⁶ 4s² 3d⁴**.

**Step 2: State the actual configuration.**
The experimentally determined configuration of chromium is: **1s² 2s² 2p⁶ 3s² 3p⁶ 4s¹ 3d⁵**.

**Step 3: Explain the anomaly.**
One electron is "promoted" from 4s to 3d, creating a half-filled 4s subshell (4s¹) and a half-filled 3d subshell (3d⁵). A half-filled d subshell (d⁵) has special stability because all five d orbitals are singly occupied with parallel spins, which minimises electron-electron repulsion and maximises exchange energy. The extra stability gained by having both the 4s and 3d subshells half-filled outweighs the energy cost of promoting the electron. This is an energetic trade-off: the system achieves a lower overall energy with 4s¹ 3d⁵ than with 4s² 3d⁴.

*Common pitfall:* Writing [Ar] 3d⁴ 4s² — this is not correct for chromium. Also, memorising the anomalous configurations without understanding why: it's about the special stability of half-filled and fully-filled subshells.

## Problem 12
**Step 1: Write the electron configuration of neutral Fe (Z = 26).**
Iron follows the normal Aufbau order: **[Ar] 4s² 3d⁶** (or more fully: 1s² 2s² 2p⁶ 3s² 3p⁶ 4s² 3d⁶).

**Step 2: Remove electrons to form Fe²⁺.**
Transition metals lose electrons from the 4s subshell first (highest n), then from 3d if further oxidation occurs. For Fe²⁺, remove both 4s electrons.

**Step 3: Write the configuration of Fe²⁺.**
**[Ar] 3d⁶**

**Explanation:** The 4s electrons are, on average, farther from the nucleus than 3d electrons (n = 4 vs. n = 3). Despite filling first in the neutral atom, the 4s electrons are the valence electrons and are more easily removed. Once the atom is ionised, the effective nuclear charge experienced by the remaining electrons increases, and the 3d subshell drops below 4s in energy. Therefore, 4s electrons are lost first when cations form.

## Problem 13
**Part 1 — Why 4s fills before 3d in neutral atoms:**
In a neutral atom, the 4s subshell has a slightly lower energy than the 3d subshell. This occurs because the 4s orbital penetrates closer to the nucleus (it has greater radial probability density near the nucleus than 3d despite having a higher principal quantum number). Electrons fill the lowest available energy level first (Aufbau principle), so 4s fills before 3d. For potassium (Z = 19) and calcium (Z = 20), the 4s subshell fills: K is [Ar] 4s¹ and Ca is [Ar] 4s².

**Part 2 — Why 4s electrons are lost before 3d in cations:**
Once electrons are removed from an atom, the effective nuclear charge experienced by the remaining electrons increases. This causes the energies of all subshells to shift, but crucially, the 3d subshell energy drops below the 4s subshell energy. In the cation, the 3d electrons are now more tightly bound than the 4s electrons. Additionally, 4s electrons are in the shell with n = 4 and are, on average, farther from the nucleus, making them easier to remove. Therefore, the outermost electrons (highest n) — the 4s electrons — are ionised first.

## Problem 14
The Balmer series consists of transitions where electrons fall from higher energy levels (n ≥ 3) down to n = 2. The key points are:

**Why the lines converge:**
The energy levels in hydrogen are given by Eₙ = −Rₕ/n² (where Rₕ is the Rydberg constant). As n increases, the energy levels get progressively closer together. For example, the gap between n = 2 and n = 3 is much larger than the gap between n = 7 and n = 8. Therefore:
- The n = 3 → 2 transition produces a photon of relatively low energy (red light, 656 nm).
- The n = 4 → 2 transition produces a photon of higher energy (blue-green, 486 nm).
- The n = 5 → 2, n = 6 → 2, etc., produce progressively higher-energy photons, with the wavelengths becoming closer and closer together.

**Why they converge at a limit:**
As n → ∞, the energy of the upper level approaches zero (the ionisation limit). The transition from n = ∞ to n = 2 has a finite, maximum energy, corresponding to the **convergence limit** of the Balmer series (at 365 nm, in the ultraviolet). No transition can have more energy than this within the Balmer series, because there are no energy levels above n = ∞. Photons beyond this energy correspond to ionisation of the atom from the n = 2 level.

*Common pitfall:* Thinking the convergence limit has infinite energy. It approaches a finite value, equal to the ionisation energy from that lower level (n = 2 for Balmer).

## Problem 18
Across Period 3 (Na → Ar), the first ionisation energy **generally increases**. The explanation involves three factors:

1. **Increasing nuclear charge:** As you move from Na (Z = 11) to Ar (Z = 18), the number of protons in the nucleus increases. This increases the attractive force on the electrons.

2. **Similar shielding:** All electrons being added across the period go into the same principal quantum shell (n = 3). The inner-shell electrons (1s² 2s² 2p⁶) provide approximately constant shielding for the outermost electrons. The added electrons in the same shell do not shield each other effectively.

3. **Decreasing atomic radius:** The increasing nuclear charge, with roughly constant shielding, pulls the electron cloud inward. The atomic radius decreases across the period, so the outermost electrons are held more tightly and require more energy to remove.

The combination of these factors means it becomes progressively harder to remove an electron, so IE₁ increases. Notable exceptions (Al vs. Mg, S vs. P) are explained by subshell effects (see Problems 20 and 21).

---

## Problem 19
Down Group 1 (Li → Na → K → Rb → Cs), the first ionisation energy **decreases**. The explanation involves:

1. **Increasing atomic radius:** Each element down the group adds a new principal quantum shell (n = 2 for Li, n = 3 for Na, n = 4 for K, etc.). The valence electron is in a shell that is progressively farther from the nucleus.

2. **Increasing shielding:** Each new shell of inner electrons shields the valence electron from the full nuclear charge. Although the nuclear charge also increases down the group (Li Z = 3, Na Z = 11, K Z = 19, etc.), the additional nuclear charge is almost entirely cancelled by the additional shielding from the new inner shells.

3. **Weaker electrostatic attraction:** Because the valence electron is farther from the nucleus and is shielded by more inner electrons, the effective nuclear charge experienced by the valence electron does not increase significantly. The electrostatic attraction between the nucleus and the valence electron is therefore weaker, making the electron easier to remove.

Overall, the dominant factor is the increasing distance of the valence electron from the nucleus, which outweighs the increasing nuclear charge. Thus, IE₁ decreases down the group.

## Problem 20
Boron (B, Z = 5) has a lower first ionisation energy than beryllium (Be, Z = 4) despite having one more proton. The explanation lies in the subshell being ionised:

- **Be:** electron configuration is 1s² 2s². The electron being removed comes from a **filled 2s subshell**, which is relatively stable.
- **B:** electron configuration is 1s² 2s² 2p¹. The electron being removed comes from a **2p subshell**.

The 2p subshell is at a **higher energy level** than the 2s subshell within the same principal quantum shell (n = 2). Because the 2p electron is less tightly bound to the nucleus than a 2s electron, less energy is required to remove it. Additionally, the 2p electron is better shielded from the nucleus than 2s electrons, as the 2p orbital has lower penetration (its radial distribution is farther from the nucleus on average). Together, these effects make boron's first ionisation energy lower than beryllium's.

*Common pitfall:* Attributing the drop entirely to shielding without discussing penetration and subshell energy differences. The key point is that the electron is removed from a higher-energy, less-penetrating orbital.

## Problem 21
Oxygen (O, Z = 8) has a lower first ionisation energy than nitrogen (N, Z = 7), which appears to contradict the general trend. The explanation involves electron pairing in the 2p subshell:

- **N:** electron configuration is 1s² 2s² 2p³. The three 2p electrons occupy the three degenerate 2p orbitals singly, with parallel spins (Hund's rule). This is a **half-filled subshell**, which has special stability due to maximum exchange energy and minimal electron-electron repulsion.

- **O:** electron configuration is 1s² 2s² 2p⁴. The fourth 2p electron must pair up with one of the already-occupied 2p orbitals, creating a doubly-occupied orbital.

When two electrons occupy the same orbital, they experience **significant electron-electron repulsion** (they are in the same region of space and repel each other electrostatically). Less energy is therefore required to remove one of these paired electrons from oxygen than to remove an unpaired electron from nitrogen's stable half-filled arrangement. The extra stability of nitrogen's half-filled 2p subshell outweighs the small increase in nuclear charge from N to O.

*Common pitfall:* Confusing this with the Be→B anomaly. Be→B involves different subshells (2s vs. 2p), while N→O involves electron pairing within the same subshell (2p).

## Problem 25
Both chromium and copper have anomalous electron configurations because of the **special stability of half-filled (d⁵) and fully-filled (d¹⁰) subshells**.

**Chromium (Z = 24):** Predicted: [Ar] 4s² 3d⁴. Actual: [Ar] 4s¹ 3d⁵.
By promoting one electron from 4s to 3d, chromium achieves a half-filled 4s (4s¹) and a half-filled 3d (3d⁵). A half-filled d subshell has all five d orbitals singly occupied with parallel spins. This minimises electron-electron repulsion (electrons in different orbitals are farther apart) and maximises **exchange energy** — a quantum mechanical stabilisation that arises from having electrons with parallel spins.

**Copper (Z = 29):** Predicted: [Ar] 4s² 3d⁹. Actual: [Ar] 4s¹ 3d¹⁰.
By promoting one electron from 4s to 3d, copper achieves a half-filled 4s and a **fully-filled 3d** (d¹⁰). A fully-filled d subshell is spherically symmetric and has maximum exchange energy, providing extra stability.

In both cases, the energy gained from the special d-subshell configuration outweighs the energy cost of promoting an electron from 4s to 3d. The system settles into the lower-energy (more stable) configuration.

*Common pitfall:* Thinking these are the only anomalies. Other elements also show subtle effects (e.g., niobium, molybdenum, ruthenium, rhodium, palladium, silver, platinum, gold), but Cr and Cu are the most important for IB.

## Problem 26
Successive ionisation energies always increase (IE₁ < IE₂ < IE₃ < ...) for any given element. The reasons are:

1. **Increasing effective nuclear charge per electron:** After each electron is removed, the positive charge of the nucleus is now spread over fewer electrons. The remaining electrons each experience a greater effective nuclear attraction. For example, after removing one electron to form M⁺, the remaining electrons are held more tightly than in the neutral atom M.

2. **Decreasing electron-electron repulsion:** With fewer electrons, there is less mutual repulsion among the remaining electrons. The cloud contracts slightly, bringing electrons closer to the nucleus and increasing the energy needed to remove the next electron.

3. **Stable core configurations:** When all valence electrons have been removed, the next electron must be removed from a filled inner shell that has a noble gas configuration. This requires a dramatically larger amount of energy (producing the "jump" discussed in Problems 22–24). Even within the valence shell, each successive removal encounters a progressively more positively charged ion.

The distinction between "gradual increase" (removing electrons from the same subshell) and "dramatic jump" (breaking into a filled inner shell) is what makes successive ionisation energies useful for determining an element's group.

## Problem 27
An **atomic emission spectrum** is produced when atoms in an excited state return to lower energy levels, emitting photons. The relationship between spectral lines and electron transitions is as follows:

**Why each line is at a specific wavelength:**
Electrons in atoms exist in discrete (quantised) energy levels. When an electron falls from a higher energy level (E₂) to a lower one (E₁), it emits a photon whose energy equals the difference: ΔE = E₂ − E₁ = hν = hc/λ. Since the energy levels are quantised, only specific ΔE values are possible, and therefore only specific wavelengths appear. Each line in the spectrum corresponds to one allowed transition between two energy levels.

**Why the spectrum of each element is unique:**
Each element has a unique nuclear charge and electron configuration, which produces a unique set of energy levels. The spacings between these levels are different for every element. Consequently, the possible values of ΔE — and therefore the wavelengths of emitted photons — are unique to each element. The emission (or absorption) spectrum acts as a "fingerprint" that can be used to identify the element, much like a barcode.

For hydrogen, the spectrum is relatively simple (one electron), and the lines fall into series: Lyman (to n = 1, UV), Balmer (to n = 2, visible), Paschen (to n = 3, IR), etc. For multi-electron atoms, the spectra are much more complex due to electron-electron interactions and subshell splitting.

**Justification:** Across Period 3, first ionisation energy generally increases due to increasing nuclear charge with similar shielding. Chlorine has one more proton than sulfur (Z = 17 vs. Z = 16). Both elements remove an electron from the 3p subshell, and the shielding from inner electrons (1s² 2s² 2p⁶) is identical. Chlorine's greater nuclear charge exerts a stronger attraction on the valence electrons, making its first ionisation energy higher.

*Additional nuance:* Sulfur has one paired electron in its 3p subshell (3p⁴: one doubly-occupied orbital, two singly-occupied), while chlorine has two paired electrons (3p⁵: two doubly-occupied, one singly-occupied). The electron-electron repulsion in the paired orbitals slightly reduces the ionisation energy of both elements relative to what a simple nuclear-charge model would predict, but the nuclear charge effect dominates, so Cl > S overall.

*Common pitfall:* Confusing the S/P anomaly trend. The anomalous drop from Group 15 to Group 16 (N to O, or P to S) is a *local* anomaly within the general increasing trend. So S has a lower IE₁ than P, but Cl still has a higher IE₁ than S because Cl is two groups further along.
