# Unit 8: Redox &amp; Electrochemistry — Solutions

## Problem 1

**Rules for assigning oxidation numbers**: The sum of oxidation numbers equals the overall charge. Oxygen is usually −2. For monatomic ions, the oxidation number equals the charge.

**(a) $\ce{MnO4-}$**: O is −2 each (4 × −2 = −8). Total charge is −1, so Mn = −1 − (−8) = **+7**.

**(b) $\ce{Cr2O7^{2-}}$**: O is −2 each (7 × −2 = −14). Total charge is −2. Let the oxidation number of Cr be $x$. Since there are 2 Cr atoms: $2x + (-14) = -2$, so $2x = +12$, $x = +6$.

Each chromium atom has an oxidation number of **+6**.

**(c) $\ce{S2O3^{2-}}$**: O is −2 each (3 × −2 = −6). Total charge is −2. Let the oxidation number of S be $x$. $2x + (-6) = -2$, so $2x = +4$, $x = +2$.

Each sulfur atom has an oxidation number of **+2**. (Note: the two S atoms are not equivalent in thiosulfate; the average is +2, with the central S at +6 and the terminal S at −2. However, the IB usually expects the average value.)

**(d) $\ce{Fe3O4}$**: This is a neutral compound. O is −2 each (4 × −2 = −8). Let Fe have oxidation number $x$. $3x + (-8) = 0$, so $3x = +8$, $x = +\frac{8}{3}$.

The average oxidation number of Fe is **+8/3**. (In reality, $\ce{Fe3O4}$ is a mixed oxide: $\ce{FeO·Fe2O3}$, containing $\ce{Fe^{2+}}$ and $\ce{Fe^{3+}}$.)

**Pitfall**: In $\ce{Fe3O4}$, students often try to force an integer oxidation number. Fractional oxidation numbers are acceptable as *average* values in such mixed-valence compounds.

## Problem 3

$$\ce{2Fe^{3+}(aq) + 2I-(aq) -> 2Fe^{2+}(aq) + I2(aq)}$$

**Oxidation number changes**:
- Iron: $\ce{Fe^{3+}}$ (+3) → $\ce{Fe^{2+}}$ (+2). The oxidation number **decreases** by 1. Iron is **reduced**.
- Iodine: $\ce{I-}$ (−1) → $\ce{I2}$ (0). The oxidation number **increases** by 1 per I atom. Iodide is **oxidised**.

**Definitions**:
- The **oxidising agent** (oxidant) is the species that causes oxidation by accepting electrons. Here, $\ce{Fe^{3+}}$ accepts an electron from $\ce{I-}$, so **$\ce{Fe^{3+}}$ is the oxidising agent**.
- The **reducing agent** (reductant) is the species that causes reduction by donating electrons. Here, $\ce{I-}$ donates an electron to $\ce{Fe^{3+}}$, so **$\ce{I-}$ is the reducing agent**.

**Memory aid**: The oxidising agent is itself reduced; the reducing agent is itself oxidised. "OIL RIG" — Oxidation Is Loss (of electrons), Reduction Is Gain (of electrons).

## Problem 4

**(a) $\ce{Zn(s) + CuSO4(aq) -> ZnSO4(aq) + Cu(s)}$**

Redox reaction: Yes. Zn goes from 0 to +2 (oxidised). Cu goes from +2 to 0 (reduced).

Species oxidised: $\ce{Zn}$. Species reduced: $\ce{Cu^{2+}}$.

**(b) $\ce{AgNO3(aq) + NaCl(aq) -> AgCl(s) + NaNO3(aq)}$**

Redox reaction: No. This is a precipitation (double displacement) reaction. The oxidation numbers of all species remain unchanged: Ag (+1), N (+5), O (−2), Na (+1), Cl (−1). No electron transfer occurs.

**(c) $\ce{2H2O2(aq) -> 2H2O(l) + O2(g)}$**

Redox reaction: Yes. In $\ce{H2O2}$, O has an oxidation number of −1 (since H is +1 each, $2(+1) + 2x = 0$, $x = -1$). In $\ce{H2O}$, O is −2; in $\ce{O2}$, O is 0.

Here, one oxygen atom in each $\ce{H2O2}$ is reduced (from −1 to −2) and the other is oxidised (from −1 to 0). This is also an example of **disproportionation** — the same element (oxygen) is simultaneously oxidised and reduced.

## Problem 5

**Disproportionation** is a redox reaction in which a single element is simultaneously oxidised and reduced — the same species (in the same oxidation state) acts as both the oxidising agent and the reducing agent.

**Analysis of the reaction**: $\ce{3Cl2(g) + 6NaOH(aq) -> 5NaCl(aq) + NaClO3(aq) + 3H2O(l)}$

Assign oxidation numbers to chlorine in each species:
- $\ce{Cl2}$: elemental chlorine, oxidation number = **0**.
- $\ce{NaCl}$ (or $\ce{Cl-}$): oxidation number = **−1**.
- $\ce{NaClO3}$ (or $\ce{ClO3-}$): O is −2 each (3 × −2 = −6), charge is −1, so Cl + (−6) = −1, Cl = **+5**.

In the reaction, chlorine atoms in $\ce{Cl2}$ (O.N. = 0) are:
- **Reduced** to $\ce{Cl-}$ (O.N. = −1) — a decrease of 1 in oxidation number. Five out of six Cl atoms follow this path.
- **Oxidised** to $\ce{ClO3-}$ (O.N. = +5) — an increase of 5 in oxidation number. One out of six Cl atoms follows this path.

Since chlorine (in the form of $\ce{Cl2}$) is both oxidised and reduced in the same reaction, this is disproportionation.

## Problem 6

**Half-equation**: $\ce{Cr2O7^{2-} -> Cr^{3+}}$ (reduction in acidic solution)

**Step 1: Balance chromium atoms.**
There are 2 Cr on the left and 1 on the right:
$$\ce{Cr2O7^{2-} -> 2Cr^{3+}}$$

**Step 2: Balance oxygen atoms by adding water.**
There are 7 O on the left and 0 on the right. Add 7 $\ce{H2O}$ to the right:
$$\ce{Cr2O7^{2-} -> 2Cr^{3+} + 7H2O}$$

**Step 3: Balance hydrogen atoms by adding $\ce{H+}$.**
There are 0 H on the left and 14 H on the right (7 × 2). Add 14 $\ce{H+}$ to the left:
$$\ce{Cr2O7^{2-} + 14H+ -> 2Cr^{3+} + 7H2O}$$

**Step 4: Balance charge by adding electrons.**
Left: (−2) + 14(+1) = +12. Right: 2(+3) + 7(0) = +6. To balance, add 6 $\ce{e-}$ to the left:
$$\ce{Cr2O7^{2-} + 14H+ + 6e- -> 2Cr^{3+} + 7H2O}$$

**Check**: Atoms: Cr: 2=2, O: 7=7, H: 14=14. Charge: (−2) + 14 + (−6) = +6 = 2(+3). ✓

**Pitfall**: Always add $\ce{H+}$ (not $\ce{H3O+}$ or $\ce{OH-}$) when balancing in acidic solution. The IB expects $\ce{H+}$ in half-equations for acidic conditions.

## Problem 7

**Half-equation**: $\ce{H2O2 -> O2}$ (oxidation in acidic solution)

**Step 1: Balance atoms other than O and H.**
Hydrogen atoms are already balanced (2 on each side, but let's proceed systematically).

**Step 2: Balance oxygen atoms.**
There are 2 O on the left and 2 O on the right — already balanced. No water needed.

**Step 3: Balance hydrogen atoms.**
There are 2 H on the left and 0 on the right. Add 2 $\ce{H+}$ to the right:
$$\ce{H2O2 -> O2 + 2H+}$$

**Step 4: Balance charge with electrons.**
Left: 0. Right: 2(+1) = +2. Add 2 $\ce{e-}$ to the right:
$$\ce{H2O2 -> O2 + 2H+ + 2e-}$$

**Check**: H: 2=2, O: 2=2. Charge: 0 = 0 + 2 − 2 = 0. ✓

**Interpretation**: In $\ce{H2O2}$, oxygen has an oxidation number of −1. In $\ce{O2}$, oxygen has an oxidation number of 0. This is oxidation (increase in O.N.), consistent with electrons being produced on the right.

## Problem 8

**Given half-equations:**
$$\ce{MnO4- + 8H+ + 5e- -> Mn^{2+} + 4H2O} \quad \text{(reduction)}$$
$$\ce{C2O4^{2-} -> 2CO2 + 2e-} \quad \text{(oxidation)}$$

**Step 1: Equalise electrons.**
The reduction half-equation involves 5 electrons; the oxidation involves 2. The LCM of 5 and 2 is 10. Multiply the reduction by 2 and the oxidation by 5:

$$2\ce{MnO4- + 16H+ + 10e- -> 2Mn^{2+} + 8H2O}$$
$$5\ce{C2O4^{2-} -> 10CO2 + 10e-}$$

**Step 2: Add the half-equations and cancel electrons.**

$$2\ce{MnO4- + 16H+ + 10e- + 5C2O4^{2-} -> 2Mn^{2+} + 8H2O + 10CO2 + 10e-}$$

Cancelling 10 $\ce{e-}$ from both sides:

$$\ce{2MnO4- + 16H+ + 5C2O4^{2-} -> 2Mn^{2+} + 8H2O + 10CO2}$$

**Oxidising agent**: $\ce{MnO4-}$ (it is reduced, accepting electrons from $\ce{C2O4^{2-}}$).

## Problem 9

**Half-equations (acidic solution):**

*Reduction*: $\ce{MnO4- + 8H+ + 5e- -> Mn^{2+} + 4H2O}$

*Oxidation*: $\ce{Fe^{2+} -> Fe^{3+} + e-}$

**Equalise electrons**: LCM of 5 and 1 is 5. Multiply oxidation half-equation by 5:

$$5\ce{Fe^{2+} -> 5Fe^{3+} + 5e-}$$

**Add**: $\ce{MnO4- + 8H+ + 5e- + 5Fe^{2+} -> Mn^{2+} + 4H2O + 5Fe^{3+} + 5e-}$

Cancel 5 $\ce{e-}$:

$$\ce{MnO4- + 8H+ + 5Fe^{2+} -> Mn^{2+} + 4H2O + 5Fe^{3+}}$$

This is the classic manganate(VII)–iron(II) titration reaction used in redox titrations. The purple $\ce{MnO4-}$ is decolourised as it reacts.

## Problem 10

**Half-equations:**

*Oxidation (iodide to iodine)*: $\ce{2I- -> I2 + 2e-}$

*Reduction (hydrogen peroxide to water)*: $\ce{H2O2 + 2H+ + 2e- -> 2H2O}$

(To derive the reduction: $\ce{H2O2}$ has 2 O. To form 2 $\ce{H2O}$, need 2 H from $\ce{2H+}$. O: 2=2 ✓. H: 2+2=4 ✓. Charge: +2−2=0, products: 0 ✓.)

**Combine**: Both half-equations involve 2 electrons, so add directly:

$$\ce{H2O2 + 2H+ + 2e- + 2I- -> 2H2O + I2 + 2e-}$$

Cancelling electrons:

$$\ce{H2O2 + 2H+ + 2I- -> 2H2O + I2}$$

**Reducing agent**: $\ce{I-}$ (the iodide ion is oxidised, donating electrons to $\ce{H2O2}$).

**Pitfall**: Hydrogen peroxide can act as either an oxidising agent or a reducing agent. Here, it is the oxidising agent (being reduced from O.N. = −1 in $\ce{H2O2}$ to −2 in $\ce{H2O}$).

## Problem 11

**(a) Anode and cathode identification:**

- The electrode with the **more negative** (or less positive) $E^{\ominus}$ value undergoes **oxidation** and is the **anode**.
- $E^{\ominus}(\ce{Zn^{2+}/Zn}) = -0.76\ \text{V}$ is more negative than $E^{\ominus}(\ce{Cu^{2+}/Cu}) = +0.34\ \text{V}$.

Therefore:
- **Anode (oxidation)**: Zinc electrode.
- **Cathode (reduction)**: Copper electrode.

**(b) Half-equations:**

- **Anode (oxidation)**: $\ce{Zn(s) -> Zn^{2+}(aq) + 2e-}$
- **Cathode (reduction)**: $\ce{Cu^{2+}(aq) + 2e- -> Cu(s)}$

**Overall cell reaction**: $\ce{Zn(s) + Cu^{2+}(aq) -> Zn^{2+}(aq) + Cu(s)}$

**(c) Direction of electron flow:**

Electrons flow through the external circuit from the anode (zinc, where electrons are produced) to the cathode (copper, where electrons are consumed). So: **zinc → copper** (through the wire).

**(d) $E^{\ominus}_{\text{cell}}$ calculation:**

$$E^{\ominus}_{\text{cell}} = E^{\ominus}_{\text{cathode}} - E^{\ominus}_{\text{anode}} = (+0.34\ \text{V}) - (-0.76\ \text{V}) = +1.10\ \text{V}$$

Since $E^{\ominus}_{\text{cell}} > 0$, the reaction is **spontaneous** under standard conditions. This is the classic Daniell cell.

## Problem 12

**Step 1: Determine anode and cathode.**

$E^{\ominus}(\ce{Ni^{2+}/Ni}) = -0.26\ \text{V}$ is more negative than $E^{\ominus}(\ce{Ag+/Ag}) = +0.80\ \text{V}$.

- Anode (oxidation): Nickel. $\ce{Ni(s) -> Ni^{2+}(aq) + 2e-}$
- Cathode (reduction): Silver. $\ce{Ag+(aq) + e- -> Ag(s)}$

**Step 2: Calculate $E^{\ominus}_{\text{cell}}$.**

$$E^{\ominus}_{\text{cell}} = E^{\ominus}_{\text{cathode}} - E^{\ominus}_{\text{anode}} = (+0.80\ \text{V}) - (-0.26\ \text{V}) = +1.06\ \text{V}$$

**Step 3: Overall cell reaction.**

Equalise electrons: multiply Ag half-equation by 2:

$$\ce{Ni(s) -> Ni^{2+}(aq) + 2e-}$$
$$\ce{2Ag+(aq) + 2e- -> 2Ag(s)}$$

$$\ce{Ni(s) + 2Ag+(aq) -> Ni^{2+}(aq) + 2Ag(s)}$$

**Step 4: Electrode gaining mass.**

Silver metal is deposited at the cathode ($\ce{Ag+(aq) + e- -> Ag(s)}$), so the **silver electrode gains mass**. The nickel electrode loses mass as it dissolves.

## Problem 13

**Construction of the SHE**:

The standard hydrogen electrode consists of:
- A platinum electrode coated with finely divided platinum (platinum black), which acts as an inert surface for the electrode reaction and catalyses the $\ce{H2/H+}$ equilibrium.
- Hydrogen gas at a pressure of exactly $1\ \text{bar}$ ($100\ \text{kPa}$) bubbled over the platinum electrode.
- The electrode is immersed in a solution containing $\ce{H+}$ ions at an activity of exactly $1$ (effectively $1.0\ \text{mol}\ \text{dm}^{-3}$ $\ce{HCl}$).
- Temperature: 298 K.

The half-cell reaction is: $\ce{2H+(aq) + 2e- <=> H2(g)}$, and its potential is **defined as exactly $0.00\ \text{V}$**.

**Why the SHE is used**: All standard electrode potentials are measured relative to the SHE. By convention, we cannot measure the absolute potential of a single electrode — only potential *differences*. The SHE provides a universal zero-point reference against which all other half-cell potentials are reported.

**Arranging reducing agents**: Reducing agents are oxidised (lose electrons). The more negative the $E^{\ominus}$ value, the stronger the tendency for the reduced form to be oxidised, i.e., the stronger the reducing agent.

Order of **increasing** strength as reducing agents: Cu < Fe < Mg.

- $\ce{Cu}$ ($E^{\ominus} = +0.34\ \text{V}$): least willing to be oxidised — weakest reducing agent.
- $\ce{Fe}$ ($E^{\ominus} = -0.44\ \text{V}$): intermediate.
- $\ce{Mg}$ ($E^{\ominus} = -2.37\ \text{V}$): most negative — most willing to be oxidised — strongest reducing agent.

## Problem 14

**(a) Overall spontaneous cell reaction:**

$E^{\ominus}(\ce{Sn^{2+}/Sn}) = -0.14\ \text{V}$ is more negative, so Sn is oxidised at the anode:
$$\ce{Sn(s) -> Sn^{2+}(aq) + 2e-}$$

$E^{\ominus}(\ce{Fe^{3+}/Fe^{2+}}) = +0.77\ \text{V}$ is more positive, so $\ce{Fe^{3+}}$ is reduced at the cathode:
$$\ce{Fe^{3+}(aq) + e- -> Fe^{2+}(aq)}$$

Equalise electrons (×2 for Fe half-equation):
$$\ce{Sn(s) + 2Fe^{3+}(aq) -> Sn^{2+}(aq) + 2Fe^{2+}(aq)}$$

**(b) $E^{\ominus}_{\text{cell}}$:**

$$E^{\ominus}_{\text{cell}} = (+0.77) - (-0.14) = +0.91\ \text{V}$$

**(c) Salt bridge function:**

The salt bridge (typically a strip of filter paper soaked in saturated $\ce{KNO3}$ or a U-tube containing a gel with an inert electrolyte) serves two essential functions:

1. **Completes the electrical circuit** by allowing ions to flow between the two half-cells, maintaining charge neutrality.
2. **Prevents the mixing** of the two electrolyte solutions, which would allow direct chemical reaction (and loss of electrical energy as heat).

**If the salt bridge were removed**: The circuit would be broken, and current would stop flowing. Charge would build up in each half-cell — the anode compartment would become positively charged (from $\ce{Sn^{2+}}$ production) and the cathode compartment negatively charged (from $\ce{Fe^{3+}}$ consumption), opposing further electron transfer. The cell potential would rapidly drop to zero.

## Problem 15

**Identifying strongest oxidising agent**: The strongest oxidising agent is the species most easily reduced — i.e., the species with the **most positive** $E^{\ominus}$ value. Here, $\ce{Cl2}$ ($E^{\ominus} = +1.36\ \text{V}$) has the most positive $E^{\ominus}$, so **$\ce{Cl2}$ is the strongest oxidising agent**.

**Identifying strongest reducing agent**: The strongest reducing agent is the species most easily oxidised — i.e., the species whose reduced form has the **most negative** $E^{\ominus}$ value. Here, $\ce{Zn}$ ($E^{\ominus} = -0.76\ \text{V}$) has the most negative $E^{\ominus}$, so **$\ce{Zn}$ is the strongest reducing agent**.

**General relationship**:
- A **more positive** $E^{\ominus}$ means the oxidised form of the couple has a greater tendency to be reduced → stronger **oxidising agent**.
- A **more negative** $E^{\ominus}$ means the reduced form of the couple has a greater tendency to be oxidised → stronger **reducing agent**.

This can be visualised: at the top of the electrochemical series (most positive $E^{\ominus}$), we find the strongest oxidising agents (e.g., $\ce{F2}$, $\ce{MnO4-}$). At the bottom (most negative $E^{\ominus}$), we find the strongest reducing agents (e.g., $\ce{Li}$, $\ce{K}$, $\ce{Mg}$).

## Problem 16

**(a) $\ce{Cu(s) + Fe^{2+}(aq) -> Cu^{2+}(aq) + Fe(s)}$**

In the proposed direction:
- $\ce{Cu}$ is oxidised: $\ce{Cu -> Cu^{2+} + 2e-}$ ($E^{\ominus}_{\text{anode}} = +0.34\ \text{V}$)
- $\ce{Fe^{2+}}$ is reduced: $\ce{Fe^{2+} + 2e- -> Fe}$ ($E^{\ominus}_{\text{cathode}} = -0.44\ \text{V}$)

$$E^{\ominus}_{\text{cell}} = E^{\ominus}_{\text{cathode}} - E^{\ominus}_{\text{anode}} = (-0.44) - (+0.34) = -0.78\ \text{V}$$

$E^{\ominus}_{\text{cell}} < 0$, so the reaction is **not spontaneous** as written. The reverse reaction ($\ce{Fe + Cu^{2+} -> Fe^{2+} + Cu}$) would be spontaneous.

**(b) $\ce{2Fe^{3+}(aq) + 2I-(aq) -> 2Fe^{2+}(aq) + I2(aq)}$**

In the proposed direction:
- $\ce{I-}$ is oxidised: $\ce{2I- -> I2 + 2e-}$ ($E^{\ominus}_{\text{anode}} = +0.54\ \text{V}$)
- $\ce{Fe^{3+}}$ is reduced: $\ce{Fe^{3+} + e- -> Fe^{2+}}$ (×2) ($E^{\ominus}_{\text{cathode}} = +0.77\ \text{V}$)

$$E^{\ominus}_{\text{cell}} = (+0.77) - (+0.54) = +0.23\ \text{V}$$

$E^{\ominus}_{\text{cell}} > 0$, so the reaction **is spontaneous** as written.

**Pitfall**: When both $E^{\ominus}$ values are positive, you must still subtract the anode value from the cathode value. Don't assume both being positive means the reaction is spontaneous — it's the *difference* that matters.

## Problem 17

| Aspect | Voltaic (Galvanic) Cell | Electrolytic Cell |
|---|---|---|
| **Energy conversion** | Chemical → Electrical | Electrical → Chemical |
| **Spontaneity** | Spontaneous ($\Delta G < 0$) | Non-spontaneous ($\Delta G > 0$) |
| **Anode sign** | Negative (−) | Positive (+) |
| **Cathode sign** | Positive (+) | Negative (−) |
| **Anode process** | Oxidation | Oxidation |
| **Cathode process** | Reduction | Reduction |
| **Electron flow (external)** | Anode → Cathode | Anode → Cathode (but driven by external power source) |
| **Ion flow (salt bridge/internal)** | Anions → Anode, Cations → Cathode | Cations → Cathode, Anions → Anode |
| **External power source** | Not required | Required (battery or DC supply) |
| **Example** | Daniell cell, batteries | Electrolysis of NaCl, electroplating |

**Key similarities**: In both types of cell, **oxidation always occurs at the anode** and **reduction always occurs at the cathode**. Electrons always flow from anode to cathode in the external circuit. The sign convention differs because in a voltaic cell the anode is the source of electrons (negative), while in an electrolytic cell the anode is connected to the positive terminal of the power source.

**Diagram** (sketch description): A voltaic cell shows two half-cells connected by a salt bridge and a wire with a voltmeter. An electrolytic cell shows a single container with two electrodes connected to a battery; no salt bridge is needed.

## Problem 18

**Electrolysis of molten $\ce{NaCl}$:**

In molten $\ce{NaCl}$, the only ions present are $\ce{Na+}$ and $\ce{Cl-}$.

- **Cathode (reduction)**: $\ce{Na+ + e- -> Na(l)}$ — sodium metal is produced.
- **Anode (oxidation)**: $\ce{2Cl- -> Cl2(g) + 2e-}$ — chlorine gas is produced.

**Overall**: $\ce{2NaCl(l) -> 2Na(l) + Cl2(g)}$

**Why products differ from aqueous $\ce{NaCl}$ electrolysis**:

In **aqueous** solution, water molecules are also present and compete at the electrodes:

- **At the cathode**: Water is more easily reduced than $\ce{Na+}$ ($E^{\ominus}$ for $\ce{2H2O + 2e- -> H2 + 2OH-}$ is −0.83 V, far less negative than −2.71 V for $\ce{Na+ + e- -> Na}$). Hydrogen gas, not sodium, is produced.
- **At the anode**: In concentrated brine, $\ce{Cl-}$ oxidation ($E^{\ominus} = +1.36\ \text{V}$) competes with water oxidation ($E^{\ominus} = +1.23\ \text{V}$). Despite the slightly less favourable potential, chloride oxidation is kinetically favoured at high concentration, so chlorine is produced. In dilute $\ce{NaCl}$, oxygen would be produced instead.

Thus, molten $\ce{NaCl}$ electrolysis gives sodium and chlorine, while aqueous brine electrolysis gives hydrogen, chlorine, and sodium hydroxide.

## Problem 19

**Electrolysis of dilute $\ce{H2SO4}$ with inert (platinum) electrodes:**

In dilute sulfuric acid, the species present are $\ce{H+}$, $\ce{SO4^{2-}}$, and $\ce{H2O}$.

**(a) Half-equations:**

- **Cathode (reduction)**: $\ce{H+}$ is reduced in preference to water (though both are possible):
  $$\ce{2H+(aq) + 2e- -> H2(g)}$$

- **Anode (oxidation)**: Water is oxidised in preference to $\ce{SO4^{2-}}$ (oxidising sulfate would require an extremely high potential):
  $$\ce{2H2O(l) -> O2(g) + 4H+(aq) + 4e-}$$

**(b) Species oxidised and reduced:**

- **Oxidised**: Oxygen in $\ce{H2O}$ (O.N. increases from −2 to 0).
- **Reduced**: Hydrogen in $\ce{H+}$ (O.N. decreases from +1 to 0).

**(c) Overall equation:**

Equalise electrons (multiply cathode by 2):

$$\ce{4H+ + 4e- -> 2H2}$$
$$\ce{2H2O -> O2 + 4H+ + 4e-}$$

Add: $\ce{2H2O(l) -> 2H2(g) + O2(g)}$ (the $\ce{4H+}$ cancels).

**Why acid concentration increases**: The overall reaction shows that water is decomposed into hydrogen and oxygen gases. The $\ce{H+}$ and $\ce{SO4^{2-}}$ ions are not consumed (they act as spectator ions for the net electrolysis of water). As water is removed by electrolysis, the volume of the solution decreases while the amount of $\ce{H2SO4}$ remains constant, so the concentration of sulfuric acid increases.

## Problem 20

**Electrolysis of concentrated aqueous $\ce{NaCl}$ (brine) with inert electrodes:**

Species present: $\ce{Na+}$, $\ce{Cl-}$, $\ce{H2O}$.

**At the cathode (−):**

Competing reductions:
1. $\ce{Na+(aq) + e- -> Na(s)}$ — $E^{\ominus} = -2.71\ \text{V}$
2. $\ce{2H2O(l) + 2e- -> H2(g) + 2OH-(aq)}$ — $E^{\ominus} = -0.83\ \text{V}$

Water has a much less negative reduction potential, so it is reduced in preference. **Hydrogen gas** is produced at the cathode:

$$\ce{2H2O(l) + 2e- -> H2(g) + 2OH-(aq)}$$

**At the anode (+):**

Competing oxidations:
1. $\ce{2Cl-(aq) -> Cl2(g) + 2e-}$ — $E^{\ominus} = +1.36\ \text{V}$ (as oxidation)
2. $\ce{2H2O(l) -> O2(g) + 4H+(aq) + 4e-}$ — $E^{\ominus} = +1.23\ \text{V}$ (as oxidation)

Although the thermodynamic potential for oxygen evolution is slightly more favourable (+1.23 vs +1.36 V), the **high concentration of $\ce{Cl-}$** in brine and kinetic factors (overpotential for $\ce{O2}$ formation) mean that **chlorine gas** is the primary product at the anode:

$$\ce{2Cl-(aq) -> Cl2(g) + 2e-}$$

**Overall cell reaction**: $\ce{2NaCl(aq) + 2H2O(l) -> H2(g) + Cl2(g) + 2NaOH(aq)}$

**Pitfall**: Students often rely solely on $E^{\ominus}$ values without considering concentration effects and overpotential. In concentrated brine, chlorine is produced despite the slightly unfavourable thermodynamics because of kinetic factors.

## Problem 21

**Electrolysis of aqueous $\ce{CuSO4}$ with copper electrodes:**

With **active (copper) electrodes**, the electrode material itself participates in the reaction.

**At the anode (oxidation)**:

Instead of water or $\ce{SO4^{2-}}$ being oxidised, the copper electrode itself is oxidised:

$$\ce{Cu(s) -> Cu^{2+}(aq) + 2e-}$$

The copper anode **dissolves**, and the solution turns a deeper blue due to increased $\ce{Cu^{2+}}$ concentration.

**At the cathode (reduction)**:

$\ce{Cu^{2+}}$ ions in solution are reduced and deposited as copper metal:

$$\ce{Cu^{2+}(aq) + 2e- -> Cu(s)}$$

The cathode gains mass as a pink-brown copper coating forms.

**Overall observation**: Copper is transferred from the anode to the cathode. The anode loses mass and becomes thinner; the cathode gains mass and becomes coated with fresh copper. The concentration of $\ce{Cu^{2+}}$ in the solution remains approximately constant (rate of dissolution at anode ≈ rate of deposition at cathode). Impurities in the anode (less reactive metals) fall to the bottom as "anode sludge."

**Electroplating and electrorefining**:
- **Electroplating**: An object to be plated is made the cathode; the plating metal is the anode. Metal ions from the anode dissolve and deposit onto the cathode object, coating it.
- **Electrorefining**: An impure metal is made the anode; a pure metal sheet is the cathode. Pure metal is transferred from the impure anode to the pure cathode, leaving impurities behind.

**Step 1: Half-equation at the cathode.**
$$\ce{Ag+(aq) + e- -> Ag(s)}$$
This shows that **1 mole of electrons** deposits **1 mole of silver**.

**Step 2: Calculate total charge passed.**
$$Q = I \times t$$

## Problem 25

**Step 1: Identify the number of electrons transferred.**

From the balanced equation: $\ce{Zn(s) + Cu^{2+}(aq) -> Zn^{2+}(aq) + Cu(s)}$, zinc is oxidised (loses 2 $e-$) and copper is reduced (gains 2 $e-$). Therefore $n = 2$ moles of electrons per mole of reaction.

**Step 2: Apply $\Delta G^{\ominus} = -nFE^{\ominus}_{\text{cell}}$.**

$$\Delta G^{\ominus} = -2 \times 96\,500 \times 1.10$$
$$\Delta G^{\ominus} = -2 \times 96\,500 \times 1.10 = -212\,300\ \text{J}\ \text{mol}^{-1} \approx -212\ \text{kJ}\ \text{mol}^{-1}$$

**Step 3: Interpretation of the negative sign.**

The negative value of $\Delta G^{\ominus}$ indicates that the reaction is **thermodynamically spontaneous** under standard conditions. The cell can do useful electrical work. The magnitude (212 kJ per mole of reaction) represents the maximum non-expansion (electrical) work the cell can perform.

**Significance**: This equation links electrochemistry ($E^{\ominus}_{\text{cell}}$) with chemical thermodynamics ($\Delta G^{\ominus}$). A positive cell potential corresponds to a negative $\Delta G$, consistent with spontaneity. If $E^{\ominus}_{\text{cell}} < 0$, then $\Delta G^{\ominus} > 0$ and the reaction is non-spontaneous.

**Pitfall**: Always express $\Delta G$ in joules (not kJ) when using $F$ in C mol⁻¹. Convert at the end if desired.

## Problem 26

**Step 1: Determine the number of electrons transferred.**

Write the half-equations:

Oxidation: $\ce{Al(s) -> Al^{3+}(aq) + 3e-}$ (×2 to balance)
$$\ce{2Al(s) -> 2Al^{3+}(aq) + 6e-}$$

Reduction: $\ce{Ni^{2+}(aq) + 2e- -> Ni(s)}$ (×3 to balance)
$$\ce{3Ni^{2+}(aq) + 6e- -> 3Ni(s)}$$

Total electrons transferred: $n = 6$ moles of electrons per mole of reaction (as written).

## Problem 27

**Key data**:
- $\ce{F2(g) + 2e- <=> 2F-(aq)}$ — $E^{\ominus} = +2.87\ \text{V}$
- $\ce{Cl2(g) + 2e- <=> 2Cl-(aq)}$ — $E^{\ominus} = +1.36\ \text{V}$
- $\ce{O2(g) + 4H+(aq) + 4e- <=> 2H2O(l)}$ — $E^{\ominus} = +1.23\ \text{V}$

**Why $\ce{F2}$ cannot be prepared by electrolysis of aqueous fluoride**:

During the electrolysis of any aqueous solution, water is always present and competes at both electrodes. At the anode, the possible oxidations are:

1. Oxidation of $\ce{F-}$: $\ce{2F- -> F2 + 2e-}$ (required $E^{\ominus}_{\text{ox}} = -2.87\ \text{V}$, or as reduction: $+2.87\ \text{V}$)
2. Oxidation of $\ce{H2O}$: $\ce{2H2O -> O2 + 4H+ + 4e-}$ ($E^{\ominus}_{\text{ox}} = -1.23\ \text{V}$, or as reduction: $+1.23\ \text{V}$)

Water is oxidised more readily (less positive $E^{\ominus}$ for reduction means it is easier to oxidise). In fact, the oxidation of $\ce{F-}$ requires the highest potential of all common anions — even higher than the oxidation of water. So in aqueous solution, **oxygen is produced at the anode, not fluorine**. The fluoride ions simply remain in solution.

**Why chlorine *can* be obtained from brine**:

For chlorine: $\ce{2Cl- -> Cl2 + 2e-}$ ($E^{\ominus}_{\text{ox}} = -1.36\ \text{V}$, or $+1.36\ \text{V}$ as reduction).

Thermodynamically, water oxidation ($+1.23\ \text{V}$) is slightly more favourable than chloride oxidation ($+1.36\ \text{V}$). However, in **concentrated** brine:
- The high $[\ce{Cl-}]$ shifts the potential in favour of chlorine (Nernst equation).
- Water oxidation has a significant **overpotential** (kinetic barrier) on many electrode surfaces, making chlorine formation kinetically favoured.

These factors allow chlorine to be the major product in concentrated brine electrolysis. No such kinetic advantage exists for fluoride; the thermodynamic gap is too large.

**Conclusion**: Fluorine is produced industrially by electrolysis of **molten** $\ce{KHF2}$ (potassium hydrogen fluoride), where no water is present to compete.

## Problem 28

**Step 1: Understand the series connection.**

When electrolytic cells are connected in series, the **same current flows through both for the same time**, so the **same total charge** passes through each. Therefore, $n(e-)$ is the same for both cells.

**Step 2: Calculate $n(e-)$ from the copper data.**

Copper deposition: $\ce{Cu^{2+} + 2e- -> Cu}$. 2 moles of electrons deposit 1 mole of copper.

$$n(\ce{Cu}) = \frac{0.159}{63.5} = 0.002504\ \text{mol}$$
$$n(e-) = 2 \times n(\ce{Cu}) = 2 \times 0.002504 = 0.005008\ \text{mol}$$

**Step 3: Calculate mass of silver deposited.**

Silver deposition: $\ce{Ag+ + e- -> Ag}$. 1 mole of electrons deposits 1 mole of silver.

$$n(\ce{Ag}) = n(e-) = 0.005008\ \text{mol}$$
$$m(\ce{Ag}) = 0.005008 \times 107.9 = 0.540\ \text{g}$$

**Alternative approach using Faraday's laws directly**:

For cells in series: $\frac{m_1}{m_2} = \frac{M_1/n_1}{M_2/n_2}$, where $M$ is molar mass and $n$ is electrons per ion.

$$\frac{m(\ce{Cu})}{m(\ce{Ag})} = \frac{63.5/2}{107.9/1} = \frac{31.75}{107.9}$$
$$m(\ce{Ag}) = 0.159 \times \frac{107.9}{31.75} = 0.540\ \text{g}$$

**Pitfall**: Note the different $n$ values: $\ce{Cu^{2+}}$ requires 2 $e-$ per ion; $\ce{Ag+}$ requires only 1. This affects the mass ratio for cells in series.

## Problem 29

**Step 1: Assign oxidation numbers for lead.**

| Species | Calculation | O.N. of Pb |
|---|---|---|
| $\ce{Pb(s)}$ | Elemental form | **0** |
| $\ce{PbO2(s)}$ | O: −2 × 2 = −4; neutral: Pb + (−4) = 0; Pb = +4 | **+4** |
| $\ce{PbSO4(s)}$ | $\ce{SO4^{2-}}$ has charge −2; neutral compound: Pb + (−2) = 0; Pb = +2 | **+2** |

**Step 2: Identify oxidation and reduction.**

- $\ce{Pb(s)}$ (O.N. = 0) → $\ce{PbSO4}$ (O.N. = +2): Pb is **oxidised** (oxidation number increases by 2).
- $\ce{PbO2}$ (O.N. = +4) → $\ce{PbSO4}$ (O.N. = +2): Pb is **reduced** (oxidation number decreases by 2).

**Step 3: Number of electrons transferred.**

The oxidation: $\ce{Pb -> Pb^{2+} + 2e-}$ — 2 electrons lost.
The reduction: $\ce{PbO2 + 4H+ + SO4^{2-} + 2e- -> PbSO4 + 2H2O}$ — 2 electrons gained.

Per mole of reaction as written (involving 1 Pb atom of each type), **2 electrons** are transferred.

## Problem 30

**Given:**
$$\ce{MnO4- + 8H+ + 5e- <=> Mn^{2+} + 4H2O} \quad E^{\ominus} = +1.51\ \text{V}$$
$$\ce{Fe^{3+} + e- <=> Fe^{2+}} \quad E^{\ominus} = +0.77\ \text{V}$$

**(a) Anode and cathode:**

$E^{\ominus}(\ce{MnO4-/Mn^{2+}}) = +1.51\ \text{V}$ is more positive than $E^{\ominus}(\ce{Fe^{3+}/Fe^{2+}}) = +0.77\ \text{V}$. The more positive half-cell undergoes **reduction** and is the **cathode**.

- **Cathode**: $\ce{MnO4-/Mn^{2+}}$ half-cell.
- **Anode**: $\ce{Fe^{3+}/Fe^{2+}}$ half-cell (the less positive undergoes oxidation).

**(b) Half-equations and overall reaction:**

- **Cathode (reduction)**: $\ce{MnO4- + 8H+ + 5e- -> Mn^{2+} + 4H2O}$
- **Anode (oxidation)**: $\ce{Fe^{2+} -> Fe^{3+} + e-}$ (×5 to equalise electrons)

Equalise electrons: multiply anode by 5:

$$5\ce{Fe^{2+} -> 5Fe^{3+} + 5e-}$$

Add:

$$\ce{MnO4- + 8H+ + 5Fe^{2+} -> Mn^{2+} + 4H2O + 5Fe^{3+}}$$

**(c) $E^{\ominus}_{\text{cell}}$ and $\Delta G^{\ominus}$:**

$$E^{\ominus}_{\text{cell}} = E^{\ominus}_{\text{cathode}} - E^{\ominus}_{\text{anode}} = (+1.51) - (+0.77) = +0.74\ \text{V}$$

$n = 5$ (5 electrons transferred in the balanced equation).

$$\Delta G^{\ominus} = -nFE^{\ominus}_{\text{cell}} = -5 \times 96\,500 \times 0.74$$
$$\Delta G^{\ominus} = -357\,050\ \text{J}\ \text{mol}^{-1} \approx -357\ \text{kJ}\ \text{mol}^{-1}$$

The negative $\Delta G^{\ominus}$ confirms the reaction is spontaneous under standard conditions.

**Pitfall**: When both $E^{\ominus}$ values are positive, students sometimes forget that the *less* positive one still acts as the anode (oxidation). The species with the **more positive** $E^{\ominus}$ is always reduced (cathode); the species with the **less positive** (or more negative) $E^{\ominus}$ is always oxidised (anode).
