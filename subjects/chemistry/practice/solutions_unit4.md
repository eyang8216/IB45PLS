## Problem 1

| Process | Classification | Heat flow |
|---------|:---:|-----------|
| (a) Combustion of methane | **Exothermic** | Heat flows from system to surroundings |
| (b) Melting of ice | **Endothermic** | Heat flows from surroundings to system |
| (c) Condensation of steam | **Exothermic** | Heat flows from system to surroundings |
| (d) Ba(OH)₂·8H₂O + NH₄Cl | **Endothermic** | Heat flows from surroundings to system (the flask becomes cold) |
| (e) Photosynthesis | **Endothermic** | Energy (light) is absorbed from the surroundings |

**Key concept**: In an exothermic process, the system loses energy (ΔH < 0) and the surroundings gain heat. In an endothermic process, the system gains energy (ΔH > 0) and the surroundings lose heat.

## Problem 2

**(a) Standard enthalpy of formation of \ce{CO2(g)}**:

ΔH_f⦵[\ce{CO2(g)}] is the enthalpy change when **one mole** of \ce{CO2(g)} is formed from its elements in their standard states under standard conditions.

\ce{C(s, graphite) + O2(g) -> CO2(g)}  ΔH = ΔH_f⦵[\ce{CO2(g)}]

**(b) Standard enthalpy of combustion of \ce{C2H5OH(l)}**:

ΔH_c⦵[\ce{C2H5OH(l)}] is the enthalpy change when **one mole** of ethanol is completely burned in excess oxygen under standard conditions.

\ce{C2H5OH(l) + 3O2(g) -> 2CO2(g) + 3H2O(l)}  ΔH = ΔH_c⦵[\ce{C2H5OH(l)}]

**Standard conditions**: Pressure = 100 kPa (1 bar); temperature = 298 K (25 °C). All substances in their standard states (e.g., carbon as graphite, not diamond).

## Problem 3

**(a) Heat absorbed by water**:

q = mcΔT

m = 200 g (mass of water)
c = 4.18 J g⁻¹ K⁻¹
ΔT = 36.0 − 22.0 = 14.0 °C = 14.0 K

q = 200 × 4.18 × 14.0 = 11 704 J = **11.7 kJ**

**(b) Enthalpy of combustion**:

Moles of ethanol burned:
n = m/M = 0.500 g / 46.0 g mol⁻¹ = 0.01087 mol

ΔH_c = −q / n = −11.7 kJ / 0.01087 mol = **−1077 kJ mol⁻¹** (approximately −1080 kJ mol⁻¹)

(Note: the literature value is approximately −1367 kJ mol⁻¹.)

**(c) Why experimental value is less exothermic**:

1. **Heat loss to surroundings**: Not all the heat from combustion is transferred to the water; some escapes to the air and the calorimeter.
2. **Incomplete combustion**: Some ethanol may undergo incomplete combustion to CO and C (soot), releasing less energy.
3. **Evaporation of water**: Some water may evaporate, absorbing latent heat.
4. **Heat capacity of the calorimeter**: The container itself absorbs some heat; using only mcΔT for water ignores this.

**Pitfall**: Always convert to kJ before calculating ΔH per mole, and remember that ΔH_c is negative (exothermic).

## Problem 6

We need: \ce{C(s) + 2H2(g) -> CH4(g)}

**Strategy**: Manipulate equations (1), (2), and (3) so they sum to the target equation.

Start with equation (1): \ce{C(s) + O2(g) -> CO2(g)} ΔH = −394

Equation (2) as given: \ce{2H2(g) + O2(g) -> 2H2O(l)} ΔH = −572

Equation (3) reversed? No — let's look at it: \ce{CO2(g) + 2H2O(l) -> CH4(g) + 2O2(g)} ΔH = +891

Sum: (1) + (2) + (3):

\ce{C(s) + O2 + 2H2 + O2 + CO2 + 2H2O -> CO2 + 2H2O + CH4 + 2O2}

Cancel: \ce{O2, O2, CO2, 2H2O} on both sides:

\ce{C(s) + 2H2 -> CH4}

ΔH = (−394) + (−572) + (+891) = **−75 kJ mol⁻¹**

This is the standard enthalpy of formation of methane. Note that the sign is negative, consistent with methane being more stable than its elements.

## Problem 8

**Reaction**: \ce{H2C=CH2 + H–H -> H3C–CH3}

**Bonds broken** (endothermic, positive):
- 1 × C=C = +612
- 1 × H–H = +436
- Total = **+1048 kJ mol⁻¹**

**Bonds formed** (exothermic, negative):
- 1 × C–C = −348
- 2 × C–H = 2 × (−413) = −826
- Total = **−1174 kJ mol⁻¹**

ΔH = Σ(B.E. broken) + Σ(B.E. formed) = +1048 + (−1174) = **−126 kJ mol⁻¹**

**Why estimates differ from experimental values**:

1. **Average bond enthalpies** are used, not the specific bond enthalpies from the actual molecules. The C–H bond in ethene (sp² C) is not exactly the same as the C–H bond in ethane (sp³ C). Published bond enthalpies are averages over many compounds.

2. **States of matter**: Bond enthalpy calculations assume gaseous species. If reactants or products are liquids or solids, the enthalpy changes of vaporisation/fusion are not accounted for.

## Problem 10

**Potential energy profile — exothermic reaction**:

```
Energy
  ↑
  |        ╱‾‾‾‾‾‾‾╲
  |       ╱          ╲___ Uncatalysed
  |      ╱  Eₐ(uncat)  ╲
  |     ╱                ╲___
  |    ╱  ╱‾‾‾‾‾‾‾╲         ╲
  |   ╱  ╱          ╲___      ╲___
  |  ╱  ╱  Eₐ(cat)    ╲ ╲         ╲___
  | ╱  ╱                ╲ ╲             ╲___
  |╱  ╱                  ╲ ╲                 ╲___
  |  ╱                    ╲ ╲                    ╲ Products
  | ╱ Reactants            ╲╲ ← ΔH (negative)      ╲
  |╱                        ╲                        ╲
  └─────────────────────────────────────────────────→ Reaction coordinate
```

**How a catalyst works**: A catalyst provides an **alternative reaction pathway** with a lower activation energy (Eₐ). The enthalpy change (ΔH) is unchanged — catalysis is a kinetic effect, not a thermodynamic one. More reactant molecules now have sufficient energy to overcome the lower barrier, so the reaction proceeds faster at the same temperature.

## Problem 11

**Why the catalyst does not affect equilibrium yield**:

A catalyst lowers the activation energy for **both** the forward and reverse reactions equally. It speeds up the rate at which equilibrium is reached but does not change the position of equilibrium. The equilibrium constant K depends only on ΔG⦵ (and thus on ΔH⦵ and ΔS⦵), none of which are altered by a catalyst.

**Why 450 °C is used**:

The Haber process is exothermic (ΔH = −92 kJ mol⁻¹), so by Le Chatelier's principle, a **low** temperature favours the forward reaction and increases equilibrium yield. However, at low temperatures the reaction is too slow to be economical. The compromise temperature of 450 °C provides a reasonable rate (with the iron catalyst) while still giving an acceptable equilibrium yield. This is a classic **rate vs. yield** compromise.

## Problem 12

**Hess's Law**: The enthalpy change for a reaction depends only on the initial and final states and is independent of the reaction pathway. In other words, if a reaction can be carried out in a single step or in a series of steps, the sum of the enthalpy changes for the individual steps equals the enthalpy change for the overall reaction.

**Why useful**: Many reactions cannot be measured directly — for example, the formation of CO from its elements is impossible to measure cleanly because some CO₂ is always produced. Hess's Law allows ΔH to be calculated indirectly from reactions whose ΔH values are known.

**Example**: The enthalpy of formation of \ce{CO(g)}. Direct combustion of graphite always produces a mixture of CO and CO₂. Instead, we use:

(1) \ce{C(s) + O2(g) -> CO2(g)} ΔH₁ = −394 kJ mol⁻¹ (measurable)
(2) \ce{CO(g) + ½O2(g) -> CO2(g)} ΔH₂ = −283 kJ mol⁻¹ (measurable)

Subtracting (2) from (1): \ce{C(s) + ½O2(g) -> CO(g)} ΔH = −394 − (−283) = −111 kJ mol⁻¹.

**Born–Haber cycle for NaCl**:

```
    Na⁺(g) + Cl(g) + e⁻
         ↑            |
  IE₁=+496            | EA=−349
         |            ↓
    Na(g) + Cl(g)   Na⁺(g) + Cl⁻(g)
    ↑                     |
 ΔH_atom(Na)=+108         |
    |                     |
 Na(s) + Cl(g)            |
    ↑                     |
 ΔH_atom(½Cl₂)=+121       |
    |                     |
 Na(s) + ½Cl₂(g)          |
    |                     |
    |                 −ΔH_lattice = ?
    ↓                     |
 Na(s) + ½Cl₂(g) ────→ NaCl(s)
    ΔH_f = −411
```

**Applying Hess's Law** (one complete path from elements to ionic solid):

ΔH_f = ΔH_atom(Na) + ΔH_atom(½Cl₂) + IE₁ + EA + (−ΔH_lattice)

(Note: lattice enthalpy is defined as the energy released when gaseous ions form the solid, so ΔH_lattice is negative. Some definitions use positive lattice energy. Here we use the convention: NaCl(s) formation path.)

Path: all steps from Na(s) + ½Cl₂(g) to Na⁺(g) + Cl⁻(g), then lattice formation.

ΔH_f = +108 + 121 + 496 + (−349) + ΔH_lattice (where ΔH_lattice is negative)

−411 = +376 + ΔH_lattice

ΔH_lattice = −411 − 376 = **−787 kJ mol⁻¹**

**Note on sign conventions**: If using the definition where lattice enthalpy is the exothermic process of bringing gaseous ions together to form the solid:

Sum of all "upward" (endothermic) steps + ΔH_lattice = ΔH_f
+108 + 121 + 496 + (−349) + ΔH_lattice = −411
+376 + ΔH_lattice = −411
ΔH_lattice = **−787 kJ mol⁻¹**

## Problem 14

**Born–Haber cycle for MgO** (more complex due to double charges):

```
    Mg²⁺(g) + O(g) + 2e⁻
         ↑
    IE₂=+1451  (Mg⁺ → Mg²⁺)
         |
    Mg⁺(g) + O(g) + e⁻
         ↑
    IE₁=+738   (Mg → Mg⁺)
         |
    Mg(g) + O(g)
         ↑                    ↓ EA₁=−141, EA₂=+798
    ΔH_atom(Mg)=+148          |
         |                Mg²⁺(g) + O²⁻(g)
    Mg(s) + O(g)               |
         ↑                    |
    ΔH_atom(½O₂)=+249         |
         |                    |
    Mg(s) + ½O₂(g)            |
         |                    |
         |                 −ΔH_lattice = ?
         ↓                    |
    Mg(s) + ½O₂(g) ────────→ MgO(s)
         ΔH_f = −602
```

**Sum of all endothermic steps to reach gaseous ions**:

ΔH_to_ions = ΔH_atom(Mg) + ΔH_atom(½O₂) + IE₁(Mg) + IE₂(Mg) + EA₁(O) + EA₂(O)

ΔH_to_ions = +148 + 249 + 738 + 1451 + (−141) + 798 = **+3243 kJ mol⁻¹**

Then, applying Hess's Law:

ΔH_f = ΔH_to_ions + ΔH_lattice

−602 = +3243 + ΔH_lattice

ΔH_lattice = −602 − 3243 = **−3845 kJ mol⁻¹**

The lattice enthalpy is very large and exothermic, reflecting the strong electrostatic attraction between the doubly charged Mg²⁺ and O²⁻ ions.

**Why the second electron affinity of oxygen is endothermic** (+798 kJ mol⁻¹):

Adding an electron to O⁻(g) to form O²⁻(g) means forcing a negatively charged electron onto an already negatively charged ion. The electrostatic repulsion between the incoming electron and the O⁻ ion must be overcome. The nucleus's attraction can only partly compensate, resulting in a net energy input. In the Born–Haber cycle, this endothermic step is more than compensated by the very large exothermic lattice enthalpy.

## Problem 15

**Definitions**:

- **ΔH_sol**: Enthalpy change when one mole of an ionic compound dissolves in water (or a specified solvent) to form a solution of infinite dilution.

- **ΔH_hyd**: Enthalpy change when one mole of **gaseous** ions is surrounded by water molecules (hydration). This is always exothermic because ion–dipole attractions release energy.

- **ΔH_lat**: The (negative) lattice enthalpy — the enthalpy change when gaseous ions come together to form the solid lattice. Defined here so that −ΔH_lat is the energy required to separate the lattice into gaseous ions.

**Relationship**: ΔH_sol = ΔH_hyd − ΔH_lat

Equivalently: ΔH_sol = (energy released when ions are hydrated) − (energy released when the lattice forms).

Or: ΔH_sol = ΣΔH_hyd(ions) + (−ΔH_lattice), where −ΔH_lattice is the (positive) energy required to break the lattice apart.

**Why some dissolve exothermically, others endothermically**:

- If |ΔH_hyd| > |ΔH_lat| (hydration releases more energy than is needed to separate ions), ΔH_sol is negative → **exothermic** dissolution (e.g., NaOH, KOH).
- If |ΔH_hyd| < |ΔH_lat|, ΔH_sol is positive → **endothermic** dissolution (e.g., NH₄NO₃, KCl).

Even when dissolution is endothermic, it may still be spontaneous if the **entropy increase** (ΔS > 0 from the dispersal of ions) makes ΔG negative.

## Problem 16

**(a) \ce{H2O(l) -> H2O(g)}**: ΔS **positive**. A liquid → gas phase change greatly increases disorder. Gas molecules have translational freedom and occupy a much larger volume.

**(b) \ce{2H2(g) + O2(g) -> 2H2O(l)}**: ΔS **negative**. 3 moles of gas become 2 moles of liquid. Gas has far higher entropy than liquid; reducing the number of gas molecules and forming a condensed phase decreases disorder.

**(c) \ce{NH4NO3(s) -> NH4+(aq) + NO3-(aq)}**: ΔS **positive**. A highly ordered ionic solid dissolves, and the ions become dispersed and solvated in solution, greatly increasing disorder.

**(d) \ce{CaCO3(s) -> CaO(s) + CO2(g)}**: ΔS **positive**. A solid decomposes to form another solid and a gas. The production of a gas from a solid dramatically increases entropy — gases have much higher entropy.

## Problem 18

ΔG⦵ = ΔH⦵ − TΔS⦵

T = 298 K

**Convert ΔS to kJ**: ΔS⦵ = −187 J K⁻¹ mol⁻¹ = −0.187 kJ K⁻¹ mol⁻¹

ΔG⦵ = −198 − [298 × (−0.187)]

ΔG⦵ = −198 − (−55.7)

ΔG⦵ is negative → the reaction **is spontaneous** under standard conditions at 298 K.

**Pitfall**: Always check units. ΔH is typically in kJ mol⁻¹, ΔS in J K⁻¹ mol⁻¹. You must convert ΔS to kJ K⁻¹ mol⁻¹ before multiplying by T, or convert ΔH to J and the answer back to kJ.

## Problem 19

**(a) Temperature at which ΔG⦵ = 0**:

ΔG⦵ = ΔH⦵ − TΔS⦵ = 0

T = ΔH⦵ / ΔS⦵

ΔH⦵ = +130 kJ mol⁻¹ = +130 000 J mol⁻¹
ΔS⦵ = +430 J K⁻¹ mol⁻¹

T = 130 000 / 430 = **302 K** (approximately)

Above 302 K, the reaction becomes spontaneous (ΔG < 0).

**(b) At 350 K** (above 302 K):

ΔG = 130 000 − (350 × 430) = 130 000 − 150 500 = −20 500 J mol⁻¹ = **−20.5 kJ mol⁻¹** → **spontaneous**.

**At 250 K** (below 302 K):

ΔG = 130 000 − (250 × 430) = 130 000 − 107 500 = +22 500 J mol⁻¹ = **+22.5 kJ mol⁻¹** → **non-spontaneous**.

**Interpretation**: When ΔH > 0 and ΔS > 0, the reaction is entropy-driven. It becomes spontaneous at high temperatures where the TΔS term outweighs the unfavourable ΔH.

## Problem 20

| ΔH | ΔS | Spontaneity |
|----|----|-------------|
| −  | +  | Spontaneous at **all** temperatures (ΔG always negative) |
| +  | −  | **Never** spontaneous (ΔG always positive) |
| −  | −  | Spontaneous at **low** temperatures (enthalpy-driven) |
| +  | +  | Spontaneous at **high** temperatures (entropy-driven) |

**Reasoning from ΔG = ΔH − TΔS**:

- Row 1: negative − T(positive) = always negative.
- Row 2: positive − T(negative) = always positive.
- Row 3: negative − T(negative). At low T, the TΔS term is small, so ΔG < 0. At high T, TΔS may outweigh ΔH.
- Row 4: positive − T(positive). At high T, TΔS > ΔH, making ΔG < 0. At low T, ΔH dominates and ΔG > 0.

## Problem 23

**Combustion reactions**:

(1) \ce{C(graphite) + O2(g) -> CO2(g)} ΔH₁ = −394 kJ mol⁻¹

ΔH_sol = ΔH_hyd − ΔH_lat

## Problem 26

**(a) ΔG⦵ at 298 K**:

ΔH⦵ = −120 kJ mol⁻¹ = −120 000 J mol⁻¹
ΔS⦵ = −400 J K⁻¹ mol⁻¹

ΔG⦵ = ΔH⦵ − TΔS⦵ = −120 000 − [298 × (−400)]
= −120 000 − (−119 200)
= −120 000 + 119 200
= **−800 J mol⁻¹ = −0.8 kJ mol⁻¹**

The reaction is spontaneous at 298 K (just barely — ΔG is only slightly negative).

**(b) Temperature above which reaction becomes non-spontaneous**:

Set ΔG = 0:
ΔH − TΔS = 0
T = ΔH / ΔS = −120 000 / (−400) = **300 K**

Above 300 K, TΔS (the entropy penalty) outweighs ΔH, making ΔG positive → non-spontaneous.

**(c) Molecular explanation**:

When ΔH < 0 and ΔS < 0, the reaction is **enthalpy-driven**: it is favourable because the products are at lower energy than the reactants. However, the negative ΔS means the products are more ordered than the reactants. At low temperatures, the favourable enthalpy term (−ΔH) dominates. At high temperatures, the TΔS term grows in magnitude and the **entropic penalty** of increasing order eventually outweighs the enthalpy benefit. The system "prefers" the more disordered state (reactants) at high T.

## Problem 27

**Enthalpy (H)** is a state function representing the total heat content of a system at constant pressure: H = U + PV. It is not possible to measure the absolute enthalpy of a substance — only changes can be measured.

**Enthalpy change (ΔH)** is the heat absorbed or released by a system at constant pressure for a given process: ΔH = H(products) − H(reactants).

**Why standard enthalpies of formation are defined relative to elements in their standard states**:

Since absolute enthalpies cannot be measured, we need a reference point. By convention, the standard enthalpy of formation of **any element in its standard state is defined as zero** (ΔH_f⦵ = 0). This provides a consistent baseline. The standard enthalpy of formation of a compound is then the enthalpy change when one mole of it is formed from its elements in their standard states.

For example: ΔH_f⦵[\ce{H2O(l)}] is the ΔH for \ce{H2(g) + ½O2(g) -> H2O(l)}. Since ΔH_f⦵[\ce{H2(g)}] = 0 and ΔH_f⦵[\ce{O2(g)}] = 0, the measured ΔH for this reaction is, by definition, ΔH_f⦵[\ce{H2O(l)}].

## Problem 28

**(a) Experimental ΔH_c**:

Moles of propan-1-ol: n = 0.600 g / 60.0 g mol⁻¹ = 0.0100 mol

Heat absorbed by water: q = mcΔT = 150 × 4.18 × (35.0 − 20.0) = 150 × 4.18 × 15.0

q = 150 × 62.7 = 9405 J = 9.41 kJ

ΔH_c = −q / n = −9.41 kJ / 0.0100 mol = **−941 kJ mol⁻¹**

**(b) Percentage error**:

% error = |experimental − literature| / |literature| × 100%

% error = |−941 − (−2021)| / 2021 × 100% = 1080 / 2021 × 100% = **53.4%**

This is a very large error — typical for simple classroom calorimetry.

**Three reasons for the discrepancy**:

1. **Heat loss to the surroundings**: Much of the heat from combustion escapes to the air or heats the calorimeter rather than the water. This is the dominant source of error in spirit-burner experiments.

2. **Incomplete combustion**: The alcohol may burn incompletely, producing CO and soot (C), releasing less than the full combustion enthalpy.

3. **Evaporation of water and alcohol**: The flame may cause some water in the calorimeter to evaporate, and some propan-1-ol may evaporate before burning, both reducing the apparent temperature rise.

4. (Bonus) **Heat capacity of the calorimeter not accounted for**: The copper can/beaker absorbs some heat, but only mcΔT for water is used.
