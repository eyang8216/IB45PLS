## Problem 1

**Step 1 — Understand what is being asked.**
We are given the volume of CO₂ produced (48 cm³) over a time interval (80 s). The average rate is simply the change in the measured quantity divided by the time taken.

**Step 2 — Apply the formula.**
Average rate = Δ(volume of CO₂) / Δt = 48 cm³ / 80 s = **0.60 cm³ s⁻¹**.

**Step 3 — Explain why the instantaneous rate at t = 60 s is lower.**
The reaction rate decreases over time because reactant concentrations fall as the reaction proceeds. At t = 0, the concentration of Br₂ and HCOOH are at their maximum, so the rate is highest. By t = 60 s, a significant amount of reactants has been consumed, so concentrations are lower. Fewer effective collisions occur per unit time, so the instantaneous rate at t = 60 s must be less than the average rate calculated over the first 80 s (which includes the faster early period).

*Pitfall: The average rate blends the faster early rate and the slower later rate, so the instantaneous rate at a time past the midpoint will be lower than the average.*

## Problem 2

**Step 1 — Identify what the mass loss represents.**
The mass lost (2.64 g) is entirely due to CO₂ gas escaping from the flask. So m(CO₂) = 2.64 g.

**Step 2 — Convert mass of CO₂ to moles.**
n(CO₂) = m / M = 2.64 g / 44.0 g mol⁻¹ = **0.0600 mol**.

**Step 3 — Calculate the average rate in mol s⁻¹.**
Time = 3.0 min = 180 s.
Rate = Δn / Δt = 0.0600 mol / 180 s = **3.33 × 10⁻⁴ mol s⁻¹**.

**Step 4 — Why cotton wool is important.**
If the flask is open, acid spray (tiny droplets of HCl solution) may escape along with the CO₂ gas, causing a mass loss greater than that due to CO₂ alone. Cotton wool allows gas to escape while trapping liquid spray, so the mass loss accurately reflects only the CO₂ produced.

## Problem 3

**Step 1 — Use the stoichiometric ratio.**
From the balanced equation: 2H₂O₂ → 2H₂O + O₂.

For every 1 mole of O₂ produced, 2 moles of H₂O₂ are consumed. So the rate of consumption of H₂O₂ is twice the rate of formation of O₂.

**Step 2 — Calculate.**
Rate of consumption of H₂O₂ = 2 × 0.012 = **0.024 mol dm⁻³ s⁻¹**.

**Step 3 — Write the rate expression.**
The general relationship is:
−(1/2) × d[H₂O₂]/dt = +(1/1) × d[O₂]/dt

Equivalently: −d[H₂O₂]/dt = 2 × d[O₂]/dt.

*Pitfall: The negative sign on the reactant side indicates consumption. Students often forget to account for the stoichiometric coefficient.*

## Problem 4

**Step 1 — State the two conditions for an effective collision.**
For a collision to lead to a reaction, it must satisfy two conditions:
1. The colliding molecules must possess kinetic energy equal to or greater than the activation energy, Ea.
2. The molecules must collide with the correct orientation (appropriate geometry) for bonds to break and form.

**Step 2 — Apply to this reaction.**
Ea = 183 kJ mol⁻¹, which is relatively high. At 300 °C (573 K), the average kinetic energy of gas molecules is given by (3/2)RT ≈ 7.1 kJ mol⁻¹ — far below Ea. Only the small fraction of molecules in the high-energy tail of the Maxwell–Boltzmann distribution have KE ≥ 183 kJ mol⁻¹. Even among those high-energy collisions, many will have incorrect orientation (the two HI molecules must approach such that H atoms can pair up and I atoms can pair up). Hence only a very small fraction of total collisions are effective.

## Problem 5

**Step 1 — Relate Ea to the rate constant.**
From the Arrhenius equation, k = A·e^(−Ea/RT). For the same A and T, a smaller Ea gives a larger e^(−Ea/RT) and therefore a larger k.

**Step 2 — Compare the two reactions.**
Reaction A (Ea = 50 kJ mol⁻¹) has a much lower activation energy than Reaction B (Ea = 150 kJ mol⁻¹). Therefore, at the same temperature, a much larger fraction of collisions for Reaction A have energy ≥ Ea, so Reaction A has the **larger rate constant**.

**Step 3 — Additional factor.**
The third factor beyond energy and collision frequency is **orientation** (steric factor). Even if colliding molecules have sufficient energy, they must collide in the correct orientation for bond-breaking and bond-forming to occur. This is accounted for in the pre-exponential factor A of the Arrhenius equation.

## Problem 6

**(a) Zinc powder vs zinc granules.**
Zinc powder has a much larger total surface area than zinc granules of the same mass. A larger surface area means more zinc atoms are exposed at the solid–liquid interface and available for collision with H⁺ ions from the acid. According to collision theory, this increases the frequency of effective collisions per unit time, so the rate of reaction increases significantly.

**(b) Higher acid concentration.**
Doubling the concentration of H₂SO₄ from 1.0 to 2.0 mol dm⁻³ doubles the number of H⁺ ions per unit volume. This approximately doubles the frequency of collisions between H⁺ ions and the zinc surface (since the zinc surface area is unchanged). As a result, the rate of reaction increases — approximately doubling, assuming first order with respect to H⁺.

## Problem 7

**Step 1 — Understand the effect of pressure on gases.**
Reducing the volume of the container increases the total pressure. For gases, pressure is directly proportional to concentration (P ∝ n/V = c at constant T). So reducing the volume increases the concentration of both NO and O₂.

**Step 2 — Apply collision theory.**
Higher concentrations mean more molecules of NO and O₂ per unit volume. The molecules are closer together, so they collide more frequently. Specifically, the collision frequency between NO and O₂ molecules increases. Since a constant fraction of collisions are effective (same T, same Ea), a greater collision frequency means more effective collisions per unit time, and therefore a higher reaction rate.

## Problem 8

**Step 1 — Sketch the Maxwell–Boltzmann distribution.**
The curve is asymmetric: it rises from the origin, peaks at the most probable energy, and tails off towards high energies. The area under the entire curve represents the total number of molecules. A vertical line marks the activation energy, Ea. The area to the right of this line represents the fraction of molecules with KE ≥ Ea — the reactive fraction.

**Step 2 — Effect of a 10 °C increase.**
When temperature increases from 25 °C to 35 °C:
- The peak of the curve shifts slightly to the right and becomes slightly lower and broader.
- The high-energy tail extends further to the right.
- Crucially, the area under the curve beyond Ea **increases significantly** (often by a factor of ∼2), even though the average kinetic energy increases only slightly (from about 3.7 to 3.8 kJ mol⁻¹).

**Step 3 — Why a small temperature rise causes a large rate increase.**
The fraction of molecules with KE ≥ Ea is exponentially sensitive to temperature (Arrhenius: e^(−Ea/RT)). A small increase in T reduces the magnitude of −Ea/RT, making the exponential term substantially larger. This means a disproportionately large increase in the number of molecules surpassing Ea, hence a large increase in rate.

*Pitfall: Students often incorrectly say the activation energy decreases. Ea is unchanged by temperature; it is the fraction of molecules exceeding Ea that changes.*

## Problem 9

**Step 1 — Recall the half-life expressions.**
- Zero order: t₁/₂ = [A]₀ / 2k (depends on initial concentration)
- First order: t₁/₂ = ln 2 / k (constant, independent of [A]₀)
- Second order: t₁/₂ = 1 / k[A]₀ (depends on initial concentration, inversely)

**Step 2 — Why this observation points to zero order.**
The fact that the half-life changes when the initial concentration changes immediately rules out first-order kinetics. For zero order, t₁/₂ = [A]₀/(2k), so the half-life is directly proportional to the initial concentration. (If experimental data showed t₁/₂ decreasing with increasing [A]₀, this would suggest fractional or negative order, which is unusual; the key diagnostic is that t₁/₂ is not constant.)

**Step 3 — Concentration–time graph.**
The graph of [NH₃] (y-axis, mol dm⁻³) against time (x-axis, s) is a straight line with a constant negative gradient equal to −k. The line starts at [NH₃]₀ and descends linearly to zero. This linearity is the definitive feature of zero-order kinetics.

## Problem 10

**Step 1 — Sketch description.**
Axes: y-axis = "Number of molecules" (or "Fraction of molecules"), x-axis = "Kinetic energy."
Draw the Maxwell–Boltzmann curve: asymmetric, rising from origin to a peak at moderate energy, then tailing off. Mark a vertical line at Ea (uncatalysed).

**Step 2 — Effect of a catalyst.**
The Maxwell–Boltzmann distribution curve itself **does not change** when a catalyst is added — the temperature is the same, so the distribution of molecular kinetic energies is identical.

**Step 3 — Why the rate increases.**
The catalyst provides an alternative reaction pathway with a lower activation energy, Ea(cat). On the graph, this is shown by drawing a second vertical line at Ea(cat), positioned to the left of the original Ea line. Because Ea(cat) < Ea, a much larger area under the curve lies to the right of Ea(cat). This means a much larger fraction of molecules now have sufficient energy to react via the catalysed pathway, increasing the rate of reaction.

*Pitfall: Students frequently draw the curve itself moving to the right or changing shape. The curve does NOT change — only the position of the Ea threshold moves.*

## Problem 11

**Step 1 — Determine the order with respect to H₂O₂.**
Compare experiments where [H₂O₂] changes but other conditions are constant:

## Problem 12

**Step 1 — Determine order with respect to A.**
Compare Exp 1 and Exp 2: [A] doubles (0.10 → 0.20), [B] is constant (0.10).
Rate changes from 1.0 × 10⁻³ to 4.0 × 10⁻³ — it quadruples (×4).
Since 2ⁿ = 4, n = 2. The reaction is **second order** with respect to A.

**Step 2 — Determine order with respect to B.**
Compare Exp 1 and Exp 3: [B] doubles (0.10 → 0.20), [A] is constant (0.10).
Rate remains 1.0 × 10⁻³ — no change.
Since 2ᵐ = 1, m = 0. The reaction is **zero order** with respect to B.

**Step 3 — Overall order and rate law.**
Overall order = 2 + 0 = **2** (second order overall).
Rate law: rate = k[A]²[B]⁰ = **k[A]²**.

**Step 4 — Calculate k.**
Using Exp 1: k = rate / [A]² = (1.0 × 10⁻³) / (0.10)² = (1.0 × 10⁻³) / 0.010 = **0.10**.
Units: rate (mol dm⁻³ s⁻¹) = k × (mol dm⁻³)² → k = mol dm⁻³ s⁻¹ / (mol² dm⁻⁶) = **dm³ mol⁻¹ s⁻¹**.

## Problem 13

**Step 1 — Derive the units from the rate law.**
General rate law: rate = k[A]ⁿ, where rate has units mol dm⁻³ s⁻¹ and [A] has units mol dm⁻³.
So: mol dm⁻³ s⁻¹ = k × (mol dm⁻³)ⁿ → k has units (mol dm⁻³)^(1−n) s⁻¹.

**(a) Zero order overall (n = 0):**
k units = (mol dm⁻³)^(1−0) s⁻¹ = **mol dm⁻³ s⁻¹**.

**(b) First order overall (n = 1):**
k units = (mol dm⁻³)^(1−1) s⁻¹ = (mol dm⁻³)⁰ s⁻¹ = **s⁻¹**.

**Step 2 — Why units depend on order.**
The rate always has units mol dm⁻³ s⁻¹. The concentration term [A]ⁿ contributes units (mol dm⁻³)ⁿ. To balance the equation dimensionally, k must supply the remaining units. Different values of n require different units for k.

*Pitfall: Students often memorise units without understanding that they arise from dimensional analysis of the rate law.*

## Problem 14

**Step 1 — Check for first-order behaviour.**
A reaction is first order if the half-life is constant. Inspect the data:

| Time / min | [N₂O₅] / mol dm⁻³ |
|---|---|
| 0 | 0.200 |
| 10 | 0.100 |
| 20 | 0.050 |
| 30 | 0.025 |

Each 10-minute interval sees the concentration halve: 0.200 → 0.100 → 0.050 → 0.025. The half-life is a constant **10 minutes**, regardless of the starting concentration. This is the defining feature of a first-order reaction.

**Step 2 — What zero-order data would look like.**
For a zero-order reaction, [A] = [A]₀ − kt. The concentration would decrease by a constant *amount* per unit time, not by a constant *fraction*. For example, if k = 0.010 mol dm⁻³ min⁻¹, the data would be:

| Time / min | 0 | 10 | 20 | 30 |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| [N₂O₅] / mol dm⁻³ | 0.200 | 0.100 | 0.000 | — |

In zero order, the concentration would fall linearly and reach zero after a finite time, with each successive half-life being shorter than the previous one.

## Problem 15

**Step 1 — Interpret the rate–concentration graph.**
A horizontal straight line when rate (y) is plotted against [R] (x) means the rate does not depend on [R]. The rate is constant regardless of concentration. This is the hallmark of a **zero-order** reaction: rate = k.

**Step 3 — Deduction.**
Both graphs independently confirm that the reaction is **zero order** with respect to R.

## Problem 16

**Step 1 — Identify the rate-determining step (RDS).**
The rate-determining step is the **slowest elementary step** in the mechanism. Here, Step 1 (X + Y → W) is labelled "slow" and Step 2 is "fast", so **Step 1 is the RDS**.

**Step 2 — Molecularity of each step.**
- Step 1 (X + Y → W): two reactant molecules collide → **bimolecular**.
- Step 2 (W + X → Z): two reactant molecules collide → **bimolecular**.

**Step 3 — Predict the rate law from the mechanism.**
The rate of the overall reaction is determined by the RDS. For the elementary step X + Y → W, the rate is proportional to the concentrations of the species colliding: rate = k₁[X][Y]. Since this is the slow step and no prior equilibrium is involved, the predicted rate law is: **rate = k[X][Y]**.
This is first order in X, first order in Y, and second order overall.

## Problem 17

**Step 1 — Identify the rate-determining step.**
Step 2 (N₂O₂ + H₂ → N₂O + H₂O) is the slow step, so it determines the overall rate: rate = k₂[N₂O₂][H₂].

**Step 2 — Eliminate the intermediate.**
N₂O₂ is a reaction intermediate — it does not appear in the overall balanced equation, so its concentration cannot appear in the final rate law. We must express [N₂O₂] in terms of reactant concentrations.

**Step 3 — Use the fast equilibrium from Step 1.**
Step 1: 2NO ⇌ N₂O₂ (fast equilibrium).
At equilibrium: k₁[NO]² = k₋₁[N₂O₂] → [N₂O₂] = (k₁/k₋₁)[NO]² = K_eq[NO]².

**Step 4 — Substitute into the rate law.**
rate = k₂ × (K_eq[NO]²) × [H₂] = (k₂ × K_eq) × [NO]²[H₂].

Let k = k₂ × K_eq (the observed rate constant). Then: **rate = k[NO]²[H₂]**.

This matches the experimental rate law, so the proposed mechanism is consistent.

*Pitfall: When a fast equilibrium precedes the RDS, always solve the equilibrium expression for the intermediate concentration before substituting.*

## Problem 18

**Step 1 — Write the two-point form of the Arrhenius equation.**
ln(k₂/k₁) = (Ea/R) × (1/T₁ − 1/T₂).

**Step 2 — Convert units.**
Ea = 75.0 kJ mol⁻¹ = 75000 J mol⁻¹. R = 8.31 J K⁻¹ mol⁻¹. T₁ = 298 K, T₂ = 308 K.

**Step 3 — Calculate 1/T₁ − 1/T₂.**
1/298 − 1/308 = (308 − 298)/(298 × 308) = 10 / 91784 = **1.0895 × 10⁻⁴ K⁻¹**.

**Step 4 — Calculate Ea/R × (1/T₁ − 1/T₂).**
(75000 / 8.31) × (1.0895 × 10⁻⁴) = 9025.3 × 1.0895 × 10⁻⁴ = **0.9834**.

**Step 5 — Solve for k₂.**
ln(k₂/k₁) = 0.9834 → k₂/k₁ = e^0.9834 = **2.673**.
k₂ = 2.673 × (2.0 × 10⁻³) = **5.35 × 10⁻³ s⁻¹**.

**Step 6 — Comment.**
k₂/k₁ ≈ 2.7, meaning the rate increases by a factor of about 2.7 for a 10 °C rise. This is broadly consistent with the "rule of thumb" that rates approximately double for a 10 °C increase, although the exact factor depends on Ea — higher Ea gives a larger factor. Here Ea = 75 kJ mol⁻¹, which is moderately high, so a factor of ∼2.7 is reasonable.

## Problem 19

**Step 1 — Recall the graphical form of the Arrhenius equation.**
ln k = ln A − (Ea/R)(1/T). This is a straight line equation: y = c + mx, where y = ln k, x = 1/T, slope m = −Ea/R, and y-intercept c = ln A.

**Step 2 — Extract the slope and solve for Ea.**
The gradient of the ln k vs 1/T graph is −8.75 × 10³ K.
Since gradient = −Ea/R:
−Ea/R = −8.75 × 10³ K
Ea = (8.75 × 10³ K) × R = 8.75 × 10³ × 8.31 J K⁻¹ mol⁻¹ = **72,712.5 J mol⁻¹** = **72.7 kJ mol⁻¹**.

**Step 3 — Verify with a data point (optional).**
Using T = 290 K: ln k = −8.80, 1/T = 3.45 × 10⁻³.
ln A = ln k + (Ea/R)(1/T) = −8.80 + (8750 × 0.00345) = −8.80 + 30.19 = 21.39.
This gives A = e^21.39 ≈ 2 × 10⁹ s⁻¹, a reasonable pre-exponential factor.

## Problem 20

**Step 1 — Determine the overall order.**
The rate law is rate = k[CH₃CHO]^(3/2). The overall order is the sum of exponents: **3/2 (or 1.5)**, a fractional order.

**Step 2 — Determine the units of k.**
rate (mol dm⁻³ s⁻¹) = k × (mol dm⁻³)^(3/2).
k = mol dm⁻³ s⁻¹ / (mol^(3/2) dm⁻⁹/²) = mol^(1 − 3/2) dm^(−3 + 9/2) s⁻¹ = mol^(−1/2) dm^(3/2) s⁻¹ = **dm^(3/2) mol^(−1/2) s⁻¹**.

**Step 3 — Could this be an elementary step?**
No. An elementary step involves molecules colliding directly as written. The molecularity must be an integer (1 = unimolecular, 2 = bimolecular, 3 = termolecular). A fractional order of 3/2 cannot correspond to any elementary step — it must arise from a multi-step mechanism. The rate law tells us this is a **complex reaction** with a mechanism involving multiple elementary steps.

## Problem 22

**Step 1 — Adsorption.**
Reactant molecules approach the solid catalyst surface and bind to active sites through adsorption. This is typically chemisorption, involving the formation of weak chemical bonds between the reactant and the catalyst surface atoms.

**Step 2 — Bond weakening.**
The adsorption process stretches and weakens existing bonds within the reactant molecules. By holding the reactants in close proximity and in a specific orientation on the surface, the catalyst lowers the energy required to break bonds and form new ones. The activation energy for the catalysed pathway, Ea(cat), is therefore lower than for the uncatalysed homogeneous reaction.

**Step 3 — Desorption.**
After the reaction occurs on the surface, the product molecules desorb from the active sites, freeing them for further catalytic cycles.

**Step 4 — Example.**
The Haber process: \ce{N2(g) + 3H2(g) <=> 2NH3(g)}, catalysed by solid iron (Fe). Nitrogen and hydrogen molecules adsorb onto the iron surface, the strong N≡N triple bond is weakened, and hydrogen atoms react to form NH₃, which then desorbs.

## Problem 24

**(a) Increasing the concentration of a reactant.**
k remains **unchanged**. The rate constant k depends only on temperature and the presence of a catalyst (Arrhenius: k = A·e^(−Ea/RT)). Concentration does not appear in the Arrhenius equation. Increasing concentration increases the *rate* (more collisions) but does not affect the *rate constant*.

**(b) Increasing the temperature.**
k **increases**. According to the Arrhenius equation, k = A·e^(−Ea/RT), increasing T makes the exponent −Ea/RT less negative, so e^(−Ea/RT) increases. A larger fraction of molecules exceed Ea, so k increases. Note that k increases exponentially, not linearly, with T.

**(c) Adding a catalyst.**
k **increases**. A catalyst provides an alternative pathway with a lower activation energy, Ea(cat) < Ea. In the Arrhenius equation, this smaller Ea makes e^(−Ea/RT) larger, so k increases. The catalyst does not change the temperature or the concentrations; it changes the reaction pathway.

*Pitfall: Students often confuse "rate" with "rate constant." Concentration changes affect rate but not k. Temperature and catalysts affect k (and therefore rate).*
