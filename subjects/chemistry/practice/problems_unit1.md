# Unit 1: Stoichiometric Relationships — Practice Problems

## Problem 1
Calculate the amount (in mol) of sodium chloride, NaCl, present in a 5.85 g sample. (Na = 23.0, Cl = 35.5)

## Problem 2
How many molecules are present in 0.500 mol of carbon dioxide, CO₂? Express your answer in terms of Avogadro's number, Nₐ.

## Problem 3
Calculate the total number of atoms in a 4.00 g sample of helium gas. (He = 4.00, L = 6.02 × 10²³ mol⁻¹)

## Problem 4
A compound was found to contain 40.0% carbon, 6.67% hydrogen, and 53.33% oxygen by mass. Determine its empirical formula. (C = 12.0, H = 1.00, O = 16.0)

## Problem 5
A compound has the empirical formula CH₂O and a molar mass of 180 g mol⁻¹. Determine its molecular formula. (C = 12.0, H = 1.00, O = 16.0)

## Problem 6
Complete combustion of 1.20 g of a hydrocarbon (a compound containing only carbon and hydrogen) produced 3.52 g of carbon dioxide and 2.16 g of water. Determine the empirical formula of the hydrocarbon. (C = 12.0, H = 1.00, O = 16.0)

## Problem 7
A reaction vessel contains 2.50 mol of hydrogen gas and 1.00 mol of oxygen gas, which react according to the equation: 2 H₂ + O₂ → 2 H₂O. Identify the limiting reagent and calculate the maximum amount of water (in mol) that can be produced.

## Problem 8
A 10.0 g sample of calcium carbonate, CaCO₃, is heated strongly. It decomposes to calcium oxide and carbon dioxide: CaCO₃ → CaO + CO₂. The mass of calcium oxide actually obtained is 4.20 g. Calculate the percentage yield of the reaction. (Ca = 40.0, C = 12.0, O = 16.0)

## Problem 9
Calculate the concentration (in mol dm⁻³) of a solution prepared by dissolving 4.00 g of sodium hydroxide, NaOH, in distilled water and making the solution up to 250 cm³. (Na = 23.0, O = 16.0, H = 1.00)

## Problem 10
A student takes 25.0 cm³ of a 2.00 mol dm⁻³ solution of hydrochloric acid and dilutes it with distilled water to a final volume of 250 cm³. Calculate the concentration of the diluted hydrochloric acid solution.

## Problem 11
A sample of impure calcium carbonate is treated with 50.0 cm³ of 0.200 mol dm⁻³ hydrochloric acid, which reacts according to the equation: CaCO₃ + 2 HCl → CaCl₂ + H₂O + CO₂. After the reaction is complete, the excess hydrochloric acid is titrated with 0.100 mol dm⁻³ sodium hydroxide solution. Exactly 20.0 cm³ of the sodium hydroxide solution is required to neutralise the excess acid. Calculate the mass of pure calcium carbonate that was present in the original sample. (Ca = 40.0, C = 12.0, O = 16.0)

## Problem 12
Calculate the volume of carbon dioxide gas produced (measured at STP: 273 K and 100 kPa) when 10.0 g of calcium carbonate decomposes completely according to the equation: CaCO₃ → CaO + CO₂. The molar volume of an ideal gas at STP is 22.7 dm³ mol⁻¹. (Ca = 40.0, C = 12.0, O = 16.0)

## Problem 13
A 0.100 mol sample of nitrogen gas is held in a container at 273 K and a pressure of 100 kPa. Using the ideal gas equation PV = nRT, calculate the volume of the gas in dm³ and verify that your answer is consistent with the molar volume at STP. (R = 8.31 J K⁻¹ mol⁻¹)

## Problem 14
A student reacts 2.70 g of aluminium metal with 100 cm³ of 1.00 mol dm⁻³ hydrochloric acid according to the equation: 2 Al + 6 HCl → 2 AlCl₃ + 3 H₂. Identify the limiting reagent and calculate the volume of hydrogen gas produced, measured at STP where the molar volume is 22.7 dm³ mol⁻¹. (Al = 27.0)

## Problem 15
A 3.00 g sample of impure magnesium ribbon reacts with excess hydrochloric acid: Mg + 2 HCl → MgCl₂ + H₂. The hydrogen gas collected occupies 2.27 dm³ when measured at STP (molar volume = 22.7 dm³ mol⁻¹). Calculate the percentage purity by mass of the magnesium sample. (Mg = 24.3)

## Problem 16
Explain the difference between an empirical formula and a molecular formula. Give an example of two different compounds that share the same empirical formula but have different molecular formulas.

## Problem 17
Explain why real gases deviate from ideal gas behaviour at high pressures and low temperatures. In your answer, refer to the two assumptions of the kinetic molecular theory that break down under these conditions.

## Problem 18
State the conditions of temperature and pressure under which a real gas most closely approximates ideal behaviour, and explain why the gas behaves ideally under those conditions.

## Problem 19
Explain what is meant by the term "limiting reagent." Use a concrete example (either a chemical reaction or a non-chemistry analogy) to illustrate why the limiting reagent determines the maximum amount of product that can form.

## Problem 20
A student claims that "one mole of any gas occupies the same volume at any given temperature and pressure." Is this statement correct? Explain your reasoning and state any assumptions that must be made for the statement to hold true.

## Problem 21
In a titration, 25.0 cm³ of sodium hydroxide solution of unknown concentration is neutralised by 25.0 cm³ of 0.100 mol dm⁻³ sulfuric acid, H₂SO₄. The equation for the reaction is: 2 NaOH + H₂SO₄ → Na₂SO₄ + 2 H₂O. Calculate the concentration of the sodium hydroxide solution. Then describe how you would prepare 100 cm³ of a 0.0500 mol dm⁻³ NaOH solution by diluting this original sodium hydroxide solution.

## Problem 22
A 0.600 g sample of a compound containing only carbon, hydrogen, and oxygen is burned completely in excess oxygen. The products are 0.880 g of carbon dioxide and 0.360 g of water. Determine the empirical formula of the compound. Given that its molar mass is 60.0 g mol⁻¹, determine the molecular formula. (C = 12.0, H = 1.00, O = 16.0)

## Problem 23
A student mixes 10.0 cm³ of 0.400 mol dm⁻³ barium chloride solution with 20.0 cm³ of 0.200 mol dm⁻³ sodium sulfate solution. A white precipitate of barium sulfate forms according to the equation: BaCl₂ + Na₂SO₄ → BaSO₄ + 2 NaCl. Calculate the mass of barium sulfate precipitate produced. (Ba = 137, S = 32.0, O = 16.0)
